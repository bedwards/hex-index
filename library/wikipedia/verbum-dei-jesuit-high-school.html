In the summer of 1962, amidst the simmering heat of Watts, Los Angeles, a group of missionaries made a decision that defied the prevailing logic of the era. They did not build their school in the manicured suburbs of the Westside or the quiet enclaves of the San Fernando Valley. Instead, they planted a flag in the very neighborhood that the city had largely forgotten. Bishop Joseph Francis, S.V.D., stood at the helm of this endeavor, leading a founding team authorized by Cardinal James McIntyre to address a specific, glaring inequity: the educational abandonment of the Black community in South Los Angeles. They named it Verbum Dei, the "Word of God," a title chosen to reflect the divine origin of their mission, but the reality they built was far more tangible than theology alone. It was a private, all-boys college preparatory school, a fortress of learning designed to serve students who were economically and academically underserved, offering them a ladder out of a system that had seemingly closed the door.

For decades, the school stood as a beacon of possibility in a landscape often defined by limitation. The Society of the Divine Word, the religious order that established the institution, remained its spiritual and administrative backbone for over forty years. Their presence was not merely ceremonial; it was woven into the daily fabric of the campus, guiding young men who were navigating the complexities of poverty and the pressures of a neighborhood on the brink. The school's promise was simple yet radical: a 100% success rate in sending graduating seniors to college. In an environment where college attendance for many was a statistical anomaly, Verbum Dei made it a certainty. They sent their graduates to the most selective universities in the United States, proving that the zip code of one's birth need not determine the trajectory of one's life.

Yet, the path from the founding in 1962 to the present day was not a straight line of uninterrupted triumph. It was a narrative of resilience, punctuated by periods of profound crisis that threatened to erase the school's existence entirely. The 1980s marked the beginning of a slow, painful decline. The educational performance of the student body began to falter, a symptom of broader societal fractures that were widening in South Los Angeles. By the 1990s, the situation had deteriorated into a full-blown emergency. The neighborhood was engulfed in gang wars, a period of violence that claimed countless lives and shattered the sense of safety that is fundamental to any learning environment.

The human cost of this era cannot be overstated. While the school walls offered a measure of protection, the reality outside was brutal. Young men were drawn into conflicts that had nothing to do with them and everything to do with the desperation of their surroundings. Enrollment plummeted as families fled or simply could not risk sending their children to school. Inside the administration, instability reigned. The institution that had once been a model of consistency was now teetering on the edge of collapse. It was a time when the very idea of a college-preparatory school for Black youth in Watts seemed like a relic of a more hopeful past, one that the harsh realities of the 1990s had rendered obsolete.

The turning point, when it came, was not a spontaneous recovery but a calculated intervention born of desperation. In the mid-1990s, significant financial aid began to flow into the school, allowing for critical improvements to the campus infrastructure. New buildings rose to replace the decaying ones, a physical manifestation of the hope that was being rekindled. However, the structural and spiritual crisis required more than just new bricks and mortar. In 2000, Cardinal Roger Mahony, recognizing the gravity of the situation, issued a stark ultimatum to the California Province of the Society of Jesus. He asked the Jesuits to take over the administration of the school, asserting that without a radical transformation, Verbum Dei would be forced to close its doors permanently by 2006.

The Jesuits accepted the challenge, bringing with them a new leadership team led by Fr. Bill Wood. This was not a merger of convenience; it was a rescue mission. At this juncture, the school officially became recognized as a Jesuit institution, transitioning from the sponsorship of the Divine Word Missionaries, whose long-time presence had ended in December 2006 when Br. Richard "Rich" Morrill, S.V.D., a beloved faculty member, passed away after a long battle with terminal illness. The transition marked a new chapter, one defined by a rigorous adherence to the Jesuit educational philosophy of cura personalis, or care for the whole person. But the Jesuits also knew that spiritual care alone would not solve the economic barriers facing their students. They needed a model that could generate revenue while simultaneously providing the real-world experience that traditional schools often failed to offer.

In 2002, Verbum Dei made a bold strategic pivot by joining the Cristo Rey Network. This decision fundamentally reshaped the school's scholastic model, introducing the Corporate Work Study Program (CWSP). The premise was innovative and counterintuitive in the context of American high school education: the students themselves would help pay for their own tuition. The model operates on a simple economic exchange. Local white-collar companies and non-profit entities in the Los Angeles metropolitan area "donate" a full-time equivalent internship position to the school for a fee of $28,000. This fee is used to cover a significant portion of the student's educational costs, effectively defraying the expense for families who might otherwise be unable to afford a private education.

The mechanics of the program are precise and demanding. Each student works five full days a month on a rotational basis, filling the position of a single intern shared among four students. On their remaining days, they attend classes and participate in extracurricular activities, balancing the rigors of a college-preparatory curriculum with the demands of the corporate world. The results of this model have been nothing short of remarkable. The student interns, selected from low-income families, maintain an average work attendance of 99 percent, a statistic that often surpasses that of their adult counterparts in the corporate sector. Ninety-seven percent of these young men receive a performance evaluation of "good" or "excellent" from their supervisors.

The experience is intended to acclimate students to corporate culture, build their character, and provide a motive for seeking higher education and productive careers.

This is not merely a job placement program; it is a transformative educational experience. While almost all students benefit from these internship experiences, the long-term impact is even more profound. Thirty-seven percent of seniors have remained at the company where they began their freshman year, building relationships and gaining a level of institutional knowledge that gives them a distinct advantage in the professional world. The school provides the necessary safety net for these students, covering transportation, insurance, workers' compensation, and work permits, ensuring that the barriers to entry are removed. The CWSP staff, led by a dedicated program coordinator, co-manages the students, ensuring that their transition from the classroom to the boardroom is seamless and supported.

On July 1, 2022, the school completed its transformation by officially changing its name to Verbum Dei Jesuit High School, a move that reflected the full transition to a school solely sponsored by the Jesuits. The Religious of the Sacred Heart of Mary, who have long been part of the school's history, continue to provide a sister to the staff, maintaining a vital link to the school's broader religious heritage. Today, the institution holds joint accreditation by the Western Association of Schools and Colleges (WASC) and the Western Catholic Educational Association (WCEA), as well as accreditation by the USA West Province of the Society of Jesus, solidifying its status as a premier educational institution.

The legacy of Verbum Dei is not just found in its administrative structure or its financial model; it is etched into the athletic and cultural achievements of its students. The school's teams have won national championships in various sports, a testament to the discipline and excellence fostered within its walls. In basketball, the school dominated the CIF championships from 1969 through 1974, with additional titles in 1979, 1994-1995, 1998-1999, and 2002-2003. They were runners-up in 1978, 1990, 1993, 1996, and 2004, demonstrating a consistency of excellence that spanned decades. The football team secured CIF championships in 1981, 1982, and 2006, while the track and field team claimed titles in 1993, 1997, and 1998.

But the true measure of the school's success lies in the lives of its alumni. The roster of graduates reads like a who's who of American athletics, a testament to the school's ability to identify and nurture talent. Raymond Lewis, a 1971 graduate, went on to become a standout basketball athlete at Cal State Los Angeles. Donny Daniels, class of 1972, served as an assistant basketball coach for the UCLA Bruins. The list of NBA athletes is extensive and impressive: Roy Hamilton (1975), David Greenwood (1975), Ken Austin (1979), Vernon Maxwell (1979), Kenny Fields (1980), Andre Miller (1994), and Amir Johnson (2005, transferred). In the NFL, graduates include Hardy Nickerson (1983), who later became a coach, Kenechi Udeze (2000), Akeem Ayers (2007), Reggie Dunn (2007), and Jerron "Blind Boy" Paxton (2007), who found his voice in music.

These individuals are not just statistics; they are the living proof that the mission of Verbum Dei has succeeded against the odds. They are the sons of a community that was once written off, who were given the tools, the discipline, and the opportunity to excel on the national stage. Their stories are interwoven with the history of the school, a narrative of struggle and triumph that continues to unfold. The school has also expanded its extracurricular offerings to include 22 different activities, including a unique partnership with an underground film company to produce films, with the assistance of Hollywood luminaries like J.J. Abrams and Will Smith. This partnership underscores the school's commitment to providing its students with access to the highest levels of professional opportunity, regardless of their background.

The journey of Verbum Dei Jesuit High School is a story of evolution. It began as a mission to serve the Black community of South Los Angeles, founded by the Divine Word Missionaries in 1962. It weathered the storms of the 1980s and the violence of the 1990s, surviving when many feared it would not. It reinvented itself in the 21st century, embracing the Jesuit tradition and the innovative Cristo Rey model to ensure its survival and growth. Today, it serves not only the Black community but also the Latino community of South Los Angeles, expanding its mission to meet the needs of a changing demographic.

The school's success has not gone unnoticed. It has attracted the attention of major philanthropists and media outlets, including a feature in the Washington Post by George Will, a segment on 60 Minutes, and a major investment from the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, which cited the "success of innovative urban Catholic school" as a model for the future of education. The school's story has been chronicled in books such as More Than a Dream: The Cristo Rey Story by G.R. Kearney, which highlights how one school's vision is changing the world.
