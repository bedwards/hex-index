On a quiet hillside overlooking the city of Nablus in the West Bank, a small community of roughly 900 people gathers once a year to perform an ancient ritual that has survived empires, conquests, and millennia of theological warfare. They are the Samaritans, also known as Israelite Samaritans, and they stand as the living embodiment of a faith that insists it is the only true preservation of the original Mosaic covenant. In 2025, their population remains stubbornly small, a number they view not as a tragedy but as the fulfillment of a specific prophecy: "You will be left few in number." This is not merely a statistic of demographic decline; it is a testament to endurance. They are an Abrahamic monotheistic ethnic religion that has refused to vanish, maintaining a distinct spiritual lineage that traces back to the very origins of Israelite identity, while existing in a state of perpetual tension with their Jewish neighbors who share a common ancestry but diverged centuries ago over the location of God's chosen dwelling place.

The Samaritan faith is built upon a foundation of absolute exclusivity regarding geography and scripture. For these people, Mount Gerizim is not just a mountain; it is the axis mundi, the true Holy Place where God resides and where the Binding of Isaac took place. This stands in direct, irreconcilable opposition to Jewish tradition, which locates that binding at Jerusalem's Temple Mount. To the Samaritan believer, Jerusalem is a site of historical error, a city that only gained religious importance because a faction of dissenters left the true sanctuary at Gerizim to follow High Priest Eli to Shiloh and eventually south to Judah. This theological schism is not an abstract academic debate; it has dictated the lives, wars, and survival strategies of two communities for over two thousand years. The Samaritans hold that their version of the Torah—the Samaritan Pentateuch—is the only uncorrupted text given by God to Moses, rejecting the later prophetic writings and historical books that form the Hebrew Bible, aside from a variation of the Book of Joshua which they do not regard as scripture.

The Great Schism: A Matter of Salt and Stone

To understand the Samaritans today, one must look back to a fracture in history that traditional historiography describes less as a slow drift and more as a violent civil war. According to the 14th-century chronicle written by Abu l-Fath, the split was triggered by a specific act of sacrilege involving the High Priesthood. The narrative details a conflict between Eli, son of Yafni, a man of wealth who controlled the treasury of the Children of Israel, and the sons of Phinehas, the legitimate high priests.

Eli, seeking to usurp authority from the descendants of Aaron through Phinehas, attempted to offer sacrifices on an altar of stones. However, in a moment that would echo through history, he neglected to use salt, a fundamental requirement for biblical offerings symbolizing the covenant. When the Great High Priest Ozzi discovered this omission, he rejected the sacrifice and publicly rebuked Eli. The humiliation was absolute. In response, Eli and his followers rose in revolt, abandoning Mount Gerizim—the site of the first Israelite altar in Canaan—and marching to Shiloh to build a competing temple.

"He built an altar, omitting no detail—it all corresponded to the original, piece by piece." — Abu l-Fath

This migration created three distinct factions among the Israelites: a loyal faction that remained at Mount Gerizim under Uzzi ben Bukki; a heretical faction that turned to foreign gods; and the followers of Eli in Shiloh. The Samaritans identify as the loyal faction, those who stayed true to the chosen place while others wandered into error. Modern genetic studies from 2004 have lent scientific weight to this claim of shared ancestry, suggesting that Samaritan lineages trace back to a common ancestor with Jews in the paternally inherited Jewish high priesthood (Cohanim). These markers are temporally proximate to the period of the Assyrian conquest, indicating that both groups likely emerged from the same historical Israelite population before their paths diverged.

For centuries after the fall of the Kingdom of Israel to the Neo-Assyrian Empire in the Iron Age, the religious practices of the proto-Samaritans and the Judeans in the south were likely indistinguishable. The biblical text suggests that the cultic reforms of kings Hezekiah and Josiah in Judah faced little opposition in the north, implying a shared cultural and religious fabric. However, as Yahwism coalesced into Second Temple Judaism in Jerusalem, the Samaritans began to forge a distinct identity. This process accelerated during the Hasmonean and Roman eras, when theological debates became increasingly bitter and institutionalized.

The central symbol of this divergence was the temple on Mount Gerizim. Built in the 5th century BCE as one of many Yahwistic temples, it underwent massive construction projects starting around the 4th century BCE, signaling its emergence as the pre-eminent place of worship for the Samaritan people. By the 2nd century BCE, the Samaritan Pentateuch had taken shape, and the theological chasm was wide enough to be documented in heated disputes. The relationship reached a breaking point around 120 BCE, when the Hasmonean king John Hyrcanus destroyed the Mount Gerizim temple and brought Samaria under his control. This act of destruction did not just burn stone; it ignited a long-lasting sense of mutual hostility that would define the region's politics for centuries. By the time of Jesus, the disparagement between Jews and Samaritans was so deep that it became a staple of religious parable, as seen in the story of the Good Samaritan, which ironically used a figure from this despised group to exemplify true piety against the backdrop of Jewish religious leadership.

The Abisha Scroll and the Weight of Continuity

In the heart of the Samaritan community lies an artifact that they hold to be the oldest existing scroll written by human hands: the Abisha scroll. Traditionally attributed to Abisha, the son of Aaron the Priest, this document is more than a religious text; it is the physical anchor of their identity. To the Samaritans, keeping the Patriarchal and Mosaic covenant as specified in their Torah is not an act of nostalgia but a continuous, active preservation of the faith as it was practiced under Moses.

The Abisha scroll represents a direct line of authority that bypasses the developments of Judaism in Jerusalem. For the Samaritan community, history did not stop with the destruction of the Second Temple or the rise of Rabbinic Judaism. Their continuity is rooted in the belief that they are the true Israelites who never left the chosen place. This conviction sustains them through a demographic reality that would have shattered other cultures. With only about 900 registered communal Samaritans as of 2025, their survival is a statistical anomaly. They do not view this smallness as a failure but as a divine sign, a fulfillment of the prophecy that they would remain few in number while maintaining the purity of the faith.

The community's structure is rigidly hierarchical, centered on the authority of the High Priesthood. Unlike Judaism, which eventually developed a complex rabbinic system of interpretation and law following the loss of the Temple, Samaritanism asserts that Moses was the final Prophet. The only authority after him is the successive line of Samaritan Priests, all descending from Aaron. This creates a religious structure where the High Priest holds absolute sway over both spiritual and legal matters, ensuring that the tradition remains unadulterated by outside influences or new revelations.

This strict adherence to the Mosaic law extends to their view of the end times. The Samaritan eschatology centers on the coming of the Taheb, a figure analogous to the Jewish Messiah but with distinct characteristics. The Taheb is expected to be a prophet from the Tribe of Joseph (specifically Ephraim), unlike the Davidic lineage expected in Judaism. When he arrives, he will not just bring peace; he will perform three specific signs and restore Mount Gerizim to its former glory. It is believed that his coming will coincide with the miraculous revelation of the hidden Tabernacle of Moses, a sacred vessel lost since the time of Joshua. Until this day, known as "the day of vengeance," the Samaritans live in a state of waiting, preserving their traditions as if every day could be the last before the restoration.

The Geography of Faith: Gerizim vs. Jerusalem

The conflict between Samaritanism and Judaism is fundamentally geographical. For over two millennia, the debate has been reduced to a single coordinate on a map: where does God choose to dwell? For Jews, it is Jerusalem, specifically the Temple Mount (Mount Moriah), where the Binding of Isaac occurred and where Solomon built his temple. For Samaritans, this belief is a historical error born of political dissent. They assert that Shechem, at the foot of Mount Gerizim, is the only true sanctuary chosen by God.

This divergence is not merely about location; it alters the entire narrative of salvation history. In the Jewish tradition, Jerusalem becomes the center of the world, the destination of pilgrimage, and the site of ultimate redemption. In the Samaritan tradition, this centrality is rejected entirely. The journey to Jerusalem is considered a journey into apostasy for a faithful Israelite. Instead, their pilgrimages are directed toward Mount Gerizim, where they celebrate festivals such as Passover with a fervor that has remained unchanged for thousands of years.

The geography also dictates the physical landscape of conflict. The destruction of the Samaritan temple by John Hyrcanus in 120 BCE was a strategic move to crush a rival center of worship and consolidate power under the Jerusalem-centric Hasmonean dynasty. Yet, despite the ruin of their temple, the Samaritans did not abandon the mountain. They continued to worship there, often in open-air settings or smaller shrines, maintaining that the sanctity of the place was inherent and could not be erased by human destruction. This resilience transformed Mount Gerizim from a site of institutional religion into a symbol of indigenous heritage and resistance.

The tension over these holy sites persists today. In modern Israel and the Palestinian territories, the Samaritan community exists in a precarious limbo, often caught between Jewish nationalism and Palestinian identity. They hold Israeli citizenship but maintain strong cultural ties to their historical homeland in Samaria. Their small population means that every individual life is precious, and the community lives under the constant shadow of potential conflict in a region where religious geography is often a matter of life and death.

The Human Cost of Theological Divergence

While the theological arguments over Mount Gerizim and Jerusalem are ancient, the human cost of their divergence has been immediate and recurring throughout history. It is easy to view this schism as a matter of ritual correctness—salt on an altar, the location of a temple—but for the people involved, it meant exile, destruction, and a perpetual state of being "the other."

When the Hasmonean forces destroyed the Mount Gerizim temple in 120 BCE, they did not just topple a building. They shattered the spiritual center of a community that had existed for centuries. For the Samaritans living on the mountain, this was an act of erasure, a declaration that their presence and their faith were illegitimate. The aftermath saw a long period of marginalization where Samaritans were often viewed with suspicion and hostility by their Judean neighbors. This sentiment intensified in the Roman period, where Samaritan uprisings against Roman rule were met with brutal suppression, leading to significant loss of life and further fragmentation of the community.

The Parable of the Good Samaritan, told by Jesus, offers a glimpse into the social reality of the time. In that story, the "Samaritan" is not a hero in the eyes of the Jewish audience; he is an outsider, a person of low status who does things differently. The fact that this figure becomes the exemplar of virtue underscores the depth of the divide. It was a society where religious difference could translate into social ostracization and even dehumanization. For centuries, Samaritans were denied access to Jerusalem's holy sites, forbidden from participating in the Temple rituals, and subjected to legal restrictions that reinforced their second-class status.

In more recent history, the small size of the Samaritan population has made them vulnerable to the violent fluctuations of Middle Eastern politics. During times of war or civil unrest, their communities have often found themselves on the front lines or in the crossfire of larger conflicts between Jews and Arabs. Their survival is a daily negotiation with power structures that rarely view them as anything other than a footnote to the main drama of Israeli-Palestinian relations. Yet, they persist. They maintain their endogamous marriage practices, ensuring genetic continuity while keeping their cultural boundaries sharp. They teach their children the Samaritan Hebrew script, a writing system that has survived millennia, and recite prayers that have not changed in thousands of years.

A Future Written in Stone and Blood

As we look toward the future, the Samaritan community stands at a crossroads between ancient tradition and modern reality. With only 900 people, they are one of the smallest religious groups on Earth, yet their influence on the history of monotheism is disproportionately large. They offer a unique window into the formative years of Abrahamic faith, preserving a version of Judaism that predates the destruction of the Second Temple and the rise of Rabbinic Judaism.

The hope of the Samaritans remains fixed on the Taheb, the Restorer. This belief in a future prophet who will reveal the hidden Tabernacle and restore Mount Gerizim provides a sense of purpose that transcends their current demographic limitations. It is a belief system that does not look backward with regret but forward with expectation. They believe that their small number is a temporary state, a necessary condition for the purity of the faith until the time of restoration arrives.

For the rest of the world, the Samaritans serve as a reminder of the fragility and resilience of religious identity. In an era where globalization threatens to homogenize cultures, they stand as a bastion of distinctiveness, refusing to be absorbed into the larger narratives of Judaism or Islam. Their story is one of survival against overwhelming odds, a testament to the power of belief in the face of destruction.

The Abisha scroll remains unrolled in their synagogues, a silent witness to a history that continues to unfold. The salt on the altar is still used, the sacrifices are still offered (though now in a symbolic manner following the loss of the Temple), and the prayers for the Taheb continue to rise from Mount Gerizim. They are the Israelites who stayed, the guardians of the original covenant, living proof that a people can be few in number but infinite in their endurance. Their existence challenges the notion that history is written only by the victors; instead, they show us how the marginalized can hold onto their truth with a tenacity that outlasts empires.

In the end, the story of Samaritanism is not just about a religious split or a geographical dispute. It is a human story of a community that looked at the map of their world and said, "This is where God is," and refused to move, even when the rest of the world tried to force them to. They are the keepers of a flame that has burned on Mount Gerizim for three thousand years, waiting for the day when the Taheb comes to restore it to its full glory. Until then, they watch, they wait, and they endure, a small group with a monumental history, proving that the most powerful forces in the world are not armies or weapons, but faith and memory.
