In 1941, the United Kingdom did not stand alone against the tide of global fascism; it surrendered to it. This is not a footnote in history books, for in our timeline that war never happened, but within the pages of Jo Walton's Small Change trilogy, this capitulation is the foundational truth of an entire world. Published between 2006 and 2008 by Tor Books, these three novels construct a terrifyingly plausible alternate reality where Britain signs a peace treaty with Nazi Germany in 1941, leading to a slow, suffocating slide into domestic authoritarianism. The series is not merely a collection of spy thrillers or political fantasies; it is a rigorous, human-scale examination of how ordinary people navigate the erosion of liberty when the government they elected becomes their oppressor. For a reader who has just finished Everybody's Perfect, these books offer the necessary historical and emotional bedrock, moving from the abstract concept of societal collapse to the visceral reality of living through it.

The trilogy begins with Farthing, released on August 8, 2006. The narrative voice is immediate and grounded, introducing us to a world where the "Great War" ended not in victory but in a negotiated peace that left Europe under the shadow of Hitler's Reich. The title itself refers to the Farthing House party, a gathering of British aristocrats who are secretly plotting against the government they once served. But Walton does not center the story on the grand strategists or the high-born conspirators alone. She anchors the horror in the quiet desperation of the characters who remain outside these inner circles. The protagonist, Lucy Cartwright, is a woman trying to live a normal life in a country that has quietly outlawed its own conscience. Her husband, James, is dead under suspicious circumstances, and her brother-in-law is a high-ranking official in the fascist regime.

The human cost of this peace is not measured in battlefront casualties, for there are no battle fronts anymore. Instead, it is measured in the silence that falls over dinner tables, the way neighbors stop speaking to one another, and the sudden disappearance of people who ask too many questions. In this Britain, the "peace" is a gilded cage. The streets are clean, the trains run on time, but the air is thick with fear. Walton writes with a specificity that makes the alternate history feel less like fiction and more like a memory of a future that almost was. When she describes the police raids or the rounding up of "undesirables," she does not use euphemisms. She names the places. She lists the ages of the victims. The civilian experience is one of constant, low-grade terror punctuated by moments of absolute violence.

The political machinery in Farthing is depicted not as a cartoonish villainy but as a bureaucratic nightmare. The government justifies its actions through the language of stability and national unity. They speak of "preserving British values" while systematically dismantling the very institutions that made Britain free. This mirrors the real-world trajectory of the 1930s, where democracy was often traded for the promise of order. Walton forces the reader to confront a difficult question: how much freedom are we willing to sacrifice for safety? In this timeline, the answer has already been given by history, and it is a tragedy written in blood and ink.

The Weight of Complicity

As the series moves into Ha'penny, published on July 1, 2008, the scope of the oppression widens. The title, derived from the British coin worth half a penny, suggests the small, incremental costs paid by the citizens to maintain this fragile peace. It is a currency of compromise. The story follows a different set of characters, including Sir James Thackeray and his niece, but the central figure remains the collective conscience of a nation that has lost its way.

In Ha'penny, the fascist regime has consolidated power. The initial shock of the peace treaty has worn off, replaced by a grim acceptance of the new normal. This is perhaps the most terrifying aspect of Walton's vision: the banality of evil. It is not always a storm trooper kicking down a door; often, it is a civil servant stamping a form that sends a family to a labor camp. The novel explores the psychology of complicity. Why do people stay silent? Why do they look the other way when their neighbors are taken away?

Walton does not offer easy answers. She presents characters who are morally ambiguous, people who try to do good within a system designed for evil and fail. Some characters rationalize their silence as necessary survival. Others actively participate in the regime's crimes, believing that they are the only thing standing between order and chaos. The narrative refuses to glorify resistance. When characters do fight back, it is messy, dangerous, and often futile. The human cost is explicit here. We see families torn apart, children raised without parents, and communities shattered by suspicion.

The setting of Ha'penny is a Britain that has become isolated from the rest of the world. While the United States remains democratic but wary, and Europe is firmly under Nazi control, Britain is a pariah state, trapped in its own internal contradictions. The society is stratified, with a new elite rising to replace the old aristocracy, all serving the dictates of the fascist state. Yet, beneath this surface, there is a resistance movement, small and desperate, struggling to keep the flame of liberty alive.

One of the most striking elements of Ha'penny is its attention to the daily lives of ordinary people. Walton describes the food shortages, the censorship of the press, and the constant surveillance by the state police. These are not background details; they are the fabric of existence for the characters. A child might grow up never knowing what freedom feels like. A young woman might be forced into a marriage to secure her family's safety. The novel forces the reader to consider the long-term psychological impact of living under totalitarianism. It is not just about physical survival; it is about the survival of the soul.

The Final Stand and the Cost of Memory

The trilogy concludes with Half a Crown, released on September 30, 2008. By this point, the resistance has moved from whispered conspiracies to open rebellion. The title suggests that the cost of freedom is high, but perhaps not impossible to pay. The narrative arc brings together threads from the previous two books, showing how the consequences of the peace treaty have rippled through a generation.

In Half a Crown, the stakes are higher than ever. The regime is cracking down harder than before, fearing that the seeds of dissent will grow into a revolution. The police state is in full force, with curfews, martial law, and summary executions becoming common occurrences. Walton does not shy away from depicting the brutality of the crackdown. She describes the violence with unflinching detail, focusing on the faces of the victims. These are not statistics; they are mothers, fathers, sons, and daughters whose lives have been cut short by the machinery of the state.

The novel also explores the international dimension of this alternate history. The United States, while still free, is deeply concerned about the spread of fascism in Europe. There are diplomatic tensions, covert operations, and the ever-present threat of war. But even here, the human cost is central. Soldiers on both sides are young men and women sent to die for ideologies they may not fully understand. Civilians in occupied territories suffer under the yoke of occupation, their lives dictated by the whims of foreign rulers.

Walton's portrayal of resistance in Half a Crown is nuanced. She does not paint the rebels as perfect heroes. They are flawed, frightened, and sometimes desperate enough to do terrible things. But they are driven by a fundamental belief that life under fascism is not worth living. This belief is what sustains them through the darkest nights. The novel asks whether it is possible to reclaim a nation that has lost its soul. Can a people who have lived in fear for decades suddenly find the courage to fight back?

The ending of Half a Crown is ambiguous, reflecting the uncertainty of real history. There is no tidy resolution where everything is fixed and everyone lives happily ever after. Instead, there is a glimpse of hope, a small but significant step toward change. This is perhaps the most powerful message of the trilogy: that even in the darkest times, resistance is possible. The human spirit is resilient, and the desire for freedom is indestructible.

Echoes Across Worlds

The world of the Small Change trilogy extends beyond these three novels. There is a short story titled "Escape to Other Worlds with Science Fiction," which is set in the United States of this same alternate timeline. This story provides a fascinating glimpse into how the rest of the world reacts to a fascist Britain and Europe. It shows that the consequences of the 1941 peace treaty are felt globally, altering the course of history in ways both subtle and profound.

In this American setting, we see a society grappling with its own demons while watching the tragedy unfold across the Atlantic. The United States is not immune to the pull of authoritarianism; there are factions within America that sympathize with the fascist cause or fear the spread of communism more than they hate tyranny. This adds another layer of complexity to the narrative, showing that the struggle for freedom is a universal one, transcending national boundaries.

The short story also serves as a bridge between the Small Change trilogy and Walton's other works, creating a cohesive universe where the themes of identity, morality, and political power are explored from multiple angles. It reinforces the idea that history is not a straight line but a web of interconnected events, where every choice has consequences that ripple outward.

The Relevance of Alternate History

Why does this story matter now? In 2026, as we look back on the tumultuous decades of the 21st century, the Small Change trilogy feels less like fiction and more like a warning. Walton's exploration of how democracies can slide into fascism is incredibly relevant in an era where political polarization, erosion of civil liberties, and the rise of authoritarian leaders are global concerns.

The series forces us to confront uncomfortable truths about human nature. It shows that evil does not always wear a uniform; sometimes it wears a suit and tie and speaks the language of patriotism. It reveals how easily people can be manipulated by fear and how quickly they can trade their rights for the promise of security. But it also shows the power of individual action. Even in the face of overwhelming odds, ordinary people can make a difference.

The Small Change trilogy is not just a story about an alternate past; it is a mirror held up to our own present. It asks us to consider what we value and how far we are willing to go to protect it. It reminds us that freedom is fragile and must be constantly defended. And most importantly, it reminds us of the human cost of failure. Every page of these books is a testament to the lives lost, the families destroyed, and the dreams crushed when democracy falls.

In reading Farthing, Ha'penny, and Half a Crown, we are not just entertained; we are challenged. We are asked to imagine a world where things went wrong and to understand how that happened so that we can ensure it never happens here. Jo Walton has created a masterpiece of alternate history, one that is as beautiful in its prose as it is terrifying in its implications. It is a series that lingers in the mind long after the last page is turned, a reminder that the small changes of today can lead to the monumental shifts of tomorrow.

The legacy of the Small Change trilogy lies in its ability to make the abstract concrete. It takes the concept of fascism and makes it personal. It shows us the face of the tyrant not as a monster but as a neighbor, a friend, a family member. And it shows us the hero not as a superhuman warrior but as an ordinary person who refuses to give up. This is the power of great literature: it changes the way we see the world and our place in it.

As we navigate the complexities of our own time, let us remember the lessons of Walton's Britain. Let us listen to the voices of those who resisted, even when the odds were against them. And let us never forget that the cost of freedom is high, but the price of tyranny is higher still. The Small Change trilogy stands as a beacon in the darkness, illuminating the path forward and reminding us that we have a choice. We can choose to be complicit, or we can choose to fight. And in the end, it is our choices that define who we are.

The dates of publication—2006, 2008—are markers on a timeline that led to this moment of reflection. The ISBNs and publisher details may seem like dry facts, but they represent the physical presence of these stories in our world. They are tangible objects that carry the weight of their messages into homes and libraries across the globe. Every copy sold is a testament to the enduring relevance of Walton's vision.

In the end, the Small Change trilogy is more than just a series of novels. It is a meditation on history, politics, and the human condition. It is a call to action for anyone who believes in the power of democracy and the value of human life. And it is a reminder that even in the darkest times, there is always hope. As long as there are people willing to speak out, to resist, and to fight for what is right, the light will never be completely extinguished. The future is unwritten, and it is up to us to decide how it ends.
