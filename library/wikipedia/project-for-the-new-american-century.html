Based on Wikipedia: Project for the New American Century

"On June 3, 1997, twenty-five conservatives signed a document demanding America embrace its role as the world’s sole superpower. They called for massive military buildup, regime change in hostile states, and a rejection of multilateral constraints. Ten of them would soon become architects of the Bush administration’s most consequential—and catastrophic—foreign policy decisions. This wasn’t a government memo. It was the founding manifesto of the Project for the New American Century, a think tank that rewrote America’s 21st-century destiny from a cramped Washington office.

The brainchild of William Kristol, editor of The Weekly Standard, and Robert Kagan, a historian with hawkish convictions, PNAC emerged from a specific moment of Republican anxiety. After the Cold War’s end, Bill Clinton’s foreign policy—diplomatic, hesitant, skeptical of military intervention—had dominated the 1990s. To Kristol and Kagan, this wasn’t pragmatism; it was surrender. They’d laid out their critique months earlier in a September 1996 Foreign Affairs essay titled 'Toward a Neo-Reaganite Foreign Policy,' arguing conservatives were 'adrift' and needed a 'more elevated vision' centered on what they bluntly termed 'benevolent global hegemony.'

'American leadership is good both for America and for the world.'

This line, from PNAC’s official mission statement, wasn’t mere rhetoric. It was a call to action for a movement convinced that U.S. power wasn’t just necessary—it was morally righteous. Their playbook? A 'Reaganite policy of military strength and moral clarity,' rejecting détente and embracing preemptive force. The founding 'Statement of Principles' signed that June day framed America as the 'world’s pre-eminent power' facing a singular challenge: to 'shape a new century favorable to American principles and interests.' The prescription was stark: hike defense spending, strengthen ties with democracies, 'challenge regimes hostile to our interests and values,' and preserve 'an international order friendly to our security, our prosperity, and our principles.'

What made PNAC extraordinary wasn’t its budget—its staff was tiny—but its Rolodex. Of the 25 signatories, ten would serve in George W. Bush’s administration: Vice President Dick Cheney, Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld, Deputy Defense Secretary Paul Wolfowitz, and key figures like John Bolton, Richard Perle, and Elliott Abrams. Stuart Elden, a political geographer, noted the staggering reality: 'The number of figures associated with PNAC that had been members of the Reagan or the first Bush administration and the number that would take up office with the administration of the second President Bush demonstrate that it is not merely a question of employees and budgets.' This wasn’t a policy shop; it was a shadow government-in-waiting.

The Iraq Obsession

PNAC’s fixation on Iraq began long before 9/11. In 1998, as UN weapons inspectors clashed with Saddam Hussein’s regime, Kristol and Kagan published op-eds in The New York Times demanding regime change. Then, on January 26, 1998, PNAC orchestrated a pivotal moment: a letter signed by 18 prominent conservatives—including Rumsfeld, Wolfowitz, Perle, and future UN Ambassador John Bolton—urging President Clinton to remove Saddam.

'The U.S. has the authority under existing UN resolutions to take the necessary steps, including military steps, to protect our vital interests in the Gulf.'

The letter framed Saddam as an existential threat to U.S. allies, regional oil supplies, and global security, dismissing UN sanctions as ineffective. It explicitly rejected 'a misguided insistence on unanimity in the UN Security Council,' signaling a unilateral turn. Crucially, PNAC leveraged this pressure to back the Iraq Liberation Act of 1998, signed by Clinton in October that year, which made regime change official U.S. policy and allocated $97 million to Iraqi opposition groups.

When Clinton launched Operation Desert Fox—a four-day bombing campaign against Iraqi weapons sites—in December 1998, PNAC declared it insufficient. In a January 1999 memo, the group dismissed containment as 'an illusion' and questioned the viability of the U.S.-backed Iraqi opposition. Their stance hardened further after 9/11. On September 20, 2001, PNAC sent a letter to President Bush explicitly linking Saddam to terrorism—despite zero evidence connecting Iraq to the attacks.

'Any strategy aiming at the eradication of terrorism and its sponsors must include a determined effort to remove Saddam Hussein from power in Iraq.'

The warning was apocalyptic: allowing Saddam to remain would be 'an early and perhaps decisive surrender in the war on international terrorism.' From 2001 until the March 2003 invasion, PNAC and its members relentlessly pushed this narrative, framing the Iraq War as the central front in the 'war on terror.'

The Blueprint for Empire

While PNAC’s letters grabbed headlines, its most consequential document was a September 2000 report titled Rebuilding America’s Defenses: Strategies, Forces, and Resources for a New Century. Drafted by key members including Wolfowitz and Rumsfeld’s deputy, Stephen Cambone, it laid out a roadmap for perpetual U.S. military dominance. It argued America must 'preserve and extend its position of global leadership by maintaining the preeminence of U.S. military forces,' demanding sweeping modernization of nuclear, space, and cyber capabilities.

One passage chilled observers:

'Advanced forms of biological warfare that can 'target' specific genotypes may transform biological warfare from the realm of terror to a politically useful tool.'

More revealing was the report’s candid assessment of the hurdles to its vision. It acknowledged that 'the process of transformation, even if it brings revolutionary change, is likely to be a long one, absent some catastrophic and catalyzing event—like a new Pearl Harbor.' Nine months later, al-Qaeda provided that 'catastrophic and catalyzing event.' When the Bush administration invaded Iraq in 2003, it deployed capabilities and strategies eerily aligned with Rebuilding America’s Defenses—from preemption to nation-building. Critics like journalist Irwin Stelzer argued PNAC had effectively written the Bush Doctrine.

The Influence Wars

Was PNAC the puppet master behind Bush’s foreign policy, or merely a chorus echoing existing hawkish sentiments? The debate still simmers. Proponents of PNAC’s outsized role point to the astonishing career trajectory of its signatories. As journalist Dave Grondin observed, the group functioned as a 'talent bank' for the incoming administration. When Wolfowitz, Rumsfeld, and Cheney took charge at the Pentagon, State Department, and White House, they implemented policies PNAC had championed for years—from scrapping arms treaties to isolating 'rogue states.'

Skeptics push back. Academics like Inderjeet Parmar and Donald E. Abelson argue PNAC’s influence was retroactively inflated. They note that Kristol and Kagan initially struggled to gain traction; their 1996 Foreign Affairs essay drew little attention, and PNAC relied on foundation grants to survive. Moreover, Bush’s early foreign policy team included figures like Colin Powell who clashed with PNAC’s vision. The group’s 2006 closure—replaced by the less influential Foreign Policy Initiative in 2009—suggests its peak relevance ended with the Iraq War’s disastrous turn.

The truth lies in the details. PNAC didn’t invent neoconservatism, but it crystallized its agenda into actionable policy. It provided intellectual cover for regime change, reframing invasion as liberation and unilateralism as moral necessity. Maria Ryan, a scholar of conservative think tanks, clarifies that PNAC’s real power came from its network: 'Supporters of PNAC’s initiatives differed from case to case,' she writes. 'It was especially well-connected, not well-staffed.' In an era when policy was shaped by op-eds, congressional testimony, and backroom briefings, that network was everything.

The Ghost in the Machine

PNAC formally ceased operations in 2006 as Iraq descended into sectarian chaos—a direct rebuke to its promise of 'benevolent hegemony.' Its successor, the Foreign Policy Initiative, dissolved in 2017, outlived by its legacy. Yet the group’s DNA persists. Its core belief—that America must dominate globally to safeguard its interests—has metastasized across the political spectrum. Today’s debates over 'forever wars,' great-power competition with China, and the very meaning of 'American leadership' still echo PNAC’s founding mantra.

Consider the context that brought you here: a recent essay arguing that modern Republicans 'have given up on America,' as revealed in a Ted Cruz interview. That critique gains chilling clarity when viewed through PNAC’s lens. The movement Kristol and Kagan built didn’t just shape the Iraq War; it severed foreign policy from democratic accountability. By framing global dominance as non-negotiable and dissent as naive, PNAC helped normalize a worldview where military solutions trump diplomacy, where 'American greatness' is measured in firepower, and where presidents—Republican or Democrat—wield near-unilateral war powers.

Ten signatories became Bush officials. Countless more absorbed its gospel.

That cramped Washington office didn’t just plan a war. It helped unmake the republic it claimed to defend.
