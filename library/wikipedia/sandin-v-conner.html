In 1995, the Supreme Court of the United States issued a decision that fundamentally rewrote the rulebook for how prisoners in America are treated when they are accused of breaking the rules inside. The case, Sandin v. Conner, 515 U.S. 472, did not merely tweak a procedural detail; it dismantled an entire legal framework that had been built up over two decades. At its heart was a man named DeMont Conner, an inmate in the Hawaii prison system, whose fight for the right to call witnesses at his own disciplinary hearing became the catalyst for a shift that made it exponentially harder for incarcerated people to claim they had been denied their liberty. Before this ruling, the law was a maze of mandatory language in state regulations that, if read literally, seemed to guarantee prisoners a voice. After Sandin, the law became a fortress of discretion, where the only thing that mattered was whether the punishment imposed was "atypical and significant" compared to the ordinary misery of prison life.

To understand the seismic shift Sandin represents, one must first understand the world Conner inhabited before the Supreme Court spoke. In the early 1970s, the Court decided Wolff v. McDonnell, a landmark case that established that while prisoners do not have a constitutional right to a full-blown criminal trial, they are entitled to certain due process protections when facing disciplinary action that could result in the loss of good-time credits or segregation. Under Wolff, if a prison regulation used mandatory language—such as stating that a committee "shall" find guilt if there is substantial evidence—courts interpreted this as creating a "liberty interest." This was a legal term of art meaning that the state had voluntarily created a right that it could not take away without following specific procedures. If a regulation said the committee had no choice but to find an inmate guilty upon seeing evidence, the court reasoned that the committee also had no choice but to follow the rules of evidence, including the right to call witnesses. This logic created a paradox: the very rules meant to give officials the power to punish were interpreted as giving prisoners the power to demand fairness.

DeMont Conner was an inmate at the Hawaii State Prison who found himself trapped in this paradox. He was charged with misconduct, a serious accusation in the closed ecosystem of a correctional facility. The prison's adjustment committee, the body responsible for judging these infractions, refused to allow Conner to present witnesses in his defense. This was a critical blow. In a prison disciplinary hearing, where the stakes are high and the environment is adversarial, the ability to call witnesses is often the only way to contest the version of events presented by guards or other inmates. Without witnesses, Conner was left to argue against the state's narrative with nothing but his own word. The committee found him guilty and sentenced him to segregation, a form of solitary confinement that isolates an inmate from the general population.

Conner did not accept this outcome quietly. He filed a lawsuit, arguing that the refusal to let him call witnesses violated his right to procedural due process under the Fourteenth Amendment. He leaned heavily on the logic of Wolff v. McDonnell. His legal team pointed to a specific Hawaii prison regulation that instructed the adjustment committee to find guilt whenever a misconduct charge was supported by "substantial evidence." They argued that this language was nondiscretionary; it created a duty for the committee to act in a specific way. If the committee had a duty to find guilt upon seeing evidence, they reasoned, they also had a duty to ensure that the evidence was reliable, which necessitated the right to cross-examine and call witnesses. The federal district court disagreed with Conner, granting the prison officials summary judgment and effectively saying that Conner had no right to a fair hearing in this context. However, the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed this decision.

The Ninth Circuit's reasoning was a perfect example of the pre-Sandin legal landscape. The appellate court concluded that Conner did indeed have a liberty interest in remaining free of disciplinary segregation. They reasoned that because the state regulation mandated a finding of guilt based on substantial evidence, it implicitly created a negative inference: the committee could not impose segregation unless it found substantial evidence. Therefore, the state had created a liberty interest, and under Wolff, Conner was entitled to the procedural protections that came with it, including the right to call witnesses. This logic was seductive in its simplicity. It turned the prison's own rulebook against the administration, using the mandatory nature of the regulations to carve out rights for the incarcerated. For years, this was the standard. Courts across the nation began recognizing a wide array of rights for prisoners based on the specific wording of state statutes and prison regulations. If a law said "shall," prisoners could claim a constitutional right to due process.

But the Supreme Court was watching, and in 1995, they decided that this approach had gone too far. The Court saw a legal system that had become unmoored from the reality of prison administration. The doctrine of relying on mandatory language in regulations to create liberty interests meant that prisons were being held hostage to the drafting habits of their own clerks. A poorly worded regulation could inadvertently grant a prisoner a right to a full trial. A specific phrase could lock administrators out of their own discretion. The Court, in an opinion that would reshape the jurisprudence of incarceration, determined that this was not the way to interpret the Due Process Clause. The Constitution, they argued, should not be interpreted to grant prisoners rights simply because a state regulation happened to use the word "shall."

The decision in Sandin v. Conner was a direct repudiation of the logic used by the Ninth Circuit. Chief Justice Rehnquist, writing for the majority, held that a statute or regulation creates a liberty interest for incarcerated people only if it imposes an "atypical and significant hardship" on the inmate in relation to the ordinary incidents of prison life. This was a radical departure. The focus shifted from the words of the regulation to the reality of the punishment. The Court declared that the inquiry was no longer about whether the state had created a right through its own rules, but about whether the punishment itself was so severe that it rose to the level of a constitutional deprivation. In Conner's case, the Court found that his segregation did not meet this new, high bar. The hardship he suffered, while undoubtedly unpleasant, was not "atypical" when compared to the daily struggles of prison life. The Court reasoned that confinement in a disciplinary segregation unit was a normal part of the prison experience, not a special deprivation that required the full panoply of due process rights.

This new standard effectively ended the era of "Wolff v. McDonnell" analysis as it had been applied for twenty years. Before Sandin, courts started their analysis by looking at the text of the regulation. If the text was mandatory, the prisoner won. If the text was discretionary, the prisoner lost. After Sandin, the text of the regulation became almost irrelevant. Courts were instructed to ignore the mandatory language and look directly at the nature of the punishment. Even cases like Meachum v. Fano and Olim v. Wakinekona, which had previously allowed prison officials to transfer inmates to any prison in the country without a hearing, started their analysis in the realm of Wolff. But Sandin swept that away. The Court made it clear that the only thing that mattered was the "atypical and significant hardship" caused by the prison administrators' discretionary decisions. This shift placed the burden of proof squarely on the prisoner to demonstrate that their punishment was uniquely severe, rather than on the state to justify why a mandatory regulation was being ignored.

The impact of this ruling was immediate and profound. The "atypical and significant hardship" standard proved to be a nearly insurmountable barrier for incarcerated people. Courts began to rule that even long periods of solitary confinement, which would have previously triggered a full due process hearing under the old logic, were often considered "ordinary incidents of prison life." The definition of "atypical" became incredibly narrow. Unless the prison conditions were so severe that they exceeded the norms of the correctional system in a dramatic way, no liberty interest was recognized. This meant that if a prison system was generally harsh, an individual prisoner could be subjected to harsher conditions without any procedural protection. The logic was circular: if the prison is a place of suffering, then additional suffering is just another day in the life of a prisoner.

The practical consequences for DeMont Conner and inmates like him were stark. Under the new regime, the right to call witnesses, the right to present evidence, and the right to a hearing itself became contingent on the severity of the outcome, not the fairness of the process. If the punishment was not deemed "atypical," the prisoner had no right to any process at all. This meant that prison officials could punish inmates with near-impunity, as long as the punishment fell within the broad spectrum of what was considered normal for that facility. The mandatory language in regulations, which had once been a shield for prisoners, became a dead letter. A regulation stating that an inmate "shall" be released if they meet certain criteria no longer guaranteed a hearing; it merely stated a policy that could be ignored without constitutional consequence, provided the resulting punishment was not "atypical."

The Sandin decision also changed the way legal scholars and judges think about the relationship between state law and federal constitutional rights. Before Sandin, there was a belief that states could create their own zones of liberty within the prison system by writing specific regulations. If a state decided to give prisoners a right to a hearing, the federal courts would enforce it as a constitutional right. Sandin severed this link. It established that the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment is not a vessel for enforcing state regulations. Instead, it is a floor, not a ceiling, and that floor is determined by the nature of the hardship, not the text of the statute. This meant that states could no longer inadvertently grant constitutional rights through clumsy drafting. They had to explicitly create a liberty interest that the Supreme Court would recognize as "atypical and significant."

The ripple effects of Sandin v. Conner extended far beyond the specific facts of DeMont Conner's case. It influenced how courts analyze transfers between facilities, changes in security levels, and even the revocation of privileges. In every instance, the first question is no longer "What does the regulation say?" but rather "Is this punishment atypical and significant?" This question has proven to be a powerful tool for prison administrators. It allows them to argue that even severe punishments are consistent with the ordinary incidents of prison life, thereby bypassing the need for due process. The standard has been criticized by legal scholars for creating a system where the severity of the punishment is measured against the baseline of the prison system itself, rather than against an objective standard of fairness. If a prison system is brutal, then brutality becomes the norm, and no punishment within that system is considered "atypical."

The legacy of Sandin is a legal landscape where the rights of incarcerated people are significantly more fragile. The era of relying on mandatory language to secure due process rights is over. The focus has shifted entirely to the subjective nature of the hardship. This has made it very difficult for prisoners to challenge disciplinary actions that result in segregation, even when the process used to reach that decision was fundamentally unfair. The Court's decision prioritized the discretion of prison officials over the procedural rights of the accused. It recognized the unique challenges of prison administration but did so at the cost of the procedural safeguards that had been established to protect inmates from arbitrary punishment.

In the years since 1995, the "atypical and significant hardship" standard has been applied in a way that often favors the state. Courts have been reluctant to find that any punishment, short of the most extreme, is "atypical." This has led to a situation where prisoners are subjected to long periods of solitary confinement without the basic protections of a hearing. The right to call witnesses, once a cornerstone of prison due process, has been eroded. The logic of Wolff v. McDonnell, which sought to bring a measure of fairness to the prison disciplinary process, has been largely sidelined. The Supreme Court's decision in Sandin v. Conner stands as a testament to the power of the judiciary to reshape the rights of the incarcerated, often in ways that limit their ability to defend themselves.

The story of DeMont Conner is a microcosm of a larger shift in American law. It is a story of how a legal doctrine designed to protect rights can be dismantled by a change in judicial philosophy. The mandatory language of a regulation, once a beacon of hope for prisoners, became a relic of a bygone era. The focus on "atypical and significant hardship" has created a high bar that few prisoners can clear. The result is a system where the rights of the incarcerated are determined not by the text of the law, but by the discretion of the administrators who enforce it. The legacy of Sandin v. Conner is a reminder that in the world of constitutional law, the interpretation of a single phrase can change the lives of millions.

The decision did not just affect Hawaii; it affected every prison in the United States. The standard set in Sandin is now the primary test for determining whether a prisoner has a liberty interest in any aspect of their confinement. It has been cited in countless cases, from the most minor disciplinary infractions to the most serious allegations of misconduct. The "atypical and significant hardship" standard is the lens through which courts now view the rights of prisoners. It is a lens that often blurs the line between punishment and process, making it difficult for inmates to distinguish between the two. The decision has made the prison system more insulated from judicial review, as courts are now less likely to intervene in the day-to-day operations of correctional facilities.

The impact of Sandin v. Conner is a testament to the enduring power of the Supreme Court to shape the legal landscape. It is a decision that has been debated, criticized, and analyzed for decades. But its core holding remains intact: the Due Process Clause does not guarantee prisoners the right to a hearing unless the punishment they face is "atypical and significant" in relation to the ordinary incidents of prison life. This standard has proven to be a formidable barrier to justice for incarcerated people. It has made it difficult for them to challenge the fairness of their punishment, even when the process used to reach that decision was fundamentally flawed. The legacy of Sandin is a prison system where the rights of the accused are subordinate to the discretion of the administrators. And in that system, the story of DeMont Conner is just the beginning of a much larger narrative about the limits of liberty in America.
