In 2026, as the global political landscape remains fractured by a decade of intense ideological polarization, a distinct and controversial movement has solidified its place at the center of Western political discourse: postliberalism. This is not merely a tweak to existing policies or a minor adjustment in tax brackets; it represents a fundamental rejection of the philosophical architecture that has underpinned Western democracy since the Enlightenment. Postliberals argue that the very concepts of individual rights, free markets, and limited government—the bedrock of classical and social liberalism—have not liberated humanity but have instead eroded the social cohesion, community bonds, and traditional moral frameworks necessary for a stable society. They contend that the liberal promise of autonomy has delivered only atomization, inequality, and a profound loss of belonging.

The movement is a paradox to many observers. It draws heavily from the right's suspicion of globalization and the left's critique of unchecked capitalism, yet it finds no home in either traditional camp. Instead, it proposes a "communitarian politics" that demands a stronger state role in shaping culture, often invoking religious thought and social conservatism to enforce a vision of the common good. Critics, however, see something far more dangerous lurking beneath this synthesis. They argue that postliberalism is not a new path forward but an intellectual framework for democratic backsliding, providing the philosophical justification for authoritarian projects and "illiberal states." The debate over postliberalism is, at its core, a fight over what it means to be free and who gets to decide the values of a society.

The Fracture in the Liberal Consensus

To understand the rise of postliberalism, one must first grasp the depth of the crisis it claims to address. For much of the late 20th and early 21st centuries, Western politics operated within a broad consensus: liberalism was the default setting. Whether on the center-left or center-right, parties generally accepted the primacy of individual rights, the efficiency of free markets, and the necessity of a neutral state that stood above culture rather than enforcing it. This era saw the triumph of globalization, the expansion of consumer choice, and the legal codification of individual autonomy in almost every sphere of life.

Yet, by the 2010s, cracks began to appear that were too deep to ignore. The promised prosperity of global trade failed to reach vast swathes of the working class, leading to stark economic stratification. Simultaneously, a cultural sense of dislocation took hold; families fractured, community institutions withered, and a pervasive feeling emerged that society was no longer held together by shared values but by a fragile contract between strangers. Postliberals argue that liberalism's focus on the autonomous individual blinded it to the reality that human beings are fundamentally social creatures shaped by their communities, traditions, and histories.

"Liberalism encourages individuals to approach commitments and relationships with flexibility, treating them as interchangeable and subject to renegotiation." — Patrick Deneen

This observation, articulated forcefully by American philosopher Patrick Deneen, strikes at the heart of the postliberal critique. In a liberal society, Deneen argues, marriage, employment, citizenship, and even religious affiliation become matters of personal preference rather than binding obligations. When everything is negotiable, nothing is sacred. The result is a society where individuals are "unmoored," lacking the deep social ties that provide meaning and resilience in times of crisis. Postliberals contend that this fragmentation has left populations vulnerable to demagoguery and unable to sustain the collective action required to address complex challenges like climate change or economic inequality.

The postliberal response is not a return to pre-modern feudalism, nor is it a simple rejection of modernity. It is an attempt to construct a political order that recognizes the limits of individual autonomy. They posit that a stable society requires a shared sense of purpose and a commitment to a "common good" that transcends personal desire. This stands in direct opposition to the liberal ideal of state neutrality, where the government refuses to endorse any particular vision of the "good life," leaving citizens to define their own values in the marketplace of ideas. Postliberals argue that this neutrality is an illusion; every social order rests on fundamental beliefs and commitments. To claim the state is neutral is merely to hide the values of the dominant culture behind a veil of proceduralism.

The British Roots: From Blue Labour to Communitarian Conservatism

The intellectual genealogy of postliberalism is surprisingly diverse, but its modern political form first coalesced in the United Kingdom during the turbulent years following the 2008 financial crisis. It emerged from a movement within the Labour Party known as "Blue Labour," which sought to reconcile the party's traditional working-class base with a more conservative social outlook that had alienated many voters in favor of the Conservatives.

Early British theorists such as John Gray, Maurice Glasman, Phillip Blond, Adrian Pabst, and Jon Cruddas began to articulate a critique that was both anti-globalist and anti-libertarian. They argued that the New Labour project under Tony Blair had uncritically embraced market fundamentalism, which had devastated industrial communities and severed the ties of solidarity that once defined the British working class. For these thinkers, the solution was not more markets or more state bureaucracy, but a revitalization of "subsidiarity"—the idea that social problems should be solved at the most local level possible by the communities themselves, supported by a state that actively nurtures these institutions rather than replacing them.

"The liberal emphases on individual rights and freedoms have diminished the roles of community, family, and tradition in fostering meaning and belonging." — Postliberal Thesis

Phillip Blond's 2010 manifesto, Red Tory, became a seminal text for this movement. He argued that the state had become too large and intrusive precisely because civil society had been allowed to wither. By empowering local institutions—churches, unions, charities, and families—the need for top-down state intervention would decrease. This "distributive" approach sought to break up concentrations of wealth and power not through socialism, but by fostering a dense ecosystem of intermediate institutions that could buffer individuals from the raw forces of the market.

The movement gained traction among a generation of politicians who felt that both major parties had lost their way. It offered a third way that rejected the individualism of Thatcherism as much as the technocratic managerialism of New Labour. However, this British iteration was also fraught with tension. As it moved from theory to practice, the emphasis on "localism" and "community values" often clashed with the liberal commitments to diversity and minority rights. The critique of immigration that began to surface in some postliberal circles signaled a shift toward a more exclusionary vision of community, one defined by national identity and cultural homogeneity rather than universalist principles.

The American Turn: Conservatism Without Fusionism

While the British version of postliberalism emerged from the center-left, its American counterpart found its home on the right, specifically among conservatives who had become disillusioned with "fusionism." For decades, American conservatism was defined by a marriage between economic libertarians (who wanted free markets) and social traditionalists (who wanted to preserve moral order). Postliberals in the US argue that this alliance has failed catastrophically.

Figures like Patrick Deneen, Rod Dreher, Adrian Vermeule, and the Israeli-American philosopher Yoram Hazony have led a charge against what they see as the inherent contradictions of modern conservatism. They argue that free-market capitalism has destroyed the very social structures—families, churches, local economies—that traditionalists claim to want to protect. Deneen's 2018 book, Why Liberalism Failed, became a sensation in conservative intellectual circles, arguing that liberalism is not a system that can be reformed but a philosophy that inevitably leads to its own destruction by atomizing society and empowering the state to manage the resulting chaos.

In this American context, postliberalism is often characterized by a deep skepticism of the Constitution as a liberal document. Vermeule, in particular, has argued for an "interpretive" approach to law that prioritizes the substantive common good over procedural rights, suggesting that judges should actively promote traditional moral values rather than remaining neutral arbiters. This represents a sharp break from the conservative legal movement's focus on originalism and textualism, which sought to limit government power. Postliberals in the US are not afraid of state power; they want it deployed aggressively to enforce social norms, regulate capitalism for the sake of community stability, and protect national sovereignty from globalist encroachments.

Yoram Hazony, in his work The Virtue of Nationalism, provides a philosophical underpinning for this shift. He argues that the liberal order's push for universal values undermines the "nation-state" as the primary unit of human flourishing. For Hazony and others, the nation is not just a geographic entity but a cultural community bound by shared history and tradition. The postliberal critique of globalism is thus not merely economic but existential; they believe that the erosion of national borders and identity leads to a loss of meaning and a rise in conflict.

The Illiberal State: Hungary as a Laboratory

No discussion of postliberalism is complete without addressing its most visible political manifestation: Viktor Orbán's Hungary. Often described by the Hungarian Prime Minister himself as an "illiberal state," the Fidesz government has become a beacon for postliberal thinkers who argue that democracy must be subordinated to the preservation of national culture and sovereignty.

Orbán's administration has systematically dismantled checks on executive power, restructured the media landscape to favor pro-government narratives, and implemented policies that sharply restrict immigration while promoting traditional family structures. For critics, this is a clear case of democratic backsliding. They point to the erosion of judicial independence, the suppression of civil society organizations, and the use of state resources to entrench the ruling party as evidence that postliberalism is a Trojan horse for authoritarianism.

"Postliberalism serves as an intellectual framework for democratic backsliding and illiberalism." — Political Critics

However, postliberals defend Orbán's model not as a rejection of democracy, but as its redefinition. They argue that liberal democracy has become disconnected from the will of the people, trapped in a cycle of elite consensus that ignores national concerns. In their view, the "illiberal state" is simply a state that refuses to be neutral about its own culture and values. It actively promotes a vision of the common good grounded in Christian tradition and Hungarian identity. This stance has resonated with postliberals who believe that liberal institutions have become tools for enforcing a cosmopolitan, individualist ideology that many citizens find alienating.

The Orbán model also highlights the tension between economic nationalism and social conservatism. The Hungarian government has pursued protectionist economic policies, rejecting the unfettered free market while simultaneously maintaining low taxes for businesses that align with national interests. This blend of dirigisme and traditionalism is what postliberals hope to replicate elsewhere: a state that intervenes in the economy to protect workers and communities but also enforces strict social norms regarding family and religion.

The Global Order: A Reaction Against Liberal Hegemony

The implications of postliberalism extend far beyond domestic politics; they offer a radical critique of the international order established after World War II. Scholars like John Milbank, Adrian Pabst, and Patrick Deneen argue that the US-led liberal hegemony is not merely failing but is inherently flawed. They contend that the "Enlightenment liberalism" that underpins the global system promotes a universalist vision that inevitably leads to conflict by framing any dissent as an attack on civilization itself.

In contrast to John Ikenberry's view that the liberal international order needs more liberalization to survive threats from illiberal forces, postliberals see the rise of populist movements and "civilizational states" (a term often applied to China or India) as a natural reaction to the contradictions within liberalism. They argue that the global order has prioritized individual identity over collective belonging, creating a vacuum filled by nationalist fervor.

"Liberalism no longer advances a substantive common good, resulting in ambiguity that both promotes individual freedoms and struggles to manage their broader consequences." — Adrian Pabst

Milbank and Pabst suggest that the US has used imperial means to export its version of liberalism, often disregarding local cultures and traditions. Since the 1970s, they argue, global governance has strengthened state power at the supranational level while weakening national democratic institutions. This has led to a situation where authority is distant from the people it governs, fostering resentment and a desire for sovereignty. The postliberal vision of international relations is one of "pluralistic realism," where different nations pursue their own conceptions of the good without imposing them on others through military or economic coercion.

This perspective challenges the core tenets of American foreign policy, which has long assumed that democracy and free markets are universal goods that should be spread globally. Postliberals argue that such an approach is not only naive but destructive, leading to endless wars and the destabilization of regions that are ill-equipped to handle rapid social change. Instead, they advocate for a diplomacy based on mutual respect for national sovereignty and cultural difference, even if it means accepting a world order that is less integrated and more fractured.

The Critics: Charges of Coherence and Tyranny

Despite its growing influence, postliberalism faces fierce opposition from across the political spectrum. Liberal theorists argue that defining a "common good" in a pluralistic society is an impossible task that inevitably leads to tyranny. They contend that any attempt by the state to enforce a particular vision of the good life will necessarily restrict individual liberty and marginalize minority groups. For liberals, the neutrality of the state is not an illusion but a necessary safeguard against the oppression of those who dissent from the majority's values.

On the left, socialist critics like Chris Wright and J.J. Porter attack postliberalism as incoherent and reactionary. They argue that the movement's focus on "community" and "tradition" is a smokescreen for protecting the interests of the ruling class. Wright asserts that the political right, including postliberals, ultimately serves capitalism and business rather than the public good. He contends that postliberalism wants to preserve the fruits of liberalism (consumer goods, technological advances) while doing away with the structures that made them possible (individual rights, democratic accountability).

"Because of its alleged interest in the public good but its conservative (Republican) orientation, postliberalism is ultimately incoherent." — Chris Wright

These critics also point out the dangers of the postliberal emphasis on the nuclear family and religion. In a society increasingly characterized by diverse family structures and secular beliefs, enforcing traditional norms through state power could lead to significant social exclusion and discrimination against LGBTQ+ individuals, single parents, and non-believers. The fear is that postliberalism will not create a more cohesive society but a more fractured one, where those who do not fit the prescribed mold are treated as second-class citizens or enemies of the community.

Furthermore, historians and political philosophers argue that the critique of liberalism often ignores the historical context in which liberal rights were won. The struggle for civil rights, labor protections, and gender equality was fought using the language of individual rights and universal justice. To reject this framework is to risk undoing decades of progress toward a more just and inclusive society. Critics warn that postliberalism's nostalgia for a "golden age" of community ignores the reality that such communities were often built on exclusion, hierarchy, and the suppression of dissent.

The Road Ahead: A New Paradigm or a Dead End?

As we navigate the mid-2020s, the debate over postliberalism remains unresolved. It has successfully identified real problems: the alienation of the working class, the crisis of social cohesion, and the failure of globalized capitalism to deliver shared prosperity. Its call for a politics rooted in community, tradition, and the common good resonates with millions who feel left behind by the liberal order.

Yet, the path it proposes is fraught with peril. The tension between the desire for social cohesion and the need for individual liberty remains the central dilemma of postliberal thought. Can a society truly be cohesive if it enforces a single vision of the good? Can democracy survive if the state is no longer neutral but an active agent in shaping culture and morality? And can the movement avoid sliding into the authoritarianism that its critics predict?

The future of postliberalism will depend on whether its proponents can offer a viable alternative to liberalism that does not sacrifice freedom for order. They must answer the question of how to build strong communities without excluding those who do not fit the mold, and how to regulate capitalism without stifling innovation. If they cannot, postliberalism risks becoming nothing more than an intellectual justification for the very illiberal forces it claims to critique.

For now, the movement stands as a potent reminder that the liberal consensus is not eternal. It has fractured, and in its place, new ideologies are rising to challenge the status quo. Whether these new formations lead to a renewal of democratic life or to a descent into authoritarianism remains one of the defining questions of our time. The answer will shape the political landscape for decades to come, determining whether the West can find a way to balance individual freedom with collective responsibility in an increasingly complex and divided world.
