Based on Wikipedia: Pivot to video

In January 2015, Facebook announced a staggering statistic that would reshape the global media landscape: the platform was averaging more than one billion video views every single day. This was not merely a data point; it was a siren call. Within months, legacy publishers and digital-native startups alike began dismantling their newsrooms, firing writers, and redirecting their meager resources toward short-form video content designed exclusively for third-party platforms like Instagram, Snapchat, and Twitter. They called it a "pivot to video." To the uninitiated observer, it looked like a bold, forward-thinking adaptation to the habits of a younger generation. In reality, it was a desperate gamble driven by the insatiable hunger of advertisers, fueled by metrics that were often misleading, and executed with a recklessness that would leave the industry scarred, bankrupt, and profoundly diminished.

The phrase "pivot to video" has since become synonymous with decline, a shorthand for the moment the media industry collectively lost its way. It represents a specific era of corporate panic where the fundamental truth—that people still loved to read—was ignored in favor of a narrative that only advertisers wanted to hear. The shift was not motivated by a genuine surge in consumer demand for video over text. As media commentators quickly pointed out, the preference for video was almost entirely a preference of the buyer, not the consumer. Yet, the pressure to follow the trend was overwhelming. Chief Financial Officers across the industry were asking the same desperate question: "How can we get more video?" The answer, it seemed, was to fire the journalists.

To understand the magnitude of this shift, one must look at the technological backdrop. Streaming media had existed since the early 1990s, but it was the mid-2000s that brought it into the mainstream with higher fidelity and broader availability. By 2007, traditional giants like The New York Times, The Washington Post, and Time Inc. had established new divisions dedicated to web video. But the real game-changer was the rise of social platforms that treated video not just as a format, but as a currency. In 2012, Twitter acquired the micro-video service Vine. By late 2014, Twitter had integrated native video streaming. In January 2015, Twitter purchased Periscope. These acquisitions signaled a clear intent: the platforms wanted content to live on them, not just link to them.

This desire for native content created a toxic feedback loop for publishers. Media critic John Herrman articulated the danger with surgical precision in 2015. He argued that the shift to Facebook video meant that the platform was no longer just interested in spreading media companies' content; it wanted to host it. To Facebook, a link to an outside page was a problem to be solved, and Facebook-hosted video was the solution. This was a strategic move to capture the value of the content within their own walled gardens, stripping publishers of their direct relationship with their audience.

The first major casualty of this new philosophy was the concept of the "home page." In February 2015, the digital video-journalism publisher NowThis made headlines by announcing it would operate without a home page entirely. Their content was to be published directly on social media platforms, bypassing their own website. It was a radical experiment in surrendering ownership for the sake of reach. Around the same time, BuzzFeed was making waves with its own massive investment in video production. An August 2014 profile highlighted the company's nascent idea of "BuzzFeed Distributed," a team of 20 people producing content that would live entirely on other popular platforms like Tumblr, Instagram, and Snapchat. The message was clear: the future of media might not even be on the media company's own website.

The allure was potent, but the foundation was rotten. The pivot was largely based on viewership metrics reported by platforms like Facebook that lacked transparency and accuracy. These proprietary algorithms could shift abruptly, devastating a publisher's traffic and revenue overnight. The situation reached a breaking point following a scandal in which Facebook revealed it had artificially inflated numbers to advertisers regarding how long viewers actually watched ads. In reality, the numbers were often meaningless; a video might be counted as "viewed" after a user scrolled past it for just a few seconds. This created a false impression of customer demand. Journalists and industry analysts eventually concluded that the shift to video was based on this mirage. Advertisers were paying for video impressions, so publishers rushed to create video, ignoring the fact that their actual audiences were still engaging with text.

By 2016, the consequences were becoming visible. In April, Mashable, a digital media company known for its coverage of technology and pop culture, fired a significant portion of its editorial staff. The company attempted to pivot away from hard news coverage, aiming to "grow Mashable across every platform" while doubling down on branded content and video. It was a strategy that prioritized the platform's algorithm over journalistic integrity. The move was a disaster. By December 2017, following a sale to Ziff Davis, Mashable retreated from this intense focus on video. Bernard Gershon, president of GershonMedia, later noted that many of these "pivots" were actually performative, aimed primarily at investors to signal innovation rather than to serve readers.

The year 2017 became the year of the great purge. By June, numerous high-profile organizations executed their own pivots, often resulting in mass layoffs. Vocativ, a media company focused on global stories, laid off at least 20 staff members, including its entire newsroom. Their justification was chillingly corporate: "As the industry evolves, we are undertaking a strategic shift to focus exclusively on video content that will be distributed via social media and other platforms." Fox Sports eliminated its entire writing staff to focus on creating "premium video across all platforms." MTV News, which had only two years prior hired Grantland co-founder Dan Fierman to lead a significant investment in longform political and cultural reporting, announced a restructuring that would cut its writing team. Fierman had left in April 2017, and by June, MTV declared it was "shifting resources into short-form video content more in line with young people's media consumption habits." The irony was palpable; the very people claiming to understand the youth market were destroying the long-form storytelling that often resonated most deeply with engaged audiences.

Vice Media, once the darling of the millennial media world, laid off at least 60 employees in July, including the editor-in-chief of Vice Sports, while simultaneously expanding its video production. In August, Mic cut ten writers and directed the remainder of the newsroom to generate videos for social platforms. CEO Chris Altchek defended the move with a statement that would come to define the era's hubris: "When you think about how many hours people spend watching video versus reading, the audience has already spoken." The audience, however, had not spoken for video at the expense of quality journalism. The move was ultimately unsuccessful. A year later, Mic laid off the majority of its remaining staff before being sold to Bustle Media Group for a fraction of its former value. The pivot had not saved the company; it had accelerated its demise.

The absurdity of the trend reached new heights with the for-profit wiki-hosting company Fandom. In September 2017, Fandom began adding commercially produced videos to its otherwise user-generated wiki subdomains. They explicitly cited the need to "keep up with user and advertiser expectations" by "diversifying their content." They claimed, without any substantiation, that "consumer patterns are changing," necessitating the addition of "complementary video." The result was a jarring clash of content. Users, who had come to Fandom for detailed, text-based information, were met with cheap, generic videos that had no place on the site. The backlash was immediate and vocal. Fandom's Chief Content Officer, Dorth Raphaely, offered a non-committal response: "I agree that with these videos in particular we did not deliver the right type of content experience." The pivot was a failure, yet the logic behind it remained unchallenged in the boardrooms of major publishers.

Movie Pilot, a company dedicated to movie news, saw its fall 2017 layoffs as a reaction to Facebook's algorithm changes. CEO Tobi Bauckhage explained that the company was moving "from a text-based publishing model to video" because they were losing money in the publishing bit of the business due to the platform's favoritism toward video over referral traffic. The majority of their staff was laid off, and their parent company was sold to Webedia. Similarly, in November 2017, the legendary magazine publisher Condé Nast cut jobs, reduced the frequency of several magazines, and shut down the print edition of Teen Vogue. In its place, they invested significant new resources in video production. A senior executive stated with terrifying certainty: "In the next 24 months, I hope that video is half our business... It's critical. It's the macro trend of content consumption."

The industry was betting the farm on a trend that was already showing signs of rot. In February 2018, Vox Media, a company that had been at the forefront of digital innovation, cut approximately 50 employees. These cuts primarily affected those assigned to "social video." Vox CEO Jim Bankoff admitted what many had suspected but few dared to say: those efforts were not "viable audience or revenue growth drivers." The pivot had failed. The video content was not driving the traffic or revenue that had been promised. The metrics were the lie; the audience was the truth.

The legacy of the pivot to video extends far beyond the layoffs of 2017. It established a precedent where platforms could dictate the content strategy of independent publishers, forcing them to sacrifice quality for engagement metrics that didn't reflect actual human interest. The trend did not end with the failure of these specific companies. In August 2020, Facebook Inc., now Meta Platforms, pivoted Instagram to video in a desperate effort to replicate the success of TikTok. They introduced "Reels," a form of short-form video, and promoted them aggressively. Reels soon accounted for more than half of the 20 most-viewed posts on Facebook. However, the content was often hollow; most of these Reels were anonymous aggregations of content scraped from TikTok, stripping creators of their attribution and context.

The cycle continued. In early 2024, Elon Musk declared that X, formerly Twitter, was now a "video-first platform." Critics immediately described this as yet another "pivot to video," echoing the same mistakes of the past. The industry had learned nothing. The platforms continued to demand video, the advertisers continued to pay for it, and the publishers continued to burn cash and talent trying to satisfy an algorithm that could never be truly satisfied.

The pivot to video was not a natural evolution of media consumption. It was a manufactured crisis, a self-fulfilling prophecy created by platforms that wanted to own the content they distributed. It was driven by the false belief that video was inherently superior to text, a belief that ignored the nuances of human attention and the enduring power of the written word. The result was a media landscape that was poorer, less diverse, and less informative. Writers were replaced by newsreaders; deep analysis was replaced by animated slideshows with captions. Publishers like Vanity Fair, The Washington Post, and Sports Illustrated began adapting their articles into cheap video content, dictated by a voiceover or presented as a slideshow, simply because the algorithm demanded it.

The tragedy of the pivot to video lies in its transparency. The publishers knew the risks. The journalists knew the quality was suffering. But the pressure from investors and the seduction of the platform's metrics were too strong to resist. They were told that the audience had spoken, that the future was video, and that to be anything else was to be irrelevant. They were wrong. The audience had not spoken for video; the advertisers had. And in the end, when the music stopped and the metrics failed to materialize, the publishers were left with empty newsrooms and a shattered reputation.

The story of the pivot to video is a cautionary tale for the entire digital age. It serves as a reminder that when content creators lose control of their distribution channels and their business models, they become vulnerable to the whims of platforms that prioritize their own profits over the health of the information ecosystem. The shift was presented as a response to changing habits, but it was actually a surrender to a flawed economic model. The media industry cut staff resources for written content in favor of short-form video, believing it was the future, only to find out they had sold their soul for a metric that didn't exist.

Today, as we look back at the era of the pivot, the scars are still visible. The loss of institutional knowledge, the departure of talented journalists, and the degradation of news quality are the lasting costs. The platforms that demanded the pivot have moved on, chasing the next new thing, while the publishers are left to rebuild. The phrase "pivot to video" remains a symbol of decline, a stark reminder of what happens when the pursuit of scale overrides the pursuit of substance. The industry learned, too late, that you cannot pivot your way out of a broken business model, and you cannot algorithm your way to a loyal audience. The truth was in the text all along, but by the time they realized it, the writers were gone.
