Based on Wikipedia: First 100 days of the first Trump presidency

At precisely noon on January 20, 2017, the @POTUS Twitter handle changed hands—the first time social media became a formal instrument of presidential transition, its blue checkmark instantly recast as a weapon in America’s new political warfare. Donald Trump’s inauguration wasn’t just a ceremony; it was a digital takeover. As Barack Obama’s final tweets vanished into the @POTUS44Archive account, Trump’s team seized control of 17 official government Twitter handles, including Melania Trump claiming @FLOTUS and Mike Pence snapping up @VP. This wasn’t mere symbolism. It was the opening salvo in a presidency designed to bypass traditional power structures—a fitting prelude to 100 days where executive orders would substitute for legislative victories and social media rants would eclipse policy white papers.

The 'first 100 days' benchmark, born from Franklin D. Roosevelt’s whirlwind 1933 launch of Social Security and the FDIC, had long measured presidential momentum. Roosevelt signed 76 laws in that span, nine of them transformative. By April 30, 2017—the endpoint of Trump’s countdown—the comparison was brutal. Despite controlling both the House and Senate, Trump’s administration had pushed through 28 bills, none deemed 'major' by political scientists. Most were administrative housekeeping: renaming federal buildings, confirming personnel, or tweaking existing programs. Fully a dozen were regulatory rollback bills using the obscure Congressional Review Act to erase Obama-era rules—like undoing methane emission limits for oil rigs or blocking paycheck transparency for coal miners. The rest? Legislative confetti. Presidential historian Michael Beschloss summed it up bluntly: 'Based on a legislative standard, this is the least productive start since records began.'

The Promises and the Paper Trail

Trump’s 2016 campaign had promised a revolution in those first 100 days. Ten specific pledges were etched into his 'Contract with the American Voter': building a border wall, repealing Obamacare, slapping 45% tariffs on China, banning Muslim immigration, appointing constitutionalist judges, and eliminating gun-free zones. The reality was a landscape of broken commitments and strategic retreats. Take the border wall—a $25 billion centerpiece of his campaign. By April 29, just one day before the 100-day mark, Congress refused to fund construction, forcing Trump to swallow a humiliating delay. He’d narrowly avoided a government shutdown days earlier by accepting a short-term spending bill with zero wall money. Similarly, his vow to label China a 'currency manipulator' evaporated by April; instead, Treasury Secretary Steve Mnuchin praised Beijing’s 'constructive' talks. Even the much-touted rule requiring 'two old regulations killed for every new one' existed only as a vague executive order—never implemented systematically.

'We’re going to have a regulatory budget,' Trump declared on January 30. 'For every one new regulation, two old regulations must be eliminated.' Yet by April, agencies had scrapped 39 rules while creating 99 new ones. The math never lied.

Only two campaign promises saw tangible movement. The first was judicial appointments: Trump signed an executive order fast-tracking 'constitutionalist' judges, and Neil Gorsuch’s Supreme Court confirmation on April 7 became his sole unambiguous victory. The second was regulatory dismantling. On March 27, he signed a bill killing an Obama-era rule requiring coal companies to monitor water pollution. It was a small win, but emblematic—13 similar rollbacks passed in those 100 days, targeting environmental, labor, and financial safeguards. These weren’t grand legislative strokes but surgical strikes against bureaucratic guardrails, often buried in omnibus spending bills. To immigration hardliners, this mattered more than any wall. One Homeland Security official privately noted, 'If you want to understand how ICE’s surveillance powers expanded later, look at these early deregulation wins. They cleared the runway.'

The Cabinet Carousel

While Congress stalled, Trump’s cabinet confirmation process unfolded like a reality TV show directed by chaos. By February 8—20 days into his term—he’d named 24 cabinet members, the largest in history. Yet fewer nominees had been confirmed than for any president except George Washington at that stage. James Mattis, the retired Marine general tapped for Defense, required a rare Congressional waiver because he’d left active duty only four years earlier (the law mandated seven). He was confirmed 98–1, but not before Trump joked, 'We’re waiving it because I don’t have a lot of time.' Others faced bloodsport. Betsy DeVos, the billionaire school choice advocate for Education Secretary, needed Vice President Pence’s tiebreaking vote after two Republicans defected—a first in cabinet history. Jeff Sessions barely survived a 52–47 vote amid backlash over his racist remarks.

The human cost was stark. On January 26, Tillerson’s visit to the State Department revealed a hollowed-out institution: career undersecretaries Joyce Anne Barr and Patrick F. Kennedy had resigned in protest of Trump’s travel ban. By April 29, most major posts were filled—Rex Tillerson at State, Elaine Chao at Transportation, Nikki Haley at the UN—but two key roles remained vacant: Trade Representative Robert Lighthizer and Council of Economic Adviser Kevin Hassett. Trump had also reshaped the cabinet’s architecture itself, demoting the Council of Economic Advisers chair while elevating the CIA and DNI directors to cabinet rank. This wasn’t administrative fine-tuning; it signaled where power would flow. When Mattis ordered the April 7 Shayrat missile strike against Syria—Trump’s first major military action—it was executed without congressional consultation, leveraging his new national security hierarchy.

The Base and the Benchmarks

Legislative failures barely registered with Trump’s core supporters. An April 2017 Gallup poll showed 96% of his voters would 'definitely' back him again—a near-perfect retention rate unmatched since Reagan. They saw victories where critics saw voids: the Gorsuch pick, the regulatory rollbacks, and the travel ban executive order that survived initial court challenges. To them, the chaos was the point. When Trump reversed positions—like backing Janet Yellen for another term as Fed Chair after calling her 'highly overrated'—his base shrugged. 'He’s a dealmaker,' one Iowa voter told pollsters. 'You pivot when you have to.'

But the broader public measured differently. Trump’s overall approval rating hovered near 40%, the lowest for any president at the 100-day mark since modern polling began. The disconnect was jarring. While 87% of Republicans praised his performance, 82% of Democrats condemned it. This tribal math explained why Trump ignored traditional benchmarks. On April 26—just four days before the 100-day deadline—he unveiled a skeletal tax reform plan promising massive cuts for corporations and the wealthy. It was vague, unfunded, and immediately panned by economists, but it energized his base. 'This is the new normal,' sighed one Republican strategist. 'He trades legislative wins for base enthusiasm.'

The Wall Street Pivot

As the April 30 deadline loomed, the tax proposal revealed Trump’s strategic shift. With the wall delayed and Obamacare repeal stalled (the 'skinny repeal' vote would fail in July), tax cuts became the administration’s fallback legacy play. The outline, drafted by Gary Cohn’s National Economic Council, promised slashing the corporate rate from 35% to 15%—a number pulled from campaign rhetoric, not economic modeling. It ignored deficit concerns and offered scant detail on individual rates. Yet for Wall Street, it was manna. The S&P 500 surged 5.5% in April, its best monthly gain since 2011. Trump seized the moment, tweeting on April 26: 'Tremendous progress being made on TAX CUTS AND REFORM!'

This pivot was no accident. By late April, it was clear Congress would grant no major legislative victories. The tax plan let Trump reframe the 100-day narrative away from broken promises and toward 'future wins.' It also defused tension with business leaders who’d balked at his trade threats. When Wilbur Ross, the Commerce Secretary, privately warned Trump that labeling China a currency manipulator would spook markets, Trump backed down. The move saved face with Wall Street while sacrificing a core campaign pledge. As one Treasury aide admitted, 'The 100-day goalposts kept moving. First it was the wall, then taxes. Whatever stuck.'

Trump’s final act before the deadline underscored the administration’s operational style. On April 28, facing a potential government shutdown, he signed a $1.1 trillion spending bill that funded federal agencies through September. Buried in its 2,232 pages were concessions: $1.6 billion for border barriers (not a 'wall' as promised) and $500 million to hire more ICE agents. It was a far cry from the $25 billion concrete barrier he’d demanded, but it kept the machinery running. The next morning, Trump tweeted: 'We will build the Wall!'—a fiction already detached from reality.

The 100 days ended not with a bang but a bargaining chip. Trump had signed 24 executive orders, 22 presidential memoranda, and 20 proclamations—more than Obama or Bush in the same span. Yet none reshaped America like FDR’s New Deal. Instead, they perfected a new governance model: governing by decree, delegitimizing opponents, and feeding base loyalty through constant conflict. When historians tally Trump’s legacy, they’ll note that April 30, 2017, wasn’t an endpoint but an inflection point. The regulatory rollbacks enabled ICE’s aggressive surveillance tactics. The judicial appointments cemented a conservative judiciary for decades. And the playbook—prioritizing base fervor over bipartisan wins—became the template for modern Republican rule.

All revolutions eat their young. Trump’s ate its own promises first.
