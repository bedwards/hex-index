In the quiet, sprawling industrial parks of South Hutchinson, Kansas, a 94,000-square-foot facility hums with a rhythm that has defined the American landscape for nearly six decades. It is a place where the mundane becomes essential, where the yellow school bus—a symbol of childhood routine and safety for millions—is not merely assembled but engineered with a specific focus on the smallest and most vulnerable passengers. This is the home of Collins Industries, a company that did not just build vehicles but fundamentally redefined how America transports its students, its emergency patients, and its workforce. While the headlines often focus on the giants of the automotive world, the story of Collins is a testament to the power of specialization and the relentless evolution of a family enterprise that grew from a single conversion shop into a cornerstone of the North American transportation infrastructure.

To understand Collins, one must first understand the gap it filled. In 1967, the landscape of school transportation was dominated by massive vehicles designed for utility and heavy loads, often based on the chassis of large SUVs like the Chevrolet Suburban or the International Harvester Travelall. These were beasts of burden, ill-suited for the narrow streets of urban neighborhoods or the specific needs of smaller school districts. Enter Don Collins Sr. In the same year that the Apollo 11 mission was still years away and the cultural landscape of America was shifting beneath its feet, Don Collins founded E-CON-O Conversion in Kansas City, Missouri. His insight was deceptively simple yet revolutionary: the future of small-scale transit lay not in shrinking a truck, but in utilizing the van. He looked at the Ford Falcon Econoline passenger van and saw a platform that offered a lower center of gravity, better maneuverability, and a chassis already designed for passenger comfort. He was among the first to recognize that a school bus could be derived from a van, shifting the industry away from the clunky, truck-based conversions that had dominated the market.

The early years were defined by this singular focus on innovation within a niche. By 1971, the company had outgrown its initial identity and the moniker E-CON-O was shed in favor of Collins Industries, a name that signaled a broader ambition. This rebranding coincided with a strategic pivot into the ambulance market, a sector that demanded the same precision and reliability as school transportation but with the added pressure of life-or-death scenarios. The move was logical; the technology required to safely transport a child to school was not entirely dissimilar to that needed to stabilize a patient on the way to a trauma center. Just a year later, in 1972, the company made its permanent home in Hutchinson, Kansas, a location that would remain its operational heart for the next fifty years. The facility was not merely a factory; it became a hub of manufacturing excellence, a place where the specific needs of the American public were met with industrial rigor.

The 1970s and 1980s were a period of aggressive expansion and diversification, a time when Collins proved that a company rooted in a single idea could branch out without losing its soul. In 1978, the company acquired American Fire Apparatus, a move that brought fire suppression vehicles into its portfolio. However, the most significant transformation occurred in 1982. It was a year that marked a profound shift in the philosophy of public transportation. Collins introduced its first bus equipped with a wheelchair lift. This was not a minor add-on; it was a declaration that accessibility was a right, not an afterthought. In doing so, the company moved decisively away from van conversions toward bodies mounted on cutaway van chassis. This technical evolution gave birth to the "Bantam," a product line that would become the industry standard for Type A small school buses. The Bantam was the physical embodiment of the company's mission: small enough to navigate tight corners, robust enough to handle daily abuse, and accessible enough to serve every child regardless of ability.

The mid-1980s saw Collins doubling down on its ambition to be a comprehensive solution provider for emergency and transit needs. The company recognized that to truly dominate the ambulance sector, it needed to own the technology of modular ambulance design. In 1984, Collins acquired Wheeled Coach Industries, the inventor of the modular ambulance, a design that allowed for rapid customization and efficient production. The following year, in 1985, the company expanded further by acquiring Capacity of Texas, a manufacturer of terminal tractors, rounding out its industrial vehicle capabilities. Yet, this era of growth was also one of strategic pruning. In 1986, Collins sold its American Fire Apparatus division, a decision that highlighted a growing focus on core competencies. The fire truck business, while lucrative, did not fit the long-term vision as perfectly as the specialized transport vehicles did.

By the 1990s, Collins Industries had cemented its status as the largest manufacturer of Type A small school buses in the United States. This was a remarkable achievement for a company that had started as a small conversion shop just three decades prior. The company's dominance was not accidental; it was the result of a relentless pursuit of quality and a deep understanding of the school transportation market. In 1998, Collins made a bold move to solidify its position by acquiring its largest competitor, Mid Bus. Mid Bus was a successor to the bus manufacturing operations of the legendary Superior Coach Company, a brand with deep historical roots. This acquisition was a watershed moment, allowing Collins to absorb the intellectual property and manufacturing capacity of a major rival. To further expand its reach, the company also acquired World Trans, Inc., entering the transit bus segment with vehicles based on both cutaway and rear-engine chassis. The company was no longer just a niche player; it was a comprehensive manufacturer offering a wide array of solutions for public transit and education.

The turn of the millennium brought another layer of complexity to the Collins story. In 2000, the company purchased Waldon Manufacturing, renaming it after its flagship product, the Lay-Mor street sweeper. This acquisition added another dimension to the company's portfolio, showcasing its ability to adapt to different municipal needs. Yet, the most significant shift of the decade was structural. Collins, which had been a publicly traded company since 1983, transitioned from a parent company to a subsidiary within a larger transportation conglomerate. In October 2006, 80 percent of the company was acquired by BNS Holding Inc., with the remaining 20 percent held by American Industrial Partners. This move privatized Collins, allowing it to operate with a new level of strategic flexibility within a larger ecosystem. The company's product line had become so diverse that by 2000, it offered the widest range of any American bus manufacturer, including three different versions of the Bantam.

The 2000s were also a period of consolidation and international reach. In 2007, Collins purchased the assets of Les Enterprises Michel Corbeil, a Quebec-based manufacturer that had filed for bankruptcy. This acquisition was a masterstroke of operational efficiency. Collins moved Corbeil's production to its Kansas facility, repackaging the vehicles as a Canadian product line. Similarly, the Mid Bus plant in Bluffton, Ohio, was closed, with production shifted entirely to the South Hutchinson facility. The branding was unified under the Bantam bodywork, marketed as the Mid Bus Guide and Corbeil Quantum, effectively streamlining the company's offerings across North America. This period of integration was not without its challenges, but it laid the groundwork for the modern Collins Industries, a company that could leverage its manufacturing scale to offer competitive products in both the US and Canadian markets.

The corporate landscape shifted again in 2010 when American Industrial Partners formed Allied Specialty Vehicles (ASV), a conglomerate that included Collins and its subsidiaries. Under the ASV umbrella, Collins was part of a massive entity that spanned fire and emergency vehicles, recreational vehicles, transit buses, and industrial machinery. In 2015, ASV was renamed the REV Group and went public in 2017, bringing a new level of scrutiny and capital to the operations of Collins. Despite the corporate maneuvering, the physical reality of the company remained rooted in Kansas. In 2009, the Collins facility suffered significant damage, but by the end of 2010, it was rebuilt, a testament to the resilience of the workforce and the importance of the site to the regional economy.

Innovation continued to drive the company forward. On March 29, 2012, Collins unveiled the Nexbus series, a replacement for the long-running Bantam line. The first Nexbus rolled off the production line on May 16, 2012, marking a new era of design and functionality. The Nexbus was not just a facelift; it represented a complete reimagining of the Type A bus, incorporating modern safety features, improved fuel efficiency, and a design that was more aerodynamic and comfortable for passengers. The branding was unified across the board; the Guide and Quantum names were retired, and all three Collins brands adopted the Nexbus moniker. By 2014, the company had begun manufacturing the Nexbus using the Ford Transit 350/350HD chassis, a move that aligned with the broader industry shift toward more versatile and capable commercial van platforms. This decision reflected a deep understanding of the supply chain and the need to adapt to the evolving automotive landscape.

The company's commitment to innovation and accessibility reached a new milestone in 2018 with the introduction of the Collins Low Floor variant of the Nexbus body. This was a significant development, as it was the first school bus derived from the Ram ProMaster body. The Low Floor featured a flat floor and a folding wheelchair ramp, making boarding and exiting significantly easier for students with mobility challenges. This design choice was not merely a technical specification; it was a human-centric decision that acknowledged the dignity and independence of every rider. By 2016, Collins had retired the Mid Bus and Corbeil brands entirely, using the Collins brand across North America to create a unified and powerful market presence.

The story of Collins Industries took a dramatic turn in January 2024, when REV Group announced its intention to exit the bus manufacturing business. This decision sent shockwaves through the industry, as Collins was a major player in the school bus market. However, the transition was swift and strategic. The company reached an agreement to sell Collins to Forest River, a subsidiary of Berkshire Hathaway, for $303 million. This acquisition brought Collins back under the umbrella of a massive manufacturing conglomerate, but one with a different focus. Forest River, known for its diverse portfolio of recreational and specialized vehicles, provided a new home for Collins, ensuring the continuity of its operations and the preservation of its legacy.

The final chapter of this era began in 2023, when Collins launched an all-electric school bus using the Ford E-Transit chassis. This was a historic moment: the first-ever battery-electric Ford Transit school bus with access for 25 students. The bus featured a wheelchair lift as an option, allowing handicapped riders to access the vehicle with ease. This launch was not just a product release; it was a signal of the future of school transportation. As cities and school districts grapple with the need to reduce carbon emissions and the rising costs of diesel fuel, the electric bus represents a viable and sustainable path forward. Collins, with its deep history of adapting to changing needs, was once again at the forefront of the industry, leading the charge toward a cleaner, more inclusive future.

The journey of Collins Industries from a small conversion shop in Kansas City to a global leader in specialized transportation is a story of vision, resilience, and adaptability. It is a story that highlights the importance of focusing on the details—the cutaway chassis, the wheelchair lift, the flat floor—that make a difference in the lives of millions of people every day. The company's ability to navigate corporate acquisitions, market shifts, and technological disruptions is a testament to the strength of its leadership and the dedication of its workforce. As Collins moves forward under the ownership of Forest River, it carries with it a legacy of innovation that has shaped the way America moves its students, its patients, and its communities. The yellow bus, once a symbol of a simpler time, has evolved into a high-tech, accessible, and sustainable vehicle, thanks in no small part to the vision of Don Collins Sr. and the generations of workers who have brought that vision to life in South Hutchinson, Kansas.
