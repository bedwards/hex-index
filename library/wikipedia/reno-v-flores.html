On July 11, 1985, a fifteen-year-old girl named Jenny Lisette Flores stepped off a bus in the United States, only to be immediately arrested by agents of the Immigration and Naturalization Service. She had crossed the border from El Salvador without authorization, fleeing a war that had torn her country apart. The government did not treat her as a child seeking refuge; they treated her as a deportable alien. Flores was taken to a detention facility designed for adults. There, in a space shared with men and women of all ages, she was subjected to daily strip searches. She was held indefinitely because the agency's rigid policy dictated that an unaccompanied minor could only be released to a parent or legal guardian. Her parents were suspected illegal immigrants themselves, making them unavailable as release contacts. For Jenny Flores, and for hundreds of children like her, the American justice system had become a cage where they waited in limbo, stripped of their childhood and their liberty.

This specific case of one girl's suffering sparked a legal battle that would ripple through American law for decades, fundamentally altering how the United States handles its most vulnerable immigrants. The litigation began as Flores v. Meese, filed by the Center for Human Rights and Constitutional Law on behalf of Flores and all minors apprehended in the Western Region of the country. It was not merely a dispute over bureaucratic procedure; it was a challenge to the very soul of due process in a nation that prides itself on protecting its children. The plaintiffs argued that the government's policies violated the Equal Protection and Due Process Clauses of the Constitution, trapping children in unsuitable conditions without a meaningful opportunity to be heard or released into safe custody.

The legal conflict centered on a policy introduced by INS Director Harold W. Ezell in September 1984. Under this framework, a detained immigrant minor could only be released to a parent or legal guardian. The stated intent was likely administrative orderliness, but the human cost was immediate and severe. Minors who had no relatives in the country were left with nowhere to go. Consequently, they remained in detention for "lengthy or indefinite" periods, often in facilities that were ill-equipped to care for them. By late 1987, the District Court for the Central District of California approved a consent decree to settle claims regarding detention conditions, but the fight over release policies intensified. In 1988, the INS issued a new regulation, 8 CFR 242.24, which codified the restriction: detained minors could be released only to parents, close relatives, or legal guardians, except in "unusual and compelling circumstances."

Judge Robert J. Kelleher did not accept this logic.

In May 1988, shortly after the new regulation took effect, Judge Kelleher rejected it outright. He held that the INS policy violated substantive due process. His ruling was a watershed moment: he declared that all minors have the right to receive a hearing from an immigration judge and that the agency's release procedures fell short of constitutional requirements. The court ordered the INS to provide every juvenile with an administrative hearing immediately after arrest to determine probable cause for detention and the necessity of any restrictions on their release. Furthermore, Kelleher ruled in Flores v. Meese, 681 F. Supp. 665 (C.D. Cal. 1988), that the government's practice of strip-searching children was unconstitutional. The district court granted summary judgment to the plaintiffs, invalidating the regulatory scheme and ordering the release of eligible juveniles to any responsible adult party, not just family members.

The battle did not end there. The INS appealed, and in June 1990, the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals issued a decision that would eventually lead to the Supreme Court's intervention. The legal machinery ground on, but the human stakes remained constant: children were being held in conditions that many argued were akin to punishment rather than civil detention.

The Supreme Court and the Limits of Due Process

By 1993, the case had ascended to the highest court in the land as Reno v. Flores, 507 U.S. 292 (1993). At this point, the names on the docket had changed; Janet Reno was now the Attorney General, and the INS regulations were under fresh scrutiny. The core question was whether the INS regulations regarding the release of alien unaccompanied minors violated the Due Process Clause of the United States Constitution.

The Supreme Court's ruling was a mixed bag for civil liberties advocates. The Court held that "alien juveniles detained on suspicion of being deportable may be released only to a parent, legal guardian, or other related adult." In a 6-to-3 decision, the justices concluded that the government's interest in preventing flight and ensuring appearance at proceedings justified the restriction, provided the conditions of detention were not punitive. The Court reasoned that the regulation did not violate substantive due process because it was reasonably related to legitimate governmental interests.

However, this legal victory for the government came with a caveat that would eventually undermine its long-term application. While the Supreme Court upheld the general principle of releasing minors only to responsible adults, it did not give the government carte blanche to detain them indefinitely in poor conditions. The decision acknowledged the tension between immigration enforcement and constitutional rights but leaned heavily toward deference to executive authority in this specific context.

The legacy of Reno v. Flores is not found solely in that 1993 Supreme Court opinion, but in what happened next. The litigation did not conclude with a gavel strike; it evolved into a negotiated peace that would govern American immigration policy for over twenty years. Following the Supreme Court's decision, both parties agreed to a stipulated settlement in the District Court for the Central District of California. This agreement, known as the Flores Settlement Agreement (FSA), was signed in 1997 and remains one of the most significant pieces of immigration law ever written.

The Flores Settlement: A Twenty-Year Truce

The 1997 settlement was a binding contract between the federal government and the plaintiffs, supervised by U.S. District Judge Dolly Gee (who would later play a pivotal role in enforcing its terms). The FSA established strict national regulations and standards regarding the detention and treatment of minors by federal agencies. It governed the policies for the legacy INS and its successor agencies within the Department of Homeland Security (DHS), including U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) and U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS).

The agreement was built on a fundamental recognition: children are different from adults, and the civil immigration system is not equipped to function as a prison for minors. The FSA mandated that unaccompanied alien children be released without unnecessary delay. It established a "release preference" hierarchy, prioritizing parents, then other relatives, then licensed programs, and finally, in limited circumstances, non-licensed facilities. Crucially, it set a hard timeline: the government generally could not detain alien children for more than 20 days before they must be released to an appropriate sponsor or placed in a licensed care facility.

This was not merely a suggestion; it was a court order with teeth. The FSA required that detention facilities holding minors meet specific standards regarding space, temperature, lighting, and access to food and water. It prohibited the use of locked cells for children unless they posed a flight risk or danger to others, and even then, only under strict supervision. The settlement effectively created a parallel system within the immigration apparatus, one that prioritized the welfare of the child over the convenience of deportation proceedings.

For two decades, the Flores Settlement operated as a stabilizing force. It prevented the kind of indefinite detention seen in Jenny Flores's original case and ensured that thousands of children were not trapped in adult facilities. However, the settlement was always fragile, dependent on political will and judicial oversight. As immigration politics shifted in the 2010s, the tension between the government's desire for stricter enforcement and the humanitarian constraints of the FSA began to fracture.

The Crisis of 2018 and the Fight for Toothbrushes

By 2017, the cracks in the system were widening. U.S. District Judge Dolly Gee, who had taken over supervision of the Flores Settlement, found that children in the custody of U.S. Customs and Border Protection were living in squalid conditions. They lacked food, clean water, and basic hygiene items. Many were sleep-deprived, held in what became known as "hieleras" or iceboxes due to freezing temperatures.

Judge Gee ordered the federal government to provide these essentials, including soap and toothbrushes. The government's response was not an admission of error but a legal maneuvering that revealed how detached some officials had become from the reality of holding children in custody. In June 2018, President Trump issued an executive order directing Attorney General Jeff Sessions to ask the District Court to modify the Flores Settlement Agreement. The goal was explicit: allow the government to detain alien families together for longer periods, including during their immigration and potential criminal proceedings. This was part of a broader "zero tolerance" policy that had resulted in the separation of thousands of children from their parents.

The request to modify the settlement was denied on July 9, 2018. Judge Gee ruled that there was no legal basis to amend an agreement that required children to be released within 20 days. The administration's attempt to bypass the timeline failed in court, but the conflict escalated into a bizarre and deeply disturbing public spectacle in June 2019.

During a hearing on June 18, 2019, Department of Justice attorneys argued against the necessity of providing minors with basic hygiene products. A video of this argument went viral, sparking nationwide outrage. Senior DOJ attorney Matthew Whitaker's team contended that the court order forcing them to provide soap and toothbrushes exceeded the original scope of the Flores agreement. They seemed to be arguing that the government had no obligation to ensure children could brush their teeth or wash their hands while in federal custody.

The public reaction was immediate and visceral. How could the United States, a nation with a constitutional framework designed to protect human dignity, argue so passionately against providing soap to children? The image of federal lawyers fighting for the right to withhold toothbrushes became a symbol of the dehumanization at the heart of modern immigration enforcement.

The Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals did not let the argument stand. On August 15, 2019, a three-judge panel upheld Judge Gee's order, rejecting the federal government's appeal. The court made it clear that the Flores Settlement was binding and that the government could not pick and choose which provisions to follow based on convenience or political pressure. Children in custody were entitled to basic hygiene, adequate food, and safe conditions.

The Human Cost of Legal Technicalities

The legal battles over Reno v. Flores and the subsequent settlement agreement are often discussed in terms of statutes, clauses, and judicial precedents. But behind every legal citation is a human story that demands attention. Jenny Lisette Flores was just one name on a class action lawsuit, but her experience of being strip-searched in an adult facility represents a systemic failure that persisted for years.

The "zero tolerance" policy of 2018 and the subsequent family separations highlighted the fragility of the protections established by the settlement. When the government attempted to detain families together indefinitely, they were essentially trying to use children as leverage to deter migration, a tactic that the Flores Settlement was specifically designed to prevent. The attempt to modify the agreement to allow longer detention of families was an acknowledgment by the administration that the existing rules were too humane for their enforcement goals.

The courts repeatedly stepped in to restore balance. Judge Gee's rulings were not abstract exercises in jurisprudence; they were interventions to stop the suffering of real children. When she ordered the provision of soap and water, she was addressing a situation where children were being held in conditions that violated basic human dignity. The government's resistance to these orders—arguing that providing hygiene items exceeded their legal obligations—revealed a bureaucratic mindset that viewed children as administrative burdens rather than human beings with rights.

The legacy of the Flores Settlement is that it forced the government to confront the reality of its detention policies. It established that even in the context of immigration enforcement, which operates under civil law rather than criminal law, the state has an affirmative obligation to care for the minors in its custody. The 20-day limit on detention was a recognition that indefinite confinement is inappropriate for children.

However, the story is not yet over. As of 2026, the Flores Settlement remains in effect, but it continues to face challenges. The federal government has introduced new regulations in an attempt to implement changes to the agreement, leading to ongoing legal disputes. The tension between the desire for strict border enforcement and the constitutional duty to protect children remains unresolved.

The events surrounding Reno v. Flores serve as a stark reminder of how quickly rights can be eroded when political will outweighs humanitarian concerns. The case began with a fifteen-year-old girl from El Salvador who was denied release because her parents were not citizens. It evolved into a decades-long struggle that defined the boundaries of American immigration law.

Today, the Flores Settlement stands as a testament to the power of legal advocacy and the resilience of those who fight for the vulnerable. It proved that even when the Supreme Court upholds broad government powers, the courts can still find ways to protect individual rights through specific, enforceable agreements. The requirement to provide soap, food, and safe housing may seem small in the grand scheme of national security debates, but these are the building blocks of human dignity.

The struggle for Jenny Flores and the children who followed her is a story of a nation grappling with its own identity. It asks a fundamental question: What does it mean to be an American when we encounter the world's most vulnerable? The answer has not always been clear, but the courts, through the lens of Reno v. Flores and the Settlement Agreement, have consistently pushed back against the impulse to treat children as disposable.

As the legal landscape continues to shift, the lessons from this era remain vital. The conditions in which we hold our most vulnerable populations define us more than any policy paper or executive order ever could. The fight for toothbrushes and clean water was not a triviality; it was a battle for the soul of the American justice system. And while the government may argue against such provisions, the courts have made it clear: in America, no child is without rights.

The story of Reno v. Flores is far from finished. It continues to play out in courtrooms across the country, where judges are tasked with interpreting a settlement that was signed nearly thirty years ago but remains as relevant today as ever. The human cost of immigration policy is not just measured in numbers or statistics; it is measured in the sleepless nights of children held in detention, the fear of those separated from their families, and the resilience of those who refuse to let these injustices go unchallenged.

In the end, the legacy of Jenny Flores is that she forced a change. She was not just a plaintiff; she was a catalyst for a legal framework that has protected thousands of children since 1997. Her story reminds us that the law is not static; it is a living instrument that can be wielded to protect the innocent when the political will fails them. The battle continues, but the precedent remains: children in custody deserve more than just a cell; they deserve care, dignity, and the promise of a future free from the shadow of indefinite detention.
