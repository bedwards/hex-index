Based on Wikipedia: Entrepreneurship

In 1935, economist Joseph Schumpeter delivered a diagnosis that would reshape how we understand economic progress: the entrepreneur isn't just someone who starts companies—he is the force that "destroys" old industries and creates new ones. He called it "creative destruction," and the metaphor has never stopped resonating.

The word itself traces back to the French entreprendre, meaning "to undertake something." But pinning down what entrepreneurship means has puzzled economists, sociologists, and business scholars for nearly three centuries. The earliest serious analysis came from Richard Cantillon in the late 1600s and Adam Smith shortly after—pioneers who recognized that some people don't just follow economic trends, they create them.

Yet for over a hundred years after Smith, entrepreneurship was largely ignored by mainstream economics. It wasn't until Schumpeter's work in the 1930s—and later economists like Carl Menger and Ludwig von Mises in Austria—that the field gained theoretical grounding. By the late 1970s, entrepreneurship became central to how we think about economic dynamism.

What Entrepreneurship Actually Means

At its core, entrepreneurship is the creation or extraction of economic value by identifying opportunities others miss and turning them into commercial reality. The process demands considerable initiative and carries substantial risk—financial, social, and often personal. This isn't merely "starting a business," though that certainly qualifies.

The French economist Jean-Baptiste Say offered a broader definition in the early 19th century: entrepreneurship "shifts economic resources out of an area of lower and into an area of higher productivity and greater yield." In this view, entrepreneurs aren't just profit-seekers—they're resource reorganizers who make things work better.

Four criteria typically define whether someone qualifies as an entrepreneur. First, there must be opportunities or situations where resources can be recombined to generate value. Second, the entrepreneur requires some differential access—insider knowledge, preferential information, or unique relationships that others lack. Third, bearing a meaningful level of risk is non-negotiable. Fourth, organizing people and resources toward a specific goal is essential.

The definition isn't narrow. A tech startup founder in Silicon Valley counts as fully as much as a social enterprise running a charitable organization in Nairobi. Small firms, medium-sized companies, large corporations, for-profits and not-for-profits—all can exhibit entrepreneurial behavior.

The Two Schools of Thought

Academics have split on what entrepreneurship actually represents. One school focuses on the entrepreneur as an individual—what traits does she possess? What does he do? This functionalistic approach treats entrepreneurs as unique creatures whose personalities explain their success.

The alternative view—the processual or contextual approach—turns the spotlight away from personality traits and onto the environment. It's not what the entrepreneur is, but what she does: how agency interacts with context, how opportunities emerge from specific circumstances, how timing and geography reshape everything.

This tension matters practically. A founder who attributes her success solely to personal genius operates differently than one who sees herself as navigating a particular ecosystem—accessing government programs, mentorship networks, incubators, and regulatory environments that either enable or constrain her ambitions.

Schumpeter's Revolution

For Schumpeter, the entrepreneur was a revolutionary figure in economic history. "The gale of creative destruction" wasn't just poetic language—it described how entire industries transform. When someone launches an innovation that makes obsolete products others depend upon, that's entrepreneurship at its most disruptive.

But economist Israel Kirzner offered a crucial counterpoint: most entrepreneurial activity isn't dramatic disruption. Much of it is incremental—finding slight improvements, cheaper materials, more efficient processes. The move from paper to plastic in manufacturing drinking straws counts as entrepreneurship if it creates value through better resource allocation.

This distinction matters for how we evaluate entrepreneurship today. Not every founder needs to build the next Amazon. Some entrepreneurs simply make things work better than they did before—more profitably, more sustainably, with fewer wasted resources.

The Modern Entrepreneurial Ecosystem

The 21st century has seen governments deliberately promote entrepreneurship as a tool for economic growth. After supply-side economics lost dominance, entrepreneurship became the favored mechanism for stimulating competition and expansion.

Today's entrepreneurial ecosystem typically includes several layers. Government programs actively support startups through grants, tax incentives, and simplified regulations. Non-governmental organizations offer mentorship, networking, and training—some through formal incubation centers, others through online communities. Advocacy groups lobby for small-business-friendly laws, recognizing that the regulatory environment shapes who can actually start a company.

Resources like business incubators provide physical space, capital connections, and operational support. Seed funding, venture capital, and increasingly diverse financing options have democratized access to growth capital—though gaps persist across regions, industries, and demographic groups.

The ASEAN Example

Data from the Global Entrepreneurship Monitor reveals regional patterns worth examining. In Southeast Asian nations like those in ASEAN, entrepreneurs often pursue opportunity while already employed, leveraging their unique experiences to spot gaps others miss. They build long-term mental models for their ventures while scanning short-term opportunities—combining strategic patience with tactical speed.

These patterns suggest serial entrepreneurship is common in the region: founders launching one venture then immediately pursuing another. It's a dynamic that's shaped by cultural values, economic conditions, and access to cross-border markets.

Why This Matters Now

The connection between entrepreneurship and economic growth remains debated in academic economics. Some argue entrepreneurship drives endogenous growth—theoretical models suggest it's a residual factor explaining why economies expand beyond what traditional inputs like labor and capital would predict. Others counter that creative destruction is largely responsible for industry dynamism and long-run prosperity.

What isn't disputed: entrepreneurs create something new and unique. They transmute value where none existed. Whether through dramatic innovation or humble process improvement, they reorganize resources toward higher productivity.

The entrepreneur's role has evolved from 17th-century French origins through 20th century theory wars into a 21st century ecosystem of support. Schumpeter's "creative destruction" remains the most useful metaphor—because every time someone identifies an opportunity, takes a risk, and organizes resources differently, the economy shifts. Not gently. Not always fairly. But forward.
