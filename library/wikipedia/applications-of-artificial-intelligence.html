Based on Wikipedia: Applications of artificial intelligence

In August 2025, a review conducted by MIT surveyed a vast array of companies and arrived at a startling conclusion: ninety-five percent of them reported no improvement in revenue from their use of artificial intelligence. This statistic shatters the pervasive narrative that AI is an instant panacea for corporate inefficiency, revealing instead a landscape where technology often outpaces value creation. Just months later, in September 2025, the Harvard Business Review coined a term to describe the phenomenon of this disconnect: "workslop." This refers to AI-generated content that masquerades as high-quality work but lacks the substance required to meaningfully advance a task. Far from being a futuristic curiosity, workslop represents a tangible erosion of trust and collaboration among colleagues, a subtle yet corrosive force that undermines productivity rather than enhancing it. The promise of artificial intelligence—computational systems performing tasks associated with human intelligence such as learning, reasoning, problem-solving, perception, and decision-making—has long been the subject of academic speculation and commercial hype. Yet, as we stand in mid-2026, the reality of AI's application is a complex tapestry woven from genuine breakthroughs, significant inefficiencies, and a critical reevaluation of what these tools can actually deliver.

The field of artificial intelligence is not a monolith; it is a collection of subfields, each with distinct methodologies and outcomes. Among these, machine learning has become the workhorse of modern industry, powering everything from language translation and image recognition to credit scoring and e-commerce recommendations. More recently, the advent of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) has shifted the paradigm from systems that analyze data to systems that create it. Using generative models, these tools produce text, images, videos, and other forms of media at a scale previously unimaginable. The potential for this technology is undeniably vast. In 2023, studies illuminated specific pockets of success: contact centers saw a fifteen percent increase in productivity, while writing tasks surged by up to forty percent when augmented by AI. These numbers suggest that when the fit between task and tool is precise, the returns can be substantial. However, these early wins mask a broader, more turbulent reality where the integration of AI into the workforce remains fraught with friction and uncertainty.

The Agrarian Algorithm

Nowhere is the tension between algorithmic promise and practical application more visible than in agriculture, a sector that has historically been slow to adopt high-tech solutions yet stands to gain immensely from them. Farmers are increasingly turning to AI not as a replacement for human intuition, but as a precision instrument to optimize the very basics of survival: water, fertilizer, and pest control. The goal is straightforward—identify areas needing irrigation or treatment to increase yields—but the implementation requires a sophisticated understanding of variable conditions across vast landscapes.

AI systems are now being deployed to classify livestock pig call emotions, a task that might seem esoteric but is critical for animal welfare and herd management. By analyzing vocalizations, these systems can detect distress or illness before physical symptoms manifest, allowing for earlier intervention. In greenhouses, AI automates the delicate balance of temperature and humidity, creating environments where crops can thrive regardless of external weather patterns. Furthermore, these tools are instrumental in detecting diseases and pests early, preventing outbreaks that could decimate a harvest.

The optimization of irrigation stands out as one of the most immediate benefits. Water is a finite resource, and the ability to apply it with surgical precision reduces waste while maximizing growth. Yet, even here, the technology is not without its critics or limitations. The reliance on data-driven decisions assumes that the models are trained on representative samples, a condition that is not always met in diverse agricultural regions. While the promise of increased efficiency is compelling, the reality for farmers involves navigating a new set of dependencies, where the health of their crops becomes tied to the reliability of software updates and sensor networks.

The Coding Paradox and "Vibe Coding"

If agriculture represents the physical application of AI, then software development represents its digital soul, a domain that has been radically transformed by generative models. In recent years, a phenomenon dubbed "vibe coding" has emerged, describing the practice of creating software primarily through AI assistance. Developers describe a workflow where they prompt an AI to generate code, review it, and refine it, often relying on intuition rather than deep technical understanding of every line produced. Tools for real-time code completion, chat-based programming assistants, and automated test generation are now ubiquitous plugins in Integrated Development Environments (IDEs).

The allure is powerful: the potential to speed up software development dramatically. However, this acceleration comes with significant hidden costs. Code generated or suggested by AI can be incorrect, inefficient, or riddled with subtle bugs that take longer to diagnose than the time saved during generation. The rush to prematurely adopt these technologies has led to an accumulation of technical debt, where companies inherit complex, poorly understood codebases that are difficult to maintain or scale.

Moreover, the cybersecurity implications are profound. AI coding software is trained on a vast corpus of public code, much of which is of inconsistent quality and often replicates poor security practices. When these models suggest vulnerabilities as standard solutions, they can inadvertently bake security flaws into the very fabric of new applications. The requirement for careful human review has not diminished; if anything, it has become more critical yet more difficult to execute, as developers must verify not just what the code does, but why it works and whether it is safe.

The history of AI in computer science also reveals a fascinating irony: many of the tools we now take for granted were originally developed within AI laboratories. Time-sharing systems, interactive interpreters, graphical user interfaces, the computer mouse, rapid application development environments, linked list data structures, automatic storage management, and paradigms like symbolic programming, functional programming, dynamic programming, and object-oriented programming all trace their origins to AI research. Even optical character recognition and constraint satisfaction techniques were once considered cutting-edge AI applications before they became mainstream computer science infrastructure. This evolution suggests that the most successful applications of AI are often those that eventually disappear into the background, becoming invisible utilities rather than headline-grabbing technologies.

The Human Element: HR, Customer Service, and "Workslop"

Beyond the code and the crops, AI has penetrated the human-centric domains of human resources and customer service with mixed results. In recruitment, algorithms are tasked with screening resumes, ranking candidates based on qualifications, and predicting a candidate's likelihood of success in a given role. Chatbots automate repetitive communication tasks, aiming to streamline the hiring process and reduce operation costs. Platforms like Pypestream have automated customer service for mobile applications, while Google apps analyze language to convert speech into text and identify angry customers through their tone, responding with appropriate de-escalation strategies. Amazon utilizes chatbots to handle order status checks, cancellations, refunds, and even the handoff to human representatives.

However, the efficiency of these systems often collides with the complexity of human emotion and judgment. The term "workslop," introduced by the Harvard Business Review in September 2025, serves as a grim reminder of what happens when AI replaces human substance. When employees use generative AI to produce reports, emails, or creative work that looks professional but lacks depth, it creates an illusion of productivity. This "work" does not advance tasks; instead, it clogs the workflow with content that requires verification and correction. Studies conducted in collaboration with the Stanford Social Media Lab indicate that workslop undermines trust among colleagues. When a team member's output is suspected to be largely AI-generated without meaningful human oversight, the collaborative fabric of the organization frays.

In the hospitality industry, AI is used to reduce repetitive tasks, analyze trends, and interact with guests through chatbots, virtual voice assistants, and service robots. The goal is to predict customer needs before they are articulated. Yet, the very presence of these automated entities can sometimes create a sense of detachment, where the human touch that defines high-quality hospitality is lost in favor of algorithmic efficiency. In education, similar patterns emerge. AI tools automate attendance tracking, grading, and marking, freeing up teachers to focus on instruction. They monitor student progress and analyze learning behaviors to facilitate timely interventions for struggling students. While these applications promise a more personalized learning experience, they also raise questions about the role of human mentorship in an era where algorithms are increasingly responsible for evaluating and guiding young minds.

Energy, Environment, and the Search for Solutions

As the world grapples with climate change and resource scarcity, AI is being positioned as a critical tool for environmental stewardship and energy management. The U.S. Department of Energy, in an April 2024 report, outlined several promising applications: modeling power grids, reviewing federal permits using large language models, predicting renewable energy production levels, and improving the planning process for electric vehicle charging networks. Machine learning algorithms are helping to manage the intermittency of renewable energy sources, ensuring that solar and wind power can be integrated smoothly into the grid without causing instability.

In the realm of environmental monitoring, AI-driven satellite data analysis is providing unprecedented insights into the health of our planet. "Global Plastic Watch," an AI-based satellite platform, tracks plastic waste sites to identify who and where mismanages plastic, specifically targeting ocean pollution. This technology helps pinpoint dumping grounds and holds bad actors accountable, offering a new level of transparency in environmental governance.

Furthermore, machine learning is being deployed to spot early-warning signs of disasters and environmental crises. From natural pandemics and earthquakes to landslides, heavy rainfall, long-term water supply vulnerability, ecosystem collapse tipping points, cyanobacterial bloom outbreaks, and droughts, AI systems are scanning for patterns that human observers might miss. The University of Southern California's Center for Artificial Intelligence in Society has launched initiatives to use these technologies to address homelessness, while Stanford researchers analyze satellite images to identify high-poverty areas. These applications demonstrate the potential of AI to serve as a force for social good, providing data-driven insights that can inform policy and direct resources where they are needed most.

The Frontiers: Quantum Computing and Self-Replicating Intelligence

Perhaps the most futuristic application of AI lies in its ability to create other AIs and to interface with quantum computing. In November 2017, Google's AutoML project achieved a milestone by evolving new neural net topologies that created NASNet, a system optimized for ImageNet and POCO F1 devices. NASNet's performance exceeded all previously published benchmarks on ImageNet, marking a significant leap in the field of automated machine learning. This self-referential capability suggests a future where AI systems design even more efficient versions of themselves, potentially accelerating progress at an exponential rate.

Simultaneously, research and development into quantum computers are being performed with the aid of machine learning algorithms. Prototypes of photonic, quantum memristive devices for neuromorphic computers (NC) are emerging, utilizing NC-using quantum materials with various potential applications in neuromorphic computing. The use of quantum machine learning for quantum simulators has been proposed as a solution to some of the most difficult problems in physics and chemistry. If successful, this convergence could unlock new materials, drug discoveries, and fundamental understandings of the universe that are currently beyond our computational reach.

The Media Landscape and the Invisible Hand

In the media sector, AI's influence is pervasive, often invisible to the consumer yet transformative for producers. Computer vision algorithms analyze movies, TV programs, advertisements, and user-generated content with a level of scrutiny that is both impressive and unsettling. Object recognition, face recognition, and scene detection are used to facilitate media search, create descriptive keywords, monitor content policy (such as verifying suitability for specific viewing times), and detect logos or celebrity faces for targeted ad placement.

Techniques like motion interpolation, pixel-art scaling algorithms, image restoration, photo colorization, and film restoration are now standard tools in the post-production pipeline. These technologies allow studios to breathe new life into archival footage, making old films look contemporary or restoring damaged prints to their original glory. However, this capability also raises ethical questions about authenticity and the potential for deepfakes, where media content can be manipulated to deceive viewers. The line between enhancement and fabrication becomes increasingly blurred as these tools become more accessible and powerful.

The Double-Edged Sword of Efficiency

The narrative of AI applications is not one of unbridled success but of a complex interplay between potential and pitfalls. While the technology offers remarkable tools for optimization, from the precision of agricultural irrigation to the efficiency of quantum simulations, it also introduces new forms of waste and inefficiency. The concept of "workslop" serves as a cautionary tale: when we allow AI to generate content without rigorous human oversight, we risk flooding our systems with low-value output that erodes trust and collaboration.

The MIT review of August 2025 and the Harvard Business Review article of September 2025 provide a necessary corrective to the hype cycle. They remind us that increased use of AI does not automatically translate into increased revenue or actual productivity. In fact, without careful implementation and a clear understanding of the technology's limitations, organizations can find themselves bogged down by technical debt, cybersecurity vulnerabilities, and a workforce struggling to distinguish between genuine innovation and algorithmic filler.

As we move forward in 2026, the challenge for industry leaders, policymakers, and researchers is not simply to adopt more AI, but to adopt it wisely. This means recognizing that while AI can automate tasks, screen resumes, optimize energy grids, and even design better algorithms, it cannot replace the human capacity for judgment, empathy, and creative problem-solving in a meaningful way. The tools are powerful, but their value is determined by how we wield them. From the pig pens of modern farms to the quantum processors of research labs, the application of artificial intelligence remains a work in progress—a journey where the destination is not just greater efficiency, but a more thoughtful integration of machine capability with human purpose.

The history of AI is littered with inventions that have been absorbed into mainstream computer science and forgotten as "AI." Time-sharing, the mouse, and object-oriented programming were once the stuff of futuristic laboratories; today, they are the bedrock of our digital existence. This pattern suggests that the most successful applications of AI will eventually cease to be noticed as such, becoming seamless parts of the infrastructure we rely on every day. Yet, until that point is reached, we must navigate the messy, often contradictory reality of a technology that promises everything but demands much in return. The path forward requires not just technological prowess, but a deep commitment to ethical considerations, rigorous testing, and a willingness to admit when the algorithm has gone wrong.

In the end, the story of AI applications is a reflection of our own aspirations and limitations. It is a mirror that shows us how we work, how we think, and how we value human effort versus machine efficiency. As we continue to integrate these systems into every facet of society, from education to energy, from healthcare to hospitality, the question remains not whether AI will change the world, but how we will ensure that it changes it for the better. The data from 2025 serves as a crucial checkpoint: we have built powerful machines, but we must now build the wisdom to use them well.
