Based on Wikipedia: Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn

On August 18, 1971, a seventeen-year-old girl from North Springs High School was sexually assaulted at a party in Sandy Springs, Georgia. She died that night. The tragedy was compounded not just by the violence she suffered, but by the confusion that followed. Initially, law enforcement believed her death resulted from alcohol poisoning or a drug overdose. It was only after investigators found vegetation tangled in her clothing that the nature of the case shifted dramatically from an accidental death to a horrific crime of murder and rape. The medical reality was grim: she had asphyxiated on her own vomit.

Seven months later, six young men were charged with her rape and murder. The legal proceedings that unfolded over the next two years would eventually reshape the constitutional landscape of the United States, pitting the right to privacy against the freedom of the press. Five of the defendants—Ronald Eugene Longo, Joe Adams Thompson, Bruce Fuson Howard, Peter Manchee, Bobby Ray King, and Craig Michael Wozniak—eventually pleaded guilty to rape or attempted rape. One pleaded not guilty. Amidst this grim tableau of youthful crime, judicial procedure, and parental grief, a reporter named Thomas Wassell stood in the hallway of a Georgia courthouse.

Wassell was working for WSB-TV. It was 1972. The court proceedings were underway, and during a recess, he approached the clerk in open court. He asked for a copy of the indictment documents. These were not secret files hidden away in a vault; they were official records of a public proceeding. The indictment contained the name of the victim. Wassell obtained the document. That same night, while reporting on the sentencing hearing for the defendants, he broadcast his daughter's name to the entire viewing audience of the Atlanta area.

The man who sued over this broadcast was Martin Cohn, the father of the girl whose life had been cut short by violence and whose final moments were marred by a botched investigation. For any parent, the exposure of their child's identity in the aftermath of a rape is a secondary violation, a fresh wound inflicted on top of an old one. Cohn sued WSB-TV and its reporter. He argued that their actions violated Georgia's shield law, which specifically prohibited the release of a rape victim's name, and also infringed upon his family's common-law right to privacy.

The legal battle began in a state trial court where the judge sided with Cohn. The court granted summary judgment against Cox Broadcasting, rejecting the publisher's defense based on the First Amendment. It seemed, for a moment, that the courts would prioritize the dignity of the victim and the grief of the family over the broadcaster's desire to inform the public. However, the path to the Supreme Court is rarely a straight line, and the Georgia legal system offered its own complexities.

When the case moved to the Georgia Supreme Court, the state justices dismantled Cohn's claim under the specific shield law. They argued that the statute created only a criminal penalty for violating the privacy rule, not a civil cause of action that allowed a victim's family to sue for damages. Yet, they did not let the matter rest there. The court allowed Cohn's common-law privacy claim to stand. In doing so, they rejected Cox Broadcasting's argument that the First Amendment protected the disclosure. The state court drew a stark comparison: publishing a rape victim's name was akin to fraud, perjury, libel, or slander—activities traditionally excluded from constitutional protection.

Upon rehearing, the Georgia Supreme Court doubled down on its stance. It ruled that the shield law represented a "legitimate limitation on the right of freedom of expression contained in the First Amendment." The court's reasoning was rooted in a specific view of public interest. They declared that there simply was no public interest or general concern regarding the identity of a rape victim that would justify elevating the disclosure to the level of First Amendment protection. Consequently, they reversed the trial court's summary judgment on the privacy claim and sent the case back for a determination of whether Cox had intentionally invaded Cohn's privacy.

At this juncture, the stakes were incredibly high. The Georgia Supreme Court had effectively told the press that even if information was obtained legally from public records, publishing it could still lead to civil liability if it violated state privacy laws. This created a chilling effect on journalism across the state and potentially the nation. Before the case could proceed further in the state trial court—potentially leading to years more litigation—the United States Supreme Court agreed to hear Cox's appeal.

The jurisdiction of the Supreme Court was not automatic. The federal courts generally wait until a state case has fully concluded before intervening, to respect the autonomy of state judicial systems. However, the Supreme Court reasoned that Georgia's Supreme Court ruling satisfied the requirement for finality regarding the federal issues at hand. If the decision stood unreviewed, it posed an immediate and irreparable harm to the freedom of the press. The potential damage was not just theoretical; every time a newspaper or broadcaster withheld information from public records due to fear of state lawsuits, the public's right to know was diminished.

The case reached the nation's highest court in late 1974, argued on November 11, and decided on March 3, 1975. The outcome would define the boundaries between a parent's grief and the press's duty to report on government proceedings.

The Clash of Rights

The central tension in Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn was not merely about a name being broadcast on television. It was about the mechanics of democracy. In a system where the citizenry is the final judge of public business, information must flow freely from the institutions that govern us to the people they serve. The Supreme Court had to weigh two profound interests: the right of an individual to privacy in the face of a devastating crime, and the right of the press to publish truthful information derived from official government records.

The majority opinion, delivered with an 8-1 vote in favor of Cox Broadcasting, struck a decisive blow for the First Amendment. The Court held that both the Georgia statute prohibiting the release of a rape victim's name and its common-law counterpart were unconstitutional as applied to this case. The logic was rooted in the source of the information. Thomas Wassell had not stolen documents from a police station at night; he had walked into a courtroom during a recess, approached a public official, and asked for a copy of an indictment.

The indictment was an open document, part of the public record. It was a formal statement by the state accusing individuals of crimes. By its very nature, such documents are meant to be scrutinized by the public. The Court reasoned that once information is released into the public domain through official channels, the government cannot then turn around and punish those who publish it. To allow otherwise would be to grant the state a power it did not possess: the ability to control the narrative of its own proceedings.

"The freedom of the press to publish that information appears to us to be of critical importance to our type of government in which the citizenry is the final judge of the proper conduct of public business. In preserving that form of government the First and Fourteenth Amendments command nothing less than that the States may not impose sanctions on the publication of truthful information contained in official court records open to public inspection."

This statement from the majority opinion encapsulates the core philosophy of the decision. The Court recognized that while the privacy interests of rape victims are real, deep, and worthy of respect, they cannot override the fundamental requirement for transparency in the judicial system. If the press is afraid to report on court records for fear of being sued, then the public's ability to oversee the justice system is compromised. The "public interest" argument advanced by the Georgia courts was dismissed; the Court found that the very act of reporting on a murder trial, including the identity of the victim as it appeared in the indictment, was inherently a matter of public concern.

The ruling established a clear principle: if the information is truthful and obtained from official court records open to public inspection, its publication is protected by the First Amendment. This protection held even when the subject matter was sensitive and the consequences for the victim's family were painful. The Court did not say that publishing the name was a good thing in a moral sense; it said that the state could not use the law to stop it.

The Dissent and the Procedural Debate

The decision was not unanimous. Justice William Rehnquist filed a lone dissent, though his objection was procedural rather than substantive regarding the First Amendment issues. Rehnquist argued that the Supreme Court should never have heard the case at all. He believed the lower courts had not yet finished their work and that the finality requirement for federal review had not been met.

"Instead of waiting until the case finished in state court, the Court construes § 1257 so that may virtually rush out and meet the prospective constitutional litigant as he approaches our doors."

Rehnquist's concern was about the role of the Supreme Court. He felt that by intervening before the state trial could determine whether Cox had intentionally invaded Cohn's privacy, the Court was bypassing the normal judicial process. He did not address the weighty First Amendment questions directly in his dissent; instead, he focused on the idea that the federal judiciary should exercise restraint and allow state courts to complete their work before stepping in. For Rehnquist, the rush to judgment by the Supreme Court threatened the balance of power between the state and federal systems.

Despite this procedural objection, the majority pressed forward, driven by the urgency of the issue. The potential for a chilling effect on the press was too great to wait for a full trial in Georgia. If the law stood as interpreted by the Georgia Supreme Court, newsrooms across the country would have to walk on eggshells whenever they reported on sensitive court cases.

The Human Cost vs. Constitutional Principles

It is impossible to discuss this case without acknowledging the profound human tragedy at its center. Martin Cohn's daughter was a real person, a seventeen-year-old student whose life ended in violence and confusion. Her name, once spoken by her father or whispered among family, was broadcast into thousands of living rooms. The pain of that exposure cannot be minimized. For a parent, the loss of a child is an unimaginable burden; having that child's identity stripped of its privacy and paraded before the public adds a layer of humiliation to their grief.

The law recognizes this pain. Privacy laws exist precisely to protect individuals from such intrusions, especially in cases involving sexual violence where the stigma can be devastating. Georgia's shield law was born out of a desire to prevent exactly what happened: the re-victimization of rape survivors through media exposure. The intent behind the statute was noble—to create a safe space for victims and their families, shielding them from the prying eyes of the press.

However, the Supreme Court's decision in Cox suggests that there are limits to how far such protections can go when they conflict with the transparency required by the Constitution. The Court did not deny the validity of Cohn's pain. Rather, it determined that the mechanism chosen to alleviate that pain—the suppression of truthful information from public records—was a tool too dangerous for the state to wield in a free society.

The dilemma is stark. On one side stands the individual right to privacy and the desire to protect victims from further trauma. On the other stands the collective right of the people to know what their government is doing, including how it prosecutes crimes. The Court chose the latter, asserting that in a democracy, the public's right to inspect the machinery of justice outweighs the state's interest in shielding specific details of court proceedings.

This decision had immediate and lasting implications. It effectively neutered similar shield laws across the country when it came to information found in public court records. News organizations could now report on the names of victims, defendants, and witnesses as long as those names appeared in documents available for public inspection. The "off-limits" zone shrank significantly.

The Legacy of a Name

The impact of Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn extends far beyond the specific facts of that 1972 broadcast. It established a precedent that continues to guide legal battles between the press and privacy advocates today. The principle that truthful information from public records is protected remains a cornerstone of First Amendment jurisprudence.

In the decades since, this ruling has been cited in numerous cases involving the release of sensitive information, from the names of juvenile offenders to the identities of victims in high-profile trials. It serves as a reminder that the freedom of the press is not absolute in every context—fraud and libel are still crimes—but when it comes to official government records, the presumption is overwhelmingly in favor of openness.

For Martin Cohn and his family, the legal victory for Cox Broadcasting was likely a bitter pill to swallow. They had sought justice through the courts, hoping the system would recognize their right to privacy. Instead, the highest court in the land told them that the public's interest in transparency superseded their private grief. It is a sobering reality of the American legal system: sometimes, the rights that protect the many must come at the expense of the few.

Yet, the decision also reflects a profound trust in the citizenry. The Court operated on the belief that people are capable of processing difficult information, that they can read about rape and murder without losing their humanity, and that understanding the full scope of judicial proceedings is essential for holding the government accountable. By refusing to let the state sanitize court records, the Court ensured that the public sees justice as it truly is: messy, painful, and often brutal.

The story of Thomas Wassell, Martin Cohn, and the seventeen-year-old victim from North Springs High School is a microcosm of the ongoing struggle in American democracy. It asks us to decide where we draw the line between compassion for the individual and the necessity of a transparent government. In 1975, the Supreme Court drew that line at the courthouse door. Information released there was free to go; information kept behind closed doors remained protected. But once a name is in an indictment, once it is written in the ink of the state's own accusation, it belongs to the public record.

This legal boundary does not erase the human cost. The victim remains a victim. The father remains a grieving parent. The broadcast remains a source of pain for them. But the law, in its relentless pursuit of democratic principles, decided that the price of privacy could not be the silence of the press. In a world where information is power, Cox v. Cohn ensured that the most powerful institution—the judiciary—could not hide behind a veil of secrecy when it comes to the details of its own work.

The case stands as a testament to the difficult choices inherent in a free society. It acknowledges the tragedy of violence while affirming the necessity of truth. It protects the right of a reporter to do their job, even when that job involves reporting on the worst moments of other people's lives. And it reminds us that in the calculus of constitutional rights, the openness of government is often deemed more valuable than the privacy of the individual.

The decision was rendered on March 3, 1975, but its echoes are still heard in courtrooms and newsrooms today. Every time a journalist accesses a public docket, every time a newspaper prints the name of a defendant or a victim from an official filing, they are operating under the shield provided by this ruling. The debate continues—privacy advocates argue that the law needs to evolve to better protect victims in the digital age—but the fundamental principle remains unshaken: truthful information from public records is free speech.

In the end, Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn was not just a legal victory for a television station. It was a statement about the nature of power and the role of the press in checking it. The Court recognized that while the state could punish those who commit crimes, it could not control how the story of those crimes is told once the records are open. The tragedy of the girl from Sandy Springs remains a heartbreaking reminder of the human cost, but the legal outcome serves as a guardrail for the freedom of the press, ensuring that the public's right to know is never sacrificed on the altar of privacy.
