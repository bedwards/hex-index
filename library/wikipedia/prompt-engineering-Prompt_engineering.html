Based on Wikipedia: Prompt engineering

In 2023, "prompt" was the runner-up for Oxford's Word of the Year—a small word that suddenly mattered enormously. The previous decade had seen artificial intelligence transform from an academic curiosity into a global phenomenon, and with it emerged an entirely new discipline: prompt engineering.

At its core, prompt engineering is deceptively simple. A prompt is simply natural language—an instruction given to an AI program that determines what that program produces. But within this simplicity lies extraordinary complexity. Research has consistently demonstrated that large language models are remarkably sensitive to subtle variations in how prompts are formatted, structured, and worded. In some studies, simply reordering examples within a prompt produced accuracy shifts of more than forty percent. Formatting changes could swing performance by seventy-six percentage points. The model responds not just to what you ask, but how you ask it.

During the 2020s AI boom, this sensitivity became a business priority. Companies across industries hired employees with the title "prompt engineer" to create instructions that would increase productivity and efficacy. These specialists crafted queries, commands, and longer statements that referenced context, instructions, and conversation history—but the individual title has since lost traction as AI models themselves began producing better prompts than humans, and corporate training in prompting became available for general employees.

The Building Blocks of Communication

A prompt for a text-to-text language model can be a query, a command, or a longer statement. The process involves designing clear queries, refining wording, providing relevant context, specifying output style, and assigning a character for the AI to mimic—all to guide the model toward accurate, useful, consistent responses.

When communicating with text-to-image or text-to-audio models, prompts become even more concrete. A typical image prompt might be "a high-quality photo of an astronaut riding a horse"; an audio prompt could request "Lo-fi slow BPM electro chill with organic samples". The discipline applies equally to these media as to text, achieving desired subjects, styles, layouts, lighting, and aesthetics.

Common prompting techniques include several named approaches. Multi-shot prompting provides multiple examples for the model to learn from in context—what researchers call few-shot learning. Chain-of-thought prompting allows large language models to solve problems through a series of intermediate steps before giving final answers, mimicking a train of thought. In 2022, Google Brain reported this technique improved reasoning ability by inducing multi-step problem-solving that mimics human reasoning processes.

Tree-of-thought prompting generalizes chain-of-thought by generating multiple lines of reasoning in parallel, with the ability to backtrack or explore other paths using search algorithms like breadth-first, depth-first, or beam. When applied to PaLM, a 540 billion parameter language model, chain-of-thought prompting significantly aided performance, allowing it to perform comparably with task-specific fine-tuned models and achieving state-of-the-art results on the GSM8K mathematical reasoning benchmark.

The Context Behind the Prompt

Context engineering is the related area of software engineering that focuses on managing non-prompt contexts supplied to the GenAI model—metadata, API tools, tokens. This process improves reliability, provenance, and token efficiency in production large language model systems. It emphasizes operational practices including token budgeting, provenance tags, versioning of context artifacts, observability through logging which context was supplied, and context regression tests to ensure changes to supplied context don't silently alter system behavior.

A model's ability to temporarily learn from prompts is known as in-context learning—an emergent property of model scale that differs from training and fine-tuning because it produces only temporary changes rather than lasting ones. Training models to perform in-context learning can be viewed as meta-learning, or "learning to learn".

The Dark Side: Prompt Injection

Not all prompt engineering serves benign purposes. Prompt injection is a type of cybersecurity attack that targets machine learning models through malicious prompts. These attacks manipulate the model to output harmful content or bypass safety guardrails, representing a significant security concern in deployed AI systems.

A Field Standardizing Its Language

A 2024 survey of the field identified over fifty distinct text-based prompting techniques, forty multimodal variants, and a vocabulary of thirty-three terms used across research—highlighting a present lack of standardized terminology. Several evaluative methods have been proposed to address model sensitivity and robustness. FormatSpread facilitates systematic analysis by evaluating a range of plausible prompt formats, offering comprehensive performance intervals. PromptEval estimates performance distributions across diverse prompts, enabling robust metrics under constrained budgets.

In 2025, "vibe coding"—an AI-assisted software development method where users prompt an LLM with descriptions and let it generate or edit code—was Collins Dictionary's Word of the Year. The term had barely existed when companies were hiring prompt engineers; now it's a defining feature of how humans work with machines.

The discipline has evolved rapidly: from a niche skill to an essential business capability, from human-crafted prompts to AI-generated ones, from text-only to multimodal, from informal practice to formalized techniques with measurable benchmarks. What remains constant is the fundamental truth discovered in that 2024 survey—the model doesn't just hear what you say, it hears exactly how you say it.
