In 1999, he graduated from the University of Lviv, an institution renowned for its resistance to Soviet homogenization and its commitment to European integration. But his education was not merely academic; it was a preparation for a specific kind of service. He returned to the region, taking a position at the Lviv City Council between 1999 and 2002. These were the early years of post-Soviet transition, a time when the structures of local government were often more theoretical than practical, and where the gap between policy and reality was vast.

It was during these formative years in Lviv that the contours of his professional philosophy began to take shape. He learned that governance was not about grand declarations but about the meticulous management of resources and personnel. From 2003 to 2006, he served as an assistant to a People's Deputy of Ukraine, a role that offered him a front-row seat to the often chaotic mechanics of the national parliament. He saw how legislation was crafted, how compromises were brokered, and how the interests of the state could sometimes be at odds with the needs of the citizenry. Yet, he did not retreat. Instead, he doubled down on the technical aspects of administration, pursuing further education at the Ternopil National Economic University, where he graduated in 2003. The pivotal moment in Nemchinov's career, and indeed a microcosm of the shifting alliances that would define Ukrainian politics in the 2020s, occurred between 2009 and 2010. He took a position within the Lviv Regional State Administration. It was here, in the bureaucratic corridors of western Ukraine, that he met Denys Shmyhal.
