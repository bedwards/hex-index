Three weeks before graduating from Phillips Exeter Academy in 1983, Stephen Metcalf was expelled. It was not a quiet departure, nor a mutual parting of ways; the school administration asked him to leave, "in no uncertain terms," just as he stood on the precipice of his final exams and commencement ceremonies. This early rupture—a moment where potential was met with dismissal rather than accommodation—would become a defining motif in the life of one of America's most incisive cultural critics. Metcalf did not vanish into obscurity after this institutional rejection; instead, he channeled that friction into a career defined by questioning authority, dissecting the machinery of culture, and finding profound meaning in the spaces where high art collides with mass entertainment. Today, as the critic-at-large for Slate magazine and the host of its flagship podcast, The Culture Gabfest, Metcalf stands as a figure whose voice cuts through the noise of the digital age with a clarity that feels almost archaic in its devotion to deep thought, yet is entirely modern in its application.

To understand Metcalf's trajectory is to understand a specific kind of American intellectual journey: one that moves from elite institutions that fail you, to the gritty reality of political campaigning, and finally to the rarefied air of cultural criticism where he has become a defining voice for millions. His story is not merely a resume of credentials; it is a narrative of resilience, curiosity, and an unyielding commitment to understanding how stories shape our world.

The Architecture of Disruption

The expulsion from Exeter serves as the prologue to a life spent challenging established narratives. Phillips Exeter is perhaps the most prestigious preparatory school in the United States, an institution steeped in tradition and designed to funnel the nation's elite into its Ivy League feeder systems. For a student to be pushed out three weeks before graduation suggests a fundamental clash between the rigid expectations of the institution and the unruly nature of the individual. While the specific details of Metcalf's transgression remain a private matter of his youth, the public record frames it as a moment of decisive intervention by the school's administration. It was a rejection that could have ended an academic career before it began.

Instead, Metcalf found his footing at Wesleyan University in Middletown, Connecticut. Wesleyan, known for its countercultural leanings and emphasis on interdisciplinary study, provided a stark contrast to the regimented atmosphere of Exeter. Here, the environment was more conducive to the kind of questioning mind that had likely caused friction at his previous school. At Wesleyan, Metcalf immersed himself in the liberal arts tradition, absorbing the rigorous intellectual discipline required to analyze complex social and cultural phenomena. He graduated with a solid foundation, but his education was far from over.

His academic ambitions led him next to the University of Virginia, where he earned a master's degree. This period of advanced study honed his analytical skills, teaching him how to construct arguments that were both nuanced and persuasive. However, it was at Yale University where the trajectory of his life took another unexpected turn. Metcalf entered the English graduate program with the intention of pursuing a Ph.D., a path typically reserved for those aiming for careers in academia. For years, he delved into literary theory, history, and criticism, engaging with the great minds of the past while wrestling with the questions of his present.

Yet, just as many scholars find themselves trapped in the "publish or perish" cycle, Metcalf made a pivot that would define his future. He left Yale before completing his doctorate. This decision to abandon the traditional academic ladder in favor of the real world was not an act of failure, but of strategic redirection. He realized that the most vital cultural conversations were not happening solely within university seminar rooms, but in the public square, on the front pages of newspapers, and in the living rooms of everyday Americans.

From Campaign Trail to Newsroom

The move from the ivory tower to New York City marked a dramatic shift in Metcalf's professional landscape. The late 1990s were a time of intense political activity, with Hillary Clinton mounting her campaign for the United States Senate in New York. This was not merely an election; it was a cultural moment that signaled a new era in American politics, where media strategy and personal narrative played roles as significant as policy positions. Metcalf joined the campaign team as a speechwriter, a role that required him to distill complex political ideas into compelling rhetoric that could resonate with voters.

Working on the Clinton campaign provided Metcalf with an inside look at the mechanics of power and persuasion. He saw firsthand how language was weaponized, how narratives were constructed and deconstructed, and how the media ecosystem functioned as a battleground for public opinion. This experience was invaluable. It stripped away any remaining pretensions of detached academic observation and forced him to engage with the messy, high-stakes reality of political life. He learned that words matter, not just for their intellectual elegance, but for their ability to move people, to change minds, and to shape history.

Following his time in politics, Metcalf transitioned into freelance writing, a precarious but liberating path that allowed him to explore diverse topics and develop a unique voice. He wrote for various publications, honing his craft and building a reputation as a writer who could tackle subjects with both depth and accessibility. His work began to appear in major outlets, signaling his arrival on the national stage. But it was his move to Slate magazine that would cement his place in the cultural landscape.

The Voice of Slate

In 2001, Metcalf joined Slate, the pioneering online magazine founded by Michael Kinsley and backed by Microsoft. At a time when digital media was still finding its footing, Slate represented the future of journalism: fast, opinionated, and deeply engaged with culture. Metcalf was hired as a staff writer, but his role quickly expanded beyond simple reporting. He became Slate's "critic-at-large," a title that granted him the freedom to roam across the cultural spectrum, reviewing everything from blockbuster films to obscure indie novels, from political speeches to museum exhibitions.

The beauty of Metcalf's approach lies in his refusal to be siloed. Unlike many critics who specialize in a single genre or medium, Metcalf brings a holistic perspective to his work. He understands that culture is an interconnected web, where the themes explored in a novel might echo in a political speech, and where the visual language of a film can influence advertising campaigns. His writing is characterized by its intellectual rigor, its wit, and its ability to find universal truths in specific cultural artifacts.

One of his most enduring contributions to Slate is the "Dilettante" column. In this recurring feature, Metcalf takes on the role of the curious amateur, tackling subjects with a mix of enthusiasm and critical distance. The term "dilettante" often carries a negative connotation, implying a superficial dabbling in the arts. Metcalf reclaims it, arguing that there is profound value in approaching culture with an open mind, free from the constraints of professional expertise. He asks the questions that a smart, curious person would ask: Why does this movie work? What does this book say about our current moment? How do we make sense of this trend?

The column has become a beloved institution on Slate, attracting readers who appreciate Metcalf's ability to demystify complex ideas without dumbing them down. He writes with the authority of someone who has done his homework, yet maintains the accessibility of someone who is genuinely excited about what he is discovering. This balance between expertise and curiosity is rare in modern criticism, where critics often feel compelled to signal their credentials or adopt an air of superior detachment.

The Culture Gabfest: A Podcast Revolution

Perhaps Metcalf's most significant contribution to contemporary cultural discourse is his role as the host of The Culture Gabfest. Launched by Slate in 2008, the podcast quickly became one of the most popular and influential shows in the medium. In an era before podcasts were a dominant force, The Gabfest proved that there was a hunger for long-form, intelligent conversations about culture. The show features Metcalf alongside two other critics: Julia Turner (later succeeded by Dana Stevens) and John Swanton. Together, they form a trio of voices that complement each other perfectly, bringing different perspectives and areas of expertise to the table.

The format is simple yet effective: the hosts discuss the latest movies, books, television shows, and cultural events, engaging in debates that are both entertaining and insightful. Unlike many talk shows that rely on sensationalism or soundbites, The Gabfest encourages deep dives into complex topics. The hosts challenge each other's opinions, explore nuances, and often arrive at conclusions that are surprising and thought-provoking. Metcalf's role as the host is crucial; he steers the conversation with a light touch, ensuring that the discussion remains focused while allowing room for spontaneity and humor.

The success of The Gabfest lies in its ability to make culture feel accessible without losing its intellectual weight. The show has cultivated a dedicated listener base, many of whom tune in weekly not just for the recommendations, but for the sense of community that comes from listening to smart people talk about the things they love. Metcalf's leadership as a host has been instrumental in establishing this dynamic. He is a skilled moderator who knows when to interject, when to let silence speak, and how to draw out the best arguments from his co-hosts.

The podcast has also become a platform for Metcalf to explore broader themes in culture and society. He uses the show to discuss issues of race, gender, politics, and technology, weaving these topics into conversations about art and entertainment. This integration of cultural criticism with social commentary is a hallmark of Metcalf's work, reflecting his belief that culture cannot be separated from the world in which it exists.

A Life in Words

Metcalf's influence extends far beyond Slate and its podcast. His writing has appeared in some of the most prestigious publications in the United States, including The New York Times, the New York Observer, New York magazine, The Atlantic, and The New Yorker. In each of these venues, he brings his unique perspective to bear on a wide range of topics. Whether he is reviewing a new film, analyzing a political trend, or reflecting on a cultural phenomenon, Metcalf's writing is characterized by its clarity, depth, and insight.

His work in The New Yorker, for instance, often delves into the complexities of contemporary society, exploring how cultural artifacts reflect and shape our understanding of the world. In The Atlantic, he tackles broader issues of culture and politics, offering thoughtful analysis that challenges conventional wisdom. His contributions to The New York Times showcase his ability to write for a mass audience while maintaining intellectual rigor.

In 2019, Metcalf began working on a book about the 1980s, a decade that has become increasingly relevant in contemporary discourse. The 1980s were a time of profound cultural and political transformation, marked by the rise of conservatism, the explosion of consumer culture, and significant shifts in media and technology. Metcalf's exploration of this era promises to offer a fresh perspective on how the past informs our present. His research for the book draws on his extensive knowledge of the period, as well as his unique ability to connect historical events to current cultural trends.

Personal Life and Legacy

Despite his public profile and professional achievements, Metcalf maintains a relatively private personal life. He currently resides in Upstate New York with his wife, novelist Koethi Zan, and their two daughters, Stella and Kate. The choice to live outside of the intense media hub of New York City reflects a desire for a quieter, more grounded existence. It also provides him with the space and time necessary to think deeply about the issues he writes about.

Koethi Zan is an accomplished novelist in her own right, known for her compelling storytelling and keen observations of human nature. Their partnership represents a meeting of minds, two writers who support each other's creative endeavors while maintaining their distinct voices. The presence of their daughters adds another dimension to Metcalf's life, grounding him in the everyday realities of family life. It is likely that this personal context informs his writing, adding layers of empathy and understanding to his cultural criticism.

Metcalf's legacy as a critic and writer is still being written. His work has influenced a generation of critics and writers who value intellectual rigor, accessibility, and a deep engagement with culture. He has demonstrated that it is possible to be both smart and popular, to challenge authority while remaining accessible to a wide audience. His ability to navigate the complex landscape of modern media, from print to digital to podcasting, speaks to his adaptability and vision.

As he continues to write, speak, and host, Metcalf remains a vital voice in American culture. He challenges us to think more deeply about the stories we tell ourselves, to question the narratives that shape our lives, and to find meaning in the cultural artifacts that surround us. His journey from a student expelled from an elite school to one of the nation's most respected critics is a testament to the power of resilience, curiosity, and the enduring value of good writing.

In a world often dominated by noise and superficiality, Stephen Metcalf offers a beacon of clarity. He reminds us that culture matters, that ideas have consequences, and that there is always room for thoughtful conversation. Whether he is dissecting a new film on The Culture Gabfest, writing a column for Slate, or working on his book about the 1980s, Metcalf continues to engage with the world in ways that are both intellectually stimulating and emotionally resonant. His work serves as a reminder of the power of words to connect us, to challenge us, and to help us make sense of the complex world we inhabit.

The story of Stephen Metcalf is not just about a career; it is about a philosophy of engagement. It is about the belief that no matter where you start, or what obstacles you face, there is always a path forward if you are willing to ask the right questions and listen to the answers. His expulsion from Exeter was not an end, but a beginning—a catalyst for a life dedicated to exploring the depths of human experience through the lens of culture. And in that exploration, he has found a voice that resonates with millions, proving that the most powerful stories are those that speak to the truth of our shared humanity.
