In 1870, inside the vast, echoing hall of St. Peter's Basilica, a single voice rose against the tide of history. Cardinal Filippo Maria Guidi of Bologna stood before his peers and dared to suggest that when the Pope speaks with final authority, he does not do so in isolation. He argued, with a tremor of dissent that would echo for a century, that the Bishop of Rome teaches only in consultation with the other bishops of the Church. His warning was specific: to separate the Pope from his brothers is to fracture the very nature of the apostolic body. Yet, the council voted overwhelmingly against him. Months later, the dogma of papal infallibility was declared, cementing a vision of the Catholic Church as a monarchy with an absolute ruler at its head. This moment did not merely settle a theological debate; it drew a line in the sand that would define the next century and a half of conflict between centralization and collaboration.

To understand the stakes of this history, one must first strip away the modern conception of the Church as a monolithic empire with lines of command running straight from Rome to every parish on earth. In the earliest days of Christianity, the structure was far more organic, almost tribal in its autonomy. The concept of "collegiality"—the Pope governing in collaboration with bishops while respecting their local authority—was not an innovation but a restoration. In the Early Church, popes often wielded moral authority rather than administrative power. They were rarely called upon to intervene in daily governance. Regional churches elected their own bishops, resolving disputes through local synods where the voices of the community held weight. The Bishop of Rome was consulted only when a crisis could not be resolved locally or when a matter of universal faith demanded a final word. It was a system built on trust and distance, where the autonomy of the local church was as sacred as the unity of the whole.

The machinery of this decentralized world began to grind and shift during the eleventh and twelfth centuries. This era saw the papacy amass power with a ferocity that would have been unrecognizable to the early apostles. Monastic reformers, desperate to purge the Church of simony and corruption among local bishops, looked to Rome as a shield. They saw in the papacy an ally powerful enough to check the excesses of wayward clergy. Simultaneously, bishops themselves began to court papal authority, seeking it as a bulwark against the encroaching interference of secular rulers who wanted to install their own favorites as bishops. Thus, a centralization of power took root, not merely by imperial decree, but through a complex web of mutual need and political survival. By the time the thirteenth century dawned, the Pope was no longer just a referee among equals; he was becoming the supreme executive authority.

But history rarely moves in a straight line, and resistance to this consolidation began to grow almost as soon as it took hold. As early as the fourteenth century, voices of dissent rose against the growing absolutism of Rome. At the Council of Vienne, Bishop Guillaume Durand proposed a radical return to balance: he argued that local hierarchies and regional synods must be strengthened to prevent the central machinery from strangling the life of the Church. This tension exploded into a full-blown crisis during the Great Schism of the Western Church (1378–1417). For nearly forty years, the Church was fractured by rival claimants to the papal throne, supported by different factions of Europe. The authority of the individual Pope had collapsed under the weight of political maneuvering.

It was in this crucible that the conciliar movement was born. A group of cardinals, allied with secular rulers who were tired of the chaos, called a council to resolve the schism. They did something unprecedented: they claimed that a general council possessed authority superior even to the Pope himself. The Councils of Pisa and Constance deposed various claimants and elected Pope Martin V in 1417, effectively ending the schism by asserting that the collective body of bishops could judge and depose the Vicar of Christ. The Council of Constance issued a stunning declaration: all Christians, including the Pope, were bound to obey councils "in matters pertaining to faith, the ending of the schism, and the reform of the church." This was the high-water mark of decentralization, a moment where the Church looked like a true republic of bishops.

Yet, like all revolutions, this one burned out. The conciliar movement ran out of steam as the papacy reasserted its dominance, refusing to accept that it could be judged by a council. The claim of superiority was short-lived, and the centralization of power resumed with renewed vigor. By the time the nineteenth century arrived, a period some historians call the "long nineteenth century," the Church had swung fully back toward monarchy. The consolidation of papal authority became a defining feature of modern Catholicism. In 1870, the First Vatican Council decreed the infallibility of the Pope's teachings when speaking ex cathedra on matters of faith and morals. Cardinal Guidi's plea for consultation was ignored. The Pope was now not just the head of state but the divine oracle of the Church.

The final nail in the coffin of traditional autonomy came in 1917 with the publication of a new Code of Canon Law. This document stripped away the ancient tradition of the free election of bishops by local clergy and people, granting the Pope universal power to appoint every bishop in the world. Coupled with modern communications technology and the system of papal nuncios—ambassadors who could override local decisions—the result was profound. The power of bishops evaporated, reduced to mere administrators carrying out the will of Rome. Critics began to describe the popes as the "last absolute monarchs" of Europe, ruling a global empire from a desk in the Vatican.

It was against this backdrop of rigid centralization that the Second Vatican Council convened in 1962. The reformers who gathered there did not seek to invent something new; they sought to return to an old truth. They looked back at the Early Church and saw a model where bishops acted "with and under the Peter" (cum et sub Petro). This was the crucial distinction that separated them from the conciliarists of the fifteenth century. Unlike their predecessors who argued a council was superior to the Pope, the reformers at Vatican II proposed a collegiality that operated within the structure of papal authority. They saw this not as undermining Tradition, but as recovering the original practice of Peter and the Apostolic College.

The debate at the Council was fierce. A traditionalist minority opposed the very idea of collegiality, fearing it would transform the Church from a divine monarchy into a human "episcopalian" or democratic experiment. They argued that sharing power was tantamount to betraying the authority Christ gave to Peter. But the momentum for reform was unstoppable. In 1964, the Council issued Lumen gentium, the Dogmatic Constitution on the Church. This document set forth the principle of the "College of Bishops," stating that this body succeeds and gives continuing existence to the Apostolic College. It declared that the Pope does not stand above the bishops but as their head, while they together form a single body.

The implementation of these principles required new structures. In 1965, Pope Paul VI issued Apostolica Sollicitudo, establishing the Synod of Bishops to advise the Pope regularly. The Council's decree Christus Dominus further urged the formation of national and regional conferences of bishops, giving them a platform to govern their own regions. For decades after Vatican II, a quiet but persistent debate simmered beneath the surface: How much power should these episcopal conferences actually have? Centralizers in the Roman Curia played down their importance, viewing them as advisory bodies with no real teeth. Decentralizationists argued they were essential for adapting the universal faith to local cultures.

The tension reached a boiling point in 1998 when Pope John Paul II issued Apostolos suos, a motu proprio on the theological and juridical nature of episcopal conferences. This document has been described as "probably the most important post-Conciliar papal document on episcopal collegiality." It drew a sharp line: declarations by these conferences only constituted authentic magisterium if approved unanimously. If passed by a majority, they required the specific approval (recognitio) of the Apostolic See to be binding. Furthermore, John Paul II warned that this recognition would not be granted if the majority was not "substantial." The message was clear: collegiality had its limits, and Rome retained the final veto.

Decades later, a new pontiff entered the picture with a radically different approach. Pope Francis, who himself had twice been elected president of the Argentine Bishops' Conference, brought the experience of a working bishop to the throne of Peter. Throughout his papacy, he has advocated for increasing the role of collegiality and synodality in the development of Church teachings. He put this concern into practice by urging the Synod of Bishops to speak with parrhesia—boldly and without fear. In earlier synods, officials of the Roman Curia had often ruled out discussion of contentious issues or steered conversations toward safe, pre-approved conclusions. Francis broke that pattern, demanding open and honest dialogue.

His encyclical on the environment, Laudato si' (2015), stands as a testament to this new spirit. It was unprecedented in its reliance on the teaching documents of fifteen national bishops' conferences and two larger regional conferences from Latin America and Asia. Instead of dictating the Church's stance from Rome, Francis wove together the voices of local churches facing the immediate crisis of climate change. The Council of Cardinals examined these themes of synodality and "healthy decentralisation" during their meeting in February 2016, signaling a shift in how the Curia approached governance.

This shift was codified into law in September 2017 with the motu proprio Magnum principium. Francis amended the 1983 Code of Canon Law to increase the responsibility of national conferences of bishops for liturgical translations. Previously, these translations required a rigorous and often stifling review process by Rome, which frequently resulted in English (and other languages) sounding stiff or foreign. By granting local bishops more authority over their own liturgies, Francis described this as "one of Pope Francis's strongest moves yet in terms of fostering greater collegiality in the Catholic Church." It was a recognition that the faith must be spoken in the tongue and idiom of the people it serves.

The evolution continued in September 2018 with Episcopalis communio, an apostolic constitution that introduced a more direct process for synodal documents. Under this new system, a final document of the Synod of Bishops could become part of the Church's magisterium simply by receiving papal approval, bypassing some of the bureaucratic hurdles that had previously diluted its impact. The constitution also provided a direct channel for the laity to send their contributions straight to the synod's secretary general, acknowledging that the "people of God" are essential participants in the church's discernment process.

The journey from the Early Church to the present day reveals a constant tension between two impulses: the desire for unity under a single head and the need for local autonomy and diversity. The history of collegiality is not just a dry recitation of councils and canons; it is a story of human beings struggling to balance authority with love, order with freedom, and tradition with renewal. From the isolation of the early bishops to the absolutism of the nineteenth century, from the chaos of the Great Schism to the hopeful reforms of Vatican II, and into the synodal experiment of Pope Francis, the Church continues to ask itself a fundamental question: How can we be one body without crushing the individual members that make it up?

The answer remains elusive. The Roman Curia still holds immense power, and the centralizers remain vigilant against any drift toward what they see as fragmentation. Yet, the voices of the local churches have never been louder. The debate over collegiality is not merely academic; it determines how billions of Catholics experience their faith on a daily basis. It decides whether a bishop in Brazil hears the voice of Rome before he hears the cry of his poor neighbors, or if he can act as a true shepherd of his flock with the backing of the whole Church.

In the end, the story of collegiality is a testament to the resilience of the Catholic tradition. It shows that even when the ship of the Church seems steered toward a single helm, there are always those pulling at the oars, reminding the captain that the sea is vast and requires many hands to navigate. The struggle for balance continues, driven by the belief that Peter's successors were never meant to rule alone, but to lead in concert with the college of bishops, preserving the unity of the faith while honoring the diversity of its people.

"The Church does not live on a single voice, but on the harmony of many voices singing the same song."

This is the promise that collegiality holds: a vision where authority is not a weapon of control, but a service to unity. It is a vision that has been fought for, lost, and regained throughout history. As the Church moves forward in the twenty-first century, facing new challenges from climate change to social justice, the question of how to govern together remains as urgent as ever. The path forward will likely be neither a pure monarchy nor a radical democracy, but something more complex—a fragile, difficult, yet necessary collaboration that honors both the head and the body.

The history is written in ink and blood, in decrees and dissents. It is a reminder that while structures change, the human need for connection and shared purpose remains constant. The Church of 2026 stands on the shoulders of these struggles, looking back at the long arc from the apostolic college to the modern synod, asking once again what it means to be truly one.

The weight of history presses down on every decision made in the Vatican today. Every appointment, every translation, every encyclical is a reflection of where the Church stands on this spectrum between centralization and collegiality. It is a dance that has been performed for two thousand years, and it shows no sign of stopping. The tension itself is perhaps the most faithful expression of the Christian mystery: the unity of the many in the one, never fully resolved, always being sought.

And so, the story continues. Not with the finality of a dogma, but with the open-endedness of a journey. From the synods of the first century to the digital consultations of the twenty-first, the Church searches for the right way to be together. It is a search that defines its very identity, proving that even in an age of absolute power, the voice of the college still matters.

The lesson for the modern reader, having just finished a reflection on cardinals feeling like a "college again," is clear: this feeling is not new. It is the echo of a rhythm that has always existed, sometimes muted, sometimes loud, but never entirely silenced. The struggle for collegiality is the story of the Church trying to be itself—a body, with many parts, moving as one.
