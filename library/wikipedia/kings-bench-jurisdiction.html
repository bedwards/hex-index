On a crisp morning in October 2020, thousands of mail-in ballots in Philadelphia, Montgomery, and Bucks counties faced an uncertain fate. County officials, adhering to strict deadlines, had moved to discard hundreds of envelopes bearing minor date errors or missing postmarks. Under standard appellate procedure, these votes would have been locked away, their owners denied the chance to speak. But in Pennsylvania, a mechanism older than the United States itself sat dormant until that moment, waiting for a crisis that demanded more than routine procedure. The state's Supreme Court did not wait for an appeal; it did not wait for lower courts to finish their work. Instead, it invoked King's Bench jurisdiction, seizing control of the election process mid-stream to declare that subjective rejections based on signature mismatches or minor dating errors were unconstitutional. In doing so, the court pulled the lever of a power that allows it to bypass every layer of the judiciary below it, acting with a speed and scope that feels less like a court case and more like a constitutional emergency brake.

This is not merely a procedural technicality reserved for dusty law books; it is a living, breathing authority that has shaped the moral architecture of Pennsylvania's justice system for three centuries. Known as King's Bench power, this extraordinary jurisdiction grants the Commonwealth's highest court the ability to vacate judgments of inferior courts whenever the public well-being demands it or when the exigencies of justice require immediate action. It is a sword and a shield, used to correct systemic corruption, halt executive overreach, and restore due process where it has been shattered. While the term may sound archaic—a relic of English monarchs sitting in judgment—the reality of its application in modern Pennsylvania is starkly contemporary. From the "kids for cash" scandal that saw juveniles sold into private detention facilities to the constitutional crisis of a pandemic, King's Bench powers have proven to be the ultimate check on judicial and political failure.

To understand why this power exists and how it functions, one must first strip away the modern veneer of American federalism and look back at the soil from which Pennsylvania grew. The concept originates in England, specifically the Court of King's Bench, a medieval tribunal that held the unique authority to intervene in any matter within the realm where justice was being denied or delayed. When William Penn received his royal charter in 1681, he brought with him not just a vision for a holy experiment, but the common law traditions of the English colonies. For decades, these traditions simmered beneath the surface, waiting for the colony to mature enough to claim them as its own.

The pivotal moment arrived in 1722. The General Assembly of Pennsylvania, recognizing that a fledgling court system needed a supreme arbiter with teeth, explicitly bestowed upon the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania the powers of the English King's Bench. This was not a subtle grant; it was a charter of comprehensive authority. The statute declared that the court should exercise its functions "as fully and amply... as the justices of the Court of King's Bench, Common Pleas and Exchequer, at Westminster" could do on May 22, 1722. This specific date became the anchor for a power that would outlive the monarchy itself. The legislature recognized that there are moments when the normal machinery of justice grinds too slowly or becomes clogged by corruption, and in those moments, the highest court must be able to step in, assume plenary jurisdiction, and "cause right and justice to be done."

Today, this ancient authority is codified in Pennsylvania statutes, specifically Title 42, Section 502. The language remains strikingly direct: "Notwithstanding any other provision of law, the Supreme Court may... assume plenary jurisdiction of such matter at any stage thereof." The phrase "notwithstanding any other provision of law" is a legal sledgehammer. It means that no other statute, no lower court rule, and no procedural hurdle can block the Supreme Court when it decides to act. Whether the court acts on its own motion or upon the petition of a party, the scope is limitless. It allows the justices to intervene in cases pending before any court, at any stage of litigation, provided there is an issue of immediate public importance.

The sheer breadth of this power was tested and redefined in the 21st century, most notably in the case of In re Bruno (2014). For years, a debate simmered among legal scholars and judges: Was King's Bench jurisdiction merely a statutory gift from the legislature that could be taken away or altered by a subsequent act? Or was it something deeper, woven into the very fabric of the state constitution? In In re Bruno, the Supreme Court answered with finality. Justice Seamus McCaffery, writing for the majority, declared that King's Bench powers are constitutionally-inherent. They do not depend on the whims of the General Assembly. The court stated that this jurisdiction is "by nature comprehensive and inherently vested in the highest authority of the judicial branch," and it can only be divested by the people themselves, either expressly or by necessary implication.

This distinction is vital. It means the legislature cannot pass a law to strip the Supreme Court of its ability to act when justice is at stake. The General Assembly has no constitutional authority to diminish, curtail, or interfere with these functions. This separation ensures that when the lower courts fail—when they are compromised by bias, corruption, or paralysis—the highest court retains an independent pathway to restore order. It is a safeguard built on the premise that the judiciary must possess an internal mechanism for self-correction that is immune from political erosion.

Yet, history shows us that this power was not always invoked with perfect clarity. The early 1990s brought a period of profound turmoil that tested the integrity of Pennsylvania's judicial branch and, by extension, the necessity of King's Bench oversight. At the center of this storm stood Justice Rolf Larsen. His tenure on the bench was marred by behavior that eroded public trust, culminating in his criminal conviction for perjury and obstruction of justice during an impeachment inquiry. The House impeached him, and the Senate subsequently convicted and removed him from office.

The Larsen affair was not just a scandal; it was a crisis of confidence. It illustrated a system where the mechanisms for holding judges accountable were slow, opaque, and often ineffective. The public image of the Pennsylvania Supreme Court was battered, revealing a need for reform that went beyond the mere exercise of jurisdiction to the structure of judicial discipline itself. Pennsylvanians for Modern Courts, an advocacy group, recognized that the turmoil caused by Larsen demanded a systemic change. They argued that the existing system allowed judges to shield one another or drag out disciplinary proceedings until the heat died down.

The result was a constitutional amendment passed by voters in 1993, a direct response to the failure of the old guard. This amendment created two new entities: the Judicial Conduct Board and the Court of Judicial Discipline. The former became an independent body tasked with investigating complaints of judicial misconduct, while the latter was empowered to make the final determination on guilt or innocence. For the first time, there was a dedicated, transparent process for policing the bench.

However, even with these reforms, the Supreme Court retained ultimate power over the Commonwealth's judicial system. The King's Bench authority remained the ceiling of that structure, ready to intervene if the new disciplinary bodies themselves faltered or if the courts failed in their duties to the public. The Larsen saga served as a catalyst, but it also left a lingering question: Would the court have the courage to use its extraordinary powers when the corruption was not just about one judge's behavior, but about a systemic rot involving money and human lives?

That question found its answer more than a decade later in the dark heart of Luzerne County. In 2008, a scandal erupted that would come to be known as "kids for cash," exposing a level of judicial corruption that shocked the nation. Two judges, Mark Ciavarella and Michael Conahan, had entered into a secret agreement with two private, for-profit juvenile detention centers. The arrangement was simple but grotesque: the facility owners paid the judges millions of dollars in kickbacks. In exchange, the judges imposed harsh sentences on juvenile offenders for minor infractions—truancy, disorderly conduct, even shoplifting—funneling children into these detention facilities to generate profit.

The human cost of this greed was staggering. Thousands of children were stripped of their liberty, denied legal counsel, and imprisoned without due process. Many were held in facilities that offered little more than warehousing, with no educational or rehabilitative services. The sentences were often disproportionate to the crimes, driven not by justice but by the need to fill beds for the paying owners. The scandal revealed a complete abandonment of judicial duty, where the gavel was used as a tool of commerce rather than a symbol of law.

When the truth finally came out, the lower courts were paralyzed by the sheer scale of the corruption. How does one appeal from a system where the judges themselves are criminals? The normal appellate process would have taken years, leaving thousands of convictions in limbo and potentially allowing more injustices to occur in the meantime. It was here that the Pennsylvania Supreme Court stepped into the void. Invoking its King's Bench jurisdiction, the court bypassed the entire lower judiciary. It did not wait for individual appeals; it assumed plenary jurisdiction over the cases themselves.

The result was swift and sweeping. The Supreme Court vacated thousands of juvenile convictions imposed by Ciavarella. This was not a minor adjustment; it was a total erasure of wrongful judgments, restoring due process to children who had been robbed of their childhoods. The court's intervention demonstrated that when the machinery of justice is broken beyond repair by those entrusted with its operation, King's Bench power serves as the necessary reset button. It allowed the state to say, unequivocally, that no contract, no bribe, and no judicial corruption could stand against the constitutional rights of its citizens. The "kids for cash" scandal remains the most potent modern example of why this 300-year-old power is not just a legal curiosity, but a vital instrument of human justice.

The utility of King's Bench jurisdiction extends far beyond criminal corruption; it also serves as a critical check on the balance of powers during times of political and social crisis. In 2020, Pennsylvania found itself navigating the unprecedented chaos of a global pandemic. As Governor Tom Wolf issued executive orders to close businesses and limit gatherings to slow the spread of the virus, the legal landscape became a battleground of competing interests. The Supreme Court was called upon to exercise its King's Bench powers three times in a single year, a frequency that highlighted both the volatility of the era and the adaptability of this ancient authority.

In April 2020, the court addressed Friends of Danny DeVito v. Wolf. This case concerned the Governor's order requiring businesses to close, including a political campaign office that had been shuttered. The issue was not just about public health but about the scope of executive power during an emergency. By invoking King's Bench, the Supreme Court could immediately review the constitutionality of these orders without waiting for the lower courts to work through the backlog of pandemic-related litigation. They provided clarity on whether the Governor had overstepped his bounds or acted within his necessary authority.

By June 2020, the conflict shifted to a separation of powers struggle between the Legislature and the Executive Branch. In Scarnati v. Wolf, the question was whether Title 35 of the Pennsylvania Code allowed the General Assembly to force an end to the Governor's emergency decrees. The court used its King's Bench authority again to resolve this constitutional standoff, ensuring that the state's governance structure remained functional even as the branches clashed over the pandemic response.

The third invocation in October 2020 proved perhaps the most consequential for the immediate future of democracy. As the presidential election approached, tensions ran high regarding mail-in ballots. In Philadelphia and surrounding counties, officials moved to discard ballots that had minor errors—misdated envelopes or signature mismatches perceived as suspicious. The lower courts were overwhelmed, and the threat was clear: hundreds of thousands of votes could be thrown out based on subjective judgments by local election boards.

The Supreme Court intervened with its King's Bench power to rule that such subjective rejections were unconstitutional. They declared that ballots could not be discarded due to perceived signature mismatches or minor dating errors without a rigorous, due process review. This decision was a direct application of the "expediency with which action must be taken" clause of the jurisdiction. Waiting for normal appeals would have meant discarding votes before they were counted, altering the outcome of an election in the most populous state in the union. The court's swift action protected the integrity of the vote and ensured that the will of the people was not suppressed by bureaucratic rigidity or political maneuvering.

The power of King's Bench is not without its critics, who argue that it allows the Supreme Court to act as a super-legislature, bypassing the democratic process and the checks provided by lower courts. There is a legitimate concern that such broad authority could be used for political ends rather than pure legal necessity. However, the history of Pennsylvania demonstrates that these powers are rarely invoked lightly. The threshold is high: there must be an issue of immediate public importance, or a situation where normal procedures would result in a failure of justice.

The evolution from the 1722 charter to the modern era shows a consistent theme: the need for a final arbiter that cannot be paralyzed by the failures of those below it. Whether it was the corruption of the "kids for cash" judges, the disciplinary failures highlighted by Justice Larsen, or the chaotic intersection of public health and civil liberties during the pandemic, King's Bench power has served as the state's immune system. It identifies the infection—be it a corrupt judge, a broken procedure, or an unconstitutional order—and acts with speed to excise it before it spreads.

In the end, this jurisdiction is about more than legal technicalities; it is about the fundamental promise of justice. When the ordinary courts fail, when the system clogs, and when the rights of the vulnerable are threatened by the powerful, Pennsylvania's Supreme Court possesses a unique tool to restore order. It is a power that traces its lineage back to English common law but has been forged in the fires of American statehood into something distinctly necessary. The ability to assume plenary jurisdiction at any stage of a matter ensures that no injustice can hide behind procedural delays or bureaucratic inertia.

The story of King's Bench jurisdiction is also the story of Pennsylvania's resilience. From the colonial charters to the modern scandals, the state has repeatedly found itself on the brink of judicial failure only to pull back through the exercise of this extraordinary authority. It serves as a reminder that in a democracy, the law must be able to adapt to its own failures. The judges who sit on the Supreme Court hold a burden that goes beyond interpreting statutes; they are the custodians of a power that can, when absolutely necessary, rewrite the timeline of justice itself.

As we look at the landscape of American law today, with its increasing polarization and procedural gridlock, the Pennsylvania model offers a compelling example of how a judiciary can remain responsive to human needs. The "kids for cash" victims did not get their freedom through a slow appellate process; they got it because one court decided that justice could not wait. The voters in Philadelphia who feared their ballots would be discarded found a defender in a bench that refused to let the machinery of democracy rust.

The legacy of May 22, 1722, is alive and well. It lives in every instance where the Supreme Court steps forward to say that the system has failed and that it will now fix it. In an era where trust in institutions is fragile, the King's Bench power stands as a testament to the idea that justice must be both blind and bold—capable of seeing the truth regardless of the law's limitations and brave enough to act when those limitations threaten to crush the rights of the people. It is a rare power, invoked rarely, but when it is used, the impact is absolute. The court does not just interpret the law; in moments of crisis, it becomes the law, ensuring that right and justice are done, no matter what stands in the way.

The story continues to unfold. As new challenges arise—whether they involve digital privacy, environmental crises, or emerging civil rights issues—the question will always remain: Will the court recognize the need for extraordinary intervention? The precedent is set. The power is inherent. And as long as the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania exists, so too does the mechanism to ensure that when justice is on the line, it will not be left waiting in a lower court's docket. The King's Bench is not just a historical footnote; it is the ultimate guardian of the Commonwealth's conscience, ready to act whenever the people demand it and the law requires it.
