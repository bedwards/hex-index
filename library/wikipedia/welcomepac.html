In late October 2022, a six-figure advertising campaign swept across Ohio, a state that had become the epicenter of a high-stakes political battle. The message was unconventional for a Democratic super PAC: it urged moderate Republicans and even Trump-aligned voters to reject their own party's candidate, JD Vance, in favor of Democrat Tim Ryan. While the national Democratic establishment largely turned its back on Ryan, viewing him as an unwinnable long shot against a popular Republican senator, one organization threw everything into the ring. WelcomePAC became the only national Democratic group to invest significantly in Ryan's campaign, deploying digital ads, radio spots, and print media designed to fracture the GOP coalition. The strategy was bold, almost insurgent in its nature, aiming not just to win an election but to fundamentally reshape who gets to speak for the center of American politics.

The Architects of the Center

To understand WelcomePAC, one must first understand the vacuum it sought to fill. In the decades following the 2016 election, the Democratic Party found itself increasingly polarized. On one flank stood the progressive wing, energized by movements like Justice Democrats, pushing for a "Green New Deal" and Medicare for All. On the other sat the establishment, often cautious and risk-averse. Somewhere in the middle, the traditional "Blue Dog" coalition of conservative-leaning Democrats began to wither away, leaving a gap in representation that seemed impossible to bridge.

WelcomePAC launched during the 2022 midterm election cycle with a singular, ambitious thesis: there was still a massive, untapped market for center-left politics in swing districts. The group's founders articulated a goal that sounded almost paradoxical in an era of hyper-partisanship. They wanted to become "the Justice Democrats of the political center." It was a declaration of war on the status quo, not by trying to move the party left, but by aggressively recruiting and supporting candidates who could welcome independents and moderate Republicans into the Democratic fold.

The New York Times described the outfit as a "Democratic-aligned entity that applies insurgent tactics to support center-left candidates in swing districts." This description captures the essence of their methodology. They were not waiting for voters to come to them; they were going out to find the voters who had split tickets in previous elections—those who might vote Republican for governor but Democratic for senator—and persuading them that a third option existed.

The Billionaire Backing

No political movement of this magnitude operates on volunteer energy alone. WelcomePAC was built on a foundation of significant capital, drawing from some of the most prominent names in American business and philanthropy. According to the magazine New York, the organization's war chest was fueled by billionaires Reid Hoffman, Michael Bloomberg, James Murdoch and his wife Kathryn, Rory Gates, the Walton family, and Joe Manchin and Heather Manchin's centrist Americans Together organization.

This list of donors reveals a specific strategic intent. These are not typical mega-donors who simply write checks to incumbents; they are investors betting on a specific ideological shift. Reid Hoffman, the co-founder of LinkedIn, was particularly active, contributing not just financially but by leveraging his network. In July 2022, reports surfaced that Hoffman had made direct contributions to WelcomePAC's endorsed state legislature candidates in Ohio. The presence of James Murdoch and Michael Bloomberg signaled a desire for a data-driven, centrist approach to governance, one that prioritized stability and pragmatism over ideological purity.

The group registered with the Federal Election Commission as a hybrid PAC, a legal structure that allows it to raise unlimited funds from individuals and corporations (within certain limits) while also making direct contributions to candidates through its affiliated 501(c)(4) arm. This dual structure gave them the agility to run issue-based ads without directly coordinating with campaigns, while still pouring money into candidate coffers. By the end of its inaugural cycle, WelcomePAC had raised more than $2 million, a substantial sum for a brand-new organization operating in such a competitive environment.

The Ohio Experiment

The true proving ground for WelcomePAC's theories was the state of Ohio. In early 2022, while other groups were focusing their resources on federal races or safe seats, WelcomePAC turned its attention to the Ohio House of Representatives, specifically in the Northeast region. Their mission was to recruit "big tent" candidates—individuals who could appeal to a broad spectrum of voters rather than just the Democratic base.

The results of this recruitment drive were immediate and tangible. Later in the cycle, the group endorsed 17 state legislature candidates, all bound by a shared commitment: building a big tent Democratic Party that welcomed independents and moderate Republicans. This was not mere rhetoric; it was a recruitment strategy that required vetting candidates for their willingness to work across the aisle and their ability to speak to conservative-leaning voters without abandoning core Democratic values.

The impact of this grassroots organizing rippled up to the most high-profile race in the state: Tim Ryan's bid for the U.S. Senate against JD Vance. The dynamic of this race was peculiar. JD Vance, a former venture capitalist and author who had risen from obscurity to become the favorite of Donald Trump, commanded the Republican base with an intensity that intimidated even seasoned Democrats. Tim Ryan, a congressman from Ohio's 17th district, was a capable politician but faced an uphill battle in a state that had moved sharply rightward.

WelcomePAC saw an opening where others saw only defeat. In late 2022, the organization organized a series of press conferences throughout Ohio featuring local leaders with Republican backgrounds who publicly endorsed Ryan. These were not generic endorsements; they were calculated moves to signal to conservative voters that it was permissible to support a Democrat if the alternative was seen as too extreme or erratic.

Simultaneously, WelcomePAC ran a sophisticated digital advertising campaign targeting a very specific demographic: voters who had split their tickets in 2018, voting for Republican Governor Mike DeWine but Democratic Senator Sherrod Brown. The data showed that these voters existed and were numerous. WelcomePAC's message was direct: if you could split your ticket once, do it again. Vote for Ryan.

By October, the strategy evolved to be even more aggressive. In late October 2022, the group announced an advertising campaign explicitly designed to persuade both moderate Republicans and Trump-aligned voters not to vote for JD Vance. The ads appeared in print, on the radio, and across digital platforms. They argued that a vote for Vance was a vote against economic stability or community cohesion, appealing to the pragmatic instincts of the suburban voter.

The Cost of Isolation

Despite the best efforts of WelcomePAC, the 2022 election cycle ended in disappointment for their flagship candidate. Tim Ryan lost the race for Senate to JD Vance. The loss was widely attributed to a lack of support from the national Democratic Party establishment. While other parties poured millions into protecting incumbents or flipping seats in more favorable districts, Ryan received minimal support from national committees.

According to Dayton-based NBC News affiliate WDTN, WelcomePAC was the only national Democratic group to invest significantly in supporting Ryan's campaign. This isolation highlights a critical tension within modern American politics: the gap between what national party strategists believe is winnable and what on-the-ground organizers perceive as possible. The national apparatus viewed Ryan's race as a lost cause, a drain on resources that could be better spent elsewhere. WelcomePAC, however, bet that the cost of inaction was higher than the cost of failure.

The human element of this political calculus is often overlooked. Behind the polling numbers and advertising spend are communities where voters felt torn between their party affiliation and their assessment of a candidate's character or competence. For the moderate Republican who voted for Ryan because they feared Vance, or for the independent who saw WelcomePAC's message as a lifeline to a more reasoned political discourse, the election was not just a statistical outcome but a personal decision with long-term consequences for how they viewed their place in the political landscape.

The loss of Ryan did not diminish the audacity of WelcomePAC's approach; rather, it underscored the difficulty of breaking through the partisan noise. The "insurgent tactics" described by the New York Times proved that while you can organize, you cannot always overcome the structural advantages held by a polarized electorate and a national party apparatus unwilling to take risks on center-left candidates in deep-red or purple states.

The California Frontier

While Ohio was the primary theater of operations for WelcomePAC's 2022 debut, their reach extended to the West Coast. In the 2022 race for California's 41st congressional district, the organization threw its support behind Democrat Will Rollins against Republican Representative Ken Calvert.

This race presented a different set of challenges. California is generally considered a Democratic stronghold, but specific districts like the 41st are competitive battlegrounds where demographic shifts and local issues can tip the balance. Will Rollins, running with the backing of WelcomePAC's centrist message, aimed to replicate the "big tent" strategy that had been tested in Ohio. The goal was to attract voters who might be disillusioned with the direction of their current representatives or who were looking for a more pragmatic alternative.

However, the outcome mirrored the Senate race in Ohio. Rollins lost to Calvert in the November general election. The defeat of both Ryan and Rollins in the same cycle raises questions about the viability of WelcomePAC's model in the short term. It suggests that while there is an appetite for centrist politics, converting that appetite into electoral victory requires more than just funding and advertising. It demands a fundamental shift in voter behavior that may take years to achieve, especially in an environment where negative partisanship—voting against the other party rather than for one's own—is the dominant force.

The Future of the Political Center

The 2022 election cycle served as a stress test for WelcomePAC's philosophy. They proved that they could raise money, recruit candidates, and run sophisticated campaigns in a polarized environment. They demonstrated that there are donors willing to fund a centrist insurgency and voters who are responsive to messages of moderation and inclusivity. Yet, the electoral results showed the immense difficulty of translating those assets into victories.

The organization's co-founder's articulation of the goal—to "become the Justice Democrats of the political center"—remains a powerful vision. It speaks to a desire for a political ecosystem where the center is not just a compromise zone but an active, aggressive force capable of defining the terms of debate. If the Justice Democrats successfully moved the Overton window to the left, WelcomePAC sought to do the same from the right, pulling the Democratic Party toward the middle without alienating its base.

The involvement of figures like Reid Hoffman and James Murdoch suggests that this is not a fleeting experiment but a long-term investment in the future of American governance. These donors understand that the current polarization is unsustainable and that the path to a functional democracy may lie in rebuilding the coalition of moderates, independents, and crossover voters that once defined American politics.

As we look toward the political landscape of 2026 and beyond, the lessons from WelcomePAC's inaugural cycle are clear. The center is not dead; it is merely underfunded and misunderstood by both parties. The challenge for organizations like WelcomePAC is to refine their message, deepen their grassroots connections, and find a way to turn the "big tent" from a slogan into a winning strategy.

The story of WelcomePAC is not just about money or elections; it is about the struggle to define what American democracy looks like in an era of division. It is a story of trying to build a bridge between two worlds that seem increasingly distant, using the tools of modern campaigning to reach voters who feel left behind by the extremes. Whether they succeed or fail, their efforts have already forced a conversation about the role of the center in American politics, a conversation that will likely define the next decade of elections.

The human cost of political polarization is measured not just in lost elections but in the erosion of trust between communities. When voters feel that their voices are ignored by national parties and that their pragmatic concerns are dismissed as disloyalty, the fabric of society frays. WelcomePAC's attempt to address this fracture, however imperfectly, represents a recognition that the stakes are too high to accept the status quo.

In the end, the story of WelcomePAC is one of ambition meeting reality. They entered the ring with a bold strategy and deep pockets, hoping to change the game. While they did not win every battle in 2022, they proved that there is a fighting chance for the political center. As long as there are voters willing to split their tickets, and donors willing to fund the fight, the quest to rebuild the big tent will continue. The path forward is uncertain, but the direction is clear: a return to the politics of the center, where compromise is not a weakness but a necessity for survival.
