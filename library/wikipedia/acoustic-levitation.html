Based on Wikipedia: Acoustic levitation

In 1933, two German physicists named Bücks and Muller achieved what had previously been the domain of illusionists: they made liquid hang in mid-air. Working with a quartz crystal and a simple reflector, they trapped droplets of alcohol between them, suspending the fluid against the relentless pull of gravity not with magnetic fields or mechanical hooks, but with sound itself. This was not science fiction, nor was it a parlor trick; it was a rigorous demonstration that high-intensity acoustic radiation pressure could counteract the weight of matter. The phenomenon, known as acoustic levitation, has since evolved from a curious laboratory anomaly into a sophisticated tool for manipulating the delicate and the dangerous, offering a way to handle substances without ever touching them.

The physics behind this defiance of gravity is rooted in the behavior of sound waves themselves. Sound is a mechanical wave, a ripple of pressure moving through a medium like air. When these waves are intense enough and organized correctly, they create regions of high pressure (compressions) and low pressure (rarefactions). If you trap an object within a standing wave—a pattern formed when two waves of the same frequency travel in opposite directions and interfere with each other—the object will naturally migrate to the points of minimum acoustic potential energy. In a standing wave, these stable points are called nodes. By tuning the frequency of the sound precisely, scientists can create a "cage" of pressure that holds an object suspended at a node, effectively neutralizing gravity's downward tug.

"The original experiment was conducted with the intention of calculating the wavelengths and therefore the speed of sound within a gas."

This is where the history of acoustic levitation begins to blur into the broader quest to understand the nature of sound itself. The first demonstration of the possibility of this phenomenon occurred much earlier, in 1866, with August Kundt's famous tube experiments. Kundt was not trying to make things float; he was a physicist obsessed with measuring the speed of sound in gases. He filled a glass tube with fine dust and set up resonant conditions that created standing waves within the air column. The result was mesmerizing: the dust particles, once chaotic, suddenly gathered into neat, evenly spaced piles at the nodes of the wave. While Kundt achieved his goal of measuring wavelength, he inadvertently revealed that sound waves exert a physical force capable of moving matter. It took nearly seventy years for anyone to realize this force could be harnessed to hold objects up against gravity rather than just arranging dust on a floor.

The transition from observing dust in a tube to levitating liquid droplets required a significant leap in engineering and understanding the sheer intensity of sound needed. Gravity is a powerful, constant force. To overcome it with air vibrations, one needs sound waves of immense amplitude—often at ultrasonic frequencies, well above the range of human hearing. If the frequency were audible, the energy required to levitate even a small object would be deafening and likely damaging to human ears. By pushing into the ultrasonic spectrum (typically above 20 kHz), researchers could generate the necessary pressure without creating a cacophony, though there have been historical instances where audible frequencies were used, resulting in a high-pitched whine that underscored the raw power at play.

The primary engine driving this technology is the piezoelectric transducer. These are devices made of special materials that change shape when an electric voltage is applied to them. By cycling the voltage rapidly, the transducer vibrates, generating sound waves with high amplitude and precision. For decades, the standard configuration for acoustic levitation was the Langevin Horn, a design that dominated research from the 1970s until roughly 2017. This apparatus consisted of a piezoelectric actuator attached to a metal transmitter horn, facing a reflector plate. The object to be levitated sat in the gap between them.

However, the Langevin Horn was finicky. It relied on a resonant cavity where the distance between the source and the reflector had to be an exact multiple of the sound's wavelength. This created a fragile equilibrium; as environmental conditions changed—temperature shifts altering the speed of sound, or altitude changes affecting air density—the precise tuning required for levitation would drift. If the distance was off by even a fraction of a millimeter, the standing wave would collapse, and the object would fall. It was a method that worked beautifully in a controlled lab but struggled with the unpredictability of the real world.

The limitations of these static systems began to chafe against the needs of modern industry and science. Researchers wanted more than just a hovering object; they wanted to move it, rotate it, and manipulate it dynamically without physical contact. This desire for control led to a pivotal moment in the history of the field: the realization that sound could be used not just to hold, but to guide. Early experiments demonstrated "dynamic acoustic levitation," where an object could be moved from one point to another by carefully modulating the intensity of the sound waves at different points in space. Imagine a chessboard made of sound; by lowering the pressure on one square and raising it on an adjacent one, a levitated particle could be coaxed to roll "downhill" through the acoustic potential field. This was the precursor to the modern ability to manipulate matter with wave-like precision.

This capability found its most profound application in the vacuum of space. In 1985, Taylor Wang led a team that brought acoustic radiation forces aboard the Space Shuttle Challenger on mission STS-51-B. The goal was not merely to float things for fun; it was to understand how materials behave when the confining effects of gravity and container walls are removed. On Earth, studying molten metals or reactive chemicals is difficult because they react with the crucible holding them or settle due to gravity before they can cool uniformly. In microgravity, using acoustic levitation as a containment mechanism allowed scientists to observe droplets in their purest state. This work continued with further experiments aboard the United States Microgravity Laboratory missions (USML-1 in 1992 and USML-2 in 1995), cementing the role of sound as a critical tool for space-based material science.

The industrial potential of this technology is vast, particularly in the realm of containerless processing. Consider the manufacturing of microchips or high-purity pharmaceutical compounds. In these fields, even microscopic impurities from a reaction vessel can ruin an entire batch. Acoustic levitation offers a way to mix, react, and process materials without ever touching a surface. It is especially valuable for non-conducting materials that cannot be manipulated by electromagnetic levitation, the other major method of contactless suspension. While electromagnetic methods are often easier to control, they require the material to conduct electricity or have magnetic properties. Sound has no such bias; it can levitate water, plastic, glass, and biological samples with equal efficacy.

Yet, for all its promise, acoustic levitation remained a complex, expensive, and difficult technology to master until a significant shift occurred in 2017. At the University of Bristol, Asier Marzo, Adrian Barnes, and Bruce Drinkwater introduced a device that would change the accessibility of the field forever: the "TinyLev."

The TinyLev was a departure from the heavy, precisely tuned Langevin Horns of the past. Instead of a single massive transducer and a reflector, it utilized a dense array of small piezoelectric emitters arranged in a chessboard-like pattern on both the top and bottom plates. This configuration eliminated the need for perfect resonance matching between source and reflector because the system relied on the interaction of two opposing traveling waves rather than a standing wave trapped in a rigid cavity. The distance between the plates no longer needed to be an exact multiple of the wavelength; the system was robust against environmental variations.

More importantly, the TinyLev was designed for democratization. By using 3D-printed frames and off-the-shelf components, including simple Arduino microcontrollers to generate the signal, the team drove the cost down from thousands of dollars to a fraction of that price. The device could be built by hobbyists and students, not just well-funded national laboratories. This was a deliberate move to open up the technology, to allow a wider community to experiment with acoustic manipulation.

"The reduction in cost was particularly important as a principal aim of this device was the democratisation of the technology."

This innovation did more than just lower the price; it unlocked new capabilities. The use of multiple small transducers allowed for the development of phased array ultrasonic systems. In these arrays, the relative phase (or timing) and magnitude of each individual speaker can be controlled independently. By adjusting the delay between the signals sent to different emitters, researchers can shape the sound field in real-time, creating complex patterns that can trap multiple particles simultaneously or move them along arbitrary 3D paths. This is akin to a light show where the beams of sound are directed with laser precision, capable of forming "acoustic tweezers" that can grip and rotate microscopic objects, or even create holographic shapes in mid-air.

Another fascinating evolution involves the use of acoustic metasurfaces and metamaterials. Instead of using active electronics to control every single transducer, researchers have begun 3D-printing physical structures that impose the necessary phase delays on passing sound waves. These passive devices act like acoustic lenses or holograms, focusing and shaping sound without the need for complex wiring or real-time computation. While these offer higher spatial resolution than some electronic arrays, they lack the dynamic reconfigurability of phased arrays. The future likely lies in a hybrid approach, combining the flexibility of active control with the precision of passive metasurfaces to create even more sophisticated manipulation tools.

Despite these rapid advancements, the old guard remains relevant. Langevin Horns are still used in research today, particularly for studies focusing on the fundamental dynamics of levitated objects. Their simple geometry makes them easier to simulate mathematically and control experimentally when complex movement is not required. They serve as a reminder that sometimes, the simplest tool is the most effective for understanding the core principles of a phenomenon.

The scope of what can be levitated continues to expand. Beyond liquids and small solids, researchers have successfully levitated small animals, including insects and even small fish, though this work is often driven by ethical questions as much as scientific curiosity. The ability to suspend a living organism without physical contact raises profound implications for biological research, allowing scientists to study developmental processes in a stress-free environment where the animal does not have to expend energy fighting gravity or navigating a tank. However, the high-intensity sound required must be carefully managed to ensure it does not cause harm to the biological tissue itself, balancing the physical forces of the wave with the fragility of life.

The history of acoustic levitation is also a story of how theoretical physics eventually meets practical engineering. Lord Rayleigh, in the early 1900s, laid the groundwork by developing theories about the pressure force associated with sound waves. His work was largely theoretical, focused on the energy contained within the wave itself. It took nearly a century for the gap between Rayleigh's equations and the actual floating of a coin to be bridged. Hilary St Clair played a crucial role in this bridge; initially interested in acoustic forces for their potential to agglomerate dust particles in mining applications, he developed the first electromagnetic excitation devices capable of lifting heavier objects. His work proved that acoustic levitation was not just a trick for light gases but a force capable of moving substantial mass.

Today, the field is maturing into an indispensable tool for industries ranging from pharmaceuticals to electronics. In drug discovery, acoustic levitation allows scientists to study crystallization processes in pure form, free from the nucleation sites that container walls provide. This can lead to the creation of new polymorphs—different crystal structures of the same molecule—that might have better stability or solubility properties. In electronics, it enables the assembly of delicate components that would be damaged by tweezers or robotic arms. The ability to manipulate droplets with sound means that chemical reactions can be initiated, mixed, and analyzed on a micro-scale without contamination, potentially revolutionizing how we synthesize new materials.

The journey from Kundt's dust-filled tube in 1866 to the programmable acoustic holograms of the 2020s illustrates a fundamental truth about scientific progress: what begins as a curiosity often becomes a cornerstone of technology. The realization that sound could push matter was once an abstract concept, a footnote in thermodynamics. Now, it is a method for handling the future of manufacturing and materials science. As the cost continues to drop and the control mechanisms become more sophisticated, acoustic levitation is poised to move from specialized laboratories into broader applications, perhaps even becoming a standard feature in high-tech production lines.

The implications go beyond mere convenience. By removing physical contact, we remove friction, wear, and contamination. We gain a window into processes that were previously obscured by the tools used to observe them. In an age where precision is paramount, and where the purity of materials dictates the performance of everything from solar panels to life-saving drugs, acoustic levitation offers a path to a cleaner, more precise future. It is a technology that asks us to listen differently to the world around us, recognizing that sound is not just something we hear, but a force we can wield.

The story of acoustic levitation is far from over. As researchers continue to refine phased arrays and explore new materials for metasurfaces, the boundaries of what can be suspended and manipulated will continue to expand. The dream of holding objects in mid-air with nothing but invisible waves of sound has been realized, but the potential applications are only just beginning to unfold. From the quiet hum of a 3D-printed TinyLev in a university lab to the complex arrays controlling droplets in a pharmaceutical reactor, acoustic levitation stands as a testament to human ingenuity—the ability to turn the invisible vibration of air into a tangible hand that can build, mix, and create without ever touching.

The evolution from static suspension to dynamic manipulation marks a new chapter. The chessboard-like arrays of the early 2010s have given way to fully programmable fields where objects can be spun, merged, split, and transported with fluid motion. This level of control transforms acoustic levitation from a passive holding mechanism into an active tool for assembly and reaction. It allows for the creation of "acoustic factories" in mid-air, where components are brought together by sound waves to form complex structures.

In the context of global research and development, this technology represents a convergence of physics, engineering, and materials science. It challenges the traditional methods of handling matter, forcing industries to reconsider how they process chemicals and assemble devices. As we move further into the 21st century, the ability to manipulate matter without contact may become as standard as the electric motor or the microchip. The silence of ultrasonic frequencies belies the roar of innovation that is reshaping our approach to manufacturing and science.

The legacy of those early pioneers like Bücks, Muller, St Clair, and Wang is visible in every droplet suspended by sound today. Their work proved that gravity was not an absolute barrier, but a challenge that could be met with the right application of energy. Today, we stand on the shoulders of their experiments, looking out at a horizon where the air itself can be shaped into tools, holding the building blocks of our future in perfect, silent suspension.
