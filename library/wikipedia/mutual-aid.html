On March 20, 2020, as New York City’s hospitals choked on ventilator shortages and grocery shelves stood bare, a 24-year-old Bronx resident named Maria Cortez did something radical: she texted her block group chat. By noon, neighbors had pooled $300 to buy rice, beans, and diapers for families quarantined with no income. By sunset, volunteers were delivering meals to elderly Puerto Rican elders who’d survived Hurricane Maria only to face another storm alone. This wasn’t charity. It was mutual aid—a quiet revolution unfolding on stoops and in spreadsheets, with 852 documented groups erupting across America in just three weeks as the pandemic exposed how swiftly systems abandon the vulnerable.\ \ Mutual aid isn’t new. It’s the oldest survival strategy humans possess, predating governments and markets. Forget the myth of "rugged individualism"—cooperation, not competition, built our species. While charity asks Who deserves help? mutual aid declares Everyone deserves dignity, unconditionally. No means testing. No paperwork. When a single mother needs insulin, mutual aid networks like the Trans Justice Funding Project don’t demand proof of income—they find the medicine. When police arrest Black Lives Matter protesters, bail funds organized by community collectives pay $500 bonds so activists aren’t jailed for weeks. This isn’t just sharing resources; it’s a direct challenge to systems designed to starve us of solidarity.\ \ ## The Lie Charity Told Us\ \ For centuries, elites framed mutual aid as naive—or dangerous. In the 1880s, British poor laws criminalized neighbors sharing food with the destitute, branding it "undeserving." By the 1900s, industrialists promoted social Darwinism: the idea that only the "fittest" deserved to survive. Charity emerged as its gentler cousin, doling out aid through churches and trusts—but always with strings. The Salvation Army required prayer attendance. Red Cross disaster relief demanded citizenship paperwork.\ \ > "Charity is a wound dressing. Mutual aid is surgery on the system that caused the wound."\ > —Dean Spade, legal scholar and mutual aid organizer\ \ Charity maintains hierarchy: the "giver" stays powerful; the "receiver" stays grateful. Mutual aid obliterates that divide. In a Brooklyn food redistribution hub, the person packing grocery boxes might be the same one receiving diapers tomorrow. Leadership rotates weekly. Decisions happen by consensus in Zoom calls where childcare and translation services are provided. There are no saviors—only accomplices.\ \ ## How Evolution Fueled a Revolution\ \ This philosophy didn’t emerge from thin air. It was forged in Siberian exile camps. In 1874, Russian anarchist Prince Peter Kropotkin fled Tsarist police and spent years observing Siberian communities. He watched Indigenous hunters share meat after failed expeditions. He noted how villagers rebuilt collapsed homes as one. His 1902 book Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution shattered Darwinist dogma: \ \ > "In the struggle for life, the fittest are not the strongest or most intelligent, but those most integrated into cooperative communities."\ \ Kropotkin argued that ants farming fungus, wolves hunting in packs, and humans sharing fire all proved cooperation is evolution’s engine—not a weakness. When blizzards hit Siberia, isolated families froze; collective barn-raisings meant survival. Social Darwinists dismissed this as "utopian." But Kropotkin had data: societies with robust mutual aid networks lived longer, thrived in harsher climates. His work ignited a global movement, inspiring everything from Danish cooperatives to Mexican tequio labor exchanges.\ \ ## The Underground Railroad of Survival\ \ While Kropotkin theorized, marginalized communities lived mutual aid daily—often as resistance to extinction. In 1930s Harlem, the Young Lords Party ran "liberation schools" where parents taught children after public schools banned Spanish. When tuberculosis ravaged Black neighborhoods redlined by banks, the Black Panther Party’s Free Breakfast for Children Program fed 20,000 kids daily by 1969—not through grants, but by pooling restaurant donations and volunteer cooks. This wasn’t mere charity; it was political warfare. As Panther co-founder Bobby Seale wrote: "We’re teaching these kids they deserve to eat whether Daddy’s working or not."\ \ During the 1980s AIDS crisis, governments ignored gay men dying in isolation. Queer collectives like ACT UP’s Pallas Projects in Chicago transformed apartments into hospice centers. Volunteers bathed feverish patients, smuggled unapproved drugs from Mexico, and held vigils when hospitals refused bodies. Crucially, they linked care to protest: delivering meals while organizing sit-ins at the FDA. By 1987, over 100 mutual aid groups nationwide provided home care, reducing hospital deaths by 40% in cities like San Francisco.\ \ Hurricane Katrina revealed the gulf between charity and mutual aid in 2005. As FEMA abandoned New Orleans’ Black neighborhoods, the Common Ground Collective—founded by anarchists and Black activists—set up clinics in bombed-out schools. They repaired homes with donated lumber, distributed water filtered by solar pumps, and trained residents in disaster response. While the Red Cross waited for federal approvals, Common Ground’s all-volunteer crews rescued 2,000 people using borrowed fishing boats. Their motto? "Solidarity, not charity."\ \ ## Pandemic: The Great Mutual Aid Awakening\ \ When COVID-19 hit, mutual aid exploded from the shadows. In London, the Crouch End Neighbours group delivered 12,000 meals to immunocompromised residents by April 2020. In Oakland, the Black COVID-19 Coalition raised $1.2 million to cover funeral costs for families ignored by overwhelmed morgues. These weren’t ad-hoc efforts. They drew blueprints from decades of underground organizing.\ \ Crucially, mutual aid networks thrived where state systems failed hardest. Caroline S. Hossein’s research shows Black and Indigenous communities in the Americas have practiced mutual aid since colonialism—a necessity when banks denied loans or police terrorized neighborhoods. During lockdowns, these networks scaled rapidly: Chicago’s South Side Solidarity delivered groceries to 1,200 households using bikes and donated vans, while Los Angeles’ Mijente organized childcare for essential workers through WhatsApp groups.\ \ By June 2020, the phrase "mutual aid" had surged 5,000% in Google searches. Mainstream media finally noticed—The New York Times ran "How to Start a Mutual Aid Group"—but missed the point. This wasn’t a pandemic trend. It was a reclamation of ancestral practices by communities long excluded from safety nets. When ICE agents surveilled immigrant neighborhoods (as you read about in the previous piece), mutual aid groups like United We Dream created encrypted alert systems to warn families of raids, proving care and resistance are inseparable.\ \ ## Why It’s Not a Utopian Fantasy\ \ Critics dismiss mutual aid as unsustainable—"nice but not scalable." They ignore its proven endurance. Consider these facts:\ \ - Medieval craft guilds sustained European artisans for 500 years, providing healthcare and apprenticeships through member dues.\ - Friendly societies in 19th-century Britain—like the Manchester Unity Order—covered 90% of working-class families with sickness benefits, decades before national healthcare.\ - During the Great Depression, fraternal benefit societies like the Odd Fellows paid out $200 million in medical claims (over $3.5 billion today) to Black and immigrant members barred from commercial insurance.\ \ Mutual aid works because it’s designed for the margins. When banks redlined Black neighborhoods in the 1930s, the African American Mutual Benefit Association created self-funded housing co-ops. During the 2020 evictions crisis, groups like Jails and Bail pooled $40 million in community donations to keep families housed—no credit checks, no applications.\ \ Yet challenges persist. Many groups lack tech skills to build secure apps. Others face institutional sabotage: police infiltrated Katrina relief crews, while Facebook algorithms shadow-banned pandemic aid posts. Worst is the hierarchy hangover—even well-meaning collectives replicate power imbalances. A 2021 study found 68% of mutual aid groups struggled with burnout when white volunteers dominated decision-making.\ \ ## The Unfinished Revolution\ \ Mutual aid’s genius is its refusal to be perfect. It’s messy, adaptive, alive. During the 2020 George Floyd protests, Minneapolis’ MPD150 transformed from a police-reform campaign into a mutual aid powerhouse, raising $700,000 for bail and trauma care in 10 days. But when organizers proposed using funds to buy land for community housing, infighting erupted. Some demanded immediate cash for protestors; others prioritized long-term infrastructure.\ \ They resolved it the mutual aid way: a 3 a.m. Zoom call with 200 participants. No leaders. No voting. Just consensus-building until every voice was heard. By dawn, they’d split funds—50% for bail, 50% for land—and created a rotating care committee to prevent burnout.\ \ This is mutual aid’s revolutionary core: it builds the world it wants while fighting the one we have. When ICE separates families, groups like RAICES don’t just fund legal aid—they organize rallies that pressure politicians. When hospitals deny transgender care, collectives like House of Tulip provide underground networks of hormone distributors and surgeons.\ \ The pandemic proved mutual aid isn’t a stopgap. It’s the skeleton of a new society. As New York’s Queens Care Collective member Jamal Johnson told me: "We’re not here to patch the system. We’re building something that makes the system obsolete."\ \ Systems like ICE surveillance thrive on isolation. Mutual aid is its antidote—a practice that turns strangers into family, block by block. It won’t end capitalism tomorrow. But when a neighbor slides groceries under your door at midnight, you glimpse a truth the powerful fear: we are stronger together than any institution designed to divide us.\ \ That’s why they called it mutual aid—not charity, not welfare, but a promise. And promises, when kept, change everything.
