In 1999, three legislators boarded a construction site on the coast of New Taipei City, expecting to see the future of Taiwan's energy independence rising from the earth. Instead, they found rusted rebar sticking out of concrete and evidence that seawater was seeping into the plant's foundation. It was a moment of stark foreshadowing for a project that would come to define the intersection of engineering ambition, political paralysis, and environmental anxiety in modern Taiwan. The Lungmen Nuclear Power Plant, often referred to simply as "Nuke 4," stands today not as a humming beacon of clean energy, but as a silent, rusting monument to a nation unable to decide its own fate.

The facility was designed to be a marvel of the early twenty-first century. Located in Gongliao District, it was intended to house two Advanced Boiling Water Reactors (ABWRs), each capable of generating 1,300 megawatts of net electricity. This was not merely another reactor; it was meant to be the first Generation III nuclear plant built outside of Japan. The engineers and planners looked across the strait to their neighbors and saw a blueprint for success: the four preceding ABWR units in Japan had been completed in just four to five years each. Taipower, the state-owned utility that owns the plant, believed they could replicate this speed and efficiency on Taiwanese soil. They were wrong.

The seeds of the plant's protracted agony were sown not in a lack of technology, but in a fundamental failure of management strategy. When Taipower initiated the project, it made a fateful decision to fracture the procurement process rather than handing the reins to a single architect or engineering firm. Instead of a unified command structure, they split the contract among multiple vendors. General Electric was tasked with building the reactors; Mitsubishi Heavy Industries was hired to supply the turbines and generators; Stone & Webster (S&W) handled the design; and dozens of other American, Taiwanese, Japanese, and Chinese companies were layered into the mix.

This fragmentation turned project management into a logistical nightmare. Without a single entity responsible for the total integration of systems, coordination broke down. The result was a cascade of delays and ballooning costs that would eventually render the project politically toxic. By 2009, the price tag had swelled to more than US$7.5 billion. By 2014, it had climbed even higher, approaching US$10 billion, or NT$300 billion. The inflation of raw material costs, exacerbated by the delays, meant that every month the site sat idle, the financial burden on the taxpayer grew heavier.

The political timeline of Lungmen is as turbulent as its engineering challenges were complex. The project was first proposed in 1978, a time when energy security was paramount and nuclear power was viewed with optimism. By 1996, after a competitive bidding process, Taipower selected the ABWR design. Construction licenses were granted on March 17, 1999, and work began with an ambitious target: completion by 2004.

The ground shook, quite literally, in September 1999. The magnitude 7.6 Chi-Chi earthquake rattled the island and forced a reevaluation of safety protocols. It was shortly after this disaster that the three legislators discovered the rusting steel and potential seawater infiltration at Lungmen. Yet, the project pressed on, only to be halted by the ballot box. In March 2000, Chen Shui-bian was elected President on a platform that included an explicit pledge to stop nuclear construction. The Democratic Progressive Party (DPP), which held a majority in the legislature for the first time, viewed the plant as a symbol of dangerous overreach and environmental negligence.

In April 2000, DPP legislators demanded a halt to operations. By October of that year, Premier Chang Chun-hsiung suspended construction. At this point, the plant was only 10–30% complete. The idling of contractors lasted for 111 days, from October 27, 2000, to February 14, 2001. This single suspension added a staggering 576 days to the timeline for commercial operation. When construction finally restarted in 2001, it was not with the momentum of a fresh project, but with the heavy baggage of political warfare and public distrust.

The physical reality of the plant became a stage for continuous protest. In 2003, Taipower constructed a wharf to facilitate the delivery of massive components, including the reactor vessels. The construction of this wharf was beset with its own delays and environmental controversies, with local residents claiming it contributed to beach erosion. When the first heavy components arrived, they were met not by applause, but by activists. Each delivery became a flashpoint for demonstrations. The reactor vessel for Unit 1, which should have been installed years earlier, sat in storage until 2005. The delays were no longer just about logistics; they were about legitimacy.

The legal battles were as fierce as the protests. In 2007, Taipower canceled its contract with the original designers, Stone & Webster. This decision triggered protracted litigation that dragged on until 2011. Meanwhile, the discovery of aboriginal artifacts at the construction site in 2010 reignited calls to halt work entirely, introducing a cultural and ethical dimension to the debate that went beyond energy policy. The Taiwan Atomic Energy Council (AEC) stepped in with sharp criticism. In 2008 and again in 2011, the AEC fined Taipower for making design changes without first obtaining approval from General Electric, the plant's designer. Taipower argued these changes were necessary to stay on schedule; GE later approved 97% of them retroactively. But the fines and the criticism served as a public indictment of the utility's management capabilities.

By the time the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster struck Japan in March 2011, the Lungmen plant had already become a symbol of everything the anti-nuclear movement feared. The Fukushima catastrophe galvanized environmental groups across Taiwan to demand a national referendum on the plant's fate. They argued that such a critical decision could not be left solely to politicians; it belonged to the people. However, the path to the ballot box was paved with legal and procedural obstacles.

The political maneuvering surrounding referendums revealed deep fractures in Taiwanese society. In February 2013, the Kuomintang (KMT)-led government proposed a referendum sponsored by 32 lawmakers led by Lee Ching-hua. The question was framed as: "Do you agree that the construction of the Fourth Nuclear Power Plant should be halted and that it not become operational?" Under Taiwan's laws at the time, for a referendum to be binding, more than 50% of all registered voters had to participate, and a majority of those participants had to vote "yes." The opposition DPP argued that achieving this turnout in a non-presidential election year was practically impossible. They viewed the referendum not as a democratic tool, but as a trap designed to ensure continued construction by making it mathematically difficult to stop the plant through voting.

This political standoff led to dramatic scenes inside the Legislative Yuan. The DPP barricaded the legislative chamber to prevent a vote on placing the Lee referendum on the ballot. KMT legislators attempted to break through the barricades, leading to scuffles that were broadcast live across the nation. The Lee referendum was never voted on and was eventually withdrawn. In a twist of irony, the very mechanism designed to give people a voice became another battleground where politicians fought over procedure rather than principle.

Other figures tried different angles. Annette Lu, a prominent political figure, attempted to use local referendum processes as early as 2012. Her petition gathered 50,000 signatures by March 2013, but the Executive Yuan Referendum Review Committee rejected it in May, ruling that the issue was of national importance and could not be decided locally. She launched another local referendum in June 2013 and filed a lawsuit against the rejection, but the legal machinery moved slowly, mirroring the stasis at the construction site.

In contrast to the Lee referendum, which asked voters to halt the plant, Professor Kao Cheng-yan of National Taiwan University proposed a referendum that required positive action for the plant to proceed. His question, formulated in 2014, asked: "Do you agree to allow Taiwan Power Co to insert fuel rods into the Fourth Nuclear Power Plant in New Taipei City for a test run?" The logic was clear: if people wanted the plant, they had to vote explicitly for it. However, the Kao referendum was rejected from the ballot due to what officials deemed contradictory and confusing language. The cycle of proposal, rejection, and political deadlock continued.

As the years wore on, the physical state of the plant began to reflect its uncertain future. In 2014, Taipower submitted a plan to "mothball" Unit 1, which was by then largely complete, while freezing construction on Unit 2. The decision was a tacit admission that the project could not be finished in the traditional sense without massive political intervention. The plant sat in a state of suspended animation, a half-finished giant looming over the coast of New Taipei.

The human and environmental costs of this limbo were not abstract. The rusting rebar found by legislators in 1999 became a potent symbol for critics who argued that safety had been compromised by haste and then neglected by delay. The erosion of local beaches caused by the wharf construction hurt fishing communities. The massive financial burden, eventually nearing US$10 billion, was money that could have been invested elsewhere—perhaps in renewable energy infrastructure or social services.

The political landscape shifted dramatically again with the rise of Tsai Ing-wen and the DPP to full executive power. Tsai had long championed a policy of "nuclear phaseout" by 2025. Her administration viewed Lungmen not as an asset, but as an obstacle to Taiwan's energy transition. In December 2018, Taipower began removing unused fuel from Unit 1 and returning it to the United States. This was a symbolic and practical step away from nuclear power; once the fuel is gone, restarting the reactor becomes significantly more difficult and expensive.

The final blow came in December 2021. A referendum was held on whether to continue construction of Unit 2. The proposal was narrowly rejected by the voters. With this result, combined with the DPP's unwavering commitment to a nuclear-free future by 2025—one year before the 2024 presidential election—the fate of Lungmen seemed sealed. While some engineers argued that it is technologically feasible to finish a legacy construction project after decades of inactivity, and while there are precedents for such feats globally, the political will simply did not exist.

The story of the Lungmen Nuclear Power Plant is a cautionary tale about the limits of technological determinism. It demonstrates that no amount of engineering prowess can overcome a fractured management strategy or a deeply polarized electorate. The plant was designed to be the first of its kind outside Japan, a beacon of modernization. Instead, it became a symbol of paralysis.

The conflict over Lungmen was never just about kilowatts and megawatts. It was about trust in institutions, the balance between energy security and environmental safety, and the role of the public in deciding the nation's future. For every year that construction dragged on, the gap widened between the politicians making promises and the reality of a rusting shell on the coast. The rusted rebar found in 1999 was not just a structural flaw; it was a metaphor for the project itself—corroded by time, politics, and a failure to agree on what the future should look like.

Today, as the sun sets over New Taipei City, the silhouette of the Lungmen plant remains. It is a structure that exists between two worlds: the world of the past, where nuclear power was the promised solution, and the world of the present, where renewables are ascendant and nuclear energy is viewed with suspicion. The reactors stand silent, their potential energy unspent, waiting for a decision that may never come.

The human cost of this indecision is measured in billions of dollars wasted, in the erosion of coastal communities, and in the deepening cynicism regarding democratic processes. When voters are told that a referendum requires 50% turnout to pass, and when political parties manipulate these rules to suit their agendas, the result is not democracy in action, but democracy in stasis. The people of Taiwan were given the chance to speak through multiple referendums, only to have those voices muffled by procedural hurdles and political maneuvering.

The legacy of Lungmen will likely be one of "what if." What if Taipower had hired a single architect? What if the 2000 suspension had not occurred? What if the referendum questions had been clearer? But history does not deal in hypotheticals. It deals in the rusted steel, the returned fuel rods, and the empty cooling towers. The Lungmen Nuclear Power Plant serves as a permanent reminder that energy policy is not merely an engineering problem to be solved with better technology; it is a social contract that requires consensus, transparency, and trust—three things that were in critically short supply for this project.

In the end, the plant stands as a testament to the complexity of modern governance. It shows how easily a project intended to power a nation can become a drain on its resources and a source of its divisions. The ABWRs, once touted as the pinnacle of nuclear safety and efficiency, sit idle, their advanced technology rendered obsolete by a simpler, more stubborn force: the inability of a society to agree on its own path forward. As Taiwan moves toward its 2025 phaseout target, Lungmen remains a ghost of an alternative future, a giant sleeping in the dark, waiting for a dawn that may never break.

The lesson here is not just about nuclear power. It is about the fragility of large-scale infrastructure projects when they are divorced from the social and political context in which they exist. You cannot simply build your way out of conflict. The rusted rebar, the eroded beaches, the legal battles, and the protests all point to a singular truth: a power plant that generates electricity but fails to generate consensus is ultimately powerless.

The story of Lungmen is far from over in terms of its physical existence, but in terms of its function as a source of energy, it has already reached its end. The fuel is gone. The political will has evaporated. What remains is a hollow shell that serves only as a reminder of the high cost of indecision. For a nation striving to define its identity and secure its energy future, Lungmen stands as a warning: without a clear vision and a unified voice, even the most advanced technology cannot save you from your own divisions.

The silence at Lungmen is deafening. It is the sound of a project that consumed a quarter-century of debate, billions of dollars, and countless hours of political struggle, only to arrive nowhere. As the world transitions away from fossil fuels and grapples with climate change, Taiwan's experience with Lungmen offers a nuanced perspective on the challenges of energy transition. It shows that the path forward is rarely linear and often fraught with unexpected obstacles. The solution lies not in the reactor core, but in the hearts and minds of the people who must decide how to power their lives. Until that decision is made with clarity and conviction, Lungmen will remain exactly where it is: unfinished, undecided, and indefinitely suspended.
