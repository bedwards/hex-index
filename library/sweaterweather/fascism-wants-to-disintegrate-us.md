### I. The Acid Queen

Legend has it that Grace Slick—famed singer of Jefferson Airplane and Jefferson Starship—was eating bread, drinking wine, and reading poetry in the Marin County countryside when a police officer accosted her. When the officer told her he was going to arrest her for public intoxication, she spat back “‘Drunk in what public? . . . What do you mean? What are we talking about, the trees, these deer here, these leaves on the ground? What public? You’re the only public here.’” She was arrested for what she called “Drunk Mouth.”

On another occasion, the engine of Slick’s Aston Martin burst into flames, and when she called highway patrol for help, the officer said,

> “‘O.K., what’s going on here?’ Remember, there are flames coming out of my car. I said, ‘What the fuck does it look like is going on? I’m having a goddamn party at four A.M. with fucking flames coming out of my car.’”

She was arrested again.[1](#footnote-1) Drunk Mouth. They didn’t care about her.

Grace Slick was nicknamed “The Acid Queen,” implicitly for the drugs—she wrote the inimitable “White Rabbit” in an hour while under the influence.[2](#footnote-2) She had her hand in another pop cultural/acidic legend: in 1970, Slick and famed activist Abbie Hoffman planned to lace Richard Nixon’s tea with LSD, in hopes of inducing a trip in the president, but the plans were foiled.[3](#footnote-3)

Slick was acidic in more ways than literal; I can’t help but recognize it in her sarcastic words for the oppressive police officers in the stories above. Not to mention the bite in her voice musically and lyrically:

> When the men on the chessboard  
> Get up and tell you where to go  
> And you’ve just had some kind of mushroom  
> And your mind is moving low  
> Go ask Alice  
> I think she’ll know
> 
> When logic and proportion  
> Have fallen sloppy dead  
> And the White Knight is talking backwards  
> And the Red Queen’s off with her head  
> Remember what the dormouse said  
> Feed your head  
> Feed your head  
> Feed your head

“When the men on the chessboard get up and tell you where to go,” also remember: They don’t care about us.

### II. The Acid Corpse

Leon Czolgosz was a Polish-American laborer/miller in the late 1800s. Czolgosz joined a workers’ strike when wages at the mill were cut, but for joining the organizers he was fired and blacklisted from working there again. The experience put the inequity between the capitalist class and the working class into clear relief for Czolgosz, and he spent time reading radical works back on his family’s farm (remember what Slick said: “Feed your head”). Anarchism piqued Czolgosz’s interest, as did the recent assassination of an Italian king as well as a speech by Emma Goldman at the Franklin Club[4](#footnote-4). I don’t know the contents of that speech, but 17 years later, when Goldman was arrested for encouraging citizens to avoid the World War I draft, she told the court:

> I wish to state here, in my own behalf and in behalf of hundreds of thousands whom you decry and state to be antipatriotic, that we love America, we love her beauty, we love her riches, we love her mountains and her forests, and above all we love the people who have produced her wealth and riches, who have created all her beauty, we love the dreamers and the philosophers and the thinkers who are giving America liberty. But that must not make us blind to the social faults of America. That cannot make us deaf to the discords of America. That cannot compel us to be inarticulate to the terrible wrongs committed in the name of patriotism and in the name of the country. We simply insist, regardless of all protests to the contrary, that this war is not a war for democracy.[5](#footnote-5)

It sounds just like “logic and proportion have fallen sloppy dead,” as Slick wrote. Emma Goldman, acid queen.

Czolgosz took his fascination with Goldman—and particularly his obsession with class inequality—with him to to the Pan-American Exposition, 1901, in upstate New York. He knew that a certain William McKinley, president of the United States of America, would be in attendance. Czolgosz approached the president and fired twice, his gun hidden under a handkerchief.[6](#footnote-6) As McKinley fell to the ground, and as onlookers began to attack Czolgosz, the president told the retaliators, “Go easy on him, boys.”[7](#footnote-7) McKinley died days later.

The “Go easy on him” edict wouldn’t last—McKinley must have known the system would do its work. Czolgosz was executed by electrocution on October 29th, 1901. In his last words, Czolgosz said he assassinated McKinley because the leader “was the enemy of the good people—the working people.”

Czolgosz’s body was placed in an unmarked prison grave and covered in sulfuric acid to disintegrate the corpse.[8](#footnote-8) The red queen took his head.

Don’t misread what I say next: I’m not claiming some sort of assassination apologetics. But I can’t help but shout that the system and its followers didn’t care about this working class man in life, and they didn’t care about him in death. They don’t really care about us.

### III. The Two-Faced Coin

I stood in the musty hallway of an old Mormon church in rural Utah, at the very beginning of 2020, watching my youngest daughter crawl along the scratchy, faded purplish carpet. She and I were alone in the hallway except for the man who stood across from me, a patriotic flag pin stuck to his Sunday-best suit coat. This was Spencer Cox, then lieutenant governor and future governor of the state, who happened to be a member of our congregation. He and I were discussing suicidality among LGBTQ youth, particularly as it related to my social-emotional health work in schools. I told him about the power of even the simplest of gestures at the middle school where I worked then—my rainbow pins alone were surprisingly impactful for a few queer youth in particular, one of whom told me they felt safe bringing up their own suicidal thoughts with me because of the rainbows.

Cox agreed then that mental health difficulties among queer youth were looming and salient in Utah. Our conversation ended, brief but positive; I felt sincerity from him, both from the conversation itself and because I had previously read [his thoughtful response](https://www.ksl.com/article/40209267/lt-gov-cox-speaks-at-vigil-for-orlando-my-heart-has-changed) to the terrorist attack in Orlando at a gay nightclub, just a few years prior. A few *weeks* prior to our conversation, I had also overheard him talking to another congregation member about a political calling he saw for himself—to change the Republican party from within, specifically as it related to LGBTQ acceptance.

I won’t pretend to know Cox personally—I no longer live in his state, and I no longer believe in Mormonism—and I’m ignorant of his current motives, but for a few years I was sure he was the singular “good Republican.”[9](#footnote-9)

At the time, the prospect of him as governor sounded to me like a twenty-first century *Mr. Smith Goes to Washington*—the small-town politician with enough gumption to take on the corruption in government, to change the system from within.

After all, in 2016 Cox said that Donald Trump represented “neither kindness nor goodness.”[10](#footnote-10) He also called Trump “disingenuous. I think he's dangerous. I think he represents the worst of what our great country stands for.”[11](#footnote-11)

Yet following a campaign-trail assassination attempt on Trump in 2024, Cox wrote a letter[12](#footnote-12) to Trump, stating:

> “I want you to know that I truly believe that God had a hand in saving you. I don’t know what it feels like to be less than an inch away from certain death. I also hesitate to even imagine what would have happened to our country if your life had not been miraculously spared. I worry that we would have experienced the unthinkable. . . . Your life was spared. Now, because of that miracle, you have the opportunity to do something that no other person on earth can do right now: unify and save our country.”

Where did the acid go? I’m open to politicians changing their minds; they’re generally human. But I have to confess my sadness when the shift of anyone I know is toward fascism, not away from it. Yes, I’m sad. I have a blind spot because of that one conversation.

Cox’s turn to bootlicking makes me sick to my stomach—how is [this](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/03/restoring-truth-and-sanity-to-american-history/) unifying from the current administration? Or [this](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/03/designating-english-as-the-official-language-of-the-united-states/)? Or [this](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/01/ending-radical-indoctrination-in-k-12-schooling/)? And those are just the most recent culprits on Trump’s rap sheet. I don’t care how nuanced Cox’s takes are on, say, DEI[13](#footnote-13) (because extremists won’t care; they’re busy using “DEI” as an ethnic slur). When you hitch your pioneer-stock wagon to fascists, you are complicit in fascism.

To reference another Mormon children’s song: “the rains came down,” the Trump came up, and Cox’s moral “house on the sand washed away.”

Cox recently wrote an opinion piece[14](#footnote-14) romanticizing the “winding down” of the Department of Education, using the same talking points as his other Republican pals, and ignoring or obfuscating what the DoE actually does[15](#footnote-15); he isn’t above his colleagues morally, despite what he wants us to think. (The piece was published by the Washington Examiner, which is owned by Philip Anschutz, who may or may not have an interest in a push toward chartered education considering he also financed the anti-public education documentary *Waiting for Superman*.) Is Cox blind to the implications of a lack of federal oversight in education[16](#footnote-16) [17](#footnote-17) [18](#footnote-18) , or is he just unmotivated by them?

“Ye shall know them by their fruits” is a common Bible quote in Cox’s and my Mormonism and in other Christian circles, a scriptural “actions speak louder than words.” I think the fruit has gone rotten. Maybe it was on the tree too long.

The fictional Harvey Dent’s line in *The Dark Knight*, however mainstream, feels apt: “You either die a hero or live long enough to see yourself become the villain.” Harvey’s words are a moral Chekhov’s gun, as later in the film half of his face is burned—by acid in the comics—and he becomes the Janus-like Two-Face, a villain obsessed with duality.[19](#footnote-19) In the movie and the comics that inspired it, Two-Face flips a specific coin, previously with identical faces/heads, but now with one side scarred. The coin flip makes Dent’s decisions for him. Dent outsources his free will to chance.

Cox outsourced his to fascism—after all, everyone else is doing it! Despite the personal words I experienced with Cox, and especially despite his documented words and actions[20](#footnote-20) of queer support previously, Cox recently rolled over for a law that bans certain flags, such as pride flags, in government buildings and schools. This bill is the first of its kind, and although Cox said he disagreed with aspects of the bill in its current form, he refused to veto it, and he intimated that pride flags were “politically divisive symbols. . . . In an attempt to make some kids feel more welcome, other kids feel less welcome. . . . I appreciate that the bill is neutral on the types of flags in question (and I find it strange that no headline reads ‘MAGA flags banned from classrooms’).”[21](#footnote-21) I don’t know, probably because the bill’s sponsor, Trevor Lee, had no qualms about revealing that banning pride flags was specifically a goal of his bill.[22](#footnote-22) And clearly Cox isn’t listening when queer folks say that our queerness is not so easily chosen as is wearing a red hat.

Cox has only sand to stand on; he helped spread misconceptions about Olympic boxer Imane Khelif being a man.[23](#footnote-23) I don’t care how many sit-downs with queer people he’s had[24](#footnote-24) if he hasn’t learned anything from them. This whole situation just feels to me like a modern, “Go easy on him, boys” but aimed at the LGBTQ community. The system will pour the acid itself.

On Saturday, March 29th, 2025, activists in Salt Lake City celebrated transgender people with a giant transgender flag on the steps of the state capitol building. The Utah Pride Center invited Cox to the event, after he told the LGBTQ community, “I want you to know that I love and appreciate you and I am grateful that you are part of our state. I know these words may ring hollow to many of you, but please know that I mean them sincerely.” As far as I can tell, he never acknowledged the invitation, and he did not show up.

About the bill, Cox also said, “The idea that kids can only feel welcome in a school if a teacher puts up a rainbow flag is just wrong. . . . Let’s do everything possible to make our classrooms one of the last remaining politically neutral places in our state.” Again with the assumption that queerness is inherently political. Not to mention the strawman—nobody is saying a rainbow flag is the *only* way to create a welcoming space. But a welcoming environment for queer students is vital,[25](#footnote-25) and limiting what that looks like is going to limit students feeling welcome, particularly in an educational atmosphere that punishes even whispering about gender and sexuality. Cox once told me mental health among queer youth was important to him. They feel like just words now. He flipped the coin.

—

This is all to illustrate that fascism has two faces, every time. This is the ironic, often self-blind, hypocrisy of autocracy—it would be hilarious if it wasn’t so sad and dangerous. And it took me too long to see it.

Fascists love to claim systemic racism is a myth and then perform a systemic racism: book bans, CRT panic, history rewrites[26](#footnote-26).

The powerful claim to care about building better systems, but they really only care about their image. Michael Jackson’s video for “They Don’t Care About Us” was filmed in Brazil and directed by Spike Lee.[27](#footnote-27) Brazilian officials worried that a music video shedding light on poverty in Brazil would damage the image they’d been trying to shed about that poverty. The image was more important to them than the actual people. Flip the coin.

They claim free speech as a value, unless it sheds a light on their hypocrisies. Like politicians who refuse to face their constituents.[28](#footnote-28) Or like this Rage Against the Machine video:

The Democrats aren’t immune to this two-faced coin, either. Not by a long shot. The Democratic platform once claimed, back when the nation was in the midst of an identity crisis about its colonial-imperialism:

> “We assert that no nation can long endure half republic and half empire, and we warn the American people that imperialism abroad will lead quickly and inevitably to despotism at home.”[29](#footnote-29)

Flip the coin: Obama’s drone murders, Biden’s war crimes, systemic and pointed attacks on university students in blue states for speaking up for Palestine. And that’s all just in the last few years. This is the Imperial boomerang.[30](#footnote-30) They don’t care about us.

Fascism is perpetuated by fear, every time. Everything you and I are currently worried about on a larger scale—queer safety, women’s safety, immigration/refugees, racism, climate collapse, a continually propagandized education system, imperialism here and abroad, etc.—is happening because the system, its billionaire leaders, and the politicians on the leash are all afraid of losing power/control over those things, and they’re unwilling to admit it. That power is also supported and fed by *our* fear.

Fascism is acidic, toxic. The acid they spit our way absolutely disintegrates, even when it comes through smiling teeth. Yet they lose a bit of power when we start admitting that they don’t really care about us. They thrive on maintaining their American dream: You should love and defend billionaires because you can be one someday! But when we reject that premise—that hoarding wealth is a neutral good and is a valuable goal—and instead accept it as slippery exploitation, that it both actively and passively harms people, then we can create a new dream, one where the people have an actual say in the future. Personally, I dream of one where a man is listened to when organizing for better pay, or one where we’re listened to, period. I dream of one where a musician isn’t punished for reading poetry among the leaves and trees and using her voice. I dream of one where the leaves and trees are still here. I dream of one where the oligarchs can finally admit how afraid of you they are.

We “love the dreamers and the philosophers and the thinkers . . . But that must not make us blind to the social faults of America.” And instead of fear, “when the men on the chessboard get up and tell you where to go . . . feed your head.” Otherwise, the red queen will be off with it easily. Keep reminding yourself, my friend: they don’t care about us. Fascism wants to disintegrate us, in every sense of the word—let’s be acid queens.
