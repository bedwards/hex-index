May 4th is today—a colloquially started but now basically official Star Wars holiday in which people tell each other “May the fourth be with you,” and retailers post sales of every type of galactic memorabilia imaginable. Prior to this pomp, the Star Wars franchise and the month of May already went hand-in-hand: the original *Star Wars* was released in the United States on May 25, 1977; and six other films in the franchise have also been released in May.

Also in May (not sorry about the whiplash here), May 15, 1948, was the recognized start of the Nakba, or the great catastrophe, when hundreds of thousands of indigenous Palestinians were forced from their homes by the new “state” of Israel[1](#footnote-1) and have been resisting ever since. That oppressive violence and that rebellion have continued to today, in which the death toll for Palestinians, just since October 7th, passed 30,000 (likely more[2](#footnote-2)) in February 2024.

Of course May as the month of connection between a space fantasy and the reality in Palestine is just a coincidence. But as both a fan of stories and as someone who self-proclaims to be on the left, I’ve been increasingly outraged by the lionizing of a fictional rebellion next to hand-wringing about what resistance materially looks like, particularly among liberals/progressives. I get it: I can’t pretend I wasn’t there morally/philosophically yesterday. I’m just a baby when it comes to empathy about world events. And of course I also understand that fiction is fictional. But I also want comrades who are willing to re-assess what they know of resistance. This post is aimed at them.

With both Star Wars and Gaza always on my mind, I can’t help but think about, with it being May 4th, how nearly half of Palestine’s population is children[3](#footnote-3), kids who should be watching fun movies and not fighting to survive. It is a privilege, I know, to be able to sit and write about the historical resonance of Star Wars in May 2024. Yet I do think the question is valuable as an early step (and absolutely not a material nor standalone one): Can resistance in Star Wars be applied to real-world uprising? Or to put the question another way: Do we *believe* Star Wars?

### **—**

#### Star Wars as Cyclical History

As a child, I viewed the original Star Wars trilogy as an allegory (although I was too young to know what *allegory* meant) for the American Revolution in the United States. My entry point for the realities of resistance, of scrappy guerillas, of an evil and organized empire, was George Washington and his underdog rebels versus the big, bad, British imperials.

Despite making this connection as a child, and despite no longer holding Washington or his contemporaries on pedestals, I still think there’s a valid reading of the Star Wars franchise within the lens of American Revolution, but it’s a criticism that I believe should also take into account the parallels of the United States as its own evil[4](#footnote-4) empire[5](#footnote-5) alongside the shifts and changes in Star Wars itself, both narratively and externally.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MTjm!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F223bffa3-ace4-4154-b997-7000a30b7363_1536x769.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MTjm!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F223bffa3-ace4-4154-b997-7000a30b7363_1536x769.jpeg)

We see this progression in the repetitions/cycles within the Star Wars narrative: a macrocosmic democracy exists, an empire rises to power in the face of war, a plucky rebellion defeats the oppressors, the rebellion evolves into a galaxy-wide power structure, and the cycle repeats within and without the trilogies. It could be said that Star Wars is as cynical as it is cyclical. Or if not cynical, at least continually tragic[6](#footnote-6). As George Lucas, the original creator of Star Wars, said of the repeated themes/characters/ideas from trilogy to trilogy: “It’s like poetry. . . . They rhyme.”[7](#footnote-7) To be more specific, the Galactic Empire of the Original Trilogy, which had risen to power in the Prequel Trilogy, is replaced by the fascistic First Order in the Sequel Trilogy, and a new correspondent resistance group begins—history repeats itself in the galaxy far, far away, and we all know the platitude on how/why that can happen here on Earth.

We also see a parallel shift in the franchise externally, in the behind-the-scenes narrative, as Star Wars progressed from a semi-independent, scrappy little movie to a billion-dollar behemoth of a franchise. Lucas went from fighting a studio system forcing editing mandates to creating a powerful system himself. Even Lucas recognized the irony, in 2004’s documentary Empire of Dreams:

> “What I was trying to do was stay independent so that I could make the movies I wanted to make. But now I've found myself being the head of a corporation. . . . I have become the very thing that I was trying to avoid.”

Of course, Lucas then sold his corporation to another pop culture empire in Disney, and he spent most of the earnings toward educational charity.[8](#footnote-8) (The Lucas-as-Anakin, good-to-bad-to-good analogue continues to feel apt?)

I need to add three quick, maybe contradictory, points here, but I think they are salient: (1) Star Wars was originally intended for “young people”[9](#footnote-9) in Lucas’s mind, and the franchise has, with a few exceptions, stuck to this guideline. Adult fans forget this too easily, in my opinion. And (2) I also don’t think we *have* to continue to view Star Wars through a childlike lens. There’s no imperative to completely unplug our adult perspectives when watching Star Wars. And (3) children can and should understand the history of revolution, and their own understanding of Star Wars does not have to be vacant of historical resonance. I can enjoy Star Wars from multiple positions: both my 10-year-old self and my 36-year-old self, with the added layer of watching it through my own children’s eyes as well (Star Wars is generational—it also rhymes meta-narratively).

### **—**

#### “Keep Politics Out of My Star Wars”

The influence of Star Wars on pop culture cannot be overstated. Similarly, many have stated, *ad infinitum*, the influence of various stories, histories, and ideas *on* Star Wars: the original trilogy in particular is equal parts fairy tale, American western, Kurosawa allusion, World War and/or Vietnam allegory, Flash Gordon intimation, Dune/Lord of the Rings reference, and on and on. In other words, each moving part is a cell that makes up the body of Star Wars, each of them necessary but also contributing to the body’s uniqueness. Each allusion “binds the galaxy together,” as Obi-Wan Kenobi says of the Force.

When I say that George Lucas was heavily influenced by the Vietnam War, it’s not to say a Vietnam allegory is the only reading of Star Wars.[10](#footnote-10) Rather, I bring up Vietnam because it’s far less discussed in the context of Lucas than it should be, and because, frankly, I think too often the audience tries to divorce reality from the fictions that spring from it.[11](#footnote-11)

I can’t help but notice that, anecdotally, the most toxic Star Wars fans (or really any mainstream franchise) tend to be the ones who feel severed from the political intentions of the latest entries in the fictional galaxy—I feel safe saying this, because these fans would likely tell you as much. But I find it much easier to hear valid storytelling criticisms versus the “anti-woke,” “keep politics out of my entertainment” crowd. (Perhaps ironically, you’re more likely to see this racist/sexist/homophobic crowd bemoaning identify politics, such as with the casting of Moses Ingram[12](#footnote-12) in *Obi-Wan Kenobi*. Oddly, I’ve heard no whining about the leftism of *Andor* hiding right under their noses—how fascism looks on the ground from the perspective of the workers, how revolutions/uprisings actually form, etc.)

I’ve got news that I hope they hear, that isn’t new: all art is political. I think that even “bad” or lowbrow or stereotypical/mainstream art is political. If it doesn’t feel political to you, that simply means (1) you’re aligned with the politics of the story, and/or (2) the art is aligned with current mainstream politics. In other words, all art is political in that it is either revolutionary *or* it is functioning to reinforce the political status quo (or perhaps a mixture of both).

Even Star Wars has *always* been political, in how it was informed and how audiences understand it, such as its relation to World War II. This was intentional on Lucas’s part. As author Chris Taylor writes in *How Star Wars Conquered the Universe:*

> “If you look closely enough, you can see that wartime influence throughout the franchise. It’s in the one-man fighters, the rebel’s helmets and boots, the Stormtrooper’s modified UK Sterling submachine guns, Han Solo’s German Mauser C96 semiautomatic, the tall fascist in the black gas mask.”[13](#footnote-13)

The dogfighting scenes in Star Wars were famously storyboarded using spliced World War II films, to match the shots and energy. The Imperial officer uniforms and boots are clearly reminiscent of SS attire. The fact that the soldiers of the Empire are called “stormtoopers” at all! The list goes on.

Still, I think connecting Star Wars thematically to World War II or the Revolutionary War is far less controversial than connecting it to Vietnam or Palestine. Why? I absolutely think racism is at play. And I also think it’s hard for many fans to stomach anti-Americanism in their escapist fiction, whereas connecting a space opera to World War II or to the American Revolution feels like barely a statement at all here in the United States—the majority narrative has romanticized these periods and these soldiers to the point that it is expected in our fictions. Conversely, it’s much harder to divorce any thematic connections to Vietnam and Palestine from criticism of the United States empire. To allegorize America-as-Empire feels anathema to America-as-heroes.

But *Star Wars* was heavily influenced by Vietnam, and I think we forget it.

### **—**

#### The Maker and Vietnam

Whether or not we remember the metaphorical math of Vietnam + Star Wars, George Lucas’s (“Thank the maker”) early politics were forming during this time of rising American criticisms and upheaval. Again, Taylor:

> “Lucas was determined now to make movies about the \[Vietnam\] war in three modes: past, present, and future; absence, reality, and allegory. *American Graffiti* would take people back to a time before Vietnam ripped America apart. *Apocalypse Now*, which Lucas hoped to direct before or after *The Star Wars*, would show it in the present tense. . . . *Apocalypse Now* was the movie he felt would lead to the government running him out of the country. The third mode that Lucas intended to use to depict Vietnam—the allegorical, futuristic lens—was only just taking shape. . . . Lucas was fascinated by the notion of how a tiny nation could overcome the largest military power on Earth, and this was baked into *The Star Wars* right from its earliest notes in 1973: ‘A large technological empire going after a small group of freedom fighters.’”[14](#footnote-14)

Lucas did not end up directing *Apocalypse Now,* the 1979 *Heart of Darkness* retelling set during the Vietnam War. But he was involved in developing the movie for four years before handing over the project completely to his friends Francis Ford Coppola and John Milius. Lucas instead focused his efforts on *The Star Wars* (as it was called then), deciding to trade the literalism of Vietnam in the former project for a metaphor for Vietnam in the latter—intended for a younger audience.[15](#footnote-15)

Years later, when discussing the development of the Star Wars prequels and their relation to real-world history, and in reminiscing about the original trilogy’s themes, Lucas said:

> “It was really about the Vietnam War, and that was the period where Nixon was trying to run for a \[second\] term, which got me to thinking historically about how do democracies get turned into dictatorships? Because the democracies aren’t overthrown; they’re given away.”[16](#footnote-16)

And also, when developing the background of the Emperor, when brainstorming for *Return of the Jedi*, Lucas said “He was a politician. Richard M. Nixon was his name. He subverted the senate and finally took over and became an imperial guy and he was really evil. But he pretended to be a really nice guy.”[17](#footnote-17)

This is all to say that, for all its often-simplistic good guys vs. bad guys, Lucas is at least consistent in “fascism bad” and “rebellion against fascism good.” Lucas [noted](https://www.amc.com/blogs/george-lucas-reveals-how-star-wars-was-influenced-by-the-vietnam-war--1005548) this historical reading of both the romanticized United States and the imperial one. Regarding the American Revolution: “We’re fighting the largest empire in the world, and we’re just a bunch of hay seeds in coonskin hats that don’t know nothing,” and regarding the connection to Vietnam: “The irony is that, in both of those, the little guys won. The highly technical empire—the English Empire, the American Empire—lost. That was the whole point.”

### **—**

#### Do We Believe Star Wars?

So if Star Wars writ large is about a rebellion versus an imperial machine, and if the moral high ground is placed narratively on that rebellion, why is it so hard to apply that morality to current events? Why do we applaud the destruction of the Death Star, a literal Imperial machine, but decry Palestinian resistance against Israeli oppression? Or when Cassian Andor, in *Rogue One* (not created by Lucas, but informed by him), says, “We’ve all done terrible things on behalf of the Rebellion. Spies, saboteurs, assassins,” why does he remain narratively/morally commendable, but real-world resistance, like Palestine, is vilified? I don’t bring this fictional galaxy into the conversation to be flippant about reality—on the contrary, I’m writing this because I take art so seriously. And I should note that I don’t see Star Wars as some sort of bastion of leftist art. But when we reduce or elevate Star Wars to that rebellion vs. fascism dichotomy, I find it incredibly applicable to both the past and present landscape in Palestine. How can you not make those connections? Do we just watch Star Wars because it looks cool?

I refuse to let the art I consume remain static in the aesthetic. Why don’t we believe what it’s saying? More to the point—do we only believe art when it’s romanticizing the past (or the future) and not when it applies to the present? I think we should sit with that question. Because it also applies to countless rebellion narratives from all fictional media, not to mention our relationship with real-world history.

Maybe pinning all this weight on an escapist space fantasy is asking too much, but I also don’t think it’s a mental stretch to connect Star Wars/Vietnam to Palestine. All are about imperial overreach and large-scale violence. The heroes are groups of resisting people, who happen to be much more diverse (in Star Wars: humans from all walks of life, aliens, respected robots) in contrast to the homogeneity of the oppressive empire (in Star Wars: almost entirely human, mostly white, mostly male, particularly at the top).

Also, I started writing this a month before the current rise of student encampments around the United States. I wish history *wasn’t* repeating itself, but the reminiscence of current protests to those in Kent State 54 years ago (or all the other Vietnam protests[18](#footnote-18)) is obvious, with imperialists/neoliberals alike both calling for “non-violent” protests without actually assessing the violence *being* protested. On May 4th (if you can believe that date), 1970, the National Guard killed four Kent State students protesting the Vietnam War. Days later (still May), police with shotguns killed one man and one high schooler during protests at Jackson State College. I think liberal circles look back on the Vietnam protests as tragic yet important. But as I write this, current leaders (yes, even democratic ones in “blue” states) have endorsed state violence toward Pro-Palestine encampments,[19](#footnote-19)[20](#footnote-20) [21](#footnote-21) and we’ve entered May 2024 with an eerily similar feeling to May 1970. Clearly the cyclical imperial machine is designed to protect itself from resistance. It’s like tragic poetry. It rhymes.

What will our art say about the genocide in Palestine 10 years from now, if it doesn’t choose to ignore it? If history repeats itself, so does Hollywood—it doesn’t take a prophet to predict how film (even Star Wars) will likely romanticize Palestinian resistance, or at least romanticize protest from the white-savior, privileged perspective. Novels and textbooks too. It all rhymes.

But I *believe* rebellious fiction. I believe when it says that moral resistance to violence is honorable, and that it deserves to win. I can either align with this resistance now when it’s right or later when it’s easy, when the mainstream art machine catches up or when the system finds it profitable (if not ignored completely). I wrote this because I can’t let art live only in the nostalgic, because then it will be too late. It’s already too late. I refuse to let myself only feel grief through entertainment as a telescope to yesterday, when so many are grieving today.

I refuse to let rebellion be only a moral necessity when written into high school history books or decade-later escapist fiction—resistance is a moral right *now*.
