*"One drop plus one drop makes a bigger drop, not two" (*Nostalghia*, 1983)*

Dear Liberal Friends,

I shifted away from your centrist politics not long ago, and I have to admit, I’m pretty terrible at this leftism thing.

I’m not good at organizing. I’m not good at community care. I *have* given mutual aid, but always outside my geographic area.

I’m also bad at this because I’m centering myself right now.

I’m bad at this because I’m “cerebral,” as my wife aptly said. I’d rather read a book than turn a doorknob. I’m stuck in theory and words—I have piss-poor praxis. I’m a slacktivist. There are no sores on the soles of my feet, so I’m sure I have blood on my hands. More than I already do, anyway.

I have no practical skills. My social skills are egregious. I contribute little outside of my job or my family. I’m in a very red state and I don’t know where to start.

I’m a bad leftist because at times I hide behind the label instead of reckoning with white supremacy in my surroundings and in myself, and white supremacy is what really won this election.

I am quick to judge your liberal politics, despite knowing shame serves no one, and despite being a former Democrat myself. I’m a hypocrite.

I’m a bad leftist.

Yet I’m better at this human thing than I used to be—this is where my hope lies. It’s egotistical, I’m sure, but in this current moment when hope is hard to grasp, hope in the one human I know best is all I have. Or, put another way: I have hope because I can barely recognize my past self (and not just because he has short hair and zero tattoos).

I’m not one for quoting Ernest Hemingway ever, let alone off-the-cuff, but as Hemingway once (supposedly) said:

> “There is nothing noble in being superior to your fellow men; true nobility is being superior to your former self.”

I can’t stand my former self, he can’t stand me, and that gives me hope.

In 2008 I voted for McCain. I thought men-kissing-men was a real evil in the world. I thought climate change was a hoax, because I read a book recommended by my old, white history professor. And “Palestine” was just a word I heard on Glenn Beck’s radio show.

In 2012, my family shared a drive up American Fork Canyon to see the newly colorful leaves, our annual Bob Rossian quest. On the way back into the valley, my dad drove us along a snaking road, with yellow-orange-red blurs moving past the window. He calmly said, “I wouldn’t call Obama the anti-Christ, but. . . .” I didn’t think twice about it. When Mitt Romney lost, my grandma sent out an email to the whole family about how she spent the night in tears, about how close the Second Coming of Christ must be now. Her words felt slightly exaggerated.

In 2015, I realized the media was infantilizing men, like me, and that it objectified women. I confessed to my wife that I might be a feminist, a dirty little secret. I started to see sexism everywhere. My world was no longer upright, and I got existential whiplash. The next year, I voted for Hillary Clinton, but few people knew I had—almost everyone I knew voted for the other guy, and I could not understand why.

In 2020, my childhood best friend, now a cop, ghosted me forever after I spoke up about Ahmaud Arbery, even though I never even said “ACAB.” I went to two rallies for Black lives, one of which took place right outside the Provo police precinct. The rally was organized to build bridges with white cops, and the rally had the *blessing* of white cops. Someone there held up a sign that said “Cops started as slave patrols,” but I ignored it, trying to listen to a speech from the top cop (a man from my old neighborhood) who basically said racism is bad and we could work together to fix it. I thought we fixed something that day, because white supremacy is also lazy.

I voted for Biden, and I celebrated when he won.

In 2024, my world is no longer upright, again. Rather, a world I thought was three dimensional has now been given multidimensional shape. I can’t stop learning about Palestine. I can’t unsee my complicity as a citizen of the United States. I won’t shut up about it online, and most people seem to ignore me, rather than fight me, and it somehow feels worse. I know, they had an election to think about.

**—**

I hope I’m not sharing this as a pat on the back, but as a reminder to myself I will never arrive morally. If I’m being arrogant again, I’m sorry. If I’m being a patriarchal Old Man Winters again, someone who claims he “knows best,” I’m sorry.

I write this because I want us all to remember that people change, and that is hopeful. I’m not necessarily hopeful that the worst humans you know will change, but I do hope that maybe the best humans among us—the reluctant ones, the latently good ones—just need some help shedding the spiritually dead parts, like old bits of skin. I think that’s what I have needed; if I’m always growing, then parts of me are always dying.

I’m not great at leftism. Maybe I won’t ever be.

But I need you to know that I’m going to keep trying. I try because I believe in people power. I try because I truly believe in human rights. I try because the teachers here on the left are more moral, more practical, and damn smarter and braver than I am. I think *you* are these things too, which is why I’m addressing this to you.

What I hope for, in the aftermath of 2024’s election news, in the portentous storm before the superstorm, is that we learn the right lessons. I hope your rage makes you radical, gives you momentum in the best direction for your own humanity. I hope the same for me. I hope I can fight white supremacy and misogyny with more efficacy.

Most of you, my close friends, have labeled yourself as liberals. If you’re like me, you thought liberal meant “politically left” because that’s what they told you. Most of you voted “blue no matter who.” And I’m sorry if I ever shamed you for it, or acted morally superior, when in reality I was of the same political persuasion just yesterday. I don’t know what I’m doing on the left, but I’d love it if you stumble around in the dark next to me, instead of defending powerful leaders who don’t care about you, who pretend to be on your side yet keep moving right—will you be honest about that? I’d love it if you search for real answers with me, rather than blaming the next presidency on minorities and anti-genocide protestors like some of your friends have. I hope we save our finger pointing for the powerful.

Even if we find different answers in the end, even if you decide to remain a Democrat post-2024, even if you never understand me, I hope you feel safe enough to ask more honest questions.

I hope you ask why the leaders don’t seem to care about you, even when you care so much about them. Why can’t they keep any of us safe? What did they promise four years ago, and what happened to those promises? Why didn’t you have a say in who the candidate would be? Why does it feel like you don’t have a say in any direction this country goes? Why doesn’t any money or help make its way to people you actually care about? I really hope you ask why [they bargained the lives of children overseas for the possibility of cheaper groceries.](https://www.caitlinjohnst.one/p/the-only-good-thing-about-this-nightmare) Why did we listen to that without question? Why didn’t she listen to our questions then? Why did the DNC decide to shake hands with war criminals? And were you okay with it?

If we’re all trees, then why do we keep voting for the axe? Because its handle is made of wood? (As the Turkish proverb says.) Why do we think we’ll be spared, when the horizon is already littered with stumps?

[It’s really scary over here](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_boomerang), but it’s been scary for much longer outside our borders—in fact, it’s been much scarier for much longer outside the United States *because* of our borders.

We need each other. “One drop plus one drop makes a bigger drop, not two.” Yes, it’s going to get dark here. It already is and has been. I don’t know where I’m going, and I could really use your flashlight.

Sincerely,

Your bumbling leftist friend
