It’s 2013, and our car starts making a strange sound. I have been at my book editing job for almost a year, my first “real” job out of college. My wife, Valerie, and I have a baby now. I am supposed to be in full adult mode, and the vehicle issue feels like a mark on my attempts at adulting perfection. I know nothing about cars, but the auto repair shop in Springville fixes the hiccup with little issue. However, now I can’t drive ten minutes to the movie theater without significant anxiety. Driving thirty minutes to my parents’ place is torture. Anything more than that is unspeakable. Driving brings tightness in my chest and overwhelming, catastrophizing thoughts. But the worst symptom is the praying.

Jumping back, it’s September 1999. I remember the month because it was right before my twelfth birthday. My parents and my older brother are watching *The Sixth Sense*, rented from Hollywood Video in American Fork, Utah. I’m in the next room over, trying to watch something more light-hearted on the other TV, maybe some *Whose Line is It Anyway?* But all I hear is the ghost story, and I glean enough from just the sounds to be terrified. I’ve always been sensitive. I feel sick for a week, even on my birthday—thoughts racing, an unending stomach ache, nightmares. But the worst symptom is the praying.

*My twelfth birthday (1999). I’ve been scared for a week.*

It’s 2001, I’m a teenager now, and the adults in church have started teaching us more and more about the dangers of “lustful” thoughts, and we’ve memorized Matthew 5:28: “But I say unto you, That whosoever looketh on a woman to lust after her hath committed adultery with her already in his heart.” In my Book of Mormon, I’ve also underlined in red Alma 39:5, which literally says that sexual impurity is second only to murder. Both my church leaders and the scriptures are telling me often that sexual thoughts are anathema to Mormon heaven.

I already have a tendency toward obsessive thoughts, and my perspective on girls has become the fabled pink elephant analogy, but on overdrive and without me understanding it fully: if you think about girls, even one time, you’re a sinner, and sinners don’t go to heaven unless they repent. Of course I think about girls. I can’t stop because I’m thirteen and they’re cute, but nobody at church tells me I’m normal. Instead, I’m fixated, and these thoughts make my chest tighten, give me tunnel vision. I repent every day. I think it will help, but the worst symptom is the praying.

It’s 2010, I’m 23, and I’m following every rule. No alcohol, coffee, or tea. No tattoos. No swearing (don’t even listen to music with swear words). No masturbation or sex. No R-rated movies (even as an adult). The fast forward button is wearing off the remote. A dozen Mormon object lessons come to mind—no cockroach in the ice cream sundae; keep the truck as far from the cliff’s edge as possible; don’t let go of the iron rod. One step out of line, one rule broken (one rule even cracked), and something bad will come.

*Wedding day (2010), at an LDS/Mormon temple.*

I got to the temple for the first time. A family friend tells me afterward, “The weirdness in the temple is how you know it’s true.” I nod my head.

I have to go back to the temple often. I have to get married there. I have to go to church every Sunday. I have to take the sacrament. I have to read my scriptures every day, adding more and more red lines. Gotta keep praying, or something bad will happen. If I don’t pray for my car to keep working, it will *absolutely* stop working. If I don’t pray for our house to be safe, a criminal will break into the house. If I don’t pray over my homework, I’ll fail my class. If I don’t pray, if I don’t pray, if I don’t pray, then heaven can’t stop hell.[1](#footnote-1)

It’s 2013, and a neighbor, visiting our apartment for a spiritual lesson, comments about my shyness, surely out of love. He says he grew up quiet, but one day he just decided to stop being shy. He’s charismatic, a leader. He’s funny, but avoids loud laughter. People like to be around him. He’s the model Mormon man. He looks at people in the eye, shakes their hand. He makes me think of Joseph Smith. I’m not offended, but I feel ashamed that I can’t find the same switch that he found, to turn off the shyness and turn on the prophet-like charm. I’ve been trying. I’ve been praying.

My car has its hiccup then, but I can’t see the pattern yet. I can’t see prayer as a symptom. I can only pray.

It’s 2014. My sister left the church a few years ago (a true pioneer), and Kate Kelly and the [Ordain Women](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordain_Women) movement is a massive topic in the Mormon sphere. I use the opportunity to spout my own juvenile, counter-philosophy on Facebook: “something something women are better than men so that’s why men need the priesthood.” The phrase *benevolent sexism* isn’t in my vernacular, yet it is still in my programming. Still, when the church [excommunicates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_membership_council) Kate Kelly, I’m surprised by the overriding feeling of confusion. I realize I’m confused because the church, my good church surely, has barred someone from heaven, my good Mormon heaven surely, all because of some very vulnerable questions.

It’s 2015, and I become fascinated by feminism. I keep it hidden from Valerie for a few months, because feminism is a bad word in mainstream Mormonism. I start to read secretly and religiously (!) the [Feminist Mormon Housewives](https://www.feministmormonhousewives.org/) blog. I read a post where the author describes being uncomfortable in the temple, and commenters corroborate. Nobody likes the sexist language in the temple ceremonies, and I first admit to myself that the temple makes *me* uncomfortable too—the weirdness is just weird. Those Feminist Mormon Housewives have given me permission to feel what I feel. These are my first feminist professors. They don’t know me, because I am too scared to participate (and I’m just as quiet on the Internet), but they have changed me.

I tell Valerie, nervously, “I think I might be becoming a feminist.” She replies, “Oh, I probably am too.”

It’s 2020 now. I am increasingly at odds spiritually/mentally with the church I grew up in—I want to make it work, but it isn’t working for me, it isn’t aligning with *my* values. Covid-19 hits, church services go online, and I realize I can breathe on Sundays. No more masking, trying to be socially adept, trying to make eye contact and shake hands, trying to be like Joseph Smith. No more rigid roles.

My old college friend Jess posts online about how she and her husband gave their child a joint blessing (rather than a priesthood, man-only blessing), and this feels massive, a gorgeous feminist act that, in a perfect world, should have been no big deal. But in Mormonism, it is a big deal for a woman to participate in her baby’s rite. She shares another Instagram account, [Life After Mormonism](http://www.instagram.com/life.aftermormonism), and I see how so many people are just as happy outside Mormonism as others are within it. I realize I’ve been lied to: the church teaches me that true happiness outside the church is impossible. That I just need to keep praying, keep adding red to my scriptures, keep going to the temple (because the weirdness is how you know it’s true).

Feminists, on the other hand, are simultaneously teaching me that my upbringing can be a mixture of good and bad. They teach me that all emotions are valuable and normal for all genders (even anxiety, even anger), that poor mental health isn’t a sign of my own badness, that church discussions should allow for more uncomfortable emotions (I have never had a lesson among men about our mental health). They teach me, every time, that Mormon feminists are my favorite people. They teach me that intersectional equality will be a lifetime pursuit, but a good, necessary one. They teach me that the stereotype of “angry feminist” is reductive—anger is powerful, and it comes from a place of compassion. All I ever see from feminists is vulnerable compassion. Kindness over niceness. They give me hope, whether they are attending members, or former members, or never-members.

I pray again. This time I pray about how the church’s teachings are killing me, and the good no longer outweighs the bad for me. I call to god to let him know that I don’t want to go back to church again, that I don’t want Sundays to feel painful, that I no longer believe, that the church is hurting women and marginalized people and even men too, anyone who doesn’t fit the mold—and everything in my head goes quiet. Not the “stupor of thought” quiet I’d been told all my life was a sign of Satan’s influence. Just quiet. A peace I’d been looking for everywhere, and that failed me in prayer, in the scriptures, in the Mormon temple.

My brain isn’t quiet in all areas. But I don’t feel the compulsion to pray the bad things away. My car might break down, prayer or not. I’ll deal with it if it does.

It’s 2024, and feminism helped me out of the church almost four years ago. My brilliant writer friend [Kristi Boyce](http://www.instagram.com/yokizzi) and I talk about all the movies we missed as attending Mormons. My friend KC and I talk about tattoos. My friend Dani and I talk about breaking cycles of parenting. My sister Lindsay and I talk about growing up in the church. My friend Kami, among so many others I admire, comments her powerful outrage on the church’s social media account after their most recent [sexist remarks](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/03/22/us/mormon-church-women-latter-day-saints.html). I read the backlash from women and queer people on the church’s post, and I listen to Valerie’s frustration. I only see compassion in their resistance, and I’m happy and lucky to know them and know of them.

I would never say Mormonism created my religious scrupulosity, but it certainly exacerbated it. And I know I’m not the only one. I thought praying was keeping me safe. I thought it was a shield and a sword. But praying kept me spiraling downward.
