I woke up today, Father’s Day, to a pink morning, with all my kids enjoying *Barbie* cartoons. They gave me a toy as a gift—it also happened to be pink. I have two boys; one is a preteen, and many would say he not only “should” be grown out of the *Barbie* show, but by their standards he should never have watched it in the first place.

I try to avoid patting myself on the back about such things, because striving for gender equitability should be the minimum, not worthy of any accolades, not to mention *Barbie* is still incredibly consumerist—baby steps, right? I’m not a perfect father.

But a part of me still wants to give some of us dads a brief pat on the back today because the Patriarchal Papas (new band name) will absolutely avoid giving us any awards for being “soft,” for working on ourselves and our systems, for prioritizing emotional intelligence. And we certainly won’t get any free cookies for rejecting imperial propaganda—yet we should reject it anyway.

(“Oh, here we go again, just like on [Star Wars Day](https://sweaterweather.substack.com/p/do-we-believe-star-wars), Casey is connecting what should be a Rockwellian holiday to the current international horrors.” I’ll try not to do this every holiday, but here we are.)

In the United States in 1972, war criminal Richard Nixon declared Father’s Day a national holiday. Today, in 2024, war criminals Joe Biden and Kamala Harris posted on social media celebrating “the fathers” and “all fathers,” respectively. Military and political accounts will likely do the same; I won’t link to them, but on their organizations’ Instagram accounts today, both the U.S. Navy and the U.S. Army posted their own special flavor of militarized paternal platitudes. (I have no idea what The Orange One is posting about today.) Maybe my personal algorithm is indistinguishable from just a few years ago, but I also remember constant “Military father returns home to surprise son/daughter on Father’s Day” posts happening like clockwork every June. I’m guessing these videos are happening again today.

I know you don’t get to pick your father, and the children of military fathers have genuine emotions upon seeing their dads again, but the more we can deconstruct the United States Miliary/Police Industrial Complex, and patriarchy at large, the better fathers we will be, because for far too long “good” fatherhood has been linked to traits prized by the U.S. empire but detrimental to community building—punishment over restorative teaching (think war in general, or think the capitalized prison system)[1](#footnote-1); physical labor over emotional labor (think the value we place on athletes compared to teachers/education); logic at odds with humanity (think the equation of “soft” skills with all women and “hard” skills with all men); *quid pro quo* in all relationships (think white knights in feminism, benevolent sexism); indirect support as a worthy substitute for direct support (think breadwinning as a replacement, not a supplement, to one-on-one time with one’s kids); hierarchy in *everything*, for crying out loud (from homes to schools to offices, not to mention the structures of government). In men, we all know which traits have been romanticized and which have been quashed. And that list goes on. What have these traits, as imperatives for all men and systems, given us but more war, broken communities over sustained ones? I know I’m not saying anything new that feminists haven’t already said and taught—women writers like Angela Davis or Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie are just two among many who have done the hard labor to shape my ideas of feminism and therefore good fatherhood, in addition to the women in my life. I’m late to the game, and I can always do better at giving my mentors and teachers their just dues.

Yet maybe my fatherly peers need to learn it or to be reminded of it, because I needed to remind myself of it today especially: being good to your own people is not worthy of any special praise. I’m not going to lionize “good” cops or soldiers who are good dads, because there’s nothing extraordinary about taking care of your own or of people in your tribe. And when certain men attack people outside their tribe (such as transgender folks), and their loved ones say “but he is a good man,” it’s not too surprising anymore, because we understand the psychological/sociological ease of aiming goodness toward one’s own insular community—plenty of evil people in history were good to their families and neighbors. So when politicians and military branches (or any celebrities, corporations, etc.) wish a happy day to “the fathers” today, the emotionally bankrupt double-speak and tribalism is doing a lot of heavy lifting. They are only speaking about the fathers inside their in-group/clan/people, nationally or politically. But that’s the easy shit. The separation is psychologically necessary to them, so that they can wish a “happy father’s day” to “all” fathers, while simultaneously aiding and assisting genocides (plural) outside their neighborhood. Otherwise the cognitive dissonance becomes overwhelming.

With all the honesty and passion I have, as a father, I reject their platitudes, particularly when I’ve seen images and videos online of the dead children in Palestine, Sudan, and the Congo, many times held by fathers and grandfathers. There’s no going back on what we know—our social media feed is full of parents holding their dying and dead children. We’ve seen the way the West continues to vilify all Arab men as terrorists. I reject Father’s Day’s colonial cliches now because, if those non-American men’s children are “lucky” enough to *merely* be injured and traumatized, they likely are now [orphans](https://www.telegraph.co.uk/global-health/terror-and-security/gaza-17000-orphans-palestine-israel-hamas-war/). Dear dads of the U.S. (cop dads, military dads, ordinary “Proud to be an American” dads), you don’t like Hamas? What do you think Palestinian orphans, displaced by joint United States-Israeli war crimes, are likely to know and do when they grow up? Have you little understanding beyond the surface-level dichotomy/lie of terrorist (bad dad) and soldier (good dad)? Resistance is inevitable and necessary, as a clear and direct result of imperialists speaking things like “happy father’s day” with their tongues while attempting to eradicate real fatherhood with their arms.

Perhaps you’d like to ask why I’d muddy the waters on a perfectly wholesome holiday. I’m not saying don’t celebrate Father’s Day. I like today—my kids made me cards and gave me an astronaut toy. We ate ice cream sandwiches too early in the morning, and later we’ll eat pizza and watch a favorite movie, likely about a dad.[2](#footnote-2) But I think we can only call ourselves real dads if we’re aware of and empathetic toward children outside our own houses. What a waste of fatherhood to do anything less.

I have hope that we can be “softer” than we’ve been. I’m not muddying the waters—patriarchal imperialism already poisoned and militarized fatherhood. And my one job, if I only have one job, is to work alongside my family community to reach for the real antidotes.
