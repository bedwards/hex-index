Let us dispense with the euphemisms. The philosophy job market isn’t a market. It is instead a guild protected by the requirement for credentials, and is oversupplied and structurally insulated—at the moment, anyway—from meaningful feedback. In that sense, it is a system that masquerades as meritocratic while rewarding conformity, identity signaling, and knowing the right people.

Before getting too far into the weeds, I must clarify that I write this post as someone who—luckily for me—won the philosophy job market. A few years ago, fortunately, I got a full-time teaching position—teaching-track, similar to tenure-track—at Arizona State University. And I love my job! I am proud of my students and my research, and I genuinely believe ASU is one of the rare universities doing important work in public-facing philosophy.

However, unfortunately none of that changes the fact that the hiring process in higher education—and especially in fields like philosophy—is deeply flawed and broken, even for someone like me with an excellent record of research and teaching. Even with a stellar academic resume—stellar!—like myself, the path was arbitrary, demoralizing, and inefficient to an astonishing degree, even for people who expect it to be a total and complete disaster. **Looking for a job in academic philosophy—especially during a global pandemic—was like looking for work at the height of unemployment during the Great Depression. No exaggeration required here.**

This post is an attempt to describe that system from the inside, and to demonstrate how it actually operates even for someone like me who publishes lots of great research, extensive teaching experience across several colleges and states, and the recipient of many research and (especially) teaching awards.

**1\. It Isn’t a Market**

Markets allocate goods and labor through decentralized feedback using prices that adjust to scarcity, demand responds to value, and incentives drive innovation. The philosophy job market does none of that. It is a guild administered by committees who face no cost for bad decisions and no penalty for inefficiency. And **[as we know from economics](https://www.econlib.org/library/Essays/hykKnw.html)**, this is a recipe for inefficiency and bloat.

One of the major problems is that universities continue to mint PhDs far beyond what the market can absorb. Graduate programs, motivated by institutional incentives (cheap labor and departmental prestige), have no reason to contract supply. The result is predictable: a generation of highly trained scholars competing for a handful of positions, while administrators point to “market forces” as if they were describing an equilibrium rather than a controlled burn.

**2\. The Incentive Problem**

As an economist would say, the incentive structure is broken in academia. Search committees face no cost for rejecting qualified applicants. Departments can post job ads that require impossible combinations of specialization and breadth because there is always—until there isn’t—a pool of desperate candidates willing to fit themselves to the mold because academic jobs, if you can land one, are great.

The screenshots of messages, posts, and rejections show how regular this has become: the same form letter, the same polite phrasing — “We received an unusually large number of highly qualified applications…” — which is true every year. However, in a real market, such an imbalance would correct itself via price fluctuations and better incentives to avoid bloat in the academic labor market. Wages would rise, or programs would contract. In academia, neither happens because it isn’t a market. In that regard, the results speak for themselves. The result is a system that hoards information instead of distributing it, and punishes the kind of independent thought that markets are supposed to reward.

**3\. My Own Case Study in Contingency and Improbability**

I spent three years on the market from 2019 through 2022. And in accordance with any rational standard, I was (and **[continue to be](https://drive.google.com/file/d/13JiWiQAE7xlaMphilffpXI8SBomsB5Gu/view?usp=sharing)**) a strong candidate—not top tier, but more than strong enough for the majority of jobs to which I applied. At the time, I had 17 peer-reviewed articles—many in solid second-tier journals—along with a book contract, teaching experience at three universities across several states, including several awards for research and teaching, and a thriving public-philosophy portfolio through Substack and other public outlets.

I had been publishing in peer-reviewed venues since 2011 — well before AI writing tools existed — and my work had been publicly praised by senior philosophers from across subfields since at the least the beginning of my doctoral studies. I won a research award in 2014 for graduate students in a nation wide competition. I had teaching awards, strong letters, interdisciplinary work in political economy. And yet despite all of that, *I still barely made it.*

I’ll say that again: **I still barely made it.**

The pandemic wiped out my first tenure-track offer — a job in Baton Rouge that was rescinded after a hiring freeze. Another search ended abruptly when the university canceled all on-campus interviews mid-cycle. In total, I had about over two dozen preliminary interviews, a handful of finals, and months of silence in between.

When I finally received an offer from ASU, I knew how contingent, and more to the point, how unlikely it was. It could have easily not happened had I said the wrong thing or not answered the right question. There are simply too many qualified candidates, among other reasons, for any one candidate to be a lock on a job (except in special cases). The difference between “employed” and “failed academic” was razor-thin. That should not be the case in a profession that calls itself meritocratic.

***4\. Why Even Strong Candidates Fail***

After a few years of postmortem reflection — and revisiting the correspondence now archived in those screenshots — and just in time for the next academic job market to get underway, I’ve realized there are three main reasons highly qualified candidates fail to land positions.

**a. Overqualification Anxiety**

Many departments — especially smaller or teaching-focused ones — view candidates who are highly productive as a potential flight risk. Academic jobs, once vacated via either retirement or migration, need not be refilled. It is increasingly common that when such jobs become vacant, for whatever reason, they are far less likely to be renewed. So, the worry is that they would hire me, I would leave them for a better position a few years down the line — because I am super productive — and then they would be left with one fewer professor slot, and less institutional clout, moving forward. Academic job committees often want a candidate with ambition perfectly calibrated to their budgetary ceiling. It is another reason for the decline of higher education—it used to be the intellectual cutting edge, and now it is more interested in protecting stuffy ideas than pressing the intellectual boundaries.

**b. Demographic Politics**

The screenshots of job ads requiring diversity statements are not exaggerations. Some postings devote more space to diversity talking points—many of which are not only insulting to the idea of a labor market, but sometimes insult the very people they are meant to protect and cultivate—than to the actual job duties. Others explicitly list demographic categories as desirable traits.

During the 2020–2022 cycle, being perceived as a straight white male—however inaccurate that may be—was a liability. That is the unfortunate reality—something that even people in my graduating department warned me about. And the faculty in question who warned me were not white or male. Departments were under immense internal and external pressure to diversify—still are these days, but from a different direction—using crude and often inaccurate or unjust demographic targeting rather than genuine inclusion.

This is not to deny the value of diversity, but to highlight that execution has become performative and self-undermining in academia—not to mention eroding the trust that the taxpayers, parents, and students place (and want to place) in higher education. When identity replaces intellectual contribution as a hiring criterion, the result is neither justice nor excellence. This is virtue signaling passing as intellectual rigor, at least in the worst cases. There are, of course, many exceptions to this trend across higher education, but they are still too few.

**c. Aesthetic and Health Bias**

The third reason is less ideological but equally corrosive: people do not like fat people as a general rule. When I was on the market, I weighed over 500 pounds—though that number has decreased dramatically since. (And no, not Ozempic. Too poor for that.) Some committees may have had some legitimate reasons to be worried, but they likely also had illegitimate reasons to discriminate against me.

I do not hold that against them as individuals — bias is a human reflex that I see even in myself sometimes — but it is worth highlighting. Even though mostly unspoken and tacit, **[factors like charm and beauty shape outcomes](https://www.the-pamphlet.com/articles/affirmative-action-for-hidden-merit)**. And in academia, where the job supposedly involves the mind, it is revealing that I didn’t get the job—in some cases, not in all or even most cases—because I either had poorer health or I was harder to look at. It is something **[I have faced at various times](https://jimmyalfonsolicon.substack.com/p/the-philosophy-of-weight-gain)** in my life depending on how overweight (or not) I am at a given time.

If two scholars achieve comparable results, and one does so while carrying a visible social stigma, the latter has demonstrated more resilience not less. Unfortunately this fails to convince academic job committees to see it that way.

**5\. The Moral Confusion of Academia**

The screenshots of emails, posts, and message from people who have personally praised my work capture the contradiction at the heart of this system. Academia claims to reward excellence but routinely punishes it when it threatens comfort or hierarchy.

A number of the committees who either didn’t interview me or hire me were chaired by faculty who know my work well. By academics over the years who cited my work, complimented, or wrote me to ask questions about it. Yet they passed without even granting a preliminary interview at times. I am not foolish enough to think that I was deserving of an interview in every case, but results taken together speak volumes. That is because the philosophy job “market” is a biased and inefficient guild that is more concerned with protecting the membership than fairness or efficiency.

The philosophy profession congratulates itself on its commitment to justice, fairness, and truth, but the hiring process contradicts these ideals. Economists would call this a coordination failure. Philosophers should call it hypocrisy.

**6\. Structural Inefficiency**

A properly functioning market transmits information by way of prices about which skills are valuable, what behaviors are rewarded, what norms promote success. The academic market transmits almost none. Candidates receive boilerplate rejections, no feedback, and no explanation for why they were cut despite hundreds of hours of preparation and submission reduced to a two-word epitaph.

Committees avoid transparency because transparency would expose the arbitrariness of their decisions. They often avoiding disclosing salaries or explaining their hiring decisions. The result is a system that violates basic economic principles—no price feedback, no consumer accountability, no supply constraint, and no measurable productivity signal.

**7\. The Bottom Line**

As I have explained, the evidence—taken as a whole—highlights why the philosophy job “market” traffics in discrimination both in terms of demographics and aesthetics in reasons both good and bad. It is **[highly inefficient](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691196459/the-case-against-education?srsltid=AfmBOor7WVVuXL3SiC3TzCGdVi5Sy41iW83Gmw8cM4uvoyUl-lBx_dTG)** wasting enormous amounts of human and financial capital that could be better used elsewhere, and would be in a proper market. It is a deceptive by branding itself as meritocratic—it is, but impartial and in bad ways—while instead performing many gatekeeping functions for the higher education cartel.

There are good people and good schools in higher education. There are people who sit on job search committees doing their best to be fair and just. I have no doubt about that. The issue is not with individual people or schools. It is a system of institutions with perverse incentives to be more transparent and efficient and to produce fewer doctorates in the field.

If philosophers want to talk seriously about justice, they should start with their own hiring practices. If we want to teach students about epistemic humility, we should model it in our institutions. And if we want to keep calling ourselves a discipline, we should stop running it like a lottery with robes. And that is why the philosophy job ‘market’ is total bullshit.
