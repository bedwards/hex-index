*I won’t really pay you 10K to sincerely believe in God.* But the idea does prompt the following thought experiment:

> Suppose Beth *sincerely* believes that there is no God. However, Sam offers Beth ten-thousand dollars to *sincerely* believe in God. Would God object to this?

Here we are assuming that God wants people to believe in Him. This is because, among other reasons, He wants a relationship with his creations, namely humans. One cannot have a relationship with someone if they believe that they do not exist. Merely believing that someone exists isn’t enough to ensure that one can have a relationship with them; however, it does look like a necessary component—even if it isn’t enough, i.e., one must believe in someone exists *who actually does*—of having a relationship with someone is that one believes they exist. One couldn’t, by example, have a relationship with someone if they believed that that person was dead and gone forever, or they never existed in the first place (e.g. Santa Claus).

The first problem, though, with our thought experiment is that it doesn’t look like people have direct control over what they believe. We cannot, like the flip of a switch, decide we believe that ‘2+2=5’, no matter how much money someone offers us to sincerely believe it. It doesn’t seem possible: we appear to spontaneously believe, without the operation of the conscious will, as to what or whether we will believe. As the philosopher, William Alston (1988) **[explains](https://andrewmbailey.com/wpa/DeontologicalConceptionEpistemicJustification.pdf)**, to think we have direct control over whether we believe something, it would,

> \[Have\] to be true that I have effective voluntary control over whether I do or do not believe that the tree has leaves on it when I see a tree with leaves on it just before me in broad daylight with my eyesight working perfectly. And it is perfectly clear that in this situation I have no power at all to refrain from that belief \[…\] We have just as little voluntary control over ordinary beliefs formed by introspection, memory, and simple uncontroversial inferences (264).

And if Alston, and a number of other philosophers, is right, then it doesn’t look like we can pay someone, who is a sincere atheist, to willing reverse course and sincerely believe in God. The intuition behind this objection is roughly to think of believing as similar to flipping a light on: assuming the light bulb works, and the power is flowing, we have direct control over whether the light is on or off; we simply flip the switch, one way or the other, to toggle between turning the lights on and off. If we think about acquiring and discarding beliefs similarly, it will strike us as psychologically impossible for people acquire, by sheer act of will, a sincere belief in God, where before they sincerely believed God didn’t exist. This view is roughly known as ***[doxastic involuntarism](https://iep.utm.edu/doxa-vol/)***: we lack the control required to change our beliefs at will. So it looks like the thought experiment from above is doomed to fail from the start.

There are, though, a couple reasons to doubt this.

First, we need not accept the light-switch model of belief when thinking about paying someone to sincerely believe in God. Often when I ask people what they think about bribing a sincere atheist to sincerely believe in God, they assume that the idea behind the thought experiment is that the atheist must change belief instantaneously, on the spot. However, that is nowhere stated in the thought experiment. One way that someone could, perhaps, change from sincerely rejecting the existence of God to sincerely accepting the existence of God would be to research the idea. Suppose that Beth, who grew up in an atheist household, is a sincere atheist who lacks good reasons for her atheism—she just always sincerely accepted atheism because that is what her parents believed. However, Sam offers her 10k to sincerely believe in God, and thereby motivates her to seek out theistic arguments and evidence, talk to intelligent and well-educated people who believe in God, and so forth. And at the end of her intellectual journey, and finding many convincing reasons to believe in God, she comes to sincerely believe. This is but one possible way we could pay someone to sincerely believe in God—it doesn’t involve flipping a belief-switch, but it does suggest that Beth have *some* control over her beliefs.

Second, it could be we have *indirect* control over what we believe, even if we lack direct control like we have flipping on a light switch. Suppose Beth, when offered 10K to sincerely believe in God, decides to start attending church, praying regularly, reading the bible and other religious texts, and avoids her overly atheistic friends. It isn’t clear why, after some time passed, Beth couldn’t *cultivate* sincere belief in God—this looks possible, at least. This point was made by the famous French philosopher, Blaise Pascal, who proposed what has come to be known as *Pascal’s Wager*: the rough idea being that one should believe in God, rather than not, since were God to exist, and one believed in Him, they would be rewarded with a heavenly afterlife; but were God to exist, and they didn’t believe, they would be damned. Among the criticism of this pragmatic argument for belief in God, critics claimed the Wager didn’t make sense because people cannot control their beliefs. Pascal responded, in ***[The Pensées](https://books.google.com/books/about/Pensees.html?id=SYkb0WuLfwAC)*** (French for ‘thoughts’), that,

> \[You\] would like to attain faith, and do not know the way; you would like to cure yourself of unbelief, and ask the remedy for it. Learn of those who have been bound like you, and who now take all their possessions. These are people who know the way which you would follow, and who are cured of an ill of which you would be cured. Follow the way by which they began; by acting as if they believed, taking the holy water, having masses said, etc.

We have *some indirect* control over our beliefs in that we can, in *some* cases, cultivate beliefs that we lacked beforehand. People can become politically radicalized, slowly over time, by hanging out with likeminded people, consuming political propaganda, and so on. People can be brainwashed by cults, and radically change their beliefs over time, just by consuming cult literature, making cult friends, and etc. People voluntarily, even if only indirectly and gradually over time, change their beliefs regularly. Why would belief in God be different?

We thus have some reason to believe that, even if only indirectly, people exercise some control over some of what they believe. Perhaps this doesn’t apply to *every* belief one could have, but it looks like it could, for some people anyway, apply to their belief in God. However, that was only the first component of our thought experiment. The second part is whether God would object to or find problematic someone coming to sincerely believe on the basis of a bribe.

Some people think that God would object. After all, it may strike us initially as disingenuous to form a sincere belief in God on the basis of a bribe. Many people intuitively think such a process of coming to sincerely believe in God, for monetary compensation, would lack something genuine or authentic. However, despite the intuitive nature of this claim, it is hard to nail down exactly what would be wrong here. Take a different case: suppose Beth, as a sincere atheist, got struck on the head by a coconut, and by sheer chance, the event scrambled her brain such that she formed a sincere belief in God. Would God object to this too? It isn’t as clear.

Perhaps the intuition here can be stated as a **[wrong-reasons problem](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/papq.12089)**: even if Beth sincerely comes to believe in God, and jettisons her atheistic convictions, her sincere belief was based on the wrong kind of reasons. Belief in God shouldn’t be a matter of monetary compensation. It would be like, say, to sincerely falling in love with someone because one was bribed to. Would we want someone to fall in love with us, even sincerely, if they were paid to? Likely the answer would be negative; there is something inappropriate about sincerely falling in love because we were bribed. This wouldn’t likely be the basis of a healthy romantic relationship.

However, this may be too quick. Suppose Beth, in the process of investigating the possibility of believing in God, had a profound and powerful religious experience, such that she couldn’t help but believe in God—but where she, in the absence of the bribe, she wouldn’t have put in the effort that resulted in her having the religious experience. If Beth became a sincere believer, then it isn’t as clear why God would object to this. We may suppose that Beth, after her sincere conversion incentivized by a bribe, regularly attends church, prays, reads the Bible, lives an honest and upright life, and so forth—just like anyone else in her religious community who is sincere in their convictions. There doesn’t, on the face of it anyway, seem to be anything wrong with this—even if we admit that a bribe is what started the whole process off.

Consider that some people are born into religious families, and yet, over time, as they become adults, they become full-fledged members of their religious community. They sincerely believe, and act accordingly. We must point out here that, just like with the bribe in our thought experiment, it looks like someone in this position originally came to the faith for the wrong reasons: it isn’t a good reason to believe in God *simply because one’s parents believe*. However, even if their upbringing began the process of coming to have a robust and deep relationship with God, this fact, by itself, doesn’t appear to be something that would displease God given that they ended up in a robust and deep relationship with God. Even though they began exploring their relationship with God merely because they grew up in a religious household, *they ended up with an actual relationship with God.* We might think the same could be said, at least in principle, of Beth: even if a bribe started off her spiritual journey, it doesn’t mean that she couldn’t end up, at the end of the journey, with a proper and robust relationship with God, along with a sincere belief in Him.

So could we cultivate *sincere* belief in God on the basis of a bribe? Would God object to this? I’ve offered some reasons to think that we can cultivate a sincere theistic belief—even if doing so would be difficult—and to think God wouldn’t object to this in *every* case (if done properly). However, we may still think there is something amiss here. **So you tell me.**
