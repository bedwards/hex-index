Bad writing is cheap. Good writing costs time, energy, and patience.

I learned this the hard way when I was freelancing for a blockchain startup last year. The CEO sent me a "simple" brief for a whitepaper. Three pages of rambling thoughts, half-formed ideas, and technical jargon that meant nothing to anyone outside their engineering team. I spent two days trying to decode what he actually wanted me to write about.

When I finally delivered the first draft, he came back with extensive revisions. Turns out, he hadn't clarified his own thinking before giving me the assignment. His unclear brief cost both of us time we didn't have. The project that should have taken a week stretched into three.

Most people think writing is just typing words until you hit your target count. That's like thinking cooking is just throwing ingredients in a pot. You might end up with something edible, but it probably won't be good.

## We Resist Clarity Because It's Hard Work

[

*Photo by [ALAN DE LA CRUZ](https://unsplash.com/@alandelacruz4) on [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com)*

Here's what happens in most companies. Someone needs to communicate a decision, a strategy, or a process. They open their laptop, bang out whatever comes to mind, hit send, and consider the job done. Then three people interpret the message differently, two follow-up meetings get scheduled, and everyone wonders why nothing gets done efficiently.

I see this constantly in client work. A project manager sends a confusing email about requirements. The developer builds something that meets the literal description but misses the intent. The client gets frustrated because what they received doesn't match what they thought they asked for. Everyone blames everyone else, but the real problem was the original communication.

People resist spending time on clarity because they think it's optional. "They'll figure out what I mean" is the battle cry of lazy thinkers everywhere. But clarity isn't a nice-to-have feature of communication. When you skip it, you're forcing everyone else to do the work you should have done.

The most dangerous part isn't just that unclear writing wastes time. When you can't explain something clearly, you probably don't understand it yourself. I've caught this in my own work more times than I want to admit. I'll start writing about a concept, realize I'm circling around the point without landing on it, and discover that I need to think harder about what I'm actually trying to say.

## What Clarity Actually Costs

[

*Photo by [Denny Müller](https://unsplash.com/@redaquamedia) on [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com)*

Real clarity demands three payments that most people refuse to make.

Time is the obvious one. Good writing happens in revision, not in the initial brain dump. I do at least three passes on anything that matters. First pass is getting the thoughts out of my head and onto the page. Second pass is cutting everything that doesn't need to be there. Third pass is making sure the remaining words do their job efficiently. Most people stop after the first pass and wonder why their writing feels sluggish.

Energy is harder to see but just as real. Staying focused long enough to untangle confused thoughts is exhausting. Your brain wants to move on to the next task, not sit with the discomfort of not knowing exactly what you're trying to say. Clear thinking requires you to hold tension until the confusion resolves. That takes mental stamina most people would rather spend elsewhere.

The ego cost might be the highest. You have to delete sentences you spent time crafting. You have to admit that your first instinct wasn't good enough. You have to kill ideas that seemed brilliant when they first occurred to you but don't actually serve the reader. This hurts more than people expect.

*Photo by [Elena Mozhvilo](https://unsplash.com/@miracleday) on [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com)*

I worked with a startup founder who transformed his team's efficiency by changing how he wrote emails. Instead of stream-of-consciousness messages that required interpretation, he started spending an extra ten minutes making his requests specific and clear. The result was fewer questions, faster execution, and less frustration all around.

One email he sent before the change: "Hey team, we need to think about optimizing the user experience for our Q4 push. There are some issues with conversion that we should address. Let me know your thoughts on how we might improve things."

The same message after he started investing in clarity: "Our checkout conversion rate dropped 12% last month. I need three specific recommendations from each department by Friday: one quick fix we can implement this week, one medium-term improvement for next month, and one long-term strategy for Q4. Focus on the biggest friction points first."

The difference in response was dramatic. The first version generated a lot of back-and-forth questions and vague suggestions. The second version got him exactly what he needed with minimal follow-up. The extra ten minutes he spent clarifying his request saved his team hours of confused discussion.

Leaders who write clearly make faster decisions because they're forced to think clearly first. They build trust because people know exactly what they're asking for. They prevent expensive mistakes because there's less room for misinterpretation.

## How to Make the Investment

[

![what do you mean? text on gray surface](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1516246843873-9d12356b6fab?crop=entropy&cs=tinysrgb&fit=max&fm=jpg&ixid=M3wzMDAzMzh8MHwxfHNlYXJjaHw0fHxjb25mdXNpb258ZW58MHx8fHwxNzU2NDE5OTY0fDA&ixlib=rb-4.1.0&q=80&w=1080 "what do you mean? text on gray surface")



](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1516246843873-9d12356b6fab?crop=entropy&cs=tinysrgb&fit=max&fm=jpg&ixid=M3wzMDAzMzh8MHwxfHNlYXJjaHw0fHxjb25mdXNpb258ZW58MHx8fHwxNzU2NDE5OTY0fDA&ixlib=rb-4.1.0&q=80&w=1080)
*Photo by [Jon Tyson](https://unsplash.com/@jontyson) on [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com)*

The process starts with admitting that your first draft is almost certainly unclear. I don't care how experienced you are or how well you think you understand your subject. First drafts are for getting ideas out of your head, not for communicating them clearly to other people.

Draft fast without editing yourself. This stage is about extraction, not refinement. Get your thoughts down before they disappear. Don't worry about organization or flow or whether you're making sense. Just capture the raw material. I don’t know if you’ve heard of “Make it exist, then you can make it better” but that’s the guiding principle in this step.

Then cut ruthlessly. Most first drafts contain at least 30% unnecessary words. Every sentence should advance your main point. Every paragraph should have a clear purpose. If you can't explain why something belongs in your piece, delete it. This is where the ego cost becomes real, but it's also where clarity emerges.

Read everything aloud before you consider it finished. Your ear catches problems your eye misses. If you stumble over a sentence, your reader will too. If a paragraph feels choppy when spoken, it needs smoother transitions. If you run out of breath reading a sentence, break it into smaller pieces.

Put distance between writing and editing. Write today, edit tomorrow if possible. Your brain needs time to separate from what you intended to say so you can see what you actually wrote. This distance helps you catch assumptions, gaps in logic, and unclear references that seemed obvious when you were in the flow of writing.

## The Real Economics of Clear Communication

[

![two men and four women meeting in office](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1530099486328-e021101a494a?crop=entropy&cs=tinysrgb&fit=max&fm=jpg&ixid=M3wzMDAzMzh8MHwxfHNlYXJjaHwyOXx8Y29tbXVuaWNhdGlvbnxlbnwwfHx8fDE3NTY0NzI2OTh8MA&ixlib=rb-4.1.0&q=80&w=1080 "two men and four women meeting in office")

*Photo by [Akson](https://unsplash.com/@akson) on [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com)*

Stop thinking about editing time as waste. When you spend an extra hour clarifying your writing, you're preventing other people from spending multiple hours trying to decode your meaning. One person's investment in clarity saves everyone else time and confusion.

I calculated this once for a client project. The CEO was sending weekly strategy updates that consistently generated follow-up questions and required clarification calls. Each update took him about 30 minutes to write, but the resulting confusion cost his leadership team about four hours of collective time per week in meetings and email chains trying to figure out what he meant.

When he started spending 90 minutes on each update to make them clear and comprehensive, the follow-up questions disappeared. The time investment shifted from distributed confusion to concentrated clarity. His leadership team got those four hours back for actual work instead of interpretation.

You're either paying the cost of clarity up front, or you're making everyone else pay it later. The math always favors doing the work yourself.

Here's the thing about clarity that nobody tells you: it gets easier with practice, but it never becomes effortless. Each piece of clear writing requires intentional effort. Each revision cycle demands patience. Each deleted paragraph asks you to trust that less can be more.

The payoff compounds over time. People start trusting your communication because they know it means what it says. Teams move faster because they spend less time asking for clarification. Projects stay on track because the requirements were clear from the beginning.

Bad writing might be cheaper in the moment, but it's expensive in every other way that matters. Clear writing costs more upfront but pays dividends in trust, efficiency, and results.

So here's my question for you: where in your work are you passing the cost of clarity onto other people? What would change if you decided to do that work yourself instead?
