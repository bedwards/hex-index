Here's what most founders get wrong about brand voice: they think writing it down equals having it.

I've watched this play out more times than I can count. A company decides they need "[consistent brand messaging](https://www.optimizely.com/insights/blog/the-power-of-consistent-messaging/)," so they hire someone to create a tone guide. Weeks later, they have a polished 15-page document with personality frameworks and do-and-don't lists. Everyone feels good about it. The document gets filed away in a shared drive, and six months later, their website still reads like corporate fluff while their emails sound like they were written by five different people.

There’s a real problem with this, and it’s one you probably are going to miss unless you know what it is. Voice doesn't come from documentation. Voice comes from practice, real conversations, and paying attention to what actually works when you talk to your customers.

The elephant in the room is this: a tone guide gives people a false sense of security. Everyone thinks the voice problem is solved because there's a document somewhere that says the brand should be "authentic and approachable." But nobody pulls up that document when they're writing a customer email at 2 PM or responding to a complaint on social media.

The truth is that most tone guides suffer from the same disease they're supposed to cure. They're written in the exact corporate language they claim to fix. You'll see guidelines that use phrases like "leverage synergies" while insisting the brand voice should be "conversational." They list personality traits like "innovative, trustworthy, dynamic" that could describe any company on the planet.

One of the most crucial things to remember is that the companies with memorable voices didn't start with perfect documentation. They started with people who cared about how they sounded and kept adjusting until it felt right. Voice gets built through repetition and attention, not through a PDF.

## What Real Voice Looks Like

](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1541592553160-82008b127ccb?crop=entropy&cs=tinysrgb&fit=max&fm=jpg&ixid=M3wzMDAzMzh8MHwxfHNlYXJjaHw4fHx2b2ljZXxlbnwwfHx8fDE3NTQ1NzMwNjd8MA&ixlib=rb-4.1.0&q=80&w=1080)
*Photo by [Panos Sakalakis](https://unsplash.com/@meymigrou) on [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com)*

Your real voice shows up in your habits. The way you naturally explain your product when someone doesn't get it. The specific words you use when a customer is confused, excited, or angry. What happens when you stop trying to sound like "the company" and start sounding like yourself.

The best analogy I can find for this is to think about musicians developing their sound. A guitarist doesn't learn to sound distinctive by reading about music theory. They play the same songs hundreds of times, making small choices about which notes to emphasize and how long to hold them. The unique sound emerges from all those micro-decisions, repeated consistently.

Your brand voice works the same way. Hundreds of small choices about how to phrase things. Do you say "folks" or "people"? Do you use contractions? Do you explain technical stuff with analogies or bullet points? These tiny decisions, made consistently over time, become your voice.

## Building Voice from What You Already Have

*Photo by [Ryan Quintal](https://unsplash.com/@ryanquintal) on [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com)*

The good news is you already have voice examples scattered throughout your business. You just need to collect them and look for patterns.

Where do you start? The best place is probably [customer conversations](https://www.answer-4u.com/blog/maintaining-brand-consistency). If you do sales calls, record yourself explaining what you do. Save chat transcripts from demos. Notice the language you use when someone asks "what exactly does your product do?" Notice which phrases make people go "oh, now I get it." Those phrases are gold.

What are the actual words you use when you discuss this with someone? Check emails to investors, pitch presentations, casual conversations with the team. When does it sound natural? When does it feel forced? The natural moments show you where your real voice lives.

Pay attention to what feels easy versus what feels like work. If you spend 30 minutes crafting a single product update email, you're probably overthinking it. If you can knock out a customer support response in two minutes and it sounds exactly like how you'd explain it to a friend, that's your voice.

An easy way to do this is to collect the phrases you use repeatedly. Maybe you always start explanations with "So here's what happens." Maybe you use cooking analogies for complex processes. Maybe you can't help making sports references when talking about competition. These aren't quirks to eliminate. They're what make you sound like you instead of everyone else.

## Exercises That Actually Work

[

![a silhouette of a man singing into a microphone](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1623517272043-cae1572afc96?crop=entropy&cs=tinysrgb&fit=max&fm=jpg&ixid=M3wzMDAzMzh8MHwxfHNlYXJjaHw0fHxzaW5naW5nfGVufDB8fHx8MTc1NDY1NzY4NHww&ixlib=rb-4.1.0&q=80&w=1080 "a silhouette of a man singing into a microphone")



](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1623517272043-cae1572afc96?crop=entropy&cs=tinysrgb&fit=max&fm=jpg&ixid=M3wzMDAzMzh8MHwxfHNlYXJjaHw0fHxzaW5naW5nfGVufDB8fHx8MTc1NDY1NzY4NHww&ixlib=rb-4.1.0&q=80&w=1080)
*Photo by [Steven Erixon](https://unsplash.com/@stevenerixon) on [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com)*

How do you develop your own voice? Here are a few practical things you can use to grow your brand’s voice without hiring someone else to tell you if you’re doing it correctly.

**Voice sampling:** Record yourself explaining your business to someone who's never heard of it. Don't script anything, just talk for five minutes. Transcribe it and look at the actual words and rhythms you used. That recording is closer to your real voice than anything you'll produce trying to "sound professional."

**Voice contrast:** Take a paragraph from your website. Rewrite it the way you'd actually say it to someone. Then rewrite it in the most corporate way possible. The gap between those versions shows you where your authentic voice lives and where you're hiding behind business-speak.

**Customer feedback:** Send something you wrote to a customer who knows you well. Ask them if it sounds like you. If they say no, ask them to point out what feels off. Their feedback beats any tone guide.

**Story practice:** Take a real story from your work, a customer win, a product decision, a mistake you learned from. Tell it in complete sentences without editing yourself for "marketing purposes." The way you naturally tell stories reveals your voice patterns better than personality frameworks.

You know you're on track when you can recognize your own writing without seeing your name on it. When customers start using your phrases in their emails back to you. When writing feels easier because you're not translating yourself into "brand language."

The test isn't whether your voice fits some predetermined framework. The test is whether it [helps you connect](https://www.frontify.com/en/guide/brand-voice) with people who need what you're building. If customers feel like they're talking to a real person who gets their problems, you're doing it right.

## Voice Gets Built Through Practice

Voice is something you develop by using it, not by documenting it. The companies with strong voices didn't start with perfect guidelines. They started with people who paid attention to how they sounded and cared enough to keep improving.

Stop waiting for the perfect tone guide and start practicing being yourself in your writing. Show up authentically in every customer email, product announcement, and social media post. Notice what works. Keep the things that make people respond with "exactly" and drop the things that make you sound like everyone else.

Your voice is already there. You just need to stop covering it up and start letting it out.
