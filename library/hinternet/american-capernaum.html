I seem to recall a description of Trump, back in his first term, as “our first Central Asian president”, though when I search it now the only hits returned are to my own use of the phrase. If in fact someone else said it first, and then disappeared from the record, this was surely a riff on an earlier description of Bill Clinton as “our first Black president”, a distinction somehow earned by playing saxophone, with his “cool” shades on, for Arsenio — and also, I suppose, by being Southern and affable, and by proving himself again and again, when stumping in his 36’’ Haggars at the finest soul-food establishments throughout the country, to be a member in good standing of the “clean-plate club”.

The closest Trumpian equivalent to Bubba’s well-timed display of New Jack Swing fluency is surely his cameo appearance in Azerbaijani post-Soviet gala-pop artist and gangster-industrialist scion Emin Agalarov’s “In Another Life (feat. Donald Trump and Miss Universe ‘13)” (2013). We can deplore the excesses of the Russiagate accusations that dominated our news headlines for so much of the later part of that same decade. It remains however the case that what we are seeing in this video is profoundly, essentially un-American, utterly incompatible with our Whitmanian spirit. You could know already, from the support that the future president lent to this gross display of Eurasianist pop kitsch, that something was deeply rotten in our Republic. Bad taste, in sufficiently concentrated dosage, can be as great a threat to our American way of life as any subversive ideology…

Nor, to say the least, has the onslaught of bad taste —bad taste of a distinctly Afro-Eurasian genus, imported from far beyond the territory covered by the Monroe Doctrine— abated in the Trump 2 era. This much has been particularly clear to me in our president’s profoundly, essentially un-American efforts, concomitant with our withdrawal from one international organization after the other, to draw our Republic not out of but into that part of the world ruled de-facto by the louche shadow-government of the Fédération Internationale de Football Association [sic].

Is there any globalist conspiracy more powerful, and more out in the open, than fucking FIFA? Is there any greater threat to our quaint American gridirons of old, down which countless mythical Gippers have run, than the purportedly “beautiful” game of Ronaldo and Cacá? Where, ask yourself, would Ronald Reagan have felt more at home: on the bleachers at Ole Miss, or in a VIP box, alongside a pack of Saudi princes, in some slave-built corporate-futurist mega-stadium?

This betrayal at the top has of course trickled down. Young American men are now wearing jerseys that say things like “Emirates” on them. Nor can such a fashion choice be passed off, as an American might still have done for his pre-Brexit Manchester United fandom, as the same sort of moderately up-market and broadly Europhile distinction that might also inspire him to praise Guinness. An American influencer like the 20-year-old iShowSpeed is perfectly happy to follow Ronaldo from Manchester to Riyadh. Neither the player nor the fan, it turns out, had any real commitment to the particular sort of “beauty” that could once be found in the working-class popular spirit of the game, a spirit historically forged in Europe and reproduced organically in Latin America and Africa, but only imported in a pre-fab and top-down way, once it became a massive global financial enterprise, into the simulacral societies of the Gulf petro-states. Ronaldo follows the money to Felix Arabia, and the hearts of young Speed and of old Trump, filled with nothing but pure thoughtless heat-seeking instinct, follow in his train.

While the Arabian Peninsula has been swept by waves of oil from traditional tribal organization straight to vapid postmodern consumerism, Iran spent much of the 20th century grappling with modernity at its best and most nuanced — an effort that still shows not only in that country’s cinema, but in the style and composure of its brave protesters. There was a hilarious moment some years back when a group of Syrian rebels hacked the now-Moscow-exiled Bachar al-Assad’s iTunes account, and revealed to the world that his tastes lean mostly towards O-Zone, and Maroon 5, and shit like that. Now everything is relative, and I’m not claiming there have been no downloads of “Dragostea din tei” or of the odious, disgraceful, civilization-ending “Moves Like Jagger” to IP addresses in Tehran. What I am saying is that our clichés about the culture that was forced underground with the Islamic revolution in 1979 are based on some truth: everything from mid-century Persian graphic design to the practically Jüngerian diagnosis of “Occidentosis” in the work of a mid-century writer such as Jalāl Āl-e-Ahmad, evidences a complicated, conflicted, but ultimately serious inheritance of a distinctly European idea of culture, and of the social and political urgency of fostering and preserving a distinctly modern and secular “high” culture. In this respect, the most comparable historical trajectory of a regional neighbor to Iran is not Arabia, but Russia.

In the early 1990s the metropolitan centers of the former USSR were bursting with avant-garde enthusiasm, at least some of which was genuinely home-grown, and not a CIA psy-op. Silly performance art would again play a vanguard role in post-Soviet society, in something like Lenin’s old sense, until roughly the moment, in the Maidan era, when Pyotr Pavlensky nailed his own testicles to Red Square, and the Putin regime determined there had been enough of that sort of thing. Such extreme modernism as this (if not generally involving testicles) had been actively fostered in the early Soviet era, prior to Stalin’s imposition of the infantilizing and predictable sotsrealizm for which the USSR is today best remembered. But Pavlensky was not simply picking up where Daniil Kharms left off —who used to do a performance for his friends, in his Leningrad living room, which involved urinating on the floor and then mopping it up, and then doing it over again—; he was also, ironically, drawing on a reserve of ideas about cultural modernity that were carefully preserved, indeed you might say fossilized, by the communist regime itself.

If high modernism was mostly suppressed or discouraged after 1932 or so —no more Black Squares or whimsical Flatland-like geometrical beings for us socialists!—, a somewhat more moderate ideal of secular modernity, inherited ultimately from the tastes and values of the fin-de-siècle Viennese bourgeoisie, survived much more robustly under the Soviet system than under any of the actual bourgeois imperialist powers, which all quickly moved on in the postwar period to new forms of distinction, newly nuanced acceptions of “cool”, with which Eastern Europe is still struggling to catch up. It is indeed a great irony that the Soviet Bloc would serve as home to the last surviving pockets of people who take it for granted that one must know how to read sheet music, or that little boys should be dressed in sailor suits, swaddled in infancy, given mustard presses, or that men must display otherwise forgotten forms of gallantry towards women in public spaces, while meanwhile in the supposedly non-revolutionary parts of the world young people were turning towards a sort of radical and leveling free-love utopianism that had not been seen in the West since the early years of the Anabaptists.

Such quaint Austro-Hungarianism as would warm the bourgeois heart of Peter Gay likewise survived, to some extent, in the Ashkenazi diaspora (a legacy beautifully anatomized by the great , in a remembrance of his father in the forthcoming Oort Cloud Review). My California Jewish stepfather and my Romanian wife managed to bond, among other reasons, over their preference for drinking coffee out of dainty ceramic cups with saucers, while my own broadly Okie-Arkie clan loves nothing more than a 24-oz. paper cup of weak-ass joe from the gas station. Through her, I was able to see what I had long taken to be curious personal quirks in him as, in fact, the faint lingering traces of tremendous historical legacies of which we are all mostly unconscious. I owe so much to both of them — not least my ability to pass as a denizen of the Old World (a place to which he at least had no interest in returning, or even thinking much about).

I suppose what I’m trying to say is I am attached to, indeed I love, the ideal of culture as it took shape in high modernity, which I date to the end of the 19th century, and which may be seen as headquartered metonymically in Vienna. I love Russians and Persians and Romanians and every other ethnolinguistic community that has done the hard work of importing, into our current much-decayed age, into our fractured context of no context, at least some memory of why all that once mattered so much.

My own people, the Americans, are of course the greatest force behind the victory of vapid postmodern consumerism as global ideology, which the Saudis have adopted in consequence of a very different historical trajectory than our own. But the thing is, when it comes to vapid postmodern consumerism, no one gives a shit what historical trajectory brought you there. iShowSpeed has no idea how he ended up wearing an Al-Nassr jersey, or what the historical significance of this fashion choice might be.

Trump in turn continues to display some of the signs of opulent wealth that bear a distant ancestral connection to the aesthetic sensibility once predominant at Versailles — but the path from the European baroque to the living rooms of Queens real-estate developers is really no different from the one that once caused African colonial subjects of British power ritually to reenact British military drills and other expressions of British administrative authority. Trump’s gilded decor, I mean, is really nothing more than a cargo-cult imitation of the way aristocratic wealth and power appear when you have no real connection to the historical legacies that produced this wealth and this power. And you might just as well expect to find such derivative hints of Versailles in a VIP box in a stadium in Riyadh as in Manhattan’s own Trump Tower, or in the newly renovated wings of the White House.

Meanwhile those Americans who object to our president’s revived claim to Western Hemispheric hegemony; or to the extraordinary rendition of foreign dictators currently, if always reversibly, on our shit-list; or to the extrajudicial murder of protesters; or to the creation of a new paramilitary force that hides behind face-masks, much like those I saw a few years ago at the airport in Tunis, worn by men otherwise covered in cheap body-spray and distressed denim and knock-off Ed Hardy shirts, and who no doubt had Maroon 5 playing on the Bluetooth speaker in the break-room adjacent to their fluorescent-lit interrogation chamber, not as an implement of torture, mind you, but simply as an expression of their sincere personal taste: meanwhile, I was saying, these other Americans are predictably piping up to express their desire to decamp “to Europe”.

For years I have had to put up with utterly false representations, from “progressive” Americans, of what life on this continent is like. In France, they write on Twitter, work e-mail platforms shut down willy-nilly on evenings and weekends; yet I read this at midnight on a Sunday, as I am simultaneously responding to an e-mail I’ve just received from the director of my laboratoire. In France, they say, you can while away your day sitting and reading in an old-style café; yet I have never been able to sit more than 15 minutes in such a place before the waiter comes to give me a list of all the rules I’m breaking, to tell me I’ll have to pack up and go because it will soon be the sacrosanct lunch hour — and so inevitably I end up at one of Paris’s many fine Starbucks locations, where I am left in peace, and where I find my students sitting and studying too.

The sort of Americans to effuse about such things, based on utter ignorance of what life here is actually like, are now expressing their intention to come and actually work here. Trust me: they will not follow through on it, unless of course they are coming for a limited time, and with American money.

I have sat through countless lunches and dinners with such ephemeral American Parisians as these. One such visitor —an emeritus Ivy League academic—, upon learning that I live in a traditionally working-class and immigrant arrondissement, asked me how my neighbors must feel about such a case of “gentrification” as he imagined I represent. Brother, I had to explain to him, I am an immigrant, and I live in this arrondissement because it is all we can afford. My neighbors see me, for the most part, as one of them.

Such Americans may orient themselves more towards Haussmann than towards the Sun King, but their Europe is no less a cargo-cult fantasy than the one still faintly seen in the tastes of their despised president. The idea that one could somehow escape the crisis that is currently swallowing their own country whole, that they would no longer be plagued by dread for the future if they were simply to relocate to “Europe”, is nothing but a childish delusion. Here in actual Europe, these days, people spend most of their time thinking about war — like, real war, not the abstract idea of war taking place somewhere else, but the very concrete matter of stocking up on canned goods and, on Ursula van der Leyen’s recommendation, iodine pills. How could coming to such a place possibly be imagined as an escape from the heaviness of the 21st century?

The answer, of course, is that it only appears so if by “Europe” you mean, so to speak, EuroDisney, a postmodern simulacrum that stands safely outside of history. The problem is that it is pretty much only within the boundaries of the United States, which remain essentially inviolable by any conventional military means, that such a simulacrum can appear real for long. To be perfectly frank, however much I dislike most of what the Trump regime is doing on the domestic front, the overwhelming and myopic focus of progressive Americans on that particular front reminds me, jarringly, of what I used to see when I spent time in Israel: academics and other educated professionals taking to the streets to protest cuts to their pension plans and things like that, as if in desperate repression of the existential predicament that their geopolitical situation would otherwise keep constantly at the front of the mind of anyone not highly adept at the art of shutting it out. Americans, similarly, excel at electoral horserace chatter, at debating the pros and cons of child tax credits and so on, while remaining basically incapable of thinking about their true plight: as inhabitants of the world’s most powerful empire, prepared to do absolutely anything to retain its primacy. Americans know what it’s like to be at odds with their own government; they do not, for the most part, know what it’s like to be afraid of America as such. And unlike the Israelis, this myopia seems to come not from some spirit of Churchillian pluckiness, but from living in a vacuum, from contextlessness, from literal idiocy.

Actual Europeans, in turn, seem utterly uncertain as to what they really want. Though the far-left was always most explicit on this point, the European center-left, which is to say the European mainstream, has often characterized US postwar presence here as a quasi-imperial arrangement. But now that the US appears to be abandoning that arrangement, anti-Americanism has quickly and easily been updated to take this new reality into account. One can’t help but wonder whether they in fact would like to be vassalized all over again, or at least to renew and reinvigorate the Pax Americana, which has permitted them to maintain robust state welfare systems while the Americans take care of their defense — which has given them license in turn superciliously to bemoan the US’s failure to see to its own citizens’ health and well-being.

I am literally a pacifist. I believe, literally, that you must love your enemy, even or especially when that entails your own annihilation. It’s in the Good Book, you heathens! “Just-war theory” [sic], by contrast, has no real scriptural basis, but is only wishful thinking on the part of late-coming and eminently human theologians.

But still. When in a more rational mode I strain to make out the shape of the near future, and I consider the reasons for preserving some amount of hope, the best I can come up with is this: what we are seeing right now is an abrupt and disorienting reassertion of American primacy, of the sort that Ronald Reagan pursued with his strategy of “peace through strength”. It worked once, which at least demonstrates possibility, if not probability.

It is much more likely however that what we are seeing is something like a gangsters’ reenactment of a Reagan-style showdown with the rest of the world. It is after all from actual gangsters that Trump himself has adapted his implicit and mostly sublinguistic political philosophy. And gangsters are perhaps the world’s great masters of the cargo-cult form of life, creating an entire perverse counter-world of rules and norms and noblesse-oblige for themselves, which keeps them all operating smoothly, in their respective spheres of influence… until someone makes a false move, and all of gangland is awash in blood.

—JSR, Paris
