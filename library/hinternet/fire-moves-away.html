In 2025 I was absolutely obsessed with a TV show called The !!! Beat, which aired for 26 episodes in 1966 from an ABC affiliate studio in Dallas. It was hosted by the amiable Nashville DJ Bill “Hoss” Allen, with a live band led by the legendary Clarence “Gatemouth” Brown. The show perfected a loving and curatorial approach to the distinctly Texas blues and soul acts it showcased. The production values are surprisingly low, even for what was expected of a local TV station in that era, and the talent is generally raw — real, but raw. Hoss occasionally mentions that some of the performers have been scouted at Dallas’s John F. Kennedy High School, which must only have been recently renamed.

Hoss himself comes across as a real institution, a symbol of the complexity of cultural identity in the desegregation-era South. Hoss is white and Gatemouth is black, and they and their ancestors have both, obviously, been stewing in the same musical and cultural sauce for centuries. But their show takes place not only against the backdrop of Civil Rights, but also of the psychedelic explosion that has been the focus of such extensive analysis in this space (mostly by JSR). So the backdrops and the outfits feature, by trickle-down or unconscious absorption, much of that effervescent and flowery spirit, even as, in other respects, what we are seeing is not at all revolutionary, and not youth-centered, but traditional, transgenerational, and, in some ways, conservative.

I keep returning to one episode in particular, which features a remarkable performance from Freddie King at the end —such a perfect synthesis of Chicago electric and Texas blues!—, but is mostly given over to several songs performed by Louis Jordan and the Tympany Five (plus an accompanying and astoundingly talentless sidekick, Marsha). You can choose any one member of this ensemble to focus on throughout, and each one will give you a different and equally powerful experience; I am especially fond of the drummer, Chris Columbo, whom I long took for blind, until I noticed him smiling and nodding at an audience member.

Jordan had been performing since the 1930s, and is 58 or so in this performance. He’s got miles on him, as he puts it, and he’s ready to cede his place to the youth, but as long as his humble services are still in demand he will be happy to sing “Caldonia” for you in 1966 with just as much good humor as he had done in 1945. Jordan was never a rock-and-roll artist, but he did inject a considerable amount of his own DNA into the style that would eventually go by that name. Like James Brown, he is above all a band-leader; his work is to keep his band tight in every way, not just musically, but also in its choreography, its temperament, its collective personality. If his “jump blues” style is not fully in the proto-rock idiom, this is mostly because Jordan is too affable to be transgressive. Songs like “Saturday Night Fish Fry” describe scenes of lawlessness, but this is hapless and comic violation of the penal code, not rebellion; the police are keystone cops, not vectors of structural racism.

Jordan makes “novelty” music, a polyphyletic taxon that paradigmatically includes such milestones as Moon Zappa’s “Valley Girl” (1983). But some people are funny mostly because humor is an effective way to launder the dangerous imagination, with the result, I suspect, that even the blandest humor, even the corniest novelty song, must be attended with an ear to its encrypted potentials for violence and its subdued explosiveness. With “GI Blues”, and the occasion this gives Jordan and Hoss to revisit their Army days, we get a particularly vivid glimpse of the key role normies have played in the ascendancy of post-war counterculture culture. Do not underestimate them.

Something like this thought has kept me listening, this year, to classic soul more generally, from Sam Cooke to Marvin Gaye. I am convinced that the greatest forgotten masterpiece of this period is The Dells’ “Stay in My Corner”, originally recorded in 1965, then redone in a much longer and sublime version three years later. The 1968 rendition seems to me to reflect the epic quality of much psychedelic rock — it is plainly post-Sgt. Pepper’s, yet its dilations and its crests also seem to have been learned in church rather than by any drug-induced shortcut.

The Dells had formed in 1953 as a conventional doo-wop group, changed their line-up after a car accident in 1958, and by the mid-1960s had taken on a smoother, more velvety quality that positions them, in my view, as a significant transitional species between the divine Ink Spots and the very differently divine Isaac Hayes. When I hear the Dells I hear the possibility opening up for what I have long, and I hope not offensively, thought of as “hot-oil” soul, which reaches its sublime pinnacle in Hayes (here he is turning Burt Bacharach, of all people, into an absolute hot-oil massage), only then to devolve, in my view, into something much more meandering, much more invertebrate, with the unnameable R. Kelly and others of his generation. But again, they didn’t get there on their own. When I hear the note that the group’s lead baritone Marvin Junior holds for around 17 seconds on the first syllable of the word “baby” (just after the five-minute mark), this sounds to me something like an echo of the large-scale orchestral texture that the Beatles hoped to adapt from Luciano Berio in “A Day in the Life” (1967). It is soul getting its mind blown (if soul may be said to have a mind, a question I believe Pierre Gassendi discusses somewhere), much as rock was doing at the same time, but with different historical antecedents, and a different set of new potentialities discovered.

My fellow critics have often made the mistake of looking for artistic inspiration primarily in lyrical content. This imbalance has caused them to pretend they enjoy listening to Bob Dylan, or to Kendrick Lamar, more than they possibly could, while entirely missing the deep —if, for some, undignified— truth that post-war popular music’s ingenious turns are far more likely to be delivered through the vehicle of words like “baby”. We’re talking about music here, you eggheads.

Occasionally, lyrical genius co-occurs with ingenious musical composition and performance. I have become convinced over this past year, for example, that Joanna Newsom is a great American artist — great like Whitman or Gershwin, and American like both of them in her ability to forge something entirely new, in an entirely new voice, out of older lineages. I have listened to Ys (2006) more than any other album this year, by far. I believe Newsom has been grossly underappreciated by the critics, who will tell you of her “Lisa Simpson voice” or, if they are more sympathetic, of the way she engages with themes of womanhood, and the like. They will not tell you she is a universal poet, whose lyrics, almost alone in the history of popular music (including Dylan and Lamar) can be meaningfully read as text-only literary creations.

In the years since 2006, unlike Whitman and Gershwin, Newsom cannot yet be seen as standing at the beginning of anything. On the contrary she can easily appear as the culmination of a process of increasing sophistication, a motion in indie music towards the ever-more-baroque and convoluted. But on this reading the centering of the harp, and the sweet string arrangements layered on top of that, unjustly keeps Joanna indexed to a period of brief efflorescence —indeed, the last, before we get the entirely virtual and unwittingly parodic “trad” phenomenon— of a historically rooted American folk sensibility. She is incorrectly anchored, I mean, to all the lesser artists with banjos and mandolins who held some cultural space throughout the aughts, and whom we tend to see as a subset of hipsters.

Some hipsters are great musicians — Jim O’Rourke and Bill Callahan both had a hand in the making of Ys, and it shows. But Joanna’s own pedigree is far more complex than anything that label can capture. In the modal drift and the cosmic textures of her harpistry one hears more Alice Coltrane than, say, Jean Ritchie with her renderings of Appalachian ballads on the comparably rare dulcimer. Neither is it insignificant that Joanna at least passed through Mills College, long home of the avant-garde composer Pauline Oliveros, who did so much to foster a culture there drawing from the best of the European tradition but with a distinctly American “anything goes” spirit (again, continuing the vital work of all great American artists, of the sort that Whitman and Gershwin perfected).

Anyhow, I love her, she’s one of the best. Other critics don’t know what the fuck they’re talking about. I mean, they like her, or liked her, but not enough — they took her to be one artist among others. With any luck, and with what is now twenty years’ hindsight, perhaps we can finally begin a serious critical reappraisal of Joanna’s work that will lead to her definitive installation alongside the other true greats.

Did any good music come out in 2025? I don’t know, maybe. Who cares. I might get around to caring about it 20 years from now. There are many ways to be a critic. Here at The Hinternet, unlike, say, the New Yorker or the New York Times, there simply is no economic imperative to pretend that we are not living in an era of decline and mediocrity, or to make as if some recent culture-industrial production is worthy of our current attention, when in fact it is not. Now it may well turn out that in 2046 I will have much to say about Doja Cat or Bad Bunny that I do not find all that urgent to say now. Anything’s possible, and I well know that time casts all our cultural productions in an ever-shifting light.

But let’s be honest: there are only two reasons people preoccupy themselves with the present as if it mattered more than the past, only two reasons why they come up with lists of their listening habits for 2025 that consist primarily of music released in 2025: because they are vapid and don’t know any better, or because their vapid and ignorant readership expects it of them and their prosperity therefore depends on their willingness to do so.

My current assessment of the musical output of 2025, anyhow, might best be expressed by this line from Joanna’s 16’53’’ masterpiece, “Only Skin”: “Cold, cold cupboard, Lord / Nothing to chew on!”
