### 1\. Universal Brainrot

We should begin with news of a disconcerting development here at our editorial offices in Quimper. It seems our interns, Augustus and Eglantine, have seized control of *The Hinternet*’s social media. We are completely locked out of [our own Instagram account](https://www.instagram.com/the.hinternet/), which Gus and Eggy now seem intent on transforming into a veritable cataract of grade-A bona-fide Gen Z brainrot.

A dissenting minority on our editorial staff, including JSR himself, has argued that this twist might not turn out entirely to our detriment. Brainrot, they maintain, is really the only significant aesthetic sensibility that matters in 2026, the only that is not moribund, but is in fact likely to continue to evolve, and, within a few years, some of us are now predicting, to ramify into high and low branches, to insinuate itself into museums and fashion and the narrative arts and whatever is left of academia, to serve as the new stone on which our lapidary critics will whet their faculties of judgment. Could this be possible? Friends, as of 2026 *anything* is possible — anything, that is, except returning to the status quo that prevailed before the massive cultural, technological, and geopolitical reset currently underway.

And so we are coming around, at least in one of the branches of our operation, to seeing the strategic appeal of placing our bet on the youngest of the currently adult generations — whose creative fecundity, significantly aided by image- and text-generating machines, is already showing us, or at least those of us who know how to see, the rough outlines of our future. And it looks something like this:

JSR tells us that some such epiphany as we have just related came to him in a supermarket aisle in Rome last month, when he saw, hanging perfectly at the level of an average six-year-old’s eyes, a bag labelled “Universal Brainrot”. There appeared to be plastic figurines inside, characters from some new fictional universe of which he was previously completely unaware — a shark with feet and sneakers on them, a crocodile-watermelon hybrid, a jacked tomato, a green monkey whose lower half is a banana, and so on. It was immediately evident that this was no venerable Disneyesque pantheon, no happy family of characters shaped over the decades by organic growth and human attention. In fact it appeared distinctly as if no attention at all had gone into the creation of these neo-Empedoclean man-faced ox-prodigies.[1](#footnote-1) Attention is care, but these figures were careless, pell-mell, n’importe quoi. And yet there they were, not on a screen, but now manifested as physical objects for the enticement of rot-hungry bambini, having, it seems, jumped the fence we might earlier have expected to preserve the distinction between the virtual and the “real”.

It may be that, as the 3rd-century Alexandrian Neoplatonist Origen speculated, these physical objects were among the “special class of beings that have voluntary control over their own visibility, and thus are able freely to ‘offer themselves’ to sight at will.”[2](#footnote-2) For how *else* could you explain their power over us? They look like absolute shit, yet we can’t get enough of them. But if Origen was primarily concerned with objects that command our attention mostly thanks to the solicitude of angels, it is sooner a demonic force that has unleashed this legion of chimeras on us.

Upon arriving in New York from Rome just before the New Year, JSR just happened to hear these same interns speaking enthusiastically about “Italian brainrot”, and when he told them he had seen an entire bagful of it at the checkout counter in Rome, and was now thinking of doing a *Hinternet* piece about what he saw, the response was: “Tread carefully. You’ll need to acknowledge you’re a latecomer and an outsider to all this.” When he next consulted Managing Editor David Lamb on the matter, David, whom we believe to be a Millennial, nodded knowingly, and likewise warned us to proceed cautiously. “This stuff can get pretty dark,” he explained.

We confess we don’t really understand the nature of this darkness. But whatever it is, we know enough, or at least our dissenting minority knows enough, to know that it’s mighty powerful, and like Prometheus before us it is our ambition to harness some of that dark energy and to use it to reach the heroic heights for which we are destined. What could go wrong?

### 2\. “100% AI-Free”? Nonsense!

We are willing to use new technologies, is what this all really boils down to, for the pursuit both of our creative vision and of the sort of worldly success we have long felt this vision merits. Many writers on Substack, we have however noticed, are not nearly so forthcoming in this matter. They’re doing it too, of course, to some extent —we all are!—, but you’d never know it from the way they speak about their work.

Have you seen them yet — those certification logos many writers have begun adding to their online work, announcing, as one might announce of eggs that they are free-range, that a post is “100% AI-free”? Have you yet come across the writers who enjoy insisting in public that they “never use AI”, that they’re “just not interested, no thanks, not my cup of tea”? Some of them may of course be telling the truth, at least in some limited sense. They may very well have never opened up a browser to go to ChatGPT or Claude or any of the others. But even so, their claim remains both supercilious and un-self-knowing: supercilious because there is really nothing at all heroic or admirable or even just reasonable about declining to inform oneself, through first-hand experience, of what is like it or not almost certainly the most important development in our 5000-year history of literacy.

These are *writers* we’re talking about, and so, one might imagine, they really do have an interest in knowing what, in the most general sense, is “going on with writing” in the present age. Yet they remain prideful about their reported abstention in a way that reminds us of nothing so much as those elderly people who declare, every time the topic of reading habits comes up, that “I just don’t like reading on the screen” and “I’ll keep getting my paper edition of the *New York Times* delivered for as long as they’re willing to deliver it.” Now from an 80-year-old this is fine, and even an important reminder of the particular strain that the perpetual displacement of our old habits and devices in favor of newer shinier ones places on the older generations. But how an 80-year-old feels about the screens-vs.-paper debate, we think everyone can agree, tells us absolutely nothing about the way media will be consumed in the very near future.

We have said that these certified free-range writers are both supercilious *and* un-self-knowing. They are un-self-knowing because, while indeed they may not be using AI, AI may well be using *them*. Now ordinarily we would not resort to such an aphoristic switch-a-roo worthy of Chairman Mao, but sometimes this is just what the world compels. In 2026, by far the prevailing “style” in all mainstream, gate-kept writing —the kind that passes through editorial boards and agents and all the hierarchies of the spreadsheet-brained publishers—, is an absence of style. And the cause of this absence may be traced back, obviously, to the “white-paper” non-style perfected over the past few years by LLMs.

There has been, in effect, an industrial revolution of language. It can now be produced, mechanically and in great surplus, in just the same way Chinese factories produce cheap plastic toys. Almost all of what gets churned out is literal garbage, destined never to be read, while perversely the ease with which it can be produced also incentivizes its overproduction. University syllabi and annual productivity reports are now bloated beyond any imaginable human proportions, and while most academics continue to play along poker-faced, we all know that we all know where all that text-bloat is coming from. It is language by machines and *for* machines, and it all foretells a very near future in which the human intermediaries will be cut out of the arrangement altogether.

This is all very tragic, but still something from which we might hope to recover, a crisis from which we might still salvage the soul of writing for other irreducibly human purposes. The greater problem is in the way LLMs have distorted and reduced our lingering expectations for what writing —even among *writers*— might be. Have you noticed the recent Cambrian explosion of species of bottomfeeding, writing-adjacent profiteers, who deign to offer their editorial services and advice on platforms like Substack, yet who for all appearances are indistinguishable from bots, who write things like: “An editor’s perfect day — warm cat in lap, a mug of hot cocoa, red pen in hand, and a riveting manuscript from one of my awesome clients!”? Can you imagine what such a person would do to a typical *Hinternet* piece?

Meanwhile in the universities, students now describe as “prompts” the paper “topics” (as we used to call them) assigned to them — the same language we also use to describe the instructions fed into our machines for the production of AI images. Across all domains what we are seeing, plainly, is a machine-human convergence, or, more precisely, a largely unconscious concern on the human side to approximate the “style” of the LLMS, itself an approximation of older human style.

Somewhere Descartes notes that the “lower” animals tend to move in predictable and stereotyped ways —if we recall correctly he draws on the example of an egret wading through a pond—, while you can never quite anticipate what crazy thing the “higher” ones will do next. To be “higher” in this sense, to give off the appearance of having a soul, is to be something of a chaos agent, to twist and to feint like some devious yet very clever monkey (Simia quam similis turpissima bestia nobis, someone once remarked, though we forget who). One way of describing the present moment would be to say that the LLMs have got most of us stalking around like egrets: rigid, stereotyped, pea-brained, and dull.

Of course such convergence has been a key force in the cultural history of technology for a very long time, and it does not necessarily *require* a loss of complexity, or chaos, or soul. The greatest and most soulful dance moves of the late 20th century, for example, developed mostly in imitation of robots. The tremendous leap forward in the musical quality of studio recordings in the late 1970s, to cite another example, plainly has much to do with the recent arrival of the synthesizer, and most of all with the arrival of drum machines, which established a standard of perfection against which human percussionists would from now on have to measure their own internal sense of time. But James Brown and Quincy Jones and all the others understood that this was a standard of *disensouled perfection*, and that the great challenge for the musical artist, now, was to enter this new realm of perfection opened up by technology, but to do so without checking their souls at the door.

We believe here at *The Hinternet* that the art of writing now finds itself in a crisis comparable to the one the musical arts began to face half a century ago with the advent of musical machines. For the most part, however, writers have not yet understood that this is our plight, and so have mostly retreated into denial — into kitsch fantasies of a pre-digital writerly idyll of fountain pens, ink-pots, notebooks, throw-pillows, and a “nice hot mug of cocoa”. It is mostly towards the sustenance of such a fantasy that Substack seems to be veering in recent months, with the result that it now often seems to have about as much to do with writing as LinkedIn motivational sales porn has to do with making money. This turn is to be deplored, and resisted, not simply by continuing to write, but by continuing to write in a way that reflects the reality of the cultural-technological conjuncture in which we find ourselves.

### 3\. AI Is Great for Creativity, Terrible for Knowledge

We believe that it is precisely in the memetic effervescence of the youth —those with no hope of or interest in pushing past the gatekeepers who still pretend to control the circulation of language, and of symbolic communication in general—, that we may expect to find the new James Brown dancing the new “robot” (or, as James Brown significantly called it, the “James Brown”[3](#footnote-3) ). The effervescent youth —or, which amounts to the same, the brainrotten youth— do not waste time with “AI-free” certifications. They are neither afraid of AI, nor subordinate to AI, but simply take AI as given, as a feature of our reality and as a powerful enhancement of our own irreducibly human potentialities.

This means, among other things, that our society is still getting AI drastically, diametrically wrong. The rate of generating false claims in commercially available LLMs is currently around 45%. Neither, if we listen to the experts, is there much reason to hope that this rate will improve in the future — it’s built into the fundamental architecture of the new technology. LLMs will themselves readily concede that they are not in fact machines for generating true claims at all, but rather machines for probabilistically generating truth-like or plausible sequences of text. Their purpose is explicitly, avowedly unconnected to anything that might reasonably be called “the search after truth”, at least if we understand this noble task as limited to discovery and inference.

But look closer at the Italian brainrot, or at that delirious AI video you’ve likely seen by now, the one that suckered all the Facebook Boomers into gushing admiration for its canine hero, of a fat lady who drops a boulder on a glass bridge, causing a baby to fall off it and into a river, only to be swept up by a gallant golden retriever who transports it out of the water like a horse. That video, we contend —*even* that video!—, is more important than Sally Rooney, more important than Ocean Vuong; it will be discussed a century from now, if any of us are still around, in the same way we ourselves discuss the great *[Fred Ott’s Sneeze](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2wnOpDWSbyw)* (1894).

Philistines will protest that AI images and videos are plainly intrinsically defective, that they can only ever give us something like an aesthetic equivalent to the 45% error rate we obtain when we attempt to use AI as a fact-yielding engine rather than as a probabilistic verisimilitude generator.

Two objections may be made in response. The first is that *Fred Ott’s Sneeze* looks terrible too: it’s grainy and distorted and defective in countless ways. These defects have accrued over time, in part, but in part they are intrinsic to the technology used to create it in 1894. These are defects that the creators would have avoided, if they had known how. And they have their analogous instances in every subsequent technology used for recording, transmitting, and indeed creating works of art with new media technologies. Grainy vinyl is perhaps the paradigmatic instance of this phenomenon — even philistines know they are supposed to appreciate the sound of it. But in truth we have consistently aestheticized, with the passage of time, every limitation of every new medium. Some of us recall the buzz about “glitch” aesthetics that one could not avoid for a brief moment in the 1990s, when cool young people in Berlin and Antwerp attempted to convince us they could hear something like a confirmation of the truth of Deleuzean philosophy in the sound of a skipping CD.

The second objection is this: the criteria of truth-fidelity in creative endeavors are simply not the same as they are in the domains of empirical discovery and logical inference — i.e., domains in which many people are still trying to pretend LLMs can be a reliable tool. Yet when it comes to creation, as opposed to discovery and inference, a verisimilitude engine can turn out to be a very useful tool indeed. So there’s a 45% error rate? All the better! “Error”, after all, is connected to the verb “to err”, and it is precisely in erring, in the sense of freely wandering without knowing quite where, that the imagination does its best work.

In the coming years, if things go well, we may expect to find the narrative arts —the irreducibly *human* narrative arts— reproducing the aesthetic effect of what is still mistakenly called LLM “hallucination”. Of course this is not really hallucination at all on the part of the machines. When the machines do it, this is because they are detached altogether from any concern for truth, and are only filling in textual gaps with probabilities. When humans do it, by contrast, at its best we may call it *imagination*. And very soon, in the exercise of this sacred faculty, we expect, we are going to see human beings creatively mirroring the language machines in just the same irreducibly human way that James Brown once danced the robot.

### 4\. What now?

Look, friends, things never turn out as planned. You might recall that some centuries back we went and had a whole big Enlightenment; we made up a Characteristica universalis, then Esperanto, then we founded the League of Nations and put on a war to end all wars… and then we had another even bigger war followed by 80 years of tense détente, only now to end up in a world dominated by psychotic medieval warlords, as if none of those higher hopes for what human life might be had ever crossed our minds.

And yet art has endured throughout. For as long as we are here, art will be here.

Art, as its very name really should be enough to tell us, involves *artifice*. It cannot thrive where technological reality is screened out or denied. In the 21st century therefore, we maintain, art must concern itself centrally with what has long been held to be a dream only of the most utopian of the posthumanist tech-enthusiasts: using technology to ensure the survival of the human soul (the tech people would be more likely to call it “the human individual”, but let’s not get mired in questions of terminology). Somewhat more loosely understood however, this dream is hardly any different from the one that motivated the sculptor of the *Löwenmensch* 40,000 years ago: to transfer, as it were to upload, into whatever external support might be available, some true idea of who we are.
