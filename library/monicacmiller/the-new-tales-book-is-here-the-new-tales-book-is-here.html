[Warning: So many spoilers ahead about all of the Tales books. Don’t read if you don’t want to know what happens!]

In 1994, PBS premiered the first Tale of the City series, based on the first book in the series by Armistead Maupin. Set in San Francisco in 1976, it begins with the adventures of Mary Ann Singleton, who spontaneously decides to leave her drab life in Cleveland behind and begin a new life in San Francisco. Finding her way to the apartments at 28 Barbary Lane, run by the wonderfully eccentric landlady Anna Madrigal, Mary Ann finds her way through her new life among the other residents of Barbary Lane, all of whom are trying to forge new lives and create what Maupin would later term a new “logical family” (in contrast to a “biological family”) for themselves.

Even though it was set twenty years earlier–and was very much set in the 1970s of discos and Mucha posters–the experiences of the characters trying to figure out how to grow into their adult selves, how to define themselves in the context of their newly chosen careers and logical families resonated strongly with my friends and me. 

I adored the series. Each week after an episode aired on PBS, my Aunt Julie would call me from her home in Tacoma, Washington, and we would freak out over that week’s episode. What was Anna’s secret? Isn’t Beachamp a bastard? Wasn’t Mona WONDERFUL in that meeting with Beauchamp? Isn’t Michael Mouse just THE BEST?

Aunt Julie had been in San Francisco some in the 1970s, and these were her people. We both inhaled all of the novels that Maupin had written in the series then, and we read them all as new ones came out. The novels are a wonderful balance between relationship-driven character development and outlandish episodic plots. In one novel, Jim Jones has faked his death and is trying to kidnap DeeDee’s children. Another features an Episcopalean cannibal cult (this one is where I learned the word “transubstantiation”). And having lived in Cincinnati and known several people who were devotees of the annual Michigan Womyn’s Festival, I especially loved the story set at the fictional “Wimminwood” camp.

Maupin’s strength is that he’s able to simultaneously convey humor and humanity even in the most ludicrous situations. And at the heart of all of these novels is the importance of creating and appreciating one’s logical family.

Maupin published Sure of You in 1989, in which Michael Tolliver and his friends have all left Barbary Lane for various reasons. Michael is HIV positive; Mona has moved to Europe; Mary Ann and Brian are splitting up, and she’s leaving California. At the end of this novel, I felt regret about the sadness with which we leave the characters–whom by this point felt like part of my own logical family. Some have characterized Sure of You as the antithesis of the first novel’s sense of exuberant hope. Nevertheless, Aunt Julie and I both enjoyed the television adaptations of the second and third novels in 1998 and 2001 and talked about the characters the same way we talked about our actual relatives and friends.

In 2005, Aunt Julie was diagnosed with ovarian cancer; she died about a month after the diagnosis. That was pretty much the worst year of my life: she was diagnosed in June, she died July 12, and then at the end of August, Mark and I had to evacuate our home in New Orleans because of Hurricane Katrina. Though it sounds melodramatic, I really did spend most of the rest of that year crying in people’s spare rooms. 

Two years later, Maupin surprised us by publishing Michael Tolliver Lives, the first new Tales of the City book in 18 years. When it arrived in the mail after I pre-ordered it, I sprawled on the couch and read it in one sitting. And then I promptly burst into tears.

I was so glad to reconnect with my old friends. And, in the midst of catching up with the characters, we learn that Mona died of breast cancer. The resonances of Mona, the free-spirited bohemian character in the novels, dying of breast cancer with my own artist aunt’s dying of ovarian cancer were so powerful for me. Among the many things that Aunt Julie taught me was the concept of synchronicity; she herself kept a synchronicity journal. Reading Michael Tolliver Lives was such a wonderful experience of it.

Since then, Maupin published Mary Ann in Autumn in 2016 and The Days of Anna Madrigal in 2017. And in 2019, Netflix produced Armistead Maupin’s Tales of the City. This new miniseries tries to take these characters and place them in the present day, but still have some of the original actors (including Olympia Dukakis and Laura Linney) reprise their roles. My opinion of the new series requires its own post, as the fiddling with the timelines angered me–primarily because of the way that it resulted in the erasure of all of Generation X, my own generation. It’s become a joke that Gen X is the forgotten generation, but there were wonderful Gen X characters throughout the series–the twins Anna and Edgar, to begin with, as well as Shawna–and I’ve been disappointed that they’ve either been forgotten or shuffled into a different demographic.

Which brings me to Mona of the Manor, the brand new Tales of the City book that just came out this week. It’s sent in 1993 in the Cotswolds, where Mona is living in the crumbling family estate that belonged to her late mail-order husband, Lord Teddy Roughton. He had been looking to escape all of the trappings of the aristocracy into which he’d been born, and the two of them worked out a deal where she married him so that he could become a US citizen and live his dreams as an out and proud gay man in San Francisco, while Mona escaped the doldrums that her life in the States had become by moving to Easley House and becoming lady of the manor.

In this novel, Mona is in her late 40s: the same age that Aunt Julie was when we watched the first series on tv, and the age that I am now. Mona and her adopted son Wilfred are mourning the loss of so many of their friends to AIDS–it’s sometimes difficult to remember what it was like then, the decimation wrought by the disease. As I sat down to read the novel after it arrived Tuesday night, I appreciated hearing Mona’s familiar voice in my head, the historical contextualization of the American guests who were fans of Jesse Helms (for whom Maupin himself once worked), and the bits of pop culture that appeared at the novel’s beginning. I especially enjoyed the appearance of this Erasure poster, which the sheltered American tourist mistakenly assumes is a religious reference.

(I admit, I went online and ordered a postcard of this poster, as I totally would have done and sent to Aunt Julie, were she here. I will instead enjoy it myself as a souvenir of this novel.)

Much of the novel is devoted to the love lives of Mona and Wilfred, and there’s an appropriately Maupinian subplot that involves having to hide a corpse. Wilfred’s encounter with a famous closeted celebrity while cruising in the woods reminds me of an earlier novel that involved a fictionalized reference to Rock Hudson (whom Maupin himself knew and talks about in his memoir). We get to see what happens between Mona and the postmistress, a relationship alluded to in an earlier novel. 

The early relationship between Mona and Poppy the postmistress is one of my favorite parts of the novel. Poppy is artistic, creating calligraphy place cards and signs for Easley House, and she’s inspired by Mona’s beauty to re-create Sir John Everett Millais’s painting of Elizabeth Siddall as Ophelia. Especially thinking about Chloe Webb’s portrayal of Mona in the first Tales series, this is such an intriguing idea.

Mona’s decision to pose for the portrait and the way that artistic creation brings them closer was such an enjoyable part of the novel.

And I’m kind of mad that it didn’t last. I get what Maupin was doing, using Poppy as an example of the appalling TERF-dom that has claimed a section of British feminism. Still, given that we know that Mona will die of breast cancer in the next decade, it seems unfair that she gets such short shrift in terms of her love life. In an earlier novel, she observes that it’s impossible to have a hot job, a hot apartment, and a hot lover all at the same time. I suppose that Easley House allows her to have two out of the three. 

I finished the novel Wednesday night, and upon closing the covers, I felt glad about having reunited with some of my favorite characters in fiction. However, now that the stories have had a couple of days to sink in, I’m starting to feel the missed opportunities. Since the last couple of novels have been quite Mary Ann-centric (and she’s become a lot less sympathetic of a character), I’m fine with her not appearing. And while I’m glad that Michael and Anna appeared, they almost seem to have been an afterthought. What I love about the earlier novels was both the heartfelt connections between the characters and the I Love Lucy-esque hijinks that took place. Mona keeps them out of the pagan-festival-corpse-plot to spare them, she says, but given the adventures that they have shared in the past, I think that both Michael and Anna could have handled it. 

By not including them in that subplot, it felt as though Mona was drawing a line between her old life and her new one. The most heartfelt conversations occur between Michael and Wilfred, and between Mona and Rhonda, the new American tourist. We only get one conversation between Mona and Michael. And while they talk a bit about their losses, and about their love lives, much of their conversation is about backing away from difficult feelings. The strongest emotions expressed are Mona’s maternal worries about Wilfred, and her warning Michael away from him. There was a missed opportunity for reflection about the nature of long-term friendships here–even the fact that perhaps the passage of time and distance as well as the grief that they have both suffered throughout the AIDS epidemic creates something of an emotional barrier between them that they find difficult to scale. 

We do, at least, have Wilfred (who has appeared in an earlier book) to represent Gen X. But unlike earlier novels, I found quite a dearth of pop culture references, which is unusual. I found myself a bit adrift when I began reading, trying to figure out exactly when the novel took place. The Erasure poster that’s referenced is from 1988. The celebrity in the woods was still closeted in 1993 and didn’t come out until 1998. The Jesse Helms reference does ground it somewhat historically. It’s true that Easley House, so far out in the Cotswolds, is cut off from much of contemporary culture–that may be what appeals to Mona about it, especially given how much grief she’s experienced from the losses from AIDS. But again, I’d have liked to have seen some more reflection on this, since most of the other novels are so wonderfully grounded in their cultural moments.

My goodness–I’ve almost filled five pages with my musings about these books. When PBS was doing their Great American Read campaign, MGA’s library posted photos online of faculty members with their favorite book from the list of 100. I posed with my stack of Tales books. They really are an important part of my life–connecting me back to my 20-year-old self, connecting me to Aunt Julie, and just as a wonderful series of books that have entertained and sustained me for decades, reinforcing the value of community and looking for ways to live authentically. I feel a bit as though I’ve returned from a family reunion, and I’m working through the complicated feelings that such things evoke.

I’ll read it again, I know, and I’ve already got books 4-6 out to re-read (I’m especially looking forward to re-reading the Wimminwood scenes, and to take another look at DeeDee’s relationship with her mother). There’s a pretty good chance that I’ll write more about that here–indeed, I have even been threatening to try my hand at fan fiction and try to tell the story of Anna and Edgar. We need to know more about them!
