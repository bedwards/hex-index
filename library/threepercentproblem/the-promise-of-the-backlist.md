Since I process the orders via our website every day or two, I’ll pick up on little runs of orders for a semi-obscure backlist title of ours. Occasionally this adds up to a significant number of direct sales—say 40-50 copies of a title initially published a decade ago, all ordered within a month—but usually the numbers are more modest, yet the pattern is identifiable. Like with the 8 copies of Juan Gelman’s *[Dark Times Filled with Light](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824689)* that we recently sold over a couple weeks. Or, pulling back a bit, the couple-year-long uptick in orders for two of our three Ingrid Winterbach titles: *[To Hell with Cronjé](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824306)* (2010), *[The Book of Happenstance](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824337)* (2011), and *[The Elusive Moth](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824771)* (2014).

Anytime this happens, I text Tony and Andy from *[Life on Books](http://lifeonbooks.com/)*, a podcast that has generated, literally, hundreds of sales through our website. Almost always for backlist titles[1](#footnote-1)—like *[The Brother](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781940953441)* by Rein Raud & Adam Cullen, or *[Rock, Paper, Scissors](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781940953168)* by Naja Marie Aidt & K.E. Semmel, two titles unlikely to be shelved at your local bookstore—and condensed into such a short window of time, making it incredibly easy to credit them with this sudden rush of interest. And for which I’d like to publicly thank them! Their podcast is one of the best book-centric podcasts out there, and the time and effort and passion they’ve put into it is inspiring. Plus, they obviously have good taste!

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CMmo!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8f55182c-20b4-4b08-b4b1-176f57cacbdb_220x340.jpeg)



](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781940953441)

But neither *[Life on Books](https://lifeonbooks.com/)* nor our periodic subject- or site-wide flash sales can account for all the various backlist blips that I pick up on. From a perspective of developing future marketing strategies it would be nice to know exactly what is driving an influx of orders for a book that’s been dormant for an extended period of time, but it’s pretty hard to pin down. Especially when you expand your scope to all book sales and not just the ones on our website. (Although to reiterate, given the nature of the bookselling business, at least 90% of our backlist is only available through special orders or online bookshops.) A sudden jump in sales for a particular title across sales channels could be the result of a mid-sized book club, an enthusiastic bookseller, a random TikTok, a mention on a local radio program, a plug by a famous author or celebrity, or, just something in the ether.

Knowledge is half the battle and all that, but if the triggering event behind a surge in interest in an older title isn’t replicable—and there aren’t that many *[Life on Books](https://lifeonbooks.com/)* out there, and even fewer with the reader participation and loyalty of their fanbase and discord group—maybe it’s not all that valuable to identify what’s causing these sales bumps. Instead, what would be more valuable is figuring out how, in the aggregate, to keep backlist titles finding new audiences and generating a level of nearly passive income to pave the way to sustainability.

\---

For a nonprofit like Open Letter, there are three main pathways to survival: 1) a runaway mega-bestseller, like *[My Brilliant Friend](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9798889661443)* by Elena Ferrante & Ann Goldstein, or, more recently and relevantly, *[I Who Have Never Known Men](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781945492600)* by Jacqueline Harpman & Ros Schwartz; 2) a truly major donor or foundation committed to the organization’s success, an extreme example of which being [Ruth Lilly and](https://www.nytimes.com/2002/11/19/books/lilly-heir-makes-100-million-bequest-to-poetry-magazine.html) *[Poetry](https://www.nytimes.com/2002/11/19/books/lilly-heir-makes-100-million-bequest-to-poetry-magazine.html)*, but I’d also put the long-running support from the Lannan Foundation for presses like Archipelago, Copper Canyon, Graywolf, BOA Editions, and others, into this category; and 3) a substantial backlist with enough annual sales to underwrite artist payments for all frontlist titles.

Achieving any one of these requires a certain amount of staff time, financial investment, strategic thinking—and luck. And on the flipside, leaning into any one of these three pathways at the expense of the the other two brings with it some level of risk.

[

](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781945492600)

The fact that a significant portion of titles that really breakout are complete surprises for everyone involved points to the fact that there’s no way to guarantee a book will be a best-seller.[2](#footnote-2) Books with the largest advances and most sophisticated marketing campaigns flop on the regular, and on a more metaphysical level, once a nonprofit publisher starts chasing best-sellers, they tend to lose sight of their mission and transform into a different sort of publisher in which producing books can become more important than art and community.[3](#footnote-3)

Finding an angel donor is also a daunting proposition that generally requires a lot of research and cultivation, a personal connection (which can require a decent amount of time and money to develop), and the right financial circumstances for the potential donor (a sudden windfall for instance).

Both of those strategies are very boom or bust, with the ceiling being sky high when you hit—selling 100,000 copies of one book or receiving a $10 million PayPal transfer from a Silicon Valley millionaire alters the calculus for a press’s survival strategy (see, for instance, how Graywolf parlayed the unexpected success of *[Out Stealing Horses](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781555978440)* into a remarkable expansion, absolutely transforming the press’s long-term outlook and ability to achieve thier mission)—but also extremely distressing when you print 8,000 copies of a book and sell 1,200. Or spend a few years kowtowing to a mercurial tech “genius” only to have to have them pivot to funding longevity research, or Mars colonization.[4](#footnote-4)

Which brings us to the idea of the backlist as a working endowment—something that John O’Brien advocated for, and which is, in my opinion, the best option for a sustainable business model not nearly wedded to chance events.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!hqCS!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa5d1b416-5e37-4c98-8dfd-5eecd88e40d1_220x340.jpeg)



](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824337)
*We will get to Winterbach . . . soonish.*

The concept is simple: If you can sell XXX number of copies on average of XXX number of backlist titles every year, you can subsidize the press’s core expenses and provide a stable base from which to best fulfill your mission and take risks on books you believe in, but which are unlikely to become best-sellers.

For example, when I started at Dalkey in July 2000, the press had been around for sixteen years and had published 219 titles. If all of those averaged 100 sales per year (a modest, reasonable figure), the backlist would generate approximately $125,000/year after removing bookstore discounts and distribution fees. That might not seem like much, but it’s the equivalent of two to three NEA grants. Or a couple salaries. It can also serve as a buffer in case you have a string of books that struggle to find their audience. Or if your annual fundraising campaigns falter.

To provide even more context, at that time, Dalkey’s overall budget was in the ballpark of $400,000, so $125,000 is a *significant* chunk of money. Especially since there was a ton of extra copies of these backlist titles just sitting in the warehouse.[5](#footnote-5) But imagine if you had, as Dalkey does now, 1,000 titles in the backlist, that figure could be as high as $625,000 a year. Now we’re cooking with gas!

\---

What I’m about to say may seem unambitious or even anti-capitalistic, and is something I want to game out over an extended series of posts, but my dream for Open Letter’s medium- to long-term future is to be financially and spiritually sustainable without harnessing our hopes to a series of sudden sales successes or manifesting a millionaire sympathetic to our particular vibe. Not that I would reject either of those! I just don’t want to rely on them in order to be a proper publishing house—meaning we pay our artists fairly and on time, do the proper amount of marketing for our titles, and never be forced to deviate from our editorial standards for financial reasons.

On the spiritual side of things: I have no desire for Open Letter to expand into being the “biggest” press out there, or “most lauded” or “most important” or any of that. I don’t personally need any special awards or accolades to feel validated. I just want to try as best I can, with what limited financial and emotional resources I have, to create a community of book people who enjoy creating the art they want to create for the readers who enjoy reading these sorts of books. This sounds simple and obvious, but we live in a world that’s overrun by grifters determined to chip away at what would allow the average person to live a comfortable life, who make business more difficult for the mid-sized press (see Edelweiss straight gouging presses in search of profit), and who operate under a belief of “infinite growth,” making the idea of stability appear as a net loss, instead of what it should be seen as—an opportunity to have a sustainable, healthy society in which we’re not all overwhelmed by stress and the internalized pressure to do more, sell more, produce more. (OK, enough of this before I either veer into mystical nonsense or a ten-page rant against the current phase of soul-gutting late-stage capitalism.)

\---

As I write this in December 2025, Open Letter has published 184 unique titles. That’s not too dissimilar from where Dalkey Archive was when I first started there twenty-five years ago. Now, I’m not equating the success of Open Letter’s first two decades with that of Dalkey—the book business is so different these days that it’s almost apples and oranges—but 184 books is substantial. Too many for me to name them all off the top of my head (although as a way to procrastinate, I’ve been trying to create a complete list . . . and I’m getting close!), and far too many to evaluate simply by looking at the raw data.

[

](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781940953694)
*A backlist book I think a lot of general readers would love.*

Which is why I became obsessed this past summer with the idea of finding a way to tease out various trends so that I could tell which backlist titles are outperforming expectations and which need a boost. I didn’t know then—and don’t know now—exactly how I would really use this information to increase our backlist sales, but with our twentieth anniversary not too far off in the future (September 2028), I have the compulsion to reread everything we’ve done to date and to try and puzzle out some mechanism for bringing more of these books to the attention of readers who might enjoy them. Rather than selecting a starting point at random for this journey through the backlist, or falling into the trap of rereading the most obvious titles, I thought I would see what’s “hot” right now and, as much as possible, approach our nearly 200 title backlist as if I were a casual reader discovering the catalog for the first time.

And since stats are cool, I created one for our backlist: tAVG+.

Although you could argue that a casual reader is most likely to come across the books that have the most life-to-date sales, that’s pretty lazy and it’s my philosophical belief that by focusing on total sales volume you’re ignoring what’s currently of interest to readers—which I think is more useful, since it’s based in present conditions, not what the world was like when a particular book came out.

So what I did was take every title with six years of backlist sales (all sales data is through June 2025, the end of the most recent fiscal year), calculated the average number of sales for each title across that period, compared the sales for each of the six years against that average, then scaled it all to 100 so that any year in which a book outsold its average its tAVG+ would be above 100, ones in which sales were below average would result in a tAVG+ under 100.[6](#footnote-6)

One benefit of tAVG+ is that it allowed me to compare titles against one another without total sales getting in the way. *[To Hell with Cronjé](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824306)* could have the same tAVG+ as *[Zone](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824269)* regardless of the fact that *[Zone](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824269)* sells way more copies per year. And by putting this all on the same playing field, I could run a macro separating the titles into quartiles, color code them, and get both a numerical and visual representation of a book’s “hotness” in terms of backlist sales.

It’s still a bit of a mess to look at, and each book likely has a story behind it—*[The Brother](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781940953441)* and *[Rock, Paper, Scissors](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781940953168)* are aforementioned Life on Books titles, and Can Xue being in the running for the Nobel explains *[Vertical Motion](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824375)* and *[Frontier](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781940953540)—*but it’s still interesting. Especially when you *don’t* have a nice pre-packaged explanation for a book’s success. Why did the two Quim Monzo books (*[Gasoline](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824184)* and *[A Thousand Morons](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824412)*) bounce around over the past couple years? And *[Traces of Time](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781940953144)*—a poetry collection from 2015—has the eighth highest tAVG+ for FY2025? I don’t know what any of this means, but it did draw my attention to one author in particular: Ingrid Winterbach.

First off, it must be stated that Ingrid Winterbach was one of E. J.’s authors. For anyone who doesn’t know, I didn’t found Open Letter by myself, but alongside Nathan Furl (operations manager, who was a longtime Dalkey Archive employee) and E. J. VanLanen (editor, had moved from HarperCollins to Dalkey just in time to relocate to Rochester and start Open Letter). Both have since moved on, but their roles in the creation of Open Letter and its ethos can not be overstated.

E. J. and I split a lot of acquisitions duties in the years when he was at the press. I can’t remember ever disagreeing on a particular novel or author that we ended up publishing, but there were different authors and books we each championed and/or discovered. And Winterbach was someone E. J. brought to the table.

Even now, I don’t know all that much about her outside of what we included on our “about the author” pages:

> Ingrid Winterbach is an artist and novelist whose work has won South Africa’s M-Net Prize, Old Mutual Literary Prize, the University of Johannesburg Prize for Creative Writing, and the W.A. Hofmeyr Prize. *[To Hell with Cronjé](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824306)* won the 2004 Hertzog Prize, an honor she shares with the novelists Breyten Breytenbach and Etienne Leroux. Her novels *[To Hell with Cronjé](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824306), [The Book of Happenstance](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824337)*, and *[The Elusive Moth](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824771)* are all available from Open Letter Books.

Thanks to her Wikipedia page, I can add that she was born in 1948, that for a time she wrote under the pseudonym Lettie Viljoen, and that there are at least four other works of hers that have been translated into English but are not currently available in the U.S. I can also say that she’s worked with a number of translators: Elsa Silke for *[To Hell with Cronjé](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824306)*, Iris Gouws for *[The Elusive Moth](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824771)*, Dirk Winterbach for *[The](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824337)* [](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824337)*[Book of Happenstance](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824337)*, Leon de Kock for *The Road of Excess*, and Michiel Heyns for *The Troubled Times of Magrieta Prinsloo*, *It Might Get Loud*, and *The Shallows.*

*[To Hell with Cronjé](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824306)* was the first of her books that we published, and the title I decided to read first in my personal journey through our backlist. It was originally published in Afrikaans in 2002, then in English translation in South Africa in 2007, with our edition coming out in 2010. It was the twenty-second book we published. It came out five months after the beloved *[Agaat](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781951142209)* by Marlene van Niekerk & the aforementioned Michiel Heyns (for more info on this title, see the [Big Book Project](https://thebigbookproject.substack.com/p/agaat-by-marlene-van-nieker-review)), which to some marked the advent of a new moment for South African literature. J. M. Coetzee won the Nobel in 2003 and Ivan Vladislavić was experiencing some success with books coming out from Penguin Random House, And Other Stories, and Archipelago, so the time was ripe for an Afrikaans Wave, so to speak.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kSCK!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Feedf7791-7d32-43a9-87d5-ec7667bc9d06_220x340.jpeg)



](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824771)

Going into my reread, all I could really remember about *[To Hell with Cronjé](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824306)* was the overarching vibe. And not even that, really. More an image of the landscape, vast and somewhat barren, in sync with the existentially bleak nature of the book. That and a scene in which a group of men sat around a campfire talking about war, which I remember liking quite a bit.

The novel is set at the very end of the Second Boer War between the British and the Boer republics (South Africa Republic and the Orange Free State, meaning the Dutch) over control of the region. As we know as readers, the British win out in the end, and at the time when *[To Hell with Cronjé](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824306)* is set, the men fighting on the side of the Boer republics are broken, defeated, and dispersed, reporting to a series of lunatics and trying to stay alive until the treaty is signed and the Boers surrender.

Our main characters are Reitz and Ben, a geologist and botanist respectively, who are extremely jaded when it comes to the war, but keeping sane by using their free time to do research. In terms of their inner lives, Ben wants to sneak away to see his family; Reitz wants to come to terms with the spirit of his wife who passed away right before he joined the fight. When the story starts they’re helping Willem escort Abraham back to his home, away from the chaos of battle. Abraham has lost his mind completely after seeing his brother killed in battle right in front of eyes, can no longer speak coherently, a tragic victim of shell shock.

What struck me most on this reread was the amount of humor to be found amid this depressing backdrop. The various commanders are all nuts and totally unstable, almost cartoon-like at times. Conversations around the campfire tilt toward the absurd with everyone talking at cross-purposes, monologuing for an audience of no one, failing to find solace amid the chaos. Reitz and Ben riffing off one another is another highlight, which has some low-level *Letterkenny* vibes as they “yes and” lists upon lists as they gallop along. And the scene in which our protagonists try and explain fossils and evolution to their comrades, all of whom are Biblical literalists, has some of the same beats as a sit-com. In other words, there’s some Beckettian humor in here (Tom Stoppard’s *Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead* also came to mind), and I’m willing to be that this is part of what drew E. J. to the novel.

None of that is to take away from the gravity of this book—at its core it is an existentially bleak book about the horrors of war, and I don’t want you to think that it’s some sort of commentary. Anderson Tepper put it best in his piece for *[Words Without Borders](https://wordswithoutborders.org/book-reviews/ingrid-winterbachs-to-hell-with-cronje/): “[To Hell with Cronjé](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824306)* is a grim, dark, unrelenting book—an exhaustive survey of the sensations of war, from headlice and crippling thirst to grief, suffering, and madness.”

Overall, I was truly blown away rereading this, by how powerful and in control the prose is, how compelling the narrative. I have to get my hands on her more recent books, and will definitely go back to her other two Open Letter titles sooner rather than later, but I think the next step in my journey through our backlist is going to be *[Landscape in Concrete](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781934824146)* by Jakov Lind & Ralph Manheim. Another war book—one with a 186 tAVG+ for FY2025.

[1](#footnote-anchor-1)

“Backlist” refers to any book we’ve published more than a year ago, with “frontlist” referring to all recently released titles that are still in their initial sales and returns cycle. Most titles see an increase in gross sales over the first month or two after release as reviews come in, word of mouth spreads, events take place, etc., followed by a spate of returns as bookstores cull their unsold inventory to make room for the next batch of frontlist titles. Net sales for a title usually plateau at this point, and the book is considered to be backlist.
