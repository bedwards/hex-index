Things move fast during awards season.

Just a couple weeks ago, the 2025 [Queen Sofia Spanish Institute Translation Prize Longlist](https://queensofiaspanishinstitute.org/literature/longlist-announcement-2025-queen-sofia-spanish-institute-translation-prize/) was announced and included one of our books: *[Of Beasts and Fowls](https://www.openletterbooks.org/products/of-beasts-and-fowls)* by Pilar Adón & Katie Whittemore. This is a great honor for a great book, and I highly recommended listening to [this podcast](https://threepercentproblem.substack.com/p/three-percent-191-pilar-adon-of-beasts-ed9?utm_source=publication-search), recorded during Pilar’s U.S. tour last fall, to get a better sense of what makes this lush, wondrous book about a cult/non-cult, about signs and symbols, about finding yourself, and about water.

That’s a hell of a commitment, and with three more coming in 2026 (*[The City](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781960385499)* by Lara Moreno & Alice Banks & Katie Whittemore, *The Family* by Sara Mesa & Katie Whittemore, and *Mantra* by Rodrido Fresán & Will Vanderhyden), Open Letter must be one of the most prolific publishers of Spanish-language literature today.

Open Letter Subscriptions!!

Since our inception, we’ve been offering 5-book and 10-book subscriptions covering a half-year or full-year of our publishing schedule. This has been our lifeblood over the past (almost) two decades, a baseline of sales for each title that can make up for the increasing difficulties of getting books onto the shelves of local, independent bookstores. (Not to mention Barnes & Noble, or cutting through the noise of Amazon and all its self-published titles and its recommendation algorithm that mostly works against us.)

It’s also a way for Open Letter fans to make sure they don’t miss out on anything—which is especially key for the completists out there—and, if all goes according to plan, starting in 2026, all subscribers will be invited to special, private “book clubs” with the author and/or translator of each Open Letter title a couple of months after release (so that you have time to read it). Still nailing down the logistics, but these will be informal opportunities to discuss our books with fellow readers and our artists. (And I’ll be there as well, although that’s probably less of a draw.)

Last thing: I’ve teased this a million times, but I’m not actively in process of updating the Translation Database—and could use your help!

This was started in 2008 to provide a comprehensive list of all titles published in translation for the first time (no retranslations, no paperback editions of previous published hardcovers) in the United States (or at least distributed here). We began by tracking fiction and poetry, and when we moved the database to *[Publishers Weekly](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/translation/home/index.html)*, we added both nonfiction and children’s books.

As the only source in the world collecting this information, it’s served as an invaluable tool for analyzing publishing trends, researching international literature, identifying who is publishing what, etc. During the pandemic . . . it all went awry. And due to changes in my relationship with *Publishers Weekly* the database has been very dormant the past couple years.

But it’s time to change that! The question as to where the [Translation Database](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/16pldMnf6fDj8LGMbdcb6zICIgRVAt177ypmNZcyXG0A/edit?usp=sharing) will be hosted in the future is an open one (anyone know how to design a website like the *PW* one? If so, hit me up), but for now, you can find [ALL the data on this Google Sheet](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/16pldMnf6fDj8LGMbdcb6zICIgRVAt177ypmNZcyXG0A/edit?usp=sharing).

As you’ll see, there’s a lot missing. Children’s and Nonfiction are still a work in (backward) progress as I work from 2025 toward 2008 to fill in the gaps; and everything post-2021ish is pretty spotty.

But you can help! If you’re a publisher of translations who sends out hard copies of your books, please consider sending them my way (Chad W. Post, University of Rochester, Morey Hall 303, Rochester NY 14627) and I’ll log them by hand, include them on a “Books Received” post, and consider including them in monthly posts about findings from the Translation Database. (Ebooks are fine as well, although I can almost assure you I will lose these in my email, or bury them in some other way.)

Also, publishers, authors, translators, literature fans can [fill out this form](https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLSfp2AtU_tOjY9n0GuAFoS3mM_8OayAfVY0utgzFCqKtWlnyhQ/viewform?usp=dialog) to the best of their ability, and, after vetting, I’ll be able to add these to the database. Don’t expect this to happen immediately, but the plan is to update the database once a week in whatever form it ends up taking.

It’s not easy to sort the old Three Percent website to capture *all* of the posts that relied on the Translation Database for info—there are a ton of seemingly random articles about things like Scandi crime novels or translations from Canada, or whatever, that originated from asking a question about the data—but here’s [an introduction to the database and some of its uses](https://www.rochester.edu/College/translation/threepercent/?s=translation+database&x=0&y=0).

Thanks in advance to anyone who helps out with this, and even if it takes six months or a year to get it all up to date and as complete as possible, I’m dedicated to making this happen. Frankly, I’m sick of not being able to find this information in one handy place, and saddened by the fact that I no longer have a tight handle on all the translations coming out . . .
