Back in the fall of 2017,[1](#footnote-1) and thanks to Polish translator [Sean Gasper Bye](https://www.sgbye.com/), I had the opportunity to attend the [Conrad Festival in Kraków](https://en.conradfestival.pl/) and spend a few days talking with Polish agents, editors, authors, translators, etc.—a fairly typical editorial trip tied to an important literary festival. I love Poland, in part because both sides of my family are Kashubian, from the area near Gdansk, and as such, I feel an ancestral kinship with the country and culture, but I also absolutely love their literature. From Bruno Schulz to Tadeusz Konwicki to Olga Tokarczuk to Jerzy Pilch to Wiesław Myśliwski to Wojciech Nowicki to the reportage writers listed below, I’m a big fan and am always excited when a new Polish translation comes across my desk.[2](#footnote-2)

Anyway, the Big Result from this trip was the decision for Open Letter to publish a series of works of Polish Reportage, starting with five titles over five years, and then expanding outward. Before getting into the texts—and, specifically, *Auroville*—I want to publicly thank Sean Gasper Bye and Antonia Lloyd-Jones for their help with this. They were critical to selecting which titles to include, and translated the bulk of them as well.

For anyone not in the know, Polish Reportage is a long-standing Polish literary tradition that, put simply, utilizes fictional techniques in reporting on real life situations. Ryszard Kapuściński is probably the most famous practitioner outside of Poland’s borders, but Hannah Krall, Artur Domosławski, and Małgorzata Szejnert are three other big names. There is a special school to train writers in this tradition, and a huge number of books falling under the banner of “Polish Reportage” are released every year.[3](#footnote-3)

Three things that I love about this type of writing (and the books we’ve done) are: 1) the subjects written about are not tied to Poland, so it’s automatically international in scope; 2) American sales reps, booksellers, and readers don’t always know what to make of this (catnip for me and my love of unique works and befuddlement), since the works are technically true and factual, but by acknowledging how the author’s viewpoint impacts the representation of the narrative (like quantum theory, in a way), these books sort of float between traditional reporting and personal essay; and 3) for whatever reason—subject plus approach—these books tend to avoid the “last chapter” problem that occurs in 95% of nonfiction works published by the Big Five in which insane shit is stuffed in, seemingly just to pad the page count (calling you out, Malcolm Gladwell).

To date, we’ve done four books in this series: *[Foucault in Warsaw](https://www.openletterbooks.org/collections/poland/products/foucault-in-warsaw)* by Remigiusz Ryziński & Sean Gasper Bye, *[Roosters Crow, Dogs Cry](https://www.openletterbooks.org/collections/poland/products/roosters-crow-dogs-cry)* by Wojciech Tochman & Antonia Lloyd Jones, *[Did This Hand Kill?](https://www.openletterbooks.org/collections/poland/products/did-this-hand-kill)* by Cezary Łazarewicz & Sean Gasper Bye, and *[Ganbare! Workshops on Dying](https://www.openletterbooks.org/collections/poland/products/ganbare-workshops-on-dying)* by the subjects of this post, Katarzyna Boni & Mark Ordon. #### **From** ***[Auroville: A City of Dreams](https://bookshop.org/a/164/9781960385475)*****:**

I first heard about Auroville by chance, in 2009, when I was traveling through southern India. I read in a guidebook that right near Pondicherry, a former French colony, there is a city in which people from all over the world are trying to build a society without money, private ownership, institutions, religion, or divisions. Everybody is invited.

I accepted the invitation. I signed up as a volunteer, planting trees in the desert. A week went by and I wanted to run away. Wherever I turned, I saw the eyes of a woman from various pictures watching me; Aurovilians called her the Mother. For me, it brought to mind the eyes of a leader who held the monopoly for truth. Everybody was quoting the words of some guru, who it seems is quite famous in India, because he fought for independence even before Gandhi appeared, but after he spent time in jail he decided that rather than liberate India, he would prefer to spiritually change the world. But I had never heard of anybody named Sri Aurobindo. Aurovilians did not have time to talk, they were much too occupied with their mission. I would spend my days with other volunteers. On our way to the seaside, we would pass through Indian villages where goats, dogs, and children played in the gutters, and women washed themselves at public faucets, covering their naked bodies with a piece of sari.

While sitting over a date shake, a French girl with a heart defect, whose father did not let her leave the house until she turned eighteen, told me that she wanted to join Auroville. I asked why. When she gave a dreamy-eyed answer that it was the only place on Earth where she could become a better person, I lost my temper.

Is this really the only place you can become a better person? So you don’t have any divisions or racism here? Is everybody really invited to come to Auroville? Why did we pay for these shakes with rupees, if money was supposed to become worthless? Haven’t they heard about Communism, a system that I grew up in? Don’t they know where belief in a utopia can lead you?

The level of naivety and blind idealism became unbearable.

I left.

Eight years later, I came back to see what I really ran away from.

THE PLOT UNFOLDS (1968)

The first guests from Pondicherry arrived as early as six in the morning. They came in school buses, stylish Mercedes, elegant Ambassadors, and more modest Tatas, in rikshas, on bicycles, or bullock carts. They also came by foot. From afar, they could see an orange balloon floating over the lone banyan tree that the Mother selected to be the center of the city of the future. For three weeks, night and day, two hundred workers dug a trench that was 2 meters deep and 75 meters wide—the amphitheater. They made a small hill in the middle of the trench; a white urn shaped like a lotus bud was placed on its peak. Twelve India cork trees were planted around the amphitheater. Their real name, as discovered by the Mother, was Transformation. Just a day before the inauguration, people were still decorating the marble urn with mosaic and sketching traditional kolams on the ground.

It was a miracle they made it on time.

Dignitaries were seated under a canopy made of keet (dried palm leaves). The governor of Pondicherry was there, wearing a white side cap. As was the head of the UNESCO mission in Delhi, sporting a pair of sunglasses. The minister of information and broadcasting was also there. Other guests included the American consul from Madras, the representative of the British High Commissioner, the former Commissioner of French India, and even a representative from the Embassy of the Polish People’s Republic in Delhi. They took their seats in the company of young delegates from 124 countries around the world. The remaining guests sought out places on bare ground and opened black umbrellas to shield themselves from the sun. The inhabitants of local villages also came, dressed in their absolute best: white lungi and shirts pressed with a charcoal iron. A separate area was designated for them and demarcated with rope.

A gong sounded at precisely ten thirty. Silence fell upon the crowd. The sound of an organ resonated from the speakers, playing the first bars of a song composed especially for the occasion. And then, the Mother spoke in French from her room at rue François Martin 28 in Pondicherry. She had turned ninety just a week earlier. Her shaky voice floated over the desert through the telephone line. For this special occasion, the Telephone Agency cut off all other users in the area.

—*Salut d’Auroville* to all men of good will. Are invited to Auroville all those who thirst for progress and aspire to a higher and truer life.

The woman read the Auroville Charter, the guidelines for a better world.

One. Auroville belongs to nobody in particular. Auroville belongs to humanity as a whole. But to live in Auroville one must be the willing servitor of the Divine Consciousness.

Two. Auroville will be the place of an unending education, of constant progress, and a youth that never ages.

Three. Auroville wants to be the bridge between the past and the future. Taking advantage of all discoveries from without and from within, Auroville will boldly spring toward future realizations.

Four. Auroville will be a site of material and spiritual researches for a living embodiment of an actual human unity.

\*

The guests later recalled that they were overcome by a mighty yet serene force. The air was filled with energy. Some saw the smiling face of the Mother in the branches of the banyan tree.

When they started reading the Auroville Charter in English, two teenagers walked up to the white urn shaped like a lotus bud. The girl was carrying a flag with the symbol of the Mother, while the boy held a porcelain bowl with soil from the burial site of Sri Aurobindo, whose teachings were an inspiration for the ideals of the city. The boy also had a long metal container holding an Auroville Charter handwritten by the Mother. He lowered it to the bottom of the urn on blue ribbons and covered it with the soil. When the Charter was being read in the Tamil and Sanskrit languages, children with soil from each of the twenty-four Indian states came up to the urn. After them, yet another pair appeared with a plaque decorated with green, red, and black ribbons with the inscription “Afghanistan.” The children poured the Afghan soil into the urn. The Charter was read out in Arabic. Following them were representatives of Australia, Belgium, Bhutan, Burundi, China, Chad, Cyprus, Czechoslovakia, the Dominican Republic, Ethiopia, and Fiji. Iraq and Iran walked alongside Israel when the Charter was being read in Hebrew. Mexicans, Nepali, and Nigerians added their native soil to words uttered in Norwegian; Saudi Arabia, Singapore, and South Yemen heard Spanish; Syria, Thailand, Türkiye, and Tibet were accompanied by Swedish, while representatives of the United States, Soviet Union, and Great Britain heard Tibetan. As the pair holding the soil from Poland walked into the amphitheater, the Charter was being read in Russian. Most of the children came from Delhi, having been dispatched by the embassies. If anybody was missing, the particular country was represented by pupils from the school next to the Sri Aurobindo Ashram in Pondicherry. If a country didn’t send any soil, sea salt was poured instead.

Once the soil from Venezuela, Vietnam, Yugoslavia, and Zambia were added to the urn, the last pair of children entered the amphitheater. She carried the flag of Auroville, while he held the soil of Auroville. Finally, the most senior of Sri Aurobindo’s disciples, Nolini, walked into the amphitheater and sealed the urn.

Seventeen languages, twenty-four Indian states, one hundred twenty-four countries in the world. Auroville is a place that belongs to everybody. Nobody can claim it to be their own. An international project for peace and Human Unity. The city that the world needs.

The ceremony lasted seventy-five minutes. At a quarter to twelve, the guests retreated from the sun to the shade of the banyan tree and perused the exhibition prepared by the chief architect of Auroville. The tree was decorated with garlands, and under it, next to the bowls filled with fruit and lotus flowers, were panels with quotes from the Mother and Sri Aurobindo (“Humanity is not the last rung of terrestrial creation. Evolution continues and man will be surpassed. It is for each one to know whether he wants to participate in the advent of the new species.”). Placed around its trunk was a metal ring with the words “Auroville, the City at the Service of Truth.” Also on display were models of the terrain around Pondicherry: the sea, the plain, individual settlements, and the emptiness between them. Two circles were marked in the void. Within the first circle was the city, while the second, bigger one, was the forest surrounding it. Also presented was a 3-D cardboard plan of Auroville. It had the shape of a galactic vortex with twelve lines of buildings spreading out from the green center. A stadium stood on the outskirts, and parks were planned between the buildings.

The guests had lunch before they departed. Tea was served from a wagon shaped like a teapot. And then the buses, Mercedes cars, Ambassadors, Tatas, rickshaws, and bicycles all made their way back to where they came from. Clouds of dust whirled behind the departing visitors. The red powder fell on the lonely banyan tree, the amphitheater, and the white urn filled with soil from all the countries in the world.

THE TRUE NATURE OF THE WORLD

Auroson waited until billowing clouds appeared on the horizon. He imagined it was a thundering army. It descended lower and lower, blocking out the world. The downpour came suddenly, as if the clouds were bursting under the weight of the water. The monsoon appeared twice a year in Auroville. Only once actually, because the monsoon in June was minor and did not do anything to the desert. The November one, however, turned the desert into a magical land. The drops washed away the rust-colored dust from the leaves, and the earth, awakened to life, smelled differently. The ravines, hollows, and ditches were filled with water and fish, which the elements would bring from the ponds located higher up. The Aurokids would set traps for the fish and then throw them back into the water. They would also organize hunts for flying ants that emerged from the ground in the evening.

Auroson would inflate his treasure—a small rubber raft—and throw it into the rushing stream which, in the dry season, was just a hollow behind his house. He made a paddle out of a stick and swam between the bushes and fields where the locals were working. He went south, to the place where the hollow joined a larger ravine that was now a real river. He continued on until he reached Kuilapalayam.

But what he liked most was the moment when the rain lashed the ground hard, as if it wanted to whip it. He would find shelter among the roots of a palm tree. A small burrow, probably dug by a jackal. He was hiding in a pit. The trunk protected him from the wind and the palm leaves from the rain. He was wearing his beloved orange shorts. He didn’t want to wear anything else, they had to be washed quite often. He looked at the gray wall of water and the billowing black clouds. They were so low, it seemed as if you could just climb to the top of the palm tree to touch them. But Auroson preferred to stay in his den. The thought of being outside and getting wet, of his shorts sticking to his legs and water dripping from his hair into his eyes, while he was dry and safe, like in a cocoon that kept out both wind and water, and from which he could look out at the gray world, gave him great pleasure.

\*

Of the many stories told by the Mother, perhaps the one about the beginning of the world was most often repeated by adults to children.

Once upon a time there was nothing, and therefore there was only nothing. Nothing was powerful and lonely. Nothing wanted to look at itself. But if nothing was everything, and everything was nothing, how could nothing look at itself?

Nothing was like god. It could do whatever it wanted, but there was one thing it couldn’t do—look at itself. And how could it understand itself without seeing itself?

Nothing really needed to find out. There are different versions of this story, but the Mother said that this was why god created consciousness. Consciousness was the Mother, the divine Mother. She was given the task of creating the world. She gave birth to four beings. The first was powerful and was called Truth. The second illuminated from within and was called Light. The third was full of energy and was called Life. The fourth was good and was called Love. The creatures were free, that was the condition given by god. Together they were to create an ideal world. They played with water and clods of earth, breathed fire, and swirled air between their fingers. They had been cavorting with the elements for a thousand years. The longer they stayed together, the more they became like god. That’s when they rebelled—we will not live under anyone’s orders, we will create our own world, they claimed. But the further they were from the beginning, the more they changed into their own opposite. Truth became Falsehood, Light became Darkness, Life became Death, and Love became Suffering. Four powerful demons, called asuras in India, would have torn the world to pieces, but Nothing asked the divine Mother to start over. This time the creatures were to be a little less free and less independent from their creator. This is how the gods—the devas—came into being. Since the beginning of time there has been a great war between the devas and the asuras. Sometimes it happens between us, sometimes it happens within us.

QUESTIONS

I wrote them down in my notebook right after I got there:

What is Auroville?

How does it work?

What problems does it face?

Who comes to Auroville?

Who stays?

Were you able to create a city that belongs to no one?

Were you able to create an economy without money?

Were you able to create architecture that supports human unity?

Were you able to create an education system that liberates the soul?

Is Auroville a cult?

Is Auroville a place, or a state of mind?

What does it mean to be born in Auroville?

What does it mean to choose Auroville?

What compromises between reality and dreams are we willing to accept?

Is it possible to get rid of the ego?

What does it mean to be a new species of human?

Who is Sri Aurobindo? Who is the Mother?

THE EYES

She watches me from every angle. She watches me buying bread at Auroville Bakery, siting at the table at Farm Fresh, practicing yoga at Pitanga, and listening at the Auroville Bamboo Center to the story of how all the bamboos planted from one rhizome always bloom at the same time—even if they grow on different continents. She’s standing at the cash register, leaning against the wall, and hanging over doors and on the walls of the Aurovilians I meet for interviews. Sometimes there are fresh flowers or strings of jasmine in front of her. Sometimes there are lit incense sticks.

The Mother.

She waits for me at the town hall (she gazes with a half-smile, as if she was mocking me), at the Solar Kitchen (red cape, sunken cheeks, staring gaze), at the Arka, where I’m making an appointment for a massage (pencil sketch—with a narrower face, smaller nose, and sad eyes), and at Svaram, where they invent new instruments. They have singing stones and xylophones with a different division of the octave than the one that has been adopted in European classical music since the time of Bach. The Mother is here wrapped in a floor-length white dress and a white veil. And at Deepam School, for children with special needs from nearby villages, there is a photo of the Mother—a little girl with bangs, a wide collar, and a very serious expression. But at the entrance to Savitri Bhavan—the center of Sri Aurobindo’s thought—the Mother hangs as a much older version of herself, shrunken and wrapped in a white shawl with hand-painted flowers. Her hair is jet black, she probably dyed her gray hair with henna.

She took care of herself. Apparently, when she was unable to get her favorite body milk, Friction de Foucaud, from France, she set up a laboratory at the ashram where, on the basis of oils sent from France, a balm with the same scent was produced for her. Yet most of the photos hanging around Auroville are the reproduction of a photo made by Cartier-Bresson, who visited the ashram in 1950. The mother stands in the doorway in an embroidered dress with short sleeves and an ornate scarf that covers her forehead and eyebrows. Her eyes appear bigger. The only dark spot on a light face—they attract attention. She is seventy-two years old, but her skin is smooth. She once mentioned that because of the inner work she did with Sri Aurobindo, she looked eighteen instead of forty. Friends from the past didn’t recognize her. In the photo she smiles shyly, with her right hand slightly raised, as if she was nodding to invite someone in. This gesture makes me cautious.

Sri Aurobindo always hangs next to the Mother. In Cartier-Bresson’s rendition he is tired and old—the photographer met him a few months before his death. Sri Aurobindo sits in a white dhoti in an armchair with a Japanese wave pattern. He has a long beard, mustache, and evenly-parted hair that falls to his shoulder blades. Heavy eyelids cover his tired eyes. Apparently he didn’t blink once during the ten-minute session.

There is also a group photo from the darshan—that is, meetings with the faithful. Cartier-Bresson was invited on April 24, the anniversary of the Mother’s final arrival in Pondicherry. The Mother and Sri Aurobindo sit propped up on cushions on a couch covered with a jaguar skin. She is in a shiny sari flowing to the floor, with her hair hidden under a white headdress. He is in white, with his right shoulder exposed. They look straight into the lens. The symbol of the Mother towers above them.

During the *darshan*, Sri Aurobindo and the Mother looked at the faithful who felt that two minutes in their presence accelerated their inner work by more than a year’s worth of meditation. And now they look out at the Aurovilians. I see them everywhere. In one of the gardens I even spy clay heads among the miniature cacti. Supposedly their photo stands on an altar in a Hindu temple in Irumbai, next to Shiva or perhaps Vishnu, between chains of orange marigolds, illuminated by the flame of clay oil lamps.

\*

The Mother said there was to be no religion in Auroville. She banned the posting of images—her own and Sri Aurobindo’s. No pictures, she repeated. The Aurovilians did not obey the ban. The black and white eyes fixed on the photographic paper haunt me. If you love someone, you want to have their likeness with you, an Aurovilian tells me. With you—yes. But not everywhere around you. Have I stepped into a cult? Yet knowledge of the texts of the Mother and Sri Aurobindo is not even a necessary condition for admission to the community. You just need to agree with the Auroville Charter.

I don’t see or hear anything except the Mother. I don’t like her gaze. I don’t trust her. I don’t understand.

NEW WORLD

Any other ashram would have closed upon the guru’s death, but not this one. Mirra and the disciples still had work to do. Mirra inaugurated the Sri Aurobindo Education Center and declared that there should be a place on earth which no nation can call its own.

Shortly thereafter she had a vision. It was February 29, 1956, and Mirra was meditating in the Playground with her disciples. She was sitting, as every evening, on a chair under a bas-relief representing the spiritual outline of India, but when she closed her eyes, she found herself in front of the golden gate. She immediately understood that behind it lay the divine consciousness—the Supermind they had been waiting for. It was enough to open the gates and it will flood the entire world. Mirra took up a powerful golden hammer in her hands. She struck. One. Two. Three. The gate shuddered, bowed, and shattered into a thousand pieces. Behind them there was light.

None of the people gathered in the courtyard felt any change. No one had unexpected visions. Mirra laughed quietly at them: unaware as children. A month later she wrote that a new world was born that day.
