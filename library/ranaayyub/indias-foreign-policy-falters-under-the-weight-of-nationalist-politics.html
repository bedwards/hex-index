Narendra Modi’s decade in power has been marked by an aggressive push for Hindu nationalism, recasting the idea of India from a secular democracy into a majoritarian state. While this ideological shift may have consolidated political power at home, it has come at a steep price abroad. India’s foreign policy—once rooted in non-alignment, pluralism, and soft power—is now faltering, increasingly shaped and limited by domestic identity politics. The result: strained alliances, emboldened rivals, and a loss of moral leadership in the region. Now, in the wake of the latest India-Pakistan conflict, India’s global relationships are being tested more severely than ever. One of the starkest casualties of this transformation has been India's relationship with Bangladesh. Once entrenched in strong ties under Sheikh Hasina’s long tenure, the relationship has frayed significantly.

Following a student-led uprising in 2024, Hasina who enjoyed an extremely favourable relationship with New Delhi was ousted from power and subsequently took refuge in India. The interim government led by Nobel laureate Muhammad Yunus has since banned the activities of Hasina’s Awami League party, marking a major political shift in Dhaka.

India is yet to respond to the Bangladesh government's request for the extradition of Hasina over allegations of human rights abuses and corruption. The Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) and National Register of Citizens (NRC), both with religious overtones targeting Muslims in India (labeling many Indian Muslims as Bangladeshi citizens) , had already fueled deep resentment in Bangladesh. Remarks by Indian leaders describing Bangladeshi migrants as "termites" further inflamed public outrage. As political winds shift and China offers non-interference alongside infrastructure and investment, India risks losing its traditional foothold in the country. Bangladesh's interim Chief Adviser, Muhammad Yunus, was invited by Chinese President Xi Jinping for an official visit to Beijing. The meeting took place on March 28, 2025, at the Great Hall of the People. During this visit, Yunus and Xi discussed various aspects of bilateral cooperation, including economic partnerships and regional stability. Notably, Yunus called for China to play a "greater role" in ensuring peace, reflecting Bangladesh's desire for a more active Chinese involvement in regional affairs .

This visit marked Yunus's first bilateral trip since assuming office and was seen as a significant step in strengthening ties between Bangladesh and China. This development is particularly noteworthy in the context of shifting regional alliances and India's evolving foreign policy. Bangladesh's increasing engagement with China and Pakistan, especially following the change in government, signals a potential realignment in South Asia's geopolitical landscape Similarly, India’s relationship with Nepal—a fellow Hindu state and historically close ally—has soured under Modi’s tenure despite the lofty rhetoric of “Neighbourhood First" policy of India. The 2015 blockade during Nepal’s constitution promulgation left a bitter legacy, with Kathmandu accusing New Delhi of an unofficial embargo aimed at political coercion. Border disputes over areas like Kalapani and Lipulekh have further strained ties, with both countries issuing competing maps and nationalist rhetoric.

Many in Nepal view India’s approach as paternalistic and intrusive, pushing Kathmandu closer to China, which offers investment without political conditions. This shift undermines India’s influence in a country that was once considered a natural partner. Relations with other neighbors have also experienced strains. Sri Lanka, while deeply appreciative of India’s substantial financial and humanitarian support during its crisis, faces a growing maritime dispute with India. Issues over fishing rights near the Katchatheevu island, including frequent detention of Indian fishermen by Sri Lankan authorities, and controversies surrounding cobalt exploration claims have added new layers of tension. These disputes risk overshadowing decades of cooperation and complicate India’s influence in the strategically vital Indian Ocean region. Meanwhile, India's political establishment complicates its role in Sri Lanka’s ethnic politics, especially through its Hindu nationalist overtures to Tamil groups.

In Bhutan, quiet unease is growing over India’s diminishing secular image, and for the first time, Bhutan is engaging China directly on border talks without India at the table. In March 2024, Modi’s visit to Bhutan, where he received the kingdom’s highest civilian honor, was marked by announcements of increased aid and infrastructure cooperation. Yet, Bhutan simultaneously continues border negotiations with China on disputed areas like Doklam and Sakteng, even as satellite imagery reveals Chinese settlement expansion—an indication that Bhutan is cautiously hedging its bets beyond its traditional dependence on India. India’s relationship with Myanmar has been severely tested since the 2021 military coup in Naypyidaw. While New Delhi refused to fully condemn the junta and rejected Western sanctions, it has tried to maintain a delicate balance between supporting stability on its northeastern border and upholding democratic principles. This pragmatic approach has involved discreet diplomacy and continued security cooperation, including intelligence sharing to counter insurgent groups that operate across the porous border. However, the coup and subsequent unrest have empowered ethnic armed organizations, such as the Arakan Army and the Kachin Independence Army, some of which have periodically clashed with Indian security forces or disrupted trade routes.

India’s deepening engagement with Myanmar’s military regime contrasts sharply with its democratic credentials and complicates its efforts to counter China’s expanding influence, as Beijing maintains strong ties with both the junta and ethnic armed groups. This evolving dynamic underscores the limits of India’s influence in Myanmar and highlights the risks of prioritizing stability over democratic values in its neighborhood policy. Adding to the paradox of India’s foreign policy is its evolving relationship with the Taliban in Afghanistan. Despite decades of hostility and the use of the “Talibani mindset” as a right-wing dogwhistle against Indian Muslims domestically, New Delhi has found itself cultivating cautious goodwill and pragmatic engagement with the very group it once vehemently opposed. This irony underscores the contradictions in India’s approach—while demonizing Muslims at home as security threats, it simultaneously seeks strategic partnerships with Islamist actors abroad when convenient, exposing the transactional and inconsistent nature of its foreign policy. Despite facing sustained challenges from major powers, India’s foreign policy under the Modi government appears increasingly reactive and domestically driven. While New Delhi has been conspicuously reluctant to confront China’s aggression or respond forcefully to humiliating rhetoric from figures like Donald Trump—including his comments on mediating the Kashmir dispute—its response to smaller, Muslim-majority countries has been disproportionately aggressive. In the wake of the India Pakistan confict, the government swiftly imposed travel and trade bans on nations like Turkey and Uzbekistan, reportedly in retaliation for their support of Pakistan. These moves, largely symbolic, seem to echo the demands of Hindu nationalist groups rather than reflect a coherent strategic doctrine. This selective assertiveness—tough on weaker nations, muted before stronger adversaries—suggests that India’s foreign policy is being increasingly shaped by domestic political optics rather than long-term geopolitical considerations.

It plays well to the domestic gallery but undermines the credibility and consistency of India’s international engagement. India’s traditionally close relationship with Russia is also undergoing a quiet but meaningful shift. Long seen as a cornerstone of New Delhi’s strategic autonomy, Moscow is increasingly courting Islamabad. In 2024, Pakistani Prime Minister Shahbaz Sharif met with Vladimir Putin, marking a deepening of ties between Russia and Pakistan. This development contrasts sharply with the Manmohan Singh era, when Putin explicitly stated that Russia did not have military ties with Pakistan due to its close relationship with India. As Russia recalibrates in the wake of its isolation from the West, India finds itself competing for attention with the very country it has long sought to marginalize. This pivot signals both a loss of exclusivity in India-Russia ties and a broader erosion of diplomatic capital. Even in dealings with major powers like the United States, the gap between rhetoric and reality is jarring. Modi’s grand public diplomacy events with Donald Trump, such as “Howdy Modi,” did little to prevent India from being humiliated on the global stage. Trump publicly took credit for mediating between India and Pakistan during their 2019 conflict, claiming to have prevented war. He also threatened tariffs on Indian goods and pressured companies like Apple to shift manufacturing to the U.S., directly undercutting Modi’s “Make in India” campaign. Far from gaining respect, India under Modi is often treated as a junior partner—one that can be pressured or sidelined as needed. The illusion of strategic autonomy is further undermined by India’s troubled border with China.

The 2020 Galwan Valley clash, which resulted in the deaths of 20 Indian soldiers, shocked the nation and shattered assumptions of deterrence. Despite multiple rounds of talks, Chinese forces remain entrenched in disputed areas, with enhanced infrastructure and supply lines. New Delhi’s reluctance to confront China economically—maintaining high bilateral trade even as troops face off—only exposes the mismatch between India’s strategic ambitions and its current capabilities. India’s challenges extend to the Gulf, where economic interdependence and a large Indian diaspora have traditionally ensured stable ties. Yet provocative comments by Indian political figures and TV personalities targeting Islam have triggered formal protests from countries such as Kuwait, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia. While oil flows and remittances remain steady, India’s image as a tolerant and inclusive democracy has taken a hit—one that could have longer-term repercussions in a region critical to its energy and labor interests. Domestically, Hindutva politics may score points, but they have stripped India of the inclusive narrative that once underpinned its global appeal. The result is a credibility crisis among Western democracies that now view India with skepticism. Human rights concerns, press freedom, and rising communal violence have all been cited in international reports. Canada went as far as to accuse India of extraterritorial assassination. China, by contrast, continues to aggresively expand its influence in South Asia. With no ideological baggage and deep pockets, Beijing is now the preferred alternative for many of India's traditional allies. It is engaging Bhutan on border talks, investing in Nepal’s infrastructure, and offering Pakistan long-term strategic collaboration under the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor, it is also said to have provided military support to Pakistan during the recent conflict between the two rival nations—all while India remains reactive and inward-looking. Despite Prime Minister Modi’s highly personalized diplomacy—151 overseas trips to 72 countries, multiple national honours, and 10 visits to the United States—India’s foreign policy has struggled to translate visibility into tangible support, pointed out Indian opposition leader Mallikarjun Kharge. The prime minister’s globe-trotting has often been more spectacle than substance. When India needed international backing for its military response to terrorism, it was forced to rely on an assortment of parliamentarians to lobby abroad, revealing a troubling gap between diplomatic fanfare and actual influence. The fact that India has had to send multiple delegations around the world to canvas support after the recent India-Pakistan conflict speaks volumes about the limitations of its foreign policy—visibility alone does not guarantee clout or solidarity in times of crisis. India has long aspired to be a regional and global leader. But true leadership in today’s fragmented world demands more than ambition and visibility—it requires moral clarity, consistency, and a steadfast commitment to pluralism. In an era marked by the Israel-Palestine war, the ongoing conflict in Ukraine, escalating tensions in the Middle East, and China’s expanding global footprint, the world is undergoing a deep realignment. Strategic influence now rests not just on military or economic power, but on the credibility of one’s values and the ability to build durable coalitions.

As democracies seek partners that can uphold liberal norms and offer principled leadership, India’s reluctance to speak firmly on global crises, coupled with selective outrage and performative diplomacy, raises uncomfortable questions: Can India still claim moral authority? Can it offer leadership grounded in justice, inclusion, and peace? If India aspires to lead South Asia, it must disentangle its foreign policy from domestic populism and rhetorical ambition—replacing spectacle with substance, and nationalism with strategic clarity
