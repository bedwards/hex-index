***[Middle Spoon](https://bookshop.org/a/3111/9780593835173)*** **by Alejandro Valera  
**A breakup novel written in unsent emails from our narrator (a man in a polyamorous marriage) to his ex-boyfriend. This book worked for me. It is funny and tender and explores more than just heartbreak — mental health, polyamory, public health, parenting, race. The email constraint allowed the broad scope to feel grounded in a the reality of the narrator just venting. Our protagonist is a relatable obsessive who borders on annoying but, I mostly found him endearing, despite the pain, whining, and turmoil. *[Middle Spoon](https://bookshop.org/a/3111/9780593835173)* is one of those books that is easy and enjoyable but has a depth that makes it resonate on another frequency. It was a total delight.  
**Fave of the week!**

I have never actually made an official “In Memoriam” section before, but it felt like I should start in honor of the passing of [Alice Wong](https://bookshop.org/a/3111/9781984899422). I didn’t want to miss the opportunity to [acknowledge her life](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/15/us/alice-wong-dead.html?unlocked_article_code=1.1k8.s00o.M6Zpa_GsjQu0&smid=url-share). Wong was among many other things a disability rights activist, Bay Area icon, author, writer, mentor, and historian.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Llul!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7dc66812-c17b-41ba-a824-a945ac155724_1246x582.png)

Her work has shaped my own thinking and understanding of disability justice and what accessibility really means. Seeing the outpouring of love and reverence for her in the last few days from some of the writers/thinkers I admire most, reminds me that her life was a powerful force for changing the world and what is possible for all of us.

If you want to know more about the BTS of how the Booker judges come to their decisions check out [this convo with Kiley Reid](https://tracithomas.substack.com/p/unabridged-behind-the-booker-prize?r=12whb4).

The end of year book lists are dropping, and I love it here! I know lots of people hate lists. I am not one of those people. I love *to* hate lists, but ultimately I love the act of list making and releasing and discussing. About a week ago, I made a commitment (to myself) to move on to 2026 books sooner rather than later[2](#footnote-2), but then these lists start dropping and I end up being swayed to read just a few more 2025 titles. Here are the lists I’ve seen so far and how many of the books I’ve read from them[3](#footnote-3).

[The best books of 2025, according to the Amazon Editors](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/books-and-authors/best-books-amazon-2025). I’ve read 1.

[Kirkus’s Best Fiction of 2025](https://www.kirkusreviews.com/best-of/2025/fiction/books/)[4](#footnote-4). I’ve read 4 (and DNF’d one).

[The 100 Must-Read Books of 2025](https://time.com/collections/the-100-must-read-books-of-2025/) (Time Magazine). I’ve read 12 (DNF’d 3).

Many more lists to come soon, including NYT 100 Notable and Kirkus Best Nonfiction of 2025, to name a few.

I finally saw *[Sinners](https://amzn.to/3XtL6VR)* and it is as good as everyone said. I loved it. There were some issues, but I loved it. I do not understand how we’re having a conversation about *[One Battle After Another](https://amzn.to/4o0FehP)* being the movie of the *year*[5](#footnote-5) when Sinners is right the fuck there.

I get it, they’re both good, you can like both. But, there is no chance in hell that *[One Battle After Another](https://amzn.to/4o0FehP)* is *that* much better than *[Sinners](https://amzn.to/3XtL6VR)* that this isn’t up for discussion by the film bros of the world.

I talked a lot more about my issues with *[One Battle After Another](https://amzn.to/4o0FehP)* as a guest host of with if you want to know why I wasn’t that moved by the film.
