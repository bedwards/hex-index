Books I Read This Week

Firestorm: The Great Los Angeles Fires and America’s New Age of Disaster by Jacob Soboroff
I read my first book of 2026 this week!2 This work of memoir and reporting is about the 2025 LA fires. Yes, it is probably a bit too soon for this book to be in the world. This book doesn’t know who or what it wants to be. Where it is its most impactful is as Soboroff’s memoir. He is from the Palisades and was on the ground from the start reporting on the fire there. His family home was burned, his siblings were evacuated, and through it all he was on camera informing the world of what he saw. Reliving that is unique and compelling as hell. The book falters when he tepidly dabbles into the politics of the moment. From Karen Bass to Donald Trump, Soboroff is wishy-washy on what was said, who was in the wrong, and what it all means. I understand the impulse to back off from those pieces, but think the book would have been better served if he laid it all out or found a way to completely avoid it.

Friday Night Lights: A Town, a Team, and a Dream by H. G. Bissinger
I knew going in this narrative nonfiction classic had had an outsized impact on the genre, but was totally unprepared for how much I would like the book. I thought it would feel trite and outdated — some bits do feel dated as you can imagine since it came out in 1990, but more of it feels current than not. Bissinger has crafted a book whose form meets its contents. It feels like watching sports, and I don’t mean that about the scenes that describe games, I mean that about the whole book. The pacing and tension build to a crescendo that left me waiting to see how it would all play out. You know the way the best games do. Thinking about this as a “sports book” diminishes the reporting and insights that are laid out on the systems at play in this West Texas town (and America — then and now — more broadly). Friday Night Lights is a book about football that is actually about everything.
Fave of the week!
