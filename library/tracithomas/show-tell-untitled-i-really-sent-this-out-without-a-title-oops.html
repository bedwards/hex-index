Books I Read This Week

Frankenstein by Mary Shelley1
You guys, I read this for The Stacks Book Club this month, I absolutely loved it. It’s the story of a young scientist who creates life. Which, sure. But it is really about what we owe each other, regret, revenge, creativity, compassion. Honestly, it is about everything. It is an allegory for everything (and nothing). It is short. There are killer scenes. Easily a new favorite book. I can’t wait to talk about it more with on the podcast on October 29th. You should read it because I am desperate for everyone’s opinions on the book2.
Fave of the week!

The Hollow Half: A Memoir of Bodies and Borders by Sarah Aziza
In 2019, Aziza was hospitalized for anorexia, during her treatment and recovery she revisited the story of her Palestinian family, namely her grandmother. A beautiful memoir that braids familial history with geopolitical policy. While the scope is large, the book always feels intimate and small. The connections between the personal/public bodies/borders were subtle and layered. The Hollow Half could’ve easily overextended itself, tipping into platitudes and cliche, but Aziza holds the reins tightly preserving a specificity needed to ground the book. Aziza is actively in conversation with so many thinkers whose work I admire, and I appreciated seeing her writing towards these traditions and not into a vacuum.

Books

I am filing the Louvre Heist3 under books, because after the news broke I got so many messages, tags, and DMs, about the heist since we read The Art Thief earlier this year for book club. If you missed it, that book is about a couple who stole what is estimated to be around two billion dollars worth of art over a decade. No, they never stole from the Louvre, but they were doing the most none the less. Turns out museums have next to no security. If you’re into the Louvre saga, read the book. Here is the most fun episode of the year on the book with Ceara O’Sullivan.

Oh, and in real actual book news, The Booker Prize is going to start awarding a children’s prize in 2027 and they will have real live children as the judges. This is extremely cool.

Pop Culture

I saw the new Frankenstein movie from Guillermo Del Toro. I din’t care for it. Mainly because it was “inspired” by the book but took such liberties with the story and like the book doesn’t need GDT to take liberties. Mary Shelley already wrote that. I think wanted to see an adaptation and not an interpolation. I am excited to see what people think of the movie after reading the book with The Stacks Book Club. It will be streaming on Netflix starting November 7th.

I will say my favorite moment of the film, and my pop culture love for the week, was when we got the big sweeping shot of Frankenstein’s workshop, and I turned to my friend (hey ) and said “Did they borrow this from…” and she finished my thought with “is this Wicked?”. I think sharing sets for Wicked and Frankenstein is certainly a recession indicator.

On a similar note, people love Guillermo Del Toro’s aesthetic. I do not. I think his stuff is ugly and over saturated and self-satisfying4. I know this is a very harsh and deeply subjective take5 but it is worth noting, because if you do like his stuff, you will likely like the movie more than I did. But to be fair, I didn’t not like the movie because it was ugly, I didn’t like the movie because the script was bad, the acting sucked, and I thought it was ugly.
