When you have an example, you have something worth striving towards.

You have someone who can do all sorts of amazing things because of their experience and knowledge in whatever you're trying to achieve. Even if you don't have them specifically training you, it's possible that you can look at what they do and try to copy what they're doing and hopefully become just as good, if not better than your example. Not because you want to take over and stop your example from doing what they want, but because you admire them. You want them to admire you, while at the same time you want to become the kind of person other people can look up to. A second example to give the world more positive examples. Because more is actually better, or so it seems.

Of course, those examples you're looking up to didn't come pre-packaged with all the knowledge and experience they have now. It took forever to get there and because of that, they know they made mistakes along the way. So if and when they do become an example for others like you, they have to make sure that you know you can't just get there overnight. Just because you know that you might be able to do something incredible, doesn't mean you should actually try it. You might need to do the work, slow down and go through the steps of learning easier things. That way you can build up to the harder things which are so worthwhile.

Previously in this space, we've looked at the how to [start the journey](https://tvphilosophy.substack.com/p/the-flash-season-1-the-beginning) of self-improvement. Then we looked at what it takes to [work harder](https://tvphilosophy.substack.com/p/the-flash-season-2-working-harder) and push yourself further than you had originally planned. After that we considered the [importance of making goals](https://tvphilosophy.substack.com/p/the-flash-season-3-achieving-goals) and trying to achieve them in specific ways. More recently we looked at the [obstacles which can get in your way](https://tvphilosophy.substack.com/p/the-flash-season-4-facing-obstacles) of making improvements. Doing that can make you feel frustrated and want to try and get over the difficulties. Enough that skipping steps feels like the right thing to do. But is it?

> “See it takes time to hone your skills Nora. You can't just skip rungs on the ladder and jump right to the big stuff. That's not how it works. Take a moment, think before you act, fix the problem the right way.”

This is very much where we find Barry during season 5. He didn't necessarily have someone to teach him to be The Flash when he was first starting out. While he did have people to look up to like his father, his mother, even Doctor Wells before he realized who he really was. Yet none of them were really speedsters. Jay Garrick makes it a point to allow Barry to learn on his own unless he's going to do something very serious and potentially destructive. He had to become who he is on his own. However, the better he got, the more famous he became and the more people look up to him.

> “You're obviously good at forensics but you shouldn't interfere. People like us need to be, responsible.”
> 
> “Right, uh timeline.”
> 
> “The timeline.”

Now he's faced with an entirely new level of responsibility. Someone who idolizes him on a scale he's never understood before. It was one thing to have individual people who he saved feel grateful and want to honor him. Or even someone like another hero or potential hero like Wally or Ralph want to be like him. It's another to have your own daughter idealizing you and be right in front of you to do it. She has grown up on all the stories of his great accomplishments. Things he hasn't even done yet but are legendary in her future time. Being able to live up to such a reputation has to seem completely impossible.

> “I've always wanted a family. You know and when we got married I knew that one day we'd decide to have a baby. We would try and you'd get pregnant. I'd be there for the sonogram and hear her first heartbeat, and then she'd come and we'd take her home. And we'd see her first smile, her first tooth, first time I read to her, her first steps, the first time she ran. But her being here now, don't you feel like we missed all those firsts? That we skipped all the good stuff?”
> 
> “Baby, we'll still have all those first, just not in that order.”

Many parents have this problem with their children. Especially when they choose a job that is worthy of being praised. Such as a police officer or firefighter. They become larger than life in the eyes of their children. Although for the parent, they're just ordinary people doing something dangerous for a living. When your child sees this though, they can want to do things like becoming a hero themselves by wanting to rush into danger and rescue people. Do something heroic to try and live up to who they see their parents as. Parents then have to find a way to balance what they do for a job and their role as a parent to protect their children. It's hard for a parent to tell a child not to do what they do on a regular basis. This is something of a contradiction.

> “And your nickname is Xcess?”
> 
> “Clearly not my doing.”
> 
> “No, it's a nickname that you gave me when I was a kid... Mom.”
> 
> “Mom?”
> 
> “You said... I did everything in excess. I guess I've always been like that, overdoing things, getting way over in my head. Like, uh, recently...”

As a literal superhero, Barry has trouble telling his daughter Nora not to be a hero and try to run into danger because he does it himself. She also has the same powers he does because he's her daughter. She literally got her powers from him. To tell her not to use them is going to cause so many problems and contradictions. Really the only option Barry has is to find a way for her to learn the right way to do things. Which doesn't necessarily mean that Nora is going to listen to him or follow his advice. Only that he has to at least try and make it worthwhile. So that if it eventually does require Nora to do what he does, she will know what to do. The problem is that Barry hasn't been around for most of Nora's life to do that for her. Meaning she's had to look elsewhere for people to look up to.

> “I think part of being a hero is being able to see the good in people. So yeah, maybe some day in the future, even Thawne could change. Besides, if we don't believe in people, who will?”

Children don't always get the best role models in their parents. Or their parents aren't around enough to be the kind of hero they can look up to. But that doesn't change the need in their kids for a hero to idealize. So they will go looking for anyone who might even present themselves as heroes or people to look up to for a role model. The problem is that if they aren't actually good people, they might not give you the best advice. Or give you advice that seems like the right thing at the time but only ends up having terrible consequences. Something that you as a young person trying to improve might not see until it's too late.

> “No, you're not a parent. Parents are supposed to make the world better for their kids, you make everything worse. I hate you, and you hate you too.”

If you're the kind of parent who isn't around, not necessarily because you don't want to be, although that's a possibility, but because you're job is important enough to spend time doing it, you won't see your children finding other role models. Meaning you can't step in before it becomes a problem. Then you have to find a way to bring them back to a better place. Show them that they have the better way and not the role model who is trying to manipulate them into doing the wrong thing. It's not the fault of the children. They're on an earlier part of their journey and can't know about the potential mistakes they might make trusting the wrong person.

> “I don't need this kind of training, I need to learn to be like you.”
> 
> “What do you think it means to be like me?”
> 
> “You know, running so fast you can stop a tsunami or vibrating something bigger than a plane or how about defeating a sand demon by throwing lightning at them.”
> 
> “Nora, look, all those things are way beyond your current skill level. You gotta stick to what you can handle right now.”
> 
> “Just because I'm your daughter doesn't mean you have to baby me. I can handle way more than you think.”

Which is what Nora ultimately does. Barry isn't around for her to look up to in any direct way. He was gone for pretty much her entire life. So she turned to someone she shouldn't have and found out how badly it can go. However, Barry isn't exactly blameless in trusting the wrong person. He trusted people like Thawne and Zolomon which ultimately ended up terrible for him. It's both a positive and a negative part of what Barry does. He trusts that people are worth trusting despite their terrible track record of turning on those he shouldn't. In that way Nora is a lot like her dad when she trusts the wrong person.

> “I guess greatness isn't hereditary.”

Kids often repeat the mistakes of their parents and rarely ever find a way to avoid them. They end up learning the same lessons. Although it's not often in the same order and sometimes are much worse for the children. Largely because they learned the wrong lessons from their parents. So in order to try and get their faster, they try and skip ahead. But at the same time, they want to do more than their parents. If their parents achieved great things, they must be destined for great things as well. Maybe even do things that your parents were never able to. Stop someone who couldn't necessarily be stopped by their parents. That would prove they are truly worthy of their parents legacy and the admiration that they got for what they did.

> “Thanks for helping me slow it down, now that I've experienced a sense of control maybe next time I can do it on my own.”

This is partly because they are determined to get to a better place. Determination is generally considered to be a good thing. It drives us forward and makes you better in the long run. Gives you a reason to get up in the morning even when it feels like the whole world is against you. Like you'll never escape what is coming for you, your destiny. As long as the determination is aimed properly and is going in the right direction.

> “I know how much you care about Grace. You're trying to make the world a better place for her. She didn't choose to be a meta, but she is. Does that mean she deserves to die?”
> 
> “No, I would never hurt Grace.”
> 
> “Then you'd let her grow up in a world of fear? Where there's someone out there hurting people like her? The cure, it will heal Grace, and you.”
> 
> “Why should I believe you Flash?”
> 
> “Because I'm a father too. My daughter, I love her more than I ever thought possible. I would do anything to make the world a better place for her, and I think you will too. So do it.”
