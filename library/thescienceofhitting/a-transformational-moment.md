From “**[Convergence Calamity, Revisited](https://thescienceofhitting.com/p/convergence-calamity-revisited)**” (November 2023):

“I’ve become more worried about incremental competitive intensity across the connectivity industries… I struggle to confidently state Charter and Comcast will continue to grow core broadband subs at a reasonable clip. **In my mind, this suggests that the Cable investment thesis is no longer valid…** **For that reason, I have decided to sell Liberty Broadband and Comcast**.”

Over the course of the past two years, the concerns discussed in that update have sustained or worsened, most notably on customer volumes: as shown below, Comcast and Charter saw their broadband base contract nearly 2% YoY in Q3 FY25 (at [\-1.7%](https://www.cmcsa.com/static-files/a1561b42-cdb6-4d3e-923c-3efc8b6f6447) and [\-1.5%](https://ir.charter.com/static-files/950427c2-c3ef-42b8-ba30-f15c9cf58970), respectively); after accounting for passings growth, both are seeing material broadband penetration declines.

That is the primary reason why their stocks have continued to struggle, with each down more than 30% since November 2023, over a period where the S&P 500 has risen ~60%. At Comcast, the result is a stock that trades at a lower price today than it did a decade ago. At Charter, as a result of their levered buyback strategy, the company repurchased ~$78 billion of stock over the past nine years at an average price of [~$441 per share](https://ir.charter.com/static-files/4aaaa5e1-296a-47fc-bfb3-971eda05ebcc), **~2x** higher than where it closed on Friday (~$220 per share). Needless to say, it has been a dreadful run for the owners of both companies, which is evident in their relative returns versus the index and shrinking EV/EBITDA multiples.
