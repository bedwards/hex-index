Infrastructure is crumbling and public services have hollowed out. This isn't by accident. France's public debt has tripled over the past 25 years, reaching 3.4 trillion. Interest payments alone now exceed the entire budget for universities and research institutions.

Meanwhile, since 2017, the country has gone through seven prime ministers, none of whom have delivered lasting reforms. Real wages have stagnated, while living costs have climbed sharply. The pension system, once a pillar of the French social model, now consumes 14% of the entire economy, which is more than education, defense, and transportation combined. Housing affordability has deteriorated and youth unemployment sits at 18% among the highest in the developed world.

At heart, France's problem is simple. Persistent overspending. For over 51 consecutive years, the state has run budget deficits, always spending more than it collects in taxes. Now the bill is coming due.

When you think about it, it's ironic. France once helped design the Euro zone's fiscal rules. Yet now it stands as the biggest threat to breaking them. For most of the 20th century, France was a model of prosperity.

It built a comprehensive welfare state based on a simple social contract. If you got sick, the state covered the medical bills. If you lost your job, unemployment benefits kept you afloat. When you retired, a pension was guaranteed.

From cradle to grave, the state would take care of you. For decades, the model worked. Growth was strong, living standards were high, and France became Europe's second largest economy. Many saw it as proof that capitalism and socialism could coexist.

But there was a catch. Revenues alone never fully covered expenditures. [music] And so, the deficit was financed through debt. In fact, France has run a budget deficit every single year since 1974.

To be fair, debt isn't inherently bad. It can fuel growth when used for productive investment. And for years, France did just that. The economy grew, the workforce was young, and millions paid into the system.

This all made the social benefits seem bearable. But over time, the arrangement became harder to sustain. In essence, France faces three predicaments. A shrinking population, an industrial exodus, and a political system unable to fix either.

These issues are not separate problems that can be tackled individually. They're interconnected, self-reinforcing, and together they're pushing the country toward a reckoning. France's demographic crisis is perhaps the most pressing, and it's worse than it looks. In 2010, the country's fertility rate stood at 2.0 children per woman, the highest in Europe.

Today, it's 1.6 and falling. The number of births dropped from 833,000 in 2010 to 663,000 in 2024. That [music] is a 20% decline, steeper than Germany's. The timing makes it even worse.

French baby boomers, those born between 1945 and 1973, are now retiring on a mass scale. Around 800,000 people exit the workforce each year, while only about 700,000 young people replace them. That's a yearly shortfall of 100,000 workers, compounding indefinitely. The numbers no longer add up.

In 1990, there were five working age adults for every retiree. By 2000, that ratio fell to four. And by 2010 to three, and today it's close to two. You can see where this is going.

By 2040, France will have 1.5 working age adults for every retiree. Pensions and health care will consume an ever larger share of the budget, while the tax base needed to fund them will continue to shrink. That means every French worker will be paying not just for themselves but for someone else's retirement. But it gets worse because productivity isn't compensating.

In a healthy economy, workers become more productive over time through better technology, education, and infrastructure. That rising productivity can offset the effects of demographic decline since one person can do the work of two people from a generation ago. But in France, productivity growth has stalled. Between 2015 and 2023, French productivity increased by just 0.3% annually, which is half the OECD average.

This creates a vicious feedback loop. An aging population demands more spending on health care and pensions, [music] but with fewer workers and stagnant productivity, there's less wealth to fund those demands. The second crisis is industrial decline. In 1980, manufacturing made up 26% of France's economy.

Today, it's about 13%. That might sound like normal de-industrialization, something that every advanced economy experiences as it shifts towards services. But in France, the fallout has been existential. Here's the thing.

Manufacturing jobs tend to cluster and multiply. One factory job supports three or four additional positions in logistics, maintenance, retail and services. So when factories close in places like Leon or Lil, entire ecosystems collapse, property values fall, young people leave, tax revenues plummet, and the remaining population ages and becomes more dependent on welfare. France experienced this firsthand.

When the euro was introduced in 1999, it lost 900,000 manufacturing jobs within a decade. Global competition played a role, but France was also uniquely exposed because of its labor laws. You see, French labor is the most expensive in the Euro zone. When including social benefits, employers pay nearly 50% more than the gross salary.

For comparison, in Germany it's about 30% and in Poland it's 20%. And so manufacturers left. Why pay triple for a French worker when someone in Poland can do the same job for much less? The outcome was an industrial exodus.

Factories shut down and moved abroad. Michelon relocated tire production from Claremont Feron to Poland and Spain. Renault shifted assembly operations to Morocco and Romania and PSA Pujo Citroan closed plants in Aln Sububois and moved production to Slovakia. Some of the headquarters stayed in France for tax purposes, but most of the actual production left.

What remained was a split economy. High value services concentrated in Paris while the provinces were left with a decaying industrial base. France's third crisis is political and it's the hardest to fix because it's embedded in the government's institutions. The problem starts with polarization.

French politics has split into five major blocks. Far right, center right, center, center left, and far left. None of these is capable of forming a majority, leaving the lower house of parliament divided. [music] Political compromises are nearly impossible and every government survives only by avoiding controversial decisions.

When Macron pushed through the pension reform to raise the retirement age from 62 to 64, he won legally but lost politically. His party shattered, his approval ratings plunged, [music] and the reform itself became so toxic that no one has dared propose anything similar since. This political climate goes far beyond pensions. It affects everything.

Take tax reform as an example. The current system is convoluted, riddled with loopholes, and discourages investment. Fixing it, however, means some groups lose benefits, which triggers protests and political backlash. Labor reform is another example.

Rigid regulations keep youth unemployment high, but any attempt at reform sparks union resistance, street protests, and political risk. The result is paralysis. Since 2017, France has had seven prime ministers. Most lasted less than 18 months, though.

Each promised reform, but delivered nothing meaningful. The bureaucracy keeps running. The state keeps borrowing, but the problems go unressed. And here's the dangerous part.

When nothing changes, voters tend to radicalize. Support for the National Rally, which is Marine Le Pen's far-right party, has grown from 18% to 33% in the last 7 years. Meanwhile, on the left, Jeanluke Melon's France unbowed movement now holds 22%. Between these two juggernauts, anti-establishment parties claim more than half the electorate.

They can't govern, but they can block others from governing, which gives them political leverage. And so, in French politics, self-preservation has become self-destruction. This matters because France's problems require urgent coordinated action across multiple domains simultaneously. The pension crisis can't be solved without labor reform.

Labor markets can't be reformed without industrial policy. And industrial policy is impossible without tax reform. Everything is connected, which means everything must move together. But the political system is now inadvertently blocking exactly that kind of comprehensive reform.

What people believe often outweighs whether something truly works. [music] The economy is proof of that. For decades, France's economic model worked because investors believed it would continue working. But [music] now, that belief is cracking.

France's 10-year bond yield has risen to 3.2%, which is the highest since 2012 and nearly on par with Italy. In fiscal policy, that is a dramatic turn. For most of the Euro era, France borrowed at rates close to Germany's, a sign [music] that markets viewed French debt as fundamentally safe. The spread between French and German bonds rarely exceeded 50 basis points.

Today, it's approaching 80 basis points and widening. This may seem like a minor technicality, but the implications are profound. Each basis point adds billions in extra interest costs [music] for France. And these payments can't be cut or delayed.

They must be paid in full and on time or the country defaults. The EU's stability and growth pact, which is a set of rules on deficits and debt, was designed to prevent precisely the kind of situation France is now in. You see, the euro was built on a simple premise. [music] Without shared safety nets, each country had to maintain fiscal discipline.

The euro has no transfer union, no shared debt, and no automatic support from rich regions to poor ones. Instead, the system relies on fiscal restraint, and most members stick to it.
