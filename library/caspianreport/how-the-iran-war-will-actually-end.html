The first pressure point in modern warfare is munitions. Everything hinges on this. At first glance, the US military appears almost limitless in firepower. Precision missiles can strike targets hundreds of kilometers away.

Satellites track movements in real time, while air defense systems intercept rockets mid-flight. It almost looks as if advanced militaries can simply keep fighting for as long as they want. But there is a quiet reality behind all of this. Modern weapons are extraordinarily difficult to produce.

A Tomahawk missile is not just a metal tube filled with explosives. It contains specialized electronics, advanced guidance systems, and components that come from long supply chains. Many of those parts are produced in small quantities by only a handful of manufacturers. The same is true for interceptor missiles, long range drones, and precisiong guided bombs, which means these weapons cannot be replaced quickly.

At the start of a conflict, this problem is easy to miss. Militaries rely on stockpiles built up over years. Those reserves allow them to sustain a high tempo of strikes in the early stages of a war. Missiles fly, air defenses intercept, and so commanders operate as if supply is not an issue.

But stockpiles don't last forever, and once they begin to shrink, the war starts to run into industrial constraints. Factories cannot scale production overnight. Doing so would require time, skilled labor, and above all, multi-year contracts with suppliers. Take the SM3 interceptor missile as an example.

The US Navy uses it to shoot down ballistic missiles. Depending on the variant, each missile costs roughly 10 to 28 million. That price tag reflects its complexity. The missile must detect a ballistic warhead in space, track it, and collide with it at several kilometers/s using a kinetic kill vehicle.

It's essentially a small spacecraft designed to destroy another object traveling faster than a bullet. But because of that complexity, the production of SM3 missiles is limited. The exact number is classified, but they're produced in relatively small batches. often only dozens per year depending on the Pentagon's procurements.

Another example is the Patriot Pack 3 Interceptor, one of the main missiles used by America and Israel to shoot down Iranian missiles. Each Pac 3 interceptor costs roughly 4 to 5 million. However, in spite of its widespread use, production is relatively limited. In 2025, manufacturer Loheed Martin produced around 620 PAC 3 interceptors.

That seems plenty at first, but modern air defense systems often fire multiple interceptors at a single incoming missile to ensure a successful kill. In a prolonged highintensity conflict, this equation gradually shifts the nature of the war. One night of missile defense can burn through months of production. That's about 2% of the Patriots annual production gone in under a minute. No war machine runs this hot forever. The same munitions constraint exists on the Iranian side, though it looks very different. Iran possesses one of the largest ballistic missile arsenals in the Middle East, estimated at several thousand.

But missiles alone do not determine how much firepower a country can bring to the battlefield. They also need launchers. These launch vehicles must constantly move, hide, and reposition. The moment a missile is fired, sensors and satellites can often trace the launch back to its origin, making the vehicle itself a prime target.

Destroy enough of these launchers, and even a large missile stockpile becomes useless. Iran has also invested heavily in drones, which push the economics of war even further. Systems like the Shahed 136 are rudimentary when compared to missiles, but they're built with small engines and basic electronics and are estimated to cost between 20 and $50,000 each with some estimates going as low as 7,000. Launched in waves, these drones force defenders to spend far more expensive interceptors, gradually saturating air defenses and exhausting stockpiles faster than they can be replenished.

Altogether, these dynamics show that modern warfare depends as much on industry as on tactics. Victory belongs to the side that can sustain the flow of weapons. Armed conflicts today face another pressure point that did not exist to the same degree in the past. Financial markets.

The moment fighting breaks out in a strategically important region, energy prices move within hours. Transportation costs rise and investors start reassessing risks. Often times, these market fluctuations happen long before governments decide how to respond. We're already seeing this dynamic unfold.

Oil prices jumped as soon as hostilities began to disrupt traffic near the straight of Hormuz. Governments moved quickly to calm markets by releasing strategic reserves and signaling that additional supplies could be brought online if needed. And this is not just about oil. Once energy markets begin moving, the effects trickle down. Freight becomes more expensive, manufacturing costs rise, and inflation begins creeping upward again. All these disruptions move far faster than military campaigns, and they ripple through global supply chains.

Fertilizer production, for example, depends heavily on natural gas. When energy prices rise, the cost of producing ammonia, the key ingredient in nitrogen fertilizers, rises as well. That increase eventually reaches farmers, which months from now raises the cost of staple crops such as wheat, corn, and rice. Global aviation is another example.

Taken together, these examples reveal just how many critical systems underpin the global economy. A prolonged conflict with Iran will shape prices, supply chains, and growth across the planet. And as those disruptions mount, the White House will gradually yield to investors and markets. Politics is the third and final pressure point.

It's less visible than weapons or markets, but just as powerful. While military campaigns can stretch for years, elections arrive on schedule. And that mismatch eventually creates pressure. At the start of a conflict, political leaders usually enjoy broad public support.

Governments have room to act and military operations move forward with relatively little domestic resistance. But that unity rarely lasts forever. As hostilities drag on, questions begin to surface. What are the objectives?

How long will this continue? What are the costs? Those questions grow louder once the war begins to affect everyday life, whether through rising fuel costs, higher inflation, or the steady drip of casualties. In the United States, the next political clock is always ticking.

Every 2 years, Americans elect a new Congress. The next one is scheduled for November 2026. In that election, all 435 seats in the House of Representatives and roughly onethird of the seats in the Senate will be on the ballot. These midterm elections often become a referendum on the sitting president's policies, especially during periods of economic stress or military conflict.

A war that drags on too long, can quickly turn into a political liability, even if the fighting itself remains far away. Lawmakers, particularly those from swing districts and swing states, are usually the first to distance themselves from unpopular policies. Not necessarily because the war has been lost, but because the domestic political environment can no longer sustain it. For a president, that creates a dilemma.

Armies can fight for as long as their supplies last. Economies can absorb shocks for a time. But political legitimacy must be renewed at the ballot box. So if the war with Iran drags on long enough to collide with the midterm election, the pressure will not only come from the battlefield or the markets, it will also come from Capitol Hill.
