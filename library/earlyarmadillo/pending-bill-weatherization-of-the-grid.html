What happened: As a result of the February freeze, nearly 200 Texans died and more than 4.8 million were without power. 

The players: ERCOT (Electric Reliability Council of Texas), Governor Greg Abbott, the Texas legislature, and the Texas public. 

Bills stuck in the legislature:

Senate Bill 3, considered “toothless” by many, would fine utilities $1,000 if they fail to meet standards set forth in the bill, but it would be cheaper for utilities to pay the fine than fix the line. This bill also allows for an amber-alert-like announcement to be broadcast when the power supply is inadequate to meet demand (aka a serious emergency), a simple, yet obvious necessity that should have been law years ago. 

House Bill 2000 would allocate $2 billion from the budget to help finance upgrades to power plants. Critics argue that giving public money to private companies is a recipe for disaster, especially since the oversight would be limited. Democratic amendments were shot down early and often. The changes would have added language to prevent companies from using the money to build new plants or to offer the money in loans instead of grants. This bill was sent to the Senate where it was promptly sent to the waste bin. 

Bills out of the legislature or nearly:

HB 16 made it through the bill-shredder gauntlet. Instead of addressing anything related to weatherization, however, this bill merely forbids electric plans with wholesale pricing for residents and small businesses. In other words, it doesn’t address the underlying problem. For example, a man dying of starvation orders a pizza, but a lawyer shows up instead to explain to him that if the dying man would like to enroll in a wholesale pizza subscription to save money, he must understand that the volatility and fluctuation of wholesale pizza pricing may cause his pizza bill to be multiple times higher in a month in which wholesale pizza prices are high. He must understand that he will be responsible for charges caused by fluctuations in wholesale pizza prices, and if he agrees, he can continue doing exactly what he was already doing: not eating pizza. 

HB 2586 also made it to the Governor’s desk, but like the previous bill, it doesn’t address the underlying problem. Essentially, the bill makes the pizza...err...electric company get audited annually. And that might sound at least mildly useful, but when you realize that the words “emergency,” “weatherization,” and “prevention” are missing from the bill, it makes the audit the equivalent of a high-five to our starving guy from earlier. 

SB 2154 would reorganize the PUC, Public Utility Commission of Texas, from three commissioners to five, and only two of them have to have any experience with actual utilities whereas before all of them required experience in the field that they would be managing. This one is a bit of a head-scratcher since the bill specifically added being a “practicing attorney” and “be[ing] a resident of this state” qualifies a person to make decisions concerning the vast complexity of the grid. This bill has a high likelihood of making it to Abbott, but again, it doesn’t address the underlying problem...it really just changes the pizza delivery dude’s outfit; their box is still empty, and our metaphorical bro really doesn’t care what the delivery dude is wearing when his stomach is imploding. 

HB 10 does effectively the same thing as SB 2154 but with ERCOT, changing the eligibility for commissioners and increasing the overall amount. So more pizza dudes at the door, several with different shirts, some live down the street, but still no pizza.

What could happen: Abbott made it clear earlier this year that he wanted legislative action “to mandate and fund winterization and stabilization of the Texas power infrastructure." If that doesn’t happen in the remaining days of the 87th session, it is likely that a special session will be called, a 30 day bonanza where the Governor sets the agenda; legislators can’t introduce any other bills or extend the time.

Final thought: Keep your eye on SB 3. If it passes, despite its alleged watered down nature, it may be enough for Abbott to claim a victory on the matter and quell the hunger for real change.

TAIL END

Three bills of note:

A bipartisan bill expanding medical marijuana access passed the House and is pending action in the Senate where Lt. Gov. Dan Patrick has successfully stymied such efforts in the past.

A somewhat bipartisan bill jailing those who block an emergency vehicle or hospital entrance is on its way to Abbott who has signaled his support for the measure in order to curtail disorderly protests. 

And a partisan bill, crafted by House Republicans, prescribing how race should be taught in schools, will be on the Governor's desk as well.

UPDATE: A previous version of this newsletter incorrectly stated HB 1535, the medical marijuana bill, passed the Senate already. It did not pass yet. It is still in committee.
