**Welcome!** It’s time for a new Thursday edition of the *Animation Obsessive* newsletter. Glad you could join us.

Before we start, a quick note of thanks. Sunday’s issue about *[Toy Story](https://animationobsessive.substack.com/p/the-toy-story-you-remember)* [and film stock](https://animationobsessive.substack.com/p/the-toy-story-you-remember) was the most popular thing we’ve published here. Much of its traffic came from [Hacker News](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=45883788), where it held the number-one spot for hours and passed 1,000 points within a day. Really grateful to everyone for the kind responses.

Now, though, we’re looking elsewhere in the animation world — to the country of Czechoslovakia, which no longer exists.

Czechoslovak animation is a large topic that’s appeared many times in the newsletter. You may know our pieces on the [wool films](https://animationobsessive.substack.com/p/a-legends-animated-wool) of Hermína Týrlová, the [cinematic trickery](https://animationobsessive.substack.com/p/a-visual-tour-of-karel-zeman) of Karel Zeman and the [career and impact](https://animationobsessive.substack.com/p/the-grandmaster-of-stop-motion) of Jiří Trnka. Yet there’s a lot we’ve mentioned only in passing, if at all.

What about *Pat and Mat*, *[Mach and Šebestová](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLrOJoSSAonip4haRjsbF69_OMwErzS4xC)* and the chaotic, joyful *[Hey Mister, Let’s Play](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SeuE4OafsZk&list=PLrOJoSSAonirsC7MpWk4YFFl4asDjTPRj&index=1)*? What about *Little Mole* and *[The Vanished World of Gloves](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Eugdw_l0A2U)* — and the nightmare visions of Jan Švankmajer? Even those are just the beginning.

Czechoslovakia built one of the world’s richest, hardest-to-summarize animation scenes. Artists worked in all styles, with all materials: drawings, but also [glass](https://animationobsessive.substack.com/p/animating-with-glass) and [wood](https://animationobsessive.substack.com/p/dark-visions-in-walnut-wood) and [pastry dough](https://archive.org/details/tyrlova-film). It was wonderful stuff. And, in 1991, an exhibition tried to map its history.

The country was changing at the time. In ‘89, the dictatorship got overthrown by a mass, peaceful uprising (the “Velvet Revolution”), and the Iron Curtain came down. Czechoslovakia itself dissolved, again peacefully, around three years later.

Between those two events, this exhibition opened at a museum in New Jersey. On display were puppets, sets, films and more; the *New York Times* found it all “utterly charming.”[1](#footnote-1) It was billed as an attempt to “widen \[America’s\] perception of this often underappreciated art form” through the magic of animated films from Czechoslovakia.

The show’s title was *Krátký Film: The Art of Czechoslovak Animation*. Accompanying it was a catalog — with photos, stills and English essays about the whole sweep of the country’s animation. It’s a great intro to the scene, and a source we’ve valued for years. We’ve decided to preserve that catalog on the Internet Archive.

You can view it online below. For a little about what it contains, and this corner of animation in general, read on.
