What is Enough (to achieve lasting change)?

*As many social studies and history teachers can attest, a significant portion of our profession consists of the surveying of struggle and progress, both domestically and internationally. From the Peloponnesian War in Greece in 431 BCE, to the Industrial Revolution within the western world in late 1700s; from the catastrophic ramifications endured by Africa resulting from the Berlin Conference in the 1880s, to the Michael Brown (2014), Brianna Taylor and George Floyd protests (both in 2020), discussing conflict and how societies emerge from them are central elements of our practice. That said, in my honest thoughts, which I do share with my students both at Camden High School in Camden, NJ and in my political science course at Rutgers University-Camden, I wonder what, in today’s contemporary era of 1% rule, is sufficient to achieve “victory”, and will whatever efforts the People put forth ever be “enough” to secure it.*

As a city high school teacher of social studies, former union president, and researcher of urban education, the bulk of my work demands that I remain aware of narratives that influence conventional wisdom and policy within education; identify errors and blindspots in popularly understood narratives and confront them by providing broader practical and research-based context - none of which can be done while wearing rose-colored glasses. In calling attention to the linking of urban education reform and redevelopment during the Chris Christie years in Camden from 2011-2016; identifying the broad [misunderstanding of “failing”](https://ccsenet.org/journal/index.php/jel/article/view/0/47605) urban schools; exploring the [insufficiency of schooling for “college and career readiness](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/12/5/357)” for specifically urban students of color, part of my responsibility as an educator and researcher is to be honest about what I’m seeing and what research indicates.

As such, it is quite often that my conclusions do not elicit a sense of joy or motivation but, hopefully, a sense of realism and contextualized understanding that could help sustain honest exploration about what exactly we are dealing with. To be sure, we are in an exceedingly tenuous moment in American history and looking away from reality will not save us any more than insufficient solutions will. The late [Barbara Ehrenreich](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/09/02/books/barbara-ehrenreich-dead.html) commented that “delusion is no way to confront reality”, and [Ruddick (1999)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11657244/) and [Musschenga (2019)](https://academic.oup.com/jmp/article/44/4/423/5540413) wrote about the dangers and delusions of false hope. Collectively, the bulk of our American public seems to be wishing and hoping that the problems that plague our modern demos will work themselves out through the good work of committed activists or traditional guardrails that existed to protect the bulk of Americans from political leaders’ worst intentions.

Popular, yet hopeful, discourse suggests that somehow the legitimation of [rightwing nationalism](https://www.washingtonpost.com/outlook/2022/02/11/history-patriotism-right-wing-politics/); the embeddedness of [racist Republican legislation](https://civilrights.org/trump-rollbacks/); the erosion of women’s right to [bodily autonomy;](https://my.lwv.org/ohio/hudson/supreme-court-strips-women-bodily-autonomy) the sustained [attacks on public education](https://newrepublic.com/article/167375/republican-plan-devastate-public-education-america) by fascistic Republican governors and [neoliberal Democrats alike](https://isreview.org/issue/71/obamas-neoliberal-agenda-education/index.html); the expanding [national](https://inequality.org/facts/income-inequality/) and [global wealth gap](https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2021/10/27/global-wealth-has-grown-but-at-the-expense-of-future-prosperity-world-bank) between the ultra-rich and everyone else; the looming collapse of our global ecosystem and many other catastrophic occurrences, cannot be corrected by remaining optimistic, electing the right people, or by the People getting “engaged”. Here, for instance, are some recent suggestions from the American Federation of Teachers on [what we can do to “protect education and help save democracy”](https://aftvoices.org/nine-things-you-can-do-to-protect-education-and-help-save-democracy-36dee80c3f9b). For sure, none of the suggestions hurt our push for a more just society, but what is becoming clearer by the day is that those traditional approaches that have been implemented routinely, are woefully insufficient in this moment wherein we find ourselves.

In 1971, [a pro-capitalist manifesto](https://law2.wlu.edu/deptimages/Powell%20Archives/PowellMemorandumTypescript.pdf) (Powell Memo) was authored by then-corporate attorney and board director at Phillip Morris, Lewis Powell, to Eugene Snydor of the US Chamber of Commerce. For thirty-one pages, the Powell Memo railed against the “attack on the American Free Enterprise System” during the era of domestic social change from 1954-1968. In identifying civil rights and women’s rights activists, environmental protectors, and “leftist” educators on college campuses and in high schools classrooms enemies of capitalism and traditional American values, Powell urged the Chamber of Commerce to push back by investing money and human resources in media, [establishing (advocacy) think tanks (Heritage Foundation, Cato Institute, American Enterprise Institute)](https://www.jceps.com/wp-content/uploads/PDFs/02-2-04.pdf), approving textbooks, drafting academic literature and publications to influence the American political and education system to advance a systemically championed capitalist ideology; a breeding a school of thought for subsequent generations of American students what 1980s British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher expressed pertaining to capitalism, “[There is no alternative](https://www.bbc.com/news/uk-politics-21703018)”.

Shortly thereafter in 1972, Powell was appointed to the US Supreme Court by Nixon and presided over [First National Bank v. Bellotti (1978)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/435/765/), a precursor to [Citizens United (2010)](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/citizens-united-explained) where he concluded that corporate influence in political elections was tantamount to free speech that warranted vigorous protection. As such, explicit and exorbitant corporate influence over our judicial system and political system was born and codified into law and has only exploded in the decades since. And as a result of the impact of the Powell memo (which receives far less attention than it deserves) and his subsequent appointment to the nation’s highest court, we’ve seen a systemic rolling back of both individual rights and collective progress, and concentration of corporate power across every sector of American society since the early 1970s.

Both worker rights and union power have been eroded in the sustained interest of maximizing corporate profits and power; sustained deregulation, detaxation, corporate mergers, industry concentration, and mass layoffs emblematic of the 1990s and 2000s yielded obscene profits for corporations and the wealthiest among us. Our earth, the only place in our solar system that sustains life, will become uninhabitable for humans as it continues to heat up thanks to the influence of corporate spending in messaging and lobbying of lawmakers by the fossil fuel industry. For forty years America’s education system has been hijacked by the demands of the [business community that seeks to optimize efficiency and testing while minimizing opportunities to develop critical thinking of younger generations](https://e4e.org/sites/default/files/a_nation_at_risk_1983.pdf) - even the [contemporary attacks](https://jacobin.com/2022/09/wall-street-culture-war-chris-rufo-critical-race-theory-public-schools) against teaching accurate history in schools are supported [by ultra-rich conservatives.](https://jacobin.com/2022/09/wall-street-culture-war-chris-rufo-critical-race-theory-public-schools) For a much more comprehensive survey of contemporary issues that warrant our attention and activism, [check out what Lois Weiner shared just a few weeks ago](https://futureschools.substack.com/p/this-moment-for-education-workers). Within our modern attention economy, corporate news channels, think tanks, and social media personalities are financially supported by wealthy corporations and wealthy individuals leading purveyors of “news” to deliver ideological content their consumers *want* to see and hear *rather than* what is accurate and true - and in the process, citizens are ill-informed and less capable of making informed decisions about their own best interest.

The list of harm corporate influence has wrought on our society could go on far beyond this submission, but sufficient answers to push back are in much shorter supply. Remaining ignorant of the entrenched corporate chokehold over our future, or hoping to take-to-the-streets or vote our way out of this morass, seem to be insufficient approaches in contesting money’s influence over politics. And it is my belief that no solutions can emerge without a sober understanding of where we are and where we find ourselves. When we consider [Page and Gilens](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/perspectives-on-politics/article/testing-theories-of-american-politics-elites-interest-groups-and-average-citizens/62327F513959D0A304D4893B382B992B) (2012) concluded that Americans live less in a democracy (rule by the People) and more of an oligarchy by conveying that across 1,779 policy issues at the state and federal levels, the “average citizens and mass-based interest groups” had virtually zero impact on what lawmakers did once in office, where conversely the wealthy counterparts and corporate interests frequently saw their wishes realized, we recognize that voting alone is not sufficient. Further, [to recognize the current Trump administration has more combined wealth than any administration in American history](https://www.usnews.com/news/national-news/articles/how-many-billionaires-are-in-trumps-administration-and-what-is-their-worth), should serve as a stark reminder of who truly makes decisions for the rest of us. And what we also know among the ranks of die-hard activists, that activism fatigue is real. People who are committed to engage in causes for which they are committed sacrifice time, peace, and monetary resources to try to push for a better society, and those individuals often experience disillusionment and often disengagement ([Gorski and Chen, 2015](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2015-47043-003)) - [this occurs even when they are on the (temporary) “winning side”](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/perspectives-on-politics/article/abs/cost-of-participating-while-poor-and-black-toward-a-theory-of-collective-participatory-debt/D645A091F05171E5EE83F189DA6BD5C2) (Nuamah, 2021). (Leah Z. Owens does, however, provide strategies for helping us preserve ourselves in the midst of struggle, which can be found [here](https://futureschools.substack.com/p/somatic-abolitionism-as-possibility).)

What prompts me to wonder “*what is enough?*” beyond some of the broader instances referenced above, were my experiences alongside many of my fellow teachers and community members trying to push back against well-resourced and politically connected opposition for decades. Whether the issue was [pushing for more residential employment within the multinational corporations that relocated to Camden after receiving billions of dollars in tax incentives beginning in 2011](https://goodjobsfirst.org/subsidized-camden-companies-hire-few-local-residents/); campaigning to state and local legislators to [return the right to vote for our local school board members to residents](https://www.phillyvoice.com/camden-voters-decide-fate-its-school-board/); or [organizing and participating in a series of demonstrations to keep local public schools open](https://www.cbsnews.com/philadelphia/news/camden-public-school-parents-make-heartfelt-pleas-to-keep-several-schools-open/) while CMO-operated schools hijack our District’s enrollment and pirate our budget which precipitates mass layoffs and school closures, what we’ve had to come to terms with in this small post-industrial city of 71,000 people, is no matter how vociferous we regular people fight and stay engaged, the money Big Money has to spend to blunt the efforts of regular people seems endless, and appears ultimately, to be undefeated. I ask again: *How much is enough? What more do we regular people have to do to achieve and secure lasting change that we’ve been pushing for?* And so, I’m not sure what the way forward is to substantively, and substantially, alter the downward trajectory we’re on precipitated by the 1% ownership of our government. But as someone who, like many of you, has volunteered for countless campaigns for elected officials for a variety of disparate issues against the priorities of well-resourced opposition forces, fatigue, for me at least, is setting in and yet there is always something new to resist.

*How do we move forward, successfully, in protecting all that we hold dear, true democracy, justice, and education within our corporate-owned society that prioritizes consolidating power and maximizing profit?* While there remains a willingness to stand up and resist was discussed [here](https://futureschools.substack.com/p/expanding-cultures-of-solidarity) more comprehensively by Erin Dyke, yet to the questions posed above, I still have no idea of what “works” in a lasting sense anymore. I do however believe openly admitting such, could possibly spur some thoughts from others that could provide a potential blueprint for success - something that’s been fleeting for far too long.

Questions for Consideration:

1.  When (and where) was the last time you recall the People winning and beating back corporate power?
    
    1.  When I pose this question often the response goes back to the Civil Rights Movement of the 1960’s or Feminist movement throughout the 1970s. Perhaps readers may know of something more current. I am intentionally setting aside short-term victories like saving a school from closure, or winning a contract as both (I’ve witnessed) are year to year “victories” that can easily be undone and thus can’t, in my estimation, be counted as a “win”.
