“IT’S BEEN HELL...IT’S JUST EVERYTHING AND ALL OF IT. EVERY TIME WE TURN AROUND.”

This is what I’m hearing from many informed, committed, experienced preK-12 teacher union activists.

If we don’t talk honestly now when we face an authoritarian onslaught destroying our rights as citizens, residents, human beings, when will we?

A generally unspoken and highly uncomfortable truth for many activists committed to transforming teachers unions to make them fight hard and smart is the reality that [we’re often in the minority when we see the need for our unions to be allies in the fight for social justice,](https://newpol.org/uft-shows-how-not-protect-unions-and-public-sector/) even now, even in school systems that are under-resourced, ones in which working conditions are brutal. What seems a national malaise is challenged by important exceptions, almost always under the radar of national media, [like the strike wave in Massachusetts](https://tempestmag.org/2025/08/strike-lessons-from-massachusetts-teachers/), encouraged by a democratized NEA affiliate that’s led by reformers. Still, talking honestly demands we acknowledge persuading our colleagues to be more active in the union is an uphill slog. The reasons are not likely to evaporate soon. They’re ingrained in how schools organize our work and the way teaching is viewed in the society, as well as how unions function in our economic system - topics that go well beyond this article.

Examining reasons many education workers ignore their unions explains much of the problem, and for many experienced activists, a frustration. Teaching is far more than a 9-5 job even if you try to set boundaries. We have more to accomplish during school hours than is possible. The hierarchy in schools, our lack of voice, can be enervating, and finding ways to assert our knowledge and judgment, do what we know is needed, makes our work even more stressful. Union activity comes on top of school work. And if we want better unions, union reform is yet another demand on our time and mental real estate. Many teachers also feel we must be politically active, supporting our students to thrive or survive, through community organizations or social movements. Given our need, our right to have personal lives, these demands made on educators’ time and energy, let’s flip the script and consider an expectation of union activism in this moment as the exception, not the norm. Those who do it are remarkable. You’re first-responders. Thanks and kudos. We can’t clone you but we can build the movement so others see the unions’ unrealized potential and join in making it real.

DO EDUCATION WORKERS NEED TO BE “POLITICAL”?

We still hear the argument that teachers unions shouldn’t get involved in “politics,” that this isn’t the union’s responsibility. I’ve had exchanges on social media with education workers who argue that being involved in our fighting for or against anything that’s not local, like cuts to Medicaid, for example, diverts resources from winning what we need as workers: good salaries, benefits, pensions, and protection from administrative tyranny. Yet I know from the way these same individuals discuss their work, they care about their students and go well-beyond the minimum to help them. Somehow they don’t connect the hard work and caring on behalf of their students in school with another reality of teachers’ work: We can’t separate what goes on outside the school from what happens in our classrooms. Students, like teachers, are human, and when most people are hungry, tired, or sick, our minds are on our physical needs. That’s why we need to provide breakfasts and lunch for kids.

Even the US Army says, “Nutrition is more than a personal choice – [it’s a mission-critical component.”](https://www.army.mil/article/284118/food_connects_us_nutrition_community_important_for_service_members) If being well-fed is “mission-critical” for an institution that trains people to kill, it’s even more “mission-critical” for our society to teach kids how to solve problems without killing. So when the federal government [guts the program that helps feed kids whose families can’t afford money for food](https://www.epi.org/blog/cuts-to-snap-benefits-will-disproportionately-harm-families-of-color-and-children/), that’s simultaneously a “political” issue and a teaching matter.[\[i\]](#_edn1) Unions that serve their members well intervene to change conditions that affect our work, even when the source of the problem goes beyond the school/workplace walls. For unions of education workers this is a moral imperative: It’s an expression of our professional ideals to insist on conditions under which students can learn.

Readers have likely heard colleagues say something like “I work with whoever shows up and wants to learn. My job ends there, whether the kids learn or not.” As is the case with blaming students or parents for systemic failings in schools, the stance expresses frustration and demoralization.[\[ii\]](#_edn2) I have seen this attitude changed, even in those in whom it has long been ingrained, when teachers and those in support roles feel hopeful and empowered to make serious change in how they are viewed and treated. A few people may begin careers in education with a what’s often called a “civil-service “attitude but we know from research the vast majority of prospective teachers want a job that is stable, pays the bills, fits with family life, and has meaning - making the world better by helping children, explaining ideas or subjects we love. A good union, one in which we have voice, one that helps us to feel and be supported to sustain the ideals that brought us into work with students, fills the vacuum created by despair, replacing it with energy and activity to win improvements. The union carries out the collective expression of what inspired us to choose work with kids and ideas.

What about political involvement in elections, endorsing candidates? Education workers in the public sector either directly or indirectly bargain with employers who are elected. Regardless of the candidates we support (and I’ve written a great deal about this elsewhere), to win political battles we need public support. This is especially true when we earn more than most residents in the communities we serve, a reality in many, many districts. We need allies who will speak up for us. Our natural allies are parents and activists in the communities in which we teach. If we want them to show up for us, they have to trust we are present for them.

Over the past twenty years the Right has orchestrated extraordinarily effective campaigns to make people think teachers are selfish, the schools are failing, and the solution is privatization - and weakening and/or destroying real unions. Since Trump2, the powerful elites who have longed tried to destroy public schools with charters and vouchers have expanded their reach and are much closer to achieving their goals. The neoliberal push to undercut teachers unions with frontal attacks on teachers’ moral and professional judgment, controlling schools and learning with standardized testing, has been converted to a Christian-nationalist project by the far Right. [Teacher Freedom Alliance](https://www.teacherfreedomalliance.com/), a “professional organization” that wants to help teachers “develop free, moral, and upright American citizens” and aims to replace teachers unions is an appendage of the Koch-funded [Freedom Foundation.](https://littlesis.org/org/83046-Freedom_Foundation) Ryan Walters, disgraced former head of Oklahoma schools, is now its CEO.

The best defense against the message that teachers care only about ourselves is to be and be seen as a respectful ally with the people who need public schools to help their kids have a better life. Our fights for better pensions are undercut when most people whose children we teach have none. Our right to have health benefits for ourselves and our families is a hard sell when the families of children we teach, working people who pay taxes to support schools, do not have health care from their employers. With each cut by Trump and the Right onof programs that help people to live, the urgency increases for us to organize and join with all those who will fight with us for the employer-based “benefits” public employees have to become rights guaranteed by the states and federal government.

The moment has changed and so must be our willingness to adopt strategies that may once have seemed too idealistic, too “out there” for members accustomed to passivity and the status quo. The only way for us to protect what we and our predecessors have won over the years is to demand the state and federal government change course, funding what we need by greatly increasing taxes on the super-wealthy and their sources of income. Asking whether it’s realistic to try to use our political power to try to win Medicare for All/Single Payer or increased funding for schools rather than just clawing back what has been cut ignores that our foes are far more powerful than they were just four years ago. They have upped the political ante. We have to not only match their game but get in front of it. We squander our strength in trying to win only incremental gains with lobbying and electing “friends” who vote with us, with the boost NGO’s that represent but are not controlled by community and receive significant funding from the unions.

NEA and AFT are well-known, notorious and villainized on the Right, for their political clout on the state and federal levels. Now is the time to use and build that power. That means going beyond the strategies they have put forward, lobbying and participating in large mobilizations organized by liberals tied to the Democratic Party. We need to not only consider but organize illegal actions, mass civil disobedience, including walkouts. This is the way we won rights that are being washed away with breathtaking speed, a process that has often been enabled by Democrats who relied on union dollars and votes in their elections.

SOCIAL JUSTICE TEACHERS UNIONS REQUIRE UNION DEMOCRACY

When I first started writing about “social justice teachers unions,” my collaborator, Mary Compton, a past president of the UK’s largest teachers union, questioned the term because defending workers’ rights is itself a social justice struggle. Mary’s observation clarifies what can be missed in use of the term “social justice union” to describe the type of union I advocated [in the book that inspired](https://www.haymarketbooks.org/books/497-the-future-of-our-schools) this collective’s work. Let’s be absolutely clear a labor union’s responsibility is to defend the well-being of its members, which includes salaries. [The “teacher pay gap”](https://www.epi.org/publication/the-teacher-pay-penalty-reached-a-record-high-in-2024-three-decades-of-leaving-public-school-teachers-behind/?mc_cid=9d11e00f28&mc_eid=bb57d49403) has consequences for us as workers, as well as for education and the society. But what neither AFT nor NEA acknowledge is that the decline in real wages relative to other occupations requiring equivalent education shows is that our national unions and many local and state affiliates have not provided the leadership we need.

The question isn’t *whether* to protect members’ interests but rather *how* we do it. Our success requires having unions that break from the “business” or “contract” or “service union” tradition, which isolate economic gains from our dignity as workers, on the job and in the society. The unrelenting employer offensive fueled by the anti-union Right, abetted by the unions’ ossification, in part a [product of “business” union practices and assumptions](https://inthesetimes.com/article/janus-right-to-work-union-labor), have made education unions vulnerable to our Right’s campaigns against us. Though the policies adopted in the bipartisan neoliberal onslaught were aimed at privatizing education and controlling what is taught and how with standardized testing, the real intent was obscured by rhetoric about creating educational opportunity. The brilliantly orchestrated media campaigns won over many parents in low-income communities of color who saw their children were ignored by public education. And fairly or not, they blamed teachers and their unions for defending an unjust status quo. AFT and its larger but politically less aggressive junior partner NEA responded to the extraordinarily powerful campaign against teachers and our unions by capitulating to neoliberal policies, endorsing/accepting testing and failing to fight against privatization. The unions and prominent liberal allies further weakened us by declining to acknowledge the education establishment’s complicity in sustaining a system of unequal schooling and its systemic manifestations of social oppression. Yet, so much research has been published and publicized about how inequality stemming from social oppression was and is embedded in our schools.

While the NEA and AFT faltered in defending us, a new movement of education workers was developing. Its power was announced when the Chicago Teachers Union, CTU, electrified labor and education with its strike against Rahm Emanuel and the Democrats’ neoliberal policies. Challenging the “business unionism” of labor, including AFT and NEA, the Caucus of Rank and File Workers (CORE) won leadership of the CTU, democratized its functioning, drew on its members for strength and leadership, and embedded economic demands in a program centering racial justice and demands for conditions in [schools that students deserved](https://www.ctulocal1.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/01/SCSD_Report-2012-02-16.pdf). [\[iii\]](#_edn3)

Some critics have told me “social justice teacher unionism” signals that (left) activists want the union to be an all-purpose (left) political organization. Yet, when I discuss specifics of how and when the union should adopt “political” positions, our disagreements are negotiable *when we start from a shared commitment to militancy and democracy*, understanding we need the union’s presence in the schools, fighting on issues that union members feel affect them as workers, building and sustaining as best we can rank-and-file activism.

A culture and practice of union democracy is very fragile, requiring time, energy, and sacrifice, sometimes at a significant personal cost. Vigilant enforcement of regulations that give members the right to decide policy and elect officers is hard work, and even that is a necessary but insufficient condition to create and maintain the unions we need in this moment. Deep, thorough union democracy depends on the union developing a consciousness among members that *they* are the union. This process is in turn nurtured by the union supporting members to bring their concerns into the union and shape its priorities, even when this process conflicts with what the leadership thinks is best. We need strong elected leaders who help refine and defend the union’s vision. *And union officers, no matter how savvy or articulate, need a democratic culture and strong voices within the membership who hold their feet to the fire.*

What does a democratic union look like, one with an informed, mobilized rank-and-file? Based on what I see and hear from union activists, I think union democracy is a moving target, best assessed by asking questions. How well do the union’s constitution and bylaws support members’ exercising their oversight of the union apparatus? How many school sites have a strong union presence? How often do working teachers/union members represent the union in public - or is the expectation (albeit encouraged by the media) that only the union president can speak for members? How often does the union president’s picture appear in materials the union produces? What are the officers’ salaries in comparison to what union members earn? How are staff selected - or elected? Is the bargaining team selected by union officials or is it elected directly by members? How are union elections conducted - and what percentage of members vote?

A new generation of activists needs to be educated about the principles that guided growth of the first movement for social justice teacher unions. We have no time to waste. If the union’s elected officers aren’t doing this job of political education, members must. I don’t underestimate the enormity of the challenge education workers face in this moment. For many, maybe most teachers, the start of this school year has been like none other, understandably so because we’ve been shaken and the society altered from what it was before Trump’s election.

Many are looking for answers and feel stranded by the enormity of the onslaught. To that I respond that acknowledging we don’t have solutions is honest and healthy; not having all the answers doesn’t mean we have none. Facing our limitations and yet continuing to struggle as best we can opens us to new understandings and can unleash energy we think we can’t produce. I’ve seen this process in the FOS collective since our initial meetings and our first post. In the process of supporting one another, admitting what we can’t do, navigating disagreements, stepping in to help, we’ve evolved in our thinking and confidence. If you haven’t read the previous articles, [all archived on the home page](https://futureschools.substack.com/), click when you have the energy. I think you may find material that speaks to where you’ve been since Trump was elected or where you are right now.

Trump, the Right, and MAGA, want us to think they’re in total control. They’re not. Change is occurring, often unrecognized even as it occurs in ourselves, in our consciousness, and in the formation of networks. Change is mostly molecular, unseen and unarticulated, in a process that produces what seems (but isn’t) a spontaneous eruption of resistance.

Our goal as a collective is to support that process. To that end we’ll be tweaking how we relate to our readers and the struggles you face. In the meantime, if this article or another has encouraged you or explained something you didn’t know that you think matters, how about if you share it with another teacher or education worker, not necessarily in your school or district? Let’s get those molecules moving!

Thanks for reading. I learn a lot from your comments, public and private. Thanks for those as well.

Lois

[\[i\]](#_ednref1)The survey AFT commissioned about [teachers paying for students’ food](https://www.aft.org/press-release/aft-survey-shows-teachers-are-spending-hundreds-out-pocket-food-kids-classroom) , which has been picked up by liberal media, in my opinion, should be ignored, by activists and education writers. The methodology has not been explained, the sample is tiny (750 members), and what little information is provided seems problematic, in particular the the demographic. A union of education workers deserves real research on issues of concern to members. At least two Canadian unions show us how to do it: the [Alberta Teachers’ Association](https://teachers.ab.ca/professional-development/education-research/research-publications) and the [BC Teachers’ Federation](https://www.bctf.ca/topics/services-information/research-and-education-issues). Both unions are involved in struggles over their contracts, mobilizing members to contest government priorities - another way they model what NEA and AFT might do but don’t.

[\[ii\]](#_ednref2) Research about improving teaching for students who have historically been “underserved” often takes up the “deficit paradigm,” which analyzes why education workers often blame students’ problems on their personal limitations. This [excellent literature review](https://www.scienceopen.com/document_file/200cbdd2-8cfa-49b0-8959-d6f765dedb18/ScienceOpenPreprint/Preprint-Combined.pdf) explains how the deficit paradigm misses how schools’ systemic characteristics undercut our seeing students’ potential. The flip side is that school workers are often held accountable as individuals for school and social failings. Daniel Jerome and I analyze how new teachers can navigate these systemic problems in city schools, in *[Urban Teaching: The Essentials](https://www.tcpress.com/urban-teaching-9780807756898)*. Several teachers have told me the book was the gateway drug to the *Future of Our Schools*.

[\[iii\]](#_ednref3) I have heard union activists I respect refer to a caucus or individual “taking power” in a union. It’s important for us to remember and say, always, that CEO’s and bosses “take power.” Union officers, no matter how big their presence and impact, are elected - by members. Karen Lewis, a driving force and a remarkable leader in CORE and the CTU, articulated this well when she described the CTU as “member driven.” And it’s vital to keep in mind that when a reform caucus is elected, the individuals who win leadership are subject to many of conservatizing pressures that affect “business” unions. We can’t avoid or ignore these influences as long as unions have to function within capitalism’s contradictions, but understanding them [can help to inform pushback](https://newpol.org/after-you-win-union-election-what-next/). So please, don’t use our enemies’ formulation about union leaders taking power, even if they’ve done so with a machine that ignores fundamental democratic safeguards.
