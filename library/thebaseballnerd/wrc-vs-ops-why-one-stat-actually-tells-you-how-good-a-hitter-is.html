There is a version of this article that hedges. One that says “every stat has its place” and wraps everything in so many qualifications that you walk away having learned nothing useful. That is not this article.

wRC+ is the best single-number snapshot of a hitter’s offensive value available to the public. Not perfect. Not the end of the conversation. But the best place to start one. Here is why, built from the math up.

wOBA: The Foundation Under wRC+

To trust wRC+, you need to understand what it is built on.

wOBA (Weighted On-Base Average) assigns empirically derived run values to every way of reaching base. Not total bases. Not a simple on-base or slugging split. Actual historical run-probability weights, recalibrated every season.

Those weights look roughly like this in a typical season: unintentional walks around 0.69, hit by pitch around 0.72, singles around 0.89, doubles around 1.27, triples around 1.62, home runs around 2.10. These are not guesses. They are derived from play-by-play data measuring how often each event actually leads to runs.

The scaling factor applied to wOBA exists only to put the numbers on a familiar OBP-style scale, somewhere in the .250-.400 range, so that traditional fans have a readable reference. The scale does not change what is being measured.

wOBA’s honest limitations are worth stating. It does not adjust for park or era. It slightly undervalues elite base-stealers. It does not account for position. These are exactly the gaps that wRC+ closes.

wRC+ vs. wOBA: Which One Should You Use?

They are not competitors. wOBA is an input into wRC+. The question is really about when to use the base stat versus the adjusted one.

Use wOBA when you want to evaluate the quality of contact and plate discipline independent of park or era. It is a clean, interpretable measure of how well a hitter performed at the plate before any external adjustment.

Use wRC+ when you want to compare hitters across different environments. A .380 wOBA at Coors Field is a meaningfully different achievement than a .380 wOBA at Petco Park. wRC+ accounts for that. A .380 wOBA in 2000, when offense was at a historic peak, is also a different achievement than a .380 wOBA in 2014. wRC+ accounts for that too.

For cross-era or cross-park comparisons, wRC+ is the right tool. For evaluating how a hitter performed in a vacuum, wOBA is cleaner.

What Is wRC+ in Baseball?

wRC+ stands for Weighted Runs Created Plus. It is a rate stat that measures how much offensive value a hitter produced per plate appearance compared to league average, after adjusting for the ballpark they play in.

The scale is intentional and simple. League average is always 100. Every point above 100 is one percentage point better than league average. Every point below is one percentage point worse. A 130 wRC+ means the hitter produced 30% more offensive value than the average MLB hitter in the same number of plate appearances. A 85 wRC+ means 15% less.

That consistency across seasons and eras is not a small thing. A 130 wRC+ in 2003 means the same thing as a 130 wRC+ in 2024. The same cannot be said of raw counting stats, or even traditional rate stats that do not adjust for run environment.

wRC vs. wRC+: What Is the Difference?

This question shows up constantly, and it matters enough to answer directly.

**wRC** (Weighted Runs Created, no plus sign) is a counting stat. It estimates the total number of runs a hitter contributed to his team, expressed as a raw number. More plate appearances mean more wRC, even if the hitter is mediocre. A player with 700 plate appearances at a .300 wOBA will accumulate more wRC than a player with 400 plate appearances at a .380 wOBA. That makes wRC useful for measuring total production but difficult to use for rate comparisons.

**wRC+** converts that counting framework into a rate, normalizes it against league average, and applies a park adjustment. The plus sign in any baseball stat, OPS+, ERA+, FIP-, signals the same thing: the number has been adjusted for context and placed on a 100-equals-average scale.

So when someone asks “what is wRC in baseball,” they are asking about the foundation. When they ask “what is wRC+ in baseball,” they are asking about the finished product. The finished product is what you want for player evaluation.

How Is wRC+ Calculated?

The math runs in two steps.

Step one: calculate wRC.

This takes a player’s wOBA, measures how far above or below league average it sits, converts it back into raw run probability units by removing the wOBA scaling factor, adds the baseline league run rate, and multiplies by plate appearances. The result is an estimate of total runs created.

Step two: adjust for park and league to get wRC+

The park adjustment is additive. It asks: given the run-scoring environment of this player’s home park, how many runs per plate appearance should we add or subtract? A hitter playing half his games at Petco Park deserves credit for suppressed counting numbers. A hitter playing half his games at Coors deserves a deduction. The park factor handles that.

The constants, league wOBA, wOBA scale, league R/PA, park factors, are updated every season and published publicly on FanGraphs’ Guts! page. Nothing in this formula is hidden or proprietary.

wRC+ vs. OPS+: Why the Difference Matters

Both stats normalize to 100. Both adjust for park. Both are trying to answer the same basic question: how much better or worse than average is this hitter, accounting for context? The difference is in what they start from.

OPS+ starts from OPS. On-base percentage plus slugging percentage, adjusted for park and league. OPS has a structural problem: OBP and SLG are not measured on the same scale and do not carry equal run-production weight. A point of OBP is worth more in run-production terms than a point of SLG. OPS treats them as equal by adding them directly. OPS+ adjusts a number that was already imprecise.

OPS also uses total bases in its slugging component, which means a double is counted as worth exactly twice a single. That is not what the run-scoring data shows. The actual run-value difference between a double and a single is smaller than 2:1, and the gap between a home run and a triple is larger than the 4:3 ratio total bases imply.

wRC+ starts from wOBA, which replaces that additive structure with actual linear run weights. Then it applies the same park and league adjustment that OPS+ applies. The result is a more precisely grounded number going into the same normalization process.

Here is the honest part: in practice, they agree most of the time. Baseball Prospectus ran the correlation between OPS+ and wRC+ across all batting title qualifiers from 1998 through 2018 and found a coefficient of 0.992. They are not telling different stories about most players. A 130 OPS+ and a 130 wRC+ are going to belong to similar hitters.

The gap matters most at the edges. High-walk, low-power hitters tend to look better under wRC+ because OPS undervalues the run contribution of walks relative to extra-base hits in slugging. Heavy ground-ball, high-average hitters with few walks can look better under OPS+. Elite contact hitters who do everything a little rather than one thing a lot are where the two stats diverge most meaningfully.

For everyday use, wRC+ is more precisely constructed. That precision does not always change the conclusion. But when it does, the more rigorous number is right more often.

What wRC+ Does Not Tell You

This is where the “use it in conjunction with other stats” section of this article lives, and it is not a disclaimer. It is part of the analytical framework.

wRC+ measures offensive value at the plate. Nothing else. It does not capture baserunning, which can be worth multiple wins per season for an elite runner. It does not capture defense, which is the other half of a position player’s total value. It does not adjust for position, so a 95 wRC+ shortstop and a 95 wRC+ first baseman are in completely different roster situations.

It also has a known issue with Colorado. Coors Field’s park factor is extreme enough that wRC+ applies an aggressive downward adjustment to Rockies hitters that some analysts believe overcorrects. A Rockies hitter with a wRC+ of 85 may be a better offensive player than the number suggests. This is worth flagging when evaluating Colorado position players specifically.

For total player value, wRC+ is the offensive input for FanGraphs WAR. The full picture adds baserunning, fielding, positional adjustment, and a replacement-level baseline. wRC+ gets you most of the offensive story. WAR finishes it.

The Bottom Line

wRC+ is not perfect. No single number is. But for answering the question “how good is this hitter, accounting for where he plays and when he played,” it is the most precisely constructed publicly available tool. It starts from real run-probability weights, not totals-base arithmetic. It adjusts for both park and era. It stays on a scale you can read in two seconds.

Use it as your first look. Let the full picture come from what you add around it.
