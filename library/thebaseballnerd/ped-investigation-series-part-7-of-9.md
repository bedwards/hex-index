*This is Part 7 of our nine-week investigation. We’ve proven Congressional inaction, documented that Bonds, Clemens, and Rodriguez had Hall of Fame careers before PED use, and exposed how MLB profited five to ten billion dollars while knowing about widespread steroid use. This week we examine whether steroids actually enhanced performance as dramatically as everyone assumes. The statistical evidence is surprising: contact-adjusted power barely changed between eras, career extension effects are nonexistent except for two outliers, and players’ best seasons often came before or after alleged PED use rather than during it. The moral panic about performance enhancement might be based on a false premise.*

What is the narrative around performance enhancing drugs anyway?  

The conventional narrative about steroids in baseball goes like this: players took performance enhancing drugs and became superhuman. Home runs skyrocketed. Careers extended into players’ forties. Mediocre athletes became stars. The numbers were fake. The records were tainted. Everything changed.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!M6Gz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe0dbc70f-712c-4b72-b45b-a4f639c6e7f4_550x128.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!M6Gz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe0dbc70f-712c-4b72-b45b-a4f639c6e7f4_550x128.png)

Except that’s not what the data shows. When you analyze the statistics instead of repeating assumptions, a different picture emerges. Power numbers didn’t increase as much as people think when you adjust for contact rates. Career lengths didn’t extend for most users. Many players performed worse while using steroids than before they started. And some of the biggest names in the steroid era had Hall of Fame credentials before they allegedly touched a needle.

This isn’t defending steroid use. This is examining whether steroids actually did what everyone claims they did. Because if the performance enhancement was minimal or nonexistent for most users, then the entire moral panic was based on a false premise.

**The Contact Rate Analysis Nobody Talks About**

In August 2013, ESPN’s David Schoenfield published an analysis that should have changed the conversation about steroids in baseball. Working with baseball analyst Joe Sheehan, Schoenfield examined whether power numbers actually increased during the steroid era when you adjusted for contact rates.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Xoxe!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F759d340a-93cb-4f01-9561-8270cbbda220_548x93.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Xoxe!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F759d340a-93cb-4f01-9561-8270cbbda220_548x93.png)

The logic is straightforward. If steroids massively increased power, you should see huge differences in contact-adjusted metrics. Home runs per ball put in play should spike during the steroid era and decline after testing began. Isolated power per contact should show similar patterns.

That’s not what the data showed.

In 2000, considered the peak of the steroid era, MLB players hit home runs on 4.19% of balls put in play. In 2012, after years of strict testing, the rate was 3.83%. In 2013, it was 3.67%. That’s a decline from one home run per 24 balls in play to one home run per 27 balls in play. A difference of roughly half a percentage point.

Isolated power, which measures raw power by calculating total bases minus singles divided by at bats, showed similar modest differences. In 2000, isolated power per contact was .205. In 2012, it was .193. In 2013, it was .186. Again, there’s a decline but it’s gradual and modest, not the massive drop you’d expect if steroids were creating wholesale fake power.

So what changed? Strikeouts. Players in the modern era strike out far more than players in 2000. Strikeouts mean less contact. Less contact means fewer home runs in absolute terms even if power per contact remains similar.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!i6V6!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F02e29b1d-1765-4a70-b85c-ae0e2f7eaf17_591x476.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!i6V6!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F02e29b1d-1765-4a70-b85c-ae0e2f7eaf17_591x476.png)

Schoenfield’s conclusion: “If Alex Rodriguez didn’t gain any benefits from using PEDs, why get all worked up over his pending suspension?” The question was rhetorical, but it’s worth taking seriously. If steroids didn’t massively enhance power when you account for contact rates, then what exactly did they do?

**The Career Extension Myth**

The most comprehensive statistical study of whether steroids extended careers came from Dr. David J. Gordon and was published in the SABR Research Journal in Fall 2021. Dr. Gordon is a physician and statistician who examined whether players alleged to have used performance enhancing drugs had statistically significant differences in post-35 career performance compared to clean players.

The methodology was rigorous. Dr. Gordon identified players who appeared in the Mitchell Report or were otherwise credibly accused of PED use. He then compared their post-35 performance to clean players with similar pre-35 performance levels. This controlled for selection bias. He wasn’t comparing Barry Bonds to random players. He was comparing players who had similar career trajectories up to age 35 and then seeing if the alleged PED users performed better after that age.

The results were startling.

For position players, Dr. Gordon found an average difference of +0.85 WAR in post-35 performance for alleged PED users compared to clean players. That sounds meaningful until you see the p-value: 0.19. In statistical terms, this is not significant. You can’t conclude that PED users performed better after 35.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kqck!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbd233111-577b-401b-a113-ca4b0911f131_544x216.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kqck!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbd233111-577b-401b-a113-ca4b0911f131_544x216.png)

Then Dr. Gordon removed Barry Bonds from the sample. Bonds is a massive outlier whose post-35 performance was unprecedented regardless of PED use. With Bonds removed, the average difference dropped to +0.26 WAR with a p-value of 0.78. Completely insignificant. Statistically indistinguishable from zero.

For pitchers, Dr. Gordon found a larger difference of +3.0 WAR with a p-value of 0.07. This approaches significance but doesn’t quite reach the standard threshold of 0.05. More importantly, when Dr. Gordon removed Roger Clemens from the sample, the difference dropped to +1.67 WAR with a p-value of 0.34. Not significant.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Wief!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc13fb3c7-ea32-4033-824f-76709d780ed8_565x222.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Wief!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc13fb3c7-ea32-4033-824f-76709d780ed8_565x222.png)

The conclusion is unavoidable: the entire narrative about steroids extending careers rests on two outliers, Barry Bonds and Roger Clemens. Remove them from the sample and there’s no measurable effect. Every other player alleged to have used steroids showed no statistical evidence of career extension. They aged normally. They declined normally. Whatever steroids did for them, it didn’t keep them productive into their forties.

**The Post-Testing Paradox**

Dr. Gordon’s study revealed another anomaly that undermines the steroid narrative. If steroids were widespread before testing and significantly extended careers, you should see fewer productive older players after testing began in 2004-2005.

That’s not what happened. The period from 2006 to 2010, after MLB implemented strict testing, had more productive older players than the peak steroid era from 1990 to 2000.

There are two possible explanations. First, testing didn’t work and players continued using undetectable substances. Second, steroids weren’t actually causing the career extensions attributed to them.

Dr. Gordon suggests the second explanation is more likely. Players are staying productive longer in the modern era because of advances in training, nutrition, medical treatment, and recovery techniques that have nothing to do with performance enhancing drugs. The improved performance of older players in recent years reflects legitimate advances in sports science, not continued widespread doping.

This doesn’t prove steroids don’t work. It proves they don’t work the way everyone assumed they did. Whatever edge steroids provided, it wasn’t dramatic enough to show up in aggregate career extension data when you control for player quality and era effects.

**The Alex Rodriguez Paradox**

Alex Rodriguez admitted to using steroids from 2001 to 2003 while playing for the Texas Rangers. This gives us three years of confirmed PED use bookended by periods where there’s no evidence of use. If steroids massively enhanced Rodriguez’s performance, his best seasons should have been during his PED years. That’s not what the statistics show.

Rodriguez’s career WAR by age: Age 20 in 1996, 9.4 WAR. Age 24 in 2000, 10.4 WAR. Age 25 in 2001 when admitted PED use begins, 8.3 WAR. Age 26 in 2002, 8.8 WAR. Age 27 in 2003 when admitted PED use ends, 8.4 WAR. Age 29 in 2005, 9.4 WAR. Age 31 in 2007, 9.4 WAR.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!M9rd!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0f2b9334-4d22-485a-8147-f2a2874bfdee_591x318.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!M9rd!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0f2b9334-4d22-485a-8147-f2a2874bfdee_591x318.png)

Rodriguez’s two best seasons by WAR came at ages 20 and 24, before his admitted PED use. His three seasons during admitted PED use averaged 8.5 WAR. His two best post-admission seasons at ages 29 and 31 matched his age-20 peak at 9.4 WAR.

During the years Rodriguez admitted using steroids, his home run totals increased dramatically. He hit 52, 57, and 47 home runs from 2001 to 2003. That’s 156 home runs in three years, a 93% increase over his pre-PED rate.

But his overall value as measured by WAR declined. He hit more home runs but was less valuable to his team. The increased power came at the cost of other aspects of his game. His defensive value declined. His baserunning value declined. His hitting approach became more homer-focused and less well-rounded.

This is the paradox: steroids increased Rodriguez’s raw power numbers but decreased his overall effectiveness. If you’re a GM trying to win games, you’d rather have age-24 A-Rod posting 10.4 WAR than age-26 A-Rod hitting 57 home runs but posting 8.8 WAR.

**The Jason Giambi Case Study**

Jason Giambi is the ideal case study for measuring steroid effectiveness because he admitted to everything. During the BALCO grand jury proceedings, Giambi testified that he used steroids and human growth hormone from 2001 to 2004. We know exactly when he used and what he used. So we can measure the before, during, and after effects with precision.

Giambi’s career splits tell a story that undermines the steroid narrative.

Pre-BALCO from 1995 to 2000, Giambi hit .314/.441/.567 with an OPS+ of 160. His 2000 MVP season came before his admitted use: .333/.476/.647 with 43 home runs and 137 RBI.

During admitted use from 2001 to 2004, Giambi hit .279/.405/.510 with an OPS+ of 145. His batting average dropped 35 points. His on-base percentage dropped 36 points. His slugging dropped 57 points.

Yes, he hit more home runs. But he was a worse hitter overall. He struck out more. He walked less. His approach at the plate became less disciplined and more focused on power. The steroids might have helped him hit the ball farther, but they didn’t make him a better player.

After Giambi apologized in 2007 and presumably stopped using, his performance continued to decline in a pattern consistent with normal aging. He played nine more years, gradually becoming a platoon designated hitter and then a bench player. Nothing about his post-PED career suggests the drugs had extended his playing days.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zAj6!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8c14eca6-ec11-4642-b938-2fd997704ae7_566x353.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zAj6!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8c14eca6-ec11-4642-b938-2fd997704ae7_566x353.png)

The Giambi case undermines almost every assumption about steroids. He was elite before using them. He was worse while using them despite increased power. He aged normally after stopping. Whatever benefits he gained from PEDs were offset by the costs to other aspects of his game.

**The Marginal Player Test**

If steroids create massive advantages, marginal players should become stars. Guys who were career minor leaguers should suddenly perform at major league levels. Fringe major leaguers should become regulars. Regulars should become All-Stars.

The Mitchell Report named 89 players. Many were stars or regulars. But the report also named plenty of marginal players who allegedly used PEDs. What happened to them?

Randy Velarde was a utility infielder who allegedly used PEDs. Career statistics: .276 batting average, 100 home runs over 16 years, 15.6 career WAR. Mediocre before BALCO, mediocre during, mediocre after.

Benito Santiago was a catcher alleged to have used. He had a solid career but was clearly declining during his alleged PED years. The drugs didn’t stop the aging process or resurrect his career.

Marvin Benard was an outfielder allegedly supplied by BALCO. Career statistics: .271 batting average, 46 home runs, 6.4 career WAR over nine years. Nothing about his career suggests transformation from PED use.

The marginal players who allegedly used steroids remained marginal. They didn’t become stars. They didn’t suddenly develop skills they didn’t have before. They played about as well as you’d expect based on their talent level.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wpzV!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F956e7f74-dbdd-44ae-8370-ba2c7fdf2fe7_462x88.png)

This is consistent with the research showing minimal measurable effects from PEDs. If steroids don’t dramatically extend careers for good players and don’t transform marginal players into stars, then what exactly are they doing?

**What Steroids Actually Did**

The evidence suggests steroids provided modest benefits that varied significantly by individual. Some players like Barry Bonds and Roger Clemens experienced dramatic late-career peaks. But they’re outliers. Most players showed minimal effects.

The most consistent effect appears to be increased recovery time. Players using steroids could work out harder, recover faster, and play through injuries that might otherwise require rest. This explains why disabled list costs exploded during the steroid era despite the alleged performance benefits. Players were pushing their bodies beyond natural limits and suffering the consequences.

Steroids also provided psychological benefits. Players believed they were getting an edge, which increased confidence, which affected performance. The placebo effect in athletic performance is well-documented. If you think you’re stronger and faster, you perform better even if the physiological changes are minimal.

Finally, steroids provided bulk. Players got bigger. They looked more impressive. This mattered for contract negotiations and public perception even if it didn’t always translate to better performance. A player who looked like he could hit 40 home runs got paid like he could hit 40 home runs even if he only hit 25.

But the massive, game-changing, record-shattering effects everyone assumes? The statistical evidence doesn’t support them for most players.

**The Bonds and Clemens Exception**

Barry Bonds and Roger Clemens are outliers in every statistical analysis of PED effects. Their late-career peaks were unprecedented. No amount of training or nutrition or recovery techniques explains what Bonds did from ages 36 to 39 or what Clemens did from ages 38 to 44.

Bonds at age 39 posted an OPS+ of 259. That’s 159% better than league average. At 39 years old. It’s absurd. It’s unprecedented. It’s statistically impossible based on every aging curve ever documented.

Clemens won his seventh Cy Young Award at age 42. He posted a 1.87 ERA at age 42. His second-best ERA of his career at an age when most pitchers are retired or hanging on as long relievers.

These performances are so anomalous that they demand explanation. And the most obvious explanation is that whatever PEDs Bonds and Clemens used, they worked far better for them than for anyone else.

But here’s the thing: both players were Hall of Fame locks before their alleged PED use began. Bonds had three MVPs, 411 home runs, and roughly 100 WAR through age 34. That’s a Hall of Fame career. Clemens had 192 wins, four Cy Young Awards, and roughly 90 WAR through age 35. That’s a Hall of Fame career.

The late-career peaks added to already secure legacies. They didn’t create those legacies. Bonds would be in the Hall of Fame without the BALCO years. Clemens would be in the Hall of Fame without the post-1997 seasons. The PEDs, if they worked as advertised for these two players, enhanced already-certain Hall of Fame careers. They didn’t manufacture them from scratch.
