*This is Part 8 of our nine-week investigation. We’ve documented Congressional inaction, proven statistical cases for Bonds, Clemens, and Rodriguez, exposed MLB’s ten billion dollar profit from the steroid era, and questioned whether steroids even enhanced performance as dramatically as assumed. This week we examine the Baseball Hall of Fame’s selective application of character standards. They display steroid-era artifacts while excluding the players who created them. They inducted Bud Selig while excluding Barry Bonds. They celebrate Gaylord Perry despite his admitted cheating. They ignore segregation advantages and amphetamine use but exclusively punish steroid users. The Hall of Fame doesn’t uphold moral principles. It protects institutional reputation at players’ expense.*

\---

The National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum is located in Cooperstown, New York. It’s a beautiful building filled with artifacts from baseball’s 150-year history. Bats, balls, gloves, uniforms, photographs, plaques. Everything that tells the story of America’s pastime.

Including artifacts from the steroid era.

Barry Bonds’s 756th home run ball, the one that broke Hank Aaron’s all-time record, is on display at the Hall of Fame. Mark McGwire’s bat from his 70-home-run season is there. Sammy Sosa’s equipment is there. The Hall charges admission to see these artifacts. They market them. They use them to tell baseball’s story.

But Barry Bonds isn’t in the Hall of Fame. Mark McGwire isn’t in the Hall of Fame. Sammy Sosa isn’t in the Hall of Fame. The institution displays their achievements while excluding them as achievers. It profits from their history while denying them recognition.

This is the Hall of Fame’s core hypocrisy. They want to be a museum when convenient and a church when expedient. Museums preserve all of history. Churches celebrate moral examples. You can’t be both.

**The Artifacts Without the Men**

Walk into the Hall of Fame and you’ll see Mark McGwire’s 70th home run bat from 1998. It’s in a display case celebrating one of baseball’s most memorable seasons. The caption describes the home run chase between McGwire and Sammy Sosa that captivated the nation and saved baseball after the 1994 strike.

What the caption doesn’t mention: McGwire later admitted to using steroids during that season. The achievement on display was chemically enhanced. The Hall knows this. They keep the bat and ball on display anyway. They charge people to see it. They market it in their promotional materials.

Barry Bonds’s record-breaking home run helmet is there too. Number 756, the ball that made him baseball’s all-time home run leader. The Hall acquired the ball itself in 2008 and displays it prominently. They profit from the artifact commemorating Bonds’s greatest achievement.

But there’s no plaque for Barry Bonds in the Hall of Fame gallery. No recognition. No honor. Just his helmet and the ball, artifacts from achievements the Hall simultaneously celebrates and condemns.

This makes no sense from a museum perspective. Museums don’t pick and choose which parts of history to acknowledge. They document everything and provide context. If the Hall of Fame is truly a museum, then the men who created those artifacts belong there with full context about the era they played in and the choices they made.

But the Hall of Fame isn’t acting like a museum. It’s acting like a church that wants to profit from sin while condemning sinners. Display the artifacts, collect admission, but deny recognition to the men who created them.

**The Selig Plaque That Says Everything**

While Barry Bonds remains excluded, Bud Selig was inducted into the Hall of Fame in 2017. Selig served as Commissioner of Baseball from 1992 to 2015. His tenure encompassed the entire steroid era. He admitted that drug testing was “not as high a priority as economic issues.” The Mitchell Report documented institutional failures that occurred on his watch.

Yet Selig is in the Hall of Fame. His plaque makes no mention of the steroid era. No context about the Mitchell Report. No acknowledgment of the twelve years between banning steroids and implementing testing. Just celebration of revenue growth and the wild card playoff format.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kBjt!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05aed8bb-7dfb-48d4-8ed1-3bb0efd668c0_1200x720.png)

The plaque credits Selig with presiding over revenue growth. But that growth came largely from the steroid era. The home run boom saved baseball after the 1994 strike. Attendance increased 44 percent from 1996 to 2001. Revenue soared. The plaque celebrates those results without mentioning how they were achieved or at what cost.

Selig’s plaque sanitizes his legacy. It takes credit for revenue growth without mentioning that some of that growth came from a steroid-fueled home run boom he enabled. It celebrates labor peace without mentioning that peace was purchased partly by not addressing drugs aggressively enough to disrupt negotiations.

That plaque tells you everything about how the Hall of Fame approaches the steroid era. Protect institutional leaders. Celebrate their accomplishments without context. Avoid uncomfortable truths. And use the character clause to exclude players for participating in the environment those leaders created.

**The Tony La Russa Exception**

Tony La Russa was inducted into the Hall of Fame in 2014. La Russa managed Mark McGwire in Oakland from 1986 to 1995 and in St. Louis in 1996. He managed Jose Canseco in Oakland from 1986 to 1992. Both players later admitted to or were credibly accused of steroid use during La Russa’s tenure. La Russa claimed he never suspected anything.

Either La Russa was willfully blind to steroid use by his star players, or he knew and did nothing. Neither reflects well on his character. If he truly didn’t notice McGwire and Canseco using steroids while managing them for years, that suggests incompetence or willful ignorance. If he knew and did nothing, that suggests enabling the behavior he now condemns.

Yet La Russa is in the Hall of Fame while the players he managed are excluded. The manager who either knew and did nothing or should have known faces no character scrutiny. Only players are held accountable.

**The Gaylord Perry Precedent**

Gaylord Perry is in the Baseball Hall of Fame. He was inducted in 1991. Perry admitted to throwing spitballs throughout his career in violation of rules that banned the practice. He wrote an autobiography titled “Me and the Spitter” detailing how he cheated. He was occasionally warned but rarely suspended. He made a joke of the rules.

Throwing spitballs gives pitchers an unfair advantage. It makes the ball move unpredictably, making it harder to hit. It’s against the rules for a reason. Perry broke those rules for 22 years and bragged about it in a book. He admitted cheating. He was proud of cheating. He profited from a book about cheating.

He was inducted into the Hall of Fame anyway because voters recognized that Perry’s rule violations were products of his era. Spitballs were widely tolerated. Enforcement was minimal. Many pitchers doctored balls. Perry just admitted to what others denied.

The standard applied to Gaylord Perry should apply to steroid era players. Rules were broken. Enforcement was minimal or nonexistent. Many players used. Some admitted to it. But voters decided that Perry’s era-specific rule violations didn’t disqualify him from the Hall.

Why is that standard not applied to Barry Bonds, who was never convicted of using steroids and whose only criminal conviction was overturned? Why not to Roger Clemens, who was tried for perjury and acquitted? Why not to Alex Rodriguez, who admitted to use but served his suspension?

Gaylord Perry sets the precedent: admitted rule violations in an era of minimal enforcement don’t disqualify players from the Hall of Fame. Yet that precedent is ignored for steroid era players. The selective application reveals the hypocrisy.

**The Segregation Era Free Pass**

Every player inducted into the Hall of Fame who retired before 1947 benefited from segregation. Babe Ruth never faced Satchel Paige. Ty Cobb never hit against Bullet Rogan. Lou Gehrig never competed against Josh Gibson. They played in a league that excluded the best Black players in America.

This was an enormous competitive advantage. The talent pool was artificially limited. White players competed against each other while the best Black players were barred from major league baseball. Records were set, achievements were accomplished, Hall of Fame careers were built against diluted competition.

We don’t hold this against pre-1947 players. We recognize they competed in their era’s environment. They didn’t create the segregation system. They benefited from it, but we don’t exclude them from the Hall for that benefit.

The standard applied to segregation era players should apply to steroid era players. Neither group created the corrupt environment they competed in. Both groups benefited from or participated in era-specific advantages that we now recognize as wrong. Both groups should be evaluated for what they accomplished in their era’s context.

But only steroid era players are held accountable for era-specific advantages. Babe Ruth is celebrated despite benefiting from segregation. Barry Bonds is excluded for allegedly using steroids in an era when testing was minimal and enforcement was nonexistent.

The inconsistency is indefensible. Either era-specific advantages disqualify players across all eras, or they don’t disqualify anyone. Selective enforcement based on which era’s corruption is more socially acceptable reveals moral cowardice rather than moral principle.

**The Amphetamine Era Nobody Discusses**

From the 1950s through 2005, amphetamines were ubiquitous in major league clubhouses. They were called greenies. They were mixed into coffee pots for communal consumption. Trainers distributed them. Players took them before games to enhance alertness and combat fatigue.

Dan Wheat, a Rangers trainer, testified before Congress in 2002 that eight of nine players on the field used amphetamines. Hank Aaron admitted trying them. The practice was so widespread that it was considered normal rather than cheating.

Amphetamines are performance enhancing drugs. They increase alertness, reduce fatigue, and improve reaction time. They’re banned by MLB’s current drug policy and classified as controlled substances under federal law. Their use was illegal without a prescription.

Yet nobody is excluded from the Hall of Fame for amphetamine use. Hank Aaron is there. Willie Mays is there. Mike Schmidt is there. Every player who played from 1950 to 2005 likely either used amphetamines or played alongside teammates who did.

The amphetamine era was longer than the steroid era and probably involved more players. But it gets no scrutiny in Hall of Fame voting. Players from that era are evaluated on their statistics and accomplishments without character questions about drug use.

Why? Because addressing the amphetamine era would implicate beloved legends. It would require excluding Hank Aaron or admitting that drug use is contextual. It would force uncomfortable questions about what performance enhancement means and whether era matters.

Easier to ignore amphetamines entirely and focus exclusively on steroids. That way the Hall can maintain its moral authority without examining too closely how that authority is applied.

**The Character Clause Farce**

The Hall of Fame voting guidelines state that voting shall be based on “the player’s record, playing ability, integrity, sportsmanship, character, and contributions to the team(s) on which the player played.”

Character matters. But what does character mean? Does it disqualify Ty Cobb, a violent racist who attacked fans and players? He’s in the Hall. Does it disqualify Cap Anson, who lobbied for segregation? He’s in the Hall. Does it disqualify Roberto Alomar, who spit in an umpire’s face? He’s in the Hall.

Orlando Cepeda was arrested for drug smuggling. He’s in the Hall. Ferguson Jenkins was arrested for drug possession. He’s in the Hall. Kirby Puckett was accused of domestic violence. He’s in the Hall. Paul Molitor admitted to cocaine use. He’s in the Hall.

All of these players are in the Hall of Fame. Their character flaws, criminal convictions, or admitted drug use didn’t disqualify them. Voters decided their on-field accomplishments outweighed their off-field problems or that era context mattered.

But Barry Bonds, never convicted of steroid use, is excluded. Roger Clemens, acquitted of perjury, is excluded. Alex Rodriguez, who served his suspension, will likely be excluded.

The character clause is applied selectively. It’s used to exclude steroid era players while giving everyone else a pass. This isn’t moral principle. This is institutional scapegoating.

**The Impossible Standard**

The Hall of Fame has created an impossible standard that exists nowhere else in baseball history. Players must have pristine character unless they’re pre-1990 players, then character doesn’t matter. No PED use unless they used amphetamines, then it’s ignored. No rule violations unless they admitted to spitballs, then it’s charming. No benefit from era-specific advantages unless it’s segregation, then it’s historical context.

This standard applies only to steroid era players. Everyone else gets evaluated on their accomplishments with era context. But Bonds, Clemens, Rodriguez, and others face moral scrutiny that no previous generation faced.

The standard is impossible because it’s designed to be. The Hall needed to demonstrate it takes steroids seriously. Excluding the most prominent suspected users accomplishes that goal. But the standard can’t be applied consistently because it would require excluding beloved legends or admitting that era context matters for everyone.
