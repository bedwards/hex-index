Capital, Vol. 1 (“The Process of Capitalist Production”) was published during Marx’s lifetime. His best friend and close intellectual collaborator Engels was left to do the best he could assembling and editing a workable book from the notebooks that Marx left for Vol. 2 (“The Process of Circulation of Capital”).

Vol. 1 starts in the sphere of circulation. The first three chapters cover general features of market exchange, and the next three narrow the focus to the kind of market exchange that only plays a central role under capitalism—the investment of money in commodities (crucially including the labor-power of the working class) in order to accumulate more money. In a striking passage at the end of Ch. 6, Marx writes that when you confine yourself to an analysis of buying and selling, capitalist markets can look like the “very Eden of the rights of man.”

But:

When we leave this sphere of simple circulation or the exchange of commodities, which provides the ‘free-trader vulgaris’ with his views, his concepts and the standard by which he judges the society of capital and wage-labour, a certain change takes place, or so it appears, in the physiognomy of our dramatis personae. He who was previously the money-owner now strides out in front as a capitalist; the possessor of labour-power follows as his worker. The one smirks self-importantly and is intent on business; the other is timid and holds back, like someone who has brought his own hide to market and now has nothing else to expect but – a tanning.

Everything that makes Capital not just a brilliant piece of economic analysis but a harrowing examination of social misery and a unique kind of literary masterpiece comes from this change of focus from the sphere of circulation to the sphere of production. In Vol. 2, though, Marx returns to the sphere of circulation and stays there.

In a letter to Pyotr Lavrov in February 1884, Engels discusses the many difficulties of working through Marx’s manuscripts and preparing them for publication, and looks ahead to publishing Volumes 2 and 3 when they’re done.

The 2nd book is purely scientific, dealing solely with questions as from bourgeois to bourgeois, but the 3rd will contain passages which make me doubt the very possibility of their being published in Germany under the Anti-Socialist Law.

The same feature of Vol. 2 that made Engels unconcerned about getting it past the censor makes even many enthusiastic twenty-first century readers of Vol. 1 far less eager to dive into Vol. 2. Much of it is very technical, and it feels “purely scientific” in a way that makes it far less obviously relevant to socialist politics.

An interesting exception, though, is Marx’s discussion of the distinction between the “productive” and “unproductive” portions of the working class. I erred on the side of pithiness (and alliteration) for the title of this essay, but really, there are two distinct issues here. First, what part of the proletariat is “productive” in Marx’s sense? Second, is whichever part is “unproductive” separated from the rest of the working class in the sense that it has fundamentally different interests?

The introductions to the Penguin editions of all three volumes of Capital are written by the great Belgian Marxist writer Ernest Mandel (probably the most significant figure in Trotskyism after the death of Leon Trotsky). Mandel is a good writer and often an insightful commentator, and I’d count his introductions among the many virtues of the Penguin editions.

In his introduction to Vol. 2, Mandel touches on both of the issues just mentioned. In my view, he gets the first question wrong, giving us a view that’s implausible whether we take it (as Mandel wants us to) as an interpretation of what Marx thinks or if we leave Marx out of it and take it as Mandel’s own contribution to analyzing capitalism. His comments on the interests question, though, are very good.

It’s worth starting out back in Vol. 1, to remind ourselves of what the “productive”/”unproductive” distinction is supposed to be in the first place. One of the most interesting chapters of Vol. 1 is Ch. 7 (“The Labour Process and the Valorization Process”). It’s made up of two sections, one each on the two halves of that title. These are two different senses in which human activity can be “productive.”

7.1 is about production in general, under any form of social organization. Every society has to find some way of feeding itself, clothing itself, sheltering itself, and so on, regardless of which economic system it uses to organize these activities. Whether we’re talking about hunter-gatherer bands or capitalist corporations or medieval serfs or groups of workers collectively owning their own means of production in a socialist future, any society is going to have people engaged in “purposeful activity” to transform the world around us so as to meet “the requirements of man.”

7.2 turns to a far more historically specific form of production—”valorization,” or economic value expanding through investment in wage labor.

Our capitalist has two objectives: in the first place, he wants to produce a use-value which has exchange-value, i.e. an article destined to be sold, a commodity; and secondly he wants to produce a commodity greater in value than the sum of the values of the commodities used to produce it, namely the means of production and the labour-power he purchased with his good money on the open market. His aim is to produce not only a use-value, but a commodity; not only use-value, but value; and not just value, but also surplus-value.

Most confusion about Marx’s talk of a distinction between “productive” and “unproductive” workers comes from the assumption that he’s talking about the 7.1 sense of “productive.” When he broaches the distinction, though, he’s at pains to remind us that he’s talking about productivity in the 7.2 sense.

So, for example, in her book Capital’s Grave, Jodi Dean connects Marx’s comments on household servants being “unproductive” with the rise of the “service sector” in the contemporary economy in a way that only makes sense if you assume that Marx thought servants were “unproductive” because of the nature of the tasks they were performing. I objected to that in my review of Capital’s Grave, pointing out that Marx says in Theories of Surplus-Value that a cook in private homes is “unproductive” but a cook in a hotel is “productive” because the second cook is doing work that generates the funds the hotel-owner uses to pay him. (You can read Dean’s response to my review here, and my response to her response here.)

In Vol. 1 Ch. 16, Marx writes that the “concept of productive labour” relevant to capitalism “becomes narrower” than the one “derived from the nature of material production itself.” This leads him to one of his most important comments on the “productive”/“unproductive” distinction:

Capitalist production is not merely the production of commodities, it is, by its very essence, the production of surplus-value. The worker produces not for himself, but for capital. It is no longer sufficient, therefore, for him simply to produce. He must produce surplus-value. The only worker who is productive is one who produces surplus-value for the capitalist, or in other words contributes towards the self-valorization of capital. If we may take an example from outside the sphere of material production, a schoolmaster is a productive worker when, in addition to belabouring the heads of his pupils, he works himself into the ground to enrich the owner of the school. That the latter has laid out his capital in a teaching factory, instead of a sausage factory, makes no difference to the relation. The concept of a productive worker therefore implies not merely a relation between the activity of work and its useful effect, between the worker and the product of his work, but also a specifically social relation of production, a relation with a historical origin which stamps the worker as capital’s direct means of valorization. To be a productive worker is therefore not a piece of luck, but a misfortune.

Pretty clearly, Marx’s distinction has nothing to do with whether the commodities any group of workers are producing are physical “goods” or immaterial “services.” Oddly enough, though, Ernest Mandel disagrees. He has to grant that Marx had that position in Vol. 1. But, he argues that Marx changed his mind by the time he wrote the notebooks that became Vol. 2.

The beginning of Mandel’s discussion gets the most important point exactly right:

A preliminary distinction which we need to draw goes to the heart of the matter. When Marx classifies certain forms of labour as productive and others as unproductive, he is not passing moral judgement or employing criteria of social (or human) usefulness. Nor does he even present this classification as an objective or a-historical one. The object of his analysis is the capitalist mode of production, and he simply determines what is productive and what is unproductive for the functioning, the rationale of that system, and that system alone. In terms of social usefulness or need, a doctor provides labour which is indispensable for the survival of any human society. His labour is thus eminently useful. Nevertheless, it is unproductive labour from the point of view of the production or expansion of capital.1 By contrast, the production of dum-dum bullets, hard drugs or pornographic magazines is useless and harmful to the overall interests of human society. But as such commodities find ready customers, the surplus-value embodied in them is realized, and capital is reproduced and expanded. The labour expended on them is thus productive labour.

But, things start to go off the rails when Mandel broaches “the question of so-called ‘immaterial goods’: concerts, circus acts, prostitution, teaching, etc.”2 He writes:

In Theories of Surplus-Value, Marx tends to classify these as commodities, in so far as they are produced by wage-earners for capitalist entrepreneurs. Although in Volume 2 he does not explicitly contradict this, he insists strongly and repeatedly on the correlation between use-values embodied in commodities through a labour process which acts upon and transforms nature, and the production of value and surplus-value. Moreover, he provides a general formula which implies the exclusion of wage-labour engaged in ‘personal service industries’ from the realm of productive labour: ‘If we have a function which, although in and for itself unproductive, is nevertheless a necessary moment of reproduction, then when this is transformed, through the division of labour, from the secondary activity of many into the exclusive activity of a few, into their special business, this does not change the character of the function itself.’ If this is true of commercial travelers or book-keepers, it obviously applies all the more to teachers or cleaning services.

The definition of productive labour as commodity-producing labour, combining concrete and abstract labour (i.e. combining creation of use-values and production of exchange-values) logically excludes ‘non-material goods’ from the sphere of value production.

The problem with the first argument is that Marx saying that activities that are in themselves unproductive don’t become productive when some people specialize in them, or even when someone has to do them for production to happen, only “obviously” excludes teachers and cleaning services if we start from the assumption that teaching and cleaning are “in themselves” unproductive jobs.

The problem with the second argument is that the definition of productive labor as commodity-producing labor “combining creation of use-values and production of exchange-values” only “logically excludes” teaching and cleaning if we assume that the knowledge imparted by teaching and the clean homes and offices provided by cleaning services aren’t “use-values.” But, why should we assume that? In the beginning of Vol. 1 Ch. 1, Marx defines “use-values” in terms of the fulfillment of any human need, whether the need in question arises “from the stomach or the imagination.”

Mandel supplements these more abstract arguments for classifying the teachers and prostitutes and circus clowns as unproductive by directly looking at how Marx actually applies the productive/unproductive distinction in Vol. 2

He writes that the way Marx draws the distinction

becomes evident when Marx sets forth in Capital Volume 2 his reasons for classifying the transport industry in the realm of the production of value and surplus-value, rather than in that of circulation. The argument is clearly summarized in the following passage: ‘The quantity of products is not increased by their transport. The change in their natural properties that may be effected by transport is also, certain exceptions apart, not an intended useful effect, but rather an unavoidable evil. But the use-value of things is only realized in their consumption, and their consumption may make a change of location necessary, and thus, in addition, the additional production process of the transport industry. The productive capital invested in this industry thus adds value to the products transported.’

Now it is obvious that none of these arguments is applicable to the carrying of persons. Passenger transport is not an indispensable condition of the realization of use-values and adds no new value to any commodity. It is rather a personal service on which individuals (whether capitalists or workers) spend their own revenue. Thus, whether it is organized on the basis of wage-labour or not, the passenger transport industry can no more be considered as increasing the total mass of social value and surplus-value than can wage-labour employed in the fields of commerce, banking or insurance.

But, a particular argument Marx using about the transportation of goods not applying to the transformation of people doesn’t in itself tell us anything about what Marx thinks about the transportation of people! If we want to know that, we don’t have to infer it from what he says about the transportation of goods. We can just look at what he says about the transportation of people elsewhere in Vol. 2.

Several chapters earlier than the passage Mandel is talking about, we see Marx raise the people-transporting issue directly, and the whole thrust of his discussion of it is to dismiss the relevance of goods vs. services distinction. Riffing on an observation by Russian economist Alexander Chuprov, Marx writes:

The result in each case, whether it is people or commodities that are transported, is a change in their spatial location, e.g. that the yarn finds itself in India instead of in England, where it was produced.

But what the transport industry sells is the actual change of place itself. The useful effect produced is inseparably connected with the transport process, i.e. the production process specific to the transport industry. People and commodities travel together with the means of transport, and this journeying, the spatial movement of the means of transport, is precisely the production process accomplished by the transport industry. The useful effect can only be consumed during the production process; it does not exist as a thing of use distinct from this process, a thing which functions as an article of commerce and circulates as a commodity only after its production. However the exchange-value of this useful effect is still determined, like that of any other commodity, by the value of the elements of production used up in it (labour-power and means of production), plus the surplus-value created by the surplus labour of the workers occupied in the transport industry. In respect of its consumption, too, this useful effect behaves just like other commodities. If it is consumed individually, then its value vanishes with its consumption; if it is consumed productively, so that it is itself a stage of production of the commodity that finds itself transported, then its value is carried over to the commodity as an addition to it.

That seems pretty decisive on the “what Marx’s position is in Vol. 2” issue. But, I also said that Mandel’s position would be implausible if we dropped the question about Marx-interpretation and just considered it as Mandel’s own unique contribution to the analysis of capitalism. So, let me briefly see if I can cash that check before we go on to the part that Mandel gets right.

If we’re trying to figure out whether to define “productive” in a way that excludes service workers, it’s worth starting with a more basic question:

What’s the point of the distinction? Why should we care about whether transportation and other “service” workers are part of the “productive” section of the working class?

There are a few reasons you might think the distinction is important. For example, maybe you’re a big believer in the falling rate of profit. You think it’s inevitable that capitalists will be incentivized to increase the proportion of capital invested in machinery and decrease the portion invested in "variable capital” (i.e. in hiring workers whose labor produces capitalists’ profits) and equally inevitable that this will cause average profit rates to go down over time.

If you’re interested in tracing and documenting this tendency, then you’ll need to look at how much capital is being invested in the wages of productive workers. Unproductive workers aren’t going to be relevant to your calculations. (If a wealthy capitalist spends the same amount of money on a bigger yacht or more cocaine that he would have otherwise spent hiring more household servants, this won’t have any direct impact one way or the other on the profitability of his businesses.) But, money invested in hiring workers who brew coffee or operate trains is definitely going to be relevant to the falling rate of profit (if they work for capitalists who sell these services in order to turn a profit).

A different reason to care about the productive/unproductive distinction is that you might think it’s relevant for socialist strategy. Workers who are “productive” in the 7.2 sense can exert more leverage by going on strike, for example, since their doing so directly threatens the ability of their capitalists to rake in surplus-value. If the capitalist’s maid and butler withdraw their labor, that would be very annoying for him, since he’ll now have to do things for himself he’d prefer that other people did for him, but it wouldn’t impact his bottom line. If the workers at the car factory he owns walk off the job, he’s in real trouble.

But equally, if he owns a chain of coffee shops or a private train service, he’s in real trouble if the workers who produce the “services” his company sells go on strike. Here, too, it’s unclear why it would make a difference whether the commodities being produced are “goods” or “services.”

There’s a great lecture by Erik Olin Wright called “Understanding Class,” where Wright starts out by talking about an old poster that says that “class consciousness is knowing which side of the fence you’re on” and “class analysis is about knowing who’s there with you.”3 Running with this framing, a third reason you might care about drawing the distinction between “productive” and “unproductive” workers is if your class analysis tells you that the unproductive workers are on the other side of the fence.

In his introduction, Mandel forcefully rejects this idea, and argues for a “broad definition of the proletariat, which includes the mass of unproductive wage-earners (not only commercial clerks and lower government employees, but domestic servants as well), and which considers productive workers in industry as the proletarian vanguard only in the broadest sense of the word.” He backs this up with the authority of a list of previous theorists (Marx! Lenin! Plekhanov! Kautsky! Rosa Luxemburg!) but he also acknowledges a “powerful reason” that some critics have offered to think that all of these people got it wrong.

If only productive labour produces value and thereby reproduces the equivalent of its own wages (besides creating surplus-value), does this not imply that the wages of unproductive labour are paid out of surplus-value produced by productive labour? And in that case, does there not arise a major conflict of interests between productive and unproductive labour, the first seeking to reduce surplus-value to a minimum, the second wishing it to be increased? How can such a basic conflict of interest be reconciled with the inclusion of both sectors in the same social class? Furthermore, should the industrial workers not be opposed to any expansion of state expenditure, even in the realm of ‘social services’, since this is financed in the last analysis through an increase in surplus-value extracted from them?

This would be quite a reversal of the more common view that one of the central aims of socialist politics is to expand the decommodified sector of the economy as much as at all possible. But, Mandel has several sharp responses.

Even if, like me, you disagree with Mandel about private-sector service workers, it’s hard to argue that public employees who operate social services that no one is making a profit off of are “productive” in the 7.2 sense. So, we can bracket the service workers and just focus on the public employees. Are their basic interests opposed to those of the productive working class?

So as not to make things too easy for Mandel, let’s go ahead and assume that the “Modern Monetary Theory” (MMT) crowd is wrong and taxes do indeed fund government services. In fact, whatever you think of MMT, that’s going to be true for at least some government services. (Neither the state of California nor the city of Los Angeles prints their own currency, for example, so even the most ardent MMT-er might have to acknowledge that the services provided by these levels of government are funded by taxation.) Does it follow that money spent on social services is surplus-value extracted from the working class?

One of the points Mandel makes in response to this suggestion is that “the concept of ‘gross wages’ (i.e. wages before tax) has no meaning in Marxist economic theory.”

You might be inclined to slam the brakes right there. What the hell does he mean it has “no meaning in Marxist economic theory”? What you’re paid before vs. after the IRS takes its cut is a pretty straightforward distinction. If Marxist economic theory denies that, you might think, so much the worse for the theory.

But, Mandel explains what he means:

Wages are means of reconstituting the worker’s labour-power through the purchase of commodities and services.

Remember, the jumping-off point for Marx’s analysis of exploitation in Vol. 1 is that the commodity that workers are selling in the labor market is their capacity to work (their “labor-power”). If that commodity is being purchased at its full value, that means the capitalists will pay high enough wages for workers to buy the goods and services they need to keep themselves going—in Marx’s terminology, to “reproduce themselves”—at a given historically determined level of subsistence. (It takes fewer hours for workers to produce the value of that package of goods and services than there are hours in a working day, which is where exploitation comes in.) So, one way of seeing Mandel’s point would be to say that the crucial issue is workers’ end-of-the-day capacity to pay whatever bills they have to pay to reproduce themselves at a given standard of subsistence.

Replacing America’s current system of private health insurance with a single-payer system of “Medicare for All,” for example, would mean increasing taxes and hiring a bunch of new public sector employees. But, it would also mean that workers would no longer have to pay premiums, co-pays and deductibles to private insurance companies. If these savings are greater than the increased tax burden, so that productive workers end up with more disposable income, then in the sense that’s most relevant for a Marxist analysis of the social role of wages, net wages have increased.

Mandel argues that

it would be absurd to construe state medical, educational or transport services which help reconstitute the worker’s labour-power (or maintain his family under normal living conditions) as derived from surplus-value; they represent rather a socialized portion of the wage, regardless of whether it passes through the form of ‘state revenue’, and regardless of whether it ‘originated’ in ‘gross wages’ (taxes paid by the worker), ‘gross profits’ (taxes paid by the capitalist), or the ‘gross income’ of independent middle classes.

In a footnote, he adds:

It has been objected that unemployment compensation can by no means be considered as the equivalent of the ‘price’ or ‘value’ of a commodity called ‘labour-power’, for by definition the unemployed do not sell their labour-power. However, this argument is based on a somewhat mechanistic reduction of the category ‘socialized wages‘. Nobody could assert that, if a worker places 10 per cent of his current wages in a chocolate box or a bank account in order to provide for the portion of his ‘active adult life’ during which he expects to be unemployed, that amount of money thereby ceases to be part of his wages. There is no fundamental difference between this and the situation where all workers use a collective chocolate box or bank account called the National Institute of Unemployment Insurance or National Institute of Social Security, and where the sums of money do not pass through the workers’ pay packets but are transferred directly from the capitalists’ accounts to these institutes.

Indeed, he argues, the considerable post-World War II growth of this socialized wage, far from “reflecting increased exploitation of productive labour or a sharp rise in the rate of exploitation," has actually “established a ceiling above which the rate of exploitation can hardly climb under ‘normal’ political circumstances (excluding, that is, fascist or fascist-type régimes).”

Warming to this point, he writes:

Moreover, the services provided by a significant sector of unproductive wage-labour have been a major factor in developing the needs and living conditions of the proletariat far beyond the purely physiological bedrock. The new minimum standard which has arisen is, at least in the imperialist countries (and in some of the most developed semi-colonial countries with a powerful labour movement, like Argentina), much higher than the one existing in Marx’s time.

Mandel was writing the introduction in the late 1970s, at the dawn of the neoliberal era, and he notes that this progress should “not be taken for granted or regarded as unassailable.” Significant attempts were already underway to roll some of it back.

Massive structural unemployment has reappeared, together with savage attacks in many ‘rich’ countries on the real wages of the working class, be they aimed at ‘direct’ or ‘socialized’ wages or at both. Correctly, the workers have reacted strongly against massive cuts in social state expenditure, thereby showing that their class instinct is clearer than the ‘science’ of those theoreticians who persist in calling all state expenditure ‘surplus-value’ (the logical consequence of which would be indifference to, or even approval of the cut-backs).

There are important ways you could complicate and nuance this analysis. Certainly, if you manage elect a socialist government to power, your comrades are now the bosses of the public sector work force, and there might sometimes be tensions to navigate in balancing the interests of that work force against the bigger picture of carrying out a platform that will benefit the working class as a whole.4

On the fundamental issue, though, I find Mandel absolutely convincing. In fact, his point is more salient now than ever, given that it sure looks like any plausible path to full socialism from where we’re starting in 2025 is going to have to be a lot slower and more social-democratic than anything Mandel and his Trotskyist comrades were hoping for when the Penguin edition of Capital Vol. 2 came out in 1978. If we’re going to build up working-class power going forward in ways that make more ambitious socialist goals achievable down the road, we need to start by pushing to massively increase that “socialized wage.” And Mandel is so good on that point it just about makes up for how wrong he gets the service workers question earlier in the introduction.
