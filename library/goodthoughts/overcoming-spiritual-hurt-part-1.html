To say that “letting go and moving on” has been a slow and difficult process would be a tremendous understatement. This past year and a half has been one of the most difficult periods of my life, and after over 20 years in vocational ministry, and coming on the back of Covid, that is saying a lot. It began in the summer of 2021 when I was informed that a group in the church I was pastoring was no longer happy with what was going on in the church. This was hard to discern at first, because the church was growing and things appeared to be going in a good direction even after all the difficulties of Covid, and all the concerns the pandemic had caused. And despite a good portion of the pre-Covid church members not attending indoor services (many had chosen to listen to services via the low-frequency FM radio transmission we had put in place so that those who desired to could meet in-person and those who were still apprehensive could listen from their cars or homes if they lived close enough to the church) things were feeling back to almost normal for those of us who had been meeting in-person enjoying corporate worship. So, to suddenly be presented with complaints of disgruntled members, was surprising to say the least.

From there things only continued to get worse, so in September I informed the leaders of the group who were upset that I would not be resigning but would welcome a church vote to decide the outcome of the situation. I was accused of asking for this because I had “set the deck” in my favor by bringing in all these new members, but in reality I wanted the church to vote, because I believe in local church authority and congregational rule, not elder, deacon, committee, or vigilante rule. Knowing, by this point, the level of anger that was being shown by those in opposition, I was not surprised on that Sunday evening when several people who had not attended church since the beginning of Covid, and many who had not even attended church the entire time I had been the pastor, showed up to participate in this vote. [Anecdotally, I was informed by someone who had been approached about coming to the meeting that they had not been a member of the church since the 1980s, and had since married a Presbyterian minister and was not even attending a Baptist church, and was still asked about showing up to help vote for my removal.] When the vote was counted I was removed by 1 (ONE) vote. However, before any vote had even been taken I knew I could no longer remain the pastor of this congregation, and had even made contact with a friend sitting nearby to say, “It’s time to go!”

There was some dispute about the vote, mostly because it was such a close margin. Plus, half the congregation knew that many had no right to even be included in such an important church decision. Yet, I stood up and thanked the church for allowing me to pastor them for the last four and a half years, and my family and I walked out of the building with our dignity and our witness intact. The pain and hurt soon began to set in. My wife broke down in the middle of the parking lot as we were walking home. My children could not understand how the people that had seemingly loved and supported us for the three years we had been there before Covid and all the chaos, could suddenly turn on us so quickly. Several of those that had supported us, their children, and others came to our house that night to show us love, it was almost overwhelming.

In their kindness, the church had voted to grant us three months salary and continued living arrangements in the parsonage. We were grateful for this gesture and praised God for his willingness to provide even in the storms of life. Yet, trying to get over pain of what we experienced could never be “healed” with a monetary gift. I had performed 30-40 funerals in just the four and a half years I had been there as pastor. I had stood over the caskets and graves of their loved ones. I had visited them in the hospital, prayed with them before surgeries, and attempted to faithfully preach God’s Word every Lord’s Day. My children had involved themselves in the food distribution ministry at the church. We had started to attract young families and others that recognized the importance of what we were doing as a body of believers. It made no sense what was happening.

I know I am not the only pastor that experienced similar pain and hurt during these times. Sadly, this type of thing happens all to often in church work. But all too often the “hardships” that people experience or talk about in church usually deal with the pain that church members experience, rarely is it talked about how pastors can be hurt by their churches. Church hurt or spiritual trauma is not good no matter the source or the victim. And one of the main reasons why I think pursuing healthy, biblical churches is so important, is to reduce the number of pastors and members that are hurt in a church context. However, the story of our spiritual abuse did not stop there. Next week, I will write about our experience as we tried to discern what God would have us to do next.
