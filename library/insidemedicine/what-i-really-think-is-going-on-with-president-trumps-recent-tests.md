### On the duality of Presidential health records.

My view on Presidential health is a bit counterintuitive. It goes like this:

1.  The public should be given extensive access; that is, much more information than we currently receive. While the President retains medical privacy, it should be implied that his health (or hers, someday) is a matter of national importance—and so, it should not be up to the White House doctor (who could easily be fired for saying an unpopular thing) to give the yes/no on the President’s fitness to serve, but little else. Currently, we are in an “in-between” space, where we receive a select summary of results via press release. As an electorate, I think we are “owed” more. In other words, if you agree to serve as President, you should also agree to opening your full medical records to us. Doctors like me can then agree or disagree with the stated summary from the President’s physicians.
    
2.  There are very few medical diagnoses that should preclude a President from serving. Many people wrongly believe that if the President were found to have cancer or serious heart disease, this would imply that they could no longer serve. That’s not only incorrect—it actually comes down to how severe the condition is, and how arduous the treatments are. I think there’s a tacit form of discrimination at work here. Many people seem to believe that certain medical diagnoses indicate weakness *and/or* that such conditions are a sign of age or disability. Well, that’s ageism and ableism, incarnate. People can function quite well with a variety of medical issues, provided they are under some degree of control.
    

So, I’m espousing what may sound like a contradiction. Tell us everything! But also, there are very few things Presidents and their doctors could tell us that would be, by themselves, disqualifying from serving.

Imagine a President with prostate cancer. Some prostate cancers warrant surveillance, not treatment. Some warrant chemotherapy and radiation for a brief period. But if the cancer spreads and other complications occur, *that* could compromise the President’s ability to serve. So, it’s not about the name of the condition, but its effect on the patient. The information we would need to determine a President’s fitness to serve is a synthesis of information from medical records and what we can see and hear ourselves every day. So, a President with prostate cancer could very well serve without any compromise in his performance. But if, after a period, he was increasingly missing in action, looking or sounding unwell, or routinely sleeping through meetings, we might piece together that his condition was deteriorating, and (depending on the diagnosis) was unlikely to improve. That would tell us more about the future—can we expect years of additional service or not?

The late Senator John McCain is a useful example. When he ran for President, he was a cancer survivor who showed no signs of any residual issues that would affect his performance—though the odds of a fatal recurrence were high enough that his pick for Vice President, Sarah Palin, was a real liability in my eyes. Even after his failed bid for the Oval Office, he served in the Senate for several more years, without incident. Until one day he started to sound [delirious](https://slate.com/technology/2017/06/speculating-over-a-politicians-cognitive-decline-is-irresponsible-using-data-to-assess-it-is-a-better-option.html). Within a short period, we learned that he had terminal cancer.

For the public to make informed judgments, we need access to Presidents’ detailed medical records, so that if and when things go awry, we know what we are dealing with.

That’s actually one good reason that our unusually long national Presidential campaigns are useful exercises. If you think about it, long campaigns are a kind of medical test, one that President Biden manifestly did not pass. Even if what we saw at that fateful debate was an unusual bad night, it’s reasonable for the public to demand that, as President, you can’t have *any* of those. My basic [conclusion](https://slate.com/technology/2024/07/president-biden-o-connor-medical-diagnosis-parkinsons.html) (in a *Slate* piece called “Biden’s Doctor Doesn’t Have the Answers”) was that President Biden was still fit to *be* President (based on a day-to-day assessment) but that he was apparently not quite fit to *run* for President without such an event occurring, which might have been an important indicator of how his condition might look one-to-four years later.

That brings us to…

### What do I think is really going on with Trump?

As I told *CNN*, and *[Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/trumps-mri-is-not-standard-preventive-care-say-experts/)*, there are two broad categories that explain why President Trump received “advanced imaging” this fall—tests which apparently turned out well.

##### **Possibility #1: Overuse/medical waste.**

The first explanation is overuse. That is, rich people pay for services, many of which are not recommended in guidelines, not because they are too expensive, but because they are pointless (as I explained in the *CNN* clips below). The President’s doctor said that these tests are “standard for an executive physical at President Trump’s age.” That’s simply false. Whether these were MRIs (which Trump told the media) or CT scans, doesn’t matter. “Advanced imaging” is [not routine](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2749861), even for “executive physicals,” regardless of whether the physician meant “Presidential” by invoking the term “executive.”

Also, as best anyone can tell (I asked some insiders), *no President* has received MRIs for preventive purposes. President Obama and President Trump had CT coronary scans as “prevention” screening, but both of them had risks for heart disease that made the tests warranted—Obama because of his smoking history, and Trump because of his cholesterol and obesity. In fact, I believe the last President to get an MRI for any reason was President Clinton, who received one after a [fall](https://clintonwhitehouse6.archives.gov/1997/03/1997-03-14-press-conference-on-clinton-knee-injury.html), which showed a torn quadriceps tendon. But that MRI was for diagnosis of a known injury, not screening to find asymptomatic disease.

Because there is no such thing as using CTs or MRI for routine screening of otherwise healthy adults of Trump’s age, if the President truly has no new symptoms (or had no unusual findings on previous tests), the advanced imaging his doctor ordered would fall into the category of [medical overuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VIP_medicine)—that is, a waste of taxpayer dollars, while possibly adding more risk than benefit to the President.

**Possibility #2: They are investigating something real, but probably not serious.**

Now, if the President had *any* new symptoms related to the chest, abdomen, or pelvis, advanced imaging *might* be warranted. That could be anything from new swelling to decreased exercise tolerance (in the case of the chest) to unexplained weight loss (or other symptoms that could lead to testing related to the organs of the abdomen). But this would not be testing for prevention or screening. It would be for diagnosis, based on symptoms or previous findings from other tests. (By the way, the President does look like he might have lost a few pounds, but I’m not certain on that.)

Given that the President had new symptoms this summer which indeed led to a diagnosis of [venous insufficiency](https://www.cnn.com/2025/07/17/politics/trump-leg-swelling-chronic-venous-insufficiency), it’s possible that the “advanced imaging” tests he received this fall were follow-up tests. We were told that he had a battery of tests this summer, the results of which ruled out conditions like heart failure as the cause of his new leg swelling. But sometimes (often, even), during testing like the President received this summer, doctors notice some “incidental findings”—that is, small abnormalities that likely amount to nothing, but should be monitored or re-examined in the future just to make sure. That’s not “screening,” but it’s also a lot less worrisome than many of the other typical reasons that such tests might be ordered.

My guess is that this is what happened, either during President Trump’s annual physical in April, or during the tests he received this summer that lead to his venous insufficiency diagnosis. If so, the “advanced imaging” studies he received this fall were *indeed* indicated, based on those findings or symptoms.

### We should normalize aging.

So, why not just admit that? Two reasons come to mind. The first is that the President, like many, has vanity around these types of issues. But, as above, because he’s the President, I think he should get over that. Second, the *public* may overinterpret the meaning of some of these “incidental findings.” A test that found, for example, a mass on the kidney, might lead to unwarranted conspiracy theories that the President is hiding a cancer diagnosis. But even if that were true, that information alone would not be adequate to prevent him from continuing to serve. Many cancers warrant nothing more than surveillance. Others need brief treatment. Others are far worse. The point, again, is not the diagnosis. It’s disease severity.

One “benefit” of radical transparency in Presidential health would be to normalize some things that currently carry shame or the appearance of weakness or frailty. Okay, so a 79-year-old has a mass in his abdomen that turns out to be a clinically meaningless form of cancer at present, but *could* become a problem in 5-15 years? So what? He’s human, like the rest of us. Such a diagnosis would not inhibit him from being President. If we are going to elect older people to be President, we should expect them to, well, be old. Aging is normal. But it’s a process.

### What I think may have happened here. *Speculation alert*!

Triangulating—that is, my speculation based on what we know, and the timing—I believe that something in the President’s recent condition or earlier tests warranted further studies, even if the likelihood was that there was nothing serious going on. If we take Trump’s doctor at his word, those tests didn’t show anything *of concern*.

Now, I should note that if the tests *did* find anything unusual, his doctor carefully wordsmithed his way out of having to tell us. Note that the doctor wrote that “all major organs appear very healthy,” and “everything evaluated is functioning within normal limits.” But what about *minor* organs which, by the way, can cause problems? And just how much work is “everything evaluated” doing? Perhaps the President’s doctor instructed the radiologist *not* to evaluate *everything* in those scans. That sounds bad, I know, but there’s legitimate debate on how much detail these study reports should contain; that is, the better our scanning technology gets, the more details radiologists can see—and the deeper in the weeds the radiologists can go. Imagine a scan being ordered to look for appendicitis. Our technology is so good that a radiologist could end up spending time characterizing a tiny (but ultimately benign) abnormality in the liver. This kind of thing probably leads to “medical misadventure” more often than it reveals any clinically important information or diagnoses.

All told, my guess is that *something* in President Trump’s recent medical history warranted “advanced imaging.” But either they found nothing, or what they did find is of no clinical importance. For whatever reason, be it a desire to maintain some modicum of privacy or because the public apparently can’t be trusted to interpret medical complexities, the President and his doctors presented the results from these tests as “perfectly normal.”

*The reality is that nothing about this is normal. But that does not mean that something major is being covered up here.*
