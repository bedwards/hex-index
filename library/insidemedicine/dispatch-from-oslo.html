Hei fra Oslo. That’s “Hello, from Oslo,” according to Google Translate.

Yesterday, I was honored to speak at the Norwegian Institute of Public Health (their CDC), at a symposium entitled “Science, Trust, and Democracy: Communicating Health Challenges in a Polarized Era.”

This was an excellent event, organized by my hosts Ulf Reidar Dahle and Fernanda Cristina Petersen. It bridged two adjacent worlds—antimicrobial resistance efforts in Norway and beyond (antibiotic resistance being the topic most non-experts are familiar with) and public scientific communication.

This will be a postcard, in two sections. First, some reflections from the symposium. Second, some travel notes.

The symposium.

My talk:

I’d like to share a highlight or two from each of the other talks. But first, I’d like to tell you about my presentation, because it was an unusual one for me. The title of my talk was “Healthcare Under Fire: Navigating Institutional and Public Pressures in a Divided America” had been suggested by the organizer, Ulf Dahle. I had mostly prepared my 45-minute lecture in advance (leaving the final preparation of the slides until the last minute, naturally), but in all honesty, I hadn’t asked an important question of Ulf: Who will be in the audience and what do they need to hear from me? Most of the audience, he said, would be public health experts (inside and outside of government) who were worried that what is happening in America right now could someday happen in Norway. As one of the other speakers said to me, “When America sneezes, Europe gets a cold.” I’d never heard that one. It quickly became clear to me that our colleagues abroad are both worried for us—they are indeed watching our public health news cycles closely, and with grave concern—and for themselves, because they now believe that the same fate could someday befall them. In other words, our tragedy is their wake-up call to start preparing for the worst. They understand that they are not immune to the forces of misinformation or anti-science currents.

So, I basically tweaked my presentation to become a kind of field manual: What should public health professionals and scientists in Europe do to prepare for a situation like the one we are now facing in the US? I described how public health officials and researchers have been reacting to the various attacks on our system over the last nine months. I noted how many HHS officials have been quietly trying to help academic researchers get their grants funded by making subtle changes to their applications. I described how many have been bravely speaking out and providing information to writers like me and other journalists. I also highlighted that some of the legal actions (against censorship and sudden grant freezes) have been successful, and others that, so far, have not (stopping Secretary Kennedy’s assault on vaccines, via the CDC’s Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices). I also described how important it is for government officials and researchers to build trusted relationships with journalists now, rather than during a crisis (though it’s certainly possible to do so). I also advised that they assess their own laws now, and write explicit policies today that clarify what is permissible so that things like the sudden destruction of USAID do not happen here.

Of course, as always, I tried to start with a humorous slide…

Highlights from the other talks:

Speaker: Artem Horbachevskyi, head of antimicrobial resistance and infection control at the Ukrainian Department of Public Health.

Title: Beyond Compliance: Rebuilding Trust in Health Systems During Crisis.

Highlight: Artem described how they have been tracking antibiotic resistance patterns during the war in Ukraine. War wounds carry specific risks, but another problem is that wounded patients often get transferred to various facilities, making it more likely that resistance spreads within a healthcare system. Everyone who heard Artem speak was impressed and moved by the dedication and bravery that he and his team exemplify. He also shared some video footage from the war that was harrowing to say the least.

Speaker: Torkjell Leira, human geographer and (mainly), a writer.

Turning the Tide: Lessons from Democratic Resurgence in Brazil and Beyond.

Highlight: Torkjell gave the most unusual of all the talks and it was captivating. Torkjell is actually not a scientist, but has written about authoritarianism, including in Brazil, which narrowly avoided calamity when former President Bolsonaro was defeated in 2022 (and recently found guilty of having attempted a coup d’état). This talk was actually not about science at all, and yet I thought that Ulf’s and Fernanda’s choice to include it was essential and insightful for the symposium. We can talk about scientific communication all day—but we also need to know what it takes to defend freedom, including freedom of speech, and that politics and science are intertwined.

Side note #1: Torkjell managed to interview Bolsonaro in person and he regaled us with details of that during dinner after, including how he landed that interview!

Side note #2: More on Torkjell in the “travel notes” section. Preview: for some reason, I suspect that Torkjell is on the short list of most interesting/mysterious people in the world.

Speaker: Torunn Eilin Gjerustad, Senior Communication Advisor, leads social media and communication for climate research at NIPH.

Title: Harnessing Social Media: Maintaining Trust When the Comment Section Contains Misinformation, Disinformation, and Conspiracy Theories.

Highlight: This talk was about how government officials deal with the comment sections of their social media posts. Interesting and challenging! Censorship is bad, but so is harmful disinformation. The question is, how do public health agencies engage with the public after posting scientific information, rather than just “posting a praying”? She also talked about the five commenter archetypes, ranging from the supporters who will cheer on anything you post, to the haters who will stop at nothing to undermine your message. I found that to be relatable.

Speaker: Laura Alonso Irujo, Communication Specialist, European Joint Action on Antimicrobial Resistance and Healthcare-Associated Infections.

Title: GLOCAL* AMR Communication: Tailoring the Message to Cut Through the Noise.

Highlight: Often, the best message is the simplest and most memorable. Using the AIDS ribbon as a guide, Laura and her colleagues held a competition for a universal symbol for antimicrobial resistance awareness. The winning design is an elegant cross of two pills. You have to hand it to good designers.

*I never heard the term “glocal” before, but it’s a nice portmanteau of global and local.

Speaker: Edward Demicoli, Communications strategist at the European Commission. (His business card is 🔥 because that logo is pretty sweet, let’s be honest.)

Title: Behind the Scenes of ‘Beat the Bug’: Successes, Challenges, and Lessons Learned.

Highlight: The European Commission literally made a game on the Fortnite platform that gamifies antimicrobial resistance knowledge. (Like: You don’t need antibiotics every time you get a cold.) They then hired influencers to play the game in six target countries, which meant that thousands of young people watched them via livestream (on Twitch, for example). (In case you didn’t know this, young people actually watch other gamers play games via livestreams, and famous gamer/influencers make a lot of money doing it). I really respect something that is this outside the box.

Speaker: Mari Marte Press, senior communications advisor in the Communication and Public Relations department at the Norwegian Veterinary Institute.

Title: Bridging the Gap: Communicating the Intricacies of One Global Health in Animal and Human Disease Without Losing Trust.

Highlight: In Norway, they cull animals that have tested positive for MRSA (antibiotic-resistant staph infections). This is not done routinely in the US, and I’m not an expert on the topic. But it sounds like an analogous situation to bird flu in the US, where scientific experts have had to work with farmers to convince them to take infection control measures. The key to this is building trust between scientists and the farmers—which has often been cited as a problem in the US when dealing with bird flu and other pathogens.

Speaker: Christina Rolfheim-Bye, Communication Director, Norwegian Institute of Public Health.

Title: Crisis Communication in Action: Insights from the Pandemic Response.

Highlight: Get this: During the Covid-19 pandemic the Norwegian Institute of Public Health had hundreds of public messengers trained to do outreach, rather than just a few major surrogates. But even more impressive? The authorities acknowledged when not everyone agreed on a complicated topic or recommendation. They even hosted events where experts with opposing interpretations of the developing science spoke. This helped keep public trust by projecting that the agency was open to alternative ideas, even when they had to choose one policy over another. I’m not sure that would fly in the US, but I certainly wish that it would.
