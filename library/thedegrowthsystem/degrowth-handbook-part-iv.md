I know I said I would dedicate this article to discussing the limits to growth, but as I was writing it became obvious that I have to embed economic growth in its historical context, and in the spirit of keeping these articles short to accommodate our 21-century attention span, I shall split this topic in two.

Economic growth is a concept that will be familiar to you, in our imaginaries and collective common senses it’s understood as, first and foremost, a good thing. We can define economic growth as the increase in the total production of *goods* and *services* in an economy over a given period of time. Goods and services can be pretty much anything that is traded- if you clean someone’s car and they pay you (a service), if you dig gold out of the ground and sell it to someone (a good), and so on. The time period we usually use to measure economic growth is quarters or years.

The way we have decided to measure economic growth and economies more generally is through GDP (gross domestic product). GDP is a *monetary* measure of total goods and services, so if you sell gold to someone for currency, that’s counted towards GDP, if you volunteer at your local homeless shelter, or take care of your neighbour’s child, that’s not counted, even though they’re services. Note that GDP includes *every* monetary exchange, so things like cleaning up an oil spill or buying missiles to send to Iraq are all included.

Economic growth seems to be pretty much beyond criticism, if you ever listen to politicians or corporations setting out their programs, they’re likely to mention that x will be good for economic growth, and the sentence will finish there. Economic growth is seen as both an end in itself and a panacea, a sort of cure-all. They say that a growing economy is “growing the pie”, or that “the rising tide lifts all boats”, implying that everyone benefits from a growing economy as there’s more stuff to share, even if some people sometimes get more than others. Economic growth is depicted as beneficial for so many reasons- in theory, more growth means more wealth, which means more wealth you can tax, which means more you can spend on hospitals, more growth means more jobs, and so on.

These two concepts of GDP and economic growth seem eternal, they are so heavily embedded in our imaginaries that it seems hard to imagine a world where we aren’t dedicated to growth for growth’s sake. Yet these concepts are incredibly young, both being born in the mid-20th century. Matthias Schmelzer’s research on the historical origins of ‘the growth paradigm’ reveals that economic growth as an *explicit, premeditated* objective for society only emerged after the Second World War. The West has operated on the lines of *expansion* for some time, think empire, the industrial revolution- yet economic growth as a *core policy goal* only manifested in the 50s & 60s, with the first proper “growth targets” for a group of Western countries being set in 1961. As is weirdly the case with a lot of big decisions made by technocrats, this decision was bungled into- at a big OECD[1](#footnote-1) meeting in 1961 with all the big-wig technocrats of Europe and North America, there was a lot of pushback against the US’ demand for sweeping growth targets to challenge the USSR, even from the British treasury, who worried that focusing on growth too much would subordinate other goals[2](#footnote-2). The US decided the most direct route to getting what they wanted was to leak the proposed growth targets to the press before it’d been agreed, so everyone was locked in for fear of looking weak[3](#footnote-3). I think this is such an insane example of how unshakeable path dependencies that impact the lives of so many can emerge in such innocuous ways, but anyway.

Let’s quickly recognize what the point of all this was. It wasn’t just some blokes doing bureaucratic somersaults for fun, the point of setting growth targets was because we had decided that economic growth and material wealth are directly correlated to prosperity. Growth targets were commissioned by those in power in the 50s to combat two foes, namely, the domestic poor who if left impoverished would eventually revolt, and the foreign enemy, be it the Soviets or USA, depending on which side you were on, because if you grow faster than the other guy, you win the game.

Now, all this chat about economic growth required a common language, or more precisely, a common metric. In order to compare prosperity and deepen economic interdependence, the metric of GDP (GNP at the time but, details) was adopted as the lingua franca of national accounting[4](#footnote-4). Invented by Simon Kuznets, an American economist, GDP has come under extreme scrutiny, yet still seems pervasive.

The criticisms of GDP worryingly start with Kuznets [himself, who warned](https://www.promarket.org/2021/10/31/gdp-invention-economic-growth-kuznets-history/) of the danger of using GDP as a measurement of well-being as early as 1934. He warned that GDP does not measure socio-economic *welfare* but the production and consumption capacity of a nation. GDP notably excludes non-monetary goods and services, effectively stating that anything that cannot be measured in monetary terms is not valuable, the most heinous of exclusions being reproductive and care labour, performed mostly by women. GDP also includes what can be termed *negative externalities,* which means an undesirable indirect outcome of a certain action, pollution being the obvious example. Included in GDP is expenditure on oil spill cleanup, reconstruction after natural disasters, etc.

A new measurement of prosperity is needed, for it is obvious that more stuff does not equal more happiness, and too much stuff leads to ecosystem and social systemic collapse, which we will discuss in my next article on the limits to growth.
