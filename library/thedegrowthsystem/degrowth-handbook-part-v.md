For anyone who knows me, you’re probably bored of me repeating this statement, but this really is the kicker when it comes to degrowth, and it really does underpin most of what we talk about.

*You can’t have unlimited economic growth on a finite planet.*

There it is, that’s the thesis. I usually qualify this with- because if exponential (more and more year on year) economic growth uses stuff, and we have limited stuff, eventually we run out of stuff. I want to use today’s article to nail down why this is the case and what it means for how we organise our lives this century and beyond. I will go over the counter-arguments to what I’m about to say- so-called ‘green-growth’ and ‘techno(logical)-optimism’, in a future article, but in the meantime, approach today’s article with an open mind.

Up until the late 20th century, life in Western modernity never really came into contact with the notions of limits. Sure, there were limits on certain people’s freedom and certain people’s access to resources, but in general, the economy and possibilities were assumed limitless, just think about the obscene optimism of garage and trance music. Westerners were told our only limits were our own capabilities, and the oppressed were told by the Soviets that their only limits were their chains. There were, we could say, no *external* limits. That which was not human was at our disposal (and many humans too). I’m using the language of ‘Westerners’ and ‘Western modernity’ here because these people I’m about to sing the praises of weren’t the first to think of this stuff. Many indigenous worldviews understand and manifest a sustainable society-nature balance[1](#footnote-1) and have done so effectively for centuries.

These “*external”* limits to growth were first popularised in Western thought through the lens of the *ecological* limits to growth[2](#footnote-2). Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen was the first to basically say- hold on, all this stuff that we use in “the economy” comes from somewhere, and usually is used, and becomes waste, what does this imply? Georgescu-Roegen’s answer was to apply the law of entropy, a key ingredient of the second law of thermodynamics, to the economic process.

I will explain the law of entropy with an example. A lump of coal when found in the ground can be said to exist in a state of *low entropy*, it is available/has energy that we can use. It can also be said to be in a state of *order*. When we burn that lump of coal in a steam engine to power a train, the lump of coal moves from low entropy to *high entropy*, or a state of *disorder*\- used-upness. It's energy is used to heat the boiler, which provides pressure, which moves the piston which moves the wheel. The point here is that the lump of coal is no longer available to use. The energy has not been destroyed (it can’t be! shouts you from your classroom seat), but it has been taken *irreversibly* *from* that object. The same happens with renewable energy, the photons from the sun’s rays hit solar panels and are converted into electricity, but those photons have been used, in order to repeat the process we have to use photons from the next ray of sunshine. All of this is not exclusive to energy, everything eventually goes through this process, albeit at various speeds- think human bodies, metals in commodities, and food.

Thinking about economies and our consumption more broadly with entropy in mind has profound implications for what it really means to be *sustainable*. Georgescu-Roegen’s entropy hourglass gives a nice representation of this. Put simply, the ‘solar stock’ in the top of the hourglass is the total available renewable energy. The top of the hourglass is open as the sun keeps shining and the wind keeps blowing. Terrestrial stock basically means fossil fuels, the very scarce and limited dowry of cheap, efficient energy that our fossilized ancestors left behind. I’ve tried to write this paragraph more succinctly but the only way of me doing it is labouring through a few logical steps, but bear with me.

Our solar stock is ‘stock-abundant’, it will keep replenishing itself. Our terrestrial stock is stock-scarce, it will not come back for millions of years. Our solar stock is, however, ‘flow-limited’, there is a ceiling on how much we can use at any one time (represented by the constricted middle of the hourglass). By this, I mean that it is impossible or *extraordinarily* unlikely that we can produce energy with renewables to the same extent as we can with fossil fuels[3](#footnote-3) (controversial statement, debated in footnotes). The terrestrial stock is ‘flow-abundant’, since coal and its pals are unbelievably efficient, we can turn the taps on so much fuller than we can solar stock, yet the fact terrestrial stock is limited and flow-abundant is precisely the reason we find ourselves in our predicament today.

The point of all this is to say that we’ve built a world that is dependent on a certain amount of stuff flowing through it in order to maintain itself. That stuff is limited, we’ve used and are using more than we can possibly sustain. What degrowth argues, really, is that in the long run, we have *no choice but to degrow*, because it will happen anyway. We cannot keep our current levels of production up without fossil fuels, the metals needed to build mega-infrastructure, and the concrete to keep up the sprawl, there simply isn’t the supply of resources to do so. So degrowth asks the question- how do we degrow in a way that is equitable, fair, and actually improves wellbeing and the flourishing of life? A question I will answer another time.

So I’ve laid out what is commonly referred to as the ‘ecological limits-to-growth’ argument. Learning this completely changed my life and the way I look at the world, and I hope it might change yours too. One key takeaway from all this is to understand “the economy” by a simple maxim. The economy is not a discrete, independent system that operates on its own logic, rather, the economy is a *subsystem* of the biosphere (the Earth). Below is the classic, very simplified diagram you get in a run-of-the-mill economics textbook that shows what “the economy” is. The contents aren’t so important, but what is important is that this diagram completely excludes the source of the physical stuff necessary to produce goods and services, and also excludes the waste that is produced by an economy.

*Ecological economics 101*

The implications of viewing the economy like this are massive. It completely blows our traditional understanding of the economy out of the water. The implication is, simply, that there are limits to how big our economy can be. This yellow sphere of the human economy is expanding, and the rate at which we can recycle resources is limited (fundamental building blocks of modern society just cannot be recycled- oil, concrete, chemical pesticides). The implication of all this, and the degrowth thesis is thus- humanity can only use the energy available in solar stocks, plus what we can recycle from our raw material inputs.

So how much can we use? This is up for debate, it’s significantly less than current levels of course. The discussion around this is outside the scope of this article but a crude summary is that average global energy use needs to be reduced by an order of 3[4](#footnote-4), and that’s the average across countries, in places like Australia they’d need to reduce by an order of 10, the UK 6. Energy isn’t everything either, resource use (measured in material footprint) would need to be reduced by an order of 3 in the UK[5](#footnote-5), but this is all content for a separate article.

The scale of the downscaling can seem daunting, yet the framework of degrowth is not that of drudgery and austerity, rather, it is framed in emancipatory terms. A downscaling of production and consumption need not be the end of human flourishing, quite the opposite, for a balanced society-nature synthesis supports flourishing for all, not just the wealthy and privileged, but every human and every non-human. We have an opportunity to reconcile with nature, and the window is still ajar.

It can be argued that thinkers like John Stuart Mill with his steady-state thesis and Malthus’ population trap predated Georgescu-Roegen.
