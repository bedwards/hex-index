The guys I study with have joined me on the novel experiment in human relationships that’s emerged recently- the virtual relationship preceding the ‘real’ one. The same 40 odd people studied together every weekday, exploring degrowth. I could paint their Zoom rectangle, describe their pets’ movement patterns, most of them are characters in my dreams.

The weekend just gone, those from Europe met for the first time in Barcelona, the city where the Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona, the university that runs our course, is based. It was a trip, one of the most beautiful and radicalizing experiences of my life, totally worth the fact I’m still here locked in a room with a mysterious illness getting my delicate ears shagged by the fireworks of San Juan. None of you reading know my classmates, so an ode to them would be silly, and I’ve already ode’d them to their faces. An ode to our backdrop is better.

Barcelona’s strong history of radical leftist politics goes well beyond that of most European cities. David Graeber once said Rojava is the “most exciting political experiment since the anarchists of the Spanish Civil War”, and he’s right, not since the streets of Barcelona and the hills of Andalucia in the 30s has the world witnessed a society as free from hierarchy and inequality and so rich in human cooperation and organization. By “the anarchists” Graeber is referring to one of the most important factions of the republicans (which included more moderate social democrats and more crazy Stalinists) who fought and lost against Franco in the Spanish Civil War of the late 1930s. Anarchism, by the way, is a political and social framework that seeks to dismantle, as much as possible, hierarchies that cannot be justified, advocating for a voluntary association of people based on participation in the decisions that affect them, and local, self-managed economies of solidarity.

Anarchism spread across Spain in the early 20th century, but its most complete expression was in the streets of Barcelona following the outbreak of the civil war. Orwell described the scenes thus in Homage to Catalonia;

“It was the first time that I had ever been in a town where the working class was in the saddle. Practically every building of any size had been seized by the workers and was draped with red flags and with the red and black flag of the Anarchists; every wall was scrawled with the hammer and sickle and with the initials of the revolutionary parties; almost every church had been gutted and its images burnt. Every shop and cafe had an inscription saying that it had been collectivized; even the bootblacks had been collectivized and their boxes painted red and black.

Waiters and shop-walkers looked you in the face and treated you as an equal. Servile and even ceremonial forms of speech had temporarily disappeared. Nobody said 'Señor' or 'Don' or even 'Usted'; everyone called everyone else 'Comrade' or 'Thou', and said 'Salud!' instead of 'Buenos días'. Tipping had been forbidden by law since the time of Primo de Rivera; almost my first experience was receiving a lecture from a hotel manager for trying to tip a lift-boy. There were no private motor-cars, they had all been commandeered, and the trams and taxis and much of the other transport were painted red and black.

The revolutionary posters were everywhere, flaming from the walls in clean reds and blues that made the few remaining advertisements look like daubs of mud. Down the Ramblas, the wide central artery of the town where crowds of people streamed constantly to and from, the loud-speakers were bellowing revolutionary songs all day and far into the night. And it was the aspect of the crowds that was the queerest thing of all. In outward appearance it was a town in which the wealthy classes had practically ceased to exist. Except for a small number of women and foreigners there were no 'well-dressed' people at all. Practically everyone wore rough working-class clothes, or blue overalls or some variant of militia uniform. All this was queer and moving.

There was much in this that I did not understand, in some ways I did not even like it, but I recognized it immediately as a state of affairs worth fighting for...so far as one could judge the people were contented and hopeful. There was no unemployment, and the price of living was still extremely low; you saw very few conspicuously destitute people, and no beggars except the gypsies. Above all, there was a belief in the revolution and the future, a feeling of having suddenly emerged into an era of equality and freedom. Human beings were trying to behave as human beings and not as cogs in the capitalist machine.

The anarchists of Spain weren’t a fringe group or some outlier like the Capitol Hill Autonomous Zone of 2020, rather, they controlled most economic and social life in Catalonia. Three-quarters of all industry in Catalonia was under worker’s control1, more specifically, they were under the control of the unions. The unions are essential to the expression of the form of anarchism practiced in Catalonia, what can be termed anarcho-syndicalism. Anarcho-syndicalism generally seeks to gain control of the economy through a collection of industrial trade unions (syndicates). In the context of 1930s Catalonia, this meant taking control of the myriad of industries in Spain’s industrial heartland (Catalonia hosted 70% of Spanish industry2) and proceeding to remove profit-seeking in industry and distribute the stuff they produced according to need rather than people’s ability to pay. Barcelona was going through a cost-of-living crisis in the early 30s. When worker’s control over the economy emerged, the price of goods dropped by 75%… 3 Put simply, people lived well, in difficult conditions, fighting a war, without a state ruling them, the people ruled themselves.

Anarchism in Barcelona and Spain more broadly began to be stamped out after a few years. Stalinists and co-opted social democrats heavily funded and supported by the USSR took control of the unions and industry and one of humanity’s most intriguing experiments was relegated to despotism. Yet the spirit of the revolution still walks the streets. You still see offices of the CNT (Confederación Nacional del Trabajo - National Confederation of Labour - the anarchist umbrella group for unions going back to 1910) lining the streets. Sindicat Habitatge Raval can be seen as a continuation of the self-organizing, localist principles embodied nearly a century ago. It is a housing syndicate led mostly by women that looks at what the government says it’s going to do, realizes its not doing it, and does it for them (direct action).

Article 47 of the Spanish constitution says all are entitled to decent and adequate housing. Those at the syndicate’s assembly were unable to afford rent and therefore decent and adequate housing, so are forced to squat or be made homeless by eviction. The syndicate offers legal advice and if/when the cops come, the members show up to resist their eviction, morning, day, or night. At 2.30 in the morning we watched the cops try to evict a family with a one-year-old child. After enough time, the residents in question may be offered social rent by the government (a rent placed at an affordable rate below what the private landlord would ask for on the market) and have their occupancy formalised. This formalisation process, however, requires long-term occupancy of the place they’re trying to stay, and one family can’t resist an eviction, but fifty can.

Barcelona’s outskirts host multiple historical squatting communities, set up less out of necessity, rather, as a space for experimenting and practicing an alternative to capitalist modernity. Can Masdeu is one of these communities, an old lepper colony, abandoned since the 50s, it was occupied by international activists in 2001, and hosts between 15-30 adults and children at any one time. It also has many links to the degrowth research community in Barcelona. On the outskirts, overlooking the city, it is understood as a “rurban” space, reaping the benefits of rural access to space, but maintaining an active and engaged relationship with the city, not acting as an “exit utopia” but rather to make visible that an alternative political, economic and social life is possible. One resident said you can theorise and intellectualise all you like, but to practice and exhibit utopia, by doing, fosters an alternative you and the masses can see.

In theory, no hierarchies persist in social relations in Can Masdeu, decision-making is done by consensus (everyone has to agree, a majority vote is not sufficient). Each individual is expected to perform 15 hours of work a week (with exception given to recent mothers and fathers) and contribute 100ish euros a month for miscellaneous costs such as energy and upkeep. Some residents still work in the city, some build an economy at home, producing organic beer, herbal remedies and hosting a communal public lunch every Sunday. It acts as a social centre for workshops on climate justice, organic agriculture and political resistance.

Can Masdeu’s “rurban” setting finds its most effective feature in its community garden setup. The picture above shows barely any of the squat’s massive community gardens, it hosts around 60 plots, most of which are given to those who don’t live in Can Masdeu itself. The plots are offered to local residents to build a network of relations between the squatters and the community. Can Masdeu offers a free space to grow food, a real-terms increase in people’s well-being and ability to live an autonomous life, and the local residents are more likely to support the squatters in case of eviction. “Nowtopias” are often lambasted as selfish, privileged groups of crusties who run to the woods and don’t bother trying to change society, but prefer to change themselves. Can Masdeu and these, let’s say, “engaged” utopias disrupt this narrative.

Yet Claudio, a resident of Can Masdeu and our Master’s convenor warned us against romanticising experiments like Can Masdeu. Conflict is rife and a lot is left to be desired. Disagreements about the sharing of workloads and forms of work between men and women persist, the emergence of leaders, debates around the legalization of the squat and more day to day disagreements pervade. Most squats and alternative communities fail not because of scarcity of resources, rather, it is conflict between those who live there and how it should be run that break a community apart. In many ways though, as the veil of perfection and harmony was lifted on Can Masdeu, I began to see it as a difficult, yet intensely human venture. A life where conflict is not ignored and the tribulations of living together are expressed and encountered publicly, rather than relegated to the private.

Whilst the practice of self-organization and left-wing radicalism4 in Barcelona is less pervasive than the 1930s, power still rests in these movements. The mayor of Barcelona herself is very familiar with them. Ada Colau, elected in 2015, set up the Plataforma de Afectados por la Hipoteca (PAH) (Platform for People Affected by Mortgages) which has a similar mission to Sindicat Habitatge Raval, resisting evictions and campaigning for housing rights5. PAH was set up to resist the wave of evictions caused by unpaid mortgage loans in the wake of the 2008 crash. What I’m trying to get across is that the current mayor of Barcelona comes from a tradition of municipality-based direct action in the face of the inadequacy of the state.

She then formed the now co-governing party of Barcelona- Barcelona en Comù (Barcelona in common in Catalan) in 2014. Presented as a ‘citizens platform’, Barcelona en Comù’s objectives are summed up in their very cool code of ethics, which I encourage you to read, but in a nutshell, they are advocates of democratic autonomy. The conventional model of electing a politician once every five years and let them get on with it is dumped in favour of a more participatory model. Citizens are encouraged to be more directly involved in real-event decision-making and are offered more concrete means of scrutinising their politicians. Politicians must publish their diaries, not hold any other paid employment and only take a salary of 2,200 euros a month, amongst other things.

Another party in Catalonia was the first major party in the world to endorse a degrowth platform last year, the CUP (Candidatura d'Unitat Popular or Popular Unitary Candidacy) state in their manifesto;

“Promoting the degrowth of the material sphere of the economy, that is, the reduction of the consumption of matter and energy in absolute terms and especially that of the ruling classes, for an effective redistribution of wealth”

These forays into a new kind of politics haven’t smashed capitalism in Barcelona. Their strained relationship with tourism persists, along with poverty and homelessness. A participatory left-wing party is still capable of making regressive policy, just this week bike rickshaws in Barcelona, popular with tourists, are set to be banned out of “safety concerns” and the fact they’re not regulated in a veiled attack on an accessible source of income for migrants.

The Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona, which hosts the cherubs who run our Master’s, is probably the most important and active centers of research on degrowth and political ecology in the world. Degrowth demigod Giorgos Kallis described the building as a “living system”, which sounded like bullshit at first but it’s not, the design is described here. To see snails 6 on office doors felt like coming home.

Whenever I visit places with a great political consciousness and appetite for liberatory politics, my first reaction is “I want to move here”. To have come out of a highly conservative, atomized part of rural England and find community in the radical hives of Europe leaves me grateful. Yet, it reminds me of one of the benefits of internationalism and networks- to visit people practicing the values you share and cherish is to learn and be able to better implement your values where your roots lie. It’s all well and good visiting, learning and maybe living with those expressing radicalism in its most intense forms, but if a transition to a better society is to materialize, values of solidarity, sharing, ecology, and equity must reach those places that lie at the opposite pole. To take these values into the world is the project of degrowth and its allied movements, from the cottages of Buckinghamshire to the streets of Barcelona, from the meeting halls to the corridors of power…
