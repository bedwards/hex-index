It’s hard to put into words just how far away we are from a degrowth-informed housing in my village. Cheddington is a village of 2,000 odd in ‘leafy Buckinghamshire’, Orwell would’ve adored its “green and pleasent-ness”, occasionally inspiring utopic moods on radiant afternoons. Yet it’s character hardly inspires a big re-jig of society, there’s no more poignant image of this than the ridiculous housing estates springing up all over rural Britain.

A man from Galicia once told me that you have to look to your local ancestors for inspiration and transgenerational solidarity when it comes to thinking about how we can build a better society. He looks to the Galician anarchist partisans from the 30s, I look out my window and see Mentmore Towers, one of many old home bases for the Rothschild family. I see colonial wealth stuffing the land in the countless manor homes dotted around, I see the PM’s countryside gaff, I see the wealth extracted from the big corps of the capital stuffed into the property of the villages, but I digress. 

Here we’re discussing housing not the Rothschilds, but the key players in my village’s housing story are really the shit sequel to the Rothschild empire. Charles Church, “a British upmarket housebuilding company”, has recently built a 100-home estate in the village. It is a subsidiary of Persimmon PLC, a FTSE 100 company with the flattering accolade of having the UK’s “most generous bonus scheme in history”, giving out £800m in bonuses to 150 of its biggest wigs in 2016 and an undoubtedly deserved £128m bonus to the Lucifer Morningstar, the CEO. It goes without saying, but Persimmon’s properties have regularly turned out to be crap, crap enough to warrant a Channel 4 Dispatch documentary, which is as good as being branded with “we’re up to something well shady”.  

Charles Church shares the local market with other benevolent enterprises, namely Taylor Wimpey, who were one of the 2% of respondents to reject a government consultation proposing reducing emissions of new-builds by 80% by 2025, and W.E. Black, who decided rather than waiting for planning permission to bulldoze a historic orchard behind our house, would just set it all on fire and bulldoze it in one morning before the council arrive and take the fine, very nice. 

Passing through Cheddington, it doesn’t exude emotive symbolism of political struggles in the way you’d get walking down the road to Wigan Pier, yet Cheddington lies on the frontline of the UK’s longstanding housing crisis and exhibits many archetypal qualities of the crisis. The UK’s housing crisis has a long history that is gracefully outlined by Nick Bano’s writing, but there are basically two competing narratives about the crisis. One is the supply deficit narrative; the problem is that there are just not enough houses to go around, and we’ve got to build more, and the most efficient way to do that is to get Charles Church to build nuketown-esque estates en masse, which is a curious arrangement when you find out that 20% of donations to the Tory party come from the property sector. Yet this is the line towed by most major political parties, and even the Liberal Democrats, who are winning seats in Buckinghamshire off the Conservatives precisely on this issue, despite advocating for more local control over housing, actually advocate for more housebuilding than the Conservatives. The problem with this narrative is that the deficit isn’t in supply, it's in affordability and access, which is the second narrative, I.e. there’s enough houses but they’re prohibitively expensive and household sizes are getting smaller. I won’t labor the point, but I wish to elucidate the problems here- a gulf between earnings and house price, corporate control over housing, crap housing, and too much of it.

To avoid pies in the sky, it’s important for those who think in utopian terms to embed their strategy in currently existing realities. I don’t want to mouth off in this article about what an idealized degrowth-informed approach to housing would look like, I’m interested in this first, tentative step towards a better world. So, I look around and what do I see? I see general disgruntlement at these new housing developments, people don’t like them. People are worried about the local schools being overwhelmed and the aesthetic obliteration of the village. We have a village plan, which lasts until 2033, written up by the parish council, it’s a fairly participatory process that covers new and existing housing amongst other items concerning the future development of the village. Our village plan outlines a “consensus” that the village should make land available for up to 100 new homes (likely due to village plans having no choice but to accommodate demands for new housing), with an emphasis on smaller, 2–3-bedroom, affordable dwellings for first-time buyers and the elderly, bungalows really. The Charles Church estate comprises 100 houses, all of which are relatively large 3-5 bedroom properties, with an average price of around £500-600k (all of whos new inhabitants, I should note, I have no gripe at all with). Considering the average salary in Buckinghamshire is £44k, it’s crass to suggest these are affordable, and with more developers nipping at the heels of a large plot of historic apple orchards, the village plan is just holding back the tide.  

This is funnily enough a conflict about growth. Large, profit-oriented companies, backed up by their allies in government are teaming up to fabricate a supply-side housing crising, wherein which expensive housing is being sold on credit to people who can’t afford them to keep capital flowing in the face of declining economic growth rates.  Whilst this might not be the lens underpinning the discussions in parish council meetings, it is, at its root, a conflict about what are the possibilities for our community with respect to certain limits. This conflict is probably the most unifying object for our community, and potential future successes in preventing further development would undoubtedly empower our belief in our collective capacity to enact change. In a place that’s somewhat of a political desert, being the Speaker of the House’s seat for so long, a Conservative fortress, and a rather good case of Reynold’s “little boxes”, its collective action like this that builds collective meaning and belief in collective agency.  

Cheddington is so far off becoming Rajneeshpuram (although when the Maharishi Foundation briefly set up in Mentmore Towers and ran the Natural Law party from there for a few years, we came closer than expected) or a community of collective living, nuclear family dissolving, car-free utopians, but that’s not the point at all. Degrowth as a process is destined to be gradual, organic, operating within the interstices of the system, and most often not in the explicit name of degrowth, but gently massaging collective and individual wishes of autonomy, care and the flourishing of human and non-human possibilities within limits, that I believe lie within all of us.
