It’s one of those Saturday mornings when seemingly everything I read is endlessly fascinating. Melissa Kirsch’s always-outstanding *NYTimes* [Saturday newsletter](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/09/28/briefing/at-capacity.html?unlocked_article_code=1.PE4.vEyR.496o4dBVXkKi&smid=url-share) sends me down a rabbit hole: memory and aging. She asks:

> If we can’t remember the things we’ve read and watched and even loved, do they still “count”? Does the hard drive of our mind get so full it changes the very experience of taking in news, culture, the stories of our days?

She brings up James Collins (who is *he*?—must find out). She links to [an essay he wrote on the subject](https://www.nytimes.com/2010/09/19/books/review/Collins-t.html?smid=url-share).

> *When we read a serious book,* Collins writes*, we want to learn something, we want it to change us, and it hardly seems possible for that to happen if its fugitive content passes through us like light through glass.*
> 
> *Now, with a terrible sense of foreboding, I slowly turn to look again at my bookshelf. There they all are, “Perjury” and “Kavalier & Clay” and those other books that I have read and of which I remember so little. And I have to ask myself, Would it have made no difference if I had never read any of them? Could I just as well have spent my time watching golf?*

And now I’ve got to read his novel, *[Beginner’s Greek](https://www.amazon.com/Beginners-Greek-Novel-James-Collins/dp/0316021563/)*.

Kirsch sends me further into memory issues, [a clarification on aging and memory](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/01/10/opinion/sunday/age-memory.html?unlocked_article_code=1.PE4.4Nsr.3pZ3jDO4fOvk&smid=url-share) (we’ve got it wrong, the author says), and another on the [normalcy of forgetfulness](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/09/08/well/mind/brain-function-memory-dementia.html?unlocked_article_code=1.PE4.ouBj.2CiAAkovpv9a&smid=url-share).

When I hit the point of being able to [hurkle-durkle](https://www.scotsman.com/news/scotland/hurkle-durkle-scots-word-meaning-origin-4491313) no longer (hunger gets me out of bed), I go to the kitchen. I listen to [The Writer’s Almanac](https://writersalmanac.publicradio.org/index.php%3Fdate=2013%252F09%252F26.html?utm_source=substack&utm_medium=email), while baking (yes, baking) bacon and scrambling eggs and putting a second pot of coffee on. This sends me down into a Sondheim hole (25 years old when he wrote *West Side Story* \[such behind-the-scenes-drama!\], bested by *The Music Man* at the Tonys in 1958). Even Wirecutter, *The Times*’s version of consumer reports, fascinates. What *are* the best slippers, I want to know? [Glerups](https://www.llbean.com/llb/shop/116620?qs=3080290_7m8EnekPF5E-1rcn9Er2yPj8V8bIQK3Faw&cvosrc=affiliate.linkshare.7m8EnekPF5E), Wirecutter says. (Ann already owns them, natch). What *are* [the best gifts under $100](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/gifts/best-gifts-under-100/)? Gift listed number 2 on the list: best womens’ vibrator. Why is a travel backpack number 1? A toolbox number 3?

I check email and find ’s Substack and her thoughts on food writing and how it has come into its own. (I made a note of Ruth’s words to address with the class I teach in food writing for NYU grad students—it will be so helpful.)

This was last Saturday, the one I’m describing. I don’t know if I simply had a great night’s sleep or if there happened to be an uncommon glut of great stories.

It was a day when, for no reason, the world seemed a delight and the tawdriness and tragedy around us during the week (Eric Adams, pet eating, the Middle East and Hurricane Helene) is a faint backdrop to the Thanksgiving feast of ideas available on my iPad. How I love the leisure of letting the mind go where it wants as opposed to where it *should* (Ann has asked for feedback on an essay on The Day of the Dead she’s working on, that I’m eager to get to it.)

But wait, one of my students brought up a new word, [solastalgia](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18027145/)—I must take a few minutes to explore this one. I’ve long been a lover of the word nostalgia. It’s a word that has devolved into meaning “a wistful fondness for the past.” But, in fact, the word is so much more powerful than that. It literally translates as “return pain.” True nostalgia is *pain*, the pain of not being able to return home, the futile search for lost time.

*Solastalgia* (“solace pain” [read its origins here](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solastalgia)) refers to the pain of being home when that home is no longer the same because of climate change (“Do you remember when we used to get so much snow?” I’ve often asked Cleveland boyhood friends).

The coining philosopher was Glen Albrecht:

> He describes it as "*the homesickness you have when you are still at home*" and your home environment is changing in ways you find distressing.

Perhaps a sense of the world’s endless fascination was set in motion by the aforementioned Kirsch and her newsletter. Her letters—short essays, actually—are easy, in no hurry whatsoever, on benign observations that she weaves into lovely cloth seemingly without effort, creating something artful and engaging when before there was nothing.

Here she is on the [pause between seasons](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/09/21/briefing/shoulder-season.html?unlocked_article_code=1.PE4.2BPk.-flUfOF5SgXo&smid=url-share), the [grief of being a lapsed tennis fan](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/09/07/briefing/the-us-open-concludes.html?unlocked_article_code=1.PE4.Kc8w.Cg1I26TJrSbm&smid=url-share), to name just a couple.

Enjoy your Saturdays and hurkle-durkle just a little bit longer.

The day after my hurkle-durkling, last Sunday, we headed back to NYC from Providence. After five trafficky hours on the road, we arrived at six, hungry and needing nourishment. “How about Orecchietti with Broccoli Rabe and Sausage?” Ann asked.

“Done,” I said. This is a favorite of both of ours and we almost always order it when we see it on a menu. Ann dropped her bags in the apartment and immediately headed out to D’Agostino while I did some cleaning and organizing (important when you live in 411 square feet with two cats). Minutes later I got a text:

> No broccoli rabe! I went to D’Ags and now I’m at Gourmet Garage. Neither place has it. \[angry face emoji\].

When she returned, she announced, “I’m doing it with broccoli instead. I prefer the bitterness of rabe, but this works, too. Mama Rose used to make it this way.”

Ann had dinner on the table 20 minutes later. So creamy and fulfilling. The achy feeling of having spent cramped hours in the car melted away.

So herewith, a fabulous weekday or last-minute Sunday night meal.

**Orecchiette with Broccoli and Italian Sausage**

-   Olive oil as needed (a few tablespoons to coat pan)
    
-   2 to 4 cloves garlic, smashed with the side of a knife an coarsely chopped
    
-   1 pound sweet or spicy Italian sausage (or a mix of both)
    
-   2 pounds broccoli, cut into florettes and 1/2-inch discs of the tender stem
    
-   1 pound orecchiette
    
-   1 cup of the pasta water
    
-   Grated parmiginano reggiano
    
-   Crushed red pepper to taste
    

1.  Put the pasta on to boil.
    
2.  Sauté the garlic in olive oil till softened, a minute or so. Add the sausage and cook it till its no longer pink.
    
3.  Add the broccoli to the sausage. Add 1 cup of the water from the pasta pot to the sausage and broccoli. Stir and cover to cook the broccoli, about 7 to 10 minutes. You want the broccoli and stems to be completely tender, not al dente.
    
4.  Cook the pasta.
    
5.  Drain the pasta. Return it to the pot, add the sausage and broccoli, and toss.
    
6.  Here’s the key. Add one cup of grated cheese, and stir it into the pasta so that it becomes creamy and coats the pasta when it’s melted into the liquid from the broccoli-sausage mixture.

### Speaking of Cocktails …

Now that Chartreuse is reliably back on shelves, I will happily discuss again a cocktail called The Last Word.

I can actually make a case that this is the best cocktail ever conceived. My cocktail of choice is a steely martini. In the depths of winter, nothing satisfies quite like a Manhattan. The aromatics in the Negroni make it one of our all-time favorites.

But one sip of a Last Word, this sweet and sour gin cocktail that includes two liqueurs and you reflexively hold it away from yourself so that you can regard it more clearly. “How can this pale yellowish elixir contain so much *flavor*?” you ask yourself. It’s a marvel—a wonder your mouth can contain all that citrusy, herbal deliciousness.

And we might have lost it were it not for a Seattle bartender, Murray Stenson ([RIP](https://seattle.eater.com/2023/9/26/23891249/rip-murray-stenson-seattle-bartender-the-last-word-zig-zag-cafe)), who discovered the recipe in a bawdy 1952 book called Bottoms Up!, which describes its origins in 1916 in Detroit.

The ratio is clean, equal parts of all four ingredients. It’s such an interesting cocktail I don’t think it needs a garnish, though a brandied cherry, or even a lime wheel, goes well if you want to garnish it.

**The Last Word**

-   3⁄4  ounce gin
    
-   3⁄4  ounce green Chartreuse
    
-   3⁄4  ounce maraschino liqueur
    
-   3⁄4  ounce lime juice
    
-   1 brandied cherry or lime wheel (optional)
