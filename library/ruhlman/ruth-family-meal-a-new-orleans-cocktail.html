I will explain the above in a bit, but first, an ode of sorts to Ruth Reichl, a woman who neither needs nor desires any further odes.

As regular readers know, I teach a course on food journalism in NYU’s graduate program, which not only allows me to share my favorite food writing and pontificate on issues that matter to me deeply, it also allows me to bring in fabulous guests who I would otherwise not see or be able to converse with (Pete Wells, Michael Pollan, Ligaya Mishan, Ted Conover—all A-listers). This year, because she is spending more time in the city and because she is a friend Ann and I have dinner with whenever we can, Ruth Reichl agreed to head down to Cooper Square and the Arthur L. Carter Institute of Journalism to join our class.

I have an especially talented and engaged class this year, but they are often told gloomy stories about the journalism world today—magazines and newspapers are dying, editors don’t reply to queries, online publications don’t pay.

Ruth would be the perfect counterpoint and inspiration as these 20-somethings move out into the shifting media world. There’s never been a better time, a more necessary time, a more important time to be a food journalist, she believes. More than 30 years ago, she gave a Lanner Turner Lecture on the subject. Last year, on the day after the presidential election, Ruth wrote in her excellent newsletter, La Briffe, the following:

But today the speech is a reminder (if we need it), that food touches everything we do. And that it is definitely a political issue. Every election analyst has pointed to rising food costs as a major concern for voters. Yet despite the evolution of food writing over the last thirty years, consumers still have very little understanding of the forces that drive those costs.

That needs to change. Most of the readers of La Briffe are aware that our food system is badly broken. If we have any hope of fixing it we need to know how the food system actually operates.

Thirty-three years ago I was convinced that food journalists were absolutely necessary.

It has never been more true.

What better words, what better person to address a classroom of aspiring writers?

I assigned Ruth’s memoir, Save Me the Plums, one of several she’s written.

I urge you too to read the 2019 book, or listen to it on audio (Ruth reads).

It tells the story of a woman, the most powerful and respected restaurant critic in the country, who takes over a venerable but cobwebby magazine filled with fluff stories and recipes for affluent middle-aged white ladies and transformed it into a magazine that brought food journalism into the present, then pushed it further.

It was the David Foster Wallace essay Consider the Lobster that did it. A 10,000-word whopper on the Maine lobster festival that became an ethical inquiry into the morality of killing live creatures because we want to eat them. It’s by turns a hilarious travel story, scientific inquiry into the biology of the lobster, and an extraordinary philosophical inquiry.

Ruth had to fight to get her publisher to sign off on it, as she did so many stories that opened up the critical facets of life in America anchored by food. Readers were crazy about it, and its success gave her the license and courage to cover subjects like the slave labor in Florida that produces our grocery-store tomatoes and an exposé on the actual practices of salmon farming (Barry Estabrook), stories far from the elite escapism the magazine had offered before.

When she went to her boss at The Times, executive editor Joe Lelyveld, to tell him she would be leaving her post as restaurant critic to edit Gourmet, he was all but gleeful she was not going to a prestigious Condé Nast mag (VF, The NYer).

Ruth asked, “Joe, do you read Gourmet?” He sniffed dismissively and replied, “I don’t have time for food magazines.”

“That’s exactly why I took the job,” she replied. “I plan to make a magazine you’ll have to have time for.”

He wished her luck.

She was good to her word.

Final Ruth love. She was in the upper echelons of Condé Nast with a regular driver, a clothing allowance, expenses galore, but she never lost her drive to report stories, and she never gave up her core, formed in her Greenwich Village childhood and her years as a Berkeley hippie in the 1960s. She’s still that Berkeley hippie as her miraculous, crazy hair embodies. Thank you, Ruth.

Family meal …

I often give a writing prompt Ann gave to me: “Family meal. You have 12 minutes.”

It never fails to bring out the details of a writer’s young life, and if there’s a chef in the group, a story about staff meal.

Thanks to my Michigan born cousins—my Mom is from Detroit—a real family meal happened last weekend. And it made me wonder, what does family mean, where we’re so spread out and scarcely see each other?

Cousin Melissa and her husband Chris live in Santa Fe; cousin Britta and Tom in Charlotte; cousin Jeni and husband Fred in Baltimore; Cousin Matt lives in Brooklyn so we see each other all the time (which, in NYC, means once a year, maybe, if we’re lucky).

Happily, Matt, an eager cook and a lover of champagne, offered to host the dinner at his home in Brooklyn.

Here’s what we cooked:

Matt brined and hot smoked (200˚F) to rare a side of salmon on his big red egg on his balcony for an app, then fired it up to 450˚F for the showpiece: a 15-pound, spatchcocked turkey. Jeni made an extraordinary squash gratin from Deborah Madison, the chef and writer whose books on vegetarian cooking are classics (see recipe below). The secret to the gratin—“we’d call it a casserole in the Midwest,” Jeni said wryly—is caramelizing a whole skillet-full of sliced onions first.

I prepped a mountain of shaved Brussels sprouts to sauté (to be seasoned at the end with evo and red wine vinegar (no bacon—Britta can’t eat that kind of protein because of that crazy Lone Star tick!). I cut the potatoes to toss with olive oil and rosemary and roast. Chris cut orange supremes for one of two salads, minced five huge shallots and picked tarragon for the butter baste and sauce for the turkey. And Matt made a pound cake for a Baked Alaska dessert. No one had made this last one before, and Matt winged it from various internet preparations.

The best part, the BEST part, of the day was naturally all the cousins cooking together in Matt’s ample kitchen, music playing, Matt’s young daughters adding some young life to the gathering, Matt keeping everyone’s champagne glass full as we prepped and chatted and caught up and cooked.

As an only child, far from my hometown, and spending so much time now with Ann’s crazy and wonderful family, it felt uncommonly warm and good to be with my own family, something I found I miss terribly. It was above all, love, I suppose. And all of it anchored by great food and the shared work of its preparation.

Of course, brunch at our place was called for the following morning …

A recap of the cousins’ fun weekend in New York—a splendid dinner out on their arrival, skating at Bryant Park, a trip to MoMa, pints of Guinness downtown—over bowls of hot pozole rojo, with avocado, radishes, cilantro, lime, and a poached egg on top (and some braised, shredded pork for the meat eaters).

A perfect conclusion to a December visit to NYC. Thank you, cousins, I love you.

What we’re watching …

We finally saw Hamnet, starring Jesse Buckley and Paul Mescal. We were eager to see it because we loved Maggie O’Farrell’s book of the same name, about the life of Shakespeare’s wife, Anne, and the loss of their child. Luminous. Shakespeare is a remote figure in the book.

The movie focuses on the love story between Shakespeare, very much in the foreground here. If you haven’t read the book and see the movie, I imagine you will think it fine, but for some ridiculous Shakespeare quoting by Shakespeare at unfortunate moments. (He recites a huge chunk the To Be or Not To Be soliloquy while standing on the banks of the Thames, contemplating suicide from the grief of his child, for instance.) I thought it was OK. But having read the book, I was disappointed. Midway through the movie Ann leaned over to me and whispered, “I hate this movie.“

It is, I should add, on just about every critic’s top picks for the best picture Oscar this year. Which I hope will go to Sinners or One Battle After Another.

We returned from The Angelika that late afternoon, made some tortellini en brodo using a chicken carcass roasted earlier in the week and watched an absolutely delightful movie: Wake Up Dead Man: A Knives Out Mystery. Daniel Craig is back as Benoit Blanc, investigating a mysterious murder of a controversial priest. It’s told mainly from the point of view of a younger priest, the excellent (and ubiquitous) Josh O’Connor and numerous other stars (such as Andrew Scott) in supporting roles. A hoot, highly recommend. Streaming on Netflix.

And now … a word from The Promiscuous Reader …

His Bloody Project by Graeme Macrae Burnet takes place in a rural farming town in Scotland in 1869, and we know from the start who the killer is—seventeen-year-old Roderick Macrae, who brutally murders three neighbors. The story is presented through police documents, villagers’ statements, and Roddy’s own memoirs. It’s totally mesmerizing. Somehow, Burnet makes us deeply empathetic toward Roddy, despite the horrific crime. If you like psychological thrillers, this is definitely for you.

Lucky me! My beloved gave me Ian McEwan’s new novel What We Can Know for my birthday and I’m hooked. A lonely scholar in 2119 is searching for a poem written in 2014 by a famous poet for his wife presented at a dinner party on her birthday. This is a literary detective story of sorts as Metcalfe finds clues and uncovers secrets and betrayals. An “all I want to do is stay home and read this” book.

What we’re drinking …

“We should serve this on Christmas Eve,” Ann said, taking a sip of the Bourbon Milk Punch. It was a splendid way to begin the day, and gave us the perfect base in our bellies for a variety of oysters at Acme Oyster House, which makes one of the best oyster po’ boys in the city, as I’ve written about before.

But with the creaminess and the nutmeg, it’s a celebratory libation and will work great in Ann’s punch bowl with a big block of frozen milk serving as the ice cube.

The Carousel Bar’s Bourbon Milk Punch

  • 2 oz bourbon (or Napoleon brandy)

  • 4 oz half-and-half (or a mix of whole milk and cream for a richer texture)

  • 1 oz simple syrup

  • ¼ oz vanilla extract

  • Freshly grated nutmeg, for garnish

Put all the ingredients but the nutmeg in a shaker with ice and shake hard to froth. Strain into a rocks glass over ice. Garnish with freshly grated nutmeg.

Note: The bar offers this drink with brandy instead of bourbon, but we prefer the latter.
