### Now, for those potatoes …

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!agKM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F00950027-0545-4ecf-96a0-52746d8098c8.heic)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!agKM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F00950027-0545-4ecf-96a0-52746d8098c8.heic)

Last summer, in the chilly, dark aftermath of Hurricane Erin, Ann’s son Sam requested her “cheesy potatoes.” They were so damn good I asked her to make them again last month. And they were, again, so … damn … *good*, I had to write a proper recipe for them to share.

Ann’s been making them for years, based on a long-ago recipe from Patricia Wells. They are stunningly simple: simmer sliced potatoes and garlic in milk, layer them in a skillet with heavy cream and grated gruyere and bake. That’s it. Absolutely the best.

**Cheesy potatoes, the best potatoes gratin I’ve had:**

-   1-1/2 pounds russet potatoes, peeled, sliced 1/8th-inch thick
    
-   1-1/2 cups milk
    
-   1-1/2 cups water
    
-   3 fat cloves garlic
    
-   8 ounces gruyere
    
-   1 cup heavy cream
    
-   salt and pepper to taste
    

1.  Heat your oven to 375˚F.
    
2.  Combine the potatoes, milk, water, and 2 cloves of garlic in a pot. Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce heat and simmer until the potatoes are tender but still firm, about ten minutes.
    
3.  Cut the third garlic clove in half and rub a skillet with the cut face of the garlic.
    
4.  Using a perforated spoon, transfer half of the potatoes to the skillet. Season with salt and pepper, add half the cream, and top with half the cheese. Put the remaining potatoes on top, season with salt and pepper, add the rest of the cream, and cover with cheese.
    
5.  Bake for 30 minutes, until the potatoes are completely tender and the cheese is bubbly brown.
    

I dare you not to have seconds.

**Cooking notes:**

-   This recipe will serve 3 to 4 people, but it’s easily doubled. We used three medium potatoes.
    
-   Ann said the Patricia Wells version instructs the cook to add minced garlic to the cooking liquid, but we like the more delicate flavor whole cloves give. We think the Wells’s version also includes some nutmeg, which would have been excellent but we didn’t have any. Ann doesn’t usually use it.
    
-   You can certainly simmer the potatoes in just water. When Ann started making these, her kids were little; they drank so much milk and she was so broke she cooked the potatoes in water only. But she confirms they’re better with milk.
    
-   She also cuts the potatoes by hand—no need to be precise. But it’s faster and cleaner with a [Benriner](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B01CZXJJBU?tag=ruhlmancom&linkCode=ogi&th=1&psc=1&language=en_US) (great gift if the cook in your life doesn’t have one). Don’t be afraid of the mandoline, just be careful—they’ll give you a nasty cut otherwise.
    
-   If you don’t have skillets you can bake in, you can use a 9”x13” baking dish if you double the recipe (and you won’t be sorry if you do).
    

### The National Eating Disorder That Won’t Go Away …

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Esjy!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbbccbe45-d81a-43fd-abe7-686780a9729b_1920x1920.jpeg)

For twenty years I’ve been railing against bad food-and-health information. Here are a few of them, followed by one that’s brand new.

**“Breakfast is the most important meal of the day.”** Baloney. This maxim comes from one Dr. Kellogg, who wanted to promote his new invention: Corn Flakes (these could, he advised, cure most people of the urge to masturbate). Listen to your body, not a crackpot. I need something in my stomach at the beginning of the day (usually a poached egg or full-fat yogurt with nuts and sour cherries). Ann won’t have a morsel till midday—and she seems to be getting her work done just fine. Oh, and that baloney I mentioned? It makes a satisfying breakfast, especially if you’re in [Bologna](https://ruhlman.substack.com/p/bologna-and-siena).

**“Eat healthy.”** What exactly does this mean? That’s right: nothing. Our food isn’t healthy, we are healthy if we eat nutritious food. This is more than hairsplitting semantics. Words matter. If we ask ourselves, is this roast chicken with buttery mashed potatoes, gravy, and green bean amondine healthy, it’s not an easy answer (lotta butter! lotta carbs!). But if you ask, Is this nutritious? Well, a good balance of protein, carbohydrates, and fats—the three fundamental components of our food—of course, it’s *loaded* with nutrition.

**“Eating too many eggs will raise your cholesterol.”** This was big in the 70s. The thing is, egg cholesterol and blood-serum cholesterol are different. In fact, eggs have not been shown to raise cholesterol, *except for those whose cholesterol is already low*. This according to nutrition guru [Marion Nestle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marion_Nestle), one of the few nutrition experts I listen to.

**“Food is medicine.”** This is a very American maxim in one of the most unhealthy, health-obsessed countries on the planet. Who wants to *eat* medicine? Food is joy, not medicine, and if you’re not eating joyfully you’re probably not eating well. It’s pretty hard in my opinion to eat a Big Mac joyfully. It may give you pleasure while you’re eating it, but it doesn’t make you feel good nor is it a pleasure to recollect. Cheese puffs give me great pleasure, but eventually they make me feel bad (I eat them anyway, not often). Does that beautifully roasted chicken bring me joy? You bet your ass it does, from the moment I start smelling the roasting bird, all the way through to the smell of the stock from the carcass.

#### **And here is the latest “health” trend …**

“Seed-based oils are bad for you.” The [NYTimes recently reported](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/05/02/dining/restaurants-seed-oils.html) that restaurants across the country are advertising they cook with seed-free oil (sugercane, [algae](https://algaecookingclub.com/products/algae-cooking-oil), as opposed to corn, canola, grapeseed, and peanut oils). Chefs and consumers alike are buying into this trend, though there is not a mote of evidence to support the belief. We believe it even without evidence. Sound like someone we know? Yes, our Health Crackpot in chief, Robert Kennedy Jr., who is on the seed-free-oil bandwagon. And not only with no evidence, but *in the face of* reams of evidence that monounsaturated fats (olive oil, canola oil) are likely better for your overall health than other kinds of fats.

I was alerted to this trend by a student in my food journalism class at NYU, Blaise Malley (thanks, Blaise!), who is writing a story not only about the seed-free-oil trend, but endeavoring to understand *why*, given that there’s no evidence for its veracity.

The reason, he seems to be finding, is this: distrust. No one knows who to trust anymore.

Which is why I always advise people not to listen to anyone making health claims, not even to me! Or at least be skeptical of all “health” claims. We cook with all their senses, one of which is common sense. Now I say, EAT with all your senses, one of which is common.

Best food-health advice I ever heard came from marketer Harry Balzer who said, “Cook your own food. Eat anything you want, just cook it yourself.”

### What we’re drinking …

I’ve been on a sweet vermouth-Campari tear recently and I want to offer here a classic whiskey variation: The Boulevardier.

This cocktail was first published in Harry McElhone’s 1927 book *Barflies and Cocktails*, and was the signature drink of Vanderbilt family scion Erskine Gwynne, a frequenter of Harry’s New York Bar in Paris and publisher of a *New Yorker*–like magazine called *Boulevardier*. McElhone is best known as the owner and bartender of Harry’s, where numerous cocktails were created that are still beloved today, including the Bloody Mary, the Side Car, and the White Lady.

Few will deny this cocktail’s complexity and deliciousness, for the same reason a Negroni is complex and delicious: the intensely herbal bittersweetness of the Campari-vermouth combo carried aloft on a heady spirit. But whereas the Negroni uses a very dry spirit, a Boulevardier uses a rich caramel spirit, bourbon, and so makes for a warm and rich cocktail.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zzZ3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb784ecd7-c100-4df4-babb-8bf5f4bd1829_468x500.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zzZ3!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb784ecd7-c100-4df4-babb-8bf5f4bd1829_468x500.png)
*From last night’s Friday Cocktail Hour.*

If your tastes run toward a drier whiskey, rye makes every bit as fine a Boulevardier as bourbon. The traditional version is equal parts whiskey, vermouth, and Campari, but I think it’s better with more whiskey.

This is a great winter cocktail.

**The Boulvardier**

-   2 ounces bourbon or rye
    
-   1 ounce sweet vermouth
    
-   1 ounce Campari
    
-   1 orange or lemon twist or cherry
    

*Combine the bourbon or rye, vermouth, and Campari in a mixing glass, add ice, and stir until it’s thoroughly chilled. Strain into a frosty coupe or a Nick and Nora glass (or into an old-fashioned glass over ice). Garnish with the orange or lemon twist or cherry.*

### What we’re reading …

We had drinks with our friend, editor and author Will Schwalbe ([End of Your Life Book Club](https://www.amazon.com/End-Your-Life-Book-Club/dp/0307739783/), [We Should Not Be Friends](https://www.amazon.com/We-Should-Not-Friends-Friendship/dp/0525564055/)), our neighbor across Abingdon Square, and he sent us home with a copy of [Playworld](https://www.amazon.com/Playworld-Novel-Adam-Ross/dp/0385351291/), a novel by Adam Ross. > In the fall of 1980, when I was fourteen, a friend of my parents named Naomi Shah fell in love with me.

And it only gets more interesting from there.

Our Promiscuous Reader, , has some more recommendations:

> I absolutely fell in love with Andrew Miller’s novel [The Land in Winter](https://www.amazon.com/Land-Winter-Andrew-Miller/dp/B0FC662DJN/). In December of 1962, the lives of two neighboring couples in the English countryside are upended during the violent blizzards and brutal cold of the harshest winter on record. The prose is gorgeous, the pace slow and languorous. A perfect winter read.

> Slender but powerful, Elizabeth Hardwick’s autobiographical novel [Sleepless Nights](https://www.amazon.com/Sleepless-Nights-Review-Books-Classics/dp/0940322722/) is about a woman looking back over her life and assembling a scrapbook of sorts. It’s full of memories, letters, people, places, dreams. I think Susan Sontag described it best: a novel of mental weather. If, like me, you are fascinated by Hardwick’s life—her tumultuous marriage to Robert Lowell, his appropriation of her letters in his Pulitzer Prize winning book [The Dolphin Letters](https://www.amazon.com/Dolphin-Letters-1970-1979-Elizabeth-Hardwick/dp/0374539154/), his return to her after his marriage to Lady Caroline Blackwood—this peek into her mind will captivate you.

> Speaking of Lady Caroline Blackwood, one of my favorite books is her autobiographical novel, [Great Granny Webster](https://www.amazon.com/Great-Granny-Webster-Review-Classics/dp/1590170075/). Heiress to the Guinness fortune, Blackwood shows the gothic lunacy inside the great house of her monstrous granny.
