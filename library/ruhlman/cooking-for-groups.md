The weekend before we traveled to Guatemala, [Ann](https://www.annhood.us/) and her colleague, the writer [Hester Kaplan](https://hesterkaplan.com/publications/), held a writers’ workshop in Providence, RI, wherein a dozen or so writers engaged in a weekend of prompts, writing, and discussion.

Ann enlisted me and Hester’s physician-writer husband, [Michael Stein](https://www.michaelsteinbooks.com/), to tend bar and cook. Once again it got me thinking about time-efficient but satisfying meals for groups that can be made in advance. Here are the menus for the three nights. To our great good fortune, there were no vegans or vegetarians and zero food allergies, all but *unheard* of these days!

**Opening night cocktail**:

The Negroni (gin, sweet vermouth, Campari), because it’s one of the best cocktails there is, and it lends itself to a lighter cocktail for those who want less alcohol, the Americano (seltzer replaces the gin, recipes in the last Saturday newsletter). If you’re entertaining and you want to offer more, put a bottle of decent bourbon on the table, for those who might, from these four bottles, want a Boulvardier or a Manhattan.

**Meal 1, Beef Bourguignon over egg noodles, and salad**, because it’s frigging cold, and what better meal is there on a dark cold November night than beef braised in wine?

Here are a couple of great tips. I often say that I don’t use recipes, but that’s not true. I don’t usually *follow* recipes. I look at recipes from trusted sources for ideas and quantities. Beef cooked in wine is a no brainer technique-wise (I looked to [this excellent recipe from my friend Ina](https://www.foodnetwork.com/recipes/ina-garten/beef-bourguignon-recipe-1942045) for quantities.) But a couple of things made the cooking easier. I did not spend forty minutes searing 5 pounds of cubed beef. I roasted it in a 425˚F oven (which sets the protein and squeezes out water that would otherwise become foam on the top of the stew).

Also, I don’t like carrots in stews because by the time the stew is cooked, the carrots are dull, mushy, and no longer have any flavor. So I cook whole carrots in the stew. When the stew is nearly done, I discard the now-tasteless carrots, which have given their sweetness to the stew. During the final 20 minutes or so simmering on the stove, I added fresh carrots, cut obliquely, so that they are vivid and have texture and flavor (along with roasted shallots and cremini mushrooms).

**Meal 2, Ribs!, corn pudding and baby Brussels sprouts sautéed in bacon fat**. The corn pudding was new. I usually like to do [baked corn](https://ruhlman.com/baked-corn), but I no longer have my [corn cutter](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/B000BI8E40/ruhlmancom). Also I needed a way to extend the corn to feed 12. So I decided to do a corn pudding, which is half custard and half corn. A make-ahead winner. (I tripled and followed [this recipe from Southern Living](https://www.southernliving.com/recipes/savory-corn-pudding-recipe) to a tee, almost—I used half-and-half, not cream.)

Ribs smoked on the grill, then covered in foil and slow cooked in the oven. Brussels blanched shocked and halved—all 3 components ready ahead to bring to Hester and Michael’s house, 30 minutes to finish.

**Meal 3, Bolognese!** One of the participants read my [Bolognese newsletter](https://ruhlman.substack.com/p/bolognese), which did a deep dive into Marcella Hazan’s version, and requested this one. Again, make midday, enjoy three hours of delicious aromas as it cooks. This with tagliatelle, garlic bread, and a hearty chopped salad (celery, carrots, fennel, radicchio, greens with a garlicky herby vinaigrette).

And don’t just wait till you’ve invited a dozen people to dinner, these are fabulous for meals two or four people as well!

### A return to routine …

I’d thought come the fall, after a summer of travel (Miami for Ann’s son’s wedding, DC for a book event, Newport for Ann’s MFA program, PEI, Maine, Hawaii, Cleveland, and Ireland), I thought things would settle down. But no, another wedding in Brattleboro, then a breather for me as Ann and Sam went to Turkey and Bosnia, followed by a wedding in London, back to Brattleboro for its literary festival, and on to Guatemala.

This, in addition to teaching and writing, makes for busy schedule.

Now with an early Thanksgiving behind us, we have five weeks of mainly empty space in front of us. This means an evening cocktail in front of the news, with some goldfish crackers and a cribbage board in front of us. A simple dinner, maybe a television show, and early to bed to read. Heavenly.

“Want to see a movie this afternoon?” Ann will suggest. Words that sound like harp music to me. [Maestro](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gJP2QblqLA0)*,* followed by a chilly walk home from the Angelika, and Ann made her formidable Pasta Amatriciana for dinner.

Yes, there are still classes and students, and continual writing work, and yes, a quick trip to Detroit for the photo shoot for Brian’s and my new book, [Meat Pies](https://www.amazon.com/Meat-Pies-Emerging-American-Craft/dp/0393541711/). And January will see the familiar routine of Ann’s MFA in Newport and her annual trip to St. Pete’s to teach at Writers In Paradise. But for now … just breathing.

### And drinking! …

The Manhattan for Ann when life is happily routine, the Martini for me, ideally with Pepperidge Farm Flavor Blasted Xtra Cheddar goldfish, or some salted nuts. So this is what I’m offering for this newsletter’s cocktails. The exact recipes I make on such a night.

**Ann’s Exact Manhattan**

-   1 large ice cube (I use [these](https://www.amazon.com/AmazonCommercial-Silicone-Cube-Trays-Cubes/dp/B08DJ6GPFK/))
    
-   3 ounces Maker’s Mark bourbon
    
-   1.5 ounces Noilly Prat sweet vermouth
    
-   1/8th ish teaspoon Bittermans Xocolatl Mole Bitters
    
-   Twist of some sort, orange preferable, or a wedge of mandarin orange
    

*Put the ice cube into a double old-fashioned glass. Add the remaining ingredients, in order, to the glass. Let it sit while you make a Martini.*

**Michael’s Exact Martini**

-   3 ounces Beefeaters gin that has been stored in the freezer
    
-   1/2 ounce cold Noilly Prat dry vermouth
    
-   1/2 ounce cold tap water
    
-   Several shakes orange bitters
    
-   1 lemon twist
    

*Add the gin to a metal martini glass that has been stored in the freezer. (Metal because they are always breaking—whether from a frantic cat dashing about or my own clumsy elbow; I finally fixed the problem). Add the vermouth, water, and bitters. Squeeze the oils from the lemon twist into the martini, then use the twist to give it a stir, then drop it in.*

Rationale for these choices are to be found, of course, in [The Book of Cocktail Ratios](https://www.amazon.com/Book-Cocktail-Ratios-Surprising-Simplicity/dp/1668003392). (I would like to say that for the first time, I’ve noticed that the word rationale and rational begin with the word ratio, which seems, if not etymologically pertinent, cosmically apt. … I just checked. Ratio is Latin for reason. Love it. Any linguists out there are welcome to chime in!)

> François Sagan was only eighteen years old when [Bonjour Tristesse](https://www.amazon.com/Bonjour-Tristesse-Novel-Francoise-Sagan/dp/0061440795) was published. It’s a deliciously wicked story of Cécile, a charming seventeen year old who plots to break up her father’s new romance one hot summer in France. For what happened to Sagan, [here’s an article from](https://amp.theguardian.com/books/2014/feb/28/francois-sagan-bonjour-tristesse) *[The Guardian](https://amp.theguardian.com/books/2014/feb/28/francois-sagan-bonjour-tristesse)*. 

> [The Ex-Wife](https://www.amazon.com/Ex-Wife-Ursula-Parrott/dp/194602256X/ref=tmm_pap_swatch_0?_encoding=UTF8&qid=&sr=) by Ursula Parrott became an instant bestseller when it was published anonymously in 1929. The story of a divorcee, much like Parrott herself, and her life in NYC after her husband leaves her, scandalized readers even as they loved every minute. It feels contemporary even today. How Parrott’s life went to hell is described in the excellent forward by Alissa Bennett. For example, after her third divorce Parrott was arrested for helping a drug trafficker escape from a Miami prison.

### What We’ve Been Watching …

[Nyad](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3anCgVSQb3Q): excellent biopic of the swimmer and her obsession with swimming from Cuba to Florida. Terrific performances by the aging stars, Annette Benning and Jodie Foster, who do nothing to attempt to hide or “fix” their wrinkled necks and wizened faces, unlike most former starlets. These women are stunning.

[Napolean](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OAZWXUkrjPc): Ridley Scott’s $200 million obession with the French emperor, starring Joaquin Phoenix, will please, one imagines, only Mr. Scott. It is a meaningless film that actually does a disservice to historical facts in the interest of a story that doesn’t exist (here at any rate). The French are especially hard on a film made by a British director starring an American actor.

**Related:** [France scoffs at the film](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/11/24/arts/french-critics-napoleon.html). (NYTimes)

[Saltburn](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lALMdJf6UUE), starring Barry Keoghan and Jacob Elordi, begins as a British class study drama. A scholarship kid, Oliver, arrives at Oxford and is befriended by the golden boy, Felix, who invites Oliver to his castle in the English countryside. But the story eventually devolves, step by weird step, into a horror show. Not in a white knuckles gripping armrests, but in a kind of what-the-hell-is-going-on-here way. Compelling but not nearly so much as the director’s previous film, A Promising Young Woman (which I recommend over this one, though Ann loved Saltburn).

[Maestro](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gJP2QblqLA0), written and directed by its star, Bradley Cooper, is about Leonard Bernstein and his wife, Felicia (Carey Mulligan), the marriage of two artists, one of them a towering figure on the world stage of music, the other a television and theater actress. It’s a gorgeous work of filmmaking and a fascinating portrayal of a marriage. From the beginning, Bernstein scarcely conceals his bisexuality—he clearly loves men—which ultimately divides the two. This is not a bio pic; again, it’s a portrait of a marriage and a portrait of a genius musician. Cooper’s performance is stunning.
