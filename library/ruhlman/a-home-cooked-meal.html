Your 11-hour flight from Istanbul lands at JFK at midnight and you are back in the snug West Village after two weeks in Egypt. You’re able to get to sleep by four and are manage to sleep till 10 am (4 pm according to your body). You move through the day in a haze, unpacking, taking laundry to cleaners, getting organized.

Your beloved can’t even do that as she’s teaching tonight and has papers to read. Her class starts at 7 pm and you’re hoping you can stay awake until she returns as you’re still on Egypt time, six hours ahead. You can’t imagine teaching a class in this state. You can barely make dinner, though you long for a home-cooked meal.

So, you make the first proper Martini you’ve had in weeks; you finish that excellent book you started on the boat down the Nile, and you plan one of the most easy and satisfying Italian pastas you know, one of the Big Four: Pasta Amatriciana: tomato and bacon.

A reader on social media asked what ingredients I used, and though I’ve written about this dish before, I don’t know that I’ve written the recipe down. So here you go, dear reader.

Pasta Amatriciana

  • 1 pound bacon (or a guanciale-bacon mix, which is preferred) cut in strips or lardons, as you wish

  • crushed red pepper (optional)

  • 1 red onion large dice

  • 1 28-ounce can whole peeled tomatoes (preferably San Marzano)

  • 1 poind pasta (your choice; Ann likes short and squat, so I used cavatappi.)

  • 1 to 1-1/2 cups freshly grated parmiginao reggiano

  1. Cook the bacon or bacon-guanciale mix) till the fat is rendered and the pork is crisp. Remove to a towel-lined plate.

  2. If you like it spicy, add 1/2 teaspoon of red pepper flakes to the fat, then add the onion. (Yes, keep all that fat.) And here is something I do so reflexively I often forget to include it in the recipe: as soon as onion goes into a pan of hot fat, they immediately get a four-finger pinch of salt. This helps draw out water and begins the seasoning process.

  3. When the onion is tender, add the tomatoes and their juices, one-by one, pulverizing them in your hands as you do so.

  4. Simmer for twenty minutes while you boil water and cook your pasta. If your sauce looks like it’s getting too thick, add some pasta water (moist is better as you’ll be adding cheese).

  5. Return strained pasta to the pot, add the sauce and toss. Add half the cheese, tossing to combine. Add a little more as you wish, but save some for the table.

  6. Serve with a good Tuscan wine or, if you’ve just returned from Egypt, a Tempernillo (“temper, Neo” according to auto correct), the last bottle in the house.

Your hard-working beloved will thank you!

On the Nile …

In last Sunday’s newsletter, I detailed our decision to travel to the Middle East during these troubled times, our arrival in Cairo and Giza, the Pyramids, the Egyptian Museum, the temples of Luxor, Valley of the Kings, Valley of the Queens, and finally our journey to Esna where the vivid hues of the ancient paint on the temple columns were preserved for millennia buried in sand.

And it was here that we boarded our Nour el Nil boat, Dendera, a dahabiya, a traditional Egyptian sailing vessel, 50 meters long with an 8-meter beam and two masts carrying large lateen sails, one fore, one aft. Above deck was a long, expansive stretch of couches, pillows, tables and chairs. Here we had morning coffee, played games in the afternoon, had cocktails in the evening, and took our meals.

Below was a comfortable salon (we had one meal here, during a sandstorm), and 10 rooms for two, 17 of us in all (three in our group canceled over travel concerns), each with AC and a full bathroom. In between, a foredeck for navigation; aft, the rudder controlled by lines and pulleys.

This shallow-bottomed boat allowed us to stop along the Nile to see temples, rock quarries, even a farming family’s home. All of them were extraordinary in one way or another. A stop in El Kab where we saw not tombs of Pharaohs, but tombs of noblemen; the hieroglyphics told the story of daily life, not the life of gods and goddesses.

Back aboard the boat, we had lunch as we sailed to Edfu for a carriage ride to the Temple of Edfu, in honor of the hawk-headed diety, Horus.

But really, after five days of Ancient Egypt and temples and desert, and with my mind overloaded trying to make sense of all this history, the boat was the place I always longed for.

The day began at seven or so, coffee above deck, watching and listening to the quiet on the Nile. Breakfast followed. The third day was all on the boat—and a great luxury to relax and write and think and read as we floated lazily toward Aswan. Many of the group were serious board and card gamers, and so some kind of fun was always going on.

There were stops here and there for a swim in the Nile. As in Huckleberry Finn, the only opportunity for trouble happened off the boat. On the boat was nothing but beauty and comfort and quiet, exceptional meals and truly fine traveling companions (from Lithuania and Portugal, North Carolina and Northern Ireland, and Stokholm).

The importance of crocodiles …

We had two lovely guides, one for the cruise and one while we were in Luxor, all arranged by Nour el Nil. We spent two days with a man named Sayed Ismael, who has studied ancient Egyptian culture his whole adult life. Yes, he told the stories of the Karnak Temple, the Valley of the Kings and so on, but it was his stressing of the way the ancient Egyptians thought that was most impressive.

How they managed to get a stone for a sixty-foot obelisk from Aswan to Luxor using ropes, pulleys, rollers, sand and water. Their structures were massive, and so ensured they would last millennia. Their buildings aligned with the environment and the solar system. They developed the 365-day calendar. They understood the importance of hygiene in medicine, understood nutrition, practiced sustainable agriculture.

Their connection to nature that Sayed stressed most. So many of their gods were half animal. Horus, the hawk, the Sphinx with its body of a lion and head of a man, a protecting cobra at his forehead.

But it was the crocodile, Sayed used as the example. It was an evil god, Sobek, but they prayed to it to protect themselves from evil and built the temple at Kom Obu for Sobek (pictured above). Below, crocodiles mummified for burial.

Sayed explained they cherished the crocodile. The crocodile somehow knew the level the Nile would rise to, so they could lay their eggs near its bank. This told the Egyptians where to plant their crops. The crocodile could predict when the Nile would flood—critical information: They would load barges on land with giant blocks of sandstone and granite and wait for the Nile to flood and carry the boats north to Luxor.

I could tell Sayed was attuned to the natural order of things and aligned himself with the ancients, not with contemporary life. Before our second day’s tour in Luxor, I sat down with Sayed, 52, born in Luxor, and recorded our brief conversation:

Sayed Ismael …

I have been guiding for 25 years. Always I noticed that when we talk about the ancient civilization, we try to [reconcile] our life and the ancient life. But my advice for all my guests in the beginning is that we have to understand who are we, the human, the reason why we are in this universe. We should understand that when we arrive as a human and this universe, we have different creatures, and it’s not only just the creatures—they are a gift for you. What I mean, as a human, you have three things. Time, brain, muscles. That is the most important. You are a creature, and any other creature, they have the same materials like you. Except, you are lucky. You control your body, you have your brain so you can sing, you can talk. You can use your muscles. And also, you have a limited time.

And eeeeeverything around you, as I mentioned, the daylight, the rocks, the animals, birds, water, it’s a gift for you. But it needs to communicate. You will not forget, as it says in the Bible, in the Jewish book, in the Muslim book, that once the human, they succeed to communicate with these animals, these birds. Someone spoke to them, the animals. Spoke to the tiger, spoke to the falcon, spoke to the lion, spoke to the ant. That’s when I mentioned the prophet, David in Islam. They talked to the animals. They succeed to communicate with all these creatures.

“Do rocks have a spirit?” I asked.

“Yes, sir. Let’s talk about ourself as a human. Who are we? Our bodies contain from what? Contain water, mineral metals, so I think we have the same communication with these rocks if we found the contact with these kind of rocks. They have at least twenty percent from the human contact. Fifty percent from the human contact. That’s what I mean. They are a connection between you.

Unfortunately, we are the human today. When we talk, we think that it is only just sentence. Lungs, you can speak, you can talk, you can understand. But there are other ways to communicate with other creatures, it’s not only just question and answer. There are many ways. You can contact with your eye, with your feeling, and we believe that the human, the first human, since the human born, smell. It’s a way to communicate with these creatures, these flowers. Even with these kinds of creatures they are affected with this kind of nature, like you, as a human also you are affected, you can get the medicine to survive your body. When we eat these vegetables and these fruits, healthy, it contains some news for us. That’s what I mean. The communication, the connection between you and these creatures, it’s not just a way to talk and to understand. Many other way.

But we are the human. Since we have started to create our modern civilization, we lost our communication. We are so far from nature. So the ancients, so kind to the nature, they succeed to communicate with the nature, that’s why the nature so kind to give them seven, ten thousand years of civilization.

Today we are in a few years, in two thousand years we lost our earth, we lost everything, we destroyed everything. A lot of disease we have nowadays, in ancient times none of any modern disease have been found. Everything is connected, its not a surprise you know. … Nature.

That is the story, sir.

“Sayed,” I asked, as we drove to Medinet Habu, the temple of war." “Where did we go wrong?”

Unfortunately, we humans have been stuck with this technology.

“Stuck,” I said.

I believe we have to take our self, our brain, out of this kind of civilization. To feel, to smell. Nowadays I got some news that senor or senora, some countries, they reach to a time, they are worried, they are scared from the dust, you know. But it should be you should communicate with the dust. We believe in the childhood time. We should lie in the dust, we should taste the dust, we should hold the dust, we should carry the dust on our hair and our bodies, you know, because that’s the way to be antiseptic, the way to surviving with these and that’s why our children today have been affected with many of these diseases because they are so clean, they are looking to clean that much, you know.

“So where did we go wrong?” I asked again.

Civilization? The human? To answer. Soon everything will be ended here. Nothing of these green sights will be found, the sky, the sun. We are reaching to the edge, to our end, sir, unfortunately.

I can’t say Sayed was a barrel of laughs—hey, I asked him—but he was jovial, knowledgable and seemed deeply humane. I could have talked with him all day. I was sorry he wasn’t our guide on the boat as well.

The food on the boat and the end of Ramadan …

The food on the boat was surprisingly good. Three full sit-down meals a day. For breakfast the 17 of us gathered around the table and ate great honeydew melon, and cheeses, crepes with honey or guava, and eggs any way we wanted them.

Lunches and dinners were varied and bountiful. Vegetable soups, chickpea salads, tomato salads, roasted chicken, whole fried fish, fried shrimp and sautéed prawns, roasted eggplant, okra stew, fava bean falafel, always rice or pasta or a mixture of vermicelli and rice.

The following is the range of food we had on the boat.

The condiments were no less outstanding. A baba-ganoush-like eggplant dish, gorgeous tahini that went well on everything, especially the kofta and kebabs. What we’re drinking …

And who doesn’t need a drink after a conversation with the learned Sayed? I’m a fan of other cultures’ clear, strong spirits. Grappa in Italy is my favorite; eau de vie in France is up there, too; and I’ll never forget the Cha Cha in Georgia. First, I love their ardent spirits (a favorite term of my mentor Reynolds Price) and the gentle sting on the palate, the background fruit. But I also love that they are typically made with the byproduct of wine-making. Humans figured out how to squeeze every ounce of goodness from the grape, and make precious.

Our flight from Cairo to Istanbul was delayed, we missed our connection and had four or five hours to kill. (Have you been to the Istanbul airport? Massive. Duty Free looks like a standalone department store; there was a pool table in the Passport Lounge.)

Desirous of some ardent spirits before the 11-hour flight, Ann’s son Sam and I sidled up to the bar for a couple of Rakis. Raki is the Turkish clear spirit, marked by its anise taste and the way water clouds it, like other licorice-flavored spirits. It is made by distilling suma, a low-alcohol grape distillate with anise-seeds.

We returned to our seats in the lounge to resume our game of Hearts as we waited for the flight home.
