In a recent essay titled How Can We Live Together, philosopher Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò contends that Ezra Klein’s call for open debate with the right—following the assassination of Charlie Kirk—ultimately “plays into the fascist script.” Táíwò argues that we should follow Bertrand Russell’s example in refusing to debate Sir Oswald Mosley—the founder of a literal British fascist party and outspoken fascist public figure—warning that liberals will only play into the rise of fascism by engaging in debate and that the proper response to the right is instead to shame them. The fact that Mosley is compared to Charlie Kirk—we are left to assume that Turning Points USA is a fascist group—tells us immediately where Táíwò stands on the debate as to whether Trump is a fascist.

While I agree in principle that self-declared fascists should not be debated, I reject a policy of strict no-platforming, because I have seen how it corrodes the left and weakens socialism. It produces what I call the Quixote Syndrome — a condition in which the left becomes more preoccupied with policing who is allowed to speak, with managing discourse itself, than with actually confronting the fascist threat. I want to argue that this is the inevitable outcome of a left aligned with liberalism: the fascist threat becomes a quest of tilting at windmills, where everything is labeled fascist, all distinction between reactionary tendencies collapses, and anti-fascism devolves into the policing of vibes and affects — applied not only to the right but increasingly to the left itself. The Quixote Syndrome leads to a scenario in which Marxists who speak of class and the working class — or even populist publications like Compact Magazine, or class-first thinkers such as Adolph Reed, Vivek Chibber, Catherine Liu — are cast as enemies, treated as indistinguishable from fascists. This is what left-liberal unity produces: a regression into an upper-middle-class, hyper-liberal politics that views working-class people in the same patronizing manner that liberal centrists view someone like Graham Platner today. That is the path I fear we are walking toward if we continue down the road of left-liberal unity.

While Táíwò acknowledges that “politically correct” politics stretching back to the 1990s and its more excessive successor “woke politics” which took off in the 2010s both ‘overreached’ and that they are out of step with “egalitarian” demands, this is a profound understatement of the problem. It misses how shame functions and why this overreach takes place. If we fail to account for the catastrophic and destructive damage that 2010s-2024 woke politics did to the left, we will fail to re-constitute socialism in our time. A politics built on shaming and no platforming inevitably runs amok because it is forged on a paternalistic relationship with liberalism. Ezra Klein may seem to advocate for engagement with the right in open debate at this moment, but what Klein needs more than that is partnership with the left. Táíwò’s piece seems to suggest that the left has a role to play in morally correcting liberals, in providing a moral arbitration to liberals. What we must avoid is the inevitable alliance between Táíwò and Klein and the return of the politics of micro aggression, control and shaming.

The liberal writer on contemporary fascism John Ganz has recently called for a left-wing Joe McCarthy to purge the spread of fascism in the ranks of the conservative movement after it was recently revealed that Young Republicans often ironically and unironically invoke Hitler and pro-Nazi messaging in their private chats. When demands for out-and-out censorship like this are made it signals that any deviation from left-liberal hegemony will be coded as reactionary. We should be clear: Ganz’s call for censorship will likely happen. And it will trickle down into the ecosystem of the left and its institutions. The problem with Ganz’s call for censorship is that it will produce a paralysis internal to the left where policing and hyperbolic treatment towards workers will once again run amok. Ganz’s call for censorship is far more direct than Táíwò’s call for shaming. But if either of these proposals are followed through with they will result in the evacuation of any possibility for new ideas or for an alternative path from liberal hegemony. And that is exactly what establishment liberals—and the right—want. Ganz after all called for a ‘left-wing Joseph McCarthy’ not a liberal McCarthy.

Is Shaming Emancipatory?

What precisely is the politics of shaming, and can it be emancipatory? Let’s examine how Frantz Fanon understood the politics of shame. For Fanon, the militant acts on the basis of a refusal of shame, which is why he called shame ‘the monster before revolt.’ The condition of shame was the key weapon of the colonizer. Shame is what Fanon referred to as the psychic infrastructure of the colonizer; it fixes you into an object. The culture of shame Fanon diagnosed in the colonial context was a culture in which citizens no longer own their own image, as he put it.

How precisely does shame affect us in today’s time? This is where psychoanalysis offers a great deal of insight. In his reflections on capitalism, Lacan argued that contemporary social life no longer has any shame. He wrote that shame, like guilt, is an affect of impotence and that when we are shamed we become an object for the other. To shame someone will inevitably produce certain effects. To make those ashamed for breaching a moral law or transgressing a taboo hits differently in a culture which has lost all sense of shame. The problem with a politics of shaming comes about in the way that liberalism as a system requires the expunging of all antagonism from the interior lives of all citizens. Without an institutional fabric that is capable of speaking to the true dimension of social struggle, which liberalism is not, a politics of shaming risks backfiring and fomenting greater and greater social violence.

I am reminded of what the erstwhile student of Michel Foucault Didier Eribon talks about in his memoir Returning to Reims when he writes that the French Communist Party at its peak in the 50s - 70s mitigated racism by creating public spaces which universally rejected racism. This universal rule had the effect of making the daily expressions of ordinary racism not bound up with ideological elements. The neoliberal period has witnessed the complete fusion of personal interior sentiments with ideological elements and this fusion has turned workers towards the right. If the way we perceive the social world is taken to be an immediate reflection of our political ideology, no room is left for processing the way our interior antagonisms affect us. Liberals prefer that there be a transitivity between our interior political outlooks and political ideology — and this is why liberals are the true heirs to the religious wars that enveloped Europe in the 16th and 17th centuries.

Eribon points out that liberal discourse in the 1970s to the present—as the left was gradually destroyed—is organized in such a way so as to encourage such racist identifications as a means to atomize and then code people ideologically. The liberal-left stands for the passive aggressive affirmation that workers cannot transcend their in-built racism whereas the right perversely stokes workers resentments by granting them permission to indulge and thereby transgress by being racist without any worry of the liberal censor. We already know that this formula leads to all kinds of confused and reactionary politics but notably it is liberals who appear as the authority in the relationship — it is the right who is granting permission to transgress, not the left.

There are far better ways to address racism on the left and we should learn from the communist parties which placed anti-racism as an enshrined and core staple of socialist politics. Left-liberals have an interest in conflating the interior lives of citizens directly to their political ideology in ways that unnecessarily divide. In this context, to shame people through micro-campaigns of public shaming misses the messiness of interior political experience, it falsely construes the task of politics to be a pseudo religious mission to cleanse our already riddled interior experience. A politics of shaming guided by liberal institutions will bottom out into a campaign to cleanse all antagonisms and contradictions, including of course those antagonisms borne from material oppression and exploitation.

What left-liberal shaming misses is the way that the market and capitalism already ensnare us in excessive feelings of shame. Lacan argued that capitalism in our day and age already makes us ashamed but it does this through riveting us to what he called sham enjoyment. Lacan’s point is that the new shame of our social life is caused from our consumptive habits — he argues that the capitalist imperative to enjoy and consume creates a plus-de jouir, an excess, that pressurizes and disorients us in such a way that a superego emerges that already punishes us automatically. This superego demands only that we consume more and that we do so without any need for a figure of authority to sanction this social bond. Modern capitalism leaves us in a crisis of shame because shame, unlike guilt, requires a confrontation with the Other in his or her nakedness, and by extension, in one’s own nakedness. 

Feelings of shame evoke a certain vulnerability, a precarity which is compounded by the economic and material precarity of our social life more generally. Shame is not only forged by consumerist investment logics that seek to alleviate our core discontent in society, we must not forget that shame is also already classed. It tells poor people that they should feel responsible for their failures by reducing their situation to a matter of choice:  you just didn’t work hard enough. Lacan’s main point is that capitalism erodes social bonds that would otherwise grant assurance in some way, whether that be from some big Other that protect us from shame. Lacan’s point is that the pressures borne from the dominance of capitalist commodification and consumerist investments make us ashamed but not in the same way that the system of colonial domination shamed.

The Internet is a space where we seek some relief from the ubiquity of these various forms of shame. It is not a shock that shamelessness is one of the main affects that we deploy online. Our naked selves that appear in private chats, Reddit, X and so on offer a space where we gain some relief from the constant sense of evaluation, pressure and surveillance that seems to envelop all other public space. In the context of a shameless culture, the politics of shaming tends to only compound the crisis of shame.

The Problem with Left-Liberal Unity

My contention is that the politics of shaming will inevitably run amok and lead to a cycle of overreach. But it is important that we understand that this overreach is not merely incidental; it is the outcome of the fusion between the left with the liberal establishment. In this arrangement, the left is invited to play the role of moral arbiter, to symbolically manage the excesses of the right. But in this relationship, the left mistakes their casting as a main character when in fact they are a mere understudy at worst or a supporting actor at best. The liberal in this relationship plays the director (the bad parent that enforces the rules of the game) and as the director, the material dimension of class and the possibility of organizing the exploited is prevented from taking place.

The 1960s motto, ‘the personal is political’ has reversed in today’s time. In the 60’s counterculture the personal domain was politicized wherein a cultural revolution in sexuality, the family and other intimate spheres were opened as zones of everyday life that could become politicized. But now it seems that the political has become personal. By this I mean that the class struggle has politicized the personal domain of politics. This is not the politicization of the personal, politics now intervenes in the domain of the personal. This is not a voluntarist insertion of the political into the personal as the 60s New Left advocated. Today we witness a historical shift of the class struggle that liberalism cannot contain or manage.

But it is not only the return of politics — class struggle, inequality, and austerity — that drives the politicization of the personal; this shift is also accelerated by liberalism’s incapacity to meaningfully respond to these material conditions. To make this clear, I want to situate a historical-materialist account of liberalism. I argue that liberalism is defined by two core imperatives: a refusal to materially improve the dignity of labor, and a moral refusal to incorporate working-class values into the universal fabric of politics. This refusal to recognize the common virtues of the working class is not incidental but constitutive of liberal rule. And when the left accepts the role of liberalism’s moral arbiter, it fundamentally overestimates its power. The left assumes that this class exclusion can be undone from within the institutions and patronage networks of liberalism, but this imperative cannot be undone and the fact it cannot be undone is the source of symbolic violence.

Liberalism requires the reproduction of a particular political subject — one stripped of determinate qualities and emptied of interior antagonism. As noted earlier, the demand for such a subject has its origins in the aftermath of the European religious wars, which produced a model of political order predicated on the pacification of inner conflict. Once capitalism develops in the industrial period in the early 19th century, it becomes the class struggle that must be pacified and thus the development of the ‘good’ liberal subject is built around the need to purify the interior of each subject. The modern capitalist liberal subject is what Michéa refers to as the “unitary subject”, a subject built around calculated gain and capitalist egoism. It is hostile to two dynamics: that antagonisms borne from economic exploitation re-shape its conception of politics and that any pretension to teach something to someone that would be apart from the laws of the market, the rights of the individual, and strictly technical knowledge.

A proof of my argument came about recently in the leaked Internet history of Maine politician Graham Platner. Let it be known that I’m not endorsing Platner whatsoever. In fact, I am of the mind that a strident opposition to the Democratic Party is of the order of the day and I therefore reject his association with the Democrats. But I’m not totally detached and quietist and I wouldn’t mind that he won his race. I’ll be honest, as I read through Platner’s Reddit posts “shitposting” I found them to be refreshing. Sure, his comments veered into territory that was cringe and at times totally unrefined but many of his political convictions were so clearly the result of a confluence of material factors that commonly affect the working class, from his war trauma in Iraq, to his witnessing of deteriorating working conditions and his conclusions were generally sound. He argued that we need to address these injustices in ways that go beyond the liberal center. His conclusion is clearly that liberals did not go far enough.

Platner’s righteous rage must be completely sidelined in order for left-liberal unity to occur. Platner affirmed that the working class must be armed in order to take power and that fighting fascism will require taking up arms. This is a far more sober view on fascism than any anti-fascist MSNBC liberal who claims to fight Trump by supporting centrist policies. Platner’s messy interior rantings are all the more interesting because he posted them in a seemingly anonymous way which makes them more humanizing.

The signaling that liberals are sending to Platner could not be more gaslighting. They are effectively telling him to calm down, you don’t have it that bad. This is how liberalism forges its true other in the working class subject. It should not be overlooked that the liberal position on the working class, particularly in the core regions of the capitalist system, tends to be that ‘you are not a victim and do not act like one.’ And of course, the condition of the working class does not align with the liberal conception of victimhood and therein lies the projection of the class conflict at the heart of socialism and liberalism. It is not inaccurate to say that this replays a middle vs. working class conflict in utero. There is nothing more scandalous to liberal pieties than the working class subject that complains and demands action beyond the representational mechanisms of the liberal status quo. The fact that Platner ended up denouncing his commitments to socialism in light of these revelations is tragic. But perhaps that was the intent of the leaks to begin with, to send the message that socialist politics is a thing for youth, it is immature, and pathological.

This is precisely where the left is invited in to function as the moral arbiter of liberalism, but the left cannot be the true arbiter of the discontent of the masses because it has not forged a proper independence from liberalism. It does not bother liberals that the left be a subculture left, that it stand for outlandish demands alongside tepid centrist policies. This is because the real threat to the centrists is not the left; it is socialism. So long as the left can help police the messy interior lives of workers, it will contribute to send those workers into the hands of the right. And the right has proved, especially in the Trump period, to make matters far worse for workers.

Socialists Shame Up

I remember the rise of the Dirtbag Left in the wake of the 2016 rise of Sanders and I thought to myself how well they deployed shame. The Chapo podcast built a non-personalizing and non-pandering form of shaming, a shaming that sought to reverse the left-liberal shaming that inevitably shames down to the working class. The Dirtbag Left grew to define the infamous Sanders-Warren split which pitted the progressive PMC liberals against the more nascent class-first left. And while this division was destined to sputter out and fragment, we should ask why it sputtered out. It was the victory of the liberal establishment that stamped out the Dirtbag left and once Sanders’ second campaign fell apart in 2020 it all came tumbling down. In moments such as the one we face today, I can’t help but feel a bit nostalgic for the Dirtbag Left.

The truth is that both bourgeois parties keep the real dimension of the class struggle off limits. This is why shaming has to be considered in its social dimension. To shame people without a compelling institutional network behind you is a recipe for sending people into the hands of the right. And of course the right is no better—they only dole out light slaps on the hand for Republican youth celebrating Hitler. This is because the right is purely a transgressive phenomenon, caught up in the contradiction of its own project, it seems to only offer transgression, and anything beyond that is prohibited. To what extent do these messages represent a seriousness of political ideology that is ready to act on fascist motivations? The answer to that is of course mixed. We do know that there is a strength of liberal institutions and that these institutions provide some modicum of protection against a full on fascist takeover.

What matters most for socialists is that we do not treat workers as already lost, nor lose patience with the contradictions of their interior lives. We cannot abandon the task of political education and commitment to their emancipation. Left-liberal unity, in practice, results in the marginalization of precisely those most harmed by the system. It proscribes the boundaries of their rage and kicks them to the margins, policed for crimes they often never committed. If socialists are not capable of speaking to the interior lives of the working class in a way that does not shame them for what this system does to them and the people they love, than we are better off just resigning politics to the liberals full stop. We are better off leaving Ganz to becoming the next McCarthy. We’ll see if he’s capable of it.
