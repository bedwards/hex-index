According to some on the left I have made it fashionable to reject Nietzsche’s philosophy which I find ironic given that to be “unfashionable” was something Nietzsche strived for in his own time. Whether Nietzsche would have wanted to be fashionable to the left or not is something we can leave to the reader of Nietzsche to decide. But as I have tried to suggest countless times, my reading method is centered on restoring the political thrust of Nietzsche’s thought in its full gravity of concern with the class struggle. Nietzsche is a robust political thinker whose thought must be assessed in its fullness and when that fullness is brought to the surface the left can learn a whole series of lessons, many of which have been ignored or buried by the Nietzsche industry in academia. By no means should Nietzsche be abandoned. He should be read differently than we have tended to read him.

To read Nietzsche anew in light of the seriousness by which he took political matters is the aim of a new book by Dimitri Safronov called Nietzsche’s Political Economy. Safronov’s book is a welcome contribution to the field of Nietzsche studies as it convincingly shows that despite what so many Nietzsche specialists have argued, he was a meticulous thinker of political economy. Safronov’s method is what he calls “triangulation”, an approach that aims to harmonize Nietzsche’s writings on political economy into three wider buckets: slavery, debt, and the division of labor. Nietzsche offers a wider “thematic topology” on these three themes that is continuous and integrated across his oeuvre. This topological approach resists reducing the core of Nietzsche to his unpublished material—as Heidegger argued is necessary—and nor does it read Nietzsche as aloof from politics and economy as the general consensus of Nietzsche scholars seem to generally insist.

In Safronov’s book Nietzsche is made into a thinker of the totality of the capitalist system. But unlike Domenico Losurdo who insists on the same point that Nietzsche thinks the totality, Safronov does not offer a full composite picture of Nietzsche’s political thought. Losurdo argues that Nietzsche’s thought is a ‘totus politicus’, i.e., a total focus of concern for politics and in particular a total focus on un-doing the revolutionary thrust of politics. If one were to identify one primary weak point in Safronov’s study of Nietzsche’s politics it would be that he does not explore Nietzsche in relation to the question of political subjectivity and the legacy of the rights of man, which would be a welcome connection to the theme of political economy, and as I argue it is the site where perspectivism emerges as a fundamentally political concept. While Safronov recognizes that Nietzsche’s transvaluation of values is tied to a logic of the unity of a total critique, he does not link Nietzsche’s theory of perspectivism to his wider concerns with political economy and he also ignores the richness of Nietzsche’s commentary on class in his wider discussion of slave morality. As I try to demonstrate in chapters three and four of my book, Nietzsche theorized the condition of the working class across his oeuvre and his class analysis should be included as a fourth primary area of concern of Nietzsche’s political economy alongside slavery, debt, and the division of labor.

The omission of a serious discussion on class in Safronov’s wider work reveals the limitations of the more narrow method that he adopts in this book. He supposes that Nietzsche should be read as “not susceptible to ideological interpretive biases.”1 But let us not be ashamed when it comes to Nietzsche; it is far better to not presuppose that as readers and interpreters we can ever transcend interpretive bias in our reading of Nietzsche. When it comes to Nietzsche’s political thought the history of apolitical and whitewashing of his thought has proven that efforts in ‘non-interpretive bias’ are often the ones that are the most deeply bias. The non-interpretive bias method of reading Nietzsche typically requires a psychological analysis of the Nietzsche scholar performing this supposed ‘neutral reading’, caught as they are with certain fetishes that are romantic and idealistic in nature, i.e., the apolitical Nietzscheans believe that the sensitive philosopher must be protected from his own radicalism, and in this grand effort to protect his genius, they are content to downplay his aristocratic radicalism. Why do they do this? Likely it is a combination of repressed identifications with Nietzsche’s own elitist maze and vortex of anti-socialism but also because they want to protect him for the young. Nietzscheans fear that others will be dissuaded from reading Nietzsche if the truth comes out. To some extent these anxieties are understandable, we have all been of the age where we are lured by a trance-like identification with a thinker. But importantly we grow out of it.

To his credit, Safronov does not fall sway to the same inanities of the French Nietzscheans such as Derrida, Deleuze or Foucault for whom the political unity of Nietzsche’s thought is never even construed as a factor in their wider assessment of Nietzsche. Safronov notes that it was Thomas H. Brobjer who was one of the first Nietzsche scholars to identify that the philosopher possessed a profound knowledge of political economy but Brobjer’s conclusion was that this knowledge was ultimately rejected because Nietzsche is fundamentally opposed to economic thinking which he saw as a threat to culture.2 Don Dombowsky on the other hand sees Nietzsche’s reading on political economy as extensive and as formative to his wider philosophy, and he notes importantly that Nietzsche “does not repudiate property rights, exploitation, competition, production, expenditure, surplus value or waste, which are all essential to his ‘general economy’.”3

When we understand Nietzsche’s thought as driven by concerns of political economy many of his wider political concepts come into greater focus and many of the wider assumptions that we have of Nietzsche go by the wayside. A case in point is around the anti-Hegelian thrust of Nietzsche which we are told centers on a rejection of teleology or the notion that history accords to some universal plan or end. Nietzsche’s critique of modernity is centered on the concept of slave morality and while slave morality is mostly theorized by Nietzsche as a psychic disorder at the core of the modern soul, what is most essential to note is that this same conflict is ‘fought most bitterly within the single individual’ and it is bound up with political economy. As such, Nietzsche’s critique of slave morality does not necessarily do away with teleology in its understanding of the wider capitalist social system. In Human, All Too Human he writes that “everything essential in the development of mankind took place in primeval times, long before the four thousand years we more or less know about; during these years mankind may well not have altered very much.”4 Nietzsche’s philosophy, as Safronov notes, is “the history (‘Geschichte’) of the next two centuries, as though the future’s narrative has already been scripted and speaks ‘in a hundred signs’ that have been seen before.”5 Nietzsche’s critique of capitalism is teleological: “underwriting ‘every thought, every feeling and every will’ is not any ‘one particular drive, but an overall [physiological] condition.’6

Nietzsche, Capitalism, Slavery

The thrust of Nietzsche’s views on political economy are set on the imperative that the system must be transformed because within the field of political economy the instrument of slave morality is used to subjugate humankind. The Nietzschean mantra is as follows: the valuations underwriting slave morality must be changed drastically.7 But at the same time, Nietzsche contends that as a barometer of modern society’s physiological well-being, as well as a repository of its externalities, slavery is an integral element of modern industrial culture. This fact introduces what Safronov refers to as Nietzsche’s most “incredibly uncomfortable insight” in relation to slavery: namely that, “one does not belong to either a nation, a country, a state, a social class or to a particular stratum within the political and economic architecture of society as deeply and as fundamentally as one belongs to a rank—a physiological rank.”8 Nietzsche’s effort to abolish slave morality is thus about preparing humanity for the introduction of new forms of subordination, and the necessity to break free from value propositions of slave morality that place material consideration atop the agenda in order to debilitate the spirit. This means that one of the barometers of modern society’s physiological well-being is found in restoring the importance of slavery as an integral element of modern industrial culture.9

But while Safronov is courageous enough to show how the psychological critique of slave morality is subtended with a political claim that slavery must be re-introduced into the modern world, he does not connect Nietzsche’s thinking of class to slave morality or to the fact that Europe’s wage laborers were on countless times referred to as an enslaved class. Philosophically, of course, the most important link between slave morality and slavery proper is discovered in Nietzsche’s celebration of the Stoic philosopher Epictetus, a Roman philosopher who was formerly enslaved and whose doctrines heavily influenced Nietzsche’s conception of amor fati. Nietzsche’s longest discussion of Epictetus is found in Dawn (Book V), Section 546 and it is worth quoting in full:

“The human being after the model of Epictetus would certainly not be to the taste of those who strive after the ideal nowadays. The constant tension of his being, the unwearied glance turned inward, the reserve, caution, uncommunicativeness of his eye if it should even turn to view the outer world; not to speak of his silence or near-silence: all signs - of the most resolute bravery what could this mean to our idealists, who are above all greedy for expansion! In addition to all this, he is not fanatical, he hates the display and vainglory of our idealists: his arrogance, great though it is, has nonetheless no desire to disturb others, it admits a certain mild intimacy and wants to spoil no one’s good humour it can, indeed, even smile! There is very much of the humanity of antiquity in this ideal! The fairest thing about it is, however, that it lacks all fear of God, that it believes strictly in reason, that it is no penitential preacher. Epictetus was a slave: his ideal human being is without class and possible in every class, but is to be sought above all in the depth of the masses as the silent, self sufficient man within a universal enslavement who defends himself against the outside world and lives in a constant state of supreme bravery. He differs from the Christian, above all in that the Christian lives in hope, in the promise of “inexpressible glories”, in that he accepts gifts and expects and receives the best he knows at the hands of divine love and grace and not at his own hands: while Epictetus does not hope and does not accept the best he knows as a gift - he possesses it, he holds it bravely in his own hand, he defends it against the whole world if the world wants to rob him of it. Christianity was made for a different species of antique slave, for those weak in will and mind, that is to say for the great mass of slaves.”10

Nietzsche suggests that the burden of living will become too heavy without slavery, and that modern slavery can be loosely defined based on vague spiritual and physiological traits. When these spiritual ‘insights’ into slavery are abstracted from the concrete class struggle of political economy and capitalism it becomes unclear how precisely they are instructive. Nietzsche’s views on slavery reveal the shortcomings of Safronov’s method of reading Nietzsche without interpretive bias — for if we took him at his word on these matters we would be left to assume that Nietzsche is correct when he says in The Antichrist, that ‘slavery in a higher sense’, is the sole and ultimate condition under which ‘the more weak-willed human being can prosper.’11 We might also agree with Nietzsche that the abolition of slavery ‘disguises its own obsequiousness and inability to alter the social fabric on any other level than the most superficial—that of small politics.’12 If slave morality errs in that it compounds physical slavery with spiritual enslavement, Nietzsche’s skepticism and critique is here disingenuous in that he argues that elements of slavery must be retained. If the modern individual ‘regards himself as free’ largely because ‘he no longer perceives the weight of the chains’13 and any confrontation with freedom necessitates an increasing of inequality, how is Nietzsche’s own critique not fostering the ‘sublime development of slavery’? This is after all what slave morality does but by a different political valence. Has the circle been broken?

Safronov is right to point out that Nietzsche’s total critique of capitalism was set on the desire to create a new foundation for the world-economic point of view. What this looks like for Nietzsche is ambiguous and changing throughout his oeuvre. In a more charitable reading, Safronov argues that Nietzsche favored economic slow growth and that he calls for the de-prioritisation and deleveraging of economic growth, i.e., when ‘its pace is slower, but the beat itself is much richer and health increases’.14 Safronov highlights an important biographical motivation for Nietzsche to theorize political economy: Nietzsche himself was personally affected by the financial crisis 1873. To supplement his reliable Basel pension Nietzsche made income from capital assets through investing on the stock exchange. In fact, so active was Nietzsche in terms of investments some contemporaries described him as the ‘philosopher of the big capital.’15 His purchases included some risky bonds issued by the ‘railway companies, banks and mining companies’ and he did not show much regard for the credit risk these investments entailed, but all of this became apparent only when the market fell sharply in 1873.16 Nietzsche’s investment ambitions were directed largely towards supporting his publishing efforts and building the Nietzsche myth (a fact that is underscored in his later life prior to his retreat from public life where in 1888 Nietzsche writes to Brandes, that he is making arrangements to translate 1 million copies of his books into foreign languages).17

This biographical detail may seem marginal but it is important as it shows the seriousness by which Nietzsche treated the market and attempted to financially fund the development of his own fame and the wider ‘Nietzsche myth’. But it is far more convincing to show that Nietzsche’s political radicalism was forged in a confluence of crisis events that drew him into a thinking of political economy and that encouraged the development of his focus on the totality of the system. Most notably, Nietzsche’s turn to political economy was also spurned on by the Paris Commune of 1871 an event that represented the rise of a threat to western art and civilization and the rise of a working class that would come to typify all the excesses he saw in slave morality. Out of various debates in the wake of Losurdo’s Aristocratic Rebel which places such great emphasis on the Paris Commune on radicalizing Nietzsche, Safronov helps me to see how the Commune as an event was compounded and intensified by the 1873 economic crash to which Nietzsche incurred financial losses that considerably curtailed his income. Both of these events undoubtedly informed his thinking on capitalism and they forged a political backdrop that militated Nietzsche.18

Nietzsche wrote in the context of a long depression that temporarily dispossessed him of his stock portfolio, he witnessed the rise of early imperialism following the Paris Commune and he published his first major work The Birth of Tragedy in the wake of the first major revolution in the Paris Commune that witnessed the working class taking control of the government. The role of the long depression helps to illuminate why debt emerges as so central to Nietzsche and Safronov’s discussion of debt is one of the book’s stronger sections. He situates Nietzsche’s critique of debt within the philosopher’s broader concerns about modernity’s physiological and psychological exhaustion. In classical Nietzschean fashion, however, repayment of debt or debt cancelation does not release one from bondage in an existential sense. In fact, it is slave morality that treats the concept of debt as 'denoting a condition of existence' that conflates a psychological disposition with a social relation.

The problem with debt for Nietzsche is tied to the division of labor: access to debt allows physiologically inferior individuals to climb the social ladder. Debt can thus signal the revenge of the weak against the strong. But on the other side of this dynamic the higher ones as creditors enter a physiological condition in which they wield power for its own sake. Nietzsche suggests that such agents lack the necessary physiological integrity for discharging power appropriately and without excess, i.e., “debt becomes power they can no longer do without: akin to having a ‘gnawing worm’ inside, which demands feeding, it compels them to amass ever more of itself.” Safronov points out that

“Nietzsche is concerned with tracing the transformation of ‘the awareness of having debts’ into ‘the burden of unpaid debts,’ which expresses itself in ‘the longing to have them settled’ (GM: II, §20). This examination of the ‘feeling of indebtedness towards a deity,’ which ‘continued to grow,’ enables Nietzsche to make inferences pertinent to understanding the levers of secular debt.”19

This analysis underscores how debt functions as both a material and psychological phenomenon, one that Nietzsche associates with the reactive valuations of slave morality. But the abolition of debt and a jubilee from debt is not made possible in Nietzsche’s wider political vision. The same reactionary ambiguity is present in Nietzsche’s critique of the division of labor in classical political economists as it is in terms of debt. He chastises the mechanistic division of labor in industrial societies with an “organic” division rooted in physiological and aesthetic values.

For Nietzsche, the optimal division of labor would make organic and mechanistic systems commensurate, so that each individual is placed ‘according to his nature, in such a position where he can achieve the highest that lies within his realm.”20 As it pertains to the extensive discussion on the division of labor, Safronov surprisingly has very little to say about Nietzsche’s concept of the “pathos of distance”—a key concept in his critique of modern egalitarianism—which would have underscored the notion that an organic division of labor is what Nietzsche is after but one that accords to a distance of physiological rank.

Safronov’s comparisons of Nietzsche and Marx are at times ambitious but he ultimately overstates the parallels between the two thinkers. He claims that both Marx and Nietzsche share a critique of modernity’s fragmentation of the individual, but he downplays their fundamental differences. For example, Safronov suggests that Nietzsche’s call for a “revaluation of all values” parallels Marx’s vision of revolutionary transformation but this seeming alignment of the thinkers is soon elided by the fact that Nietzsche maintains an ardent embrace of private property which is Marx’s primary target in his critique.

“Nietzsche would argue that Marx’s call for the abolition of private property becomes not only the nexus of the qualitatively different economic and social relations, but also the portal through which slave morality successfully smuggles its valuations. The abolition of private property becomes the Trojan horse inside which slave morality would successfully pass between the fledgling social order of capitalism and the, then theoretical, world of socialism.”21

Here we return to Nietzsche’s total critique of slave morality, not only does the economy have its basis in slave morality, a revolution that would overthrow the existing economy [i.e., capitalism] only compounds ressentiment (a problem that Safronov does not broach in any great detail). Perhaps the refusal to exert interpretive bias leads Safronov to miss these connections. But for Nietzsche it is revolution in the name of equality that retains slave morality in the very act of revolution. This is why Nietzsche’s spiritual revolution requires “curing economy with inequality”, in other words it is through inequality that life’s vitality and the will to build genuine communities will emerge.

Nietzsche’s position is not anti-revolutionary—he insists that slave morality must be overcome—it is a revolution in reverse, a revolution that begins with “a declaration of war on the masses by the higher men.”22 The thrust of this argument is well known: the revolution must not repeat Christianity and its doctrine of the equality of all souls. One thing is clear, for Marxists that wish to incorporate Nietzsche’s elitist vision, this will upend the revolutionary tradition from Rousseau to Marx. With Nietzsche in the drivers seat, revolution will either be forever altered at best or at worst revolution will be abandoned for something far more convoluted and spiritual. The stakes of these questions and the total gravity of their concern do not leave any room for a neutral reading of Nietzsche.
