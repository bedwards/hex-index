The contradictions of the family have to be understood through the prism of class conflict and the problem of value or we mistake the family as a quasi-religious institution and fail to understand its crucial political function. To deny that the family is riddled by class contradictions is to assume that there is just one form of the family. In a way, this is the bourgeois conception of the family, forged as it is in its own image: every family is required to aim for the reproduction of the private middle class family. The bourgeois view of the family is not so far from the religious view after all.

In my book on the family I offer a definition of the family as an institution in capitalist social life that is distinct due to the forms of exchange that sustain and reproduce itself, namely the family engages in non-instrumentalized forms of gift exchange.1 This is what introduces the problem of value. The basis of this value exchange is preceded by the social reproduction needs of the family, i.e., the family is a fundamental unit in perpetuating and ensuring the reproduction of its class basis in the wider system of production. But while the family knows its role to be such it disavows this knowledge through its exchange practices. This is why the middle class family is founded on a lie. Its primary aim is to provide a shelter from the pressures and demands of the market while simultaneously preparing children for eventual labor demands.

But in order to fulfill these requirements—while at the same time preserving the class material basis of the family—the family must cultivate personally singular subjects. It is to this end that the bourgeoisie originally promised a liberated social form with the family: it promised that each member would retain the rights to a cultivated and singular individual life beyond the family. In order to ensure this process, the family engages in forms of exchange that involve gifts that go beyond any utilitarian calculus, i.e., the children of the middle class are introduced to forms of cultivated leisure, care, training and extra services for educational improvement. While these gifts seem to be exchanged out in the open in reality there are taboos placed on these exchanges because in the realm of the capitalist market these gifts would be deemed unequal. This introduces us to what we might call the primal lie of the middle class family: the gifts it exchanges appear to be disinterested but in fact they are meant to be reciprocated through each subject ensuring their productive status, success and singularization. Without the exchange in intangible goods that ensure this singularization, the social reproduction of the family would become strained. And often such strains impact families — in fact we have seen a massive decline in the promises of the middle class family in the last two decades. But let us not lose sight of the core logic and function of the middle class family because it is these logics that are repeated, and repeated to some extent regardless of economic conditions.

At this point, one might ask: why focus so exclusively on the political and economic basis of the family? What about the fact that many family’s harbor a series of pathological forms of abuse, neglect and violence? This is true of course. The family is at once a beautiful exception from the demands of labor and work as much as a potentially pathological monster where trauma, neglect and abuse fester. But the politically salient function of the family does not rest on its potential for pathological abuse, it rests on its capacity to regenerate the material forms of the class interest of the family. This is what makes the family such an essential feature of politics in capitalist social life — it ensures the practical regeneration of family inheritance, of the division of labor, and the establishment of relations of domination and it is this process that the family works ideologically to make appear as a seemingly natural set of social relations when they are in reality not natural. The family perpetuates systems of domination. And yet if we assumed that the family is itself the source and cause of abuse and trauma we would mistake two dynamics which interrelate but not necessarily in any mechanistic or causal way. The family dispenses with non-instrumental forms of love and care while also overseeing the preparation for one day sending its subjects to sell their labor power. This is the heart of its contradictory status, it is not a univocal entity, it is always already fractured by class interests. Any quasi theological conception of the family as unmediated from systems of domination is at once too idealist. And to abolish an idealist social form may very well be all too easy to achieve. What makes the family so interesting is the way it coexists within this metabolic system of control and domination in capitalism and yet manages to dispense with forms of value that defy the wider logics of value that compose this system. The family at a germinal level is resistant to commodification while also deeply complicit in it.

Where the ‘Personal’ Came From: Class Struggle and the Family

To understand this form of value the family dispenses with, it is important that we historically analyze this particular form of non-instrumentalized value that is at the heart of the regeneration of the bourgeois family—I use bourgeois family and middle class family interchangeably—because it is the working classes conflict with this form of value that must be grasped. In a historical account of the family we discover that the class formation of the family is a dialectical process in which the working class demanded forms of value for themselves and this propelled great leveling of the promises of the private bourgeois family. This fact introduces a conundrum for Marxists because the following is true: even in the case of the Russian and Chinese communist revolutions in the 20th century, the basic nuclear structure of the family has remained constant. The Marxist sociologist Göran Therborn has argued the family has remained the least susceptible to structural change in the wake of communist revolutions in the 20th century. The utopian socialist dream of the 19th century that aimed for a commune or collective structure of the family did not transpire in the wake of communist revolutions. However, these revolutions did manage to expand the rights of women on scales that were formerly thought unimaginable by bourgeois standards. What the class conflict of the middle and working class family shows us is that regardless of class there are psychic attachments to the family that have made it inflexible to structural change and that have prevented a truly collective family to emerge historically. But this does not account for the problem of value and self-making and the type of non-instrumentalized love and care that the family exchanges, but let’s examine this.

In Eli Zaretsky’s book Capitalism, the Family & Personal Life from 1976 he argues that the very project of the personal—or the notion that we have an autonomous personal project—was born from a series of political demands of the working class. It is difficult to understand at this point in time but the working class family had historically been barred from the private bourgeois family and this only really changed around the dawn of the 20th century. The family historian Stephanie Coontz in her work The Social Origins of Private Life notes—in a way similar to Zaretsky—that the working class was only permitted to marry on the basis of love and romance by the time of the early 20th century. Before this time the working class family was bonded by economic necessity, precarity and struggle; its ties were collective and not reliant on reproducing the cultivated individual. Not only was it the working class that demanded the personal for its members but another dynamic occurred on behalf of petit-bourgeois intellectuals during the period of the Second International. Marxist intellectuals experimented with forms of freedom outside of wage labor and this accelerated the bourgeois family and its conception of the cultivated personal individual.

In a recent interview with Julie Reshe I discuss the history of the family and elaborate on many of these themes of class, paternalism and the changing authority of the father.

A new strain of class conflict thus emerges when the working class is introduced to the middle class private family and what we witness is a pancaking of the class formation of the family into the demands of the bourgeois family. This is a process that only accelerated with the rise in welfare state capitalism following the wars. It is this enmeshing of the proletariat with the middle class family that bears our attention: the middle class family forms the horizon of demands and yet these demands are unfulfillable. This means that the modern family as a structure remains circumscribed by bourgeois requirements that are unfulfillable and unrealistic for the working class. And this becomes the primary political problem that warrants study: what are the class distinction of the contemporary family?

Eli Zaretsky shows that it was the demands of the working class to participate in the project of the personal and thus the private family that made way for the eventual expansion of the breadwinner family following the Second World War. The very idea that the personal domain where singular self-becoming could ever emerge as a demand for workers was itself born historically from great struggle. As we see in the portrait of David from 1793, the class aspirations for the promises of a better life, for a material betterment to one’s own and one’s family’s way of life are a motor of history.

Once the Family is Understood from the Perspective of Class Struggle, What is Family Abolition?

My argument is that the family introduces a domain of value that is non-commodified and this form of exchange become the building blocks of self-making such that without the infusion of family gifts, of the introduction of leisure, the exemption from wage labor etc. the family would already be abolished, dissolved into the sea of the market and its domination. In this regard, those discarded from families already experience an abolition of the family because they only know the socialized paternalism of the market. This brings us to the great lie of the middle class family and what it must conceal: domination and exemption from labor. The middle class family is at a distance from domination—and it knows that it is—but this knowledge must be disavowed in order for the family to prepare its members to abide by the false equivalence of labor routines once they are imposed on family members.

The matrix of class conflict still hangs over the family today and it revolves around the inequality of the family. We live in a time in which the old middle class ideals of the family have to be adopted in order for one to make it. These ideals are so unevenly available but yet they remain the primary locus of value-making for all families. The working class family finds these demands to be impossible and due to this impossibility they experience acute inequality. One form of the family faces repression, the other a fundamental deceit. It is not a surprise that the class conflict of the family is largely muted because the exchange of the family itself is already complicit in a concealment operation. The gifts of inheritance are an excellent example of this ideological concealment. If they were made out in the open the entire premise of bourgeois fairness and rights would dissolve and the family would be exposed as a purely class formation. We can’t help but fight for this value to be a full person. But we fight for a different basis in how this is to be determined. We fight for a different material and institutional arrangement in which the promises of the personal are delivered, distributed and exchanged. Put simply, we Marxists fight for the abolition of class domination. What the family will be in the wake of that may very well resemble the contemporary family, just as past communist revolutions. But one thing we know is that in the wake of the revolution the family and its promises will be governed in more egalitarian ways.
