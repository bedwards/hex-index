*This is a follow-up piece to my first essay on [the concept of self-censorship in philosophy](https://open.substack.com/pub/danieltutt/p/self-censorship-and-philosophy?utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=post%20viewer). This piece is also a discussion of Domenico Losurdo’s book *Hegel and the Freedom of Moderns*.*

The Hegelian formula, 'what is rational is actual; and what is actual is rational' is something of an enigma. Does this formula represent an unqualified blessing on the political status quo, and should it be understood as a reflection of the implicit conservatism of Hegel’s *Philosophy of Right* where he embraces constitutional monarchy? Or does such a reading miss the subtlety of Hegel’s dialectical thinking. This phrase would certainly be a reactionary position if Hegel were to be read as embracing an anthropological conception of the subject of history or a subjectivism in which actuality is construed as the empirical subject of history embodied in the king or monarch.

But Hegel’s subject of history is not the king and nor is it found in any appeal to a more bland anthropological humanism. Marx argued that Hegel is incoherent within his own philosophy, that he is a “double dealer.” For Marx, Hegel is split between a latent political core that pushes his thought towards an egalitarian direction and a more reactionary core in line with the status quo. Engels pinpoints a similar dynamic in Hegel when he claims that there is a split between Hegel’s method and system: the *system* is the more explicit and latent political adherence to the status quo, i.e., in *The Philosophy of Right* Hegel infamously defends “constitutional monarchy” against the restoration and against the divine right of kings. That the absolute idea must be realized in a representative monarchy is clearly something that Engels rejected, but at the same time, one of Hegel’s strengths was to reject unrealizable ideals. Here is how Engels argues that we must read Hegel beyond the limits of his system:

> With all philosophers it is precisely the “system” which is perishable; and for the simple reason that it springs from an imperishable desire of the human mind — the desire to overcome all contradictions. But if all contradictions are once and for all disposed of, we shall have arrived at so-called absolute truth — world history will be at an end. And yet it has to continue, although there is nothing left for it to do — hence, a new, insoluble contradiction. As soon as we have once realized — and in the long run no one has helped us to realize it more than Hegel himself — that the task of philosophy thus stated means nothing but the task that a single philosopher should accomplish that which can only be accomplished by the entire human race in its progressive development — as soon as we realize that, there is an end to all philosophy in the hitherto accepted sense of the word. One leaves alone “absolute truth”, which is unattainable along this path or by any single individual; instead, one pursues attainable relative truths along the path of the positive sciences, and the summation of their results by means of dialectical thinking. At any rate, with Hegel philosophy comes to an end; on the one hand, because in his system he summed up its whole development in the most splendid fashion; and on the other hand, because, even though unconsciously, he showed us the way out of the labyrinth of systems to real positive knowledge of the world.[1](#footnote-1)

Engels reads the reactionary thrust of Hegel’s philosophy as bound up with the interest of lower middle class Germans at the time. This is a slightly different reading of Hegel’s politics than offered by Lukács in *The Young Hegel*, when he argues that Hegel did not develop a refined theory of capitalism because of the primitive economic conditions of the pre bourgeois revolutionary situation in which he lived in Germany. What Lukács reveals about Hegel’s method is that while he fully eschews any directly empirical or anthropomorphic conception of the subject of history, Hegel refers to the ‘valet’s knowledge of human nature’ as a continual motif throughout his work. In other words, Hegel situates a latent theory of the proletariat in his work, and this is supported by Frank Ruda’s early work on Hegel and the concept of *rabble*.[2](#footnote-2)

In addition to these dynamics of an implicit critique of political economy and of the proletariat, Hegel supports revolutions both from below and from above, and while he was not hostile to Jacobinism, he was ensconced in a political milieu that seemed to prevent him from adopting more directly radical commitments. Lukács, reading Hegel at the level of his method, will claim that the entirety of *The Phenomenology of Spirit* is an implicit rebuttal to the aristocratic direction of Schelling. Such a reading should not be understood as the bias of a socialist reading of Hegel. We have to identify not merely the method of Hegel that allows us to see this thrust to his thought. We also have to examine the implicit political dynamics of Hegel’s time, from the political spectrum itself, to the rationale behind his adoption of certain orientations to this thought and not others.

### Why Hegel’s Self-Censorship Matters: A Question of Method

As I have argued in my [first piece on self-censorship in philosophy](https://open.substack.com/pub/danieltutt/p/self-censorship-and-philosophy?utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=post%20viewer), the problem of self-censorship is to be understood as a sunk dynamic, not as an aberration. There I argued that philosophers occupy a peculiar non-hierarchical social role, the seeds of which are planted in Ancient Greece. This means that philosophers negotiate the public-private distinction as authors in ways that upset social class. This is significant because at the origins of philosophy we discover a discourse which permits, at least in principle, access to *sophia* for all, regardless of social class or station. Only after a careful reading of a given philosophers lectures, letters and primary texts combined with a careful comparison across and between them can we begin to arrive at a fuller picture of a thinker. In short, philosophy has a radically open and egalitarian presupposition in its original state (*Isonomia*) and with certainty, philosophers face situations of repression either from the state, the scholarly discourse, a reigning ideological formation (religion, political movement) all of which is a constant presence which of course varies in its degree of authoritarian enforcement.

So what was Hegel’s situation and how did he self-censor? It is one of the great merits of Losurdo’s study *Hegel and the Freedom of Moderns* that he identifies a specific case of Hegel’s political commentary in the run-up to his work *The Philosophy of Right* that illustrates the interesting dynamic Hegel faced and thereby reveals his latent political core. In his lectures, Hegel weighed in on the problem of the “emergency rights for the preservation of life” or *Notrecht*. But rather curiously, Hegel does not discuss nor does he embrace these rights of *Notrecht* in *The Philosophy of Right* but he does in his lectures running up to the text. In embracing this conception of the rights of the poor, Hegel argued that the poor have the right to steal and violate the laws of private property if they are facing a threat to their survival such as starvation or deprivation. This opens a more radical dimension in Hegel’s conception of rights which is not shared by other leading liberal philosophers such as Benjamin Constant or Condorcet.

This reading of Hegel’s politics seems to go against the many cliche’s that we hear of Hegel’s *Philosophy of Right*, namely that it is as a work that exhibits a full embrace of the restoration. This discrepancy is also the source of an obvious case of censorship, which we [examined last time in the thought of Leo Strauss](https://open.substack.com/pub/danieltutt/p/self-censorship-and-philosophy?utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=post%20viewer). As I mentioned, in *The Philosophy of Right* Hegel defends constitutional monarchy but this defense is offset, to a degree, through his embrace of the very concept of rights.

In *Hegel and the Freedom of Moderns* Losurdo performs a careful philological analysis of Hegel’s lectures at the time of writing *The Philosophy of Right* and contrasts them with Hegel’s lectures leading up to the published text itself only to discover that in Hegel’s lectures on the concept of right there are a series of comments on what is known as the “trucksystem”, a form of exchange in which laborers were paid in goods not money. Hegel was opposed to this contract system due to the way it exploited workers, i.e., the private contract eroded the personal freedoms of the laborer, he argued that within this private contract laborers who are starving lack the accordance of right to defy the laws of property. This points to the radicalism implicit in Hegel’s account of rights, Hegel argued that laborers should have the right to assistance beyond the tyranny of a particular private labor contract.

In this contrast between the published text of *The Philosophy of Right* and the lectures on rights we can see that Hegel self-censored his full on support for *Notrecht* in *Philosophy of Right*. What this inconsistency points to is a classic case of self-censorship, and it indicates that Hegel was adhering to political norms of his time while seeking to upend those norms in ways that do not necessarily reject established convention. Hegel writes: “any contract or positive right that violates the fundamental freedom of a person is in fact an injustice” and this is expressed through his wider criticism of natural law in *The Philosophy of Right*. But in Hegel’s lectures on *Notrecht* we find an even more egalitarian embrace of the concept of the rights of extreme need when he writes, “not only do they have the right to steal the bread that will keep them alive, the poor person has the “absolute right to transgress the right of property, that legal norm which condemns theft.” While the concept of *Notrecht* points to the radical kernel of Hegel’s political thought—as the ‘right of extreme need’—what makes this radical is the fact that this right can be invoked and applied to justify the absolute right of citizen action facing oppressive situations of apartheid, state repression or genocide.

We can see the implicit methodological radicalism of Hegel’s position when we examine his wider critique of natural law. If a person’s freedom—the ‘rights of man’—are inalienable this does not grant an eternal protection on the individual because the original contract is the result of a historical struggle that is ongoing and therefore not completed. Natural law thus errs precisely in that it freezes history and the struggle for freedom. This is why for Hegel natural rights have to be understood as nature itself and this is why Hegel strategically sides with natural law over that of contractual law by insisting that natural law be *historicized*. In this move, crucially, Hegel condemns slavery through his support for anticontracturalism because any contract that sanctions slavery would be null and void from the standpoint of the rights of the citizen. This is different than Locke who will justify slavery in the colonies through the justification of the legitimacy of the contract.

For Hegel, Losurdo states, “freedom is the subsumption of every human being into the category of man, including ex-slaves to the extent that he has inalienable rights.”[3](#footnote-3) Hegel rejected contracturalism because Rousseau’s state of nature had become co-opted by reactionary restorationist thought and the egalitarian basis of Rousseau’s thought had lost its foothold in absolute equality. Edmund Burke, for example, condemns absolute equality and justifies slavery in the colonies but in Hegel the conception of man as a ‘rights bearing creature’ points to the core idea of *The Philosophy of Right* that ‘the actual is rational’, i.e., freedom is understood as a historical process of recognition that is achieved in concrete social struggle. Hegel is therefore an anti-contracturalist because he views the contract as a limitation on possible freedom: “Any contract or positive right that violates the fundamental freedom of a person is in fact an injustice and therefore the act whereby I take possession of my personality becomes the re-establishment of the right.”[4](#footnote-4)

What makes the state ‘ethical’ is that it secures the right to work, well-being and the right to life. Hegel focuses on the inalienable rights of man and this focus can be read as possessing a material content such that , as Losurdo says, “freedom is the subsumption of every human being into the category of man, including ex-slaves to the extent that he has inalienable rights.”[5](#footnote-5) This is why freedom for Hegel is understood as “the subsumption of every human being into the category of man, including ex-slaves to the extent that he has inalienable rights.”[6](#footnote-6)
