Erica Komisar—psychoanalyst and crusader in the mommy wars—is sitting across from me on her couch, sipping an oat milk cappuccino and smiling a winsome smile.

It’s past 4 p.m., and she’s been saving her caffeine hit for my visit.

We’re meeting at her apartment on the Upper West Side—a gorgeous condo with high ceilings and stunning views. Her cream-colored cockapoo puppy, Chester, joins us with an agenda of his own: gnawing at his pink toy rabbit.

In recent years, Komisar has become a social media superstar—with over 300,000 followers on Instagram, she describes herself as an “author helping parents to live their best lives and raise healthier children.” She talks a lot about how so many modern mothers do not securely attach to their babies, and is best known for her zingers—parents today are “pussies,” day cares are “warehouses for children,” sleep training is “neurologically damaging.”

“Many people that work for me have tried to curb how I describe things,” she says, laughing. “But it is the way I describe things!”

And it works. Her podcast appearances have accumulated millions of views. TikTok is full of viral clips from interviews she’s given. As a new mother, I find some of them unnerving. But in person, Komisar is less intimidating. She is elegant and bold, and has an air of authority: a mix of highly respected schoolteacher and fabulous auntie.

Komisar is 61, and at the peak of her career. She’s written three books. The first, published in 2017, is called *[Being There: Why Prioritizing Motherhood in the First Three Years Matters](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9780143109297)*; it earned an endorsement from Kourtney Kardashian. (“Erica’s wisdom validated what I’ve always felt instinctively—that presence, especially in the early years, is everything.”) The second, *[Chicken Little the Sky Isn’t Falling](https://bookshop.org/p/books/chicken-little-the-sky-isn-t-falling-raising-resilient-adolescents-in-the-new-age-of-anxiety-erica-komisar/f49cd08912aad4b0?ean=9780757324000&next=t),* focused on raising teenagers in what she calls “the new age of anxiety.” And her latest, which will be published in a few weeks’ time, is called *[The Parent’s Guide to Divorce](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9781682689769)*.

“The truth is that raising a child in a healthy way is very uncomfortable. It’s also very joyful,” says Komisar.

“Although divorce is really bad for all children in one way or another, a good divorce, a healthy divorce, is better than a bad marriage,” she says. “There are ways to mitigate the damage.” That’s what she says her new book is trying to do.

Erica and I met on Tuesday. The next day, she left for Australia. “I’m doing a press conference with a senator who’s presented a bill to promote a year of paid \[maternity\] leave, plus more years of stipends for families so they can choose how to care for their own children instead of institutional day care,” she says.

“My messages are heard more in other countries,” she goes on. “It’s very hard here.”

The reason, she explains, is “the brand of capitalism that is American capitalism” is a “work at all costs” mentality, combined with a feminism that has “told mothers they should think of themselves first.”

It’s anti-instinctual, she says. And it stops mothers from realizing the truth: that raising children is both the hardest and the most rewarding work there is.

Komisar wasn’t always a career woman. Before becoming a mom, she worked nearly 40 hours a week, but when her three kids were little, she and her husband had a plan. She worked 90 minutes a day, Monday through Friday, just enough to keep her therapist’s license and to afford to pay Alba, her mother’s helper, who she describes as like a grandmother to the family. (Komisar’s own mother had Lewy body dementia and was too unwell to help out.) Her husband, an optometrist, earned enough for the family to live on while Komisar raised their children.

Now, years later, “when I’m sitting in front of my Zoom sessions with my patients all over the world and working really hard again, he’s out mountain biking or going with his friends on a hike or skiing,” she says. In other words, they’ve taken it in turns to be the breadwinner.

“People don’t think of the arc of a life. They just think about this idea of fairness and 50-50,” she said. But 50-50 is “an illusion.”

I ask Komisar why she thinks she’s having a moment.

“The brand of capitalism that is American capitalism is a work-at-all-costs mentality,” Komisar tells me.

When her first book came out, she says, she was “ahead of the curve.” But now, we’re increasingly aware of the sharp decline in children’s mental health. [One extensive study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11445688/) of teenagers in California has shown that between 2017, when her first book was published, and 2021, depression rates among adolescents rose a shocking 60 percent.

At the same time, Komisar says, women have begun to wake up to the lies they’d been sold about working motherhood.

The notion “that you threaten women with the idea that if they step off this very fast conveyor belt, they will never, ever be able to step back on,” she says, is “a particularly American narrative.” It’s also “a bunch of baloney.”

You [can have it all](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DHxqG49t1P9/?utm_source=ig_web_copy_link&igsh=MzRlODBiNWFlZA==), says Komisar, just not all at the same time.

Chester jumps onto my lap and begins eating my notes. Komisar, apologizing through laughter, restrains him.

I mention a recent [viral](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/01/opinion/baby-formula-marriage-equality.html) *[New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/01/opinion/baby-formula-marriage-equality.html)* [op-ed](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/01/opinion/baby-formula-marriage-equality.html), which argued that parents should “use baby formula early and often” in order to avoid “the infuriatingly lopsided dynamic” the writer had experienced with her first child, in which she did all the feeding. The piece ran under the headline: “[The Secret to Marriage Equality Is Formula.](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/01/opinion/baby-formula-marriage-equality.html)”

“I found it very upsetting,” Komisar said. “There are really good reasons to use formula,” she said—for instance, some women experience health problems that prevent them from breastfeeding—but in her eyes, gender equality isn’t one of them.

“The truth is, breast is best,” she says—not only because breast milk is immunologically better for babies, but also because feeding fosters intimacy between mother and child.

She hands me Chester’s pink rabbit, saying: “Cradle this baby.”

Though soggy and stinky, I cradled it.

“Look what you just did,” she said. “Why didn’t you cradle the baby on the right side? Because your instincts told you to cradle the baby on the left side.” This leads to a more direct and deeper connection, she explains, because a person’s left-side visual field is processed by the right hemisphere of the brain—the domain of emotions, facial expressions, and tone.

When I return the rabbit to Chester’s jaws, I feel a dart of concern for it.

Komisar, with her knack for wading into the most controversial debates in the mom-osphere, has perhaps even stronger opinions about day care— “warehouses for children”—than she does about breastfeeding. To leave a baby in one, she says, mothers have to “turn off empathy.”

What would you say to a single mother who has to work and can’t afford a nanny? I ask. Komisar tells me there’s “a false narrative that a poor woman must use day care, and that’s the only financially viable option.”

“She may not be able to hire a surrogate caregiver for herself,” she says. But a woman with limited funds “could share a caregiver with another family. She could grab her next-door neighbor or her best friend and say, ‘Do you want to share a babysitter?’ ”

I ask what she thinks about state-funded day-care programs, such as those proposed by New York City mayor Zohran Mamdani—who is [putting together a plan](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/30/nyregion/mamdani-nyc-mayor-free-childcare.html) to provide free day care to every child age 6 weeks to 5 years old. Komisar says her opposition to such policies is why she’s flying halfway around the world tomorrow. Institutionalized care is in no one’s best interests, she says—including the government’s, given that it costs around [$26,000 annually](https://comptroller.nyc.gov/reports/child-care-affordability-and-the-benefits-of-universal-provision/#:~:text=In%202024%2C%20the%20average%20cost,minimum%20wage%20jobs.%5B3%5D) per child.

“We’ve told mothers, ‘There is no discomfort. You shouldn’t feel any discomfort in raising children,’ ” Komisar says.

“If you give some of that money—let’s say you give $20,000 or $18,000—to the families to use as they see fit, they’ll find a better way to care for their children,” she says. The answer, in her opinion, is to give families choice. “Either the mothers will stay home, or the mother will work part-time, or they’ll give the money to a grandmother or trusted neighbor who they call auntie who’s going to have more of an investment in that child’s future than some transient stranger in a day care center.”

If you go into a day care center, she says, you see “the distress of so many children at once that is not met by the day care workers.” This, she caveats, “is not because the day care workers aren’t good people, but because there’s no way for one person to meet the distress of five or eight or 12 children.”

And when an infant’s distress isn’t met, Komisar says, “their cortisol levels are high, which it is not meant to be in those early years. And so a lot of those children go into silent mode”—where they cease to cry, because they know they won’t be answered.

At this point, Chester began tearing the legs off his pink rabbit. Komisar intervenes to stop him from choking on them.

On social media, Komisar speaks a lot about attachment disorders—which, understood simply, she says, are “a maladaptive way of coping, a strategy to cope with the loss of your mother.” In day care, she says, “babies have to develop maladaptive coping strategies, which in the moment save them, but in the long run make them vulnerable to mental illness.”

For the same reason, Komisar thinks sleep training—the practice of not responding to a baby’s cries at night, so they learn to fall asleep on their own—is “neurologically damaging.”

But Emily Oster, the economist (who hosted a podcast for *The Free Press*), says there’s [no evidence](https://parentdata.org/babies/why-is-everyone-afraid-of-sleep-training/) to suggest it’s harmful, I say. “That’s not true,” Komisar says. “There’s a lot of research.”

Of Oster, she added, “I think she appeals to mothers because she tells them what they want to hear, which is: *You can be disconnected and raise healthy children.*”

Of course, one of the most difficult things about parenting an infant is sleep deprivation. But Westerners, says Komisar, make this more difficult than it needs to be: In other cultures, mothers and babies bed-share. This allows mothers to nurse their babies in the middle of the night in a dreamlike state, sort of like when you go to the bathroom to pee without waking up fully. It also allows babies to be comforted when they’re grumbling, before they wake.

The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends against bed-sharing, citing the risks of Sudden Infant Death Syndrome and suffocation. But Komisar says this is because so many Americans drink alcohol or use sleep medications before bed. If a mother is healthy, sober, and not taking any drugs—and especially if she’s breastfeeding—bed-sharing is the way to minimize exhaustion.

What does she think about parents who hire a night nurse, [a practice that’s booming](https://www.usatoday.com/story/life/health-wellness/2025/07/23/night-nurses-midwife-pregnancy-parenting-newborn-baby-childcare/84415099007/) among high-income, dual-working American families, because they allow mothers to get a full night’s sleep?

Unsurprisingly, Komisar does not approve. She thinks it is a “narcissistic trend.”

“We’ve done this to mothers. We’ve told mothers, ‘There is no discomfort. You shouldn’t feel any discomfort in raising children,’ ” she says. This idea all started with epidurals, she says, though she has nothing against those.

“The truth is that raising a child in a healthy way is very uncomfortable,” she says. “It’s also very joyful, and the reward is that your baby is more attached to you.”

Does it bother Komisar that much of what she says makes working moms feel guilty?

Not really. “The kind of guilt that most mothers feel is warranted.”

And ideally, she explains, it should spur them into resolving their guilt by putting their children first.

\---

Komisar says her work is sometimes confused with advocacy for “gentle parenting,” which is the idea that your decisions as a mother or father should be governed by the whims of your child. She tells me she is pro boundaries, but there is a difference between toddlers, who can understand the word *no*, and infants, who are helpless.

More than anything, she says, she wants mothers to trust their instincts. When most women have a baby, she says, “something gets turned on. It’s transformative.” Their heart tells them what to do.

Some women don’t have the choice to do what feels right, Komisar acknowledges, but: “What I always say is, when you have any option at all, listen to your heart. Your heart is telling you something important. It’s telling you that you need that baby and that baby needs you.”
