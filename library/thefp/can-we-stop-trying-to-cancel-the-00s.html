Welcome back to Second Thought. I’m in the driver’s seat this week because Suzy heard me ranting about how I’ve had it with documentaries about early-aughts reality shows being highly abusive. I was there at the time; I remember. Buckle up.

The new Netflix docuseries Reality Check: Inside America’s Next Top Model opens with a flurry of Zoomer influencers staring into their phones cameras, mouths aghast in feigned horror and outrage. Headlines pop up across the screen: “The TV That Aged like Milk,” “The Problem with America’s Next Top Model,” “The Legacy of America’s Next Top Model Is Anything but Fierce.” To borrow a phrase, the past is a foreign country, one where a size six was fat and Tyra Banks could force aspiring supermodels to dress up like homeless people, drug addicts, and different races. But now we’re a new people in a new land, and it’s time for everyone to atone for the sins of who we once were: television viewers in 2003.

Reality Check is the latest production of what I like to call the Reckoning Industrial Complex. Prominent examples from the last few years include Quiet on Set (about the Nickelodeon channel being terrible behind the scenes), White Hot (about Abercrombie & Fitch being terrible behind the scenes), and Britney vs Spears (about Britney Spears’s life being terrible behind the scenes).

The formula goes something like this: Take a pop-culture product from roughly two decades ago, point out how unsavory it was in retrospect—even if this was obvious and remarked upon at the time—then make the viewer feel somehow complicit, and then offer some fairly tepid insight from behind the scenes.

So it goes with Reality Check. The series begins with the show’s creator, supermodel Tyra Banks, laying out all her good intentions. As one of the first black supermodels, she broke barriers within the industry and wanted to help others do the same. Inspired by American Idol (a novel concept of a show in 2003), she wanted to find the best undiscovered model in America and turn her into the next, well, Tyra Banks. The show is called America’s Next Top Model, and it is a hit. Then things predictably, and quickly, go off the rails. As early as season 1, contestants begin complaining about the hallmarks of what makes good reality television: selective, storyline-driven editing, producer-manufactured drama, and a high-stress environment designed to make contestants break down emotionally. Composure on camera is bad TV.

The producers front-load the most plainly egregious and actually unforgivable storyline early on, that of Shandi Sullivan, a season 2 contestant who accuses production of allowing her to be sexually assaulted on camera while she was blackout drunk at a house party in Milan. Shandi says that at a certain point during the encounter, she’d passed out completely—but the cameras kept rolling. To add insult to injury, the incident was portrayed in the show as infidelity, with producers going so far as to film Shandi sobbing as she told her boyfriend about it over the phone.

The true horror of this makes the rest of the series look like a petty amalgamation of gossip, gripes, and gratuitous repetition: Of course this sort of thing would never fly today. As the seasons go on, America’s Next Top Model goes to increasingly extreme lengths to keep viewers glued to the screen. Producers force contestants into Fear Factor-esque scenarios with cockroaches and tarantulas. They have them pose as bulimics, or murder victims, all under the tutelage of Tyra Banks, who berates her contestants and forces them into jigsaw-esque obstacle courses, humiliating situations, and, on at least one occasion, unsolicited dental work.

The formula goes something like this: Take a pop-culture product from roughly two decades ago, and point out how unsavory it was in retrospect.

Despite the heavy-handed moralizing, there are some darkly and unintentionally funny moments in the documentary. For example, at one point, a very serious contemporary interview about how contestants were dressed up as different races for a photo shoot (a challenge that occurred more than once) cuts to archival footage of Tyra Banks compelling a seductively posed white contestant in brownface to “think about Egypt, the people, what they’ve been through!”

Like what? The Suez Canal crisis? The burning of the Library of Alexandria? What does that even mean? The entire situation is so absurd. One suspects that, in some way, people might be secretly watching the show for moments like this: a sort of highlight reel of ANTM’s most shocking, ridiculous, controversial moments. By “exposing” the fact that ANTM made good TV by humiliating contestants in increasingly elaborate ways—a fact that was obvious at the time and not hidden from anybody—Reality Check creates a permission structure by which the viewer can voyeuristically consume the psychological suffering of beautiful young women once again, only this time with righteous indignation. Watching ANTM was a guilty pleasure, and watching Reality Check is how you atone for it.

Only you aren’t gawking this time. You’re bearing witness. Right?

The Clavicular Show

If you don’t know who Clavicular is, it means you’re free of the internet addiction spell I’m under and you also didn’t read last month’s article I mentioned him in. Clavicular is a 20-year-old guy from New Jersey whose real name is Braden Peters. He’s a Kick streamer best known for bringing the term “looksmaxxing” to the attention of the mainstream internet. Looksmaxxing is a subcultural movement of hyper-online young men who will do anything and everything to make themselves look hotter. Clavicular is a proponent of “bonesmashing”—hitting yourself in the face with a hammer to supposedly make your jawline and cheekbones more prominent. He’s also admitted to using meth to make his cheeks more hollow, and says that despite his young age, he’s currently infertile due to years of anabolic steroid abuse.

In the past month or so, Clavicular mania has swept the nation. The gawking masses cannot get enough. Clips from his near-24-hour daily livestreams have become impossible to miss online. Recent stops along what appears to be a nationwide tour have brought him further fame and controversy. Last month, Clavicular appeared in a Miami Beach nightclub with a gaggle of far-right influencers that included Nick Fuentes and Andrew Tate and partied to the Ye hate track “Heil Hitler,” although he recently told The New York Times he met with a group of Jewish nightclub owners shortly thereafter in a show of diplomacy.

Clavicular mania has swept the nation. The gawking masses cannot get enough.

Soon after the Miami incident, he found himself in an Arizona jail cell on suspicion of possessing and using a fake ID and carrying dangerous drugs. (He had one Adderall pill and one Anavar pill on his person, and the charges were dropped due to “no reasonable likelihood of conviction.”) After being released Clavicular made his way to Manhattan to record an interview with The Adam Friedland Show, a popular YouTube interview show, before donning what appears to be some kind of waxed shirt and strutting the runway in designer Elena Velez’s Fashion Week show. Clavicular’s strange rise to internet stardom has been so fast that people have begun concocting conspiracy theories to explain it. Over the weekend, Clavicular kicked an interviewer out his house for asking about his alleged connections to billionaire tech founder Peter Thiel—connections he says do not exist.

Clavicular, or “Clav” as his fans call him for short, is not the first person to attempt a sort of Zoomer Truman Show in which nearly all aspects of his life are recorded for public consumption. Lots of people do this, many quite successfully. But Clavicular is different. He says he is on the autism spectrum, and looksmaxxing is his special interest, one that shapes all of his actions, vocabulary, interactions, and opinions, even on who will win the presidency (Gavin Newsom, apparently, because he “mogs” and J.D. Vance is an ugly “subhuman”). The result of Clavicular’s all-encompassing obsession is a new world, ClavWorld, wherein something as banal as standing next to another man is, by the dictates of Clavicular’s looksmaxxer perspective, a competition of male beauty in which he risks being outdone.

Clavicular’s gamification of daily life is part of what makes him interesting. Even more fascinating is watching ClavWorld clash with the real one, as he interacts with bemused and befuddled normies who aren’t quite sure what to make of him but often find themselves along for the ride, using his words, picking his brain, and drawing out what appear to be an endless well of unique opinions, all singularly informed by his ever-consistent looksmaxxing principles. The fact that he is, by virtue of his lifestyle, very handsome, only adds more intrigue. Physically, he fits in among fashion models and influencer types. But the minute he opens his mouth, it becomes clear that Clavicular isn’t like them, at all. He isn’t like anybody.

To watch the Clavicular show is to experience life through the eyes of a genuinely strange young man, an outsider artist dutifully documenting his unusual perspective on the world and the way people respond to it. Is that perspective basically just an elaborate form of body dysmorphia? Who’s to say. Is it good that this is popular? Probably not. Will there be a Reality Check-esque documentary about him in a decade? Almost certainly. But I’m not here to tell you why America shouldn’t watch him; I’m here to tell you why it can’t seem to stop.

Here’s What Else I’m Thinking About

Like seemingly everyone else on the internet, I have spent the last several days developing a deep and emotional parasocial attachment to a baby monkey who lives in a zoo in Ichikawa, Japan. Panchi-kun, or Punch in English, was raised by human caretakers who gave him a stuffed orangutan from IKEA as a surrogate mother, after his real one rejected him. Viral videos show Punch carrying the toy animal around with him in his little enclosure, as he struggles to make friends with the other monkeys, who frequently bully him—although some progress has been made. Adult monkeys in the troupe were recently seen holding and grooming Punch. Our baby is going to make it.

Sad-pop princess Lana Del Rey just released a new single called “White Feather Hawk Tail Deer Hunter,” off her forthcoming album Stove, which is supposed to come out within the next few months, although we’ve been lied to before. My initial impression was “weird Lynchian nursery rhyme” and I stand by that, but it’s kind of growing on me.

Last month, former NFL star Matt Kalil sued his ex-wife, swimsuit model Haley Kalil, alleging that she invaded his privacy and unjustly enriched herself by implying in a November 2025 Twitch stream that his large dick size was a primary factor in their divorce. This week, the case took a new turn, with Haley’s lawyers arguing the case should be tossed out because she never mentioned Matt by name. I think we’re missing the forest for the trees here. She described somebody’s penis as “two Coke cans, maybe even a third.” Maybe we should all be a little grateful for what we have, Matt!
