Even as he relentlessly campaigns to be awarded the Nobel Peace Prize, Donald Trump is also a man who fetishizes violence and shows of force, particularly fascistic displays of military force used to intimidate unarmed civilians, and action movie-like videos of blowing things up.

As he orders military strikes against alleged drug smuggling boats in Latin America and sends U.S. National Guard troops into Democratic-governed cities that don’t want them, allegedly to fight crime, Trump seems to think matters of law are of less importance than what he evidently thinks is good politics for him and the GOP.

On Friday, Trump will sign an executive order giving the Department of Defense “a secondary title” as the “Department of War,” according to a White House fact sheet, the Washington Post’s Dan Lamothe noted.

“The Order instructs the Secretary of War to recommend actions, to include legislative and executive actions, required to permanently rename the U.S. Department of Defense to the U.S. Department of War,” the White House fact sheet said.

“The name ‘Department of War’ conveys a stronger message of readiness and resolve compared to ‘Department of Defense,’ which emphasizes only defensive capabilities,” the White House fact sheet says, Reuter’s White House correspondent Nandita Bose wrote.

“Department of War!” the ever desperate to be seen as a tough guy Pete Hegseth tweeted tonight, evidently relishing his new title, Secretary of War.

The attempted displays of military prowess come as questions swirl about Trump’s health after he had no public events for seven days including the Labor Day holiday, and as Trump’s efforts to advance peace between Russia and Ukraine have failed, and indeed Russia has increased its attacks on Ukrainian cities following Trump’s controversial summit with Russian President Vladimir Putin.

With Trump evidently unable to make headway with Putin, he seems to have chosen Venezuela’s dictator Nicolas Maduro as a bully he would rather toy going to war with, on the pretext of alleged support for drug cartels.

Speaking at the White House on Tuesday (Sept. 2) to announce the move of U.S. Space Command from Democratic-leaning Colorado to Republican Alabama, Trump--who until then had conspicuously not made a public appearance since a full week earlier--rambled at one point semi-coherently about having just blown a boat out of the water –apparently in reference to a likely illegal U.S. military operation targeting what the administration claimed was a drug smuggling boat off of Venezuela.

“We just, over the last few minutes, literally shot out a boat, a drug-carrying boat,” Trump said as he wrapped up his remarks on the space command move and prepared to take a few questions from members of the White House press pool.

“It just happened moments ago and our great general, head of the joint chiefs of staff, who has been so incredible, including what took place in Iran,… he gave us a little bit of a briefing,” Trump rambled on, referring to chairman of the joint chiefs of staff Dan Caine, and the US strikes on Iran nuclear facilities in June.

“And there's more where that came from,” Trump said. “We have a lot of drugs pouring in from our country…These came out of Venezuela. So, we took it out.”

The administration has yet to provide evidence of exactly who and what was on the boat the U.S. military blew up Sept. 2, and where exactly the strike took place, except to say that it took place in international waters.

“Early this morning, on my orders, U.S. Military Forces conducted a kinetic strike against positively identified Tren de Aragua Narco-terrorists in the Southcom area of responsibility,” Trump subsequently posted at Truth Social. “The strike occurred while the terrorists were at sea in International waters transporting illegal narcotics, heading to the United States. The strike resulted in 11 terrorists killed in action.”

The timeline suggests that Trump has been wanting to go to war against drug cartels in Latin America, and was looking for targets to blow up in the hemisphere.

Since February, the Trump administration has designated several Latin American drug cartels as foreign terrorist organizations, including groups from Mexico, Haiti, and Venezuela. It subsequently added Venezuela’s Tren de Aragua, Mara Salvatrucha (MS-13), and Venezuela’s Cartel de los Soles (Cartel of the Suns) to a list of specially designated global terrorist groups.

On July 27, Secretary of State Marco Rubio accused Venezuela’s Maduro to be illegitimate, as well as “the leader of the designated narco-terrorist organization Cartel de los Soles, and he is responsible for trafficking drugs into the United States and Europe.”

On August 8, the New York Times reported that Trump had secretly signed a directive “to the Pentagon to begin using military force against certain Latin American drug cartels.”

“The order provides an official basis for the possibility of direct military operations at sea and on foreign soil against cartels,” the Times report said.

And as Trump was looking for drug cartel targets to blow up in the western hemisphere, he was also looking for U.S. Democratic cities to force US miltary troops into, under various pretexts.

On August 11, Trump signed an executive order putting the federal government in charge of Washington, D.C.’s police force, claiming a crime emergency, though crime was actually down. Some 2000 national guard troops have since been sent to DC, though many of the cases brought against people arrested since then have been tossed out or otherwise been reduced.

On Tuesday, Trump vowed to send federal troops to more Democratic-run cities around the U.S., not coincidentally in states with governors Trump considers political rivals, like California governor Gavin Newsom, Illinois Governor JB Pritzker and Maryland Governor Wes Moore.

“Chicago is a hellhole right now. Baltimore is a hellhole right now,” Trump said. “Parts of Los Angeles are terrible. … It's so badly run…If I didn't send the National Guard into Los Angeles,… I would be making an announcement today.”

“Well, we're going in -- I didn't say when, we're going in,” Trump continued, claiming that, “I have an obligation. This isn't a political thing.”

“If the governor of Illinois would…call me up, I would love to do it,” Trump continued. “Now, we're going to do it anyway. We have the right to do it, because I have an obligation to protect this country and that includes Baltimore.”

“I would love to receive calls from governors and mayors, saying they need help,” he said. “We'll help them. We have a lot of people. We have a great force. We have a great military force.”

On Monday, however, a federal judge ruled that Trump’s use of thousands of military troops in Los Angeles over the summer was illegal, and a violation of the Posse Comitatus Act, which restricts the use of the military against American citizens.

“President Trump’s recent executive orders and public statements regarding the National Guard raise serious concerns as to whether he intends to order troops to violate the Posse Comitatus Act elsewhere in California,” U.S. District Judge Charles Breyer wrote.

Trump and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth ran the risk of “creating a national police force with the President as its chief,” Breyer wrote.

On Tuesday, Trump bristled at a question about the ruling, accusing the reporter who asked about it of failing to note that the judge stayed his ruling until September 12.

“The judge said, but you can leave the 300 people that you already have in place,” he said. “They can continue to be in place. That's all we need. …They can stay, they can remain, they can do what they have to do,” an evidently disgruntled Trump said, then concluded the event.
