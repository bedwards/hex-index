The optics were warm and cordial.

President Trump praised Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy’s wearing of a dark suit to the White House, and put an arm over the Ukrainian leader’s shoulder when he greeted him at the White House entrance. Zelenskyy-- though he presumably has no shortage of maps of Ukraine marking the current front line of Russia’s invasion--made a point of admiring a map of Ukraine that was placed on an easel in the Oval Office during their meeting, and Trump graciously offered to get him one.

But some European leaders accompanying Zelenskyy to the White House today made clear that they did not accept Trump’s abrupt abandonment of a push for a ceasefire in Ukraine after his meeting with Russian President Vladimir Putin last week, evidently because of Putin’s refusal to accept one.

“We all would like to see a ceasefire,” German Chancellor Friedrich Merz said, when Trump went around the table inviting the assembled heads of state to say a few remarks ahead of their closed door meetings. Present, in addition to Merz, Zelenskyy, and Trump, were French President Emmanuel Macron, British Prime Minister Keir Starmer, Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, Finnish President Alex Stubb, European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen, and NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte.

“I can’t imagine that the next meeting would take place without a ceasefire,” Merz continued. “So let’s work on that and let’s try to put pressure on Russia … I would like to see a ceasefire from the next meeting, which should be a trilateral meeting wherever it takes place.”

“To stop the killing as we discuss [a permanent deal] is a necessity,” Macron said.

“All of us would obviously prefer an immediate ceasefire while we work on lasting peace,” Trump acknowledged in remarks at the top of meeting before going around the table inviting the assembled – “Mr. President” Zelenskyy, “Mark” Rutte, the NATO Secretary General; the European Commission President “Ursula” von der Leyen; Finnish President “Alex” Stubb, “Emmanuel” Macron of France-- to say a few words. “And maybe something like that could happen. At this moment, it’s not happening.”

Trump remarked several times today on his great relations with Putin to a room-full of leaders more clear-eyed and skeptical about Putin’s continued maximalist demands, as well as the leader of the country which continues to be bombarded day and night by Russia. Trump said he planned to call up Putin after the meeting today, and hoped to get a trilateral meeting with him, Zelenskyy and Putin scheduled.

(Macron, for his part, suggested that it might be worthwhile to have a four-way meeting, also including the Europeans, since they would play the key role in any sort of security guarantee for Ukraine under a peace deal.)

Indeed, Trump paused his meeting with European leaders this afternoon to call Putin, CNN and other media reported. The reports said the European leaders were waiting to hear how it went.

It is not hard to imagine that while Trump was once pressing for a ceasefire and now a three-way meeting, that he may be learning that Putin is not interested in either of those.

Trump “malleable”

Trump in flip-flopping on his push for a ceasefire after meeting Putin “has once again shown himself to be malleable,” Russia expert Sam Greene, professor at Kings College London Russia Institute, wrote on Twitter. “Who knows what will happen when he gets off the phone with Putin later today.”

“If Zelenskyy and his European allies all go home and leave the ball in the Trump/Putin side of the court, the best outcome they can hope for is more confusion,” Greene continued.

The fact that there was no February-like blow up today between Trump and Zelenskyy may have European leaders breathing a sigh of relief, Greene said. But “if we’ve learned anything thus far, it’s that we should give it 12-18 hours before drawing conclusions about anything involving Trump and Putin.”

Trump ‘in a rush’ for peace deal

“Trump's biggest problem is that he's in a rush,” International Crisis Group Ukraine analyst Lucian Kim wrote on Twitter. “That’s why he's so bendy on an immediate ceasefire before talks.”

Trump’s zeal for a quick deal and advisors without much experience on Russia/Ukraine mean he may be misunderstanding key elements at issue.

“Question 1: What is a security guarantee for Ukraine acceptable to Putin?” the Crisis Group’s Kim asked. “If it is one in which Russia is also a guarantor, as in the Budapest Memorandum or Istanbul talks, then Ukraine will understandably balk, especially if Moscow gets a veto on decisions on other guarantors.”

“Question 2: How likely is a trilateral summit? Putin disdains Zelenskyy, who defied him by staying alive and fighting back,” Kim continued. “The Europeans’ support for a ‘trilat’ seems to be one way of getting behind Trump and heaving the ball back into Putin’s court after Anchorage.”

View from the White House: Trump enjoys playing statesman

I went to the White House for a few hours today, to catch Zelenskyy’s arrival (and the press room chaos), as I did for his (catastrophic) visit to the White House in February. I was struck today by a sense that the White House staff seemed to be proud to be hosting the visit of all the world leaders, reflecting Trump’s desire for it to go well, play statesman and advance peace deals.

Trump, it seems, would like to be a great statesman, convene world leaders, and ratchet up peace deals – including in his comically open campaigning for the Nobel Peace Prize, but also because he seems to genuinely enjoy the role. (For all his hostility to Nato his first term, I was struck today by the warm rapport that Trump seems to have developed with several of the leaders who were at the White House today, particularly Rutte, Macron, Meloni, Starmer and Stubb.)

But I am not sure that Trump has the patience, experienced advisors, appreciation of the complexities, or moral scruples to negotiate with truly malign actors like Putin, in order to work to successfully end wars that can’t be solved in a half dozen meetings. Or that he can discipline his tendency for wishful thinking, and projection and narcissism, especially when it comes to Putin. (“I think he wants to make a deal for me,” Trump, referring to Putin, was overheard on a hot mic today telling Macron. “Do you understand? As crazy as it sounds.")

And as the Crisis Group’s Kim notes, he may be in too much of a rush.

Tonight, Trump reported in a Truth Social post that in his call with Putin, they “began the arrangements” for a meeting between Putin and Zelenskyy, “at a location to be determined.”

But Kremlin foreign policy advisor Yuri Ushakov was more circumspect in his readout of the call on the prospect of Putin meeting the Ukrainian leader: “The presidents discussed the idea to study the possibility of raising the level of Moscow and Kyiv representatives in talks."

“We don’t have any date” regarding a possible meeting with Putin, Zelenskyy told reporters in a press briefing tonight after his White House visit.

Macron and Finland’s Stubb said tonight that Putin had agreed to a Putin/Zelenskyy bilat.

But one wonders if Putin may be tapping Trump along, as Trump once put it.
