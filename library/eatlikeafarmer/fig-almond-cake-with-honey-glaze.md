It’s been a big week on the farm… on Tuesday we officially wrapped our Summer CSA and DAMN it feels good.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zAb-!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe970d171-7fc9-460c-a2cf-067d97335e04_2751x1988.jpeg)

*a look at our final CSA share of the summer (large size)*

For the past 10 weeks, my team and I harvested, washed, prepped, and packed up all of our freshest, ripest produce for our wonderful CSA members. (And let’s not forget the months of seeding, transplanting, weeding, watering, pruning, and trellising that started way back in February). Looking back on the summer season so far, I can honestly say it’s been our most successful and abundant yet. Which is pretty amazing, considering that my time in the dirt has been limited, as I’ve been mostly focused on spending time with our young kiddos this season.

So how can our little farm churn out so many beautiful vegetables with minimal attention? First things first – I have the *best* team (looking at you Hannah and Ginger!) who have picked up all my slack and kept this small but mighty farm running. Truly, none of this would be possible without these two hardworking, kind, and wildly talented humans. But their time is limited too, and I only have their help for a total of about 12 hours each week (which isn’t much, considering how much food we are growing). So how can we produce all these vegetables with such limited labor? Honestly, **the secret is the soil.**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9PpJ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F369086c9-ac7d-420d-abc0-55feb5070ff7_2016x1163.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9PpJ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F369086c9-ac7d-420d-abc0-55feb5070ff7_2016x1163.jpeg)

For the past 5 seasons, we’ve poured so much intention (and high quality compost) into building truly healthy, resilient, biologically active soil, *and it shows.* Through heat waves and record breaking rain events, our soil has stayed intact and nourished our crops all season long. And this year we’ve dealt with the least pest pressure ever – which I directly attribute to the health of our soil, which in turn produces strong plants that are more resistant to pests. But this work of soil-building is never complete, and every year we keep working to enrich the soil so that it can enrich us.

By the way, if you want a more in-depth look at \*exactly\* how we built our farm and our permanent raised beds, [check out this post](https://eatlikeafarmer.substack.com/p/how-i-turned-an-empty-field-into). And [here is a full guide](https://eatlikeafarmer.substack.com/p/cultivating-healthy-soil-101) all about cultivating healthy soil.

Even though summer is certainly not over, we intentionally end our summer CSA in early August so we can rest, recover, and prep for fall crops. Speaking of which…

When life gives you figs… make [fig jam](https://eatlikeafarmer.substack.com/p/homemade-fig-jam?utm_source=publication-search) or [fig pizza](https://eatlikeafarmer.substack.com/p/fig-ricotta-and-arugula-pizza-with?utm_source=publication-search) or this very excellent **fig almond cake with honey glaze!**

Despite years of trying, I’ve yet to produce one single ripe fig from our farm, so I’m lucky to have several neighbors who are rich in figs and who generously let me pick from their fig trees. So this week, when I was blessed with a heap of gorgeous ripe figs, I was inspired to do some baking… which is a miracle in itself, because if you know me, you know I’m *not* a big baker.

**But this cake!** Hot dang, this is my kind of cake. I was first influenced by [this recipe](https://cookieandkate.com/gluten-free-almond-cake-recipe/) from Cookie & Katie, and I decided to tweak it and add fresh figs for a little something extra.

This cake is wildly easy to whip up (no standing mixers or separating of eggs required), it’s absolutely stunning, and I love that it has \*just\* the right amount of sweetness, without being overly sweet. It’s the kind of cake that tastes light and fresh and doesn’t leave you with a bellyache afterwards. The kind of cake that you could eat for breakfast alongside some yogurt and not feel like crap an hour later.

A note that the figs may sink into the batter a bit while it bakes, but don’t worry about it! You can always add more sliced figs to the top when it comes out of the oven. And feel free to use whatever kind of fruit you have… berries, peaches, apricots, etc would also be fabulous.

PS – [This is my go-to almond flour](https://www.bobsredmill.com/product/almond-meal-flour) from Bob’s Red Mill.
