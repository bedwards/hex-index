Until about the age of 24, I could barely boil water, let alone confidently cook a multi-course meal. As a budding farmer, I knew how to grow some damn good broccoli and carrots and all sorts of vegetables, but knowing how to *cook* them? I was truly, embarrassingly clueless.

That is, until I attended cooking school at [Ballymaloe](https://www.ballymaloecookeryschool.ie/). It’s an experience that changed *everything*.

So today, I’m sharing the nitty gritty details (and some hilariously low-quality 2013 iphone photos) from my life-changing time at Ballymaloe, which is something I really haven’t talked about here. In fact, for the next 3 weeks I’m writing a whole series about Ballymaloe, and I’m excited to kick it off today with an in-depth look at **a day in the life as a 12-week student.**

I’ll be sharing lots about this magical farm on the coast of Ireland in the coming weeks, but **here’s the gist**: Ballymaloe is a 100-acre organic farm + cooking school on the coast of County Cork, Ireland. The matriarch of the school is Darina Allen (she’s a total legend), and she runs it with her fabulous brother Rory, effervescent daughter-in-law Rachel, and husband Tim – alongside a large and dedicated team of instructors, gardeners, cheesemakers, animal caretakers, etc. Ballymaloe offers all kinds of culinary classes, from afternoon workshops to 1-week or 5-week long courses. But their flagship program is the [12-week course](https://www.ballymaloecookeryschool.ie/certificate-cookery-course/course-outline), where students from all over the world come to live on the farm and cook their hearts out for 3 action-packed months. (If you follow Hannah Neeleman of , then you’ll recognize Ballymaloe as the school where she and her husband [took the 12-week course](https://hannahneeleman.substack.com/p/how-did-we-get-here) this past January).

Coming from a farming background, I was instantly drawn to Ballymaloe’s focus on cooking nourishing food using the freshest ingredients possible. Their whole ethos is simple: when you start with good, seasonal, locally-sourced ingredients – *cooking delicious food doesn’t have to be complicated.*

As soon as I learned about Ballymaloe, I knew I would figure out a way to get there. So after graduating college, I worked for 3 years co-founding and managing an [educational farm for UVA](https://morven.virginia.edu/mkg), and I scrimped and saved every penny until I could afford the 12-week course. It was the best decision I ever made, and the best money I’ve ever spent. I can’t wait to tell you more…

### *But first, a few important caveats before we dig in:*

-   I want to be *super clear* that while I attended the 12-week course at Ballymaloe, **I do not consider myself a chef.** This is just my opinion, but I use the term *chef* solely for people who manage a professional kitchen (which could mean working as a solo private chef, running a bustling restaurant kitchen with a fleet of cooks, and anything in between). You certainly don’t have to go to culinary school to become a chef, and simply attending cooking school doesn’t automatically make you a chef. There are plenty of students who take the 12-week course and continue their education to eventually become full fledged chefs, and the program at Ballymaloe is a great launching point to get started on a culinary career (in whatever form it may take).
    
-   I went to Ballymaloe back in 2013, and surely some things have changed since then. That said, I know from speaking with more recent students that the overall structure of the days and weeks that I’ve outlined below are very much the same. If anything, the amazing team at Ballymaloe has only added new spaces and features to the program, such as the Bread Shed, [Fermentation Shed](https://www.ballymaloecookeryschool.ie/ballymaloe-cookery-school/fermentation-shed), and a fabulous [new greenhouse](https://www.ballymaloecookeryschool.ie/organic-farm/glasshouses-overview) structure that was just recently completed.
    
-   The 12-week course takes place three times a year: January, April, and September. My 12-week course started in late April, and I’d argue it’s the best time of year to go! The days only get warmer, brighter, and more beautiful as the weeks go on, and the fresh produce available that time of year is tough to beat.
    
-   Hopefully it goes without saying that all opinions are all my own, and not sponsored in any way. *(though hey Ballymaloe, if your reading this and want to sponsor me, I’M YOUR GIRL)*

### 6:15am – Wake up

As soon as my alarm jolted me awake each morning, I pulled on my wildly unflattering chef pants, buttoned up my chef coat, and slipped into my [Dansko clogs](https://dansko.com/products/professional-black-box?variant=40366122893354&country=US&currency=USD&utm_medium=product_sync&utm_source=google&utm_content=sag_organic&utm_campaign=sag_organic&utm_campaign=Performance_Max&gad_source=1&gad_campaignid=16740314524&gbraid=0AAAAADRNXcU64CdOaOtQgnHluVmhuX6p5&gclid=Cj0KCQjwzt_FBhCEARIsAJGFWVkxmVWApaS_Rei4wfHpydyXRaLB69cxmnEwYtxpF20woSiOmTjCnjQaAigaEALw_wcB) (aka the unofficial footwear for people standing on their feet 8+ hours a day). I lived in the very adorable “Pink Cottage” with a group of six lovely classmates, all roughly the same age as me (amazingly, students in the 12-week course range from 17 to 70+ years old). My room was small and sparse, but it had everything I needed (and my introverted self was *very* grateful that I paid a bit more for a single room). Breakfast in the cottage was light – I usually made a bowl of farm-made plain yogurt with granola and fresh fruit. And heaps of coffee, of course.

### 7am – Early start in the kitchen

Because I came to Ballymaloe with a total inability to cook, I felt like I needed every second of extra time to catch up to speed, so I usually arrived to the kitchen an hour early to get extra practice and prep for the recipes I was responsible for cooking that day. As a student on the 12-week course, you’re pretty much given free reign over the kitchen / pantry of ingredients, and I was determined to take advantage of every opportunity to learn. I certainly wasn’t the only nerd who showed up early, but I’d say the majority of students rolled in closer to 8am, which is the usual start time.

### 8am – Official start in the kitchen

By 8am, the kitchen was buzzing with activity as everyone filed in and set up for a full morning of cooking. Because I usually arrived early, by 8am I had already weighed up my ingredients and organized my station with everything I would need for the recipes I was assigned to make that day. Before I could start cooking, I would review my “order of work” with my teacher (keep reading for what that’s all about), to make sure I was set up for success and wasn’t missing anything. From there, it was non-stop action for over 4 hours – which I know *sounds* like a lot of time, but I swear it FLIES by.

### 12:30pm – Plate recipes for evaluation by teacher

Along with properly cooking each of our assigned recipes, we were expected to plate each dish beautifully, as though they would be served in a restaurant. And honestly, one of the main skills I gained at Ballymaloe was learning out how to cook multiple recipes and have them all ready at the same time (which is way easier said than done). I would give my teacher a 5 minute heads up when I was almost ready for tasting, and then they would try each dish, giving each recipe a rating from 1 to 10. Thankfully, all the teachers were good about giving fair but helpful feedback, and even if I totally bombed a dish, they would never berate me or make me feel bad. It’s generally a very kind and supportive environment, not at all like a cut throat restaurant kitchen.

### 12:45pm – Clean up station

One thing you learn at Ballymaloe is the importance of keeping a clean and tidy station – though inevitably there was always a big mess to clean up after cooking all morning, and you can’t leave your station for lunch until everything is cleaned up and every utensil is back where it belongs.

### 1pm – Lunch!

Maybe the best part of the day?! After cooking like a maniac all morning, I would sit down, yap with friends, and enjoy all the recipes that my kitchen-mates and I had whipped up. Every day was an incredible smorgasbord of soups, salads, pastas, entrees, fresh bread, cookies, cakes, etc. But honestly, after cooking lots of complex and rich recipes all morning, usually all I wanted to eat was a giant green salad and some warm sourdough bread slathered in golden Irish butter. After lunch I would sprawl out in the grass and decompress by soaking up some sunshine before heading back inside for the afternoon demo.

### 2pm – Demo

With full bellies after a gourmet lunch, everyone ambled into the demonstration kitchen and settled in for a 3-3.5 hour whirlwind of culinary mastery. The lead instructors took turns running each demo session, and over the course of 3 hours they would expertly whip up 20+ different recipes – a mix of starters, soups, sauces, entrees, desserts, breads, etc – carefully explaining each technique as they went. (As a very stereotypical straight-A / type-A person, you *know* I was sitting in the front row every afternoon at demo, taking copious notes). Yes, 3+ hours is a long time to sit and watch someone cook food, but I was like a transfixed sponge, soaking up every morsel. And it was important to pay careful attention during demo, because we would be assigned to cook a selection of those recipes the next morning in the kitchen.

### 5:30pm – Tasting after demo

After 3+ hours of sitting in a chair, everyone would grab a plate and get a bite of each of the recipes prepared during demo. Getting to *see* how each recipe was cooked from start to finish and then being able to *taste* everything was incredibly helpful for my inexperienced palate. Immediately following the demo tasting, I would check the board outside the kitchen to see which recipes my cooking partner and had been assigned for the next day, and together we would decide who would cook which recipes.

### 6pm – Get outside!

A big benefit of taking the 12-week course that started in April was getting to enjoy the incredibly long, gorgeous summer evenings. After demo, I almost always threw on my tennis shoes and struck out on a 5-6 mile walk to the coast with some friends and fellow classmates. Some of my most vivid memories from my time in Ireland are from these daily strolls through the epic countryside, with cows happily grazing in lush fields on my right, and the Atlantic Ocean on my left. Looking back now, I know these walks were key for maintaining my mental (and physical) health during this intense season of life. Oh, and I know Ireland gets a bad rap for terrible weather, but honestly that wasn’t my experience! On rare rainy days when we couldn’t get outside, after demo we’d cozy up in our cottage and binge some bad Irish reality TV.

### 8pm – Light dinner with friends

After being surrounded by food all day and eating a decent amount of food at the 5:30pm tasting following demo, I usually didn’t eat a big dinner during the week. It was usually a basic [girl dinner](https://www.bonappetit.com/story/girl-dinner?srsltid=AfmBOopnG3EETcMnIhY3qgUfhpXND82JRb-FmIbTfnIXhJmzrteHUjmY) with local cheeses, fresh fruits and veggies, yummy dips, and whatever odds and ends we had leftover in the fridge. Because it stayed light so late in the evenings, we almost always ate outside in the communal courtyard. Weekends were a bit more celebratory, and I’d often prepare feasts alongside friends so we could practice new recipes and enjoy laid back, wine-filled dinners together. After dinner, there were frequent trips to the local pub, either the [Goal Post](https://www.thegoalpostbarshanagarry.com/) in Shanagary, and on the weekend we would venture a bit further to the [Blackbird](https://www.blackbirdballycotton.com/) in Ballycotton. And yes, it’s absolutely true that Irish pubs are the BEST. Great vibes, great food, great people, and great music.

### 9pm – Take notes + complete my order of work

The sheer amount of information you absorb in ONE day at Ballymaloe is frankly mind-boggling, so it takes some real effort to organize your notes and recipes for 12 jam-packed weeks. To make sure I didn’t fall behind, I made a commitment each night before going to bed to take detailed notes (which I typed out in an ongoing googledoc) about my time in the kitchen – listing each recipe I cooked, what went wrong, what went well, etc. I also typed up any important notes gleaned from the afternoon demo.

Finally, before going to bed I would complete my “order of work” for the next day – which was basically a timeline we brought to the kitchen each morning to review with our teacher, demonstrating that we had clearly thought about the timing of each dish, and to show *exactly* how we planned to execute multiple recipes at once. Many of my classmates found this process of creating a daily order of work to be super annoying, but I personally found it to be an incredibly helpful exercise. To this day, any time I have to cook multiple recipes at once, I write out an order of work to help me stay on track!

### 10:30pm – Head to bed

As someone who usually stays up waaay too late and normally has trouble falling asleep, I had *zero* problem getting to sleep after the physically and mentally demanding days at Ballymaloe. It was actually an incredible feeling to fall asleep knowing that I’d genuinely accomplished so. many. things. with my hands and my brain. What a dream!
