The Democratic Party of the United States has a long and contradictory history. In fact, these are several stories united by a common brand, the content of which has changed as the eras changed, along with which the party reinvented itself: in the 21st century, it is synonymous with liberalism, support for minorities and opposition to racist Republicans; but in the 19th century, the Democratic Party was the party of slave owners who fought for the preservation of slavery in the Civil War against more progressive Republicans. And between these two extremes, in the 20th century, for 60 years from Roosevelt to Reagan, the Democratic Party was, in fact, the American social democratic party — “far left” by today’s Democrat standards.

## 1968-1992: From FDR's Party to Clinton's Party

1.  The New Deal for the Democrats ended with Lyndon Johnson's presidency.
    
2.  Under the election of Jimmy Carter in 1976, the party pivoted to roll back New Deal reforms, introducing a neoliberal revenge of capitalism.
    
3.  The Democratic Party of Bill Clinton, elected in 1992, became a fully fledged neoliberal party.
    

Clinton's Democratic Party is today's Democratic Party, in which the pro-Roosevelt, pro-labor tendencies have been marginalized, in order to limit progressiveness only to the moral progressiveness of cultural policy, while economically the modus operandi of the party became "triangulation" - a constant movement to the right, toward the Republican position, in pursuit of an elusive imaginary political "center." A pursuit that reached its logical conclusion **in 2024, when the Democratic Party platform caught up with the Republican Party so much that it lost its voters in the pursuit.**

Clinton prescribed the same economic course for the USA that the Americans then advised post-Soviet Russia: a course for the restoration of pre-reform (for Russia — pre-revolutionary) capitalism, and with the same political results: as for Yeltsin’s “young reformers”, Clinton’s economy turned into a political catastrophe for the Democratic Party: the midterm elections of Bill Clinton's first term in 1994 resulted in the so-called Republican Revolution:

-   Having won an additional 54 seats, the Republicans gained a majority in the House of Representatives for the first time since 1952 (before that, the Democrats had controlled the House continuously since 1955, and with two two-year breaks since 1931),
    
-   regained control of the Senate, which they had lost in 1986,
    
-   increased by 10 (to 30) the number of states with Republican governors (the Democrats lost 10, to 19). It was in 1994 that Texas became a Republican state - when George W. Bush won the gubernatorial election.
    

The Democrats got a respite only after 12 years: first in 2006, due to Bush's unpopularity already as president, the Republicans lost their majority in Congress, and in 2008, the Democrats, led by Obama, won the White House, the majority in the lower chamber and a supermajority (60 seats) in the Senate.

This happened because Obama was running as a candidate of hope and change, whose leftist, populist rhetoric activated leftist activists and groups to participate politically on a scale that the left had not been involved in American politics since the 1960s. For the only time since 1992, the Democrats ran on their own positive agenda, daring to briefly release in Obama's campaign the leftist energy, otherwise securely blocked by the neoliberal establishment — and it brought them their biggest victory since 1992.

However, after winning the election, Obama and his team bottled up left-wing energy back, completely denying any left-wing participation in government, installing left-hating neoliberals and Wall Street lobbyists in all positions, and abandoning the populist campaign agenda[1](#footnote-1), returning to Clinton's pursuit of compromise with the Republicans instead.

In the 2008 financial crisis that erupted under Bush, Obama saved the bankers, not the homebuyers, allowing millions of mortgage holders to lose their homes, and used his supermajority in Congress to pass a conservative health care reform aimed at keeping private health insurers in business.

Following all that, in Obama's first midterms in 2010, the Democrats faced a second "Republican Revolution" - the Tea Party Revolution, suffering an even worse defeat than in Clinton's first midterms in 1994, and losing their majority in Congress, governorships in six states, falling back to the 1994 level (20 states for Democrats, 29 for Republicans), and state congressional seats - a total of more than 1,000 seats lost in one election. The Democratic base, energized by the 2008 campaign of hope and change and abandoned immediately after, simply did not show up.

After that, the Democratic Party never recovered, struggling and having varying success in the fight with the Republicans for the presidency, winning twice in 2012 and 2020 and losing twice in 2016 and 2024, and Congress — even as the Republicans increasingly became a minority party, winning presidential elections, Senate elections, and even House seats with fewer votes after 2000.

## Lessons from the Clinton Democratic Era

Clinton’s transformation of the Democratic Party from a social democratic, labor-friendly party to a bourgeois liberal party abruptly ended its dominance since 1931, turning it *from a party that Republicans could not defeat into a party that, after 1992, won only when Republicans failed big enough to create an opening for Democrats.*

Every major Democratic election victory since 1992 came in response to a massive Republican failures of governance: in 2006 and 2008 under Bush, in 2018 and 2020 under Trump.

**I. The political center is a shadow you cast, not a direction to follow.** There is no such thing as a political center in material reality, it is just the result of different political forces. In a two-party system, it is impossible to occupy the center, because there is no one to hold the left flank, and by shifting to the right, the Democrats just shifted the center between them and the Republicans to the right, and gave the Republicans room to move further to the right as well.

But the political spectrum does not stretch infinitely, ending on the left and right with known ideological extremes, and the Democrats' ill-conceived centrism had only one ending from the start:

-   it would facilitate the Republican shift to the right until they were entirely fascist, and
    
-   the Democrats would pursue compromise until they themselves became indistinguishable from the Republicans.
    

Chasing the shadow cast by their own deeds, the Democratic Party has lost itself.

**II. Strong victories with a large margin in elections are available to the Democratic Party when it is associated with a return to the social democratic agenda**, to the party of the working class and trade unions, as in 2008.

Whereas the neoliberal, centrist Democratic Party has only limited, fueled by Republican policy failures, with narrow margins, and unstable (lasting only an election cycle) victories available.

**III. The Democratic voter responds to the hopes given and the hopes betrayed by turnout.**

-   When the Democrats give the electorate hope, they respond with turnout that can deal the Republicans a crushing defeat;
    
-   when the Democrats promise nothing, they slowly cede ground to the Republicans;
    
-   when they offer hope and then fail to deliver, voter turnout drops, ensuring a crushing defeat for the Democrats in the next elections.
    

**IV. For the Clinton-Obama Democratic Party, a victory of the Democratic base is more dangerous than the Republican one.**

For the neoliberal party elite, fighting the Democratic base and the politicians who come from it is a fight for survival, and fighting the Republicans is a gentleman's sport.

-   Losing to the Republican Party means for the neoliberal Democratic establishment several years of fundraising off of the Republican threat without having to govern.
    
-   Losing to the Democratic base means for the neoliberal Democratic establishment losing the party.
    

This is why the Party's only possible response to every defeat since 1994 has been to blame the "far left" part of the base and move a little further to the economic right — further away from the base and the chances to expand it. By doing so, the Democratic Party depresses its voters, making it easier for the Republicans to win and pushing the Democratic Party to move even further to the right next time. This dynamic has cemented the Clinton’s triangulation as the Democratic Party's only modus operandi: moving to the right until the Republican Party becomes fascist and the Democratic Party becomes Republican.

**V. Demography is not destiny if you are picky about your voters.** In the battle with the left part of the Democratic base, the Democratic Party has defeated destiny itself: the "demography is destiny" principle on which the Democrats pinned their hopes for future victories has proven hollow against the deliberate, relentless eradication of any populist instinct within the party, and a shrinking base that left the Democrats without any way to reach potentially promising demographics.

### 2024: no room left to triangulate

By 2024, Democrats had so few economic differences with Republicans that they had resorted to flipping the culturally liberal issues at the core of their platform as economic issues in order to avoid addressing the surrender of their values, and took such a hawkish foreign policy stance that they allowed Donald Trump to position himself as a dove against Kamala Harris.

-   On immigration, the Democrats abandoned their traditionally left-wing policy, pretending to see it as an economic rather than a moral problem, and adopted the Republican fascist anti-immigrant platform instead. It didn't win them the right-wing voters for whom this pivot simply confirmed the Republican “crisis at the border” narrative, but it deprived the supporters of humane immigration policies, the former Democratic base, of representation in the 2024.
    
-   Biden/Harris' far-right stance on Israel and Palestine has also alienated significant Muslim/Arab voters and left-wing youth from the Democrats.
    
-   The Democrats' hawkish foreign policy on Israel, NATO, the war in Ukraine, and relations with Russia and Iran has given Trump the opportunity to outflank them on the left, rhetorically positioning himself as the dove and candidate of peace, and the Democrats and Harris as the party of war.
    

After 32 years of moving to the right, having broken away as far as possible from “the far-left”, and having alienated a significant part of their base, the Democrats led themselves into an electoral desert where there weren't enough votes for them to pick up: in 2024 Harris won 15 million fewer votes than Biden did in 2020—and that alone ensured victory for Trump and the Republican Party.

### The end of the neoliberal rope

Having lost Congress, the Electoral College, and the popular vote to the Republicans this time, in 2024 the Democrats did not experience a regular setback, but have reached the end of the neoliberal path.

1.  Besides abortion and support for LGBT people, Democrats have no more old principles to give up to further please the conservative electorate.
    
2.  Republicans can't get any more right-wing: they have already fully adopted ethnofascism, openly running in 2024 on a platform of mass ethnic deportations, in connection to which even the issue of denaturalization of American citizens is no longer out of question. They can only expand on it, for example by opposing no-fault divorce and women's suffrage, which would become a huge, yet another unearned gift to the Democratic Party. But it's not a given that the Democrats will even be able to use it given the current state of the Democratic base.
    
3.  As 2024 has shown, the Democrats have abandoned so many of the left-wing policies that opposed the Republicans that they have alienated a portion of the base large enough that the Republican presidential candidate can now win even the popular vote.
    
4.  And for all that, the conservative electorate showed no interest in switching to the Democratic Party, which by shifting to the right and adopting Republican positions only confirmed that they were always right to stick to the Republicans.
    

So far to the right, the Democrats where they no longer have enough electoral support to survive i.e. win elections and regain seats without changing course, leaving them with a choice for the next midterm (2026) and the next presidential (2028) elections:

1.  Either Democratic candidates are unable to win in the electoral desert, lose relevance and donor support, and without voters and money the party loses its meaning and significance to candidates, threatening the party's very existence.
    
2.  Or the party will have to reverse course for the first time in 32 years and begin to move back towards its former base, adopting left-wing, contrasting Republican policies, threatening the neoliberal establishment's control of the party.
    

There’s no “business as usual” for the Dems even in the most cynical terms of the self serving bureaucratic logic and the selfish interests of the party establishment.

### The coming betrayal

There is little doubt that the neoliberal establishment of the Democratic Party will try to continue the path that led them to lose their own electorate, and in an attempt to survive, they may come to direct collusion with the Republican Party or its donors, in exchange for surrendering pro-LGBT or pro-choice positions. The first signs of a move in this direction are the wave of criticism, thrown through the Democratic media, of the 2024 Democratic election platform for its non-existent wokeism. And it will still do nothing but dig the party deeper into a dead end, ensuring complete political dominance for the Republicans, which they will use to cement their monopoly position at the systemic level in a way that will turn the United States into an electoral dictatorship in which no one but the Republicans can win a majority without a real revolution.

But forcing candidates to abandon "far-left" ideas (which in the neoliberal era of the Democratic Party is considered not even democratic socialism, but ordinary, capitalist-based, European social democracy) worked as long as the party brought victories in elections in return - and could justify one thing with another. But if the party stops winning even against outright fascists and candidates with such baggage and anti-ratings as Trump - there is no point in listening to it.

### Either capitalism or liberal cultural values

Democratic politicians would do themselves a favor by recognizing what happened in 2024 for what it really was: the outcome and result of the Clintonian experiment in the Democratic Party: maintaining liberal moral political gains by surrendering leftist economics.

**There is no room in politics for such a thing as a gayer, pro-choice Republican Party.** The marriage of the cultural political gains of the 1960s with capitalism proved unsustainable. By voluntarily abandoning a leftist economic platform to become the party of cultural liberalism, the Democratic establishment has brought the party to the brink of survival, where now, just for a short extension of its power, it can surrender cultural liberalism too.

**To carry the weight of cultural liberalism on LGBT, race and women's issues requires a left economic platform.**

As the neoliberal era of the Democratic Party has clearly demonstrated, the only chance to successfully preserve and carry liberal cultural values in politics further over a historically significant distance is by adopting an economic platform as anti-capitalist as it takes to completely eradicate the threat of fascism.
