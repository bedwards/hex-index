I have always been incredibly conscious and intentional when deciding what to wear. More so pre-COVID, but I still do catch myself ruminating on what pair of trousers would go with this or that top, if I have the right jacket for it and what bag should drape over my shoulder. Each piece, its fabric, texture and every accessory is a layer of who I am, who I aspire to be and how I want others to perceive me. The way I dress is an expression of the zeitgeist we live in, the culture I am embedded in and the politics that run today.

Patterns in taste making constantly shifts in conjunction with evolving generational zeitgeists. At the tip of everyone’s tongue for the spirit of our times seems to be individualism. It’s an elusive concept because it’s hard to define and retrospectively in 10 years, you might look back and see the very evident similarities. The notion of individualism is packaged through acts like thrifting and finding pieces unique to you. Just look at the booming resale market with ‘second-hand fashion’ seeing an increase of 104% since last year.1 It’s really about the rejection of anything alluding to the word ‘mass’, I mean the literal meaning of the word speaks for itself. Who would want to be associated with ‘a large body of matter with no definite shape.’

Let me be clear that this nothing to do with the difference between fast fashion or luxury fashion, because you can still be part of the masses by wearing anything over a grand. Refer to everyone who ever bought into Balenciaga sock shoes. That’s sheep mentality right there, that’s Cardi-B in your ear, as the proverbial consumption devil on your shoulder singing ‘I like those Balenciagas’ convincing you to buy them.

Looking for ways to standout through vintage handbags and thrifted clothes goes so much deeper, beyond just being trendy or fashionable. If you want to ascribe a definition to it we’d call it enclothed cognition, a theory suggesting that ‘the relationship between our identity and the group identity symbolised by our clothes is a reciprocal one.’2 In this case, we embody the meaning of being individual through our unique finds that are exclusively ours. You want others to coo at you and take pride in the symbolism and smugness of saying ‘sorry it’s vintage.’ It’s knowing they probably won’t find it, not at least in the same colour or exact shade, so you can firmly stand a part from the rest. There is definitely a swelling tide of people wanting to set themselves apart. Just think of the workplace, right now I sense more and more people around me wanting to be ‘self-employed’ or a business owner. People are increasingly wanting to escape the corporate grind. Last year’s third quarter saw an 86% increase in new businesses, which is again some what a rejection of being part of a mass.3

It all feels like a strong response to the ‘sameness’ and homogeny popularised by social media and maybe even the humdrum monotony of life now. Whatever we’re feeling we’re externalising. Our lockdown uniform reflected that, primarily consisting of the same pair of sweatpants. Even the rise of ‘regencycore’ feels like a reaction to the aforementioned sameness. The appetite for regency-era inspired clothing after the release of Netflix’s Bridgerton, a period drama set in 1813 saw a 123% increase in searches for corsets and a 93% increase for empire line dresses.4 I mean if we can’t look forward to the future then why not romanticise and admire the simplicities and styles of the past? Why not imagine yourself an Elizabeth Bennett or better yet emulate the late Princess Diana?

While the tide seems to shift towards individualism in the ways that we dress, the notion of clothing representing or influencing our behaviour really isn’t something new. In our need to understand the world as ‘a meaningful cosmos’, we have partly, used our clothing to narrate and influence our modern human experiences and define ourselves. The red heel of the shoes worn by Louis XIV of France symbolised royalty, authority, richness and power. The Ralph Lauren polo shirts worn by Mexican youths mimicking violent drug barons signified the aspirational crisis that deeply pervaded Mexican society.

I know tonight I will think meticulously about what I’ll be wearing tomorrow. I’m sure I’ll grab one of my vintage bags and I will consciously think of the influence that these symbols have on my identity, my behaviour and the external forces that are guiding how I might feel. I also hope I might stand-out in the crowd embody that sense of individualism we’re all seeking.
