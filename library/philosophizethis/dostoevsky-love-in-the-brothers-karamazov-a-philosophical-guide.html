So today we’re going to be talking about the book The Brothers Karamazov.

Which means among other things we’re going to be talking today about the faith of Dostoevsky.

Faith was a big part of how he lived his life. And if you listen to the people that have spent years studying his work, they often describe his view of faith as a kind of existential, tragic form of Christianity.

Now, to a modern person who just reads that it can sound on the surface like it’s an oxymoron.

Tragic Christianity? Existential questions? Isn’t Christianity the thing you believe in so you don’t have to be in agony with existential dread every day?

Someone could even say when it comes to how most people seem to be using this Christianity thing: Jesus was basically a fidget spinner that had 12 disciples following him around.

How can that ever be anything but something that helps people cope with a painful reality they can’t fully affirm?

The first thing we have to do if we want to understand where he’s coming from with his faith, is to make sure we’re not reducing Christianity, and all of religion for that matter, to just a superficial, lazy kind of religion that gives people easy answers.

There may be plenty of people out there you can find that use religion like this, but again, Dostoevsky, and many others, are not this kind of Christian.

In fact, if you remember the episode we just did before we started this series where we talked about Keiji Nishitani and his views on the value of a deeply religious quest, the truth is that there’s a similarity to the ways that Nishitani and Dostoevsky each viewed religion.

Dostoevsky thought that Russian Orthodox Christianity was not some sacred institution that people should blindly commit themselves to. More than anything, he saw an organized religious approach like this as something that gave people a language they could use to navigate the purpose they served in a larger network of being.

And when someone truly walks that kind of religious path, to him it just ends up being something very tragic and existential. It was an existence that at times was absolutely miserable for him, but then at other times it was deeply beautiful. It was a range of experience that he no doubt sought to emulate in the complexity of his novels.

It’s worth just basking for a second in the level of honesty that this man tried to live in.

The level to which he affirmed everything as much as he could. The good, the bad, the mysterious; the level to which he tried to understand the different psychological framings of the world that other people lived in, and how much effort he put in to never idealize or demonize them just so he had an easy way to file them neatly up in his own head.

This is a level of affirmation about the world that can be really tough to relate to when someone tries to just jump in and read his books off the shelf not knowing the kind of man he was.

People will read his books like The Brothers Karamazov, which as we’ll see is an enormous commentary on so many aspects of human existence, and they’ll try to turn it into the kind of idealized movie they’re used to watching in our modern world.

They’ll say this book is a battle between good and evil. That this is a debate that’s going on between Ivan’s perspective and Alyosha’s perspective. And that the reader's job is just to pay attention and find out which one Dostoevsky thinks is the correct perspective to have.

But this was not at all what he was going for as he wrote it.

We’ll talk more about it here in a minute but what needs to be said right now is that faith (like love to Dostoevsky) is not a noun. Faith is a verb.

And to say something like, “Dostoevsky’s faith mattered to him a lot,” and to think that’s the same as saying, “Dostoevsky's Christianity mattered to him a lot,” – well, by the end of this post we’ll understand why this misses a big piece of how he saw faith as an active process, and not a doctrine to believe in.

What we’ll also see is how he thought that multiple, different framings of reality might be necessary if we ever want to engage with our existence more fully than any one of these characters do in the book. That any single framing of our existence will always run into its built-in limitations.

Anyway, the plot of The Brothers Karamazov, this is going to be shocking I know, but it centers around the Karamazov family.

Another shock will be that a significant portion of the book is dedicated to the three brothers of the family: Dimitri, Ivan, and Alyosha.

But then more than just the brothers, the family also includes another central character of this book, the father of these three boys whose name is Fyodor.

Now Fyodor was obviously Dostoevsky’s own name in real life, but this character he wrote into the book wasn’t meant to be symbolic of himself. In fact, in a letter he said that what he meant for Fyodor to symbolize was a piece of something that’s inside of all of us to an extent.

He thought we’re all a little like this Fyodor guy because at least when it comes to the way he treats his family, he regularly comes up short in ways that are completely disappointing to them. Most people can relate to this feeling if we’re being honest, I think.

For example, Fyodor doesn’t really know any of these sons that we just mentioned. At least not very closely. Certainly doesn’t know them like you’d expect a father to know their sons.

Dmitri, his oldest son, is from his first marriage. Ivan and Alyosha are sons that are from his second marriage.

And when they were children, Fyodor would often just leave them to be taken care of by someone else. He’d go out, have a night on the town, instead of caring for the kids he’d do all the stuff he really wanted to be doing with his time. Even if he was around, most of the time he’d just outright neglect the kids.

But now that the three brothers have grown up and become adults, things haven’t gotten any better. Life is still pretty chaotic between the Karamazov family.

For example, Fyodor is in a love triangle with one of his sons and another woman named Grushenka. That’s one way to bond with your kids, I guess.

He gets verbally abusive with them on a regular basis in the book, gets into scuffles with them. He gets drunk all the time. He’s the kind of guy that you’ll be out with, he’ll be walking down the street, half in the bag, and he’ll just yell at some stranger that he doesn’t even know that’s on the side of the road.

He manipulates the people who are close to him to get what he wants. He abuses the people close to him when they’re inconvenient to him.

And the point of Dostoevsky presenting a father in this way is more than him just wanting a manipulative narcissist in the book to add a little flavor.

Technically the people that Dostoevsky is critiquing with this character are a group of what he saw as self-indulgent landowners that were growing in number during the 19th century in Russia when he was living.

But this criticism applies more generally for him to the growing number of people in our modern societies that are so focused on themselves all the time.

The self-centeredness of people that think almost entirely in terms of their own achievements and projects has led to what he thinks is a considerable decline in the focus people choose to put on family relationships, like the ones between father and son that he lays out in this book so much.

Part of his argument here is that if you wanted to find out what’s wrong in a particular society that’s having problems, one place to start is to look at the relationships between the members of an average family in that society.

And not only is looking at family relationships going to be the perfect site for Dostoevsky to explore some of the darkest and most wonderful things that we can do to the people we’re close to, but this also becomes a perfect way to illustrate one of the big macro-level points that he wants to make in this book about how to philosophically frame the world, and that is: that we should think of the family as a microcosm of society at large.

It’s common for people to want to make a positive impact on the world around them. The difficult question to answer becomes, “How do I actually do that in a way that isn’t just me cosplaying as someone that wants to have a positive impact?”

Someone might think, “Well I live in a democracy. To make a difference in a democracy means I have to put in the work and become an informed voter. Which means I have to watch the news and consume content that makes me an informed voter.”

“For two hours every day on the way to work, when I’m eating, whenever I get a little free time; I will do the work to keep up on the events that are going on politically. I do this not because it’s fun, but because it’s what it takes to make this world a better place.”

What often goes along with this kind of thinking is that if you’re somebody out there that’s not keeping up with all the stuff that’s going on in the news every day, then you must be an irresponsible person. That to care more about your video games and your TV shows than you do about taking responsibility for the world you live in makes you somebody that is part of the problem, not the solution.

But Dostoevsky looks at things in a very different way than this.

He’d no doubt appreciate this person’s theoretical concern for all the people around them. But he’d also think that engagement at this theoretical level is not the most enduring way of improving the world if that is, in fact, what they are going for.

For example, when it comes to those two hours you spend every day watching a screen, getting educated on what’s happening in the world. Let’s ignore the criticism that you aren’t really getting an education there. That this is really just someone indulging in entertainment like most people do in the modern world. Let’s say the news is actually getting to the bottom of what’s happening at the political level.

Then do a comparison of the two hours of someone’s day that they spend watching content, and all the good they’re doing every couple years or so when they can be one vote of millions, and now imagine that same two hours every day where they instead reflected on all the ways they’re needed in the world immediately around them, the roles they serve right now that they’re always, already performing, and now imagine them spending that two hours every day genuinely applying themselves to being better at what is needed from them by the people and things around them.

Some questions to ask: which one of those two are you more familiar with? Which one of those two are you more capable of actually changing? Or qualified to know?

You could say that when it comes to knowing how you’re needed by the things around you, especially when it comes to the people, there may be no one that is more qualified for that task than you are. You are in a special position to be there for them.

And Dostoevsky might say, imagine dedicating two hours every single day to doing just that. How would your life change? How would the lives of the people around you change?

To not only be able to spend that time reflecting on how you fit into this particular life you’re living, trying to understand things more on their own home ground. But then to be able to spot weak points in your abilities, where you might want to use this time every day to develop the skills that’ll allow you to be there better for this network you’re a part of.

And the point is not that Dostoevsky would ever think we should be structuring our lives around trying to save the world anyway.

But if you are one of these people that has making the world a better place as a priority in your life; how many of society’s problems wouldn’t be problems if more people lived in this way? We would have strong, qualified people dedicating the time every day (moral people, you could say) and these would be people that would intervene before many of these issues became larger problems.

The question for Dostoevsky is, if you consider yourself a revolutionary and you want to change the world for the better, which one of these two is more revolutionary?

There are of course moments in history where it’s important to take to the streets and act politically, but consider how easy it is to fall into the trap of waving a sign around not really changing anyone’s mind about anything, especially in the type of societies we live in that promote self-centeredness and alienate us from each other.

Much like we talked about in the recent post we did on his book Demons, think of how easy it is to believe you’re doing things for the sake of saving the world, when you’re really just an ego that’s turning all of humanity into a theoretical abstraction so that you can try to control it.

Now there’s a lot of things you could say back to this idea that’s been presented so far.

Let’s pause here for a second though, because I think his point becomes a much stronger one when we consider it next to an important individual section of this book. It’s come to be known as The Grand Inquisitor.

The Grand Inquisitor may be one of the most famous parables in all of classic literature.

As a section in this book, it was written by Dostoevsky in a way where you can read it completely on its own and it still makes total sense. He did this on purpose.

In fact, there’s a lot of people who hear about The Grand Inquisitor section of this book first, and then only after reading it will they then get turned onto The Brothers Karamazov as a book more broadly. That’s how good it is!

Anyway, here’s how it goes though. One of those three sons that Fyodor doesn’t know much about is named Ivan. Ivan is the classic example of a highly educated, rational, skeptical kind of person who’s a bit tortured by living in a world that he’s so good at critiquing.

See, Ivan’s the kind of person that has a hard time believing in any religious stuff that gets sent his way, though he does try to respect what it’s saying and he takes it very seriously. He certainly doesn’t think it’s all simply a bunch of nonsense.

Ivan also is someone that looks around him at the world and sees the suffering of untold numbers of innocent people with seemingly no end in sight.

So feeling the frustration that comes along with witnessing this kind of stuff, he tells this story in the book, called The Grand Inquisitor, as a way to put into words what he thinks is going on with these religious institutions and all the suffering in the world.

He tells it to his younger brother Alyosha in the book.

And real quick before we get into it, what makes this poem so interesting is that it’s not just saying: look at all the suffering in the world. Therefore, it makes no sense for a God to exist.

No, what Ivan’s saying is something that’s in a way far more radical than that. The argument he gives is sometimes called an “anti-theodicy” by scholars that study this section.

What does that mean? The word theodicy, if you just break down the different parts of the word, literally means justification of God.

And throughout history there have been a lot of attempts by thinkers to justify God.

One of the most common things that people try to justify, especially when it comes to the Abrahamic religions, is how a loving God could ever exist and still allow for a horrific amount of completely unnecessary suffering that’s going on at scale.

Well again, Ivan’s not saying you shouldn’t believe in God because this suffering can’t be rationally justified. He’s saying: even if this God does exist, this isn’t a God that is worth honoring anyway. Doesn’t matter if you can rationally justify it with a theodicy, because any God that allows for this kind of world to exist should just be rejected.

The Grand Inquisitor delivers this argument as it gets recited by Ivan in story form.

In the story he tells Jesus comes back to earth. It’s the famous second coming of Christ we’ve all been waiting for, hooray! And where does Jesus choose to come back to when he decides to do it?

Well, 16th-century Spain of all places, right in the middle of the Spanish Inquisition.

Almost immediately he shows up among the people and starts performing miracles on the side of the street like he’s David Blaine. But this time when he’s doing the miracles he doesn’t get an entourage of followers; he just gets arrested when he does it.

Not long after he gets arrested he’s interrogated by a really forceful, high-level clergyman that people in the story call the Grand Inquisitor.

The Grand Inquisitor looks at Jesus as he’s interrogating him and says: you know what, I believe you. You’re the son of God. And since you’re the representative that’s available here on behalf of the creator of the universe, I have to tell you something that’s been bugging me: you absolutely failed when you created this world when it comes to the concept of human freedom.

Because, he tells him, it’s the possibility of human freedom that leads to all the evil that goes on in the world.

Here’s the process: people are born, they’re able to make choices, they inevitably are tempted by evil, then they have the freedom to choose that evil sometimes, and then innocent people by the billions, including children who have no frame of reference for any of this stuff, suffer unnecessarily.

Look, you chose for the universe to be this way, Jesus. And the Grand Inquisitor tells him because of this completely broken setup, the church had no choice but to step in over the years and correct for your big mistake there.

He says the church realized long ago that people can either have freedom or they can have happiness. You can’t ever have both.

And when it comes down to it, human beings don’t actually want freedom as much as they say they do.

People just want full bellies, he says. They want happiness and peace.

And the way you ensure that is not by giving them freedom, but by giving them authority and miracles and mystery. You give them a doctrine to live by with strict consequences if they don’t, and when they obey a power greater than themselves like that, it removes the whole freedom thing from the equation and ends up producing a human being that is actually happy.

The bottom line is this for the Grand Inquisitor: institutionalized religion, the church, is an enormous lie, but it is a noble lie, it’s a necessary lie. People are better off living with this lie. Just ask any blindly religious person how great it is to be them every day. They really think they have it all figured out!

And how can Jesus, of all people, sit there with a straight face and not wail in agony over how unjustifiable all this suffering really is? He can’t justify it. In fact, go ahead Jesus, just try to give me your argument back to all this stuff I just said!

At which point Jesus, staying quiet during this entire speech, listens patiently. And when the Grand Inquisitor finishes, he stands up, walks over to him, kisses him on the mouth, and then walks away. A move that Dostoevsky thinks is a complete and powerful counter-perspective to everything that the Grand Inquisitor has just said.

Now how does kissing the guy make any sense at all as a counter argument?

There’s a lot here to examine. See, it’s a very specific move that Dostoevsky’s going for when he has Jesus not say anything, but just get up and kiss the Grand Inquisitor.

When Jesus doesn’t say anything, it’s not like he’s just being nice to him. This isn’t Jesus having mercy and not wanting to embarrass the Grand Inquisitor in front of his friends.

No, the point is that he’s not even going to argue with the Grand Inquisitor, because argument is something that is always limited to the theoretical, abstract framing of events in the world, which is incapable of even receiving the response that Jesus is giving here.

Jesus is looking at the world through a completely different kind of experiential framing than the Grand Inquisitor is. And by kissing him it underscores the built-in limitations to the way the Grand Inquisitor is trying to frame, supposedly, all of human existence.

Now if that sounds confusing let me try to clear it up. The good news is we’ve been talking about the possibility of this pre-theoretical framing of our reality a bunch lately on these posts. I’m talking about the more phenomenological, religious, embodied framing of our reality.

We’ve talked about Heidegger’s Dasein and the immediacy of experience in that regard. We’ve talked about his later work with his concept of letting be and its connection to the practice of attention in the work of Simone Weil.

We’ve talked about the great mystics who, throughout history, by a process of self-emptying, have tried to get to communion with something greater than themselves that they are connected to. We’ve talked about Nishitani and how he thinks most of Western philosophy throughout history has gone on in the experiential framing of consciousness, and that he thinks it’s deeply lacking in these other framings of our experience that he calls Nihility and Sunyata.

Well look, this is part of why Nishitani saw Dostoevsky as such an exceptional thinker from the Western tradition. He seems to be going deeper in his work into the value of these other framings that are outside of mere theoretical abstractions. Most thinkers in the West never do this.

See, there’s nothing wrong to Dostoevsky with arguing or with framing the world in terms of abstractions while trying to get to a better understanding of it. We need to do this kind of stuff.

But what he would say is: we are always limiting ourselves whenever we make the mistake of believing that we are accessing everything to human experience simply by coming up with abstract definitions of things.

To only have grandiose conversations about human life as a defined thing and human suffering as a defined thing, is to always be limiting yourself to getting a fuller understanding of the thing.

It’s a similar move to the one that we saw from Nishitani a few decades later. The move he makes with nihilism in his book The Self-Overcoming of Nihilism that we talked about.

He says that what many Western thinkers have typically done is they look at a concept like nihilism and they’ll approach it merely from the abstract, theoretical framing.

They’ll come up with definitions as to what nihilism is. They’ll look at tons of different examples of nihilism throughout history. They’ll find similarities and differences between them, and then they’ll argue with each other and try to refine their definitions to capture the essence of what nihilism is.

But Nishitani points out that while this is one piece of nihilism for sure, nihilism is just as much an experienced, embodied reality for a subject that is going through a nihilistic experience.

His point was if we want to take nihilism as seriously as we can, we have to honor this phenomenological framing of it, and not just stop short with having arguments about nihilism as simply an abstract concept.

Well notice the similarity there to the Grand Inquisitor and his broad, abstract arguments about what human life is or whether suffering makes sense or not.

One of the big points for Dostoevsky is this: there’s a way to talk about suffering where it’s part of an abstract rational framing of the world.

For example, there’s a tsunami that takes out 10,000 people and they’re suffering. Or there’s a deer out in the woods somewhere whose leg gets crushed by a giant rock. There’s a way to have a conversation about suffering where the goal is to work out a way to rationalize it in some giant utilitarian calculus you’re doing on the level of the universe.

But there’s another way to talk about suffering from this more pre-theoretical framing, where we’re simply trying to affirm the universe as it is. Where we’re not trying to demonize suffering. We’re not trying to idealize suffering. And when we just experience suffering as an aspect of our existence, suffering in another real sense just is a part of the life of the types of creatures that we are.

In other words, far from telling these people that they shouldn’t be trying to rationalize suffering on these massive scales, the other question for Dostoevsky that often gets ignored is: how do we find a way to authentically experience a world where suffering is always something that’s present in it?

Well, it’s certainly not by rationalizing it away. And again it’s certainly not by idealizing it or demonizing it.

The answer for Dostoevsky is going to be faith, or to strive through the difficulty of learning to actively love the world, and affirm our existence unconditionally.

Much more on that framing of faith and love here in a second, but first a couple loose ends to tie up here that are interesting.

Notice how this isn’t an argument that’s trying to refute the idea that suffering is unjustifiable. If Dostoevsky did that, then that would concede the point that suffering can be rationalized away, and it would turn the book into something more along the lines of a theodicy.

But remember, he’s not making a theodicy here.

For Dostoevsky there isn’t a rational justification for the suffering in the world. In fact, he acknowledges the complexity here and the validity of this other framing.

He would absolutely say that the Grand Inquisitor makes some really valid arguments in his pitch to Jesus. But again the real point for Dostoevsky that he wants us to understand here is: these are just arguments.

In fact he makes this point in the book at multiple different places. There’s a character named Father Zosima who’s a religious man who represents one of the voices of the saint in The Brothers Karamazov. He has a really great conversation with a woman named Madame Koklakov along these same lines.

She tells him about her own existential struggle she’s going through. How she’s really bothered by the question of what happens after you die. And similar to Ivan, she’s also really bothered by all the suffering she sees in the world, and she wishes she could do more about it.

And Father Zosima says back to her when they’re talking: look, I hear you. He validates what she’s saying and says that he’s also felt the way that she does. He says these problems can be some of the hardest things to try to reconcile when you’re living mostly in this way of framing things.

But he says to her: I can’t offer you any words or an explanation that will rationalize this stuff away for you. The only thing I can offer you, he says, as a man that is living a deeply religious life, is to say that when you approach the world more from the experiential framing of love instead, you don’t get a rational explanation for why all this suffering exists or for what happens after you die.

But from that framing of love, the presence of suffering or the mystery of death, requires less of an explanation. Because suffering becomes a reality to be experienced, not just an intellectual problem to be solved.

Now this is also where we benefit from having read the other four of his novels that we’ve covered so far.

Because we know by this point that when a saint figure in one of his books is talking about love, they are not talking about a superficial kind of love.

To say I’m going to live life in a more loving way towards the people and things around me doesn’t mean that you’re going to go out and start doing more nice things for people. Compliment people more. Hold the door open for them.

No, loving the world around you is not becoming a cartoonishly nice person.

Active love, like the kind that Father Zosima is talking about in the book and the kind of love symbolized by Jesus when he kisses the Grand Inquisitor, is a way of framing our experience that tries to affirm everything about it, as unconditionally as we can.

So, the Grand Inquisitor in the story is not stupid for the arguments he’s making. He’s not even wrong. He’s just framing reality in an incomplete way. One that has blind spots.

Jesus responds to this person in the story with love by modeling the exact counterpoint that the Grand Inquisitor needs from him in this moment.

In other words, this moment isn’t about Jesus to Jesus. He’s considering how he is needed most, and how he can best fulfill that role in the moment. Kind of like what Dostoevsky was saying earlier about how we can choose to be towards our family.

And think of how this kind of love applies to an actual situation you might find yourself in.

Whatever’s going on in your life; whether it’s something with your best friend, your child, even something like the local swamp that’s got a lot of trash inside of it and you want to clean it up. The point is to see these things not in some idealized way where we think about how they should be all the time.

“The swamp should be clean! My child should be better at math! My best friend should be better at knowing what to say here!” Or more ambitiously, “The universe should have a plan that all this suffering is a part of!”

It’s easy to make this move when we frame things purely in terms of abstractions and try to control them. But to love existence is also to affirm things as they are. To see things as they are. To experience things on their own home ground.

Love is the orientation of care we have towards this world when we truly seek to understand it on its own terms.

And if you’re Dostoevsky: just saying that is one thing. But if you wanted to actually live more in this way, it’s clear that he thought this was not going to be an easy thing for someone to just decide they’re going to start doing one day. You don’t just flip a switch on and all of a sudden you’re loving everything.

Turns out it’s a very painful thing at times to love the world around you as it is.

Things are often frustrating, and people are often disappointing. More than that, affirming these things as legitimate pieces of a network that you’re embedded in often isn’t something that comes with a lot of credit that you get for doing it.

For example, when you choose to be patient towards someone close to you in a moment that they’re struggling in. That kind of love is often something that goes on behind the scenes.

It’s something most people don’t even notice is happening. Many people are ungrateful for the love they do receive; it’s something they only notice years later, if they even take the time to do the reflection required to see it.

And again, in the type of societies we live in that incentivize getting recognition for things, where to be visible and seen is what validates your existence, Dostoevsky would no doubt see this as a unique challenge for someone to have to face in this particular culture we live in.

Now, notice the similarities here to the shift in experiential framing that we heard about from Keiji Nishitani. Where an important piece of what an authentic religious quest is always aiming for is this shift in perspective from thinking: how do the things in the world serve me, to for what purpose do I exist in this larger network.

The similarities here to this active love that’s being dramatized by Dostoevsky are obvious.

Because when you do the work to see the world more in this way, Dostoevsky says that things like compassion, and patience, and forgiveness become things that aren’t some moral performance you’re putting on every day because you know they’re the right things to do.

They start to become the genuine way that you orient yourself towards everything around you, because you increasingly see how everything around you is a part of you. It’s co-constituting you.

The illusion of separation that’s so common in our modern societies gets lifted. And then at that point, to purposefully harm something else around you would be the equivalent of stabbing your left hand with a fork so that your right hand can get a piece of chicken. Harming something around you is ultimately just you harming yourself in this framing.

See the Grand Inquisitor was right in a way: it may be that human freedom is something that leads to more suffering in the world than there may otherwise be.

But Dostoevsky would say that freedom is both the cause of suffering as well as the only way to love, or to have faith, or to gain any possible enduring connection to the world around us.

After all, someone can’t force me to affirm something about the world. It has to come from an authentic place of freedom. Without that, if I’m just forced to do it by a church or something, it just becomes me following orders.

Imagine being commanded by someone else to love something. It doesn’t work like that.

And that’s part of why Dostoevsky puts family relationships on display so much in this book. Weathering the frustration that you’re no doubt going to face when trying to love something like this; that’s something that is no doubt going to be easier when you practice with your immediate family, friends, and the things you know best.

Point is: it’s going to be difficult. And someone who has never had to face difficult realities, or has had the privilege to live in an idealistic framing of the world for most of their life, is likely someone uneducated on the limits of what a human experience can be.

It’s in this way that love is described by Father Zosima in the book as a kind of teacher.

Love is a gateway into this more immediate connection to being we’ve been talking about in this larger series on religious phenomenology.

And to Dostoevsky, it’s a particularly beautiful one of these gateways because love really is something that’s available to almost anyone, regardless of their lifestyle.

Now on that note, one thing that needs to be underscored when I say this is that love is not just a teacher. The active part of active love is very important here to recognize as well. And the kind of commitment that active love requires is on the level of something that to Dostoevsky really only makes sense to describe as faith.

There’s a history of talking about faith and reason as things that are mutually exclusive. There’s also a long history in the West of breaking things down in very dualistic ways where we say there’s a doctrine called reason, and a doctrine called faith, and one of these doctrines has to be superior to the other.

But to Dostoevsky, doing anything meaningful in the world always requires an act of faith. It requires us to recognize that we don’t know everything all the time. And more than that, that it’s okay to not know. We shouldn’t have to have perfect information to be able to make a decision in our lives.

The real challenge for him lies in sticking to the path you’ve chosen when you’re inevitably going to run into things that are frustrating.

Faith is the commitment that’s required to live in a framing like this long enough to uncover what it has to show to us.

But because of how difficult doing this is in practice, it is a faithful commitment specifically to active love that he thinks is crucial.

And for whatever it’s worth this is the framing he thinks is most embodied by the character of Alyosha in the book. Though it’s clear he had a lot more he wanted to expand on in this area. He had plans to write a second part of The Brothers Karamazov following mostly Alyosha, but he died before he could write it.

Now it’s clear by the end of the book that there are no winners here for Dostoevsky.

I mean it’s clear he thinks the message of Father Zosima and his student Alyosha is what you’ve most likely missed in your life in the modern world if you’re coming to this book.

But the idea that Ivan has been destroyed here and is totally wrong; that’s just too simple of a take.

Remember, with an entire career behind him of writing in characters like Raskolnikov or the Underground Man, he could have made Ivan into somebody that was a complete moral failure if he wanted to.

But he didn’t. No, in fact he purposefully makes Ivan in this book a very respectful, thoughtful version of somebody over indexed on a rational framing of the world. It’s clear he saw a lot of value in what Ivan was bringing to these conversations.

And someone can say back to all this, “Okay, but which one did he really believe in though? You can’t live in both doubt and in faith simultaneously. It’s a paradox. He had to choose one or the other.”

But is that true? Is doubt never a part of living in faith? Or is faith as Dostoevsky understood it, precisely that iterative battle that goes on every day against the possibility of doubt to serve a higher purpose?

Dostoevsky once said that he only arrived at faith very late in his life, and he says he got there through what he called a furnace of doubt. That was the actual word he used.

Couldn’t it genuinely be that some days he felt like Ivan and other days he felt like Alyosha?

Can’t faith and commitment be stronger some days more than others? And can’t doubt and argument be more needed on some days than others?
