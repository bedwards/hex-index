Hello, everyone! I’m Stephen West. This is Philosophize This!

So today we’re talking about the book Demons, another one of the five great novels of Dostoevsky.

And as usual, apologize for the slight redundancy at the beginning of these posts, but this will be covering the philosophical themes of the book. This isn’t intended to be a replacement for reading the book.

I just feel the need to clear that up every time I write a post like this.

Another important thing to mention is that this is now the third book from Dostoevsky that we’ve covered. So, Notes from the Underground and Crime and Punishment will be referenced throughout this entire post. Just a heads-up.

With that said, how do you begin to describe a book like Demons, one of the most complex books that Dostoevsky ever wrote in his lifetime? I mean, there’s a lot to this book; it’s about 750 pages long and full of symbolism.

And, for what it’s worth, it’s also the book Nietzsche stumbled upon in a bookstore that made him a fan of Dostoevsky as a thinker.

It might be best to start this way: if you read my last post on Crime and Punishment, you’ll know that while it may look like one kind of book on the surface, it’s actually something deeper than that for Dostoevsky.

Crime and Punishment kind of masquerades as a book about a guy that murders a couple of people, but the true drama of the book is in his internal experience and his slow, painful coming to terms with his relationship to something greater than himself. And further, it’s about him finding a way to consent to that fact.

Well, if that’s what Crime and Punishment is, then Demons is a book that masquerades as a political novel. It’s a book that seems to be about a group of revolutionaries, a bunch of people upset about the state of Russian society. It shows them planning and executing acts of political violence to ultimately bring about revolution.

That may be what the book looks like.

But actually, to Dostoevsky, it’s more about (and he made a big point about this in a personal letter he wrote later to a friend) exploring the thing he maybe wrestled with most in his entire lifetime: the belief, or non-belief, in the existence of God.

The way this connects to the political philosophy in Demons will make a lot more sense by the end of this post.

See, if part of the beauty of Crime and Punishment was that it went deep into the emotional experience of someone like Raskolnikov, and if the book never idealized him as a good or bad person. If it instead revealed how many moving parts there are to his thinking, and if it did this on an individual scale (as well as Notes from the Underground), then Demons is where Dostoevsky tries to show an example of this on a larger scale.

He shows the chaos that often comes along with collective psychology.

That ideas, when they find expression in the world on this scale, are particularly dangerous.

To understand what I mean by this, all you have to do is consider the entire first part of Demons.

The book is separated into three large parts. Part One has become something of a legend in literature because it confuses basically everyone who jumps into it and just tries to start reading.

Because if you just opened the book and started reading, you’d see it introduces the reader to about ten different characters, and all of them are pretty complex in their own unique ways.

Most of the first part of the book is made up of arguments between people on a giant estate that’s owned by a woman named Varvara.

Varvara is the employer of a very smart, highly respected intellectual by the name of Stepan. She pays this guy to be a tutor and to come over to her house to teach her son from time to time.

Stepan has a son named Pyotr, who’s going to be the leader of this little revolutionary cell we’ve already mentioned. Pyotr wants to use Varvara’s son, whose name is Stavrogin, as the face of this revolutionary movement he wants to kick off.

Stavrogin is apparently very handsome and articulate. If you needed a messiah-like figure that people can rally behind then this Stavrogin guy is exactly the sort of person you would want to pick.

Now, while these four characters are all going back and forth about their own complex business; Varvara tries to get Stepan to marry someone who’ll help her out politically, suddenly, and very randomly, a disabled woman named Marya appears in the room.

And just by listening to her speak, we can tell that she clearly has some kind of connection to Stavrogin (Varvara’s son). But everybody denies that anything is going on between them, so the reader is mostly just left to wonder about it.

Then a drunken captain enters the room, staggering around.

It turns out he’s Marya’s brother, and he starts yammering on about all his own stuff.

Then we swap over to another conversation entirely, where a few other characters we just met are all in their own conflicts with each of these original characters we just mentioned.

And Dostoevsky, at no point throughout this entire scene (which goes on for many, many pages), ever lays out any of the details of these characters in a straightforward way.

He never introduces them fully. He never tells you exactly who they are. And this whole conversation references tons of events that have obviously happened before the book even started.

So the characters seem to know each other, but the reader doesn’t.

The reader is just left to infer certain things and try to piece together the best picture they can with the information they have.

Part One ends with Stavrogin getting punched in the face by another character named Shatov, who, in that moment, needed to let him know that while he used to respect him, he doesn’t anymore.

The punch is absolutely necessary.

The whole first part of this book can seem like a totally dramatic, confusing, and chaotic mess to be in the middle of while you’re reading it. But this is actually exactly what Dostoevsky was going for as an author when he was writing it.

But why would any writer want to make a reader feel a little disoriented like this?

Well, it can only be understood if you know the philosophy Dostoevsky was putting on display over the course of these first couple of hundred pages.

Every single one of these characters is a deep reference to a real person in Russian culture at the time.

Sometimes it's a blend of a couple different people, and sometimes it just embodies a common type of person. But this whole opening part of the book has been described as a kind of encyclopedia of the ideas that were in conflict during this period of Russian history.

And by the way: if the whole scene feels a bit chaotic or disorienting to you, well, to Dostoevsky, isn’t that sometimes exactly what it feels like when we try to understand the state of the ideas that make up the world we live in?

Collective psychology is chaotic and disorienting at times.

We’re born into a world where conversations have been going on politically and philosophically long before we were ever here. We just get dropped into the middle of it without having perfect information about all the complex characters and their histories.

And unless you’re willing to become a political moron and sacrifice your self-respect to some ideology, then we’re forced, as honest people, to do the best we can.

We’re forced to feel a bit disoriented and try to piece together the most educated picture of what’s going on.

Every character in Demons represents something or someone in the political or philosophical space that Dostoevsky was living in at the time.

The good news is you don’t need to be a Russian historian to relate to the people he’s talking about here. It turns out that 1870s Russia has some surprising similarities to 2025.

For example, Varvara (the wealthy owner of the estate) represents for Dostoevsky the existing aristocratic order that’s still in power in Russia at the time. But you may have seen somebody in your own time who resembles the kind of role she plays.

Because regardless of the time period, she represents the kind of government or cultural elite that has the power to choose and fund certain intellectuals over others. The fund the kinds of people that they think are going to save the world for them and keep them in power.

And to Dostoevsky, these elites often do this without understanding the full consequences of where the ideas they’re supporting will eventually lead society.

When it comes to Stepan, the highly respected intellectual who’s been tutoring her son (and the one she’s trying to get to marry someone else for the sake of her political benefit), he’s going to represent the kind of intellectual who’s bought and paid for.

And during Dostoevsky’s time, this was unmistakably the Western liberal intelligentsia who were spreading from Europe and dominating Russian thought.

This is the type of intellectual who thinks we should be moving away from people having a religious connection to the world and moving more towards things like rational utilitarianism.

It’s those who believe rational utilitarianism is a better way for people to organize their lives or their relationships. That it’s a better way for us to plan how we coordinate society more generally.

Well, this is a fitting description of the character of Stepan in the book.

And following on from the last couple of posts, Dostoevsky might want to weigh in on the discussion when it comes to collective psychology.

He might point out that when utilitarianism becomes the primary way we frame our reality, and you couple that with a decline in people’s religious connection to the things around them, it certainly leads to great results in some areas.

For example, it can definitely reduce the infant mortality rate, it can make electricity cheaper and more widely available; it’s great at making iPhones for people to read Substack posts on.

In fact, it’s so effective at doing these things, you might think it’s capable of predicting and coordinating every problem a human being could ever possibly run into in their lives.

But Dostoevsky predicts that if you set society up in this way without replacing the connection people have to something bigger than themselves, you might also start to notice people having a hard time not seeing the world through a utilitarian lens.

And from this, you might see several things start to happen.

People in that society would tend to see themselves as separate from everything and everyone else. Their relationships would become instrumental, transactional, and antagonistic. Social norms and traditions would become arbitrary and pointless.

Even things like the truth and social unity would become a thing of the past.

Because this type of Western liberalism, to Dostoevsky, might be an impressive achievement in our ability to rationally critique things: to break them down into parts and understand them.

But it is absolutely horrible at putting these things back together in any sort of enduring way.

It lacks any coherent moral foundation. Moral pluralism becomes not only a reasonable possibility, but the only true reality at the scale of society.

So, you end up with people (as we’ve seen over and over again in Notes from Underground and Crime and Punishment) who live to endlessly rationalize their own behavior based on a limited framing of reality, rather than aiming for any sort of feeling of connection to the world around them.

You get people feeling completely alone while simultaneously embedded in a network that they always, already care about and are a part of.

What I mean is, the level of conditioning that goes on from birth to be able to see things in such a third-person way all the time is a very real thing in the modern world.

And Dostoevsky says Western liberals often assume that if anybody feels bad, there must be some sort of inefficiency in the ways our societies are set up.

In other words, if anybody has a problem, it’s just because we’re not rationally coordinating our societies in a perfect enough way. And we can fix it.

They assume that if we come up with a better way to produce the right outcomes, we can solve most people’s problems.

But Dostoevsky would say even if you gave people every material thing they could possibly want, they would still just suffer in other areas.

Maybe they’d suffer from boredom or alienation instead. Maybe material envy would just turn into envy for people’s natural advantages.

The point is, for every ever-more complex solution you come up with for problems, the new problems you create by solving those problems will just become more complex themselves to solve.

Suffering just is a part of the human experience for Dostoevsky, and trying to rationally coordinate it away entirely is always going to be a losing battle.

Now you might be thinking, Okay, but come on… What is this guy saying? That we shouldn’t even try? That we should just consult the Ouija board or the giant wheel of fortune in the town square whenever election season comes around?

Well, no.

It’s not that we shouldn’t be trying to coordinate our societies to aim for outcomes we think are good. That’s fine. The question Dostoevsky’s asking is whether that’s truly where enduring moral or social progress comes from.

Like when you say, "I want the world to become a better place," Where does that progress come from? Does it lie in the policies or rational protocols we come up with?

Or does it lie in the carrying out of those policies by people, the inner virtues of those people, and in the ways they feel connected to those around them?

Which of those is more enduring?

Because what Dostoevsky would undoubtedly say is this: if you had a society that wasn’t especially well ordered from a rational perspective but was filled with people who felt connected to one another, who shared genuine love, and where virtue was something people truly cared about cultivating every day, then despite whatever rational or utilitarian flaws it might have, it would still be a great place to live.

On the other hand, you could have a society where tons of rational progress has been made and is materially abundant, but still have high levels of drug addiction, mental illness, suicide, alienation, and constant distraction.

Because without the connection and the moral foundation, people can have everything but still feel like they have nothing.

And to Dostoevsky, the removal of things like religion (as in, the tools people have historically used to find a connection to this network they’re a part of) comes with obvious costs.

And Western liberalism, despite being a great critical endeavor, doesn’t offer a sufficient replacement for these things.

What we’re left with, on the other side of framing reality only in this way, is no true moral leadership, but just people who are left holding the bag, living in a meaningless universe.

This is why Dostoevsky writes a character like Stepan into the book: to represent the kinds of ideas that were highly respected by people at the time.

This is also why his relationship with Varvara and with the aristocratic order is so important in the story. It’s Dostoevsky pointing out what he sees as an obvious hypocrisy.

He’s showing that Western liberal ideas like rational utilitarianism require the social stability that the former aristocratic ways of thinking once guaranteed just to get off the ground.

But the entire sales pitch of Western liberalism is that it’s somehow transcending those older, outdated forms of thinking by getting rid of the religious "nonsense" we used to need to control people.

So, which is it?

Are we moving past them? Or are we still benefiting from what they provided for people?

Are we still living off the residual moral leadership that’s embedded in norms that are barely clinging on?

Because Dostoevsky thinks that any society that makes utilitarianism its primary framing above all else will be a society in a state of moral decline.

Not only will the society slowly consume itself, since the entire operation is built around pluralistic argument and individuals rationalizing their own worldview, but he would also predict that societies organized in this way will find ways to rationally justify violence and the horrible treatment of people.

And all of this will be in the name of some utilitarian outcome that they have supposedly collectively agreed upon.

Just like Raskolnikov from Crime and Punishment, but now on the scale of society, they’ll say this utilitarian outcome they’re shooting for is ultimately for the greater good of everyone.

They’ll say, I mean, it’s unfortunate what we have to do, but this is just what has to be done sometimes.

And Dostoevsky is saying that this is the entire rationalizing game that’s played over and over again when this is your moral foundation.

When this is done by an individual, that person just turns into an egomaniac who is consumed by their own agendas.

But what happens when these ideas find expression at this far more dangerous scale? When they’re being expressed by an entire society that’s centered around utilitarian projects?

(Side note: Dostoevsky is saying all of this years before the Bolsheviks, before Lenin, and even before the assassination of Tsar Alexander. This is a man who sees exactly what’s coming in his society; he’s basically on a Miss Cleo level of clairvoyance.)

He is foreshadowing a day when our understanding of how we connect to the world around us, will be replaced by some cheap connection to a utilitarian goal we are trying to carry out as a whole society.

Now, of course, one way that can look is Leninism or Stalinism, where the people just become rational cogs in a giant, ego-driven project. Their experience gets reduced to something as though they’re simply these rational cogs.

And of course Dostoevsky would have a problem with this happening. Especially when it’s being carried out in a monolithic way at the level of government, with millions of people dead, being rationally and morally justified right up to the bitter end.

But his point was much deeper than saying, The Soviet Union was bad because look at all the people who died.

Dostoevsky’s talking about the rational premises, the climate of ideas, that made it possible for that horror scene to ever become a thing in the first place.

Remember: he’s the harbinger of the story here. He’s old man Withers saying, Don’t go up to that old mansion on the hill.

Because (to return to the symbolism of the characters in the book for a second), if Stepan and Varvara represent Western liberalism and the aristocratic order of things, then consider what Dostoevsky means for each of their children to symbolize.

For example, Stepan has a son named Pyotr who is the leader of this revolutionary cell that’s planning to do something violent to bring about their worldview.

Well, what is Dostoevsky doing there with that character?

He’s turning something that usually happens at the level of ideas into a person.

It’s so much easier to see how certain ideas create a climate for other ideas to spread when we can visualize the whole thing like it’s a relationship between father and son.

This is what Dostoevsky is going for.

Stepan, as a father, creates a climate that a nihilist committed to destruction, like his son Pyotr, can justify himself in.

In other words, he’s saying this is the cycle that liberalism lives and dies in.

In the name of open-mindedness and progress, Western liberalism allows for truly toxic behavior to be justified. This then leads to social decay, which then leads to an inability to even recognize the moral decay as decay. This then makes leaders and intellectuals incapable of solving problems, which then creates a need for some kind (or any kind) of revolutionary response from a group.

This is the cycle.

It’s the open-mindedness of a father that allows dangerous ideas to flourish in the son. It’s the father’s idealism and all his big talk that allow him to ignore how his ideas have failed the very son he is raising.

Because to Dostoevsky, it’s not like Stepan, or Western liberalism, leads with the idea that morality and connection should be slowly disintegrated. It just ends up working out that way after a ton of well-intentioned, rational discussions that end up removing moral leadership.

So Western liberalism isn’t so bad on the surface, but it’s the offspring of Western liberalism that you have to be worried about.

And you’ll see through Pyotr's actions throughout the book (whether it’s manipulating people, selling people on violence, bribery, or even the outright killing of those who are a political threat to him) that it’s bad enough when these ideas are embodied by a single person. But his larger point is that at the scale that we’re talking about, these ideas are not always embodied by a single person.

At least when it's a single ego-obsessed person who’s rationalizing their behavior, they can take a step back and reflect on themselves. They can confess like Raskolnikov did. They can humble themselves and find the limitations of their own ego.

But when this starts happening at the level of ideas, the kind that end in “-ism”, the kind that so many people build their identities around in the modern world, there isn’t any kind of tribunal to hold those ideas accountable at that scale.

This is what makes them so dangerous.

Ideas alone can’t be humbled. They can’t take accountability. How visible they are in the world just often comes down to whether enough people manage to believe in them or how aggressively these ideas are being sold by people like Pyotr.

Couple this with the fact that ideas, when they take on the form of an -ism, almost always evolve into something that moves away from the original intent of the people who pioneered them.

The point is, without some kind of moral foundation to ground these things and hold people accountable at the level of the individual, how can we ever hope to hold the ideas themselves accountable?

And more than that, is this really the scale we want to trust to bring about our social progress?

Dostoevsky thinks if we do, we’ll inevitably create ‘progress’ in the form of people like Pyotr. A character based on an actual murderous revolutionary figure in Russia at the time, Sergey Nechayev, who he was fascinated by.

In fact, if we’re talking about these isms that people build their identities around, this brings us to the title of the book.

Dostoevsky would say that when ideas start operating at this scale and possess people in this way, they begin to be more like demons than ideas.

This is obviously a reference to the title, but it’s also a reference to a story in the Bible told in Matthew, in Mark, and in the Book of Luke (specifically chapter 8, verses 26 to 39). That’s the version Dostoevsky mentions in a letter when he’s writing to someone about it later.

The story is about a man who gets possessed by demons in the Bible. Then Jesus comes along and exorcises the demons from him. Expecto patronum I believe is the spell he used here.

Then at that point, the demons are released from the man, go up into the air, and fly around for a bit.

It turns out there’s a herd of pigs nearby.

The demons go into the pigs, which start squealing and freaking out. Then they run down to the river and proceed to drown themselves.

Now, the demons in this story are the isms that possess people’s thinking in a demonic sort of way. And the pigs are us.

They represent us when we believe in utilitarian constructions and build our relationships and lives around them. That when we try to prove to ourselves how powerful we are living fully through these isms, we often end up destroying ourselves in the process.

We’ll look at this more closely in a second, but there’s another character from the book that can help make this point a little better.

Remember, Pyotr’s character wanted to make Stavrogin (Varvara’s son) into the messiah figure of the revolutionary moment he was the head of.

Well, what could the symbolism be there? What does Stavrogin represent to Dostoevsky that exists in the real world?

If Varvara represents the aristocratic order, then Stavrogin represents the next generation of would-be leaders that are absolutely crippled by the moral and spiritual bankruptcy that his mom (or the existing order) has created for him to live in.

Remember that Stavrogin is described as a very handsome, smart, and articulate man. Multiple characters in the book look up to him as the person who’s next up to lead. And to Dostoevsky, he’s the kind of person that’s capable, in this generation, of bringing about what we would undoubtedly call positive change.

But because he is a product of a world that is so morally directionless, all of the capabilities and talent he’s developed don’t end up doing any good in the world.

It just ends up turning Stavrogin into maybe the most horrible character Dostoevsky ever wrote about, in any novel.

Look, Dostoevksy certainly brings some bad characters to life in his writing, but Stavrogin’s on a totally different level.

He confesses to a crime in the book that was so bad it got censored by Dostoevsky’s publisher. They wouldn’t even let Dostoevsky release the book originally with this confession in it. Even when he tried to edit out and reword the more graphic parts of it, they still wouldn’t let him publish it.

But the gruesome details aren’t really the point of this post (you can look them up or read the book if you want them). The point is that this guy, Stavrogin, is capable of doing so much in this world, but he can’t bring himself to truly care about anything.

He doesn’t become the messiah figure for the revolutionary movement. He takes interest for a bit, but then ultimately sees the whole revolutionary effort as made up by a bunch of morons.

For Dostoevsky, Stavrogin is the human embodiment of the promises of nihilism and the disappointment that soon comes after trying to live them out.

On the one hand, a nihilist seems to have a ton of freedom from the traditions of the past, and this is often marked as the selling point of steering into nihilism in your life or in policies.

If you can get rid of the arbitrary traditions and religion, then you will be unencumbered by these silly things people used to believe in.

Think about how much you can get done.

But one problem Dostoevsky wanted to point out was highlighted in the last post in the experience of Raskolnikov. The problem was that sometimes, even if we think we’ve moved beyond the moral traditions of the past, we haven’t really.

And if you don’t happen to have perfect self-awareness, you might end up being someone like Raskolnikov, who murders two people (or whatever it is) and then finds life unlivable on the other side of doing that thing.

Well, another problem with this kind of nihilism lies in the character of Stavrogin.

Dostoevsky’s asking: even if you could reject that moral order of the past and live in a totally self-aware, controlled place of nihilism, how would this amazingly free life really look in practice?

You may find yourself ending up like Stavrogin.

He’s a person who lives in a constant cycle of belief and non-belief about everything.

He can’t ever commit himself to genuinely care about anything.

He can’t morally ground his behavior in anything enduring, so he’ll do things for a couple of days and then lose interest in whatever it is, then move on to something else and lose interest in that too.

And what this kind of person turns into, when we’re talking at the scale of ideas discussed so far in this post, is an example of what having no moral direction produces in a highly capable person.

Their abilities just end up getting used by other people who can manage to persuade them to come over to their cause for a while. (These days it would probably be done through something like Instagram by a person like Pyotr.)

The point is that a highly capable person is particularly at risk of this happening. They’re very attractive and useful to a person like Pyotr.

And, if this did happen, how would they know it was happening? And how could they morally object to it?

That’s one of the big tragedies of Stavrogin.

Even more than that, when a person really can’t care about most things in this world, it just produces a malaise that they start to live in everyday, where most of the time they feel almost nothing.

Sure, there are moments where it seems like Stavrogin knows he wants to be feeling a connection here or there. But unencumbered by any sense of morality and apparently beyond all that stuff, he ends up doing horrible things in his life just to feel something at all.

Stavrogin’s whole existence becomes one huge misunderstanding.

Because he sees through the futility of all these traditions, it gives him a type of fearlessness in the face of them. This gives him the appearance of being a fearless and powerful person that other people around him mistake as a type of moral strength.

There’s a theologian and an author named Rowan Williams that talks about Stavrogin as a character in his work. He says that Stavrogin becomes a kind of character that is a twisted parody of the concept of apatheia.

You may recognize the term apatheia from conversations surrounding Stoicism.

But if you don’t, the general idea is about understanding what a wise person looks like. They certainly don’t look like someone who’s flying off the rails with a bunch of intense emotions all the time. They’re not someone completely controlled by the different vices they have.

A wise person, if you ever spotted one in the wild, would likely be someone who has rationally learned to control themselves. They would be someone who’s learned not to be too up or down as life comes their way.

Now, if you’re a Stoic, you achieve this through maybe thousands of hours of rational contemplation and practice; it’s a skill that is hard-fought and earned.

But if you’re Stavrogin in the book: then to the people immediately around him, his behavior looks more or less the same as that of someone who is wise.

Little do they know, in actuality, he hasn’t mastered his experience of the world. He’s just dead inside. He’s not up or down, and it’s not because he’s wise; it’s because there’s just nothing that matters enough to him to make him feel up or down.

This is where the issue of belief or non-belief in God starts to come into the picture for Dostoevsky.

If you’re good at recognizing patterns, you may notice something similar between Stavrogin’s downfall and how he views the world, and those of other characters from Dostoevsky’s novels that we’ve looked at before.

Whenever you’re reading a famous book from the 1870s, there have always been a lot of people who’ve commented on Dostoevsky’s work.

And when it comes to trying to organize the characters in his novels, a common thing people notice is that many of the characters he explores are faced with something that forces them to consider their belief or non-belief in the existence of God.

And depending on which character we’re analyzing, they take one of two paths for Dostoevsky, which determines everything else about them.

They either take the path of the madman, or they take the path of the saint.

Characters like Stavrogin, Raskolnikov, and the Underground Man are the people that fall under the madman category.

And as you can probably guess, Dostoevsky ultimately traces the problems going on in these characters’ lives (no matter how political, social, or economic they seem) to their non-belief in God, which grounds every decision they choose to make after that and shapes every way they make sense of the world.

For Dostoevsky, non-belief in God forces you to posture yourself in your life in a way that leads to very predictable outcomes. Each one of them does it in their own unique way, but they’re all still an example of the madman.

The common mistake (if you wanted a general description) is that the madman chooses a divisive way of making sense of the world that fundamentally lives in denial of their connection to the things and people in the network of existence around them.

For Dostoevsky, the mistake comes when we live in denial of the love and connection to everything that represents what he calls God.

Because this forces us to find a way to relate to the world that’s ego-centered, fragmented, and alienated from others, and ultimately to live in a way where we create false idols out of things that destroy us.

We become the pigs from the story.

In the process of creating false idols out of ourselves and of the ideas of others in the form of these isms, we build our identities and relationships out of our connection to these isms.

And when we do that without moral guidance that’s rooted in true love and connection to the roles we play in this network of being, we will always, in the process of trying to bring about these isms and prove our own power, end up destroying ourselves in the process.

Now you can certainly see this mistake in characters like Raskolnikov or Stavrogin. But the ultimate example of what Dostoevsky sees as this sort of flaw (and the example he was probably most proud of creating in his lifetime) is the character of Kirillov in Demons.

Kirillov has what he thinks is a great idea in the book.

His idea is that because God does not exist, his will must be entirely his own. But more than that, because his will is his own, it is also his duty to sacrifice himself in the name of his own values.

Some people will do this with their weekends; they’ll sacrifice time with family over the holidays to build the company they care about, or something like that.

Kirillov, though, thinks the ultimate way to sacrifice yourself is to kill yourself in the name of a cause.

So his life becomes him sitting around, looking out at the world, listening to arguments, and waiting for the right cause to come along so he can finally fulfill his ultimate purpose and kill himself for the sake of that cause.

What an idea for a character.

And this is exactly what happens in the book. He waits around until Pyotr convinces him to do it for his revolutionary cause.

Dostoevsky sees Kirillov as the ultimate example of the flaw in the thinking of the madman. His thinking is that as long as he’s radical enough and as long as he’s got the right revolutionary cause, if he destroys himself in the process, this somehow makes his sacrifice redeeming for all of humanity.

Think of how this becomes particularly dangerous as a message to tell young people in a Western liberal society.

Something else needs to be said here that no doubt a lot of people are thinking at this point. You might be thinking:

Okay, I buy what you’re saying here, Dostoevsky. That these theoretical systems we use to break down the world are never capable of offering an enduring moral foundation. And that when I build my worldview around these isms, I’ll always have shallow friendships, a shallow love life, and a shallow connection with being in the world around me.

But if ultimately what you're scared of is that these isms are going to lead people to a kind of hubris, something that might drive them to commit acts of violence, if what you're scared of is people doing horrible things and then rationalizing it after the fact, then how, in God’s name (no pun intended), do you see something like religion as being excluded from that?

Some of the least moral people I know are religious, or at least say they are. And these religions, these supposed geysers of morality you’re talking about, are the same things that lead to treating gay people horribly based on a sentence in a book. They’re what led to people throwing acid in women’s faces.

Or how about the Crusades?

Am I just supposed to accept this as part of the world? And why? For fear that I might be making a bigger mistake than they are?

Well, this is probably the point in this series of posts to say something important.

Much like Keiji Nishitani, who would say that just because you call yourself religious doesn’t mean you’re on a truly religious quest, it’s possible, and even very common, for people to have a superficial relationship to a religious institution.

Similar to Nishitani, Dostoevsky might say that if you’re a modern Christian and you don’t take your faith very seriously, if you’re rarely uncomfortable in your faith and it seems to come easily to you, that’s something that should make you truly pause.

The faith of Dostoevsky is something we’ll explore far deeper in the next two posts. And for anyone thinking Dostoevsky is a kindred spirit simply because he’s using the word “God”, then you may be surprised by the level that he committed himself to this quest.

Dostoevsky’s faith has often been described as a tragic, existential form of Christianity. It’s very mystic.

Some people think he resembles a type of Buddhism more than he does Christianity.

What I mean is that it’s deeply along the kinds of conversations we’ve been looking at in recent posts; it is a way of viewing faith that never idealizes the institutions or the approach laid out in something like the Bible whatsoever.

Faith for Dostoevsky was a struggle. A constant struggle.

It was a difficult engagement with the sometimes deeply flawed institutions and scripture that we inherit as people. But this was nonetheless a struggle for him that needed to go on if someone ever wanted a deeper communion with God and their own being.

And if there is ever anyone who is born into this world who is actually trying to do that in good faith, religion just will be a part of that process.

Now, with that said, while Dostoevsky is not your average Christian by any stretch of the imagination, it’s also true that to remove Christianity from the way he engaged with existence would be to take away something deeply important to what he saw as truth.

We’ll have plenty of time to talk more about that in the next couple posts.
