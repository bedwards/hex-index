There’s a story from Dostoevsky’s life that’s usually one of the first things you hear about him when you start looking into his work. I personally think the story resonates a bit better when you already know a little about what he was going for when he wrote, which at this point we do, this being the fourth book we’ve covered of the five great novels from Dostoevsky.

The story is about the time in his life that he almost got shot by a firing squad. Classic story I’m sure he used to tell around the campfire to all the kids.

There’s a couple different versions of this story. One version comes from the main character of the book we’re talking about today; it’s called The Idiot.

We’ll talk more about the book here in a second, but first it’s important to understand how some of the intense experiences that Dostoevsky had in his life led to the conclusions in the rest of his work.

Conclusions about what he thinks a good life is. His views on beauty and love and more specifically what he means when he says a now famous quote that’s been associated with this book, The Idiot: what did he mean when he said that he thought that “beauty will save the world”?

There’s a lot of discussion centered around this statement, and we’ll be a lot closer to understanding it by the end of this post.

When Dostoevsky’s writing the book Demons we just talked about, and when he talks about a revolutionary cell that’s planning stuff to potentially bring down the government in the book, that’s not him sitting around method acting what it would be like to be a revolutionary.

No, early in his writing career, he really was a member of what’s now known as the Petrashevsky circle. Meaning he was part of one of these revolutionary groups, which is part of what gives him so much insight into the psychology of these kinds of people.

So, like any revolutionary at the time that’s living in Tsarist Russia, he was always at high risk of being prosecuted. Turns out Russia doesn’t take too kindly to you if you’re plotting to overthrow the government every day.

And as the story goes Dostoevsky gets arrested. He gets processed, charged, and sentenced to death. He gets led out into a field. There are stakes driven into the ground. The kinds of stakes they tie you to when you’re about to get shot by a firing squad.

He and his friends have been dressed up in the sort of white uniform that they put people in right before they shoot them. The bags are ready to go over their heads.

Dostoevsky is apparently told at this moment by the people with the guns that he has five minutes left to live. They tell him go ahead, make your peace, say a prayer, do whatever it is you’re going to do.

And he says when you’re in this kind of moment, your whole relationship to time starts to change. That five minutes that he was given felt to him like it was actually an enormous amount of time.

He says each minute felt to him like it was a different lifetime that he was living. And much like we normally do in life, when you always feel like you have all the time in the world to do the stuff you want to do, he said that in that moment he came up with a plan for how he was going to use each of these five minutes that he had left.

First couple minutes were going to be for saying goodbye to his friends around him. The two minutes after that were going to be for going over his life, thinking about what he did while he was here on this planet.

The last minute he was going to dedicate to looking around him, and taking in a glimpse of the world for one last time. These last three minutes, he says he’s going to really think about it, and he’s going to finally come to a decision as to what his whole life has been for up until this point.

And while he’s doing this, he looks off into the distance, and he sees a small church a little ways off just sitting there.

This church had a spire on top of it, and he remembers that the light from the sun was bouncing off the spire shooting off in all directions. It was beautiful to him.

Dostoevsky thinks in this moment that in about two minutes, he is essentially going to become one of these rays of light that he’s looking at. He starts to reflect. He thinks, “I certainly won’t be a thinking, feeling person anymore. What will I be, if anything at that point?”

But then this question leads to another question for him, and any time that he might have been able to spend marveling at the light show that was going on came to an end real quick.

Because he all of a sudden becomes consumed by the follow-up question: what if I wasn’t going to die right now?

What would I do with my life if somehow I were able to keep on living?

He says he couldn’t stop thinking about it. He becomes overwhelmed by what he calls the eternity of days that would be ahead of him; all that he could do with that time if he took it as seriously as he was taking time right now.

He says in that moment he knew that he would count up every minute of it. He wouldn’t waste a single second of it.

And eventually this thought, that started as a very nice thought, at some point it just starts to weigh on him.

You can imagine why. The number of seconds he’s wasted up until this point. The fact he’s having this insight just right now when he’s about to die.

He says the feeling that comes over him eventually is that he wishes that they’d hurry up and shoot him so he doesn’t have to feel the weight of this thought any more.

At which point, in what he thinks are the final seconds of his life, a person rides up on a horse carrying a piece of paper. It’s a stay of execution from Tsar Nicholas I.

The whole thing it turns out was set up, staged as a mock execution.

And the people in power did this to not only psychologically torture the people like Dostoevsky, but also as a political maneuver to make the king look like he’s a really decent guy. Look at how benevolent our leaders are that they let these people live even despite their treason.

Now just because they aren’t being put to death, it’s not like they’re just going to take off Dostoevsky’s handcuffs and let him go free. He gets sentenced to four years in a Siberian prison and then is required for five years after that to serve in the Russian military.

You can imagine what kind of an impact this event has on him and his writing that comes after this.

Just as some time context here: he gets out of the prison/military in 1859, and he comes out with Notes From Underground just five years later in 1864.

It’s not a coincidence that this execution story is one that he wanted to tell in this book in particular, The Idiot. And it’s not a coincidence that this story is told by the main character of the book, a guy named Prince Myshkin.

First of all, just for clarity, a lot of modern readers might expect that someone we’re calling Prince Myshkin would have to be a prince that’s part of a royal family, living in a castle somewhere.

But that’s not the case when it comes to this Prince Myshkin.

In Russian society at the time, a person could theoretically come from a family that has a kind of “royal” status in terms of respect, because of what their family used to be back during the feudal system. To be called a prince like this usually meant that you were part of some kind of aristocratic family from the past. But it doesn’t necessarily mean your lifestyle today looks anything like what we’d typically think of as royalty.

Prince Myshkin in this book is a great example of this. He starts the book almost completely broke, sitting on a train, returning from years that he had just spent getting treatment from what somebody today might call a mental hospital.

But even these details about Prince Myshkin are me getting ahead of myself.

Because the real thing we need to understand about Myshkin is what Dostoevsky was going for when he wrote him.

To put it simply he was trying to create a Christ-like figure that was living on planet Earth among all the humans. Or, as he wrote to his niece in a letter later about the character of Myshkin, he was trying to create an example of the “positively beautiful man.”

In fact, since we’re talking about his notes and letters about this book, he wrote a couple other people letters about his mindset when he was getting ready to write this book.

He says that there’s a lot of attempts he sees out there where great authors will try to bring a Christ-like figure to life in one of their novels.

He cites Don Quixote as an example of this. Pickwick from The Pickwick Papers by Charles Dickens. Jean Valjean from Les Misérables. He gives several other examples.

But to Dostoevsky, every one of these attempts by these other authors come up short for him in some crucial way at depicting someone who is Christ-like.

And knowing what we know about Dostoevsky so far with his work, think of what is uniquely interesting about him exploring a character like this.

What is it that he’s so good at as an author? Well, for the 1860s, his realism is one big thing that sets him apart. As we know, he’s always trying to genuinely explore the complexity and the messiness of the internal human experience.

But how could that ever work if you have someone like Christ as a character? That might be one of the most idealistic characters you could possibly try to create!

What would happen if you dropped someone like Jesus into the middle of actual, messy, human relationships, psychology, and society?

Well, Dostoevsky thinks most people in the kind of modern society we live in, from the outside at least, would probably look at a Christ-like person, and think they’re just an idiot.

And not only is this a funny idea, but if you think about it, this is actually an incredibly honest thing for Dostoevsky to do as a thinker as well.

Remember towards the end of last episode how we were talking about commentary on his work over the years, and how people typically break the characters in his books down into two different categories: they’re either the madman or the saint.

The madman being people like Raskolnikov, the Underground Man, Stavrogin.

The saint being people like Liza, Sonya, and now Prince Myshkin.

No doubt you can hear this and you might assume that Dostoevsky thinks we should all be saints. He’s probably even got an online course he’s selling. Five ways to be more saintly in your morning routine.

But no, Dostoevsky’s not simply saying that the madman is evil, and the saintly way is always the right way to live.

He thinks there are downsides to being a saint sometimes. Where even if you could be some sort of morally ideal figure that’s levitating above everyone else, there’d still be a “curse of sainthood,” as it’s called, that is crucial for a thinking person to understand.

And this was a big part of what Dostoevsky wanted to get across in this book.

To put this another way, and to connect the episodes of this series together a bit: if Crime and Punishment was Dostoevsky testing the ideals of Raskolnikov and showing where they come up short, and if Demons was him testing the ideals of western liberalism and the idea of building our lives around these -isms; then in this book, The Idiot, the ideal he’s testing and finding the limitations of here is his own ideal. The ideal of sainthood.

This is why many people see this as one of Dostoevsky’s most honest books as well.

And for whatever it’s worth, have you ever had a similar thought along these lines he’s getting into? You ever thought about an example of some moral sage, like a monk or Jesus or something, and have you ever wondered how they would act if you planted them in a scenario that’s not designed to show how moral they are, but in an actual scenario that’s supposed to be annoying to them?

What does the Christ-like figure do when someone is harassing them at the mall and won’t leave them alone?

Trying to manipulate them, take advantage of them, and they refuse to stop? Picture someone harassing a monk, and then picture the person doing the harassing as someone who sees the monk can be abused and won’t defend themselves, so this person keeps going.

What would a moral sage do in this situation?

The point is not that you couldn’t come up with an answer as to what Jesus would do in this spot. The point for Dostoevsky is: how helpful is that kind of Jesus response if you actually acted that way in the real world?

Because as we’ll see, Prince Myshkin in the book, as the resident Christ-like figure, doesn't end up making the world around him a better place at all.

In fact, what Dostoevsky wants to get across is that sometimes the kind of religious self-sacrifice, the humiliation that someone like Christ endures for the sake of helping other people doesn’t really help much. Sometimes in the real world, when you’re dealing with insecure people, or severely damaged people, or sick people even; Christ-like behavior colliding with the true complexity of human life can actually end up just making people’s lives even worse than they were before they met you.

So let’s talk about some examples of this curse of saintliness that go down in the book. And let’s at the same time talk about beauty; the other main subject he wants to explore here at a deep level.

Prince Myshkin’s entire journey that he goes on throughout this book has been described by some people as one giant conversation about beauty that’s going on at multiple different covert levels.

It’s been said that every main character in this book has a different way that they think about beauty, that then determines a lot about how they’re different as people.

The story begins with Prince Myshkin on a train. Again, he’s returning from the last couple years he’s just spent in a hospital where he’s been getting treatment for epilepsy. This is a parallel to Dostoevsky himself, by the way, who also suffered from epilepsy.

As Myshkin is sitting on this train he runs into a guy that’s going to be one of the central characters for the rest of the book. His name is Rogozhin.

If Prince Myshkin is the resident Christ-like figure in this book, then Rogozhin is going to be the resident shallow, selfish, kind of cringe character of this book. Because from the moment Prince Myshkin meets him he is the definition of someone that is only really thinking about himself.

He speaks very loudly on the train. He’ll have emotional ups and downs. He’s a very intense kind of person. He’s strong-willed. He’s the kind of person where if he wants something, he doesn’t have too many moral hangups that are going to stop him from trying to get what he wants.

And if you wanted some evidence for this, he tells Myshkin not long after meeting him that he’s on the train that day because he just inherited a ton of money from his father, and now he’s traveling to use the money to impress a woman he’s met through social circles that’s named Nastasya.

Nastasya is going to be another one of the main characters for the rest of the book.

Rogozhin shows Myshkin a picture of Nastasya. He talks for a while about how beautiful she is to him. He says that he wants to marry her. And strong-willed as he is there’s nothing that he wants more in this entire world than for this woman in the picture to be his.

But Nastasya in the book is much more than just this picture. And she’s more than an idea in Rogozhin’s head as to how he wants to possess her and claim her as his own.

Through conversations about her we find out that Nastasya lost her parents at a very early age. She was taken in by a man named Totsky, who from the day he took her in has been grooming her to become his mistress, which she now is.

The even more messed up part about it is that even though she’s the one that’s being taken advantage of and really has no choice but to go along with it, the society around her judges her as the one that’s been tainted by this whole situation.

Still, even in spite of this unfair judgment from people, Nastasya turns out to be a force of nature. To almost everyone she meets, she comes off as so smart and charismatic that she still has guys that are lining down the block trying to be with her.

In other words, this Rogozhin guy on the train has some competition, and he knows this.

For example, there’s this other guy named Ganya that also wants to marry Nastasya. But he wants to marry her because her father has a ton of social connections that Ganya thinks would be really good for his position in the world.

For Ganya, the way he sees love, a marriage is more about social mobility than it is about romance or a commitment to someone else.

And for him to have someone at social events that is so beautiful and good at speaking, to have someone who’s so well connected, someone that checks all the boxes on my checklist of things that I want a woman to be; this makes Nastasya seem like a perfect candidate for what he wants from a relationship.

If these two weren’t enough to deal with for Nastasya, Prince Myshkin decides to throw his hat into the ring when he starts to feel love for her as well.

Because when he sees the picture and hears about her past, he can’t help but start to see an incredible amount of beauty in this person that goes far beyond anything these other two guys are looking at her as.

So just to recap here: if a love triangle can be something that’s hard for people to deal with, then what we have here, folks, is a love quadrilateral, with a Jesus-like figure named Myshkin being one of the points of this love quadrilateral.

And in true Dostoevsky fashion, as chaotic as this can all seem, for him this is going to be a perfect setting to explore things like beauty and love at a deeper level than he ever has in any other book.

For clarity’s sake, the following arguments go beyond simply how we view people as beautiful. It applies to everything in the world. It’s just in a novel like this, a love interest like Nastasya is the metaphor he uses to get this all across.

See, to Dostoevsky, when someone says that something is beautiful to them, and then someone else looks at the same thing and says they think it is beautiful as well, it can be tempting to think that both people are talking about the exact same feeling they’re having. That what they’re feeling is equally as deep.

But words can be very misleading when we’re trying to sync up our experiences of the world.

What one person calls beautiful or love, no matter how passionate their behavior may look, could actually be based on things that are surprisingly shallow.

Take Rogozhin as an example of this. Rogozhin is an intense dude, for sure. But everything that he thinks is beautiful about Nastasya is based on having no understanding about who she is more fully as a person.

Think of what he knows about her: she’s essentially a well-formed molecule to him. And now he wants to own her. He couldn’t care less about her inner complexity or what she loves and cares about. Those things never cross his mind.

To Dostoevksy, Rogozhin has a superficial understanding of her, which then leads to a superficial way of describing his feelings as “love.”

It really is a superficial connection to reality overall. She is a thing to him. A shiny thing that he wants to possess.

So what naturally happens when this level of connection is what grounds your relationships?

Well, jealousy, possessiveness, angry passionate outbursts of entitlement for how this person is owed to you in some way. That’s the kind of stuff that happens.

And usually this kind of person makes excuses for these sorts of outbursts.

“It’s just how passionate I am about you, the reason I do this is because I love you so much!”

Well, this is the kind of behavior that we see out of Rogozhin for the entire book.

Take Ganya as another example of a perspective on beauty and love. Ganya thinks Nastasya is beautiful, but only as much as it helps him check the boxes on this list of things he wants from his wife socially.

I mean, sure she’s beautiful now and is great at parties, but what happens if she’s not? What happens if she can’t be on all the time. What if she goes through a period of her life where she’s feeling sad and not very social? What happens if her father gets caught for sexually abusing his daughter, becomes disgraced, and now being connected to this family becomes a liability for Ganya?

What happens with love like this when it’s so conditional? Is this even love? Ganya would certainly say it is.

We can throw these words like love around so casually in everyday conversation. But to Dostoevsky, to truly respect something like love or beauty is to critically examine it and get to a better understanding of what specific people mean when they say it.

Clearly for Rogozhin, when he says he loves Nastasya, all that it means is that he wants to possess her. Clearly for Ganya, when he says Nastasya’s beautiful, all that it means is that he sees her as one piece of a project that fuels his own ego.

But when Prince Myshkin says that he finds Nastasya beautiful, this is something that’s coming from a totally different place. He didn’t fall in love with her picture on the train. He didn’t fall in love with how good she is at social events.

The place he’s coming from is more of a tragic perspective, where he acknowledges the good and the bad about her, and tries to understand her more fully as a person, on her own terms.

Yes, she’s strong and charismatic on the surface, but he also realizes that she’s emotionally wounded from all the things she’s been through.

Yes, she’s great in conversations a lot of the time and that’s something to admire, but she also does stuff sometimes that hurts the people around her because her inner experience is actually very conflicted.

Now to Myshkin, none of these things are seen as black marks on her record. There aren’t caveats next to the person that he chooses to love. Loving someone, in a more Christ-like way, is to affirm these things about someone and to not just see them in terms of how they benefit you and your own immediate projects.

Prince Myshkin then is able to find her beautiful at this deeper level because he’s done the work to understand who she truly is at a deeper level.

And part of loving her in this way is that he sees the situation that she’s in.

He sees how she has multiple people trying to marry her based on stuff that’s going to end up poorly for her and whoever she decides to marry. So what this leads Myshkin to do in the book is to offer to marry her himself.

From his perspective: here is a beautiful person, who’s in a bad situation with her fake father, who is going to marry someone bad for her, simply because they’re a little less bad than her current situation.

But nobody should have to marry for those kinds of reasons. And Myshkin, who it turns out is getting an inheritance soon himself in the book, offers this money to her as an act of self-sacrifice and self-humiliation.

Meaning he doesn’t want to tie this woman down, have kids with her, and make her his wife in that sense.

No, in a very Christ-like way, he wants to sacrifice his own finances so that this beautiful person doesn’t have to sacrifice her own life.

Some modern readers may hear about this move from Prince Myshkin and be shocked. I’ve heard plenty of people over the years read this book and say that Rogozhin sounds like an incel. And that Prince Myshkin sounds like a total simp. They say this guy isn’t a great human being; this guy is an idiot.

Well, hence the title of the book.

See from the outside: why would anyone ever sacrifice themselves in this way for someone?

It’s not healthy, they would say! It’s not setting good boundaries! He’s just going to lose all his material stuff that he’s worked so hard to collect over the years!

But for Prince Myshkin, that sort of utilitarian calculation when it comes to people just isn’t the way he’s looking at the world.

But there’s a counter argument to what’s been said so far about beauty. Don’t people say that beauty is in the eye of the beholder? That it’s entirely subjective?

When someone looks at a painting, or the view from the top of a mountain, or when they look at a good looking person say something is beautiful, all that is, is a subjective, aesthetic preference of the person, nothing more.

How can Dostoevsky talk about beauty as though it’s anything other than an aesthetic preference? Rogozhin may be shallow, but his view of beauty is just as valid as Prince Myshkin’s.

Dostoevsky doesn’t see it this way. To him beauty is not simply an aesthetic judgment that someone is making about the world. It’s partly aesthetic. But whenever someone says that something is beautiful, whether they’re aware of it or not, to him they are also making an implicit moral judgment about the thing, right along with the aesthetic judgment.

This is sometimes called by people who analyze Dostoevsky’s work his moral-aesthetic spectrum of beauty.

Here’s where he’s coming from: whenever we say something’s beautiful, we’re not just saying that we like the photons or the sound waves and how they’re being processed by our eyes and ears right now. We are always noticing something deeper about the thing that connects to some set of higher ideals, that are embedded into a network, that we are a part of.

For example, notice how a newborn baby, if you looked at it purely from an aesthetic perspective, it’s often something that’s very ugly.

Sorry to any newborns that may be reading.

And yet we find a newborn baby beautiful for what they represent to us at a higher level.

Notice how something aesthetically beautiful can be seen as ugly: Triumph of the Will.

It’s a movie from the 1930s, made in Nazi Germany, influenced heavily by Nazi propaganda. Pretty widely considered to be a breakthrough moment for cinema in terms of the stuff it did. But for so many people, they would have a really hard time ever seeing it as beautiful because of what it represents and connects to beyond just the mere aesthetic appearance of the thing.

To Dostoevsky, beauty and morality are inseparable, no matter how many people may throw around the word “beautiful” in a surface-level way.

Think of how this applies to Rogozhin and Ganya and their relationship to Nastasya. Does Rogozhin think that Nastasya is beautiful? Or is he really just talking about something like the form and symmetry of her face? Does Ganya really think she’s beautiful? Or is he more talking about how useful she is to him right now, or that she makes him feel good about himself?

See, once we get beyond a superficial engagement with the world, because the things we think are beautiful are always connected to something we see as ethical, then it stands to reason for Dostoevsky that our ethical progress is best described as a type of beauty that we’re striving for internally.

In other words, beauty and ethics are far more connected than most modern people realize.

And what this means for him is that someone’s ability to spot beauty in the world and their ability to strive for beauty in their own character, are really two skills that are very linked together.

This is why, when Dostoevsky’s creating a character that is morally a bad person in his novels, he will often make them incapable of seeing beauty in the world around them.

See, if Rogozhin and Ganya in the book represent a superficial framing of beauty. An egoist, utilitarian, rational framing of the world that limits our understanding of things to how they can be manipulated to benefit me and my own projects.

Then on the other side of this, being able to see the beauty in the things and people around you, to practice seeing a more full picture of what they are and how they co-constitute the world you’re in, that becomes a gateway into this more immediate connection with being that we’ve been talking about for quite a while on this show.

What I’m saying is: for Dostoevsky, just like Prince Myshkin did with Nastasya, doing the work to uncover the beauty that’s in the world around you becomes another one of these exercises that is built around self-emptying.

Think about this point in the context of everything else he’s been going for in his work.

Remember Crime and Punishment was about the power of confession. How to confess to something is to acknowledge the limitations of your own ego and consent to your place in a higher network. Self-emptying.

Notes from Underground, among other things, talked about love. How Liza’s unconditional embrace of the Underground Man and all his flaws, was a self-emptying that became a direct attack on his whole existence and all the barriers he puts up.

Demons was about the self-emptying process of taking true responsibility for ourselves; of not just outsourcing who we are to a set of -isms that can demonically possess us.

Well here we are reading The Idiot, and Dostoevsky is saying that the ability to uncover the beauty in things requires a similar type of self-emptying.

That to be able to spot beauty is to have the ability to suspend your ego for a while, take a step back, and see the thing you’re looking at on its own home ground.

And again, it’s in this way that someone’s ability to see beauty in the world has a direct relationship to their level of moral development.

Because no person that is obsessed with themselves all day long can ever step outside of themselves long enough to see the beauty in the world around them.

They’ll often just think the world is boring, or horrific.

More than that, is it possible to be a passive nihilist, thinking everything around you is completely meaningless, but still be someone who sees immense beauty in everything?

Dostoevsky would say no.

Beauty for him is something that is always connected to truth and goodness. He says that beauty, truth, and goodness co-inhere each other.

That these three things, while they may seem like they’re very different things, are all actually pointing you towards the same thing. That same thing, in this case, being God.

Now, on the surface that may sound like the biggest load of BS you’ve ever heard in your life.

From an atheist perspective, that could sound like the pastor down at your local church, who has no idea what he’s talking about, just taking three words that sound really wonderful that everybody approves of, and then trying to get a monopoly over them by saying they’re all really just God.

But Dostoevsky’s not the first guy to be saying this kind of stuff. He’s continuing a very long tradition in Christian theology and philosophy, specifically in the Russian Orthodox variety that he’s a part of. And it’s a tradition that, for many reasons, will group these three things into a trio: beauty, truth, and goodness.

Which brings us to the quote that’s often associated with this book: that “beauty will save the world.”

If beauty, truth, and goodness are all things that co-inhere each other, then you might ask,

“Can’t we just interchange the words there in that quote of his? If beauty will save the world, then couldn’t we also just say that truth will save the world? Or goodness will save the world? Why does Dostoevsky think beauty holds a special place here?”

Well, think about what truth and goodness do that beauty doesn’t.

You try to show somebody the truth, and if the truth is inconvenient to them or uncomfortable, they’re likely to just deny the truth. The truth becomes antagonistic.

Similarly, when it comes to goodness: to show someone goodness, and make them aware that some way they’re behaving is not good, that becomes antagonistic as well, like you’re just going around moralizing at people.

But talking about beauty doesn’t come off as moralizing. Nobody feels attacked when they’re told they can see more beauty in the world. Beauty is something that just hits you, viscerally. It has the power to make you feel love or awe; it has the power to captivate someone at that immediate experiential level, even when they’re about to be put to death.

Meaning beauty is not something that’s theoretical. Beauty is phenomenological. Beauty has the power to change someone without argument. It has the power to direct someone towards a fuller understanding of how the world is connected.

And it’s in the context of this unique accessibility and redemptive power that beauty has that Dostoevsky believes that beauty can save the world.

Especially considering, for him, the specific kind of world we’re in. A very superficial world that he thinks needs saving. And this is why Prince Myshkin in the book is meant to embody the positively beautiful man.

It’s the reason why his actions, as in touch with true beauty and love as they may be, just make him look like an idiot to the people in the world he’s living in.

But to be called an idiot by a society that’s fundamentally sick may in fact be the greatest compliment that anyone could ever get.

Consider a couple other difficult moments from the book where Prince Myshkin feels the curse of sainthood as he navigates the real world.

He offers to marry Nastasya and tells her the loving, kind intentions behind why he wants to do it. And you may expect someone to jump for joy that someone would want to do something so nice for them.

But when Nastasya, a person with a history that makes her question her own worth, hears about what he wants to do, she doesn’t feel worthy of love like this.

Truth be told, she kind of thinks this guy’s an idiot if he really wants to give her his inheritance. The whole thing is confusing to her. Nobody has ever treated her this way.

So far from this being something that makes her feel happy and calm, this marriage proposal sends her into a tailspin.

She accepts the proposal, then she goes back on it and decides she wants to be with Rogozhin instead, then she thinks about it and goes back to Myshkin, then goes back to Rogozhin.

In other words, Myshkin’s Christ-like maneuver here doesn’t end up making the world a better place at all. It just ends up creating more problems. And it gets even worse.

Because Rogozhin, being in the presence of a Christ-like figure, and being such a morally shallow person himself, these things combine together in his own psychology, and it breeds resentment towards Myshkin as a person.

Turns out Jesus’s presence in the real world wouldn’t be all performing miracles and making people start singing the gospel.

Rogozhin’s not grateful at all to be around a walking, talking Jesus figure. He hates this goody two-shoes guy that in his mind is trying to steal his possession in Nastasya.

Being around moral strength to a deeply insecure person just ends up making him angry. And when you combine that with his strong will and lack of moral hangups, Rogozhin tries to kill Prince Myshkin.

Now in the story, Myshkin has an epileptic seizure right before Rogozhin can do it. Rogozhin gets scared at that point and runs away.

And on the other side of waking up, Prince Myshkin, being a saintly kind of person, treats Rogozhin with forgiveness and love. You can almost imagine Jesus saying it,

“There’s no need to report you to the police. I forgive you for what you have done here, my child.”

Fast forward to later in the book. Rogozhin, who may have been picked up by the police if Myshkin wasn’t so forgiving, crosses the point of no return. Nastasya for the 12th time goes back and forth between him and Myshkin. Rogozhin’s possessiveness and jealousy take over, and he can’t stand to lose his shiny object yet again. So he kills Nastasya. To him, the ultimate expression of possessing her.

Dostoevsky ultimately thought that he failed at presenting the positively beautiful man in this book.

And if you read his accounts of what it was like to write this book, he was under a ton of financial pressure at the time. Most of it, he was traveling around Europe trying to pick up the pieces for his family after his brother died.

He actually had two epileptic seizures in the process of writing the last couple chapters of the book with Rogozhin and Nastasya.

I guess one way of measuring the intensity of a book is how many epileptic seizures you have while writing it.

That said, one thing I think most people agree he succeeded at is in showing how the ideal of sainthood, when we try to live in a way that experiences things on their own home ground, these ideals come up short when we’re in the real world sometimes. And if we don’t have a vigilant, tragic perspective towards the things in the world that matter to us, then even something done out of genuine love or generosity can be something that destroys us or other people.
