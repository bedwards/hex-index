Hello everyone. I’m Stephen West. This is *Philosophize This!*

Practically everyone has heard of when Nietzsche declared that *God is dead* at the end of the 19th century. And when you hear a statement like that it can sound like it’s something that’s hostile towards religion.

Imagine Nietzsche jumping up from his desk river dancing on amphetamines. Religion is stupid. It’s outdated. It leads people to lives that aren’t good for them or the world.

But as we’ll see by the end of this post, through the eyes of someone like Keiji Nishitani, the statement God is dead is not an anti-religious statement in any sense. In fact, to him it is a foreshadowing to what you could call a *golden age* of religious participation that may come about in the near future.

It’ll make more sense by the end of this.

And the way we get there is to talk about religion along the same lines that we talked about nihilism in the last post. Remember to Nishitani, it’s a mistake to only consider a *piece* of what something is to people, and then to pretend to understand the essence of the thing in its entirety.

We talked about how it’s possible to have a shallow relationship with nihilism and death, and how it’s possible to experience these things more deeply if you examine the ways that people usually frame them in an incomplete way.

Well, what would happen if we did this *same* thing when it comes to religion?

For the sake of covering as many sides of this as we can today let’s start with what an atheist might say about religion in the kind of world we live in.

Similar to the person from last time that sees nihilism as a problem to be solved, this person might think of religion as something that’s mostly just a security blanket for people.

That religion is obviously a sociological construct designed to not only control people but to give people easy answers to difficult questions in a complicated universe.

Things like morality, for a religious person under this view, aren’t difficult problems—just listen to what God has to say about it and then it’s objective and easy.

Who you are, identity, isn’t a complex or difficult thing—just read the book of Job or Matthew and it’ll more or less tell you who you should be.

In other words, religion, it’s said by some, is a type of mythology that simplifies reality down for scared, weak people that either are too busy or unwilling to affirm reality in all of its complexity.

These people have to deny big pieces of reality, so they turn to religion to be able to do it.

Now contrast this with somebody that’s maybe a bit more favorable towards religion. Since I mentioned the book of Matthew, let’s stick to modern Christianity as an example.

And by the way, this isn’t me intending to exclude other religions. It’s just the one I’m most familiar with to be able to make Nishitani’s point here without speaking out of my lane too much.

Talk to an average Christian going to church on Sunday, ask them what religion is, and they might say something like: “well, religion for me is the path to personal salvation.”

*I was born in sin. I believe in the divinity of Jesus of Nazareth. And through the sacrifice of God’s one and only son, I am absolved of my sins and earn admittance into the kingdom of heaven.*

To Nishitani, both of these opposing takes probably cover a *piece* of what religion is to some people.

First of all, no doubt some people do use religion as a security blanket. And I think he’d say that’s probably something you see more commonly in the Western world, where this opinion is more popular just given the type of religion that’s prevalent.

And no doubt to some people, religion is most experienced by them as the one and only ticket they have to salvation.

But both of these, while they’re not wrong—to Nishitani you could say they are *incomplete* when it comes to describing a full picture of what religion is. That both of these only cover a *piece* of the terrain religion encompasses.

In his book *Religion and Nothingness*, perhaps his magnum opus, Nishitani starts out by giving a few slightly more sophisticated takes on what religion is that are popular among scholars.

And just like with nihilism, these common takes are going to be important for us to consider if we want to understand religion more fully—that is, if we want to understand what’s missing from these definitions.

He says one popular way people in the West will describe religion is that it's *the relationship of man to an absolute*. Like God, for example.

That’s one way of distilling religion down if you wanted to.

Another common way to describe religion is to say that it is simply *the process of getting ever closer to an idea of the Holy*.

That’s another distillation.

Nishitani says some scholars describe religion as *the abandonment of self-will in order to live according to the will of God*.

Some other thinkers have framed it as *the intuition of the infinite found in the finite*.

All of these attempts at a description are incomplete if you’re Nishitani. In fact, if you were to examine them all and try to find something similar about them, Nishitani says notice something here that his colleague Martin Heidegger may have pointed out about the modern Western world and the way that we typically think about things:

*These are all attempts to describe what religion is by framing it in terms of its utilitarian function, or in terms of what use it serves a particular person or to a society.*

This is what we do with practically everything in the Western world. You ask someone what something is, and as if by default they’ll respond with what *use* it serves to them.

We’ll say something like art is a way for you to express your emotions, or to liven up a room.

Education, we’ll say, is a tool to prepare people for the workforce. A friend is someone who’s there for you when you’re having a bad day. We frame things in terms of their utility.

And remember, Heidegger would say that this is a symptom of the technological enframing we give to everything. And that while framing it in this way certainly helps us in specific areas like the sciences and technology, and while it’s very important, it also has consequences when it’s the only way you see things.

It blocks your ability to see deeper levels of the world. And to Nishitani, *religion* is going to be something that often falls into this category.

He thinks similar to the way we treat nihilism in the west, where we often try to view it totally from the outside— religion is also something that gets treated this way.

But if we always do this then our understanding of it will always be incomplete. Because religion, in some other very important sense, is something that can only be known in the subjective experience of the person that’s immersed in a religious quest.

That it's from the *inside*, the phenomenological experience *of* a religious quest that an important *vantage point* reveals itself about religion and its full scope.

The obvious first question you may have is: what is a religious quest?

To Nishitani, while there are many different ways that a religious quest can play out in terms of the specifics in someone’s life, there *is* a general movement you could expect to see going on in the internal experience of someone that's truly on one. And that is:

*A reformulation of the self and its whole relationship to its reality.*

To put it way too densely here at first—but then I’ll obviously go back and explain more of the context—a truly religious quest to Nishitani is one where a person moves from a dualistic, utilitarian framing of the world where the individual self is always the center lens that you see everything through; to one where the self is more emptied.

Whereby emptying the self we then liberate the self from the illusion of separation.

Where then, from this new liberated standpoint, the further religious quest is to discover our place within the interdependent network of being, whereby truly affirming reality at this level, we then realize that this network is something we are always already in a meaningful relationship with.

A religious quest truly begins then for Nishitani when you stop framing religion and the world around you in terms of *“What use does this serve to me?”* and start framing it in terms of *“For what purpose do I exist?”*

*This* is the shift in someone’s thinking is going to be a critical piece of what he would call any truly religious transformation.

Now don’t be deceived here.

You may hear that and think, “Well, that’s what I’m already doing! In fact, that’s what I’ve been doing in church since I was about eight years old. I’m always asking for what larger purpose I exist!”

But as your designated, philosophical sherpa here I have to advise you: you’re probably *not* doing this at the level that Nishitani’s talking about. Most people that call themselves religious don’t meet the criteria.

Because as you may have guessed, just like most people have a pretty shallow relationship with nihilism or death, most people have a pretty shallow relationship with religion to Keiji Nishitani.

The same way you can be a goth kid that wears all black, writes death poetry— you can be a platinum loyalty card holder at your local Hot Topic *and* have stock in Orange Julius and *still* not be facing death very seriously— and similarly you can be a person that sees your religion as a *cornerstone* of how you live your life everyday, but never experience religious transformation of the class that Nishitani is talking about in this book.

To explain what he means by this point forgive me for staying in my lane and using Christianity as an example again. Christians…it’s all love.

Say I’m someone who looks around me at the mostly Christians in the United States where I live. I’ve been to quite a few church services in my day, and the people there are not generally emptying the self and affirming the complexity of the interdependent network that they’re a part of.

No, these are generally people eating cookies and talking about the football game that’s going to be on later.

In other words, what’s going on at these church services for a lot of people is not all that transformative given the criteria Nishitani is interested in.

Far from this being a weekly exercise in rethinking the self and its relationship with the world, for a lot of people this becomes an *emboldening* of the self and the ego. A reinforcement of the grasp they have over the objective moral truth of the universe.

It becomes something they wield around in conversations as an authoritative stance that ends up shutting down any further discussion about complexity.

As a Christian you may hear that and think this is more than a little unfair.

Because let's say some Christians do use a service in that way sometimes. But there are also plenty of people that sit in the pews of their church when a service is going on, and they *do* feel a sense of humility in the moment. Going to church *does* make them a little less self-centered and a little more in line with their role in a larger plan that’s being carried out.

And what, is that not religion? Does that count for nothing if you’re Nishitani?

To Nishitani, it doesn’t count for nothing. But to him, if we’re being honest, it is a pretty shallow experience of the kind of transformation that something like religion has a unique capacity to provide for people.

Think of how special a domain religion is as something that can lead to spiritual development if taken actually seriously.

Think of the mystics we talked about a couple episodes ago and their experiences—a lifetime of devotion, daily focused practice towards a deeper and deeper communion with God and their own being. Think about Heidegger’s point about how the theoretical, scientific abstractions that we usually live our lives in are secondary, and how there is a more immediate connection with being that is possible. Think of Nishitani’s work on nihilism just from last post.

Religious language and devotional practice seem to have a unique ability to transform people in this area when taken seriously. There’s a unique level of guidance that’s possible at the higher levels of these religious communities. Shouldn’t we be paying respect to the differences here?

What percentage of people do you really think take their religion seriously to the point that it transforms them? And what percentage of people are using it at this point in their lives more or less like a self-help book—or as Nishitani might see it, like yet another ego-centric way of projecting themselves onto the world so they can have mastery over it?

Now before we get more into what Nishitani thinks a religious quest looks like, let’s clear a couple things up around using the word *religion* here to describe this transformation he’s talking about.

For the sake of argument, let’s buy his premise that barely anybody is taking their religion seriously in the modern world. Well then why does Nishitani need to use the word *religion* here at all?

Religion, as a word, carries with it so much baggage—he must realize that. If most people who think of themselves as religious are not authentically religious, then why not just call *them* the religious people and whatever small percentage of people are *actually* engaging with religion— why not call them, I don’t know, *contemplatives* or something?

Because this would distract from how unique of a path religion is, in all of its varying forms, as a gateway into this specific kind of relationship to being.

Just as a comparison: think of the way we use the term *university* when people go off to school.

What is most people’s experience going to a university? It’s not studying all day long. It’s not a devotional practice. Most people go to college because society tells them if they do, then they’ll get credentialed and hired in a field they want to work in. It’s essentially personal salvation for the career world—I’m just doing it for the certificate on the wall so I can make more money.

And they’ll do the assignments, they’ll study for the test at the end of the week, but if you came to most people ten years later— how much of this stuff they’re learning are they really gonna be able to remember for you?

However, you could make an argument that there is a very *small* percentage of people that apply themselves deeply when they go to a university, and that those people are uniquely capable, when they’re in a university setting, of becoming part of something bigger than themselves—part of an intellectual tradition, someone that can be a torchbearer for whatever field they’re a part of.

University then, seen from one angle, can be almost a complete waste of money. Seen from another angle, it’s one of the only places with the resources to produce someone with a very particular, highly desirable skill set.

It’s a transformative experience, in other words.

Well, so too, Nishitani thinks, with religion and religious practice. And it would be silly for us to not use the word *religion*, call people *contemplatives* or something, simply because of the shallow way religion is often practiced by people of this particular time.

Real question Nishitani might ask: is *religion* the thing that is shallow? Or are the people shallow? Do most people just usually experience religion in a dulled and appropriated way?

More than that, in reference to our atheist friend from earlier: what are these different religions all truly doing?

Is everything—from Buddhism, to Hinduism, to Christianity, Islam, Judaism—are these all different attempts to just give people a security blanket? To control people?

When these were created was there a council of people that all sat around trying to come up with a scary story to keep everyone in line?

Of course not. For Nishitani, the origins of these traditions lay in the stories of individuals. Individuals who were exploring their own experience of being at an extremely deep level.

And these people, in whatever tradition they existed in, encountered nihilism in a unique way at the bottom of their search. Then they discovered some kind of purpose or connection to something greater. And then they formulated a way to live authentically on the other side of this insight.

*These* are typically the kinds of stories we find at the origins of these religions that we know today.

In other words, these weren’t politicians trying to control people. These were individuals who, in their existential experience of the world, moved from asking questions like, “What use do these things in the world have for me and my own ego-driven agenda?” and they started asking questions like, “For what purpose do I exist in this larger network?”

Now, whatever dulled and appropriated form this may take in the lives of billions of people in the modern world is beside the point. Yeah, practically everybody is distracting themselves with one ego-driven agenda or another—their own career, their political beliefs, their TV shows, fantasies, drugs—and yes, they do it with what we often call *religion* as well. These shallow forms of religion become something people just project onto the world so they can control it.

But to Nishitani, this is transparently a way to *avoid* a truly religious engagement with the world.

Ironically, the mass-produced versions of these religious approaches end up becoming things that allow people to ask religious questions *less* frequently. To Nishitani, religion ends up blocking religious engagement.

And this place where most people live— trapped thinking only in terms of how the world serves their own agendas— this is an area of our experience that Nishitani refers to in the book as the experiential field of *consciousness*.

Think of there being three different experiential fields of awareness—three existential standpoints that you could use as a model to understand your experience of the world, if you wanted to.

One is *consciousness*, two is *nihility*, and three is *Śūnyatā*.

And understanding the movement we go through internally between these three different fields of awareness will help us understand what he sees as an authentic religious quest.

The short version of this is that a religious quest is going to be moving from primarily framing things in terms of consciousness, traveling through the field of nihility, and revealing a deeper connection with the field of Śūnyatā— in the process removing the illusion of our separation from being. Some people may say God here.

He describes each of these in the book. Nihility and Śūnyatā were the focus of the last post on overcoming nihilism. But what are some more details about this field he calls consciousness?

*Consciousness* is the field of awareness where most of us usually spend almost every second of our lives. It is representational. Meaning not only is this where representations of the actual things in the world become more real to us than the things themselves, but this is also the kind of awareness where there’s always an individual subject—or a *you*—and you’re always looking for how you relate to objects that you then try to control.

Or in the language Nishitani uses, these are objects that we “reify.”

What he means is, we’re always trying to turn these objects, that really have no fixed, durable meaning, into something that *is* fixed and durable—something we can manipulate for our own agendas.

And in order to reify them in this way, we have all sorts of tools we use in the west: Platonic forms are one commonly assumed thing. Or the idea of there being essences to things. Or we smuggle in meaning as it’s supposedly ordained to us by some external God.

*Consciousness* then is the field of our awareness where we create these elaborate theories about how subjects relate to objects for the sake of utility. It’s also the field of our awareness that keeps us in a constant state of distraction from these deeper religious questions.

And it should be said: basically all of Western philosophy, Nishitani says, goes on solely in this field of consciousness. In fact, if you look back at the history of Western thought, you’ll see that thinkers have mostly ignored these other two fields of *nihility* and *Śūnyatā*.

To be entirely clear, Nishitani is *not* saying we should reject consciousness or try to get *away* from it. We *need* consciousness and representational thinking. What he’s saying is that to only spend your life with an awareness of this realm of consciousness, and to ignore the other two, would put you in a place where you’re always projecting yourself—or your ego—onto the world, and trying to control it in some way.

But a truly religious transformation is to take this individual self from the field of consciousness and to liberate it—to understand the groundless ground that what we call the self ultimately rests upon, and then to expand our ability to see our connection to the vast network of being that we’re a part of.

Now, how exactly would this look in practice? Seems like a couple examples might be helpful for us to have here.

We’ve already talked on the podcast a bit ago about the work of the philosopher Simone Weil and her concept of attention.

Simone Weil writes about how when you come across someone and start having a conversation with them, there are different *standpoints* that you can be having that conversation from.

On one hand, you can be talking to someone, and if you’re operating in this field of consciousness that Nishitani’s talking about, you might have the entire conversation with this person filtering all of it through what use this person has to you.

How does this person benefit my own projects or agendas? How is what they’re saying here similar to what I already believe in? You may only consider differences in the ways that they view the world insofar as they are commensurate with your ways that you view things.

There’s a way to have every conversation you ever find yourself in from a dualistic, utilitarian framing where you’re ultimately just projecting yourself onto this other person.

But then there’s another way to orient yourself toward this whole process.

You can talk to a person and just try to listen to them as they are.

Where there’s no projection of the self that’s going on. You’re really just trying to receive this person on their own terms as they’re attempting to present themselves to you.

Simone Weil says a couple things happen when you do this. First, you potentially become a catalyst for this person being seen at a level they’ve never really experienced before. You become a catalyst for their own transformation.

But more than that, instead of being an ego or a self projected onto the world, you become more like an antenna for the universe to express itself through.

This is important. To Simone Weil in these kinds of moments you become a *node* that is part of a larger network.

Your existence becomes about fulfilling a larger *role* that you always already occupy, rather than always needing to be asserting yourself onto every situation.

This is a real-world, practical example of the kind of move that Nishitani’s talking about when someone undergoes a religious transformation. This is an everyday life example of self-emptying. This is an example of what Nishitani calls, “receiving the emptiness of things on their own home ground.”

Consider the anatomy of what just happened during this whole interaction. We went from the field of consciousness, where we asked: for what purpose does this person’s existence serve me and my own agendas?

Then we moved through the field of Nihility as we questioned the stable boundaries that supposedly exist between ourselves and this person.

And then we found ourselves more openly aware of the field of Śūnyatā. Something that was always there, immanent, where we started receiving the emptiness of things on their own home ground.

For Nishitani, this movement applied across the entire landscape of our being *is* the anatomy of a religious quest.

Once again, this is a move from seeing things less in terms of what utility they serve to me, and thinking more in terms of for what purpose do I exist.

Where instead of starting everyday plotting how I’m going to project myself onto the world. I might start to think more about the roles I am always already occupying, given the relationships I currently have.

Am I a parent? Am I a partner? Am I a member of a nation?

But then outside of the human-to-human relationships I have, aren’t I also part of this larger network of things in very different ways as well?

Aren’t I a part of an ecosystem where every breath I take depends on trees and phytoplankton producing oxygen, where they also depend on me?

Am I a co-creator in the life of the soil, where the food I eat to survive comes from a collaboration between microorganisms, minerals, and many other things that have been going on for thousands of years?

Am I a part of the water cycle? Where every sip of water I drink has traveled through countless rivers, clouds, even the bodies of other organisms before it’s reached this exact moment where it happens to help me?

More than just my place in an ecosystem though — just think of the technology I use.

The smartphone I have is not an isolated object. It is the culmination of labor, materials, global systems — from the cobalt that’s mined in the Congo, gallium from Kazakhstan, to the hands of the workers that put it together in factories.

Even when it comes to my thoughts— can I really say these are entirely my own thoughts? Or are they shaped by books, conversations, videos, culture created by who knows how many others since the dawn of history?

See, we fill many roles in this larger network whether we like it or not, and whether we realize it or not. And when Nishitani talks about having an authentic religious quest, it’s about the process of awakening to this interdependence.

Because when the illusion of separation is taken away from the world, you just start to feel like treating anything else around you in a self-serving way, at the expense of whatever it is, just seems like it’s you ultimately hurting yourself in some way.

Now, you can imagine how complicated this network might get. You can imagine how many moving parts there are, and how this might take somebody who has this insight— say, a Mahayana Buddhist— their entire lifetime to be able to explore.

And while there are many Buddhists that view Śūnyatā as a transcendent category— meaning it’s a state of being that you need to ascend to in some way, usually by nullifying the self or normal existence— it’s important to remember that for Nishitani, this field of Śūnyatā is immanent, not transcendent.

Meaning for him, Śūnyatā is *always* there as a part of our experience. It’s just usually there are things in the way of our ability to see it.

This is why we have to self-empty if we want to open our awareness to it — it’s to clear up room to notice Śūnyatā as it’s always there.

And certain approaches to Buddhism are filled with religious exercises that can help you try to see things in less of a dualistic way. But the key thing to remember is that the goal for Nishitani shouldn’t be to chase Śūnyatā, because that would be the very kind of projection of the self that blocks our ability to experience it. The goal would be to remove those sorts of projections, and to open ourselves to experiencing what’s already there.

Now to wrap this up today: you can probably see now why the statement *God is dead*, to someone like Keiji Nishitani, is not an anti-religious statement at all.

Because if we buy what Nietzsche’s selling there— and that to be an intelligent person living in the modern world makes it practically impossible to believe in the Western religious stories about a God that gives you a meaning to your life— then where does that leave the modern person?

Well, for many people it is exactly where Nietzsche predicted them to be. Their starting point will be an existential crisis and a need to confront nihilism.

Their starting point would be the *very* kind of engagement with nihilism that Nishitani thinks is necessary to be able to reach this deeper awareness of our own being.

What this also means then for Nishitani — and he says this in one of the first sentences of the book — is that the person that thinks they need religion the least is actually probably the person that could benefit from religion the most.

Because to be the person that sees through how superficial a lot of people’s relationship to their religion is, is to be someone actually seeking a greater level of awareness about existence. And in that case religion, tragically, usually becomes something someone might write off because they see people not taking it seriously and then assume that’s all of what religion must be.
