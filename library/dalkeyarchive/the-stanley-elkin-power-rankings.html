I can’t come up with a better opening to what I plan on writing below than to say that everything that follows is likely to be pretty silly. Very silly.

I mean, “Power Rankings” are inherently silly. No matter how you dress it up, it’s just another listicle, and listicles are all silly. And one that tries to magic up some sort of mathematical gumbo in order to pretend I’ve created a quasi-objective basis to evaluate and rank Staley Elkin’s books, is truly absurd, especially since my only motivation for doing this is to get Adam Levin to podcast with me because I want to talk to him about Mount Chicago, his most recent novel

I got a signed copy of Mount Chicago a few years back from Exile in Bookville (top 10 store nationwide) during an event for Juan Goméz Bárcena and Katie Whittemore’s Not Even the Dead (top 10 all-time Open Letter title?). Adam was there with his wife, Camille Bordas—whose How to Behave in a Crowd was also available, also signed, and which I also got and just bookmarked to start reading tonight—and we all hung out and hit it off. I saw them again, some months later at a Pilar Adón event at Exile, and same thing—exuberant, invigorating book conversation and a lot of laughs.

All of which led to Adam writing an incredible introduction to the Dalkey Archive Press Essentials edition of The Franchiser by Stanley Elkin. You’ll have to buy the book to get the full thing, but here’s the opening to give you a taste:

The trouble with introducing The Franchiser in 2025 isn’t just that doing so is, as says William H. Gass in his introduction from 1980, “the act of an upstart, an interloper, an interventionist,” but that William H. Gass, back in 1980, already wrote a perfect introduction to The Franchiser. As close to perfect an introduction, at least, as one could possibly hope to encounter.

I say so as someone who doesn’t often read introductions to novels; as someone who, when he does read an introduction to a novel, only ever reads it after having read the novel that the introduction introduces. As a reader, I fear the spoiler, reader. As an introducer, I fear it far more. Far more, even, than seeming like an upstart.

I’ve met plenty of people who believe the fear of spoilers is foolish. They like to tell you that Anna jumps in front of a train, that the wife sells her hair to buy a chain for the watch of the husband who sells the watch to buy combs for her hair, that Norman’s mother is Norman himself, and Vader Luke’s dad. I’ve heard them make highly self-assured pronouncements along the lines of, “Possessing advance knowledge of how a novel operates cannot possibly detract from the experience of reading that novel for the first time unless that novel is not a great novel,” and I believe they believe that. I do. I believe. Same as I believe those who claim they would rather sext than have sex.

I just fail to relate.

I’m not yucking yums. I wish I could relate. Relating is good. I try to relate. Am trying to relate. I mean, to sext seems so . . . hygienic. I like hygienic stuff, too. I think hygienic stuff rules.

The whole intro is fun. Actually, the whole book is fun. Elkin, for all of his literary chops, his “writer’s writer” bonafides, is just a joy to read. And I doubt anyone would say his books are hard to read, although they may be a bit confusing, as works of fiction. They’re not always Freytag’s Triangle-shaped, and lean more on extended riffs and set pieces that don’t really propel the plot in an efficient manner, which isn’t everyone’s cup of tea. But for those cool with these sorts of structural shenanigans? Elkin is, again, a joy to read.

And when Elkin gets on a heater—like with the godfather speech in The Franchiser, or God’s speech in The Living End—there’s no one better at writing comedic bits, rant and voice-driven tangents that are thrilling and laugh out loud funny, thanks to his phrasings, his rhythms.

Which is also true of Adam Levin. Especially in Mount Chicago, which features a 100-page (or 3-hours in audiobook form, the whole of which is voiced perfectly by Dennis Boutsikaris) stand-up set by Gladman, an underground cult comedian in Chicago, the seed of which was planted on page 12, when Gladman thinks to himself about how “somewhere out there or in there was a bit involving a duck and pants.”

I love this book and recommend it whole heartedly. And will inevitably bring it up about 200 times during the podcast with Adam.

As mentioned above, to try and prevent myself from doing that, and to gin up a little excitement for this Elkin-centric episode, I decided to invent an “Elkin Power Rankings.” Which, yep, is still silly. And to make it even sillier, I invented a bunch of metrics that ranged from the very concrete, through the absolutely random, to the entirely subjective. There are nine categories to be exact, and I assigned points from 1 to 12 in each, added it all up, and arrived at a detailed ranking of all twelve of Stanley Elkin’s Dalkey Archive titles.

Another selfish reason I have for spending so much time creating such a silly exercise is because over the past year and a half, I’ve reread a huge chunk of Elkin’s backlist. Thanks to the Dalkey Archive/Deep Vellum team—Sarah McEachern, Linda Stack-Nelson, and, now, Eliana Gala—print copies of all of Elkin’s books are available once again, which is such a blessing for anyone who encounters Elkin for the first time and gets caught up in his style. And having reread so many thousands of pages of Elkin, I have the urge to talk about all of his books, and not just the two Essentials: The Magic Kingdom and The Franchiser.

What you find below is information about the Elkin books that place 6 through 12 in the first ever Stanley Elkin Power Rankings.1 On the podcast with Adam, we’ll unveil the top 5, argue like we’re on sports talk radio, and hopefully have a good time discussing this sort of humor and how to write it, what makes Elkin special and worth reading in 2025, and, I’m sure, because I won’t be able to help myself, Mount Chicago.

The Rabbi of Lud [Power Ranking: 12]

First Published: 1987

First Line: “They say it’s flat as sea level and, so far as the eye can tell, it sure looks like it—some great, blunt stub of the earth, level as a table, as if the creator had meant to mimic in dirt and pure planes of real estate the dark ascensions and black declinations of space, all His monotonous deep celestials.”

Last Line: “I offered the broches you say when you see a rainbow, when you eat ripe fruit, when you hear good news, when you laugh out loud, when you buy new clothes, when you kiss a woman, when you repair an appliance, when you touch a giant, when you smell sweet wood.”

Total Power Ranking Points: 32.5

Overall, poor The Rabbi of Lud didn’t fair too well in these rankings. This might be because I don’t really remember it at all, so its “Personal Ranking” score was quite low. Also, that cover. No thank you. Not a good cover. Not the worst of the Elkin covers, but not a very good one.

The Rabbi of Lud did, however, come in first place on one of the metrics: the most italics used on page 197.

This is, yes, a silly metric. (I warned you!) But it’s also a critical part of Elkin’s style. Elkin writes to be read aloud, or at least voiced inside your head. And so he gives you clues: double em-dashes that both interrupt a thought and trail off, em-dashes followed by a space, and italics. Especially italics. (Adam Levin also uses a lot of italics in Mount Chicago.)

Since I couldn’t easily calculate which book of his used the most italics, I chose to just look at page 197 because, well, it doesn’t matter. But in The Rabbi of Lud there are eight italicized words and phrases on page 197, including two in this sentence:

But I depose that even if those holes were only for the Poverman family, two kids and their parents killed the day before in a tragic accident where no one wore their seat belts, harrowing was pretty grisly anyway, probably all the more so because nothing was disturbed— not the graves or the gravestones or the perpetual care.

Van Gogh’s Room at Arles [PR: 11]

First Published: 1993

First Line (of the titular story): “When the Foundation sent him there, Miller had absolutely no idea that he was to be put up in Van Gogh’s room in the small yellow house in Arles.”

Last Line (of the titular story): “Rendering everything unrendered, all the still lifes and unpainted masterpieces of Cannes— what shot by his all-but-shut eyelids on the bus and, spilling over onto the unpainted fourth wall of Van Gogh’s room at Arles, the ghost Rey, and the ghost Zouave, and the ghost Ginoux, and all the other ghosts, the beautiful ruin of the world he couldn’t quite catch, like everything else he couldn’t quite catch, everything untranslated and left unsyntaxed in his inadequate French, the guts, the soul, the brains and eyes, all the inner extremities and other moving parts of vision, of vision.”

Total Power Ranking Points: 42.5

Another bland cover, although it did fit a design style that Dalkey was using at the time, so it is what it is.

This is the next Elkin book I’m planning on reading, but for now, it also scored pretty low on the “personal ranking” metric, which really dinged its overall score.

But it did score really high on the “pages from the mean” metric. My idea was that it would be easy to overvalue the books of his that are at the extremes—George Mills (518 pages) and The Living End (144 pages)—and that over the course of twelve books, he calibrated his writing to the perfect length. Given that his average book is 320.67 pages long, I made that the ideal, and ranked all books by how close they were to that exact number of pages. Van Gogh’s Room at Arles is 312 pages, or 8.67 from the mean. Only The Magic Kingdom (317 pages) is closer to average.

A Bad Man [PR: 10]

First Published: 1967

First Line: “One day a young man in an almost brimless fedora burst into the office where Feldman was dictating a letter to his secretary.”

Last Line: “And indeed, he felt so.”

Total Power Ranking Points: 47

I promise this whole post/conversation will get more fun, but, well, ditto to everything I said above: why so many white covers?, and aside from being about a dude in a “Kafka-esque prison” (which is all literary prisons?), what happens in this book?, and why didn’t Elkin use more italics?

Not every title or team in a “power ranking” deserves an essay backing up its position, but I will say that I have a feeling I would really love this book if I reread it now. It’s his second novel, and in my memory, this is where he really started developing the “Elkin voice” that is so crystal clear in his follow-up, The Dick Gibson Show.

Anyway, if I reread these last handful of titles, these power rankings might shift quite a bit . . . But for now, A Bad Man can sit here in 10th, which is as much a testament to the strengths of Elkin’s body of work as it is a commentary on this novel.

Mrs. Ted Bliss [PR: 9]

First Published: 1995

First Line: “Sometimes, two, three times a year, there would be card parties, or at least invitations to them.”

Last Line: “Until all that’s left is obligation.”

Total Power Ranking Points: 53

I’m totally depressing Mrs. Ted Bliss’s power ranking score via my nonsense. The cover, the not having read it in 20+ years, the lack of any italics on page 197 . . . But it won the National Book Critics Circle Award for Fiction! It was one of two of his books to do so, and although he was honored in many ways throughout his lifetime, these two NBCC victories are probably the peak of his public appreciation.

That year, the other finalists for the NBCC were Independence Day by Richard Ford (who was a friend and fan of Elkin’s), Galatea 2.2 by Richard Powers, Moo by Jane Smiley, and The Tent of Orange Mist by Paul West (another Dalkey author).

Mrs. Ted Bliss was Elkin’s final work, and was published a few months after his passing at the age of 65. It’s about a widow, and in my Swiss-cheese memory is rather emotional, very funny, but also given the circumstances of its writing and release, also quite melancholy.

George Mills [PW: 8]

First Published: 1982

First Line: “Because he knew nothing about horses.”

Last Line: “‘Brothers and sisters,’ he acknowledged lightly.”

Total Power Ranking Points: 56.5

Finally, a grey cover!!!

George Mills coming in at 8 is probably the first real controversy on this list. After all, this is the other novel of Elkin’s to win the National Book Critics Circle Award for Fiction over Levitation: Five Fictions by Cynthia Ozick, Dinner at the Homesick Restaurant by Anne Tyler, The Color Purple by Alice Walker, and Shiloh and Other Stories by Bobbie Ann Mason. Of course, the only book written by a man won . . .

It’s also the book of his with the most Google hits at 6,400. And has a very solid opening line that set the first huge chunk of this book in motion and has a fantastic payoff. I’m certain this is the favorite of many an Elkin fan, and there’s no fault in that. It’s a wonderful book that just missed being in the top five. (It’s only 3.5 points short.) But it is very long, and some of the other titles are a bit sharper, although George Mills is definitely the best book at articulating Elkin’s Everyman.

Boswell [PR: 7]

First Published: 1964

First Line: “Everybody dies, everybody.”

Last Line: “‘Down with The Club!’”

Total Power Ranking Points: 57.5

This cover ranks high for me. (Just to be clear about my cover ranking metric, I only considered the covers from the original, early-2000s reprints. I set aside the Essentials covers, since that didn’t really seem apples to apples, whereas all the others were designed by Todd Michael Bushman and sort of fit together. Even if the steam started to run out at some point in the whole project and white covers took center stage . . . ) But what really bumped this up was that opening line: “Everybody dies, everybody.” The appeal this line has to me might say more about me than the line itself, but still, it’s a great opening line! And the closing one is solid too. “Down with The Club!” I don’t remember what “The Club” is, but fuck yes! Down with it!

This was Elkin’s first book, about a con man who “collects celebrities as his insurance policy against death.” Along with A Bad Man, I have a feeling I will absolutely adore this book when I finally reread it. My memory is that it’s one of the straight up funniest of his books, the sort of funny that comes from a first novel in which you leave it all on the field.

The Living End [PR: 6]

First Published: 1979

First Line: “Ellerbee had been having a bad time of it.”

Last Line: “‘Because I never found my audience,’ said God and annihilated, as Mother Mary and Christ and Lesefario and Flanoy and Quiz in their Y.M.C.A. seafront room in Piraeus and all Hell’s troubled sighed, everything.”

Total Power Ranking Points: 59.5

The Curtis White afterword to this book is so good that it overcomes the strangely confusing front cover. (I feel like there must be a rationale for this image, but at the moment I can’t see how it relates to the book, and instead it just reminds me of a Thirsty Ear release.) What really sold this book though is that final line, which is the summation of God’s rant in which he basically gives up on the world because he “never found his audience.” It’s brilliant, it’s free-wheeling, it pops.

For anyone new to Elkin, this is a decent starting place given how short it is, clocking in at a tight 144, and even if it’s light on italics, it’s definitely one of his funniest novels, through and through. It’s also one of the best-selling of all the Elkin books—possibly because of its length (this really is a novella), but also because this sort of fantasy about the afterlife and its metaphysics is absolute gold in the hands of such a manic, exuberant writer. It’s laugh out loud funny, which is the sort of book I’m personally gravitating toward at the moment.

Anyway, Adam and I will unveil the order of the top five titles according to my goofy power rankings on the upcoming podcast. For those keeping score at home, the final five titles are: Criers and Kibitzers, Kibitzers and Criers; The Dick Gibson Show; The Franchiser; The MacGuffin; and The Magic Kingdom.

I’m sure we’ll discuss all the weird metrics for these books on the podcast—including the best last line, best-selling, best cover, and personal favorites—but I’ll be sure to post a follow up to this post with the main stats (pub date, first & last lines, power ranking points) and a bit of commentary for the top five. (All of which I have reread recently and can add in some more specific details.)

Till then, enjoy this silliness and/or trip through Elkin’s backlist, and gear up for a heated discussion about which of his books ended up on top . . . Spoiler: It’s neither my favorite, nor Adam’s.

1
