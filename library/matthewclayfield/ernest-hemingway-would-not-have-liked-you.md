When I was twenty-five, I visited Ernest Hemingway’s grave. I was at the tail-end of a six-month trip around the United States, Mexico, and Cuba. I was carrying a small brick of old HarperCollins paperbacks, which I’d made my way through as I made my way around. (You can read my piece about Havana’s cottage Hemingway industry, which was one of my very first pieces of freelance foreign correspondence, [here](https://www.crikey.com.au/2010/11/16/letter-from-cuba-on-the-hunt-for-hemingway/).) As I [recounted later](https://medium.com/@mclayfield/geoff-dyer-for-people-who-cant-be-bothered-to-read-him-19e730c8d339), in a piece that was otherwise about Geoff Dyer:

> I was reading *The Garden of Eden* at the time, the best of Hemingway’s posthumously published novels, and brought it along in case I felt the urge to say something. But when I started reading a random passage out loud, it seemed entirely out of place. Here I was, shin-deep in snow, feeling it seep through my inappropriate boat shoes, reading about beautiful people enjoying summer (and experimenting with gender fluidity, as it happened) on the south coast of France.
> 
> Luckily, someone had brought a copy of the collected stories and left it on the grave, where it had frozen solid. I picked it up, prised it open, and pulled the pages apart until I found a [‘A Day’s Wait’](http://crmsl.weebly.com/uploads/6/3/1/4/63143381/a_day%E2%80%99s_wait_by_ernest_hemingway.pdf) from *Winner Take Nothing*. It is a story about a man with a sick child. The doctor has given the child his temperature in Fahrenheit, but the child only knows Celsius. As a result, he believes his temperature is through the roof and is worried he’s going to die. It was a good story to read, because it’s short and it was cold out. I took a selfie with the headstone and walked back to my hotel.

My interest in Hemingway never faded. I still own a small library of books about the man: Carlos Baker’s 1969 biography, Anthony Burgess’ 1985 appraisal, Lesley Blume’s splendidly gossipy *Everybody Behaves Badly*, about the writing and publication of *The Sun Also Rises*. I have been to Pamplona seven times. I know Hemingway’s grandson and great-grandson. I have watched Ken Burns’ six-hour documentary three times. (It’s good, though I still get annoyed by how it was [discussed in the media](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2021/04/12/a-new-hemingway-documentary-peeks-behind-the-myth), as though Burns had uncovered some great trove of new information, particularly about Hemingway’s relationship with gender. The documentary, which in the end spends less than five minutes on the topic, doesn’t say anything that anyone who has read *The Garden of Eden*, or any biography written since Kenneth Lynn’s *Hemingway* in 1987, didn’t already know. The best thing about the documentary is Edna O’Brien, who defends Hemingway against charges of misogyny but takes him to task for the “schoolboy writing” of *The Old Man and the Sea*. The recent documentary about O’Brien, *[Blue Road](https://youtu.be/bEErqpfb3HM?)*, is worth tracking down if you haven’t already seen it.) My point is that I’m pretty well-versed in the man.

As my friend [Dave](https://davidmaney.substack.com/) once gently warned me, though: “You should not treat Hemingway or Hitchens as models to be emulated.” (I'd write about Hitchens worship, but Padraig Reidy has [already done so](https://open.substack.com/pub/padraigreidy/p/reidys-digest-8-time-to-ditch-the).) I did and didn’t take that warning to heart. My vices are, or have been, what they are. But I can’t see myself standing at Hemingway’s grave and earnestly reading aloud to it today. What I did at the age of twenty-five is not what I would do at the age of forty.

There are plenty of others who would, though. Because I’m an idiot, I remain a member of a Facebook group of Hemingway fans. With the exception of one overly obsessive guy from India, who spams the page with old photos of Hemingway two or three times a day—and whom I once met in Pamplona, as it happens—and a woman who claims to be the granddaughter of the Finca Vigía’s Hemingway-era maid, they are almost all American men, usually somewhere north of fifty, in what appears to me to be luxurious early retirement. Based on their dress sense, I would say that the majority of them live in Florida.

My problem with the members of this group—or at least the handful of them that do the vast majority of the posting—is that their love of Hemingway is entirely back-to-front: they idolise the man, not the work. Where they do idolise the work, they idolise all of it, believing *Across the River and Into the Trees* to be every bit as good as *A Farewell to Arms*. (Believe me when I say that it isn’t. *Across the River and Into the Trees* is bad.) In a lot of cases, though, they explicitly prefer the man to the work. Indeed, it sometimes seems as though it would be better if there were no reading involved at all, and I’m sure that in a lot of cases there isn’t. This strikes me as strange. Even I, who flew to Idaho on Thanksgiving when I was already in Los Angeles and about to go home, did so because I’d just spent six months reading everything from *A Farewell to Arms* to *The Garden of Eden*. When I did come to learn about him, I mostly jettisoned him in favour of the books.

This idolisation of the life rests on a very selective reading of it. I’m sure the men in this Facebook group aren’t interested in cycling through wives, alienating friends, pretending to hunt U-boats so they can get shitfaced with their mates, contravening the Geneva Conventions, giving themselves multiple concussions, and shooting themselves at the end of it. If their travel photos are anything to go by—and the group’s page, when it doesn’t consist of memes misattributing quotes to Hemingway or else of AI-colourised photos of him and Ingrid Bergman, mostly consists of photos of these guys posing with statues in bars—what they’re interested in is travelling, drinking, attempting to hook marlin, pretending to hunt U-boats so they can get shitfaced with their mates, and, much less occasionally, running with bulls. (I suspect that more than one of them has at least tried to kill an African elephant, though they’ve been wise enough to keep those photos to themselves.) A lot of them, it goes without saying, are self-published authors. Their books are almost all about Hemingway or at least include his name in the title.

Naomi Kanakia recently wrote a piece calling Hemingway [the most important and influential American fiction writer of the twentieth century](https://www.woman-of-letters.com/p/he-is-the-greatest-20th-century-american). I don’t think there’s anything controversial about this. Being important and influential is not the same as being the best, though I personally think that Hemingway vies for that title, too. His influence, though, can’t be doubted with any great seriousness. Especially after *For Whom the Bell Tolls*—the point, coincidentally, that his own batting average began to go down—much American writing was either explicitly in debt to Hemingway or else a shot across his bow. The debt was not always obvious—Joan Didion taught herself to write sentences by [copying Hemingway’s](https://www.theparisreview.org/interviews/3439/the-art-of-fiction-no-71-joan-didion)—nor was it always stylistic. Michener owed a great deal to Hemingway, even though he wrote a very different kind of prose, and Algren wrote a strangely compelling book, *Notes from a Sea Diary: Hemingway All the Way*, that to my mind owes more to Henry Miller. Norman Mailer wanted to be Hemingway, or at least to fight him.

What Kanakia doesn’t mention is the importance and influence of Hemingway’s fame. I’m not talking about the influence of that fame on him, which was pretty obviously bad. I’m talking about that way that, from the 1930s onwards, his face could sell magazines, his life could sell products, his travel choices could sell package tours. Prior to Hemingway, Twain was the most famous author America had known. But Hemingway, as far as I know, was America’s first author-as-lifestyle-brand, and the influence of this, throughout the middle part of the century, cannot be understated. It’s arguably because of Hemingway that Mailer and Vidal could become television stars, or that Didion could become a lifestyle brand of her own. It’s ironic, given how opposed she was to the posthumous publication of Hemingway’s work—let along things like the announcement of “an ‘Ernest Hemingway Collection’ at the International Home Furnishings Market in High Point, North Carolina, offering ‘96 pieces of living, dining and bedroom furniture and accessories’ in four themes, ‘Kenya,’ ‘Key West,’ ‘Havana,’ and ‘Ketchum’”—to note how her [diary](https://www.theguardian.com/books/2025/feb/05/joan-didion-diary-notes-to-john), and her [belongings](https://www.theparisreview.org/blog/2022/11/17/at-the-joan-didion-estate-sale/), have been treated since her own death.

I am not surprised that Hemingway’s work has lasted, especially the short stories and the first two novels. What I find remarkable is that the brand has, too, and that it still in some cases overshadows the work. Hemingway has been dead a long time. A lot of his extracurricular activities, such as killing things, have gone more or less the way of the things he killed.

*The Atlantic* recently ran a piece about Thomas McGuane that asked what we will lose [“when we lose the ‘literary outdoorsman’”](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2025/12/thomas-mcguane-writing/684617/). It didn’t answer the question—which rather made me wonder why it had asked it—and generally struck me as a pretty lazy piece of writing. (Calling Hemingway’s prose “undecorated” is either a failure of reading or a wilful misrepresentation.) But its descriptions of McGuane’s characters struck me as broadly relevant to this rant:

> In a deeper sense, \[*Ninety-two in the Shade* is\] about being a man with no good wars to fight, no great causes to cling to, and no duty that calls him in a culture whose norms and customs are in flux. “Nobody knows, from sea to shining sea,” its memorable opening line reads, “why we are having all this trouble with our republic.”

There was also this, about McGuane’s more recent fiction:

> His protagonists are now mostly men stuck in middle age or older, who have realised that purpose has permanently eluded them. Strip malls, dull office jobs, emptied-out prairie towns, and frayed families dominate the foreground. For his characters, fishing and hunting are hobbies, not burning obsessions. These characters often reflect on the past—theirs, their fathers’, their country’s—and feel regret.

Read in a blinkered, romantic way, Hemingway’s life seems vital and important in a way a lot of our lives now do not. Read in a blinkered, romantic way, it scratches certain itches for the kind of man that McGuane writes about. The impulse to live lives of action and purpose, like Hemingway’s during the 1930s, is understandable, and, to me, familiar. It is also wrongheaded. As many of us learned the hard way during the pandemic, the anvil of history only looks attractive when you’re not actually on it. (You can also become a wingnut in your desperation to experience its face, as Hemingway, whose politics towards the end of his life were what we would today describe as libertarian, might well have done had he not fatally insisted upon exercising his Second Amendment rights. There’s a sad old man in Cullen Hoback’s *Q: Into the Storm,* whom I think about from time to time, telling his fellow conspiracy theorists that parsing Q’s “drops” is the most important thing of which he has ever been a part. This strikes me as a desperate unmet need for community more than anything, but it’s nevertheless related. John Hemingway, Ernest’s grandson, has himself been a vocal Trumpist since the pandemic.) There is also a strong homosocial aspect to Hemingway’s life and work that I think a lot of heterosexual men find semi-secretly appealing. There remain few places where men, at least of a certain age, can express love and affection towards one another outside of the hunting or playing field, the encierro, the bar, or the front line. I know many of them from Pamplona.

But I think the main reason the personality cult persists, as opposed to or at least alongside mere love of the work, is that Hemingway’s life gives a certain type of man permission to be something in which the rest of the world is, correctly, no longer particularly interested: a boor. This is why they use Facebook: to find likeminded boors, and bores. The great irony is that Hemingway was a deeply competitive man who saw a threat in anyone straying on his turf, literary or otherwise. (I adore *A Moveable Feast* as much as the next reader, but we’re not doing ourselves any favours by ignoring that a lot of it amounts to [literal penis measurement](https://www.michaelakahn.com/does-size-matter-an-nsfw-episode-in-american-literature/).) Not only was Hemingway not, as Dave warned me, the kind of man you want to be, he was also not the kind of man who’d want you around—or whom, I suspect, you’d want around—if you were. (“Until Bill Belichick came along, I can’t think of anybody more disagreeable,” McGuane says in the *Atlantic* piece.) Ernest Hemingway is not your friend, and not only because he’s dead. He wouldn’t have been your friend had he lived. The fact that he would most likely have loathed you is evidenced by the work.

But then these guys wouldn’t know that, having not read it properly in the first place.

While we’re on the topic of Ken Burns, I recently listened to [Ted Danson’s interview with him on the](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jv5xtEpivyE) *[Where Everybody Knows Your Name](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jv5xtEpivyE)* [podcast](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jv5xtEpivyE). His Hemingway press tour may have been misleading, but the one he’s doing for his series about the American Revolution is positively unhinged.

[I wrote about Burns’](https://www.themonthly.com.au/blog/seeing-both-sides-ken-burns-and-lynn-novicks-vietnam-war) *[The Vietnam War](https://www.themonthly.com.au/blog/seeing-both-sides-ken-burns-and-lynn-novicks-vietnam-war)* [when it came out](https://www.themonthly.com.au/blog/seeing-both-sides-ken-burns-and-lynn-novicks-vietnam-war) and I think it is a remarkable piece of work. What was striking about that press tour, compared to this one, is that Burns did it with his co-director, Lynn Novick, who mostly kept him from saying anything stupid. I do say “mostly”. While promoting *The Vietnam War*, Burns continually kept telling interviewers that Novick had to convince him to include Vietnamese voices. [“\[W\]e’re making an](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2017/09/04/ken-burns-american-canon) *[American](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2017/09/04/ken-burns-american-canon)* [film,”](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2017/09/04/ken-burns-american-canon) he said. He just didn’t get that Vietnam was a Vietnamese story as much as, or more than, an American one. As Việt Thanh Nguyễn observed in *Nothing Ever Dies: Vietnam and the Memory of War*, this is not uncommon. But it is a bit loony.

Well, Burns told Danson that he considers the American Revolution the single most important event since the crucifixion of the Christ. I guess loony is what we’re dealing with. I shouldn’t be surprised, because Burns is essentially Norman Rockwell with a movie camera, but I’m always struck, whenever I hear him speak, by how myopic and nationalistic he is. I have to remind myself that he, the man who rightly made [the history of baseball a story about racism](https://matthewclayfield.substack.com/p/children-have-hit-balls-with-bats), insisted on describing the Mỹ Lai massacre as “killing” rather than “murder,” [despite his military advisor pushing for the latter](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2017/09/04/ken-burns-american-canon).

I can deal with American exceptionalism when it’s coming from the right. I expect those people to be ignorant and insane. It’s when it’s coming from liberals that I am forced to remember that there is a brainworm abroad in the United States, from sea to shining sea.

For the record, I’d say that the most important event since the crucifixion was the invention of the printing press, but that’s because I love a bit of moveable type. We all have our biases.

If American exceptionalism has its insidious liberal forms, then Hindu nationalism, or Hindutva, has only one: an odious superiority complex born of a much more odious inferiority one. Narendra Modi, [my bête noire](https://meanjin.com.au/latest/modi-operandi/), made headlines last week by [declaring war on the long-dead 19th Century colonialist Thomas Macaulay](https://www.ndtv.com/india-news/pm-narendra-modi-spoke-of-macaulay-mindset-british-politician-india-impact-decoded-9697755). Modi said that Macaulay’s aim was to create Indians who “are Indians by appearance but British at heart,” leading, he continued, to the deep-seated belief that the Western or foreign was superior to the local. Macaulay destroyed India’s “self-confidence and instilled a sense of inferiority. In one stroke, he discarded thousands of years of India’s knowledge, science, art, culture, and entire way of life.”

It says something about India—either something positive, about the level of debate, or more likely something negative, about the fact that the man’s every utterance is worthy of air-time—that this prompted a televised debate featuring William Dalrymple and Amish Trapathi. (I know I’ve been mentioning William a lot, and I am writing a long essay about his early work at the moment, so you can expect at least a little more. To be fair, I *am* in India.)

Macaulay was a racist and isn’t really worthy of defence. But his transformation into an all-purpose bogeyman last week was tendentious in the extreme. Modi’s most transparent bit was claiming that India should spend the next ten years abolishing its sense of so-called “psychological slavery”. What he meant was that he, or at least the BJP, should remain in power for at least another decade to oversee the process. There is no way to judge the success or failure of such a campaign, so it’s all a bit of a furphy, but then most of Modi’s culture war shtick—when it doesn’t lead to [actual war](https://www.thedailybeast.com/when-they-want-war-india-and-pakistan-will-always-have-kashmir/)—is a furphy. I had two responses to his comments. The first was to think: You’ve been in power for more than a decade. It is hilarious to think that, eleven years into the BJP’s rule, he hasn’t had the opportunity to change the Indian mindset for the better. Indeed, it’s hilarious to argue that he hasn’t successfully changed it for the worst. From the very beginning, Modi and his Home Minister, Amit Shah—Palpatine to Modi’s Vader, Cheney to his Bush—established a permission structure that enabled and rewarded Hinduism’s worst and most violent instincts, towards enemies foreign and domestic alike. The second thing I thought was that you have to have a lot of gumption to admit that you’ve failed so thoroughly at governing that you have to blame the cold dead hand of Thomas Macaulay and then ask for another decade.

This is fairly standard stuff. Where Trump blames the Democrats for his policy failures, Modi blames the British Empire. (He also implicitly blames the Indian people, though this tends to go unremarked upon.) This is Hindutva’s entire game plan and always has been: over-correction to the point of silliness. That there is value in Hindu religion and philosophy seems self-evident to me. The idea that [Ganesha is proof that Indians invented gene-splicing](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2014/oct/28/indian-prime-minister-genetic-science-existed-ancient-times) is, conversely, silly. I didn’t think much of Trapathi’s arguments about ayurvedic medicine and the rest of it, but even he was saying that learning English seems pretty useful.

I was very impressed by [Kalim’s recent piece](https://themonolithbykalim.substack.com/p/a-government-by-the-insecure), which, like [Yogendra Yadev’s comments a few months ago](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/iunHVe-Q-Ik), got at something quite important, not only about Modi and Hindutva, but about authoritarians in general. They’re really insecure. They have imposter syndrome. It’s why Modi’s chest needs to be fifty-six inches and why the [Statue of Unity](https://matthewclayfield.substack.com/p/the-colossus-of-gujarat) needs to be five hundred and ninety-seven feet tall. It’s why Hanuman, the monkey god, needs to have been [the first being to have gone into space](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RbpMXNI1UH0).

This, far more than English-style schooling, is where the long tail of colonialism is at its most apparent: in the BJP’s sense that it is in fact a silly and ineffectual government, and in its need to insist very loudly that it isn’t, or that, if it is, the British are to blame. I think it explains most reactionary governments, except, perhaps, for Israel’s, which is, whatever else we might say about it, nothing if not murderously effective.

As an aside, India and Israel last week [initiated a free-trade deal](https://www.thehindu.com/business/india-israel-may-implement-proposed-fta-in-two-phases/article70313658.ece), with “technology transfer” one of its key points. Read between those lines, if you can stomach it. India is, [as Antony Loewenstein has written](https://antonyloewenstein.com/indias-the-wire-positively-reviews-the-palestine-laboratory/), already Israel’s largest weapons buyer. He has also written at length about India’s use of Israeli technology in Kashmir and elsewhere. The gradual pull of Modi into Netanyahu’s fatal orbit, to the point that India’s long-standing support of the Palestinians now seems very much a thing of the past, is to be deplored.

Nehru commissioned Chandigarh in 1950. After Partition left the historical capital of the Punjab, Lahore, in Pakistani territory, India required a new administrative centre for its own Punjabi state. Nehru, my favourite of the founding fathers after Ambedkar, saw in this necessity an opportunity: Chandigarh would embody the forward-looking ethos he believed essential for a modern India. He insisted on a break with historical styles, selecting Western modernists—first the American planner Albert Mayer, then later Le Corbusier—to design an urban environment guided by rationalism and functionality. At Chandigarh’s inauguration, he described the city as “symbolic of the freedom of India, unfettered by the traditions of the past.”

But the city also revealed the tensions inherent in this vision, especially between materials and climate, technocracy and lived reality, idealism and, if I’m being honest, India. In *City of Djinns*, Dalrymple describes the commission as “disastrous,” which I think is perhaps laying it on a bit thick. “Chandigarh is now an urban disaster,” he writes, “a monument to stained concrete and discredited modernism.” I think this might have something to do with William’s personal architectural tastes, which don’t tend much towards brutalism, though I must also admit that the place left me cold.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vvcc!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb798852b-0ff7-4994-9f9a-ee677984e365_4096x3072.jpeg)

Like Canberra, Chandigarh is a planned city, and I actually got very strong Canberra vibes the whole time I was there. The roundabouts, the lake, the sense of it being, at heart, a company town. It’s one of those cities that you can tell has been designed—come what may on the ground—to be looked at primarily from the air. (It’s designed to look, from the air, like a body, with the complex as the head or brain, the residential and commercial areas as the torso, the green spaces as the lungs.) That said, it works, and is one of the cleanest and seemingly most liveable cities I’ve visited in India outside of the hills.

But the Capitol Complex is still pretty ugly. Its concrete is streaked, almost oozing, with dirt. There is much to admire in the High Court building, its use of colour, its heat-regulating design. But as in Canberra, where, [as I wrote at the end of](https://matthewclayfield.substack.com/p/terms-of-service-c52) *[Terms of Service](https://matthewclayfield.substack.com/p/terms-of-service-c52)*, the major buildings seem inordinately spread out over the Parliamentary Triangle, like children’s building blocks left out in long grass, the Capitol Complex is mostly one vast prison yard, with the usual dust and weeds and rutting monkeys and fetid water the colour of the parliamentary backbench. It was disappointing because I wanted to love it. I was reminded, instead, of Volgograd, or perhaps of [Pripyat](https://medium.com/@mclayfield/spring-in-the-exclusion-zone-a-visit-to-chernobyl-1c9a6d1641e5).

It is in the nature of my travels in India that I always wind up missing something. It is why I wind up returning everywhere I’ve already been, even while adding new places to the list. I had no idea there was a Le Corbusier museum directly opposite my hotel, taking up the building in which the Swiss-French architect designed the city, the only time he ever applied his theories to urban design. Those theories—best summed up by his rather dystopian claim that a house is a machine for living in—were actually the basis for one of the stranger pieces of academic film criticism I ever wrote. It compared Cronenberg’s *Shivers* to Tati’s *Playtime*, the former of which is about the resistance of the human body to such buildings, and the latter of which inculcates such resistance in the viewer by teaching her, and her eyes, to wander off-grid, beyond the straight lines, and take in an entire frame, like a *Where’s Wally* spread, of termitic action. But that’s another story. In reality, these places just get old and become disgusting. I asked the guide if they ever clean the complex. He said they do so once a year, with high-powered hoses. It isn’t working.

There is a type of audience member who wants so badly for you to know that they get it, that they’re hip, that they intentionally, embarrassingly, turn themselves into a sideshow. They are almost always men.

On Saturday night, a world away and then some from [Kheerganga](https://matthewclayfield.substack.com/p/to-kheerganga-and-back), I went to the National Centre for the Performing Arts with a friend from Pune to attend Mumbai’s International Jazz Festival. The Australian bassist, Nicki Parrott, performed, as did the American guitarist Mike Stern and his band.

It was during Stern’s set—a riotous wall of sound, made strange by the fact that Stern, from a distance, could plausibly pass for Jamie Lee Curtis in an Aimee Mann wig—that people started behaving obnoxiously. In Ubud, during Sam Dalrymple’s session, two women behind me and my friend, Ted, kept trying and failing to finish Sam’s sentences, loudly. Here, Indian guys, who I assume thought they knew something about guitars, did something similar. A middle-aged guy in front of us kept playing air guitar. Three guys behind us—we were at the back of the auditorium—kept telling the sound guy to turn certain instruments up or down. At one point, the air guitar guy turned around in annoyance. Dude, I thought, you are also annoying.

Is it about wanting to be on stage? Is it about wanting people to know that you know things? What does it achieve? I understand involuntarily gasping when something is remarkable, because I involuntarily gasped myself, usually when the Australian saxophonist Blaine Whittaker was playing. The problem is trying to anticipate that remarkability, or, worse, nodding and exclaiming approval once it has happened, as though you are the arbiter, and that you saw it coming.

I give a pass to Parrott, not only because her name is fantastic, but because when I caught a glimpse of her watching Stern, down in the front bopping around like a madwoman, it was clear that she was not performing at all. She was simply into it, too far gone. The bald air guitarist in front of us looked down and thought: I wish I could be so entirely without inhibition, that I didn’t need to make sure that strangers know I know things, that I didn’t have to audibly and visibly judge things out of fear that, if I didn’t, I might myself be judged.

Actually, I have no idea what he was thinking. He probably wasn’t even looking at her. But the difference between his performative enjoyment and her actual enjoyment was something he might have done well to reflect upon. Isn’t it pretty to think so?
