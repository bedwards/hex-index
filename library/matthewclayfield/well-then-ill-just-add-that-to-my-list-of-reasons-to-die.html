When McLean Stevenson left M*A*S*H at the end of the show’s third season, he did so with all the confidence of a man who did not yet know he was committing career suicide. Like Wayne Rogers, who played Trapper John, Stevenson resented Alan Alda’s growing stardom and the show’s increasing focus on Hawkeye. “I know I will not be in anything as good as this show,” he told Loretta Swit at the time, “but I have to leave and be number one.” Things didn’t quite work out that way. After a decade-long string of one-season failures—The McLean Stevenson Show, In the Beginning, Hello, Larry, Condo—he came to an uncomfortable realisation. “The mistake was that I thought everybody in America loved McLean Stevenson,” he told an interviewer. “That was not the case. Everybody loved Henry Blake.”

What fresh Hell is this?

A spin-off of Cheers was never the plan. By 1993, when the series ended, Kesley Grammer had been playing Frasier Crane for the better part of nine years, and he wasn’t especially keen to keep doing so. The audience wasn’t especially keen on it, either. When Pew Research polled audience members about which Cheers characters they’d most like to see in their own series, Frasier garnered a paltry 2 per cent. But the network wasn’t in love with the idea that Cheers alumni David Angell, Peter Casey and David Lee had come up with—their proposed sitcom about a paraplegic magazine publisher would have been, if nothing else, unique—and insisted that Frasier was a better bet. Loathe though I am to give any kind of respect to the suits, who were doubtless thinking, like the jackals they are, that they could capitalise on the post-Cheers hangover before ignominiously calling closing time, it seems to me that they were right, especially as far as Grammer’s career was concerned. I can’t imagine we’d be talking about him now—except, of course, in the context of The Simpsons’ Sideshow Bob, the other decades-long role he’s made his own—had it not been for the suits’ insistence. It’s certainly telling that, having starred in a string of non-starters since Frasier ended in 2004—Back to You, Proven Innocent, Hank, Partners, The Last Tycoon—as well as the excellent but short-lived Boss, Grammer is once again playing Frasier in the entirely underwhelming reboot. As in the case of McLean Stevenson before him, it turns out that the audience didn’t love Kelsey Grammer. They loved Cheers and, eventually, came to love Frasier Crane as well.

Of course, the irony of that is that Frasier’s creators went out of their way to distance the show from Cheers, or at least to avoid direct comparisons with it. They set the series on the other side of the country primarily so that NBC couldn’t insist on a conga line of cameos. They also very quickly put a lot of daylight between Cheers’ Frasier and, well, Frasier’s. While they have always said that they toned the character down for the spin-off, grounding him and giving him depth as befit his transition from supporting character to lead, I don’t think that’s accurate at all. If anything, they ramped him up. Cheers’ version was occasionally a bore, occasionally haughty, occasionally on the edge of a nervous breakdown. (“I’m running with scissors!”) But the beer-drinking shrink who watched games with the barflies was a lot more grounded than the sherry-sipping collector of African art who couldn’t abide watching sports with his father. While not quite so cartoonishly arrogant in the series’ first couple of seasons—the better to throw Niles’ role as elitist fop into sharper relief—it doesn’t take long for Frasier to become a raging egomaniac. This is not a criticism, by the way. It is only once the writers turn Frasier’s arrogance and status anxiety up to eleven and start gleefully kicking his feet out from under him that the show begins to find its own.

I’ll never understand how two men like you could have been spawned by that sweet, courageous old astronaut

If M*A*S*H is three shows masquerading as one, and Cheers is the same show played twice at different speeds, then Frasier is a single episode played on an eleven-season loop. This is not a criticism, either. The template of the episode in question—Frasier allows his pomposity, presumptions, passions and priggishness to get the better of him and is punished with humiliation and celibacy as a result—is a good one. It is also custom-built for the mode in which the show best operated: farce. (It’s not surprising to learn that, a couple of months before the series ended, the five core cast members staged a reading of The Importance of Being Earnest at a Los Angeles benefit event.)

Farce litters Fraiser’s seasons like confetti. In ‘The Seal Who Came to Dinner’, Frasier and Niles discover the rotting carcass of a seal on the beach outside Maris’ seaside bungalow. Worried that the smell might detract from their dinner party, which Niles is hosting to win some award, they wrap it in one of Maris’ peignoirs, douse it in perfume, and dispose of it in the ocean. When it washes back in, Niles stabs a series of holes in it in order to encourage it to sink, leading a nosy neighbour to call the police and the guests to conclude that the brothers have committed murder. In ‘Merry Christmas, Mrs. Moskowitz’, Frasier allows himself to be set up on a date with the daughter of a woman who listens to his show. It isn’t until the daughter comes over, with her mother not too far behind, that Frasier, preparing the apartment for Christmas, learns that the mother is under the impression that he’s Jewish. Obviously, because telling the truth doesn’t occur to him, he proceeds to pretend that that’s what he is, despite the fact that there’s a ham in the oven and Niles dressed as Jesus Christ hiding a Christmas tree in the bathroom.

Of course, when I say that the show is a single episode on an eleven-season loop, I’m not being entirely accurate. For one thing, you could make an argument that the loop is only seven seasons long, ending or slackening when Daphne learns that Niles is in love with her, or perhaps ten, terminating when they marry. More relevant to the discussion here is the level of variation—from cosplaying as Christ to stabbing a seal—that exists within that repetition.

Whatever the situation, however, and however absurd its inevitable escalation, the one constant in Frasier’s use of farce is the way it always emerges from character. According to one of his English translators, French farceur Georges Feydeau—who is namechecked in the intertitles of ‘The Ski Lodge’—once told his son that the players in a farce must never be allowed to say or do anything that is not strictly demanded, in the first instance, by their characters. This is where Frasier surpasses its predecessor. In my piece on Cheers, I wrote about the show’s relationship with and debt to Fawlty Towers. It’s obvious that episodes like ‘Home Malone’ and ‘An Old-Fashioned Wedding’ were influenced by the earlier series. But the results seem superficial somehow, the silliness silly but hollow and unmotivated. Everything seems a little back-to-front, the writers increasingly shoehorning the characters into situations rather than allowing the situations to arise organically from the characters. “[I]f the audience ever says, ‘Hey, wait a minute. Why would that person do that?’ you’ve lost them,” said Frasier writer Joe Keenan in the oral history of ‘The Ski Lodge’. A Cheers episode like ‘Let Sleeping Drakes Lie’ makes this mistake and then some, ultimately collapsing on the grounds that the characters as we know them and the slapstick that they’re engaged in have next to nothing to do with one another.

While the co-creators of Frasier had left Cheers by that point of the latter’s run, it’s clear that they were very much aware of what the show was trying to do in its old age. It’s also clear that they thought they could do it better, aiming at something more polished and precise. (In the oral history, Keenan uses the word “mathematical”.) Kevin Levine, who coincidentally wrote on all three of the series I’ve written about over the past two months, defined his own approach to farce in a blog post twenty years ago. For him, the key elements were jeopardy (“something the characters need very badly and are willing to go to the greatest lengths to achieve,” such as Frasier’s desire to keep dating his girlfriend and Niles’ need to impress his dinner party guests) and lies (“a character lies and then to keep from getting caught must lie again”). You can see both of these elements at work in Levine’s own semi-farcical episode, ‘Room Service’, in which Bebe Neuwirth’s Lilith comes to town and winds up having a one-night stand with Niles. When Frasier rocks up at her hotel the next morning, hoping to rekindle their own sexual relationship, Lilith and Niles are forced to conceal, not only their encounter, but also the breakfast they ordered the night before. Hiding Niles and a serve of eggs benedict in the bathroom is all very good and well, but Lilith is also waiting for ketchup. When it rocks up, she’s at a loss to explain it, let alone to explain to the room service attendant why the breakfast cart is now in the bathroom. (When he notices that Niles has been replaced by Frasier between the delivery of breakfast and the delivery of condiments, the attendant cocks an eyebrow. When he comes back at the end of the episode—Frasier having ordered eggs benedict, too—to find that Lilith has been replaced by Niles, well, it’s fair to say that he cocks two.)

It helps that Frasier, like Fawlty Towers, is more of a comedy of manners than Cheers, and that Frasier Crane, like Basil Fawlty, is the kind of character whose obsession with status, sense of superiority, and inability to shut up and listen make the kind of misunderstandings that fuel farce all but inevitable. Watching someone try to save face and fail is always inherently funny. In ‘The Two Mrs. Cranes’—an episode in which everyone gets their comeuppance—Daphne pretends to be married to Niles in order to put off a visiting ex-fiancé. Before long, Frasier is pretending to be married to Niles’s actual wife, Roz is pretending to be the wife in question, and Marty, who doesn’t want to be left out, is pretending to be a retired astronaut. At this point, with everyone lying and trying to keep their lies straight, both women realise that the ex-fiancé is a catch and start shamelessly trying to flirt with him. He makes to leave, disgusted by the perversity of it all, at which point the attempts to save face reach a fever pitch of desperation. (You can tell that things are getting desperate on Frasier when someone deigns to tell the truth.)

DAPHNE: Really, we’re not the awful people you think we are!

FRASIER: No, the truth is we’ve been lying to you all night!

Unsurprisingly, this doesn’t do the trick.

All the motivations in this episode, and therefore all the escalations, track with the characters as we know them: Daphne isn’t interested in her ex but doesn’t want to hurt his feelings, Niles is in love with Daphne and only too happy to pretend that they’re married, Frasier will always grudgingly help his brother, and Roz and Martin are both inveterate shit-stirrers who take pleasure in watching hair-brained schemes unravel. It all makes a lot more sense, and is funnier, than Norm carrying a rich man in pyjamas around the yard.

Quick, Niles, kill five eels

While Levine’s jeopardy-plus-lies equation accurately describes a lot of Frasier’s farces, my favourites—‘Ham Radio’, ‘The Innkeepers’, and ‘The Ski Lodge’—mostly lack the supposedly necessary element of stacked or nested falsehoods. In the case of ‘Ham Radio’ and ‘The Innkeepers’, the only lies that are being told are the ones that Frasier’s telling to himself. In both cases, his deluded self-aggrandisement and inability to admit when he’s licked work a little like compound interest on the deteriorating situations in which he finds himself. Levine had something to say about this as well. “The pressure must never let up,” he wrote. “Constant roadblocks must be introduced. Complications on top of more complications. The vice tightens…and tightens…and tightens.” In ‘Ham Radio’, Frasier over-directs a radio play to the point that he turns his entire cast against him, leading to a live broadcast that’s characterised by vows of silence, petulant improvisation (Gil Chesterton refuses to allow his character to die before he has delivered the rather suggestive monologue that Frasier has cut), and more than a little sabotage. It’s also characterised by Roz’s drooling, novocaine-induced line deliveries, which are barely comprehensible.

‘The Innkeepers’ follows the same basic trajectory—pride going once more before the fall—on a larger, more cataclysmic scale. It’s the closest that either Frasier or Cheers ever came to actually besting Fawlty Towers at its own game. It begins with Frasier and Niles visiting a restaurant that they remember fondly from childhood, only to learn that it’s fallen on hard times and become bit of a shambles. They decide to buy it and do it up, namely because they fancy the idea of running an exclusive hangout for the hoi aristoi. But because of their inability to communicate with one another—they give contradictory instructions to the chef, causing him to walk out, and continually add slugs of booze to the cherries jubilee, unaware that the other is doing so, too—things on opening night go south. In addition to losing the chef, they also manage to injure the busboys and scare off the rest of the kitchen staff by announcing that someone from the immigration department is in the house. This leads to Niles taking over the kitchen, Daphne stepping in as his sous chef, Roz becoming a waitress, and Martin tending bar. (Like ‘The Two Mrs. Cranes’, this is one of the show’s great five-handers, in which every member of the core ensemble is given a moment in the spotlight.) Following an amazing sequence in which Daphne proves herself to be more than adept at killing eels—probably the funniest thing Jane Leeves ever did on the show—Roz goes to flambé the cherries jubilee, blows herself up, sets off the sprinklers, and causes everyone in the place to evacuate. As they’re leaving, one of the restaurant’s old waiters, whom the brothers have retained as a valet, drives a car through the wall for good measure. Only Basil thrashing one with a stick could be funnier.

So, it’s a threesome you want—well, I don’t do those anymore

Actually, no, ‘The Ski Lodge’ is funnier. It is to Frasier what ‘The Chinese Restaurant’ is to Seinfeld, what ‘Modern Warfare’ is to Community, what ‘Marge vs. the Monorail’ is to The Simpsons: not necessarily the show’s best episode (though it is) or its most representative (which it isn’t), but rather the one that so encapsulates its spirit that that it could, were the rest to be irretrievably lost, stand in for the whole. Perfect in its construction and execution—a Rube Goldberg machine of finely calibrated misunderstandings, a clockwork orrery of carnal rather than celestial bodies—it mostly stands as a testament to itself and to the talent and ambition that went into its creation. This, it says, is what firing on all cylinders looks like.

Everything about this bedroom farce is worth the price of admission. The guest performers and set design are perfect. The editing is a masterclass. (The bedrooms were built on different parts of the soundstage, necessitating various camera setups and resulting in the kind stop-start shooting rhythm that can occasionally be fatal when you’re shooting comedy in front of an audience. The theatrical vibe of the final cut is largely down to the edit, which creates the illusion of contiguous space and stage time and thus gives the episode its propulsive sense of movement.) I would also highlight David Lee’s direction, especially of the camera. As I discuss towards the end of this piece, Frasier was never as clever visually as it was in various other ways, but ‘The Ski Lodge’ is a good example of how a camera can tell a joke as well. Watch for the pan that reveals Guy—on the other side of the room only seconds before—sitting next to Niles on the couch. Watch for the longer one that takes in the characters’ faces as they absorb Martin’s words about the chances we don’t take, each of them drunkenly deciding, in that moment, to pursue the object of their lust for the remainder of the episode.

It’s Keenan’s writing, though, that’s the star. He doesn’t put a foot wrong. Every decision he makes—from Guy being gay to Martin being deaf to Fraiser’s final, humiliating realisation that he’s the only one who’s not being pursued—turned out to be the right one:

I realised the fun of it would be if everybody was completely unaware, or they were only dimly aware, of the person who was chasing them. So the person you were chasing never was quite aware that you were chasing them, and you were never quite aware of who was chasing you. I thought, “Everybody has to have some kind of incorrect signal that they’ve gotten the green light, so they’re not just barging into people’s bedroom with no reason to think their advances would be welcomed.” What if Martin were somebody people went to, and he kept giving people incorrect information because he was not hearing stuff correctly?

What follows is one the most madcap, ribald, and suggestive half hours the show ever put to air. It also feels, in retrospect, like a kind of high watermark for the three-camera sitcom. I’ll come back to this a little later.

In retrospect, I’m reasonably sure that you’re not the devil

While the show managed to avoid Cheers cameos in its first season, it was not, in the end, without them. Not including Bebe Neuwirth’s appearances as Lilith—there are only twelve, but like the scenes of gratuitous violence in American Psycho they colour everything around them to the point that you’d swear there had been more—there are four crossover episodes. With one exception, which we’ll come to in a moment, they are not especially strong entries, but they are nevertheless of interest for the way they colour and complicate Cheers and its ending. What is especially interesting about this is that they were helmed by Cheers’ creators themselves: James Burrows directed three of the episodes and either one or both of the Charles brothers co-wrote all four. In doing so, they corrected some of Cheers’ mistakes—its lionisation of Sam, its treatment of Diane—and made it at least a little bit clearer that the series finale was not in fact a happy ending.

This is especially true of Sam’s episode, ‘The One Where Sam Shows Up’, in which Sam, well, shows up, having run out on the woman he was about to marry. Can there be any doubt that this man is broken? That nothing has changed since the last time we saw him? Even before the episode trots out its get-out-of-marriage-free card—the revelation that Sam’s fiancée, Sheila, once slept with Cliff, the writers’ favourite punching bag—there’s never really any question that he’s actually going to go through with it. There is never any question of forgiveness, either, despite the fact that Sheila is, like Sam, a recovering sex addict. He, of all people, should understand. But that would require him to be more than the narcissistic, commitment-averse cad he was for the entire duration of Cheers’ run, the guy who never tired of the chase—he puts the moves on Daphne, to Niles’ chagrin—but whose one true love was and remains a long piece of varnished wood with a brass railing. (The casual cruelty of Cheers creators has long gone overlooked. Even the little that did change for the better at the end of the series’ run has, they inform us, reverted to the depressing norm. Rebecca’s husband? Got rich and dumped her. “She’s back at the bar.” “Working at Cheers again?” “No, she’s just back at the bar.”) ‘The One Where Woody Shows Up’ is a sweeter, more melancholic affair in which Frasier and Woody, who had nothing in common but the bar to begin with, realise that memories alone are insufficient to sustain an ongoing friendship. ‘Cheerful Goodbyes’, in which Frasier, Niles, Daphne, and Martin visit Boston and wind up attending a going away party for Cliff, is little more than a half-hour of fan service, though Rhea Perlman has a meltdown for the ages when Cliff announces that he’s decided to stay. (She tries to kill him with a harpoon gun.)

‘The Show Where Diane Comes Back’ is different, a genuinely cathartic experience on multiple on- and off-screen levels. Having left Frasier at the altar in Cheers’ fourth season, and the show itself at the end of its fifth, Diane arrives in Seattle for the debut of her new play. While Niles advises Frasier to be honest and to tell Diane about the pain that she’s caused him, Frasier has a better idea: he will rub his success in her face instead. But Diane, characteristically oblivious, doesn’t even notice, bragging about her own meteoric career until the play comes up and her face begins to twitch. It turns out that her producer has backed out and that things aren’t as rosy as she’s been letting on. Frasier agrees to produce the show himself, but is gutted when he attends rehearsal and discovers that it’s a piece of tepid autofiction about Cheers and Diane’s love for Sam. When the actor playing the thinly-veiled Frasier character stops the rehearsal to ask for direction—he isn’t sure how he’s meant to be feeling in the wake of being left at the altar—Frasier explodes: “What you are feeling is that this woman has reached into your chest, plucked out your heart, and thrown it to her hell-hounds for a chew toy! And it’s not the last time either! Because that’s what this woman is! She is the devil!” He storms out having said his piece.

But he eventually comes back. In the final moments of the episode—before trying to walk out the bar’s front door, having forgotten that the whole thing’s a set—Frasier tells Diane what she meant to him in the past, and she tells him him what he meant to her. While her life remains stunted because of her association with Cheers (I really cannot stress enough how much damage that bar did to everyone who spent time there), this feels like a moment of grace for them both, and is a gentler, kinder, more meaningful goodbye, to character and actress alike, than either of those they were afforded on Cheers.

The bar has, according to Grammer and Burrows, closed in the world of the Frasier reboot. I’ve only watched one episode of the new series and then only to see Bebe Neuwirth as Lilith. I imagine it will be her final appearance in the role, or in case very close to it. (I can always rewatch the criminally cancelled Julia when I need a Neuwirth and David Hyde Pierce fix.) Neuwirth remains as good as ever, but the whole thing left a metallic taste in the mouth. Levine has said that, when writers from Frasier’s original run came in to share their ideas for the reboot, those ideas had not been well-received. There have also been grumblings about the reboot’s unwillingness, despite Frasier’s return to Boston, to even mention the bar’s name. Nevertheless, Grammer has reportedly expressed interest in bringing Diane back one last time. There are things, he says, that remain unsaid. This is patently absurd. What needed to be said was said in ‘The Show Where Diane Comes Back’. Frankly, I hope Long says no.

I’m a humane man, but in the mood I’m in I could kick a kitten through an electric fan

After completing M*A*S*H and Cheers in their entirety, I was a little bit hesitant to do the same with Frasier. For one thing, it lacked the historical interest that the other two had held for me. M*A*S*H predates me by more than a decade, and Cheers’ fourth season premiered exactly two weeks after I was born. That was not the case with Frasier, which aired throughout my adolescence and ended in my first year of film school. (I can’t remember ever watching it, though. My family was more into Home Improvement and The Nanny.) For another, based on what I had seen, it didn’t look particularly different to anything else that was airing at the time. I am glad to have corrected the oversight, not least because I think that Frasier is a kind of masterclass in sitcom writing. While the ‘The Ski Lodge’ demonstrates its bona fides at the level of structure, it’s the sheer auditory delight of the show’s dialogue that really floats my boat. Frasier and Niles’ pretensions allow, and in fact demand, a significant amount of writerly play, whether in the form of intellectual allusion, double entendre (Frasier is almost Allenesque in his obsession with sex), or archaic, acrobatic syntax. (“It’s almost as if you’ve forgotten that, not three days ago, I was punched in the face by a man now dead!”) From the beginning, the writers granted themselves the freedom to be as smart (or smarter) than their characters, never deigning to dumb things down for the audience, whom they trusted would be able to keep up. This results in some of the most enjoyable dialoue of its time, on par with, though obviously different to, Seinfeld’s neologism- and catchphrase-heavy writing.

At the same time, I don’t think you can reasonably argue that Frasier comes close to M*A*S*H for experimentation, or to Cheers for subtle, intelligent camera direction. Like every other three-camera sitcom of the 1990s, with the exception of Seinfeld in its more cinematically ambitious years, it’s a largely disappointment as far as the eyes are concerned. ‘The Ski Lodge’ may be a semi-exception to this rule, but the times were changing, or had already changed. The Larry Sanders Show, which finished the year that ‘The Ski Lodge’ aired, had already created the visual template (and plenty of other templates besides) that would go on to inform the single-camera sitcoms that would follow, from Curb Your Enthusiasm and Veep to both versions of The Office. Malcolm in the Middle, which would premiere a couple of years later and immediately be labelled a “live-action Simpsons”, would do something similar with its whip pans and cuts-to-black in advance of shows like My Name is Earl, Arrested Development, Community, and 30 Rock. (As for The Office, especially in its American incarnation, it would go on to have an even greater, more deleterious impact than any of them by convincing the world that a documentary crew would spend multiple years in an office or local government building or the various houses of a large extended family. My favourite moment of Big Mouth is the one in which Coach Steve is being interviewed for some reason and points out the inherent laziness of the mockumentary format: “Boy, these straight-to-camera testimonials are great for narrative structure. They’re a crutch, but they cut right to the chase.”)

Of course, it’s unlikely that a prolix, farce-heavy show, three-camera or otherwise, was likely to inspire many imitators to begin with. But then, by the early 2000s, three-camera sitcoms in general were unlikely to inspire many imitators. There would still be huge, juggernaut-like hits—the kind of slop that has made Chuck Lorre a wealthy man—but the format itself had ossified and the interesting work was being done elsewhere. This is obviously a wild generalisation, but the trajectory I’m describing, the vibe of the thing, was real, and plenty of people were saying so at the time, long before the results were in. The reviews of Frasier’s series finale were often elegiac as a result, with a number of critics, such as Dana Stevens, lamenting the demise of “situation comedies for adults”. This was obviously more than a little hyperbolic. But in terms of the three-camera sitcom, at least, it was probably also more than a little right.
