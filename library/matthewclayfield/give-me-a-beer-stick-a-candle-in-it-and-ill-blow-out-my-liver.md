When the weight of the world has got you down, and you want to end your life—bills to pay, a dead-end job, and problems with the wife—well, don’t throw in the towel, because there’s a place right ’round the block where you can drink your miseries away.

Wait. No, that’s [the](https://youtu.be/-EwwJnJPs8A?si=47uZoQS4MhoypAT1) *[Flamin’ Moes](https://youtu.be/-EwwJnJPs8A?si=47uZoQS4MhoypAT1)* [song](https://youtu.be/-EwwJnJPs8A?si=47uZoQS4MhoypAT1). This is a piece about *Cheers*.

But I suppose it’s only appropriate that I once again open with *The Simpsons*. If my childhood awareness of *M\*A\*S\*H* was [due to re-runs](https://matthewclayfield.substack.com/p/my-guts-are-not-here-for-you-to-love), which led into *The Simpsons* on weekday afternoons, then my awareness of *Cheers* was based almost exclusively on the animated series’ parodies of it.

I was also aware of its reputation: it consistently tops, or nearly tops, lists of the greatest sitcoms ever made, regularly edging out *M\*A\*S\*H* and, somewhat less regularly, *Seinfeld.* (On the last such list I read, it came second to—what else?—*The Simpsons*.) But unlike *M\*A\*S\*H*, I had never seen an episode until recently. For whatever reason, *Cheers* wasn’t really syndicated here. (My first exposure to Ted Danson was through *Three Men and a Baby* and its sequel, and, on television, *Becker*. These are not the sort of productions that send one racing to the archives for more.) In any case, after finishing *M\*A\*S\*H* in its entirety a couple of months ago, I decided to give *Cheers* a try.

The show arrives fully formed. Not only does it know, from the moment it begins, exactly what it wants to be, it also manages to be that thing, or at least a very convincing version of it. The thing in question is a 1930s or 1940s romantic comedy, with sexual chemistry and cutting dialogue providing the momentum that a plot usually might. (The show doesn’t leave the bar at all during its first season and I am honestly at a loss to recall a single actual story from it.) The show’s creators—Glen Charles, Les Charles, and James Burrows—liked to cite Tracey and Hepburn as their models, but what I am reminded of, when I watch Sam and Diane, is *His Girl Friday*, with Danson in Carey Grant’s role and Shelley Long in Rosalind Russell’s. Watch any of their verbal sparring matches—[not least the lengthy, contentious one that ends the first season](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=02qbFlzivW0)—and note the way their bodies advance, ostensibly in the interest of saying something cutting, but actually in the interest of something else, then retreat in exasperation, only to immediately repeat the process. While the show’s physical comedy would become broader in later seasons—think [Carla kissing Norm half to death](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rrQ9H7bR4yg) in season eight—the show’s real bread-and-butter, at least at the beginning, was this older, more Hawksian, Sturges-like style, which denied or at least deferred physical intimacy and so turned banter and repartee into sex. Like Sam Malone’s, the show’s self-confidence on this front was preternatural. Unlike Sam’s, it wasn’t misplaced.

This confidence extended to almost every aspect of the show’s production. In order to maintain a consistent style, Burrows remarkably (and I think uniquely in sitcom history?) directed all but thirty-eight of the series’ two hundred and seventy-five episodes. He told the actors that they had to be on at any given moment lest his constantly-moving camera—think of all those rapid dollies from one end of the bar to the other—found them rubbernecking when they were supposed to be drying glasses. According to Danson, it was, as a result, occasionally more like being in a play than being on a television show. Burrows was so confident in both the writing and his style that he rarely shot things twice: “I only reshot jokes that didn’t work or I went back and picked up shots I missed.” During the first season, he was told to shoot video in order to save money. He ran a couple of tests and said that video made the set look ugly. He continued to shoot on film, costs be damned. (In a couple of episodes, you can see that he was right, in scenes where a shot or two has been lost and the video backup has been spliced in. The richness and warmth of the bar give way to something the colour and texture of wet cement.) This was, as I say, a supremely confident show.

Nevertheless, like *M\*A\*S\*H* before it—and *Seinfeld* after it—*Cheers* wasn’t at all an instant hit. Given the times in which we now live, when network shows are told to succeed within a couple of episodes or die, and in which streaming shows, even when they do succeed, are regularly killed off after one or two seasons on the grounds that they have attracted as many new subscribers as they are likely to ever attract, it is strange to remember that executives used to go into bat for shows they believed in, occasionally for several years, even when those shows were rating so badly that the executives in question might well have been forgiven for slowly backing away. Strange, too, when you haven’t had free-to-air television in nearly a decade, to remember that something’s time slot can determine its fortunes far more than its quality. (*Seinfeld*’s own fortunes only began to improve after it started airing after *Cheers.*)

Danson is characteristically winning as Sam, to the point that one regularly misses the fact that Sam is not a very good person. But they caught lightning in a bottle with Long. When I was growing up, I knew her primarily as Carol Brady in the two (very strange) *Brady Bunch* movies. In *Cheers*, she is a supernova, or perhaps a black hole, effecting, or perhaps infecting, every scene she’s in. A lot of this is down to her chemistry with Danson, which is as undeniable as it was fraught. Like the characters they played, Long and Danson had different, even incompatible, styles of working: he was instinctive, open to the moment, and she was particular to the point of persnickety. While Danson has admitted to telling the producers that casting Long was [“a bad idea”](https://www.newsweek.com/ted-danson-reaction-cheers-shelley-long-bad-idea-1951258)—and that he occasionally found it difficult [to be in the same room as her](https://www.cracked.com/article_43533_ted-danson-says-it-was-sometimes-hard-to-be-in-same-room-as-shelley-long.html?newsletter-cat=movies-tv)—he has also said that he was wrong about her and that she was ultimately the best thing about the show’s first season. She certainly brought out the best in him, usually by bringing out the worst in Sam, the clash between their styles and temperaments mirroring the characters’ own. Danson is never as good, or as close to having a nervous breakdown, as he is when he’s with Long. He loses a certain unpredictable volatility and becomes goofier in the series’ second stretch. (Perhaps this is a good thing. Even some of their most famous moments—as when he threatens to [“bounce \[her\] off every wall in this office”](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=02qbFlzivW0) or when they stand there [slapping one another in the face](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4IWLRlr3W2Y)—make for uncomfortable viewing today.)

Even more of Diane’s impact is down to the writing. While there is nothing especially original about a fish out of water scenario, it’s comparatively rare that the fish in question doesn’t know that it’s flopping around on the dock, let along that it thinks that now is the time to recite some of its poetry. Diane’s cheerful, wide-eyed obliviousness to reality—made all the more amusing by her unwavering certainty that she’s the smartest person in the room—is matched only by Long’s abilities as a physical comedian and her unflagging willingness to make herself look ridiculous. Consider [‘Diane’s Perfect Date’](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0539732/), when she bursts into the bar looking like a crazy cat lady after a blind date with an ex-con, or [‘One Last Fling’](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0539824/), when she bursts out of a novelty cake at Sam’s bachelor party, dressed in a corset and furious at him for what he’s been saying in her supposed absence.

There’s no question in my mind that the Sam and Diane years are *Cheers*’ best. (The classic moment—“Are you as turned on as I am?” “More”—has been endlessly referenced, parodied, and ripped-off for a reason.) In the two hundredth episode, Kirstie Alley says that her first appearance as Rebecca was one of the scariest moments of her career on the grounds that she wasn’t Shelley Long. She needn’t have worried, because she’s excellent, too (though the show eventually runs out of things to do with her), but there’s no getting around the fact that Dianne was a once-in-a-blue-moon character, the squarest of square pegs, and that Long, playing her, nailed her to the wall.

Then she left. While Long’s reasons at the time were that she didn’t want to play will-they-or-won’t-they forever and was interested in pursuing a film career, I can’t help but feel that there’s more to it than that. Beginning in *Cheers*’ fourth season and continuing through to her departure at the conclusion of its fifth, the show becomes increasingly cruel to Diane. Like *M\*A\*S\*H*’s later treatment of Frank Burns, it leans more and more into the character’s worst qualities, to the point that she becomes a caricature. Her behaviour—whether litigating Sam into proposing to her again or inviting the former owners of their new house to celebrate the holidays with them—becomes so erratic and off-putting that a 1987 profile of Danson, written before the season finale had aired, [suggested](https://people.com/archive/cover-story-ted-danson-leers-again-on-cheers-vol-27-no-19/) that “the smart money says Diane goes bonkers and gets carted off for another, longer than usual, nervous breakdown”. I might have left as well.

In my piece on *M\*A\*S\*H*, I wrote that *Cheers* “is the same show played twice at different speeds”. This is not to say that it simply repeats itself: repetition with variation is key. Where Diane walked into the bar and never left—until she did—Rebecca walks in and blindsides Sam by telling him that his services are no longer required. While the show certainly has fun, for a couple of seasons, teasing a romance between the two—which is putting it a little strongly, because all Sam really wants to do is hook up—it becomes pretty clear pretty early on that this isn’t a will-they-or-won’t-they affair so much as a how-many-times-must-she-say-no one.

More important than the introduction of Rebecca is the reintegration of the rest of the cast into the warp and weft of the show. After five years focusing on its central couple, *Cheers* opens its eyes and realises, not that it’s been underserving its supporting players—it hasn’t been—but rather that it’s paid scant attention to what it actually means to be a regular at a bar. That means helping one another out, running errands for one another, driving people places, and so on. It means becoming, if not friends exactly, at least acquaintances with benefits, a community. There is a lot more of this kind of thing in the second stretch of the series.

This is what I mean when I talk about different speeds: *Cheers* repeats itself, first as romcom, then as broader ensemble comedy. Norm, originally an accountant, becomes a roaming jack-of-all-trades. Woody falls in love with a dopey socialite. We spend way more time with Cliff and his mother than we ever have before, even as the show, through Carla (and increasingly Norm), becomes ever nastier towards him. (I’m possibly overthinking it, but I do think it’s interesting that, as soon as Diane leaves, the show, which was never especially nice to Cliff to begin with, becomes positively disdainful of him. It is though the writers needed someone, not only for Carla to loathe, but for them to loathe themselves. Over the course of the last six seasons, Cliff, too, is given what I have come to think of the Frank Burns treatment.)

As I mentioned earlier, the comedy becomes broader and more physical in the later seasons. The set pieces are more elaborate, too. This is doubtless a function of multiple things: larger budgets, a desire to shake up the format, the need to come up with twenty-six new stories every year. (You see the same thing happen with *Seinfeld* a few years later.) I’m certain that at least one of the reasons is [the influence of](https://www.insidehook.com/television/cheers-television-show-creation-setting) *[Fawlty Towers](https://www.insidehook.com/television/cheers-television-show-creation-setting)* [on the show’s creators](https://www.insidehook.com/television/cheers-television-show-creation-setting). Always a touchstone for Burrows and the Charles brothers—they initially saw *Cheers* as being an American version of the show and early drafts of the pilot were set in a dowdy Las Vegas inn—you can see its impact clearly in a number of the second stretch’s more elaborate episodes. In [‘Home Malone’](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0539763/), Sam is babysitting Frasier and Lilith’s son, Frederick, when the latter locks himself in the bathroom. Sam climbs out the bedroom window and into a tree, in order to reach the next window along, at which point Frederick reemerges, walks over to the bedroom window, and locks it. Hilarity ensues. In [‘Let Sleeping Drakes Lie’](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0539791/), Norm, who has been painting Evan Drake’s bedroom, comes up with increasingly ludicrous ways to get Drake out of bed (such as claiming that it’s his fantasy to carry a rich man across the lawn in his pajamas) so that Rebecca, who is hiding under it, can get out of the house. She is ultimately rescued by a group of guys from the bar, who spirit her out the window and down a ladder.

Perhaps I’m misremembering *[Fawlty Towers](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IsDFXE6aFG4)*[’ love of ladders](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IsDFXE6aFG4), but this is all very familiar in its contours. It’s also a very long way from the bar and from *Cheers* in its *His Girl Friday* mode. Like [‘An Old-Fashioned Wedding’](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0539688/)—complete with its very *Fawlty-*esque dead priest—such episodes anticipate the high farce that *Frasier* would later perfect and make its mainstay. (I won’t spend much time on Frasier here—I’ll be writing about him and his spin-off soon—except to say that, as with Long, the creators clearly realised they’d caught lightning in a bottle with Kelsey Grammer, too. The only thing more electric was Grammer with Bebe Neuwirth, the pair proving that Danson and Long weren’t the only ones with undeniable chemistry. It’s rare that one show gets to become such a combustible science lab twice.)

I have been a regular at bars on a number of occasions. My first novel, *A Death in Phnom Penh*, is about the guys I knew in Ho Chi Minh City, at a place called Gumbo’s, ten years ago this year. My novella, *[Terms of Service](https://matthewclayfield.substack.com/p/terms-of-service)*, is largely set in and around the Durham Castle Arms in the Canberra suburb of Kingston. I wouldn’t give either of those experiences back, as both gave me stories and lifelong friends. (The true test of a bar-born friendship is one’s ceasing to be a regular at the bar.) It is true that one’s local can and often does foster a sense of community. It is true that people often become close, and help one another out, whose lives would scarcely have brushed up against one another except in such a setting. Sometimes you really *do* want to go where everybody knows your name.

But I’d be lying if I said that *Cheers* doesn’t sometimes cause me to cock my eyebrow a bit.

I’ve had my struggles with alcohol and have been [very open about them](https://meanjin.com.au/latest/not-drinking-to-my-health/). I’ve also written at length about [addiction as it has pertained to others](https://www.themonthly.com.au/blog/end-road-anthony-bourdain-documentary-roadrunner). I don’t want to rehash it all here because that isn’t what this post is about. I also don’t want to suggest that I think a half-hour sitcom is under any obligation to deal with alcohol abuse merely because it’s set in a bar—I love *It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia*—still less that I would expect a forty-year-old sitcom, a product of its time, to do so. But I do think it’s strange that *Cheers* never gets into it in any meaningful way. Yes, we are told that Sam had a drinking problem when he was a relief pitcher for the Boston Red Sox. Yes, we are told—although I don’t remember it being said so explicitly—that this precipitated the end of his career. We see him fall off the wagon once, in the first episode of the third season, though this is merely a pretext on the writers’ part to get Diane back to the bar. He is sober again by the following episode and his relapse is never spoken of again. (By the end of the show’s run, his alcoholism is hardly mentioned at all, though he is, by that time, in therapy for sex addiction.)

That’s pretty much all *Cheers* has to say on the subject. Norm’s drinking—which begins when the bar opens and ends when it closes—is played exclusively for laughs, despite the fact that, [in the words of George Wendt](https://www.deseret.com/1990/10/16/18886405/george-wendt-norm-reflects-on-his-tv-series-life-and-drinking/), the character “is an indictment of what hanging out in a bar can do for you.” He ignores his wife, ignores his tab, can’t hold down a job, and is deeply unhealthy. We nevertheless shake our heads in amusement when it turns out he has a set of keys to the place. (Meanwhile, Carla drinks while pregnant without anyone saying so much as boo.) The network was adamant that Norm, in particular, never appear to be getting loaded, which is all very good and well, I suppose, in the context of commercial television, though I don’t know what message you think you’re sending by showing a guy drinking fifteen beers and *not* getting drunk, either. It’s the uncritical nature of *Cheers*’ anti-wowserism that causes moments of pause for me. It’s the way in which that anti-wowserism at times tips over into other, more questionable positions. (There is an anti-intellectual streak to the show that starts at Diane and runs through Frasier and Lilith to any number of minor bit players.) It’s the way it depicts drinking at scale as not having any consequences that gets my goat.

*‘One for the Road’ (S11E25) (Burrows, 1993)*

[The final scene of the series](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hP21b-AAXJk) throws the strangeness of some of its positions into sharp relief.

It’s closing time. Having briefly eloped, Sam and Diane have taken advantage of a well-timed flight delay to realise they’re not right for one another. (Long returned for the series finale.) Sam has returned to the bar and the series regulars have all made their exits. Norm, too, is on his way out.

> NORM: Sammy, can I let you in on a little secret?
> 
> SAM: Sure.
> 
> NORM: I knew you’d come back.
> 
> SAM: You did?
> 
> NORM: You can never be unfaithful to your one true love. You always come back to her.
> 
> SAM: Who is that?
> 
> NORM: Think about it, Sam.

Sam stands alone behind the bar, looks at it a moment, and laughs. “Boy, I’ll tell you,” he says to no one. “I’m the luckiest son of a bitch on earth.” He places a palm on the wood, gives it a light pat, and turns away. A latecomer knocks and Sam—in a shot that, intentionally or otherwise, [directly recalls the final shot of Cassavetes’](https://youtu.be/Yxv0U9EDUrY?si=G0RQQJd9Htwyb_Gt&t=149) *[Love Streams](https://youtu.be/Yxv0U9EDUrY?si=G0RQQJd9Htwyb_Gt&t=149)*—waves him away, telling him, and us: “Sorry. We’re closed.”

There’s something about this scene that reminds me of *[Breaking Bad](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=J9RP-KfvdKc)*[’s final moments](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=J9RP-KfvdKc), when Walter White, bleeding out from the gut, wanders through the neo-Nazi’s meth lab, smiling as he handles the accoutrements of his trade, the luckiest son of a bitch on earth. The affection, even nostalgia, he displays for this shiny, miserable junk is perverse. He places a palm on one of the tanks, as fondly as Sam placing his own on the bar, before falling over backwards, dead.

Obviously, *Cheers*’ final moments are not nearly so extreme. “Sometimes he hurts me and seems to like it,” Diane says in one of the series’ more unsettling moments, diagnosing Sam’s toxicity with striking simplicity. It seems to me that sometimes he hurts himself as well, and seems to like that, too. He walks out into the darkness of the pool room, as alone as he ever was.

You can never be unfaithful to your one true love, but it’s still better that you love the right thing.
