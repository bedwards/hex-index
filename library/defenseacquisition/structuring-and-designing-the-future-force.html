Overmatched

The NYT did a 3-part series on the potential conflict between the U.S. and China and our likely preparedness for that level of protracted warfare. Below is a short summary of those pieces.

  • Why the U.S. Military Needs to Reinvent Itself

  • This Is the 21st-Century Arms Race. Can America Keep Up?

  • America Pours Billions Into the Military. We Don’t Know Where It All Goes.

Summary Points

  • Pete Hegseth said in the Pentagon’s war games against China, “we lose every time.”

  • When a senior Biden official received a China brief in 2021, he realized that “every trick we had up our sleeve, the Chinese had redundancy after redundancy.”

  • The assessment shows the Pentagon’s overreliance on expensive, vulnerable weapons as adversaries field cheap, technologically advanced ones.

  • It is an ancient and familiar pattern. Despite ample warnings, leaders trained in one set of assumptions, tactics and weapons fail to adapt to change.

  • A strong America has been crucial to a world in which freedom and prosperity are far more common than at nearly any other point in human history.

  • One reason is inertia in Congress and the Pentagon. The channels through which funds flow to weapons systems are deep and difficult to reroute.

  • An entrenched oligopoly of five large defense contractors wants to sell the Pentagon ever-costlier evolutions of the same ships, planes and missiles.

  • Another factor is military culture. Senior officers tend to be wedded to the technologies and tactics in which they made their careers.

  • There is also a conceptual failure: the idea that more sophisticated is always better - with systems that are bespoke, complex and wildly expensive.

  • In the short term, the transformation of the American military may require additional spending, primarily to rebuild our industrial base.

  • Ultimately, a stronger U.S. national security depends less on enormous new budgets than on wiser investments. Spending heavily on traditional symbols of might risks shortchanging the true sources of American strength: relentless innovation, rapid adaptability and a willingness to discard old assumptions.

  • DoD has built a gilded fortress of people and processes that is slow, wasteful and married to the past. Of all the obstacles to fielding the military that America needs, the Pentagon’s bureaucracy may be the hardest to overcome.

  • Bureaucracy may sound boring, but the safety of the country — and our allies — depends on officials getting serious about how it finances and equips our military.

  • The problem starts with Congress. The proposed 2026 defense budget is loaded with pork for unnecessary programs.

    • $300M+ that DoD didn’t ask for to buy and upgrade Humvees.

    • $240M for Gray Eagle, the Model T of UAS that the Army labeled obsolete.

    • $360+M for an Apache helicopter that the Army didn’t request.

  • Bases the government targeted for closure more than a decade ago remain open.

  • There are some signs that the current administration may be willing to change. The Army has canceled the unsafe and unreliable M10 Booker light tank and other problematic programs.

  • Mr. Trump should push for real change by forcing the Pentagon to experiment with market approaches to budgeting in place of centralized planning.

  • He should force services to place bets on start-up companies that get results rather than established ones that have mastered the broken system.

Our Take: These pieces highlight some key points. Encouragingly, there is strong progress in pockets throughout DoD…and hopefully the implementation of the Acquisition Transformation Strategy will scale that even further.

Pentagon Plan Calls for Major Power Shifts Within U.S. Military

Senior Pentagon officials are preparing a plan to downgrade several of the U.S. military’s major headquarters and shift the balance of power among its top generals, in a major consolidation sought by DoW leadership.

  • If adopted, the plan would usher in some of the most significant changes at the military’s highest ranks in decades.

  • It would reduce in prominence the headquarters of U.S. Central Command, U.S. European Command and U.S. Africa Command by placing them under the control of a new organization known as U.S. International Command.

  • Such moves would complement other efforts by the administration to shift resources from the Middle East and Europe and focus on expanding military operations in the Western Hemisphere.

  • The senior defense official said the proposed realignment is meant to speed decision-making and adaptation among military commanders.

  • The plan also calls for realigning SOUTHCOM and NORTHCOM, which oversee military operations in the Western Hemisphere, under a new headquarters to be known as U.S. Americas Command (AMERICOM).

  • The Pentagon has shared little to no details with Congress, a lack of communication that has perturbed members of the Armed Services committees.

  • Other remaining combatant commands would be INDOPACOM, CYBERCOM, SOCOM, SPACECOM, STRATCOM, and TRANSCOM.

Our Take: We have a different view on whether it makes sense to consolidate and reduce command structure at the different CCMDs or to centralize more and focus most resources on INDOPACOM and NORTHCOM.

Point: While streamlining measures always needs to be explored, merely reducing the number of 4-star GO/FOs and consolidating combatant commands does not equate to efficiency or effectiveness. The complexity of global relations requires a combatant commander in today’s military to be part diplomat, part warrior, part strategist and part technologist to manage the many equities that come across their desk. Forming relationships is as much a part of their job as any ambassador. Some have equated their roles to that of a Roman proconsul which Rome had 28 of across its provinces (which mostly constituted of Europe and smaller parts of Asia and Africa). Even China has established 5 PLA regional commanders and that is contained within their own borders. The new proposed International Command would cover over 36 million square miles and 125 countries! That span of control cannot be effective and would likely result in the creation of smaller commands potentially adding more unintended layers and less efficiency. Some reorganization may be needed but this level of consolidation is unlikely to last long-term.

Counterpoint: This restructuring represents a bold and necessary pivot, reallocating critical resources and personnel to high-priority theaters like INDOPACOM and a unified AMERICOM, while trimming decades of accumulated bureaucratic excess. Behind every 4-star general lies a sprawling hierarchy of 3-, 2-, and 1-star officers, along with bloated staffs not only at the Combatant Command level but across each military service. Downgrading EUCOM and AFRICOM to 3-star commands under a broader umbrella would preserve—and potentially enhance—warfighting capabilities by streamlining operations, without slashing frontline forces. Consider the personnel disparities: EUCOM boasts 100,000 troops, CENTCOM 45,000, and AFRICOM a mere 2,000 (with 1,500 stationed in Germany). In contrast, the National Security Strategy emphasizes the Western Hemisphere, where NORTHCOM and SOUTHCOM operate with just 2,700 personnel combined, and INDOPACOM covers a vast 100 million square miles with 375,000 troops. These under-resourced commands urgently need reinforcements to deter aggressors and safeguard U.S. interests—making this realignment not a retreat, but a strategic imperative for global readiness.

The Trump Administration’s Executive Order: Reining in Defense Contractors’ Excesses and Its Potential Economic Implications

In a move aimed at curbing costs and increasing accountability within the defense industry, the Trump administration is reportedly planning an executive order that would restrict dividends, share buybacks, and executive compensation for defense contractors that exceed budget and experience delays in their projects.

  • According to a GAO report, the top 100 major defense acquisitions accounted for ~$1.7T in total cost between 1996 and 2010, with an average overrun of 26%.

  • The proposed executive order, which is still under development, would reportedly target defense contractors that exceed their budgets by more than 5% or experience delays of more than six months on projects worth over $500M.

  • Under the order, these contractors would be prohibited from issuing dividends, repurchasing shares, or increasing executive compensation until they bring their projects back on track.

  • The executive order, if implemented, could have far-reaching implications for both the defense industry and the broader business community.

Our Take: We do not know what the final order will contain but this level of governance from the White House would certainly be unprecedented. One other thing is certain: it would send an unambiguous message to contractors who are willing to rake in billions while routinely missing key deadlines that there is a consequence beyond a marginal CPARs rating.

America’s Drone Delusion: Why the Lessons of Ukraine Don’t Apply to a Conflict with China

After nearly four years of fighting, few aspects of Russia’s war in Ukraine have gained as much attention among Western militaries as the rapid expansion of drone warfare.

  • Since 2023, both sides have deployed millions of cheap quadcopter-type drones across the battlefield. In some parts of the front, these small drones now account for up to 70% of battlefield casualties.

  • Watching these developments, Western defense strategists made urgent calls to shift military priorities including an EO to accelerate drone production.

  • However, the assumption that large-scale acquisition of AI-enabled drones will strengthen U.S. defenses against China is misguided.

  • For one thing, lessons from the war in Ukraine—an attritional struggle between two fundamentally land-centric armed forces—likely do not apply.

  • Despite having the largest and most advanced drone industry in the world, China has actually been prioritizing crewed military hardware.

  • The expansion of drone warfare is arguably not what has prevented Ukrainian forces from holding key positions against Russian forces in 2024 and 2025.

  • Instead, it is the hundreds of heavy glide bombs that Russia is delivering by Su-34 fighter-bombers against the frontlines each week.

  • These 500kg–3000kg glide bombs can demolish even deep, hardened fighting positions and kill dug-in troops far more effectively than small drones.

  • In sharp contrast to the operational conditions in Ukraine, any likely conflict between U.S. forces and China’s People’s Liberation Army would unfold predominantly in the air and at sea, with combat between land forces likely limited to key islands such as Taiwan or the Senkakus.

  • In this context, success for the U.S. would depend on the ability to rapidly and repeatedly bring decisive airborne and maritime firepower to bear at those key points at critical moments.

NDAA Gives New Counter-Drone Office Veto Over Service Programs

The NDAA gives the Pentagon’s new counter-small-drone task force extraordinary authority over acquisition programs across the armed forces — including the power to set technical standards, run field tests, and, if a system doesn’t pass muster, forbid the armed services from acquiring it.

  • This authority only covers defenses against small UAS - Groups 1-3.

  • Services can override the JIATF-401 veto with the SAE or USD(A&S) justification in writing to Congress.

  • JIATF-401 replaced the Joint Counter Small UAS Office (JCO), which was widely considered too weak to impose discipline on the sprawling profusion of programs to defeat small drones. Lea Kirkwood (PEO Enterprise) said “The JCO didn’t have a lot of teeth, nor acquisition authority.”

“My interpretation of that law is that we won’t let a service procure something that doesn’t perform and if they want to, JIATF-401 gets to say no.” Lt Col. Jonathan Beha, Joint Inter-Agency Task Force Chief of Requirements

Our Take: Empowering a true joint organization is a critical step to align efforts and drive deliveries of C-UAS capabilities. The JCO was too Army centric. The JIATF must overcome decades of entrenched thinking as one Service is too wedded to a specific technology that never seems to get there. It must overcome homegrown biases favoring long S&T projects that always require more research over industry developed solutions. Rapidly pivoting to production, iterating, and scaling with proven solutions is critical to a layered C-UAS defense.

SBIR Reform Should Build Launchpads, Not Lifelines

Brian Miller

SBIR reform should be judged by one question: does it turn grants into a launchpad toward real markets and customers, or lock firms into permanent, grant-dependent life support?

  • Washington is treating the SBIR program like a pawn in a partisan fight. One side wants to make it bigger and permanent. The other wants to bolt on caps, metrics, and new award types.

  • Meanwhile, SBIR’s core authority lapsed on October 1, and agencies are delaying or cancelling solicitations while Congress debates the fine print, leaving numerous early-stage innovations in limbo.

  • The real story is that America’s research and defense agencies are trying to move from funding proposals to funding outcomes. SBIR is one of the few levers they have for early-stage innovation and commercialization, and it is misaligned with that shift.

  • SBIR should be a launchpad into the warfighting and commercial economy, not a lifestyle subsidy. Reform is about making that launchpad real.

  • When SBIR works, it does two things:

    • Funds a real experiment against a government problem. Not a white paper, not a science project, but a testable step toward a capability.

    • Sends a credible signal that helps a team raise follow-on capital or win a true production contract.

  • Four Design Tensions of SBIR Reform

    • Access vs. Concentration

    • R&D Activity vs. Fielded Capability

    • Security and Foreign Risk vs. Openness

    • Complexity vs. Speed

  • A healthy SBIR ecosystem

    • New companies could get in quickly.

    • We would track and reward what happens after SBIR.

    • Agencies would have the tools to cut off SBIR Mills and real foreign risk without freezing the entire pipeline.

  • Reforms that increase the number of companies that successfully navigate the pipeline and graduate matter more than reforms that simply increase the total dollar figure or add new titles with “commercialization” on the door.

Our Take: This is a sound first principles perspective by Brian. We cannot let SBIR be another victim of Congressional partisan battles. It is an important pipeline to fuel innovative capabilities into the DoW and USG, with some key reforms addressed.

The Military Black Swan

Nestor Levin

Black Swans are not only an important concept in financial markets. They are also what anyone in the DoW or in the defense industry should fear - and try to prepare for. It is a concept that presents a strategic risk to a military caught unprepared.

  • What happens when the DoW over-invests in building exquisite platforms assuming a permissive operational environment?

  • What happens when decades of precision-driven warfare doctrine and a hollowed out defense industry are stressed against a certain unnamed juggernaut across the Pacific and where there are only enough munitions for several weeks?

  • What happens when decades of deep-penetration armored offensive tactics are met with a wall of cheap FPV drones which used to be marketed as toys?

  • What happens when a suite of offensive strike capabilities is predicated and built on the assumption that Space is a permissive environment and sanctuary?

  • General Billy Mitchell is remembered as a revolutionary in airpower not because he invented a new technology, but by rewiring how the U.S. military perceived airpower, not as a supporting arm, but as a core strategic force that would fundamentally change how how war is fought.

  • A series of technological developments paved the way for new capabilities that would enable a more rapid and lethal kind of warfare.

  • The New Space Age: Reusable launch; proliferated constellations; maneuverable satellites, a new economy. Advantage? Unkown.

  • Military Inertia to Anticipate Black Swans: Institutional; prestige platform bias; linear extrapolation from previous wars; underestimating multi-dimensional forces and second order effects; and ignoring unlikely but dangerous possibilities.

  • KPIs for Defense Tech in the 21st century: Cost per effect; adoption speed; replacement rate; kill chain latency; and operational elasticity.

  • Anduril treating C2 software as a core weapon system. Palantir’s common data layer linking interoperable data flows. Shield AI autonomy software stack and drones to operate in EW environments. Epirus counter-UAS that scales economically against UAS threats. Hawkeye 360 space-based persistence beats exquisite capability.

AI Adoption in Defense Runs into Regulatory Hurdles

One of the most significant barriers to efficiently introducing new AI capabilities into the U.S. government is the authorization and accreditation process required to obtain an authority to operate. Getting this traditional authority can take over a year and cost substantial sums, which can be very burdensome for small businesses and a barrier to rapidly delivering relevant software capabilities to agencies.

  • Two very prominent examples are the Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program (FedRAMP) and Impact Levels 4 and 5.

  • To be clear, these certifications are necessary. However, they are unnecessarily long and laborious, often preventing innovative startups from adding value to the U.S. government, if they even try.

  • Another potential emerging barrier to the adoption of AI within the defense ecosystem is the growing interest of states in enacting AI regulations in the absence of a national regulatory framework.

  • The opportunities for applying AI technologies are effectively limitless. However, meeting the department’s mission requires a diverse set of stakeholders and industry partners with access to critical technology.

  • Policymakers must work to reduce barriers to its adoption to ensure warfighters have the most tech advanced systems to deter and, if necessary, win a fight.

New Laser Could Beam Power Mid-Air to US Aircraft at 5,000 Feet For Infinite Range

PowerLight Technologies has completed development and testing of a new wireless power system that uses lasers to recharge aircraft while they are flying, as part of a project backed by the US military to increase the time drones can remain in the air.

  • The company said the testing was conducted under the Power Transmitted Over Laser to UAS (PTROL-UAS), program, which is sponsored in part by CENTCOM.

  • At the center of the system is an autonomous, high-power laser transmitter paired with a lightweight airborne receiver.

  • Together, the components are intended to create what the company describes as a wireless power line in the air, capable of delivering kilowatt-class energy over distances of several miles to drones operating at altitude.

  • Tests showed that the system can supply power to aircraft flying up to 5,000 feet.

After Delays, Air Burst-Maneuvering X-65 to Fly in 2027

A DARPA X-plane employing bursts of air to initiate climbs, turns and descents rather than traditional flight control surfaces is scheduled to fly in 2027 after a long delay.

  • Two years have passed since DARPA announced in January 2024 that Boeing subsidiary Aurora Flight Sciences had been chosen to build the X-65, a subsonic, 7,000-plus pound unmanned aircraft with a 30-foot wingspan capable of maneuvering without traditional flight control surfaces like flaps, rudders or ailerons.

  • The X-65 will not fly until late 2027 — a three-year delay from the 2024 first flight originally planned when DARPA launched the effort in 2020.

  • The effectors as steady blowing jets that emit bursts of air generated from the X-65’s onboard auxiliary power unit to shape the flow of air over the aircraft’s surface, controlling the plane’s roll, pitch and yaw.

The State of AI in 2025: Agents, Innovation, and Transformation

McKinsey conducts a yearly survey on the use of AI. These were their findings:

  1. Most organizations are still in the experimentation or piloting phase: Nearly two-thirds have not yet begun scaling AI across the enterprise.

  2. High curiosity in AI agents: Sixty-two percent say their organizations are at least experimenting with AI agents.

  3. Positive leading indicators on impact of AI: 64 percent say that AI is enabling their innovation. However, just 39 percent report EBIT impact at the enterprise level.

  4. High performers use AI to drive growth, innovation, and cost: Eighty percent say their companies set efficiency as an AI objective, but the companies seeing the most value from AI often set growth or innovation as additional objectives.

  5. Redesigning workflows is a key success factor: Half of those AI high performers intend to use AI to transform their businesses, and are redesigning workflows.

  6. Differing perspectives on employment impact: Respondents vary in their expectations of AI’s impact on the overall workforce size of their organizations in the coming year: 32 percent expect decreases, 43 percent no change, and 13 percent increases.

Our Take: These same advances being rewarded to those companies setting innovation and efficiency as objectives could be ascribed to DoW organizations.

Talent Development to Unleash the DOD Acquisition Workforce

Karen Thornton & John Willison

Delivering on the Warfighting Acquisition System vision requires an equally modern approach to the acquisition workforce through investment in talent development.

  • In the DoW memo, military departments are required to prioritize education and rotational opportunities that expose acq professionals to commercial practices, manufacturing insights, operational realities, and hands-on problem-solving.

  • This was followed by the passing of the NDAA, where Title 8 includes an entire subsection devoted to provisions relating to workforce development.

  • Two provisions in particular suggest reflects Congress’ realization that culture change is essential to acquisition transformation.

    • Section 826 directs the secretary of defense to establish acquisition workforce key performance objectives, including strategic outcome objectives and workforce behavioral objectives.

    • Section 823 adds streamlined authorities to ensure the DCTC prototype can be scaled to meet expanded need.

  • We cannot rely solely on the training that got us here to achieve culture change.

  • But there is a way forward. A&S has been prototyping a program — the Defense Civilian Training Corps (DCTC) — to develop precisely these critical skills and behaviors, over the past two years.

  • You can think of DCTC as a talent-development “factory” built to produce a more agile, cross-functional, and digitally fluent civilian acquisition workforce.

  • The DCTC model develops the critical thinking needed for adoption of innovative authorities through hands-on team learning, experienced coaching, and exposure to real-world acquisition scenarios that mirror operational pressures.

  • DCTC is a proven prototype, ready for broader deployment.

Billy Mitchell: Lessons a Hundred Years Hence

Exactly 100 years ago, on Dec. 17, 1925, Brig. Gen. Billy Mitchell was convicted by court-martial for violating an order that required approval before he could engage with the media.

  • In Mitchell’s mind, airpower would be “the determining factor in international competitions - he knew that its promotion was vital to national security.

  • In preparing to publish “Mitchell’s Winged Defense: The Development and Possibilities of Modern Air Power, Economic and Military,” his publishers asked that he back off from his more flamboyant claims and radical concepts.

  • They feared it would hurt his book sales. Mitchell didn’t care, saying he wanted his writings to be read and relevant “a hundred years hence.” They are!

  • His advocacy for airpower, though far beyond the capabilities of the time, would be a natural fit for the headlines of our current day a century later.

    • His concepts on the decisiveness of airpower were directly relevant to the 12-Day War between Iran and Israel

    • His ideas on the potency of strategic bombardment were demonstrated by Operation Midnight Hammer’s B-2 strikes on Iranian nuclear sites

    • His belief in the necessity of air superiority to break the stranglehold of attrition is playing out daily in Ukraine

    • His concerns about shattered American isolationism are relevant to prompting major investments in Golden Dome.

  • Mitchell also understood that carefully orchestrated events could capture the attention of decision-makers and captivate the focus of the public.

    • The sinking of the Ostfriesland in 1921 was the singular event that transformed the future of air warfare.

    • The Ostfriesland was deemed impervious to attack from the air, yet in spite of U.S. Navy inputs to the test concept that drastically curtailed the probability of success, Mitchell’s team sent this ship to the seabed.

  • A joint board that followed Mitchell’s trials “called for the maximum development of aviation, the rapid addition of aircraft carriers to the Navy, and the improvement of antiaircraft armament.”

  • At the cost of his career, Mitchell’s arguments propelled his cause.

  • Prompted by the spectacle of his trial, the Morrow Board established the nation’s first broad aeronautical policy and created a structural foundation for what would eventually become the separate service of the United States Air Force.

Our Take: Sadly, Ukraine has served as this devastating but highly compelling example of the future drone warfare that will be as disruptive to forces organized around Cold War military strategies as it was to those ground-centric forces in WWI that saw airpower as primarily for observation.

  • Based on computer simulations of multiple conflict scenarios, the brief concludes that the PLA could, under a wide range of conditions, successfully neutralize or sink the Navy’s newest Gerald R. Ford-class aircraft carriers.

  • The assessment underscores that China would not rely on a single weapon or tactic, but instead employ a layered campaign combining missiles, cyber operations, and attacks on US space assets to overwhelm American defenses.

  • A central finding of the study is the critical role of cyber and anti-satellite operations since without reliable space-based support, carrier strike groups struggled to conduct targeting, routing, and air operations, leaving them vulnerable to follow-on strikes.

  • The brief highlights China’s expanding arsenal of anti-ship weapons, particularly its inventory of ballistic and hypersonic missiles designed to hold large surface combatants at risk.

  • These include the DF-21D and DF-26 land-based anti-ship ballistic missiles, the YJ-21 missile deployed on Chinese destroyers and submarines, and additional ballistic missile variants carried by H-6 bombers.

  • The study emphasizes the complementary nature of China’s missile forces.

    • Lower-cost anti-ship missiles could be fired in large numbers to saturate and exhaust a carrier group’s defenses.

    • At the same time, more advanced systems are intended to penetrate those defenses and deliver decisive blows.

  • Analysts estimate the PLA now fields up to 600 hypersonic missiles, capable of traveling at more than five times the speed of sound while maneuvering in flight, making them extremely difficult to intercept.

  • According to the simulations, Chinese forces could coordinate salvos of different missile types using targeting data from satellites, over-the-horizon radars, and unmanned aircraft.

  • These systems would create overlapping engagement zones around a carrier strike group, sharply increasing the probability that US ships would be neutralized before completing their missions.

  • High-value assets such as aircraft carriers, fifth-generation fighter jets, and large satellites are increasingly vulnerable to relatively inexpensive weapons that China can produce in large quantities.

Our Take: As one of our former colleagues wrote over a decade ago in The Death Star: A Pentagon Purchasing Nightmare relying too heavily on large, vulnerable assets is a lesson that George Lucas taught us quite awhile ago. Instead buy the practical weapons.

Indo-Pacific Nations Reliant on Partnerships to Combat Growing Threats

Many Indo-Pacific nations rely on maritime security for their way of life and growing threats — from menacing Chinese activity to illegal fishing — make effective governance and a rules-based order more important than ever for modest navies with limited resources.

  • One Indo-Pacific nation heavily reliant on goodwill and respecting rules is Fiji, a small island country in the South Pacific comprised of some 300 islands covering 18,000 square kilometers, with a population of 930,000.

  • Maritime security for smaller nations like Fiji is about protecting sovereign rights and upholding a rules-based order at sea.

  • Adm. Zulhelmy Ithnain, chief of the Royal Malaysian Navy, echoed the need for rules-based frameworks and cooperative agreements, particularly in the face of pressures from great power competition and nontraditional security threats.

  • International teamwork is important as well. Strengthening cooperation and practical integration is an “enduring principle” of a collective future, Ithnain said.

  • “No single navy can safeguard the vast Indo-Pacific alone,” and collective security is achieved through collaboration, transparent information exchange and regional exercises that foster unity.

Our Take: The US has an opportunity to support these different country goals and continue to build a buffer of influence against China’s growing ambitions.

Thales Develop UUV Sonar Prototype in Ten Months

Informed by the frequency of disrepair among their sonar systems in service, Thales recognised a capability gap prior to Project CABOT.

  • The prototype fits into the Royal Navy’s Atlantic Bastion strategy to cultivate a network of disaggregated sensors across the opaque underwater domain, within which Russian submarines have had an advantage.

  • Thales confirmed that their sonar systems were worked harder than they have been previously

  • Using building blocks from heritage systems – Sonar 2076 and 2087 – Thales developed Nano76 to be integrated onto the hull of UUVs.

  • The UK Royal Navy will test the efficacy of the 75x75cm tiles against a calibrated sound source.

  • The project, developed in just ten months, cost the company more than £2m ($2.67m) of its own funds.
