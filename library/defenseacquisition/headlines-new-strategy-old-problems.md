*A “strategy” is a concrete, realistic plan that explains the essential connection between ends and means: it begins from an accurate assessment of what is desired and what tools are available, or can realistically be created, to achieve the desired outcomes. A strategy must evaluate, sort, and prioritize.*

-   *We want to recruit, train, equip, and field the world’s most powerful, lethal, and technologically advanced military to protect our interests, deter wars, and—if necessary—win them quickly and decisively.*
    
-   *We want the world’s most robust industrial base. American national power depends on a strong industrial sector capable of meeting both peacetime and wartime production demands.*
    
-   *That requires not only direct defense industrial production capacity but also defense-related production capacity. Cultivating American industrial strength must become the highest priority of national economic policy.*
    
-   ***Reindustrialization** – The future belongs to makers. The U.S. will reindustrialize its economy, “re-shore” industrial production, and encourage and attract investment in our economy and our workforce, with a focus on the critical and emerging technology sectors that will define the future.*
    
-   *We will do so through the strategic use of tariffs and new technologies that favor widespread industrial production in every corner of our nation, raise living standards for American workers, and ensure that our country is never again reliant on any adversary, present or potential, for critical products or components.*
    
-   ***Reviving our Defense Industrial Base** – A strong, capable military cannot exist without a strong, capable defense industrial base. The huge gap, demonstrated in recent conflicts, between low-cost drones and missiles versus the expensive systems required to defend against them has laid bare our need to change and adapt.*
    
-   ***America requires a national mobilization to innovate powerful defenses at low cost, to produce the most capable and modern systems and munitions at scale, and to re-shore our defense industrial supply chains.***
    
-   *We must provide our warfighters with the full range of capabilities, ranging from low-cost weapons that can defeat most adversaries up to the most capable high-end systems necessary for a conflict with a sophisticated enemy.*
    
-   *Encourage the revitalization of the industrial bases of all our allies and partners to strengthen collective defense.*
    
-   *Regions*
    
    -   ***Western Hemisphere** - enforce the Monroe Doctrine to restore American preeminence in the Western Hemisphere, and to protect our homeland and our access to key geographies throughout the region.*
        
    -   ***Asia** - win the economic future, prevent military confrontation. We will rebalance America’s economic relationship with China, prioritizing reciprocity and fairness to restore American economic independence. We will build a military capable of denying aggression anywhere in the First Island Chain.*
        
    -   ***Promoting European Greatness** - Reestablishing conditions of stability within Europe and strategic stability with Russia; Enabling Europe to stand on its own feet and operate as a group of aligned sovereign nations, including by taking primary responsibility for its own defense*
        
    -   ***Middle East** \- Shift Burdens, Build Peace - the region will increasingly become a source and destination of international investment — including nuclear energy, AI, and defense technologies.*
        
-   *In the long term, **maintaining American economic and technological preeminence is the surest way to deter and prevent a large-scale military conflict.***

-   **Nuclear Enterprise and Missile Defense.** Several major nuclear enterprise and missile defense programs are vulnerable to significant risks to cost and production schedules.
    
-   **Unmanned Systems.** The DoD faces mounting challenges posed by the rapid advancement of UAS, to include resource limitations, potentially exacerbated by expanded border operations, coordination diff
    
-   **Space Domain.** Joint space operations encounter challenges as the Space Force seeks to modernize its aging ground infrastructure, upgrade legacy capabilities, and accelerate the acquisition and integration of data‑centric systems.
    
-   **Electromagnetic Spectrum.** EMS supremacy is crucial to the DoD’s capability to maneuver and operate within all domains. The greatest challenges in managing the EMS are its complexity, high level of congestion, intense competition, and the evolving, adversarial tactics used to gain access.

#### [Acquisition Reform vs. Congress: Who Will Win?](https://warontherocks.com/2025/12/acquisition-reform-vs-congress-who-will-win/)

[Matt Vallone](https://www.linkedin.com/in/matt-vallone-4677b023/)

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OpHR!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa0619825-c512-407f-8821-30c83c17914a_1278x417.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OpHR!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa0619825-c512-407f-8821-30c83c17914a_1278x417.png)

[According to Hegseth](https://www.rev.com/transcripts/hegseth-at-national-war-college):

> *We will shift funding within portfolios’ authorized boundaries swiftly and decisively to maximize mission outcomes. If one program is faltering, funding will be shifted within the portfolio to accelerate or scale a higher priority. If a new or more promising technology emerges, we will seize the opportunity and not be held back by artificial constraints and funding boundaries that take months or even years to overcome.*

-   The goal is to provide acquisition teams with more flexibility to shift money between various programs in reaction to real-time developments.
    
-   This will allow more rapid integration of quickly changing commercial technologies, and faster shifts from programs that are off-track to those more likely to deliver mission capabilities.
    
-   The biggest obstacle to shifting the DoD to a portfolio management structure comes from congressional appropriators.
    
-   Regardless of party affiliation, appropriators jealously guard their role as the arbiters of spending allocation in defense and other Federal roles.
    
-   They have consistently opposed giving the Pentagon this degree of flexibility.
    
-   These reforms reflect recommendations made by the [Commission on PPBE Reform](https://ppbereform.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/Commission-on-PPBE-Reform_Full-Report_6-March-2024_FINAL.pdf) in 2024, and are reflected in two portions of the FY26 NDAA.
    
-   This year provides strong evidence that a transition to portfolio managed acquisitions faces a hostile reception with appropriators.
    
-   In the FY26 PB, the Army proposed a few new portfolios - neither the [House of Representatives](https://www.congress.gov/119/crpt/hrpt162/CRPT-119hrpt162.pdf) nor the [Senate](https://www.congress.gov/119/crpt/srpt52/CRPT-119srpt52.pdf) funded this proposal in their appropriations bills.
    
-   At a minimum, the department will need to ensure robust and sustained communication with the relevant committees.
    
-   Creating a looser portfolio structure that continues to provide line-item level detail on individual programs but with enhanced reprogramming authority within the structure seems like an easy way to both maintain existing authorities and create the flexibility the department desires.
    

**Our Take:** As long time champions of PPBE reform, Matt raises a critical issue between the Pentagon and Congress. The DoW cannot afford to operate with 1,700 separate program elements that are laid out years in advance whereby it takes an act of Congress (all 4 Committees) to shift more than $10M or 20%. To effectively manage portfolios focused on maximizing operational mission impact, it must have greater flexibility to shift funding to higher priority and impactful investments. However, this requires significantly increased engagements and information sharing with Congress to rebuild trust. An iterative approach to roll up smaller, dynamic budget accounts and increased Congressional notification of significant funding shifts and ongoing budget execution must be worked through ASAP.

#### [Decades of Lost Potential in Defense R&D](https://warontherocks.com/2025/12/decades-of-lost-potential-in-defense-research-and-development/)

[Amanda Bresler](https://www.linkedin.com/in/amanda-bresler-2578a314/)

America’s greatest national security threat is an enemy born from and nurtured by the Pentagon: crippling bureaucracy. It has also infiltrated the **[Small Business Innovation Research](https://www.defensesbirsttr.mil/)** Program.

-   More than four decades and [$68B later](https://www.sbir.gov/api), a program intended to seed commercial innovation instead functions as a taxpayer-funded subsidy for entrenched players, including a handful of ostensibly large firms known as “SBIR mills.”
    
-   Reorienting the program towards its stated objectives requires common-sense size standards, imposing a cap on participants’ lifetime Phase I and Phase II funding, and setting aside a share of Phase I awards for new entrants.
    
-   From FY05 to FY24, DoD awarded $27.5B in Phase I and Phase II funding to 8,945 unique companies.
    
-   More than $4.1B — 15% of all DoD Phase I and Phase II funding — went to just 25 mills - with the top five mills alone capturing 5% of DoD’s entire SBIR budget.
    

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!L_Ex!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F90b984b0-96b1-4dc6-bdea-19bc6847b143_1024x854.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!L_Ex!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F90b984b0-96b1-4dc6-bdea-19bc6847b143_1024x854.png)

-   Although the small companies possessed a clear technical advantage, evaluators typically selected the mills. They felt more comfortable awarding to familiar entities, justifying the decision based on the quality of the mills’ proposals.
    
-   Both we and the [GAO](https://www.gao.gov/assets/d24106398.pdf) have argued that some mills transition at a lower*–*than-average rate compared to companies with far fewer awards.
    
-   The capture of the program by mills is a betrayal of the program’s mission and a disservice to national security. While promising startups die in the weeds of compliance, mills thrive courtesy of institutional knowledge and influence.
    

**Our Take**: We have stayed relatively neutral on this issue because we want to trust that government evaluators are getting value from the SBIR awards they are making. However, the point Amanda makes about the breadth of projects these “mills” are undertaking is very compelling. It is hard for a company to build up expertise in multiple technology areas and be able to scale it without incredible resources and organization (that should make a company way too large for a SBIR contract).

Just doing some basic research on the top two companies that have consumed a disproportionate share of SBIR funding, with the help of LLMs, here’s what we found:

-   [Physical Systems Inc](https://www.psicorp.com/our-company/) mixes materials science, optics, energetics, energy storage, sensors, and advanced manufacturing to tackle complex, multi-domain system-level problems. They occupy 175,000 square feet of custom, state-of-the-art laboratory, manufacturing, and office space in multiple locations and are expanding its Northeast operations (R&D and test facilities) to ramp up development and testing of energetics, propellants, and solid-rocket / rocket-motor technologies.
    
-   Physical Optics Corporation was actually [bought out](https://www.navysbir.com/success/spotlight/STP-POC-21.pdf?) by [Mercury Systems](https://www.mrcy.com/) for $310M in 2020. They have evolved some product lines, especially for aircraft video recorders which were installed in thousands of aircraft. However, they primarily operate as a systems integrator packaging available tech into deployable avionics, communications, and mission systems. Last year, Mercury Systems had a record backlog of $1.4B which the company says is up 6.5% year-over-year.
    

While we won’t argue about the utility of the technology and solutions being provided or the lower commercialization rates (since there’s likely a lot of nuance there), **one thing is for sure**: no sane person would think companies of these sizes (based just on physical space, company scope and revenue) should qualify as small businesses in need of government non-dilutive capital. If they are providing great value, DoW should work with them to scale and start competing for real (Phase III) contracts.

-   Dubbed the **[Drone Dominance Program (DDP)](https://drone-dominance.io/)**, it is an implementation of [War Secretary Pete Hegseth’s July memo](https://www.documentcloud.org/documents/25995487-250710-dod-memo-unleashing-us-military-drone-dominance/) authorizing major changes to how the department procures and uses these weapons.

-   By 2027, Drone Dominance intends to purchase over 200,000 drones that can produce lethal effects in the toughest battlefield environments.
    
-   The plan calls for a total of more than 300,000 drones to be purchased by early 2028.
    
-   Through the drone dominance program, $1B from the Big Beautiful Bill will fund the manufacture of approximately 340,000 small UASs for combat units over the course of two years.
    
-   The first competition under the new DDP program is scheduled to begin Feb. 16, 2026, among 25 invited vendors.
    
-   Participants will be selected based on responses to a Request for Solutions (RFS) anticipated to post 17 December, with final selections and notifications during the week of 26 January.

The Pentagon recently formed the U.S. military’s first kamikaze drone squadron under U.S. Central Command in the Middle East, marking its latest-to-be-revealed move to accelerate the adoption of affordable unmanned assets for modern warfare operations.

-   CENTCOM’s new Task Force Scorpion Strike already set up a unit that’s delivering so-called Low-cost Unmanned Combat Attack Systems (LUCAS) — just a few months after the SECDEF [issued guidance to “unleash U.S. military drone dominance”](https://defensescoop.com/2025/07/16/pentagon-seeks-to-surge-its-multi-domain-drone-arsenal/) by speedily equipping forces with expendable platforms
    
-   As autonomous technologies have improved in recent years, the deployment of [weaponized](https://defensescoop.com/2025/06/02/ukraine-drone-attack-russia-strategic-bombers-lessons-us-army/), inexpensive [drones](https://defensescoop.com/2025/04/09/red-sea-disruptions-transcom-expand-information-sharing-gen-randall-reed/) has become a defining feature in multiple contemporary military conflicts.
    
-   Task Force Scorpion Strike (TFSS) was created to equip warfighters with new combat drone capabilities faster.
    
-   LUCAS drones deployed by CENTCOM have an extensive range and are designed to operate autonomously - and can be launched with different mechanisms to include catapults, rocket-assisted takeoff, and mobile ground and vehicle systems.
    
-   In September, [CENTCOM created the Rapid Employment Joint Task Force](https://defensescoop.com/2025/09/23/centcom-rapid-employment-joint-task-force-drones-ai-counter-uas-rejtf/) (REJTF) to help strategically fast-track the military’s drone applications and other cutting-edge combat capabilities.

#### [Alex Miller, Army CTO on Interoperability](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alex-m-0983a5201_interoperability-is-a-time-travel-problem-activity-7398459326425624576-6ByC?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAACRMeYBUP67LQoOQBeSygka-2vadYTeozI)

**[Alex Miller](https://www.linkedin.com/in/alex-m-0983a5201/)** posts regular videos on LinkedIn discussing meaty topics in defense tech. -   Interoperability is when things are working together to share information to enable decision making. Often our most advanced piece of technology has to be interoperable with our least advanced piece of technology developed decades ago.

The result is predictable: [programs that begin with confidence often end in costly delay](https://breakingdefense.com/2025/09/f-35-block-4-upgrade-delayed-until-at-least-2031-gao/).
    
-   Speed alone will not fix the problem. The same biases that slowed yesterday’s programs can just as easily derail today’s transformation if they are not confronted directly.
    
-   The aim is not to slow progress but to learn fast while change is still affordable. [Programs that invest in learning early will move faster later](https://doi.org/10.1093/oxrep/grac009) because they will pivot less in production and reduce costly rework.
    
-   Enforcing [shared standards](https://www.cto.mil/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/MOSA-Implementation-Guidebook-27Feb2025-Cleared.pdf) for hardware, software, and data will let the department build once and reuse often.
    
-   Digital twins, mission-engineering, and shared test data should be in place before programs scale production so work can continue uninterrupted once it begins.
    
-   [Streamlining paperwork](https://breakingdefense.com/2024/12/incoming-sasc-head-lays-out-acquisition-reform-vision/) matters only if it creates clarity through [better data, clear accountability, and defined decisions](https://breakingdefense.com/2025/11/dod-draft-memo-acquisition-reform-hegseth-commercial-scorecard-exclusive/).
    
-   Building faster requires [learning from evidence](https://hbr.org/2025/03/the-uniqueness-trap). Successful programs constantly measure performance and use those findings to improve the next cycle of planning.
    

#### [Pentagon On the Cusp of Open Systems Breakthrough](https://www.nationaldefensemagazine.org/articles/2025/12/4/pentagon-on-the-cusp-of--open-systems-breakthrough)

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Jg-Y!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9d06a7d8-2532-46ec-92c0-b046c2069cfc_990x311.png)

In recent years has pushed for the use of open standards in military systems, but a renewed emphasis on commercial technology could lead to a breakthrough for industry and government alike.

-   Since 2021, all DoD acquisition programs are to use a modular open systems approach (MOSA), to the maximum extent practicable.
    
-   By using widely accepted standards for a platform’s key interfaces, MOSA is meant to make it easier for the military to add or replace parts over time.
    
-   The Open Group, a global business and technology consortium, has set up a pair of government-industry consortia that develop the technical standards for different defense systems.
    

> *“I think that’s going to be one of the keys to allowing us to go fast. If we want to go fast, you have to allow industry to invest, invest, invest, and then not take away all their IP, because then they will never invest. Progress is being made in regard to commercial vendors maintaining intellectual property rights. We’re certainly seeing it at card level on programs where … that’s 100 percent a commercial product inside of a militarized box. On the software side … that is also starting to turn where we see commercial companies beginning to adhere to open standards. Industry is starting to recognize these reference architectures are every bit as good, if not better in some senses, \[as\] what we do internally, so we might as well just start developing in accordance with the standards. Now, I can apply it to commercial or to the government stuff — I think that’s the other reason why we’re going to start seeing a dramatic increase in the use of those standards.”* [Matt Sipe](https://www.linkedin.com/in/matt-sipe-12180218?lipi=urn%3Ali%3Apage%3Ad_flagship3_profile_view_base_contact_details%3BincCrfrzTW%2BCe8U4kw5n3Q%3D%3D), MOSA Consultant

**Our Take**: This is exactly the balance that needs to achieved with MOSA. In the past, they were seen as inhibiters since legacy primes often influenced the standards to conform to how they were already developing systems and commercial systems were forced to readapt their whole architectures only to also have to hand over data rights to key aspects of their proprietary systems. By marrying up improved IP requirements with an enduring standard that can also be adapted over time with influence from a broader consortium, the commercial sector seems more inclined to play along. The time may just be right to finally make MOSA at scale a reality.

#### [Modeling the Future of Acquisition Reform](https://www.nationaldefensemagazine.org/articles/2025/12/2/modeling-the-future-of-acquisition-reform)

Modern modeling and simulation tools are used across the DoW and industry, helping shape technology and force development.

-   The Pentagon carefully models and then tests complex physical systems like body armor or munitions, making full use of physics-based tools coupled with live fire development and operational testing before systems are certified for deployment.
    
-   In contrast, the Pentagon and Congress routinely change complex non-physical systems like acquisition laws and regulations, affecting contractors and impacting the spending of billions of dollars, with no use of similar data-based modeling and simulation techniques or capabilities.
    
-   Just as these tools are used in the development of weapons systems, acquisition decision-makers should have access to predictive modeling and simulation tools that enable them to test the potential impacts of their decisions and policies before implementation.
    

**Our Take**: There is potentially some value here in modeling policy but more likely than not the modeling will be wildly inadequate, and this just becomes an academic thought exercise without any real practical value. We subscribe more to the [Jen Pahlka approach](https://www.recodingamerica.us/) of acknowledging that “*the farther policy creators are from the PoS, the lower the service quality*.” Policy that limits the checkers checking checkers and puts PMs 3 levels between the operators (or industry) will result in worse outcomes. Improve accountability by reducing that distance, everything improves. More to come on how we see this being applied.

#### [Do We Need to Bring Back the Navy Department?](https://www.firstbreakfast.com/p/do-we-need-to-bring-back-the-navy)

[Arthur Herman](https://www.hudson.org/experts/1005-arthur-herman)

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!md3s!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F321bf37a-375a-413d-966b-99fb0e2d7182_897x438.png)

How decisive will the Navy’s legacy systems actually be, if we find ourselves in a protracted conflict with our superpower rival, China? Even more important, how far will these systems go in deterring China from committing to such a conflict, if the People’s Liberation Army (PLA) believes its vast anti-ship missile arsenal, hypersonic weapons, and electronic and cyber warfare capabilities—not to mention its fleet of warships, now larger than our own—give it the winning edge?

The task of rebuilding a 355-ship Navy began under the first Trump administration and continues today as part of the renamed War Department. Nonetheless, thinking about restoring a separate Navy Department with its own budgetary authority and appropriations schedule might make sense, if it does the following three things.

1.  A separate budget ought to promote more competition among defense contractors, lowering costs and boosting productivity.
    
2.  A separate Dept of Navy should be focused on winning as well as deterring wars, and on implementing a proactive and aggressive strategy with regard to China, not only in the South China Sea and Taiwan Straits but also in the Arctic.
    
3.  A hypothetical Dept of Navy could be empowered to see naval and maritime dominance as two sides of the same coin.

#### [Spiral Development as a Strategic Asset](https://www.usni.org/magazines/proceedings/2025/december/spiral-development-strategic-asset)

[Austin Gray](https://www.linkedin.com/in/austinelliottgray/) and [James Wirtz](https://www.linkedin.com/in/james-wirtz-28841b120/)

The Navy is trying to replicate commercial iteration, which is often described as spiral development, to reach wartime speed.

-   When a new weapon generates significant battlefield effects, officers must exploit the technological advantage while it lasts.
    
-   Weapons development is mostly guided by long, detailed, requirements-centric plans that schedule years of technology development far in advance. These plans are brittle and slow-moving. Programs often fail as priorities shift, budgets shrink, and technical hurdles arise.
    
-   The Navy wants to build a [fleet of oceangoing drone warships](https://sam.gov/workspace/contract/opp/50f14282db7c48a0802563fac9d2921d/view) that will evolve with warfare. At a moment when it [faces a shipbuilding crisis](https://www.defensenews.com/congress/2025/06/24/senators-concerned-with-navy-shipbuilding-delays-budget/), Navy program offices are starting to buy platforms more like they purchase tech products.
    
-   A company using commercial spiral development builds MVPs rather than perfected systems. Designers and engineers can sprint to reach an 80% solution that meets basic customer requirements.
    
-   Ship customers and developers, however, must recognize that foundational choices are generally locked into a MVP.
    
-   The Navy recognized that iterative development can unlock rapid advances in capability, and is starting to spiral some of its most important acquisitions.
    
-   The core tool used for Replicator-style procurement is DIU’s Commercial Solutions Opening (CSO), which pairs the process with simple contracts.
    
-   The [Innovation Adoption Kit](https://www.peodigital.navy.mil/Portals/96/Documents/PDFs/PEODigital_Innovation_Adoption_v3.pdf) championed by the Navy’s PEO Digital Office focuses on piloting, evaluating, and iterating before scaling—not on thorough, rigid requirements.
    
-   The Navy’s Modular Attack Surface Craft program is taking an [accelerated acquisition approach](https://defensescoop.com/2025/05/19/navy-future-usv-program-industry-day/) with emphasis on commercial standards, rapid demonstration, and speed to delivery.

-   As things stand, Owen warned, airlift crews are left both vulnerable in enemy airspace and unable to adjust to changing mission requirements.
    
-   The massive C-5s have suffered from very low mission capable rates, last reported in 2024 at 49 percent. Even the relatively stalwart C-17, at 76 percent mission capable, pales in comparison to commercial airlifters.

We still need new aircraft of course - and the focus should be on being able to conduct contested logistic deliveries autonomously.

#### [To China’s War Planners, AI Is Just Another Thing to Deceive](https://www.defenseone.com/threats/2025/11/chinas-emerging-counter-ai-warfare-playbook/409757/?oref=ds_update_nl&utm_source=Sailthru&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=Defense%20Systems%20Update:%20Nov.%2026%2C%202025&utm_term=newsletter_ds_update)

Under the banner of “counter-AI warfare”, the People’s Liberation Army is teaching troops to fight the model as much as the soldier.

-   Forces are learning to alter how vehicles appear to cameras, radar, and heat sensors so the AI misidentifies them, to feed junk or poisoned data into an opponent’s pipeline, and to swamp battlefield computers with noise.
    
-   Leaders are drilling their own teams to [spot](https://url.avanan.click/v2/r01/___http:/www.81.cn/yw_208727/16244259.html___.YXAzOmJsdWVwYXRobGFiczphOm86N2IwNGY2NDE0YzE2NzhmYzkwZDUzMzMxYTFjYjg5NDU6NzozMjM0Ojc1ZTIzMzRmZmEwNzFjZDZlN2RmZjdiM2IxMzNjODcyODZkZGYyMzZkM2U5ZTk0Mjk4ZDQ2ZjIyNWRhNTcxNzU6cDpUOk4) when their own machines are wrong.
    
-   The goal is simple: make an enemy’s military AI chase phantoms and miss the real threat.
    
-   The PLA conceives its counter-AI playbook as a triad that targets **data**, **algorithms**, and **computing power**.
    
-   In May, *PLA Daily* described the concept in its [](https://url.avanan.click/v2/r01/___http:/www.81.cn/ll_208543/16387159.html___.YXAzOmJsdWVwYXRobGFiczphOm86N2IwNGY2NDE0YzE2NzhmYzkwZDUzMzMxYTFjYjg5NDU6NzpiZDcxOjYxY2ExNTczZGM3Yjg5M2ZlMDEzMzVhMmJlN2U3NWUxMzY0NjYxZmFlNmJjY2Y2MTE4ODZhMWE4YzEyYjA3MGE6cDpUOk4)*[Intelligentized Warfare Panorama](https://url.avanan.click/v2/r01/___http:/www.81.cn/ll_208543/16387159.html___.YXAzOmJsdWVwYXRobGFiczphOm86N2IwNGY2NDE0YzE2NzhmYzkwZDUzMzMxYTFjYjg5NDU6NzpiZDcxOjYxY2ExNTczZGM3Yjg5M2ZlMDEzMzVhMmJlN2U3NWUxMzY0NjYxZmFlNmJjY2Y2MTE4ODZhMWE4YzEyYjA3MGE6cDpUOk4)* series where it argued that the most reliable way to “break intelligence” is to hit all three at once.
    
-   [Commentary](https://url.avanan.click/v2/r01/___https:/www.sohu.com/a/873513408_358040___.YXAzOmJsdWVwYXRobGFiczphOm86N2IwNGY2NDE0YzE2NzhmYzkwZDUzMzMxYTFjYjg5NDU6NzplNWNhOjNhYTEzZThmMmQzYTc5YjY1YjkxODIzYzkwYWIwY2Q4YjliNjgzMjlkZmYzMGE5MjMwOTQ5YmFhOTJkNmEyZmQ6cDpUOk4) from PLA analysts casts the contest as algorithm-versus-algorithm in joint operations.
    
-   It urges planners to defeat enemy algorithms by testing how the algorithms make decisions, scrambling the signals that guide drone swarms, and maneuvering in unexpected ways to throw off the patterns those systems are trained to favor, with the aim of tricking enemy sensors and models into misidentifying targets.

#### [Biggest Reform in 50 years Coming for Australian Defense Acquisition](https://breakingdefense.com/2025/12/biggest-reform-in-50-years-coming-for-australian-defense-acquisition-minister-says/)

Australia is launching a new independent acquisition system to better cope with increasingly complex weapons systems, one that will report directly to the defense minister and not pass through the secretary of the Defense Department.

-   Pat Conroy, [the Minister for Defense Industry](https://www.minister.defence.gov.au/transcripts/2025-12-01/press-conference-parliament-house) noted Australia’s military faces a rise in complexity of projects: 15 years ago, 27% of defense projects were complex; that is now doubled to almost 60% of projects being complex.
    
-   The reform announced today creates the Defense Delivery Agency, which will report directly to the defense minister and the minister for defense industry.
    
-   The new agency, which will absorb the Capability Acquisition and Sustainment Group, the [Guided Weapons and Explosive Ordnance Enterprise (GWEO)](https://breakingdefense.com/tag/guided-weapons-and-explosive-ordnance-enterprise-gweo/), and the Naval Shipbuilding and Sustainment Group, will be stood up on July 1 and led by a national armaments director.
    
-   It will oversee roughly 40% of Australian defense spending.
    

**Our Take**: This is an interesting approach to solve what was viewed as too many bureaucratic structures, with too many high-level decision-makers. Consolidation improves span of control but dilutes depth of knowledge. We would probably have recommended a more distributed approach with accountability and discretion at lower levels. One person being accountable for 40% of a nations’ defense budget will make them a target to influence. Hopefully they structure the new organization in a way that gives smart leaders at lower levels the control they need to be successful.

#### [Anduril Demonstrates Connected Defense on NATO’s Eastern Flank](https://www.anduril.com/news/anduril-demonstrates-connected-defense-on-nato-s-eastern-flank)

The U.S. Army Europe and Africa, working closely with NATO Allies, is developing the Eastern Flank Deterrence Line (EFDL), which includes a distributed mission command architecture designed to integrate national and Allied sensors, shooters, and unmanned systems into a shared live-data network.

-   Rather than a fixed formation or location, the EFDL functions as a digital shield stretching across NATO’s eastern border.
    
-   A radar in Estonia could detect incoming aircraft and instantly share that data with air-defense batteries in Latvia or command centers in Poland.
    
-   Each nation remains responsible for defending its own territory, but through the EFDL, their systems contribute to a collective deterrence posture.
    
-   In early November, Anduril joined the U.S. Army’s 10th Army Air and Missile Defense Command (AAMDC) and the Estonian Defense Forces in Tallinn, Estonia, for exercise Digital Shield 1.0, one of the first major event to put the EFDL concept into practice.
    
-   Within 48 hours of arrival, the team set up multiple Menace-T tactical compute and communication kits and established Lattice nodes running in the cloud.
    
-   Together, these nodes created a resilient network that kept data moving, even when connections were jammed, weak, or cut off, a critical capability for any fight along NATO’s eastern flank.
    
-   Multiple Allied sensors were integrated into one live operating picture.
    
-   By connecting these systems through Lattice, Anduril enabled real-time data fusion across previously separate radar, acoustic, and commercial networks.
    
-   Feeds that once operated independently were synchronized and shared instantly across U.S. and Estonian command nodes
    

#### [Small Sea Drones Destroy Giant Enemy Vessels in Real War, Pr0ove Unmanned System’s Efficacy](https://interestingengineering.com/military/sea-drones-destroy-giant-enemy-vessels)

Two sea drones have destroyed two giant enemy vessels in the Black Sea. The operation involved two Baby maritime drones, a type of unmanned surface vessel designed for sea operations.

-   The oil tankers, Kairos and Virat, were hit off Turkey’s Black Sea coast while the vessels were en route to the Russian port of Novorossiysk.
    
-   Officials in Ukraine also claimed that the vessels were helping Russia bypass international sanctions.
    
-   Developed by the Security Service of Ukraine (SBU), Ukraine’s Sea Baby maritime drones have become a central tool in Ukraine’s asymmetric naval strategy in the Black Sea.
    
-   Originally conceived as single-use explosive boats, they have evolved into reusable, modular, long-range naval platforms capable of reconnaissance, direct strikes, and specialized missions such as underwater mine deployment.
    
-   Their capabilities have expanded significantly as the newest versions reportedly feature ranges exceeding 1,500 km, payload capacities of up to 2,000 kg of explosives, improved navigation and communications systems, and optional weapon modules such as remote machine-gun turrets or even small rocket-launcher systems.
    

**Our Take**: We love this example. A low-cost capability that is iterated on and can provide asymmetric cost and capability advantage by employing still high-end capability in new and novel ways.
