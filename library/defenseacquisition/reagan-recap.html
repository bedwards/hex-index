Kudos to the Reagan Institute for another successful Reagan National Defense Forum. Executives from the Department, Congress, and Industry offered key perspectives on the current national security environment and how we can restore deterrence with peace through strength.

The Nation’s Voices on National Defense: Shaping Public Perspectives

  • Honorable Mike Gallagher, Head of Defense, Palantir Technologies

  • Ms. Rachel Hoff, Ronald Reagan Institute Policy Director

  • Sen Rick Scott, Florida

  • Rep Adam Smith, Ranking Member, HASC

The panel provided commentary on insights from the Reagan National Defense Survey

Mike Gallagher: “There is a moment right now for the tech industry with an administration that is committed to doing things differently. Congress has invested a lot of money in big non-traditional bets. The proof will be in the pudding of the next six months in terms of how this money is awarded and whether industry steps up to deliver for the American warfighter. We are going to need to integrate AI rapidly into military operations, not just the sexy pointy end, but all the boring back office functions. In the agreement with MP Materials, the Pentagon essentially guaranteeing demand, unleashing a flood of private sector investment. That is something that could be replicated in other industries.”

Fireside Chat with The Honorable Russell Vought, OMB Director

Director Vought focused on resourcing national defense priorities, fiscal responsibility, innovative funding mechanisms, and rebuilding the DIB - especially shipbuilding.

  • Resources will be there for Golden Dome and shipbuilding

  • Defense spending is non-negotiable with sustained demand signals, creating opportunities for long-term investments and contracts.

  • One Big Beautiful Bill provided $150B in mandatory defense funding over two years. This creates greater certainty outside annual CRs and appropriations chaos.

  • US commercial/defense shipbuilding critical underdeveloped and pushing foreign direct investment.

  • Fixing bureaucracy, performance, and outcomes. Companies must deliver on contracts on time and budget.

  • National mobilization to re-shore supply chains, scale munitions production, and innovative low-cost options.

  • Expect rapid decisions and paradigm shifts, and bulldoze bureaucracy.

Deterrence by Design: Advancing AI for Competitive Advantage Over China

  • Mr. Stephen Ehikian, CEO, C3 AI

  • Mr. Joe Lonsdale, Co-Founder and Managing Partner, 8VC

  • Honorable Emil Michael, USW for Research and Engineering

  • ADM Samuel Paparo, INDOPACOM Commander

  • Mr. Horacio Rozanski, Chairman and CEO, Booz Allen

  • ADM Paparo framed three meta-trends: Information/cyber dominance; commoditized drones making assault costlier, precision penetrating strike; all amplified by AI’s ubiquity in data, compute, algorithms, and human-AI teaming.

  • ADM Paparo: “The mega trend is the ubiquity, the use, the quality of data, of compute, of algorithms and human employment of those at the tactical level (swarming drones, mass data analytics), operational level (how units move, affect fires, are protected), and strategic level (decision superiority).”

  • Mr. Michael: “We’re ahead in AI. The four premier companies are all American. We have the most advanced chips, but when China makes something a national priority, they can dictate top down, connect their data sets. They’re developing indigenous chipsets.”

  • Adoption at speed and scale are the new battlegrounds.

  • China prioritizes mass deployment over perfection; U.S. must counter with faster fielding.

  • Over 1,000 state-level AI laws proposed; patchwork regulation could cripple scalability and innovation.

  • Joe Lonsdale: “It’s now possible to build an autonomous 180-ft ship with as much weaponry as a 400+ ft destroyer at much lower cost. You can have a lot more ships.”

  • This is the most favorable environment in decades for non-traditional defense firms. Move fast—prototype aggressively, accept managed risk, and align with warfighter needs.

  • Mr. Michael: “We have to have a robust small drone program, a robust large drone program, and an even more robust counter drone program.”

  • Mr. Michael: “In the next few weeks to months, not years, you will see AI proliferate throughout the entire Department across three different layers. Corporate and enterprise use-case layers that any big organization would use for efficiency purposes. The intelligence layer — how do you get intelligence, fuse it, analyze it, get more out of what you’re collecting? And then the warfighting layer, which is logistics, modeling, simulation, targeting — all the things that, you essentially are trying to increase the human context window using AI as a tool for that. And we’re going to be doing that on all networks at DoW. It’s going to be my number one priority for the rest of my term.”

  • ADM Paparo: “AI is the rules of autonomy: Don’t send a human being to do something that a machine can do. Don’t lose human agency over offensive operations. The more defensive, the more you should rely on machines.”

Reindustrializing Defense: Recapitalizing and Revitalizing the Industrial Base

  • ADM Daryl Caudle, Chief of Naval Operations

  • Honorable Michael Duffey, USW Acquisition and Sustainment

  • Mr. Steve Parker, President and CEO, Boeing Defense, Space, and Security

  • Mr. Chris Power, CEO, Hadrian

  • Rep Rob Wittman, U.S. House of Representatives

  • Mr. Duffey: “The creation of the Portfolio Acquisition Executive a shared interest between what Congress has promoted what we believe as necessary is consolidating decisions in those officials within the department who have the singular responsibility to deliver capability on a fixed schedule so that we can get equipment and systems and weapons in the hands of the warfighter with a priority for schedule. We want to maintain performance, we want to hold costs down, but schedule is the name of the game when we’re looking at the threats that we’re facing.”

  • Mr. Duffey: “There’s going to be a steady demand signal and we’ve been working very aggressively with the industry to help create that incentive to invest their own private capital to help expand the capacity that we need.”

  • Rep Wittman: “Today, the acquisition process with the requirement writing and actually fielding of these systems on average is 800 to 900 days. This year’s NDAA is going to bring that time down to 90 to 120 days. We want to make sure with this new process is that we are taking risk and we’re highlighting what we learn from taking those risks.”

  • Shift burden of capital investment back to industry via profit incentives on efficiency gains. Predictable demand + schedule focus = green light for private CapEx.

  • Government equity stakes/offtake agreements seen as fair for strategic areas (e.g., rare earths) if competitive and taxpayer returns protected.

Partners in Production: Investing and Building for National Defense

  • Mr. Christopher Calio, Chairman and CEO, RTX

  • Mr. Jamie Dimon, Chairman and CEO, JPMorganChase

Jamie Dimon: “If we’re going to write a book about how the West was lost, it will be because... we didn’t get our act together here... didn’t have the strongest military... and allowed Europe to fall apart. We did a deep analysis of our national security business and we want to do at least 50% more over the next 10 years so that’s the $1.5T and then the $10B of investing is to do special financing for vendor supply chains to double or triple production. If you need to build more welders we may go in and help double that welding school.”

Chris Calio: “Half of our supply chain is what you’d call a medium or small business. We are making sure that they have the access to the capital, the confidence to purchase that long lead material, or even better, hire and train people. Ukraine taught us a significant lesson about the fragility of our supply chain. There are huge production gaps, Patriot a two-year gap. Javelin, multi-year gap. Reconstituting that supply chain is quite a challenge. We are responsible for that entire ecosystem.”

Honorable Pete Hegseth, Secretary of War

  • The War Department is working to get back to basics on the topics of restoring the warrior ethos, readiness, accountability, standards, discipline and lethality.

  • Along the fourth line of effort — defense industrial buildup — which he believes may be the most important, he said that supercharging America’s defense industrial base underwrites all other lines of effort.

  • “Our objective is simple, if monumental: transform the entire acquisition system to rapidly accelerate the fielding of capabilities and focus on results.”

  • “The key to that objective is moving away from the current, prime contractor-dominated, low-competition defense industrial base to “a future powered by dynamic vendor space that accelerates production by combining investment at a commercial pace, with the uniquely American ability to scale and scale quickly.”

Forging Ahead with Speed: Transitioning to New Technologies at the Pentagon

  • General Randy George, Army Chief of Staff

  • Mr. Dan Jablonsky, CEO, Ursa Major Technologies

  • Sen Mike Rounds, South Dakota

  • Mr. James D. Taiclet, CEO, Lockheed Martin

Sen Rounds: “I’ve shared in the past and I continue to believe that Russia really doesn’t want peace. I still think that Russia really doesn’t want peace. I should say Mr. Putin really doesn’t want peace because he thinks he can win against Ukraine. I think Ukraine is putting up a marvelous fight. I think the vast majority of us in the United States Senate feel that we should do whatever we can to assist them.”

GEN George: “There’s obviously a lot we can learn from the Ukrainians and everybody gets really focused on just the drones sometimes and the technology, but you know, how they’re organizing for the fight that they’re doing, and you know, coming back and we are trying to take those lessons on a near daily basis. A lot of that is feeding into how we are going to have to build up our industrial base. We’re going to have to move at speed. We’re going to have to cut through the bureaucracy.”

Jim Taiclet: “We need transformational change in the way our military operates and the relationship between the department of war and industry and broadly speaking industry. You have technology moving at the fastest rate in human history, both digital and physical technology. and you have a relatively fixed resource to address this equation and the only way to change the outcome which is a diminishing outcome of effectiveness is to change the system.

The way to do that in my view is to make the defense and aerospace industry as much like the tech sector as we can. And how do we do that? I think you start with the service approach to a business. I use the example of Google maps. It was pretty rudimentary when it came out, but every 3 to six weeks it got better by ingesting data, making corrections, having a better wireless network along the way, better chipsets that you could process the mapping faster and all the traffic issues and data. And so, every three to six weeks that system got better and now it’s pretty darn good.

What we did for HIMARs was we took satellite data to identify moving targets of interest. We took a F-35 sensor system to aim it towards the target to get a weapons quality track. We sent that up back through the satellites back into the HIMARS vehicle using a component that we invented and put on the network - and now it can hit a moving target. The problem is there’s no organization in acquisition system able to buy that service to buy that improvement and that’s something that really has to change in the acquisition system.”

Dan Jablonsky: “Factories are a great example. We need them for munitions, we need them for ships, ships, shipyards, we need them for hypersonic capabilities, and space capabilities. They have to be built on first principles, I mean 2030 manufacturing principles. So, they should be agile, they should be additive, they should be flexible, and you should be able to build lots of stuff in them, so you don’t have to lay capital in the ground every time you want to change something. That gives the military a lot more strategic advantages here and a better cost point as the ecosystem changes.”

Defending the Homeland: Establishing Superiority in Space and Missile Defense

  • Senator Deb Fischer, Nebraska

  • General Michael Guetlein, Director of Golden Dome

  • Honorable Troy Meink, Secretary of the Air Force

  • Ms. Kathy Warden, Chair, CEO, and President, Northrop Grumman

Gen Guetlein: “Golden Dome is about building a layered defense capability for the nation to protect the nation against a tack against the homeland... building a systems systems architecture collaborating with our industry partners and our national labs to come up with next generation technologies to build that layered defense capability to protect the homeland.”

Gen Guetlein: “The technology exists. This is not a technology problem. We have proven all elements of the technology in one way or another. The real challenge is how do I bring together capabilities that have never been integrated network together into a system of systems type architecture.”

Gen Guetlein: “Golden Dome is about partnering with industry in new and innovative ways to tap into the innovation... and to do it with transparency... I’ve met with well over two to 300 companies one on one to explain to them what we’re trying to do.”

SECAF Meink: “I think you actually kind of hit a little bit on it in the question and that’s integrating a lot of the technologies with sensors and the defensive systems together in a way that will meet the timelines—pretty tight timelines in some cases. That’s going to be a big challenge, but it’s actually a challenge we’ll see not only within Golden Dome, but really across everything we do in the department.”

Funding the Force: Aligning the Defense Budget with Global Priorities

  • Mr. Chris Brose, President and Chief Strategy Officer, Anduril

  • Rep Ken Calvert, Chair, House Appropriations Defense Subcommittee

  • Rep Pat Ryan, New York, 18th District

Chairman Calvert: “We cannot continue to operate the DoD, the largest enterprise on the planet under a CR. It costs us probably a billion dollars a month to operate under a continuing resolution just because of the inefficiencies that are created throughout the enterprise. To me that is not an option, and I would hope that my colleagues on both sides of the aisle agree that that is not an option. From what I’ve heard today, and I think everybody in this room has been hearing today too we have all the momentum, all the energy to do this. Of all the things in this country where we’re not aligned, this is one of the few places where we have bipartisan and bicameral support.

As we get into FY27, if we get to a top line that we can start looking at our ship building capability, planes, missiles, ammunition, we’re low on everything and so we’re going to have to plus up those numbers to get to where we have to be.”

Congressman Ryan: “We are spending more money, and we are getting less capability for the money that we are spending. I agree the top line needs to go up, but we need a model of military power where we actually use technology to generate abundance and capability.

The deal flow on defense tech venture is going to reach $10B this year. That’s double what it was just a year ago. So, it’s what can we do at a policy level to reward, incentivize and unleash those investments to be complimentarily, synergistic and enabling with the direct federal spending.

I think the emphasis needs to be on fielding new things quickly. I think if you do that, you can then have a far more nuanced conversation about getting rid of old things.”

Chris Brose: “We’re an eight-year old company. We’ve been doing a great job, we’ve been succeeding, but most of our success is still in the future. CRs are catastrophic for us in the sense that if there isn’t flexibility it becomes very difficult for the department to identify new priorities to move flexibly to adapt, to move at the pace that technologies are moving.

With new technology, particularly the emphasis on the space-based approach to Golden Dome, I think you can actually field these game changing capabilities, you can do it much faster and you can do it at a fraction of the cost that people are assuming because they’re assuming that, well, if I take the current architecture that I have and multiply it by 2500, it costs $300B. And it’s like I majored in philosophy, but I think that’s a really bad way to cost estimate.

I’m sitting here as the president of Anduril, but I say we need Virginia class submarines. We need B-21s. We need F-47s. I would get rid of the F-35. I would move to F-47 and F/A-XX to end the long national nightmare of fifth gen and get to the next iteration. We can do it quickly. We need to do it quickly. I would collaborate those systems with robotic systems to achieve a real high low mix. You need to do it across the entirety of the joint force. Our weapon systems are archaic. We need to complement legacy weapons with new weapons that can be cheaply built and mass-produced. This is not hard. America can do this.”

Harnessing Innovation to Enable Lethality: Modernizing Business Systems and Overcoming Contested Logistics

  • Ms. Tara Murphy Dougherty, CEO, Govini

  • Honorable John Phelan, Secretary of the Navy

  • General Kenneth Wilsbach, Air Force Chief of Staff

  • SECNAV: “I brought in VADM Seiko Okano to lead the Navy’s Rapid Capabilities Office with a clear mandate: the warfighter is the decision-maker not Pentagon bureaucracy. We are iterating faster, testing in the field, getting real feedback, and investing in game changing capabilities with war-footing speed.”

  • SECNAV: “Any change order comes through me. We are going to take our warfighters’ requirements, translate them into stable, producible designs, and stick with them once they’re set. If anyone wants to tinker with them, I’ve reserved Fridays at 5 PM in my office for change order decisions—no drift, no delay.”

  • Gen. Wilsbach: “The biggest challenge for Air Force readiness is the tension between sustaining the old and investing in the new... you can’t just throw away all of your current hardware and get new the very next day.”

  • Tara Murphy Dougherty: “Trade acquisition risk and increase that in order to buy down operational risk. It’s going to require the culture change. There is significant interest in experimentation and in new innovations. Over the past five years significant support from the capital class to invest in defense. This is the perfect time to bring in commercial tech for defense challenges. We have to use AI to drastically decrease sustainment costs and increase readiness levels. There are foreign parts in 100% of our weapon systems. Huge numbers of Chinese parts. 90% of raw materials are processed in China.”

Secretary Phelan posted on X afterwards:

  • This week, President Trump made it clear: we will build a Golden Fleet. We will invest smartly in the cornerstones of American seapower—carriers, destroyers, amphibs, and submarines but we also need new ships.

  • A new frigate, based on an American design, with flexible capability tailored to requirements from our warfighters and CNO, and built on a timeline faster than the program we cancelled.

  • Unmanned vessels to bring options to the fight and pull new, non-traditional partners into the American shipbuilding ecosystem. Generational investment in tankers, oilers, and logistics ships—the unsung heroes that keep the Fleet in the fight—and the spark that will ignite the industrial base and help power the commercial shipbuilding renaissance the President has called for.
