**WHAT’S DANGEROUS** [about people dancing in front](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZtosviKAxzc) of a cell phone camera?

The U.S. government has been trying to answer that question for the past two years with TikTok, a wildly addictive [app used by many U.S. military service members](https://taskandpurpose.com/news/army-first-sergeant-tiktok-social-media/) and an [estimated](https://www.statista.com/statistics/1299807/number-of-monthly-unique-tiktok-users/) 135 million other Americans.

It’s especially popular with young people, who increasingly use TikTok [as a search engine.](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/09/16/technology/gen-z-tiktok-search-engine.html) And a “small but growing share” of American adults say they regularly [get their news there](https://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2022/10/21/more-americans-are-getting-news-on-tiktok-bucking-the-trend-on-other-social-media-sites/).

Owned by the Beijing firm Bytedance, TikTok has been under a U.S. security review [since 2020](https://www.reuters.com/technology/senators-seek-update-us-security-review-tiktok-2022-06-24/), and President Joe Biden on Sept. 15 ordered “additional national security factors” to be considered.

[Hannah Kelley](https://www.cnas.org/people/hannah-kelley), a research assistant for the technology and national security program at CNAS, says there’s substantial debate among policymakers on the exact nature of the threat ([or if there even is one](https://news.northeastern.edu/2022/06/14/tiktok-data-collection-concerns/)). But “the potential is a really important part of the threat analysis.”

Indeed, considerable ink has been spilled on the [potential uses of TikTok that sound terrifying](https://twitter.com/robertcobrien/status/1583537532800622593) alongside evidence-backed issues raised about the app over the years: In 2019, TikTok [ordered moderators to censor](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2019/sep/25/revealed-how-tiktok-censors-videos-that-do-not-please-beijing) videos that Beijing didn’t like. In 2020, it [collected unique identifiers](https://www.wsj.com/articles/tiktok-tracked-user-data-using-tactic-banned-by-google-11597176738) on millions of users for more than a year to track online behavior without their consent—and in apparent violation of Google policies. In 2021, it [began to automatically gather](https://time.com/6071773/tiktok-faceprints-voiceprints-privacy/) biometric data. And this year, [an independent researcher found code allowing TikTok to log keystrokes](https://krausefx.com/blog/announcing-inappbrowsercom-see-what-javascript-commands-get-executed-in-an-in-app-browser), while others found that [moderators failed to catch](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/10/21/technology/tiktok-youtube-facebook-misinformation-ads.html) 90% of ads “featuring false and misleading messages about elections” on its platform.

“I think everything feels so drawn out and thorny because it’s high stakes for all involved,” Kelley said of [the yearslong U.S. review](https://www.reuters.com/technology/senators-seek-update-us-security-review-tiktok-2022-06-24/), adding that whatever the Biden administration decides will likely be “a precedent-setting moment on tackling a new suite of emerging vulnerabilities.” As she and others [have pointed out](https://warontherocks.com/2020/08/beyond-tiktok-preparing-for-future-digital-threats/), digital privacy, data rights, and security issues require thinking beyond a single app.

In response to critics, TikTok has made changes and insisted that its U.S. data is walled off from China. But [audio leaked in June from 80 internal TikTok meetings](https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/emilybakerwhite/tiktok-tapes-us-user-data-china-bytedance-access) showed it was clearly not.

A TikTok Trust and Safety team member said during a Sept. 2021 meeting, “everything is seen in China,” according to the leaked audio. And that same month, a TikTok director said a Beijing-based engineer was a “master admin” who “has access to everything.”

[I find it difficult](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2022/aug/09/twitter-saudi-arabia-dissident-spying) to trust any [social media company](https://www.forbes.com/sites/kashmirhill/2014/06/28/facebook-manipulated-689003-users-emotions-for-science/?sh=4d6fc502197c), especially one with a [Chinese government official](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/beijing-tightens-grip-on-bytedance-by-quietly-taking-stake-china-board-seat) on its board. As TikTok CEO Shou Zi Chew [confirmed to lawmakers](https://int.nyt.com/data/documenttools/tik-tok-s-response-to-republican-senators/e5f56d3ef4886b33/full.pdf) in June (under U.S. employee oversight, he claimed), “employees outside the U.S., **including China-based employees, can have access** to TikTok U.S. user data.”

And really, that’s what we’re talking about here: Not dance videos but data, a massive amount of it, is being sucked up by a Chinese app that [the Pentagon](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/01/04/us/tiktok-pentagon-military-ban.html) is clearly worried about for [obvious reasons](https://www.businessinsider.com/tiktok-us-lawmakers-warned-house-of-representatives-high-risk-2022-8): the U.S. military has [killed people based on data.](https://www.nybooks.com/online/2014/05/10/we-kill-people-based-metadata/)

“By itself, I don't think that the TikTok app on people's devices is a significant threat,” [says](https://www.wired.com/story/tiktok-nationa-security-threat-why/) Jake Williams, a former NSA hacker, “but the potential for Chinese data collection across the platform is a larger concern, especially when combined with other data already acquired by Chinese state actors.”
