Personally, I like to avoid buying things. For the past few years, especially, I have derived significant fulfillment from cancelling subscriptions and eliminating costly habits. I do not spend much time reading gift guides and product recommendations because I have little personal use for them. When I saw Brian Merchant’s “[How To Quit Spotify](https://www.bloodinthemachine.com/p/a-complete-guide-to-quitting-spotify)” explainer going around on Notes this past week, for example, I felt confused as to why anyone would bother shopping around for subscriptions to Tidal, Apple Music, or Qobuz when options like “hosting your own library with [Jellyfin](https://jellyfin.org/)” or “using YouTube with an adblocker” exist. If I’m going to jump through any hoops, I want the reward to either be a delicious meal or liberation from the necessity of buying things at all. Instruction manuals that are supposed to teach you how to do a better job of buying things typically do not help me achieve either of those goals.

Nevertheless, I don’t operate under the presumption that what feels right to me would be good for anyone else. That’s why I don’t do record reviews, and it’s why this newsletter is generally more concerned with asking questions than detailing my own opinions. The fact that I don’t personally enjoy taste round-ups or gift guides doesn’t mean I look down on people who do. I have always liked that the Country Music Association’s top award, for example, goes to the “[Entertainer of the Year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Country_Music_Association_Award_for_Entertainer_of_the_Year)” and not whoever wrote the best song or made the best album. I think it’s admirable for an artist to be at least as concerned with making people happy in the present as they are with creating something that will theoretically be revered long after they’re dead.

So, in that spirit, and as an exercise in writing things that aren’t just the big, sprawling essays that I personally find satisfying, here is an instruction manual for spending money.

### I. Books

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!me86!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdddc573b-0049-433c-a86f-3c455eb94f5e_1200x1600.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!me86!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdddc573b-0049-433c-a86f-3c455eb94f5e_1200x1600.png)

For anyone who enjoys this newsletter, there’s no better book recommendation I could make than *Death of a Pirate: British Radio and the Making of the Information Age* by University of Chicago history professor Adrian Johns. This thing is an absolute heater from cover to cover, tracing a narrative thread from the birth of radio to the sixties UK counterculture that will forever change how you feel about the internet, pop music, and every electronic mass media platform that will ever exist in your lifetime. I would say it reads like the script for the greatest Adam Curtis documentary never made, but Curtis actually did cover some of this material in a [blog post](https://www.bbc.co.uk/webarchive/https%3A%2F%2Fwww.bbc.co.uk%2Fblogs%2Fadamcurtis%2Fentries%2Ffdb484c8-99a1-32a3-83be-20108374b985) for the BBC about a year after *Death of a Pirate* was published and the result was much less satisfying than the book itself.

For exceptionally wealthy gift-givers, I can recommend *The Manual: How to Have a Number One the Easy Way* by the Timelords, better known as Bill Drummond and Jimmy Cauty of the KLF. This is probably the greatest book about music ever written. If you’re not familiar with it, the book was first published in nineteen eighty-eight after Drummond and Cauty scored a UK number one with “Doctorin’ the Tardis,” a sample-based novelty record that played on listeners’ childhood nostalgia for the glory days of the venerable BBC science fiction program *Doctor Who*, which was, at that point in time, running on fumes and teetering on the verge of cancellation.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Vv0F!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdfc7e850-7cfc-4951-95e9-0ed4f577b243_930x1210.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Vv0F!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdfc7e850-7cfc-4951-95e9-0ed4f577b243_930x1210.png)

It was meant to serve as an instruction manual for other aspiring hitmakers who wished to follow in the Timelords’ footsteps and create a Number One of their own. Though it does paint a very detailed picture of a particular moment in time, rendering most of the specific instructions nearly useless for anyone wanting to earnestly follow the recipe in modern times, the core insights about pop music and technology remain deeply resonant even now. Like the rest of the KLF’s catalog, the book has been out of print for decades, which is why it would make such a compelling gift. Used copies go for hundreds of dollars online. Very few people have one, and I would imagine that everyone who does treasures it deeply.

The contents of *The Manual* are [freely available online](https://freshonthenet.co.uk/the-manual-by-the-klf/) and I would consider the book essential reading for anyone who likes this newsletter. If you’re interested in giving it as a gift but don’t have hundreds of dollars to spend on a thirty-year-old pamphlet, you could always track down the text or a PDF and print a copy out yourself. The value of such a volume may even exceed that of an “official” copy by virtue of the care invested in its creation, and I doubt very much that Drummond and Cauty, who named their musical project the “Kopyright Liberation Front,” would think less of you for going that route.

### II. Television

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!81cl!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5bbcb9be-6534-41cb-bd70-011da411b065_1000x1264.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!81cl!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5bbcb9be-6534-41cb-bd70-011da411b065_1000x1264.png)

For anyone looking for something to watch, I can recommend *Dragon Ball*. In nineteen eighty-four, Akira Toriyama began publishing chapters of *Dragon Ball* in *Weekly Shōnen Jump* magazine. The manga became a hit and quickly spawned two animated adaptations: *Dragon Ball*, and *Dragon Ball Z*. American audiences didn’t get turned on to *Dragon Ball* until Cartoon Network began airing episodes of *Dragon Ball Z* during their late-night “Toonami” programing block in the late nineties. That version, which had a hard-driving techno-rock soundtrack created exclusively for the American dub by Texas-based composer Bruce Faulconer, is the one I was first exposed to back in high school.

Though it was wildly successful—*DBZ* protagonist Son Goku is now a regular fixture at the Macy’s Thanksgiving Day parade—it made for a strange introduction to the series. Toriyama’s manga begins when Son Goku is twelve years old, and the early chapters introduce characters who will go on to be an important part of his life for decades to come. The *DBZ* anime begins when Goku is already an adult with a son of his own, and it often declines to explain who the supporting cast are or where they came from. Faulconer’s guitar-driven soundtrack is also a world away from the whimsical score that Shinsuke Kikuchi composed for the original Japanese version of the show.

The first *Dragon Ball* anime, which tells Goku’s story from the beginning, makes for a much more satisfying viewing experience. The stateside success of *DBZ* allowed for the creation of an excellent English dub featuring most of the same actors, and no attempt is made to interfere with Kikuchi’s wonderful score, which perfectly suits the material. While *DBZ* often revolved around snarling antiheroes beating on one another over the course of long, multi-episode brawls, *Dragon Ball* hews closer to Toriyama’s original conception of the book as an adventure story modeled on the sixteenth century Chinese fantasy novel *Journey to the West*.

Each new story typically offers something meaningfully different than the last, and they tend to wrap up quickly. By the time he was creating the stories that form the basis of *DBZ*, Toriyama understood what the audience wanted from him and was doing his best to meet that demand, but *Dragon Ball* adapts the stories he came up with when he was still experimenting. It’s a much more playful, stimulating viewing experience as a result. Even if you end up preferring *DBZ* in the end, you’ll still enjoy it a lot more if you got to know the supporting cast ahead of time by watching *Dragon Ball* first. When I originally watched *DBZ* as a teenager on Cartoon Network, I thought Krillin was just some guy, and was often confused about why he was getting so much screen time. When I started watching it later on in life after having finished *Dragon Ball*, I cheered basically every time he showed up and wondered where he was whenever he was absent.

Both *Dragon Ball* and *Dragon Ball Z* are widely available on streaming platforms. So is *Dragon Ball Z Kai*, a new adaptation of *DBZ*’s source material with updated visuals and less of the filler content that *DBZ*’s producers added in order to give Toriyama more time to complete new chapters of the manga. This is *The Seat of Loss*, though. We don’t like streaming platforms very much around here. Too many ads, too many recurring fees, and too much incentive to waste time scrolling aimlessly through libraries full of garbage instead of watching with intention. As such, we definitely cannot recommend giving streaming subscriptions as gifts, even to specialized platforms like the anime-focused Crunchyroll.

Official releases of the English dub of *Dragon Ball* on DVD are difficult to come by. The best available version, which enthusiasts refer to as the “blue brick” editions online, went out of print some time ago. Amazon and eBay are so lousy with low-quality bootleg copies that you’ll likely have a devil of a time tracking down a complete set of the genuine article if you decide to go that route. Very frustrating if you’re the type of collector who cares deeply about whether or not the boxes on your shelves are the same size and color. For the rest of us, though, the lack of an easily obtainable official release can function as an opportunity for creativity.

Akira Toriyama is dead. *Dragon Ball* first aired three decades ago. Toei Animation doesn’t seem to have access to master materials that are of any higher quality than what you can currently find online, making future physical releases unlikely. There’s been an industry-wide shift towards streaming, which has led to reduced residuals for voice actors relative to what they would have been making on physical releases. The argument that one should go through the effort of tracking down officially-sanctioned blue brick editions at this point is indistinguishable from the symptoms of obsessive compulsive disorder. In a world with more sensible copyright laws, *Dragon Ball* might be in the public domain right now, and there would be nothing stopping you downloading the entire series from the internet and gifting it to your loved ones on DVD-R or a USB drive, packaged with homemade artwork. Perhaps whatever safeguards are meant to stop you from doing that in *this* world aren’t really worth fussing about.

Either way, I recommend watching one episode per day, preferably with someone you love. The exception would be the tournament arcs, which it might make sense to consume all at once on weekends, like a wrestling pay-per-view. You’ll know what to do once you get there yourself.

### III. Video Games

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!WzYn!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdab04aa8-45fb-491b-a7ed-e7d242a9abeb_4032x2268.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!WzYn!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdab04aa8-45fb-491b-a7ed-e7d242a9abeb_4032x2268.png)

In the world of video games, it is precipitously easy to waste vast amounts of time and money. That’s where the real money seems to be as far as this particular medium is concerned: once you buy a PlayStation, you feel like you’re not getting the most out of the purchase unless you’re paying for the monthly subscription that enables online play. Once you buy one of the big-budget triple-A tentpoles that you told yourself you needed the latest and greatest console to gain access to, you feel like you’re not getting the full experience unless you buy all the requisite season passes and paid expansions. Even if you stick to free-to-play titles, which sometimes allow for online multiplayer even without a PlayStation Plus subscription, you are constantly being reminded of how much more you’d be getting out of the experience if you paid for a “battle pass” or some other tacked-on expense.

In the PC ecosystem, online multiplayer isn’t gated behind any subscription fees and games tend to go on sale much more often and for much lower prices. In their own way, though, those low prices can be a trap in and of themselves. PC gaming enthusiasts frequently complain about the massive size of their “[backlogs](https://backloggd.com/),” that is to say, the gargantuan number of games they’ve purchased at a discount and then never actually bothered to play. The idea that one can derive “value” from scrolling storefronts and scooping up licenses at bargain-bin prices is, in and of itself, kind of a scam. This approach, as well as the subscription-based model that powers Microsoft’s Game Pass offering, does little to prevent an ocean of mediocre flotsam from getting between the user and the best experiences the medium actually has to offer.

Nintendo’s first-party games remain a compelling reason to opt-in to their platform, and when the next *Animal Crossing* title comes out, anyone who decides to pick up the current-gen Nintendo hardware in order to play it with their friends is probably going to stand a decent chance of getting their money’s worth. Even so, the PC ecosystem is ultimately superior to any console-based alternatives, and there’s certainly no argument that anyone is better off buying Sony or Microsoft hardware at this point.

So, for a discerning consumer with unlimited time and money, there’s really no substitute for the pleasure and functionality of building one’s own machine. Solid information about what parts to order is easy to come by in online hobbyist communities, and even though the process of assembling them isn’t really that hard, it will still teach you things that will meaningfully deepen your understanding of the world around you. There are computers everywhere for those with the eyes to see, and tinkering can sometimes be more rewarding than spending time in the virtual worlds that your hardware is meant to be able to conjure up.

As such, *The Seat of Loss* cannot recommend any traditional game consoles, gift cards, or pre-built gaming PCs. These gifts would either nudge the recipient into predatory, parasitic software ecosystems that exist only to siphon away their time and money, or rob them of one of the most rewarding experiences the hobby can offer. There is only a single all-in-one boxed gaming product this publication can recommend for the gamer in your life: Valve’s Steam Deck.

The Steam Deck is effectively a Linux PC with the form factor of a handheld gaming console, a bit larger and bulkier than a Nintendo Switch but similar in shape and layout. Unlike a Nintendo Switch, you don’t need to jailbreak the thing or sign up for any subscription products in order to unlock the full potential of the hardware. Online play is free by default, and Valve does nothing to prevent you from using it to play games that you obtained somewhere other than their storefront. It’s incredibly easy to set up a suite of emulators on the device that will allow you to download and play games from a variety of consoles. It can run Xbox-exclusive titles like *Lost Odyssey* and many users have even had success using it to play *Bloodborne*, a well-regarded title from *Elden Ring* developers From Software that is currently only officially available as a PlayStation Four title.

The Steam Deck has been on the market for a few years now, which means it is not powerful enough to run many triple-A new releases, but that’s honestly more of a feature than a bug. I’m really struggling to think of a single title it can’t run that I can conclusively say is worth your time. There are only two versions available: one with an LCD screen, and one with an OLED screen. The OLED version is much nicer, but the LCD is easier to find used. If the gamer in your life does not already have a Steam Deck, they would likely be delighted to receive either one.

As far as software is concerned, *The Seat of Loss* recommends the work of [Analgesic Productions](https://analgesic.productions/), who have developed titles like *Anodyne 2: Return to Dust* and *Sephonie*. Independent works by small studios often distinguish themselves from mainstream titles through bold, stylish writing, music, and visuals. Analgesic’s games do all of that in spades, but they also work very hard to create innovative gameplay that defies expectations just as successfully. This results in experiences that feel very distinct to me from most other indie titles, which often succeed by bolting unconventional aesthetics onto predictable, tried-and-true game mechanics that are usually lifted wholesale from existing works. Their latest game, an Action RPG called *Angeline Era* that takes light inspiration from Falcom’s *Ys* series and twists it in thrilling new directions, releases next week.

Analgesic’s Melos Han-Tani is also active on [Substack](https://melodicambient.substack.com/) and [BlueSky](https://bsky.app/profile/hantani.bsky.social). I always find his takes on the medium to be worth reading.

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mHED!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb7b0bc19-06d6-4e70-b4cc-8968f7dd3617_2670x1802.png)

For anyone who likes to cook, *The Seat of Loss* recommends [Tatung](https://yunhai.shop/collections/tatung-electric-rice-cooker-and-steamer) stainless steel rice cookers. For many years, I used a Zojirushi rice cooker every day and loved it. I was so attached to the device that even after I got rid of all the other teflon in my kitchen, I resisted replacing it for a long time. I loved how easy it was to use, I loved the way it looked, and I loved the little song it played whenever it finished cooking up some brown rice or oatmeal. Nevertheless, it still used teflon, which kept coming off the bottom of the internal cooking surface and getting into my food. The convenience I treasured came at a cost I was no longer willing to pay. This year, my partner got us a Tatung six-cup stainless steel cooker, and I’ve been very happy with it. If anyone you love is currently making rice in a scratched-up teflon cooker, I think they’ll appreciate the opportunity to make a change.

### V. Music

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!r4xj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb77eec10-d41a-4f61-9495-4e2f608a0634_1946x1946.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!r4xj!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb77eec10-d41a-4f61-9495-4e2f608a0634_1946x1946.png)

*The Seat of Loss* does not recommend any music streaming platforms. While this publication has never shied away from criticizing Spotify, it also finds the notion that Spotify is uniquely bad relative to competitors like Apple Music sort of confusing. It is distasteful, for example, that Spotify runs [advertisements for ICE](https://spotifyunwrapped.net/) in the United States, but the only reason Apple Music does not also do this is because they do not currently run any advertisements at all.

*The Seat of Loss* is also a bit surprised by the intensity of the backlash that erupted in response to the news of Daniel Ek’s [involvement with Helsing](https://www.reuters.com/business/media-telecom/spotify-founder-daniel-eks-next-act-long-bets-european-defence-health-2025-10-01/), a German firm that makes military drones. There is currently a hot war being fought on the European continent between Russia and Ukraine. Have you ever, like, looked at how far away Sweden and Germany are from Russia on a map? While this publication recognizes the possibility that Russia has neither the inclination nor the capability to extend the scope of this war beyond Ukraine’s borders, it humbly suggests that maybe the idea that western Europe might need some military drones in the not-too-distant future is, perhaps, [a bit less controversial](https://spotlight.ebu.ch/p/behind-the-spotify-boycott-daniel) than some folks are making it out to be.

Now, I have a lot of issues with Spotify. I have declined to put my own music on there for over a decade. I just don’t know that I find the argument that it’s worth boycotting them in favor of other, nearly identical platforms very compelling. My understanding is that if the majority of Spotify’s userbase were to abandon them and switch to Apple Music or Tidal, artists wouldn’t necessarily make more money. Whichever service replaced Spotify as market leader would effectively just become what Spotify currently is. The differences would be minuscule.

This past September, Democratic congresswoman Rashida Tlaib [re-introduced](https://tlaib.house.gov/posts/tlaib-introduces-living-wage-for-musicians-act-2) the [Living Wage for Musicians Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/house-bill/7763), a piece of legislation crafted with the help of a non-profit called United Musicians and Allied Workers that is designed to increase the amount of money artists make from streaming. During the early days of the pandemic, when the impossibility of making money from live shows forced even the most resolutely offline artists to start thinking very hard about this issue, UMAW tried as hard as they could to come up with the best possible solution to the problem. Their hope was to draft and pass a bill that could guarantee artists a minimum penny per stream on platforms like Spotify.

Do you know how the Living Wage for Musicians Act proposes to do that? By increasing the cost of a subscription by fifty percent. The bill wouldn’t force Spotify itself to press some kind of magic “pay artists a living wage” button that they’ve secretly had hidden in their headquarters this whole time. It wouldn’t force record labels into equitable splits with artists. It would just force the consumer to pay more. That’s how you “fix” the issue of low streaming payouts. Individual consumers can’t do it by choosing one service over another. The problem is the model that underpins the entire business. Wherever you go, there it is.

Furthermore, one could make the argument that for most people, low streaming payouts aren’t necessarily a problem at all. The value of recordings has been decreasing at a fairly consistent rate since the dawn of the vinyl era in the fifties. There have been booms and busts, but if you zoom out far enough, the overall trajectory is pretty clear. When discussing big technological shifts like the move to streaming or the disruptions of the iTunes era, it can be tempting to describe the devaluation of recorded music as an aberration caused by some sort of malicious outside actor like big tech or peer-to-peer file-sharing, but maybe there’s a simpler explanation. Maybe the more recordings there are on the market, the less any single one of them is worth.

Does it really make sense, then, for the government to step in and insist that consumers pay more? Should the plight of “struggling artists”—who are effectively small business owners—really be a serious priority for the left? Even if those advocating for the Living Wage for Musicians act know it has no chance of actually becoming law, is it even particularly effective as a means of engaging the disenfranchised back into electoral politics? In this economy, how many people care enough about “struggling artists” to demand a steep increase in the cost of a Spotify subscription?

All of which is to say, *The Seat of Loss* does not currently recommend gift memberships to to Apple Music, Tidal, YouTube Music, or Qobuz. *The Seat of Loss* remains critical of Spotify and other streaming platforms, but does not see much cause to view one of them as uniquely worse than any of the others. We also don’t really see the notion of a return to physical formats like CDs or vinyl as being particularly viable or desirable, either. Stories about the “resurgence” of such formats usually decline to mention that they each represent a single-digit percentage of overall consumption even when they’re in the throes of a so-called revival. [More power](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BUhCKYIMbDg) to you if you’re into that sort of thing, though.

At this time, the official position of this publication is that music is most rewarding as a participatory pastime rather than something purely consumption-oriented. *The Seat of Loss* does not believe that much good can be achieved solely by seeking to become a more enlightened, ethical consumer of musical recordings. Therefore, in addition to streaming subscriptions, this publication also declines to recommend any turntables, CD players, or individual recordings.

(We do encourage you to curate and host your own streamable digital media libraries if that’s something that interests you, especially if you plan on making those libraries available to your friends. In the long-term, in fact, we’d like to see the construction of mesh wireless networks in communities all over the United States that would allow enthusiasts to share those libraries with entire communities. Something akin to Havana’s legendary SNET, or perhaps something more similar to Barcelona’s Guifi or Oaxaca’s TIC-AC.)

For the music lover in your life, then, *The Seat of Loss* recommends buying an instrument. The dizzying number of cheap, low-quality options available online can make this challenging—learning an instrument can be intimidating, and that initial barrier to enjoyment might prove impenetrable if the instrument always sounds bad no matter how hard the operator tries to play it properly. Your gift will stand the best chance of having the desired impact if you can be certain it will sound good the moment the recipient begins to play it.

One option *The Seat of Loss* can recommend is an acoustic stringed instrument like a banjo or mandolin. If a local luthier isn’t a viable option for you, the online retailer Elderly only sells reliably high quality instruments and sets them up properly before mailing them out. A solid entry-level instrument will cost at least five hundred dollars, but it will sound great and look beautiful. Get a few of these instruments in the same room together, and magical things can happen even between players of very limited ability. No computers, microphones, or amplifiers necessary.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OwAg!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff47e3f1e-8b58-4bb3-87cd-56eceb63c49d_1600x1047.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OwAg!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff47e3f1e-8b58-4bb3-87cd-56eceb63c49d_1600x1047.png)

In the event that your intended recipient has a non-negotiable lack of interest in the sound of such instruments, *The Seat of Loss* recommends giving them a “rompler,” specifically the Korg M1 or the Korg Triton. These are keyboards that come loaded with a wide variety of very high-quality sounds, many of which you will [instantly recognize](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TVmZarPUc-Q&list=RDTVmZarPUc-Q) from popular songs in which they were used. They’re less versatile than proper synthesizers and less customizable than proper samplers, but the tradeoff is that they always sound good, even if the operator doesn’t really know what they’re doing. These machines have a tried-and-true capability to make people feel good, especially people whose formative years as a listener took place during a time when these instruments and their sample libraries were often heard in mainstream popular music.

*The Seat of Loss* also encourages donating to your local community radio station. Community radio is decentralized, largely volunteer-run cultural infrastructure, so each station is different. The specific benefits of donating will differ on a case-by-case basis. You may be able to donate on your intended recipient’s behalf, which could sign them up for email updates, or potentially even a print publication that gets mailed to their home periodically. This publication is aware of a significant level of discourse surrounding the disappearance of “third places” where human beings can congregate freely outside of work and school. Community radio stations make pretty good third places, and their ability to serve that function correlates directly to the amount of support they receive. Making a donation in your intended recipient’s name might nudge them to participate, opening a door to social experiences that may not have been possible otherwise.
