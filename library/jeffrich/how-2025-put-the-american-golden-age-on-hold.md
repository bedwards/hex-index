This January I am recapping my world power world history tour, starting with the USA, and thinking deeply about the idea of the ‘civilization state.’

## Recap of the World Power World History Tour

In 2025 I took readers on a world tour of the history of five great powers or ‘civilization states’: in reverse order, Russia, Europe, India, China and the USA. Through the weekly essays, deep dives and book recommendations, we explored an astonishing range of stories: from the volcano in Guatemala that created the Viking Age; to the 1926 visit of ballerina Anna Pavlova to my home town where she danced the Dying Swan and drank billy tea.

Since many readers have joined after the start of the world tour, I wanted to bring this world tour to you in the convenient form of a compendium post, where you can find all the stories about each region.

Two questions lurked behind all my essays of the World Power World History Tour:

-   How do the best current histories help you to reimagine the stories of these states, nations and cultures?
    
-   How does the concept of ‘civilization state’ improve, or impair, your understanding of those histories?
    

The power elites in each of these five states claim the status of ‘civilization state’. The idea has caught on in much popular commentary on international affairs. But the history underpinning such claims is often weak. For example, the claim that China has a 5,000-year, continuous, largely peaceful history as a civilization-state does not mention the Mongols or the Qing, or the many rebellions, state breakdowns, border wars or civil conflicts. I will suggest an alternative approach to the concept of the ‘civilization state’ in the first of my Long Reads at the end of January.

## Major Theme of the USA World Tour

The world history tour began in January with the USA. In the same month, Donald Trump (or at least his speechwriters) declared his own reimagining of the history of the USA as a ‘civilization state’.

> Above all, my message to Americans today is that it is time for us to once again act with courage, vigor, and the vitality of history’s greatest civilization. . . . And we will pursue our manifest destiny into the stars, launching American astronauts to plant the Stars and Stripes on the planet Mars.
> 
> [USA President, The Inaugural Address 2025](https://www.whitehouse.gov/remarks/2025/01/the-inaugural-address/)

The future of the continent, the planet and the universe, he declared, on the basis of some tired historical tropes of the American Frontier, “is ours, and our golden age has just begun.”

By the end of the year, official Washington had pushed back the timeline of the golden age, while keeping the rose-tinted glasses of nationalist history. The *USA National Security Strategy 2025* declared, “We want an America that cherishes its past glories and its heroes, and that looks forward to a new golden age.” By November 2026, I suspect, the golden age will be another broken promise.

Readers of my USA world history tour would never have been fooled by Trump or any other American exceptionalist promises. We learned from the best historians about retelling the Hispanic history of the USA, the Broken Promised Land of Barack Obama, the USA’s healthcare failure, the frontier myth, oligarchy, nihilism and the failing global hegemony project.

But one key insight stood out for me as I looked back over the posts at the end of the year. The USA’s history is inseparable from Europe. It is a theme I insisted on against the crescendo of denigration of Europe by USA-affiliated officials, commentators and media identities. Europe is finished, they said. Its civilization will be erased. It suffers from a military-industrial censorship complex, which dared to challenge the abusive market powers of American tech oligarchs. Only America (and not América) - home of the brave, land of the free, hope of the New World, pioneers of the frontier - can offer hope of a new golden age for ‘Western civilization’.

This chorus of bitter, resentful and angry rhetoric belittling Europe has infected even otherwise sensible minds. There is a way to vaccinate yourself against this infection, which I discussed several times during the USA world history tour.

And that is the historical concept of “Greater Europe.”

The USA’s history is best understood as part of what historian John Darwin called “Greater Europe.” Greater Europe is Darwin’s term for the broadly related zone in which the idea of “the West” was invented. Russia, Europe, and North America formed this Greater Europe – a broadly cohesive political, economic and cultural zone.

This common European home (to adopt Gorbachev’s similar idea), however, was the scene of a troubled domestic drama. For two centuries Russia, Europe, and North America have been entangled in a toxic threesome.

Most discourse about America vs Europe simply does not get this, including the idea that ‘500 years of European colonialism’ is breaking down and that somehow the USA was separate from that. It too was a ‘European’ colonial power. It too, as Marlowe might have said in *Heart of Darkness*, was one of the “dark places of the Earth.”

America was and remains the Western Frontier of Greater Europe.

But that toxic threesome is now finally breaking up. The USA has acted too long like an abusive, violent partner in a *ménage à trois*. A lot of the dynamics we are seeing in international relations reflect how the trapped Russia and Europe want out, but struggle to find their way out without provoking America’s violent revenge. Russia has largely left, even if every now and then it is tempted to return. Europe is stuck or imprisoned, but will it plan its escape in 2026?

And can the USA develop respectful, reciprocal relationships with Europe, Russia, Latin America and the rest of the world - without the abusive partner’s controlling, violent behaviour? Unlikely in 2026. And longer term? What do you think?

## The Complete USA World History Tour

Below is your guide to the complete USA world history tour, comprising eighteen posts: essays, deep dives, bonus commentary and my interview with historian Don Watson on his *The Shortest History of the USA*.

Here are your links and highlighted passages from all eighteen posts.

> The time of monsters has arrived, and our historical imagination struggles to adapt. The farcical world historical personage of this time is Donald Trump. He is a symptom of this time of monsters. He is not its cause, nor its champion, and he has next to no control over the events he seeks to stage manage. His statements are impulsive outbursts of the American leadership mind, beset by morbid symptoms after the breakdown of the old world history, in which the USA dominates the world.

> The fall of American Primacy does not imply the collapse of American Power. Rather, it means that America no longer ranks at the top of all sources of power. It no longer has the capacity to pursue its stated goal of “full-spectrum dominance.” Indeed, the world is starting to question whether the USA dominates any source of power at all.

> The USA has a Hispanic Past and Hispanic Future. Indeed, the USA is a Latin American country. Moreover, Anglo-America was not formed as an independent republic, but in imitation of the Spanish Empire. These three statements are drawn from my history book recommendation this week: Felipe Fernández-Armesto, *[Our America: A Hispanic History of the United States](https://amzn.to/4jBNTX4)* (2015).

> Burr took the *machismo* of the *caudillo* two steps further. In 1804 he fought a duel with Alexander Hamilton, described by one historian as a “genius—the only one of the Founding Fathers fully entitled to that accolade.” The duel killed Hamilton and ruined Burr’s national political career. After the duel, Burr channelled his dark soul of *caudillo* towards Latin America. He became involved in an elaborate scheme, the “Burr Conspiracy,” to establish an independent nation spanning the southwestern United States and Mexico. Some claimed he sought to install himself as the King of [America Mexicana](https://jeffrich.substack.com/p/the-old-world-history-is-dying-and?r=azb1g). He was tried for treason in 1807, but acquitted. The macho *caudillo* has been part of the North American political tradition from the days of the Founding Fathers.

> One of the tough lessons I learned in my intellectual adventures in government was that the world is too perplexing for even the finest minds. Reality always trumps intellect. History always sidesteps philosophy. Consequently, even the minds that you admire the most still make mistakes. Great minds have blind spots where they fall in love with ideas and fail to sense reality. So it is with Hannah Arendt’s writing on Western history. She was an incurable Atlantic Romantic.

> Unhappy empires twist their stories, but not in conditions of their own making. The conditions conceal each uniquely unhappy way. There are always money problems. Debt, exploitation and luxury do their work. There is often military over-extension, and generals who relive borrowed glory. There is the usual human frailty. Wayward minds. Prodigal sons. The addictions of fame, power, drugs, and ideas. Grandiose ideas, like occupying Mars or renewing greatness by seizing territory or the Panama Canal, march their leaders into swamps. Sometimes, the climate has the final say.
> 
> But, unhappy empires are dangerous. So much depends today on knowing the unique unhappy way of the American empire.

> In these conditions, railroading the ‘hegemony project’ onto the world will steam power the USA over the cliff. Yet most foreign policy thinkers in America are trapped in the mental framework that there must be a hegemon. They fail to see the “radical novelty” of historical changes over the last thirty years.

> When someone you love does something evil, or wrongs you violently, you face a moral, psychological and intellectual dilemma. When the fist strikes your face, you tell yourself, ‘He is not the man that I loved.’ It is Dr Jekyll doing this violence. Mr Hyde, whom I love, is, on balance, a force for good in the world.
> 
> The wronged party cannot escape the abuse unless they admit that the violence of Dr Jekyll and the sweet words of Mr Hyde come from the same psyche. So too the world faces a problem today. The wrongs that America does in the world come from the same psyche that whispered sweet nothings in our ears for decades. The USA was our indispensable nation. Both these Americas overpower their captives with threats. The weak cannot survive in the jungle world, outside the star-spangled cage the bully has built since 1945. But the real truth is that the abuse will only end when the world breaks the spell of love of America.

> The degeneration of the soul of the deep state from Kissinger to Clinton to Blinken parallels the loss of collective ethos in the West, as argued by Emmanuel Todd in *La Défaite de l’Occident*. Secularisation has led the Protestant West to pass from active state, through zombie state, to zero state. The foreign policy apparatus of the US government, which Kissinger’s idealistic grandeur once energised, has similarly fallen. It has no depth anymore. It has degraded into, as Emmanuel Todd argues, a shallow state of a nihilistic oligarchy.

> Donald Trump’s speechwriters evoked both *Star Trek* - crossing space, the final frontier, all the way to Mars - and a classic historical essay on American character and the spirit of the frontier. Frederick Jackson Turner’s 1893 essay “The Frontier in American History” defined an American tradition of history writing. You ought to read it to understand its influence. That is why I have recorded an audiobook mini of the essay for this post. But you should also read more recent histories, so you do not fall for the rhetoric of the USA’s final frontier fantasy.

> The USA is not an exception to the Old World. It is the Western third of a ‘Greater Europe’, comprising Old Europe, Russia, and North America. This triangular relationship has dominated international politics for nearly two centuries. JD Vance’s and Donald Trump’s boorish treatment of this troubled *ménage à trois* can be best understood through the history of the invention of the West after 1830.

> Through the noise of American leaders attacking Europe, one pure signal can be heard. On average, Europeans live five years longer than USA citizens. And they \[USA citizens\] pay much more for their health care, endure more risk, and suffer worse rates of disease. All to fail on the fundamental measure of the good life, how long you live.
> 
> Why are the USA’s health outcomes the worst in the high-income world, and, given the wealth of this superpower, the worst in the world?

> It may be surprising that a book about the geopolitics of the war between Russia, Ukraine and NATO discusses America’s health system. But health and America’s failure to build a health system are central to Todd’s argument that the West is experiencing a self-inflicted defeat. Defeat is demonstrated on the battlefield but is primarily driven by a social crisis in the centre of the Western empire, the USA. The social crisis includes worsening health. In Todd’s brutal phrase, Americans spend more to die more. Yet the ‘world’s greatest democracy’ fails to fix the problem.

> Assessments of Barack Obama are as yet passionate and partisan. They range from the “worst president in history” to hagiographies of a political saint. There is a reason for these polarised views; they are all animated by the political mythology that the “American Experiment” will be redeemed by the Prince who was promised.

> The failure of Barack Obama’s Presidency is not his fault alone. He failed because US state institutions were incapable of delivering aims. Obama’s impotent Presidency and the USA’s incapable government exposed the paradox of American power. The indispensable divided superpower of the world cannot govern itself.

> *The Great Gatsby* expressed rival modernist visions. While European (including Russian) modernism explored the limits of high culture with depth, shock and radical questioning, American modernism oriented itself around consumer spectacle, popular fashion and material display. Thomas Mann wrote *The Magic Mountain*, in which, from memory, the only American characters are Mexican. Wallace Stevens, who published *Harmonium* in 1923, wrote “The Emperor of Ice Cream.”

> But more profoundly, American commercial power has overwhelmed its culture. In the 1920s, American culture rose above shallow materialism to the tragedy of *[The Great Gatsby](https://jeffrich.substack.com/p/the-great-gatsby-and-american-power?r=azb1g)*, while dreaming of expatriate life in Paris. In the 1950s and 1960s, [Hannah Arendt dreamt in her essays on the American Revolutionary Tradition](https://jeffrich.substack.com/p/atlantic-romantics-how-america-forgot?r=azb1g) that the USA was the Last Best Hope for Western Civilization. She was a representative voice of an astonishing few generations of émigré intellectuals converted from European sophisticates to American supremacists.
> 
> But today too much power, too much easy money, and too much internet have overwhelmed American culture. It has become a crude mash-up of Shallow Hal, Elon Musk’s tweets, Niall Ferguson’s bombast on the West, and the Kardashians on repeat.
