## The Spectre Haunting Europe

On 15 December 2025, Britain’s newly minted spy chief, Blaise Metreweli, i[nvoked the spectre of Russian invasion](https://www.gov.uk/government/speeches/speech-by-blaise-metreweli-chief-of-sis-15-december-2025). Britain, NATO, Ukraine and the world, she said, were harassed by “the menace of an aggressive, expansionist and revisionist Russia.” “Putin’s Russia” was the spider at the centre of an “interlocking web of security challenges – military, technological, social, ethical even.” This spectre haunting NATO’s strategists threatened more than physical invasion by Russian tanks.

> Our world is more dangerous and contested now than it has been for decades. Conflict is evolving and trust eroding, just as new technologies spur both competition and dependence. We are being contested from sea to space, from the battlefield to the boardroom. And even our brains, as disinformation manipulates our understanding of each other and ourselves. . . . We are now operating in a space between peace and war.
> 
> [Speech by Blaise Metreweli](https://www.gov.uk/government/speeches/speech-by-blaise-metreweli-chief-of-sis-15-december-2025), Chief of SIS, 15 December 2025

Her artfully articulated fears were more than lurid propaganda. They were the chiselled certainties of the NATO doctrine of permanent attrition war - [as brilliantly analysed by Nel Bonilla](https://open.substack.com/pub/themindness/p/weaponizing-time-elite-anxiety-and?r=azb1g&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web) - not just in physical reality, but the psychological warfare of our minds.

> The export of chaos is a feature not a bug in this Russian approach to international engagement; and we should be ready for this to continue until Putin is forced to change his calculus.
> 
> [Speech by Blaise Metreweli](https://www.gov.uk/government/speeches/speech-by-blaise-metreweli-chief-of-sis-15-december-2025), Chief of SIS, 15 December 2025

Does their history make Russians seek peace or war?

The Black Legend of Russian History presents Russia as the treacherous, war-mongering Hun, always poised to invade Europe. Similar panics have spread across Europe as unidentified toy drones in open fields have been whipped up into hysteria, justifying vast new military expenditures on a drone army in Europe. The fears are backed by bizarrely wrong historical claims by Foreign Minister of the European Union, Kaja Kallas, that over the last century Russia has invaded many countries and been attacked by none.

Such phantoms of Russia the Invader are not grounded in present realities or historical fact. And Mark B. Smith, *The Russia Anxiety*, Chapter 8 Does History Make Russians Seek Peace or War? makes this clear.

On balance, Russians - leaders, diplomats, soldiers, writers and people - seek peace, not war. At times it has been driven to war, including in the Napoleonic Wars, and French Europe’s invasion of Russia, the events Tolstoy wrote about in *War and Peace*. It has acted like a great power: badly at times. But less aggressively than its slanderers in Britain and North America say.

> Risk-averse, defensive, prickly, fearful, Russia has had to deal with the reality of overstretch brought on by the world’s longest borders. It has faced uncertainty and danger, and has been prone to reimagine plausible threats as existential crises.”
> 
> Smith, *Russia Anxiety*, p. 272

Nevertheless Russia has responded predictably, within diplomatic norms of the day, and less aggressively than its rivals. Contrary to Madame Kallas, Smith wrote, “Russian invasions and foreign occupation have been the exception rather than the rule and can’t be explained as an historical tendency.” (Smith, *Russia Anxiety*, p. 272)

Jacques Baud dispelled the spectre of Russia as Invader. He must be punished.

He undermined the fear campaign of the Russia Anxiety, by questioning one of its central historical assumptions: that Russia prefers war to peace; that Russia is, as Margaret Thatcher said “bent on world dominance.” In the 1920s, the spectre was World Revolution. In the Cold War, the ghosts of Totalitarianism haunted the West. Today, not real individual Russians but the menacing spider of “Putin’s Russia” undermines Western civilization and exports chaos.

The invader obsession pays no heed to practical realities. It is as much a fevered projection as the British invention of the ‘Great Game’ of the nineteenth century, an obsession of British imperialists that was not played by Russian officials. That old British madness was fear, anxiety and resentment of the challenge of other powers. The British alarmed themselves that Russia would invade India, and they fought the Afghan wars in a disastrous paroxysm of their own imagination. The fear paid no attention to practical realities, such as the need for Russia to march troops by foot from Moscow over the Himalayas.

Fears of Russian invasion are the hypomanic phase of the mood swings of the Russia Anxiety syndrome. They exaggerate risks of invasion and distort assessments of Russian diplomatic signals. They entrench bad foreign policy in deeply dug distorted history. They make Europe and the world less secure. In fact, Smith writes,

> Europe is much more secure when Russia is at the centre of international affairs, an indispensable partner, given its due. The most spectacular examples of that were during the Napoleonic Wars and the Second World War.
> 
> Smith, *Russia Anxiety*

Alexandra Kollontai (1872 – 1952) was a Russian revolutionary, feminist, politician, diplomat and Marxist theoretician. She was a leading woman in the Bolshevik party. Her appointment as People’s Commissar for Welfare after the October 1917 Revolution made her the first woman in history to be a cabinet minister. She was one of the first women to be appointed as a diplomatic representative of a modern state, and the first to be promoted to the rank of ambassador. Her role as the Soviet Ambassador to Sweden from 1930 was crucial in the interwar years.

Mark B. Smith observes that the talented Kollontai could be “technocrat, revolutionary and old-style *intelligent*.” Her talents, temper and character defied the stereotypes of anxious Western diplomats. Her life story dispels the spectre of Russian monstrosity. She was no Soviet caricature or Russian stereotype. She combined qualities that were admired in the West and made her indispensable to Soviet diplomacy.

> This was the combination of values which in other contexts could be mutually exclusive - Bolshevism tempered by warmth, revolutionism alongside humanity - that she brought to the conduct of Soviet foreign policy.
> 
> Smith, *Russia Anxiety,* p. 276

Kollontai played a key role before and during the Winter War between Finland and the USSR, discussed in Michael Jabara Carley’s third book on Soviet diplomacy between 1930 and 1941, *Stalin’s Great Game, War and Neutrality, 1939-1941.* Carley quotes a diplomatic report by Kollontai on the difficulties for then neutral Sweden in the tussle between Britain, France and the Soviet Union. Britain and France had still not allied with the Soviet Union against Germany. They saw the war in Finland as an opportunity to weaken the USSR strategically, if I may use the phrases of contemporary NATO strategists. In the report Carley cites, there is an uncanny echo of current day Western NATO strategy that urged Ukraine for over a decade to abandon its former constitutional neutrality in the long war of the West against Russia. She complained of how the press in Norway, “inspired by London and Paris, is full of malicious slanderous and false information about us and has a tone of anti-Soviet propaganda.” She noted how the French and British preyed upon the Swedes and urged them to be “participants in the war for the glory of the imperialists.” Then she noted how the French press was most ardent, cynical and abusive of the fate of poor neutral Sweden. They used rhetoric that is replayed today in the US Congress, NATO headquarters and Blaise Metreweli’s information operations.

> Especially trying is the French press, which calls Sweden its “ally” and, according to an ironic remark of the Swedes themselves, offers Sweden to protect the interests of the Entente on the fields of Finland “to the last Swede.”
> 
> Kollontai quoted Carley, *Stalin’s Great Game,* p. 68

## How Russia sought peace before and after the Napoleonic Wars

Smith’s second spectacular example of how Russia has been an “indispensable partner” of European peace and security was the Napoleonic Wars.

The Napoleonic Wars gave rise to the greatest nineteenth-century historical novel, *War and Peace* by Lev Tolstoy. Napoleon’s France - surely an ‘aggressive, expansionist and revisionist’ state - and its empire across Europe invaded Russia in 1812, not the other way around. Russia did not seek war. Before 1812, it sought peace while preparing for defensive war. The defensiveness of the war provoked controversy within Russia at the time. Some thought Russia pursued a policy of dishonourable retreat and excessive caution. The Battle of Borodino and the Retreat from Moscow were central events in Russian history that resonate today, and, of course, are relived imaginatively by every reader of Tolstoy’s novel. They resonate in contemporary Russian literary, popular and strategic culture too, including in Vladimir Putin’s Valdai Forum speech of 2025 which I discussed in this post.

But Tolstoy may have got the history wrong, even as he perceived deeper emotional truths. Certainly most Anglophone history since 1815 has seriously distorted events. Waterloo was a punctuation point, not the main story. Wellington was not the decisive general. Those roles were played by a galaxy of multinational leaders in the Russian empire. Winter did not defeat Napoleon. Russian strategy, soldiers and statecraft did. Russia did not stop at Minsk in 1812. They marched to Paris in 1814. They liberated Europe before going on to play a decisive role in establishing the peace of the Congress of Vienna that provided reasonable security and stability for a century to Europe and North America, as part of Greater Europe.

This story is narrated brilliantly in my history book recommendation, Dominic Lieven, *Russia Against Napoleon: the Battle for Europe, 1807 to 1814.* Especially today, amidst our pandemic of Russian anxiety, this book is essential reading because Lieven shows beyond a doubt, from multiple archival sources, how British, French, German and American historians, and even Clausewitz and Tolstoy, misread this war.

The final paragraph of Lieven’s history refers to Tsar Alexander I, who led Russian strategy and who was referenced at the start of Fyodor Lukyanov and Vladimir Putin’s conversation at Valdai 2025. Its lesson is more powerful today than in 2009 when Lieven published *Russia Against Napoleon.*

> The basic point was that Alexander was convinced that Russian and European Security depended on each other. That is still true today. But perhaps there is some inspiration to be drawn from a story in which the Russian army advancing across Europe in 1813-14 was in most places seen as an army of liberation, whose victories meant escape from Napoleon’s exactions, an end to an era of constant war, and the restoration of European trade and prosperity.
> 
> Lieven, *Russia Against Napoleon, p. 528*

Maybe it is time for British spooks and American geostrategists to abandon their fever dreams of Russian hatred and dispel their conjured spectres of Russian invaders. Lieven, Smith, Carley and dozens of historians, cured of the Russian anxiety, show that Russia has historically preferred peace to war. The dreadful ruin of Ukraine is no exception. It was not unprovoked. It “followed failures of international and domestic politics that were partly given shape by the Russia Anxiety” (Smith, *Russia Anxiety*, p. 292). Those failures have morphed into a wicked shape, especially in Britain and in the strategies of Ms Metreweli.

But Smith also points out that the USA and its crusading mentality bear unique historical responsibility for unleashing the haunted house of Russophobia, and presenting through its propagandists, like Timothy Snyder and Ms Metreweli, a black legend of Russia, the warmongering, sabre-rattling, life-hating invader. Russia’s history of warfare has been normal. At the most crucial times, despite resistance by Western leaders, Russia has been on the same side as the West. The big exception is the Cold War, the era of American imperial expansion after 1945.

> Recent and important, it \[the Cold War\] is understandably a dominant presence in the popular memory of international affairs. Yet this distorts the wider pattern of history, making Russia seem exceptionally aggressive and a default adversary of the United States.
> 
> Smith, *Russia Anxiet*y, p. 307

The spectre of Russian invasion need no longer haunt the political imagination of the American networked empire. There are many counter spells to this conjuration of intelligence agencies: Lieven’s *Russia Against Napoleon*, Michael Jabara Carley’s trilogy, Smith’s *Russia Anxiety*. Maybe Putin’s invocation of Borodino might be a lesson to Europe: that Russia is its neighbour, its indispensable partner in security, and a better long-term friend than the new Napoleonic conqueror of Europe, the truly “aggressive, expansionist and revisionist” state, the USA.

But to come to that realization, Europe’s leaders may need to accept defeat in Ukraine and then stop listening to the NATO doctrines spewed by Britain’s spy chief. Instead, they might read some better history than that told by the agents of the Black Legend of Russian history.
