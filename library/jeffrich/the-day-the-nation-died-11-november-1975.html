Today marks the 50th anniversary of the dismissal of the Whitlam Government (Labor or progressive) in Australia. Does this event still matter amid the churning of the world in 2025?

On 11 November 1975 Prime Minister Gough Whitlam was sacked by the Governor General using the ‘reserve powers’ of the Constitution, reserved for the monarch’s representative. Yes, for non-antipodean readers, the Queen of England was still the Queen of Australia. In response, Whitlam defiantly stood on the steps of Parliament House and after the Governor-General’s secretary completed his formal proclamation with the salutation, “God Save the Queen,” Whitlam, the barrister, responded with the words, legendary in Australian political history,

Well may you say ‘God save the Queen’ because nothing will save the Governor General.

Gough Whitlam, 11 November 1975

The history of Australia’s greatest constitutional crisis remains passionately debated, although after 50 years clear memories are clouded by political mythologies. Was it a coup arranged by the CIA or the Palace? Did it expose irredeemable flaws in Australian democracy? Who was to blame? Whose villainy was blackest? Has Australia ever since enjoyed full national sovereignty within the American alliance?

And why, despite Gough’s plea to the nation to “maintain the rage,” did the Australian electorate validate the decision by the despised Governor General, Sir John Kerr, his dismissal of the legendary reforming Labor Government, not once, but twice. In both the 1975 and 1977 elections Whitlam and the Labor Party were defeated in landslides. In 1975 the ALP lost 30 seats and suffered a 7.4 per cent swing in votes. New Liberal-National (conservative) Prime Minister Malcom Fraser enjoyed a 91 to 36 majority in the new Parliament. In 1977 the majority changed marginally to 86 to 38.

Democracy threw out another inconvenient truth. In spite of Gough’s ominous warning, or because of it, the electorate chose to save the Governor-General’s judgement. Nothing however would save his soul, which would dissolve into jeering crowds, bitter recriminations and daily alcoholic binges.

These tragic events, however, formed the political characters of several generations of elites in politics, bureaucracy, law, universities and media. I have met and worked with many of them. Some dedicated themselves to constitutional reform, including forlorn attempts to make Australia a republic. Some committed to academic and journalistic inquiry about what happened. They ferreted out incriminating documents from the Palace and the Whitehouse. The generation of Australian journalists who covered 1975, like Paul Kelly or Michelle Grattan, have clung on to their mastheads long after they stopped being interesting. And for most people of voting age in 1975, these events shaped political loyalties and nurtured political mythologies. In a recent Pearlcast, editor of Pearls and Irritations, Catriona Jackson said everyone over 50 could remember - like when JFK was shot - where they were that day.

Well, I was twelve on that day and I cannot recall. Many events and social processes since 1975 strike me as more formative of my political experience and Australian national character, if such a thing exists. It made me think: has Australia’s 1975 generation fixated on the constitutional crisis for too long? Is the Australian political imagination stuck in a historical trauma which it would do better letting go?

I ask these questions with a generous spirit, knowing how interwoven stories of 1975 are with the political experience of most Australians. There are many versions of the stories of what exactly happened on 11 November and the preceding three years (Whitlam was elected in 1972) in the high politics between the elected government, the determined opposition, the judiciary, bureaucracy, the Queen’s representative, spies of allies, and the local power elites. I have never really sought to untangle the conflicting accounts in memoirs, histories, studies, theories and personal anecdotes.

Earlier this year, however, some talk on the internet of a coup in both 1975 and 2010 (against Kevin Rudd) led me to author a video on the topic.

It was a reaction in part to the tendency of many American based commentators to see every political event around the world as being manipulated by omnicompetent deep state operatives from the USA.

Since making the video I have thought about the 1975 events some more and considered the strong views of many readers on the topic. I think the argument that the USA regime changed Australia in 2010 is nonsense; but the case for 1975 is less clear cut.

However, I also wanted to connect Australia’s great constitutional crisis to some broader historical changes. Three historical formations converged in the mid 1970s and played out in political, social and cultural crises that simmered beneath the constitutional malfeasance of Sir John Kerr and the over-certain grandeur of Gough Whitlam.

First, the 1970s saw a phase-shift in the processes of decolonization that had been occurring across the world during the twentieth century, including in Australia and its region. In 1975 Australia decolonised Papua New Guinea and Portugal decolonised East Timor. A tide of American neo-colonialism surged through its “pointillistic empire of bases” (as Nel Bonilla says), financial institutions and culture industries. This tide of recolonization would drown the Whitlam Government and the 1960s/1970s surge of Australian nationalism that went underwater in search of a republic. It changed the meaning of “national sovereignty” worldwide.

Second, in the 1970s social changes accelerated due to the emergence of new modes of identity (“liquid modernity”), shifts in demography (gender, family, ageing and migration), new forms of social stratification linked to higher education, and global media and communication networks that made nationalism a dirty word. I have described some of these changes in my recent piece on 1968 and writing on Emmanuel Todd.

Third, profound changes in political ideas and institutions occurred across the world in the 1970s. The relationship between market and state was renegotiated across the West and East. The role of media and cultural elites in the public discourse transformed, with politics becoming more of a performed spectacle. “Neoliberalism” formed and marched through the institutions of every state. The Whitlam Government was an early victim of these changes. In 1972 Whitlam was elected on the slogan of “It’s Time.” Many remember it as a visionary reformist government. But others see it as stuck in a different past. Rex Connor’s scheme to nationalise the resource assets of one of the liberal capitalist world’s largest miners proved to be a relic of more dirigiste times. In many ways, Whitlam was the last government in Australia to aim for democratic socialism. Compare his difficulties with socialist Francois Mitterand in France 1981. By then the global reconfiguration of market and society strangled plans of socialism in the crib. By 1984, when Bob Hawke was elected Prime Minister of Australia, its key ministers mocked the incompetence of Whitlam, aimed to prove Labor was a natural party of government, and allied with business and corporate union interests to promote market reforms.

These three global historical changes reframe the story of 1975 to me. The processes of decolonization - the rebalancing of powers between a dominant and dependent state - affected Australia too. The social and cultural transformations of the 1970s were more enduring than the constitutional process missteps, and had a deeper legacy. The political reconfiguration of state, market and society showed Whitlam to be not a grand visionary who was tragically cut down, but a man of the past comically misjudging character, power and events.

I mean comically in its strong and not mocking sense (for any Australian readers who may feel that might be a step too far). Whitlam has become in Australian left nationalist circles the father of the nation, like Garibaldi or Washington, and the saint of progressive tradition. For example, Australian historian and republican Mark McKenna writes in his new history:

“Australia only began to feel like an independent country after the election of Whitlam’s Labor government on 2 December 1972.”

Mark McKenna, The Shortest History of Australia (2025)

But even fathers sometimes need their grand balloons deflated. And maybe there are more inspiring political ideas for the 21st century than the clapped-out idea of an ‘independent nation’? It is an open question.

Let me offer seven other brief asides about 1975 before closing with some reflections about how we might free ourselves - in Australia or elsewhere - from political mythologies of newly birthed political nations.

One: Read John Menadue’s Eyewitness Account. If you can read just one or two short pieces on 1975 then read John Menadue’s eyewitness account of the events. In 1975 John Menadue was the Head of the Department of Prime Minister and Cabinet. In his article written for the 50th anniversary, he asks what was the role of the CIA, MI6 and the Austral Americans? His observation after a lifetime of reflection and inquiry:

The revelation that came latest to me was the role of the CIA and MI6.

John Menadue 2025

Two: Australia’s Original Oligarch Rupert Murdoch. John Menadue also spoke of his memories of dealing with Rupert Murdoch before, during and after the 1975 political crisis. Menadue shares memories that imply Murdoch may have had foreknowledge of the removal of Whitlam. Curiously, one year after the Dismissal, in 1976, Rupert Murdoch became a major media player in the USA by purchasing the New York Post. American and global media has never been the same. Perhaps Rupert earned his stars and stripes in Canberra in 1975? In any case, his story exhibits the paradoxical power of provincial talent in all vast multinational empires.

Three: Suicide by Coup? There are conflicting accounts of whether 1975 was a coup or regime change operation. I am not going to try to resolve them. Read John Menadue. Listen to Brian Toohey. Read James Curran. Do not rely on accounts by former spooks or big-talking Yanks alone. There is no doubt key people in the USA and British Governments wanted Whitlam gone. But did they pull the trigger? Were there more than enough local political assassins? Who organised the conspiracy? Was it a conspiracy or a very delicate matter requiring the utmost discretion? And why was Whitlam oddly naive and strangely passive at certain key moments? His government was mired in scandal and mistakes, despite all its achievements. Sometimes his rhetoric was grander than his actions. Some doubt his resolve to suspend the Pine Gap lease. Did the Americans really need to cross that line when they could read the polls forecasting that the voters might turn viciously against Whitlam in an election? Did Whitlam commit political suicide by coup and create a national legend?

Four: Illusions of Proper Constitutional Processes. Jenny Hocking and other investigators of the ‘revolt of the establishment’ in 1975 are still fired by the rage that Kerr, Fraser and others did not follow ‘proper constitutional processes’. But have they bought into a political fiction of liberal democracy? When a society and polity are in such a crisis as the 1970s is a ‘realistic analysis’ to expect power elites to fight by Queensberry’s rules?

Five: Nothing Will Save The Whitlam Legend. Whitlam’s famous words - ‘nothing will save the Governor General’ - were more thunderously theatrical than intelligently insightful. The electorate delivered a more severe admonishment to Whitlam in 1975 and 1977. He was left to rework the legend of A Certain Grandeur and his great reforming three years in government. But perhaps his most enduring legacy was the Hawke-Keating Government, whose members would joke among themselves about who was the most incompetent Minister in the Whitlam Government. That great reforming government of third way neoliberalism did everything it could to distance itself from the heroic ‘crash or crash through shambles’ of Labor’s fallen heroes of the 1970s. After 50 years of life experience with the Whitlam legend, perhaps this commemoration can end the fantasies that modern political parties can deliver a reform vision for the nation.

Six: Australia’s Aborted Decolonisation. The Whitlam Government coincided with a brief flowering of cultural nationalism. Patrick White won the Nobel Prize. The film industry took off and progressed from Barry McKenzie films to Picnic at Hanging Rock. But this period of cultural decolonisation - between the London-fawning cultural cringe before the 1960s and the Hollywood-seeking celebrity culture industries from the 1980s - was ever so brief. It is occasionally resurrected in arts policy visions and nostalgic historical stories. But the internet changed the platforms for culture. Cultural nationalism began to look like the Jindyworobak nationalism of the 1930s and 1940s. The three big historical changes I described churned the new multi-civilizational world beyond the dreams of late twentieth century radical nationalists. I discussed these themes in this post, originally published in John Menadue’s Pearls and Irritations.

Seven: A World without Sovereigns. Finally, the big changes I have sketched transformed the nature of national sovereignty. Perhaps Australia should worry less about the impaired sovereignty created by its constitution and alliances? A world of nations was dreamt in 1945. A world of undeclared empires, hegemons, unipolar powers, civilization-states, multinational organisations, global citizenship, and networked transnational identities took hold. Australian sovereignty died in 1975; or maybe it was stillborn in 1945. In 2025 is even the hyper-power the USA sovereign over its own destiny? Have the historical processes of exchange across borders, which we know as globalization, gone so far as now to take us into a world without sovereign nations? 1975 did not scar this Australian exile at home. It introduced me to a global ‘new normal’.

You can tell history in many ways: tragedy, epic, satire, and comedy. It may be time to heal the wounds of Australian political culture inflicted by the Dismissal with a little comic history. It’s time to lighten the emotional load of 1975 and see the human comedy, in its deepest sense, in the events. Our fates since have been shaped by some other deeper forces. We may not any longer need to nurse our wounds. But we should always look back and reflect on the many stories that can be told about the way Australia’s and the world’s ideas of nations died.
