**That question poses three dilemmas**:

-   **How to negotiate a peaceful resolution of the hostage situation?**
    
-   **How can the UN constrain, punish and rehabilitate the kidnapper, the USA?**

## **The First Dilemma: How to Negotiate a Peaceful Resolution of the Hostage Situation**

The peoples of Latin America, and the world, face a dilemma common to every kidnap or hostage situation. Do you give into the ransom demands? How do you prevent the kidnappers killing the hostages? How do you negotiate with the outlaw to secure the safe release of the hostages? How do you capture the kidnappers and ensure they face justice?

In the USA, this situation might be resolved by shooting first and avoiding questions later. The local sheriff might then bluster at a press conference about what happens when you FAFO, just as USA Secretary of War did at the press conference on the attack on Venezuela.

But in the society of nations, beyond that rogue state, public authorities aim to resolve hostage situations peacefully, tactfully, and in accordance with the law. It does not always work out without further violence. Sometimes a police shooter has to take the kidnappers out - for the safety of the hostages, the community, and, in this case, the world.

It is too early to know how the Venezuela situation will be resolved. The USA is proceeding with its show trial of the hostages in New York, while claiming ransom payment over Venezuelan oil, resources and state institutions. Claims by USA officials to be “running Venezuela” appear inflated and designed for another “mission accomplished” press conference of the US American political reality TV show. They seem disconnected from on-the-ground realities, the mood of Venezuelans, and global opinion. At the UN Security Council on 5 January, China and multiple American states called on the USA to return Maduro and his wife immediately. When Maduro appeared in the US kangaroo court he stated he was a political prisoner of war who was owed protection under international law. It may be that the trial will end in further embarrassment of the US justice system, and that the USA may be forced to negotiate the return of Maduro. Operation ‘Absolute Resolve’ might yet fail just as it did when the USA attempted to remove Chavez in 2002.

The one thing that is certain is that although the USA might excel at Jack Ryan style special operations, the USA sucks at the basic functions of government and diplomacy. The stunt has been staged, with an uncanny resemblance to the scenario presented in season two of *Tom Clancy’s Jack Ryan* (2019), but what now? There are no answers to that question. As ever with US interventions, after the conspiracy comes the cock-up.

### **Bringing the USA Under the Control of Law**

As a result, having secured the hostage and contained the violent kidnapper, the UN should bring the USA under the control of the law. It should unwind the chain of the USA’s criminal actions against Venezuela.

On 5 January 2026, Professor Jeffrey Sachs proposed to the UN Security Council that the UN should demand:

1.  The United States shall immediately cease and desist from all explicit and implicit threats or use of force against Venezuela.
    
2.  The United States shall terminate its naval quarantine and all related coercive military measures undertaken in the absence of authorization by the Security Council.
    
3.  The United States shall immediately withdraw its military forces from within and along the perimeter of Venezuela, including intelligence, naval, air, and other forward-deployed assets positioned for coercive purposes.
    
4.  Venezuela shall adhere to the UN Charter and to the human rights protected in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.
    
5.  The Secretary-General shall immediately appoint a Special Envoy, mandated to engage relevant Venezuelan and international stakeholders and to report back to the Security Council within fourteen days with recommendations consistent with the Charter of the United Nations, and the Security Council shall remain urgently seized of this matter.
    
6.  All Member States shall refrain from unilateral threats, coercive measures, or armed actions undertaken outside the authority of the Security Council, in strict conformity with the Charter.
    

These actions would represent the equivalent of bringing the criminal kidnapper into custody and returning the victims to safety and security in the community. Once the criminal is in custody, the prosecution, trial and punishment must begin. That is the dilemma the world’s leaders, diplomats and citizens face in the months ahead. How will the world punish *and* *rehabilitate* the USA and restore the rule of law to the world?

To respond to that question requires action by the United Nations.

## **The Second Dilemma: How Can the United Nations Constrain, Punish and Rehabilitate the USA?**

The world faces a fundamental question about how the United Nations and other international institutions should act given the USA’s persistent breach of the UN charter and its *de facto* secession from the international order. How can the UN reassert its authority over a criminal, defiant USA?

### **The USA’s Defiance of the UN Charter**

In its current mood, the USA thumbs its nose like a bandit *caudillo* at the UN, and any constraints of membership of international society. This mood has driven it to this latest gross breach of the UN Charter.

This mood of defiance has been building for some time. To be frank, USA elites have treated the UN with undue contempt for decades. But over the last year, the defiance of the bandit *caudillo* has spiralled out of control.

At the UN General Assembly in September, amid self-obsessed complaints about momentary malfunctions of a lift and teleprompter and long ago rejected tenders for the renovation of the building, USA President Trump told world leaders that “Not only is the U.N. not solving the problems it should, too often, it’s actually creating new problems for us to solve.” *Every accusation is a confession*. He repeatedly abused the UN in a widely mocked performance unworthy of the international stage.

At the end of 2025, defiance turned into a declaration that the USA is both above and beyond international law and custom. The 2025 US *National Security Strategy (NSS)* declared the USA in favour of a doctrine of the “primacy of nations.”

> “We stand for the sovereign rights of nations, against the sovereignty-sapping incursions of the most intrusive transnational organizations, and for reforming those institutions so that they assist rather than hinder individual sovereignty and further American interests.” (*NSS*, p. 9)

Yes, you read that right. The purpose of the UN and international law, in the view of the US American elite, is to further US American interests.

The *NSS* declared a principle of ‘sovereignty and respect’ in which the USA accepted no constraints on the actions of the USA government and its citizens worldwide. “The United States will unapologetically protect our own sovereignty. This includes preventing its erosion by transnational and international organizations.” “The United States,” the *NSS* declared, “will chart our own course in the world and determine our own destiny, free of outside interference.”

This is a credo of exceptionalism and supremacy. It is the USA *über alles*. The Trump administration screams these doctrines like a resentful *desperado* state. But do not be deluded that there is a better USA waiting in the wings. The same arrogant contempt has been expressed routinely by USA officials, public intellectuals and citizens for decades. It is accompanied by a [systematic campaign of starving the UN of resources](https://open.substack.com/pub/thetransnational/p/the-uns-financing-crisis-who-pays?r=azb1g&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web). The USA is the worst laggard in paying its dues. But shamefully, as Jan Oberg has exposed, China and Russia follow suit despite high-minded rhetoric. Even more damaging is the rushing stream of contempt expressed across the US American commentariat, who rarely have any recent diplomatic or government experience. Mainstream and alt media alike: the UN is America’s favourite whipping boy, closely followed by the European Union.

The Venezuelan kidnapping operation could be a last straw that breaks the back of the UN. But the UN is too important for the citizens of the world, to permit the actual and rhetorical violence of the USA to break the only legitimate, comprehensive and established institution of global governance. Rather, the real offender should be put in the dock.

### **Venezuela Is Not the First Violation**

Let us be clear that the Venezuela kidnapping operation is an outrageous breach of the UN Charter. But it is not the first such breach of the Charter in the USA’s dealings with Venezuela. Nor is it the new idiosyncratic policy of Donald Trump or the passion project of Marco Rubio.

The USA government and its public intellectuals, political class, media celebrities, entertainment industry, military, and ‘intelligence community’ have conducted a 25-year multi-dimensional war against Venezuela, including regime change attempts. It is standard US practice. You see that in the pathetic response to Maduro’s kidnapping by so many Democrat American officials and commentators across the spectrum t.

It is not even an unprecedented abduction. The USA attempted the same with Hugo Chavez in 2002. President George H.W. Bush kidnapped Manuel Noriega and killed many civilians of Panama in 1989. That operation was condemned by the United Nations General Assembly and the European Parliament as a violation of international law. Indeed, Mikhail Gorbachev raised the issue in his *Memoirs*, recalling a conversation with President Bush the Elder, which also included discussion of the USA’s never-ending blockade of Cuba.

> “I asked the President about the American relations with such countries as Panama, Colombia and, lately, the Philippines. People in the Soviet Union wondered why the independence of these nations did not seem to stop the United States from interfering in their affairs. What right had Washington to lay down the law, bring in the verdict and execute the sentence? Was the ‘Brezhnev doctrine’ about to be replaced by the ‘Bush doctrine’?”
> 
> Gorbachev, *Memoirs* (1995), p. 662

Today serious people utter nonsense about the Donroe Doctrine. How we have fallen?

Regime change operations are in direct breach of Article 2(4) of the United Nations Charter, which prohibits the threat or use of force against the territorial integrity or political independence of any state. The long history of such USA outrages is well known. Lindsey O’Rourke’s *Regime Change* (2018) documents 70 attempted US regime-change operations between 1947 and 1989 alone. Since 1989, major United States regime-change operations undertaken without authorization by the Security Council have included Iraq (2003), Libya (2011), Syria (from 2011), Honduras (2009), Ukraine (2014), and Venezuela (from 2002 onward). A day does not go by when you cannot watch such criminality glamorised and reinvented as magical omnipotence by Hollywood, Netflix and all the willing scriptwriters at a movie cinema or streaming service near you.

In the post-1945 world, regime change operations are the exorbitant privilege of the USA. The USA may have helped to draft the UN Charter. But at the same time, no power in the world has done more to subvert the Charter, the UN and international law. Since 1945, it has acted like an undeclared empire that will have its way with the world despite all moral constraint; or, in the euphemisms of Trump’s National Security Strategy, it will “chart our own course in the world and determine our own destiny, free of outside interference.” But the USA claimed this exorbitant privilege long before post-1945 CIA and ‘deep state.’

### **Imperial Precedents and the Monroe Doctrine**

Since 1776, the USA has proclaimed itself as a democratic republic, opposed to the abusive practices of ancient European empires. But, in truth, it has been the worst of the Greater European empires, sworn to a credo of conquest, known as the Monroe Doctrine of 1823.

With similar hypocrisy, in the nineteenth century, the British Empire proclaimed itself a liberal empire, while it kidnapped and humiliated in colonial prisons the heads of state, including the last Mughal Emperor of Delhi and several African princes. Its practices of imperial rape, pillage, dispossession, kidnapping and mass murder are well established.

The USA though still invokes the Monroe Doctrine. US Americans are taught that the Monroe Doctrine is the foundation of US foreign policy. It protected weak young nations from abusive European empires, defended democracy against Old World tyranny. In 1823, President James Monroe proclaimed, "The American continents are henceforth not to be considered as subjects for future colonization by any European powers."

Even today well-meaning critics of American policy - like Jeffrey Sachs and [Larry Johnson](https://open.substack.com/pub/larrycjohnson/p/donald-trump-and-most-americans-do?r=azb1g&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web) - misinterpret the intent of these words. They find in the ambiguous statement that European powers should not interfere in American affairs, an early articulation of a “policy statement that every US president in the 20th and 21st Century has ignored… No interference in the internal affairs of other countries.” Both Johnson and Sachs seek to redeem the doctrine through this principle. Johnson writes:

> The Monroe Doctrine was intended to combat foreign interference by imperial powers . . . The US has bastardized that doctrine and now uses it as an excuse to feed our own imperial ambitions. Venezuela is just the latest casualty.
> 
> Johnson, *[Donald Trump, and Most Americans, Do Not Understand the Monroe Doctrine](https://larrycjohnson.substack.com/p/donald-trump-and-most-americans-do?r=azb1g&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&triedRedirect=true),* 2026

The question these writers do not ask is: why did the USA, in 1823, demand non-interference by European powers on the American continent?

They needed a rationale for expansion and conquest of all the other states that shared the “Western hemisphere,” especially in the context of the breakdown after Napoleon of the Spanish American empire. But the other immediate problem was the neighbouring and co-resident states of the native nations who blocked US colonial expansion south and westward.

Monroe and most US colonists wanted their land and did not want to pay for it. They were infuriated by the meddling of Spanish jurists who sought to protect those peoples with humanitarian concepts of international law. They bridled at the moral qualms of the European empires about genocide and dispossession, such as later President Andrew Jackson’s punitive raids into Spanish territories to clear the lands of the Indians. These were the qualms the US elites saw as political interference in America’s internal affairs. President James Monroe’s 1823 State of the Union address set out out ‘red lines’ to stop Spanish, French and British restriction on American violence and dispossession of Native Americans and to justify future conquests. It never got any better.

The historian Greg Grandin, in his book *America América,* by contrast, documents what the real intent of the Monroe Doctrine was and how it worked in practice. It was he wrote “a rousing defence of conquest.”

The Monroe Doctrine has always been about American expansion, cloaked in anti-colonial rhetoric. It was not a statement of principle. It did not reflect ‘reality’. Look at a map of the ‘Western hemisphere’ in 1823! It did not protect new North or Latin American states from revanchist European interventions. It reserved the ‘hemisphere’ - most of which Monroe did not control - for U.S. conquest. It excused and enabled murder, dispossession and genocide on a continental scale. And, of course, as it does today, it excused kidnapping,

The US *National Security Strateg*y better understands the true spirit of the Monroe Doctrine. That spirit has been reflected in 200 years of regime change operations, violence and conquest by the USA. Indeed, Grandin documents the first regime change operation in Mexico in 1825.

The Monroe Doctrine is simply a unilateral declaration of a right to conquest outside the restraints of morality or international law. It is an assertion that the USA has a unique right to pursue its geopolitical interests without regard to others. It is an assertion of the supremacy of one state over all others, first across half of a continent, then a hemisphere, and from 1991, if not 1945, across the whole globe.

The Monroe Doctrine is a credo of conquest.

It cannot be excused by so-called ‘realism’, that the strong do as they will, and the weak do as they must. The purpose of law, including international law, is to subject the strong and the weak to the one set of constraints. It cannot be accepted as part of ‘great power’ politics since it clashes with the fundamental Charter principles of sovereign equality. The Monroe Doctrine is an expression of American supremacism and its belief that it is a chosen nation whose conquests are concealed crusades. This kind of thinking is enabled by the Monroe Doctrine. It inculcates a deep sense of impunity among US decision-makers. It justifies kidnapping, assassinations, illegal invasion and the defiance of international law.

**It is not compatible with the UN Charter.**

If international law and the UN charter are to be respected, then the USA must repudiate the Monroe Doctrine, the original sin of its empire.

## **The Third Dilemma: How Can the UN Be Founded Anew so This Never Happens Again?**

At the UN Security Council, Jeffrey Sachs warned of grave consequences if no effective action was taken after its deliberations on Venezuela:

> Peace and the survival of humanity depend on whether the United Nations Charter remains a living instrument of international law or is allowed to wither into irrelevance. That is the choice before this Council today.

What became clear from the meeting was that the United Nations Charter is also hostage to the USA through its exploitation of the flawed institutions of the United Nations. That hostage situation plays out daily at the UN Security Council. The Council is paralysed by the prisoners’ dilemma embodied in its membership, with the USA and its NATO allies, Britain and France, arrayed against China and Russia.

These flaws are rooted in the post-1945 Anglo-American ascendancy at the UN, which slowly and reluctantly conceded equal sovereignty to the nations of the imploding and undeclared empires after the Second World War. The flaw has remained through decades of decolonisation, détente and the development of a vastly different distribution of ideas, people and resources around the world since 1945. Still, after 80 years, the world’s most populous nation and proudest advocate of peace, India, is excluded from the Security Council due to the arrogance of US Americans, the moral rivalry of the Chinese, and the empty longing for the lost empire of its former oppressor, Britain.

For decades, since the early 1970s, when the real China replaced the USA military colony of Taiwan on the Security Council, diplomats have debated the reform of the Security Council and UN institutions. Fifty years later, there is no clear path to reform. The United Nations Charter remains a battered, abused prisoner of the lawless ‘great powers’ of the world, principally the USA.

The dilemma facing the member states of the UN then is not only, as Jeffrey Sachs posed it, whether to treat the UN Charter as a living instrument of international law or allow it to wither into irrelevance.

The dilemma is how to free the hostage of the UN charter from its bondage by the USA. The dilemma is how to give the UN charter a new home, safe from the violence of states that declare doctrines of supremacy over others.

The dilemma is how to rebuild the United Nations on the firm foundations of its principles, without the faulty post-war construction of the Western rules based order.

That dilemma connects the Venezuela situation to a long-standing debate on reform of the UN, specifically the Security Council. The failure to progress reforms has exasperated many. Now some prominent voices appear to be calling time on the UN.

### **Chas Freeman’s Proposal for Multilateral Separatism**

In an [interview](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=x33LoXHqUCk&t=1803s) with on the Venezuela situation, former USA government official Chas Freeman condemned the USA’s actions and urged the world to take action to prevent future violations of international law and disrespect for the UN charter by the country of which he is rightly ashamed.

Aware of the long record of ineffectual talk of UN reform, he condemned the UN as useless:

> We \[the USA\] have a dictatorship, we might as well just recognize that. Same thing internationally. Why do we have the United Nations Security Council? What the hell does it do? . . . Why do we have this institution? It’s clearly ineffectual. What can be done to restore respect for international consensus-based norms? That is the question now before us. And I’ve always thought, we’re far too hung up on the post-World War II institutions. Of course, they give great advantages to the United States and the Russian Federation, Britain, France, and China, the nominal victors of World War II, and they don’t allow the same privilege to major countries like Japan or India. So the system isn’t working. Yeah. So what are we going to do about it?
> 
> Chas Freeman, *Neutrality Studies*, 2026

His proposal is an international conference that would partially and incrementally replace the UN and that could impose trade sanctions on the USA through individual states, in conformance with norms but outside the deadlocked UN:

> Here’s my proposition. If the existing institutions don’t work then convene ad hoc conferences and make the rules among the people who attend the conferences. So if the United States doesn’t want to attend the conference, fine let the United States do what it wants. But among the other countries, starting with the BRICS as a kernel or some other group.
> 
> Chas Freeman, *Neutrality Studies*, 2026

It is a proposal worthy of discussion, but requiring adaptation.

### **The Strengths and Weaknesses of This Approach**

In exasperation with the lack of action, Chas Freeman has proposed bypassing the UN in separate multilateral conferences. This is understandable, but dangerous. This proposal could slide into a Quit the UN campaign. It will encourage the worst tendencies of American techno-libertarian exceptionalism.

The strength of Freeman’s proposal is to demand and imagine the possibility of action. Quit the grumbling, podcasters. How can we improve matters? He acknowledged that all international law and all international institutions can change in response to the initiative of the participants. They are the products of human imagination, dialogue and creative collaboration. Our minds, when subordinated to our captors, pin us to our cages. Our minds can also build new houses free of our kidnappers. If this incarnation of the UN Charter is not working, then let us meet, talk, create a new institution that honours the living tradition of international law, and cooperate together in free responsibility. Freeman has dared to suggest that we take action and no longer accept the paralysis of the status quo. I thank him for and was inspired to write this piece by his initiative.

The proposal’s weakness, however, is that it allows the USA to get away with murder - and kidnapping, and robbery, sabotage, bullying and all the other crimes against the UN Charter it has committed for so long.

There is a tendency, encouraged by the USA’s anti-statist political culture on left, right and centre, to say there is no international law. Why? Because the USA and Israel act lawlessly. And if there is no law in town, then let’s just move out of town, leave the gangsters in charge, and set up some ideal community over the frontier in the hope the lawlessness will not follow.

But there is international law, even if the USA defies it. Every day citizens of states, including the USA, travel, trade and communicate across borders through the institutions of that international law. Every day complex issues are settled by negotiation, guided by legal principles.

The law endures, despite the acts of criminals. The law remains, even if only a small proportion of crimes are prosecuted and the criminals jailed. This is true for law within nations and between nations. The law endures, despite all the human failings of legal, governmental and civil institutions, so long as we contain, punish and rehabilitate the worst law-breakers.

As UN Secretary-General António Guterres said:

> In situations as confused and complex as the one we now face, it is important to stick to principles. Respect for the UN Charter and all other applicable legal frameworks to safeguard peace and security. Respect for the principles of sovereignty, political independence and territorial integrity of States. The prohibition of the threat or use of force. The power of the law must prevail.
> 
> International law contains tools to address issues such as illicit traffic in narcotics, disputes about resources and human rights concerns. This is the route we need to take.

Chas Freeman indulges the American frontier tradition of stepping outside the law when it frustrates your purpose. His intent was to get action started. Let’s just meet and talk. But the action must be chained to a constructive long-term purpose. Otherwise we will fail to rebuild a safe house in which international law can thrive, secure from the abusive behaviour of the USA.

Instead of bypassing the UN, the world should **found the UN anew**. The fundamentals of the Charter are sound and accepted by all, but the USA. Let us rebuild systematically for the world of 2025 and beyond, not that of 1945 or 1991.

Freeman’s welcome proposal may, therefore, be adapted quite simply. Let a kernel of UN member states convene a conference. But make it a conference to build a new home for the principles of the UN Charter. Then turn the conference into a Peace Congress, as significant as Westphalia in 1648, to reconstitute the United Nations and uphold the principles of its Charter, which many from around the world designed, not just the USA.

The question is not whether to abandon the principles of the UN Charter or the institution itself, but how to create institutional arrangements where those principles can finally and fully be upheld by all member states, including the USA. The goal is not exclusion but inclusion, on the basis of genuine acceptance of the Charter’s authority rather than defiance of it.

If you will forgive me, I have indulged my old bureaucratic virtues to shape a throw-away line into a proposal that could be implemented over five years.

## **A Proposal to Found the UN Anew: 2026-2031**

### **Historical Precedents for Institutional Transformation**

This proposal follows in a tradition of the establishment of major institutions and norms in international relations. The Peace of Westphalia (1648) established principles of sovereignty and practices of diplomacy, such as the peace conference, after the devastating Thirty Years’ War. The 1814 Congress of Vienna established the five-power concert system in Europe after the global Napoleonic wars. The Congress of Panama in 1826, Greg Grandin argues, founded in Latin America the world’s first true community of equal sovereign nations. The League of Nations (1920) represented the first attempt at collective security after World War I. After World War II, the international community did not abandon the project of international law, it established the United Nations, learning from past failures while preserving core principles. It did so with contributions from around the world, not just the Washington beltway.

We now face a similar moment. The UN’s institutional structures, designed for the world of 1945, have proven inadequate to constrain violations of the Charter by its most powerful members. The solution is not to abandon the UN Charter, but to create new institutional arrangements capable of upholding it. The first-best option is for this transformation to occur within the existing UN framework, preserving legal continuity with the thousands of treaties, conventions, and specialized agencies built over eight decades. If, however, existing structures prove immovable, the international community must be prepared to establish a parallel body that honours the Charter’s principles.

### **Phase One: Peace Congress and Coalition Building (2026-2027)**

**The Founding Coalition**

A kernel of states committed to the UN Charter should convene an emergency international conference in 2026. This coalition would comprise member states and organisations such as:

-   **BRICS members** (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, and expanded members), representing major powers across the Global Majority
    
-   **ASEAN member states**, bringing the diplomatic traditions of Southeast Asia
    
-   **The African Union**, representing the continent’s 54 states
    
-   **Latin American states** through existing regional bodies with particular urgency given the Venezuela crisis
    
-   **The European Union**, whose commitment to international law could prove decisive
    
-   **Other appropriate multilateral and regional organisations**
    
-   **The United States and Israel**, invited to participate conditionally
    

The USA would be invited to participate in these conferences on the condition that it accepts the binding authority of the UN Charter and any reformed transnational institutions. It must also cease its aggression against Venezuela.

This is not exclusion but an insistence on the fundamental principle of membership: that all states, including the most powerful, are bound by international law. The USA has effectively chosen to abandon this principle through its actions. It has stated its defiance doctrinally in its *National Security Strategy*. The door remains open for the USA to return, but only on the basis of genuine commitment to the Charter, not continued defiance of it.

**Conference Objectives**

The 2026 conference would address:

-   Immediate resolution of the Venezuela crisis and return of President Maduro
    
-   Documentation of Charter violations and their systemic causes
    
-   Formation of working groups to draft institutional reforms
    
-   Establishment of a timeline for transformation
    

This conference should set the scene for a Peace Congress in 2027 that would refine proposals and build consensus for the transformative steps to come.

### **Phase Two: Charter Revision and Institutional Reform (2028)**

The 2027 Peace Congress would prepare proposals and negotiate the widest possible agreement by the international community to two options for the future UN:

-   **Option A**: Comprehensive reforms to the existing UN, including the Charter, General Assembly, Security Council and associated structures, or
    
-   **Option B**: A new charter establishing a parallel body, if reforms prove impossible within existing structures.
    

If not adopted by the UN previously, these options would include institutional reforms to the Security Council based on the 7-7-7 Governance Formula. Following Kishore Mahbubani’s proposal, new institutional governance arrangements should reflect the multipolar world of 2025, not the world of 1945:

-   **Seven permanent members** representing major powers with substantial global responsibilities and contributions to international peace and security. This would likely include China, India, the United States (if it accepts Charter obligations), Brazil, the European Union (or major European states), Russia, and one rotating seat for the African continent
    
-   **Seven semi-permanent members** elected for longer terms (e.g., 5-7 years) from mid-sized powers, ensuring regional representation and continuity
    
-   **Seven rotating seats** for smaller states serving shorter terms (e.g., 2 years), maintaining broad global participation
    

Crucially, permanent membership would not carry veto power in the same form as the current Security Council. Instead, decisions would require supermajorities (e.g., two-thirds or three-quarters), making unilateral obstruction impossible while still requiring broad consensus.

**USA Participation: Conditional on Compliance**

The USA would be eligible for permanent membership in this reformed body, but only upon meeting specific criteria demonstrating genuine acceptance of international law:

1.  **Withdrawal of ICC arrest warrants and prosecutions** issued without Security Council authorization, and acceptance of ICC jurisdiction for future allegations of war crimes
    
2.  **Establishment of an international truth and reconciliation commission** to investigate and publicly document CIA and military regime change operations from 1947 to present, with full cooperation and declassification of relevant documents
    
3.  **Congressional endorsement** of key principles of international law, specifically:
    
    -   Recognition that the UN Charter prohibits unilateral use of force except in self-defence
        
    -   Repudiation of the Monroe Doctrine as inconsistent with sovereign equality of states
        
    -   Acceptance of binding international arbitration for disputes
        
    -   Commitment to pay all assessed UN dues within 90 days of assessment
        

Israel’s participation would be subject to compliance with any International Court of Justice determinations with respect to the genocide in Gaza.

If at that point the conflict in Ukraine is not settled, UN member states may also wish to consider making Russian and Ukrainian participation conditional on agreement to a UN-supervised peace process.

These criteria are not punitive but restorative. They create accountability for past violations while establishing the basis for future compliance. The timeline for meeting these criteria would extend through the transition period, with progress reviewed annually.

**Continuity of International Law**

To preserve the legal architecture built over eight decades, the 2028 charter would:

-   Explicitly affirm continuity with all existing UN treaties and conventions
    
-   Transfer responsibility for specialized agencies (WHO, UNESCO, IAEA, etc.) to the reformed body
    
-   Grandfather existing treaty obligations for all member states
    
-   Establish that the new body is the legal successor to the UN for all purposes
    

This approach ensures that international law does not fragment, but rather that its institutional enforcement mechanisms are strengthened.

### **Phase Three: Parallel Operation and Implementation (2029-2030)**

If the Peace Congress adopts Option B, beginning in January 2029 - coinciding with the anticipated inauguration of a new USA president - a period of parallel operation would begin. This timing is strategic, offering a new administration the opportunity to break with past policies.

During this phase:

-   The reformed body begins operating with its new governance structure
    
-   States may choose to work through either the old or new institutional framework
    
-   The existing UN continues operating for states not yet participating in the reformed system
    
-   Specialized agencies transition their reporting and oversight to the new body
    
-   A joint secretariat manages coordination between old and new structures
    
-   Resourcing of the two structures would reflect the proportion of global population their member states represent.
    

This parallel period serves several purposes:

-   It demonstrates the viability of the new arrangements
    
-   It creates incentives for non-participating states to join
    
-   It allows time for the USA to meet rehabilitation criteria if it chooses to do so
    
-   It provides a safety net if unexpected problems emerge.
    

### **Phase Four: Full Transition (2031)**

By 2031, the international community should complete the transition:

-   The reformed body becomes the sole authoritative institution for international law
    
-   All member states of the old UN are invited to join the new body on equal terms
    
-   States that have not joined face the consequences of non-participation, including:
    
    -   Loss of voting rights in international institutions
        
    -   Restrictions on participation in treaty negotiations
        
    -   Potential for coordinated sanctions by member states
        
    -   Exclusion from specialized agencies and their benefits
        
    -   Withdrawal of international legal protections and rights from citizens and corporations of non-member states
        

**Relocation and Symbolic Renewal**

To mark this transformation and to root the institution in regions whose diplomatic traditions emphasize consensus and sovereignty, the reformed UN should relocate its headquarters to South or Southeast Asia. This relocation would signal that international law is no longer hostage to the geopolitical arrangements of 1945.

In a similar vein, the Peace Congress may consider renaming the institution. The United Nations was initially the designation for the alliance between some powers (the British Empire and the USA) during World War Two. The United Nations remains associated with the partial bonds of that wartime alliance. Similarly, the Peace Congress may wish to emphasise harmonious difference, rather than unity, in the name of the reconstituted institution.

## **The USA’s Choice**

Ultimately, this roadmap places the choice squarely with the USA. The international community is not excluding the USA from the system of international law. Rather, the USA has excluded itself through its persistent violations of the UN Charter and its declared intention to operate “free of outside interference.”

The door remains open. A USA willing to accept the constraints and responsibilities of UN membership - one prepared to embrace rather than defy the Charter - would be welcomed as a full participant in the reformed institution. Indeed, given American capacity and resources, such participation would strengthen the system.

But the world can no longer wait for American cooperation to uphold international law. If the USA chooses continued defiance, the international community will proceed without it. The standard we walk by is the standard we accept. The time has come to walk by a higher standard.

That standard is the fundamental principle that binds the society of nations: that we are all, powerful and weak alike, subject to the rule of law.
