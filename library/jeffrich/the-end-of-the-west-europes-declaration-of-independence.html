Ruptures That Do Not End Empires

Large empires, or, in a modern euphemism, civilization states, sometimes break apart. They rarely collapse. They commonly crumble, but rarely without attempts at restoration. Frequently, they branch off like a fern. After the rupture, each fractal state makes it way anew, infused with new genes and tempered by other circumstances.

This fractal branching occurs because empires, or civilization states, are rarely cohesive. Their rival statelets, power elites and cultural waves form uneasy partnerships. Divorce is always a more likely prospect than marriage unto death. Separation is always a more appealing choice than accelerating into the burning apocalypse of civilizational erasure imagined by some mad emperor.

This pattern occurred in the archetypal European empire, Rome. Despite millennia of historical speculation on the fall of Rome, there is no agreement on the causes. Disagreement persists on when the Roman empire ended. Rome was not built in a day, nor did it collapse in a year. 235 CE is a turning point of decline. Constantine the Great divided East and West in 337. Disasters befell Rome in 410, 455, or 476. In that latter year, Odoacer, the Gothic ‘barbarian’ from some unidentified place in Central or Eastern Europe, deposed the continuous line of Western Roman emperors. But the Eastern Roman Empire continued till 1453, and the Holy Roman Empire revived a bloodline of sovereignty to last until Napoleon dissolved this thousand-year Reich in 1806.

I told the story of the fall of Rome in these two podcasts in late 2021.

In other words, the Roman empire broke apart, and branched off in new and different directions in response to ruptures, contingencies and the deep rumbles of historical processes.

Despite the outpourings of Atlantic scholars this new civilization state has never been cohesive. “Americans are from Mars and Europeans are from Venus; they agree on little and understand one another even less,” wrote the Deep State Svengali, Robert Kagan in 2002. But rest assured, he whispered with his knife at your throat, the gunslingers from Texas rule the West, or so all the realist geopolitical theories say. Europe Tour Recap

Then in the European leg of the tour, I followed a serious of years that made European history.

We began in 540 CE with Norse myths and a geological rupture in the Americas.

Most Anglophone accounts of the Vikings describe their eruption into history at Lindisfarne in 793 CE. A monk trembled out the first version of a tired historical meme: “heathen men came and miserably destroyed God’s church on Lindisfarne, with plunder and slaughter.” So let us shake up our timeline and our perception of Europe in world history. The story of how 540 and the Vikings made Europe begins not off the coast of Northern Britain, nor the fjords of Norway, but in the old caldera of a volcano in El Salvador.

Then we went to 1054 and the multipolar civilization of the Eastern Roman Empire, also known as the Holy City of Byzantium.

This week we sail to Byzantium, the Eastern Roman Empire, and its capital Constantinople. Serving as a five ways bridge between the Asian, African, European, Islamic and Norse/Russian worlds, Constantinople was the authentic cultural metropolis of medieval Europe. It was by far Europe’s largest city, if dwarfed by the great cities of East and Southern Asia.

The Peace of Westphalia was made in 1648, but that was not the year that a world order of European nation states came into being.

The scholarly consensus has discarded the grand strategic claims of Kissinger, Mearsheimer and other realists into the dustbin of history. 1648 does not mark the emergence of the nation-state and national interest as the sole obsession of international society. Ideas of order preceded Westphalia. Practices of empire followed. Interference in the affairs of other states carried on from the Partition of Poland to the Ukrainian coup of 2014.

In 1848 Europe erupted in liberal, multi-ethnic and national revolutions; an astonishing truly European revolution, still much misunderstood.

Marx noticed the entanglement of past and present, dream and action, aspiration and manipulation, insight and illusion in revolutionary moments, even as he mocked the historical cosplay. Today, when political unrest breaks out in some faraway country, and history repeats itself for the third, or tenth or one-hundredth time, instant experts do not quote Marx or Hegel on history as tragedy and then farce. They just cry, “Fake.”

Geopolitical commentators convert these complicated phenomena into the cliché of the ‘colour revolution,’ the engineered ‘regime change operation’, planned and executed by some omnipotent intelligence agency. This cartoon explanation for complex events is now unfortunately the standard interpretation of political upheaval anywhere in the world.

In 1910 the modern world and modernist Europe changed utterly.

After the Great War, Virginia Woolf wrote: “on or about December 1910 human character changed.” She posed the riddle of the modern. She expressed the inner experience of Europe. Why did Woolf believe that, in that month, in that year, human character itself changed, changed utterly? At least, in Europe.

In 1936, the European part of the Great Imperial War 1931 to 1945 began.

In July 1936 the Spanish Civil War broke out and the Second World War began in Europe. By 1939 Spain was ruled by the victor of that civil war, the Caudillo of the West, General Franco. The Long ‘Cold’ War against the Soviet Union/Russia paused for six years, while the West turned against Germany. But in Spain, Franco entrenched himself brutally, backed by Western democracies, until his death in 1975.

In 1968 Europe and the world revolted against the older generation of modernity.

If human character changed in 1910, then, in 1968, everything, absolutely everything, about human character became politically unstable, or worse, ‘historically contingent’. The human could change at any moment. Or so some European intellectuals of 1968 believed. Welcome to the Age of Liquid Modernity.

And in 1989, the Berlin Wall fell.

Vaclav Havel left his first experience of living in a lie. Angela Merkel crossed into a soon-to-be reunified Germany. But the North American Empire decided it did not want to let go of its most treasured part, Europe.

In 1989 peaceful revolutions reunited East and West Germany and Eastern and Western Europe. It was the year that Europeans chose freedom, rejected socialism, bound democracies in an ever closer union, and embraced “living in truth.” It was also the year that USA strategists armed the Gates of Europe to expand NATO ever eastward. Thirty-six years later the fabric of the Velvet Revolution is stained and torn. Did Europe choose freedom and truth in that heady year, or did 1989 make Europe into a vassal of the Atlantic Ring of Power?
