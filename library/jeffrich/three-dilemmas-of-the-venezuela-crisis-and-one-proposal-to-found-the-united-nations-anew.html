A Proposal to Found the UN Anew: 2026-2031

Historical Precedents for Institutional Transformation

This proposal follows in a tradition of the establishment of major institutions and norms in international relations. The Peace of Westphalia (1648) established principles of sovereignty and practices of diplomacy, such as the peace conference, after the devastating Thirty Years’ War. The 1814 Congress of Vienna established the five-power concert system in Europe after the global Napoleonic wars. The Congress of Panama in 1826, Greg Grandin argues, founded in Latin America the world’s first true community of equal sovereign nations. The League of Nations (1920) represented the first attempt at collective security after World War I. After World War II, the international community did not abandon the project of international law, it established the United Nations, learning from past failures while preserving core principles. It did so with contributions from around the world, not just the Washington beltway.

We now face a similar moment. The UN’s institutional structures, designed for the world of 1945, have proven inadequate to constrain violations of the Charter by its most powerful members. The solution is not to abandon the UN Charter, but to create new institutional arrangements capable of upholding it. The first-best option is for this transformation to occur within the existing UN framework, preserving legal continuity with the thousands of treaties, conventions, and specialized agencies built over eight decades. If, however, existing structures prove immovable, the international community must be prepared to establish a parallel body that honours the Charter’s principles.

Phase One: Peace Congress and Coalition Building (2026-2027)

The Founding Coalition

A kernel of states committed to the UN Charter should convene an emergency international conference in 2026. This coalition would comprise member states and organisations such as:

  • BRICS members (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, and expanded members), representing major powers across the Global Majority

  • ASEAN member states, bringing the diplomatic traditions of Southeast Asia

  • The African Union, representing the continent’s 54 states

  • Latin American states through existing regional bodies with particular urgency given the Venezuela crisis

  • The European Union, whose commitment to international law could prove decisive

  • Other appropriate multilateral and regional organisations

  • The United States and Israel, invited to participate conditionally

The USA would be invited to participate in these conferences on the condition that it accepts the binding authority of the UN Charter and any reformed transnational institutions. It must also cease its aggression against Venezuela.

This is not exclusion but an insistence on the fundamental principle of membership: that all states, including the most powerful, are bound by international law. The USA has effectively chosen to abandon this principle through its actions. It has stated its defiance doctrinally in its National Security Strategy. The door remains open for the USA to return, but only on the basis of genuine commitment to the Charter, not continued defiance of it.

Conference Objectives

The 2026 conference would address:

  • Immediate resolution of the Venezuela crisis and return of President Maduro

  • Documentation of Charter violations and their systemic causes

  • Formation of working groups to draft institutional reforms

  • Establishment of a timeline for transformation

This conference should set the scene for a Peace Congress in 2027 that would refine proposals and build consensus for the transformative steps to come.

Phase Two: Charter Revision and Institutional Reform (2028)

The 2027 Peace Congress would prepare proposals and negotiate the widest possible agreement by the international community to two options for the future UN:

  • Option A: Comprehensive reforms to the existing UN, including the Charter, General Assembly, Security Council and associated structures, or

  • Option B: A new charter establishing a parallel body, if reforms prove impossible within existing structures.

If not adopted by the UN previously, these options would include institutional reforms to the Security Council based on the 7-7-7 Governance Formula. Following Kishore Mahbubani’s proposal, new institutional governance arrangements should reflect the multipolar world of 2025, not the world of 1945:

  • Seven permanent members representing major powers with substantial global responsibilities and contributions to international peace and security. This would likely include China, India, the United States (if it accepts Charter obligations), Brazil, the European Union (or major European states), Russia, and one rotating seat for the African continent

  • Seven semi-permanent members elected for longer terms (e.g., 5-7 years) from mid-sized powers, ensuring regional representation and continuity

  • Seven rotating seats for smaller states serving shorter terms (e.g., 2 years), maintaining broad global participation

Crucially, permanent membership would not carry veto power in the same form as the current Security Council. Instead, decisions would require supermajorities (e.g., two-thirds or three-quarters), making unilateral obstruction impossible while still requiring broad consensus.

USA Participation: Conditional on Compliance

The USA would be eligible for permanent membership in this reformed body, but only upon meeting specific criteria demonstrating genuine acceptance of international law:

  1. Withdrawal of ICC arrest warrants and prosecutions issued without Security Council authorization, and acceptance of ICC jurisdiction for future allegations of war crimes

  2. Establishment of an international truth and reconciliation commission to investigate and publicly document CIA and military regime change operations from 1947 to present, with full cooperation and declassification of relevant documents

  3. Congressional endorsement of key principles of international law, specifically:

    • Recognition that the UN Charter prohibits unilateral use of force except in self-defence

    • Repudiation of the Monroe Doctrine as inconsistent with sovereign equality of states

    • Acceptance of binding international arbitration for disputes

    • Commitment to pay all assessed UN dues within 90 days of assessment

Israel’s participation would be subject to compliance with any International Court of Justice determinations with respect to the genocide in Gaza.

If at that point the conflict in Ukraine is not settled, UN member states may also wish to consider making Russian and Ukrainian participation conditional on agreement to a UN-supervised peace process.

These criteria are not punitive but restorative. They create accountability for past violations while establishing the basis for future compliance. The timeline for meeting these criteria would extend through the transition period, with progress reviewed annually.

Continuity of International Law

To preserve the legal architecture built over eight decades, the 2028 charter would:

  • Explicitly affirm continuity with all existing UN treaties and conventions

  • Transfer responsibility for specialized agencies (WHO, UNESCO, IAEA, etc.) to the reformed body

  • Grandfather existing treaty obligations for all member states

  • Establish that the new body is the legal successor to the UN for all purposes

This approach ensures that international law does not fragment, but rather that its institutional enforcement mechanisms are strengthened.

Phase Three: Parallel Operation and Implementation (2029-2030)

If the Peace Congress adopts Option B, beginning in January 2029 - coinciding with the anticipated inauguration of a new USA president - a period of parallel operation would begin. This timing is strategic, offering a new administration the opportunity to break with past policies.

During this phase:

  • The reformed body begins operating with its new governance structure

  • States may choose to work through either the old or new institutional framework

  • The existing UN continues operating for states not yet participating in the reformed system

  • Specialized agencies transition their reporting and oversight to the new body

  • A joint secretariat manages coordination between old and new structures

  • Resourcing of the two structures would reflect the proportion of global population their member states represent.

This parallel period serves several purposes:

  • It demonstrates the viability of the new arrangements

  • It creates incentives for non-participating states to join

  • It allows time for the USA to meet rehabilitation criteria if it chooses to do so

  • It provides a safety net if unexpected problems emerge.

Phase Four: Full Transition (2031)

By 2031, the international community should complete the transition:

  • The reformed body becomes the sole authoritative institution for international law

  • All member states of the old UN are invited to join the new body on equal terms

  • States that have not joined face the consequences of non-participation, including:

    • Loss of voting rights in international institutions

    • Restrictions on participation in treaty negotiations

    • Potential for coordinated sanctions by member states

    • Exclusion from specialized agencies and their benefits

    • Withdrawal of international legal protections and rights from citizens and corporations of non-member states

Relocation and Symbolic Renewal

To mark this transformation and to root the institution in regions whose diplomatic traditions emphasize consensus and sovereignty, the reformed UN should relocate its headquarters to South or Southeast Asia. This relocation would signal that international law is no longer hostage to the geopolitical arrangements of 1945.

In a similar vein, the Peace Congress may consider renaming the institution. The United Nations was initially the designation for the alliance between some powers (the British Empire and the USA) during World War Two. The United Nations remains associated with the partial bonds of that wartime alliance. Similarly, the Peace Congress may wish to emphasise harmonious difference, rather than unity, in the name of the reconstituted institution.

The USA’s Choice

Ultimately, this roadmap places the choice squarely with the USA. The international community is not excluding the USA from the system of international law. Rather, the USA has excluded itself through its persistent violations of the UN Charter and its declared intention to operate “free of outside interference.”

The door remains open. A USA willing to accept the constraints and responsibilities of UN membership - one prepared to embrace rather than defy the Charter - would be welcomed as a full participant in the reformed institution. Indeed, given American capacity and resources, such participation would strengthen the system.

But the world can no longer wait for American cooperation to uphold international law. If the USA chooses continued defiance, the international community will proceed without it. The standard we walk by is the standard we accept. The time has come to walk by a higher standard.

That standard is the fundamental principle that binds the society of nations: that we are all, powerful and weak alike, subject to the rule of law.
