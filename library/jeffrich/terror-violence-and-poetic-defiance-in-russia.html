Is Russia built on a history of violence? Do its people live under the shadow of terror? And, if that is so, how did its poets, like Pasternak, Akhmatova or Sergei Eisenstein, defy terror and state violence to create the most enduring, compassionate art of the 20th century?

In Eisenstein’s film Ivan the Terrible, directed under the scrutiny of Stalin, Ivan’s shadow haunts the Kremlin walls, just as the history of terror and state violence haunts Russian history. The shadows generate the Russia Anxiety.

Welcome to week three of the Russia World History Tour. This week we ask whether Russian history is exceptional in its terror and violence.

Ivan’s Shadow & Terror in Russian History

Mark B. Smith begins chapter five of The Russia Anxiety, “The Terror Moment: Is Russia Built on a History of Violence?” with the stories of the most spectacular state violence which “Russia’s cruellest Tsar” inflicted during the years of the oprichniki.

Ivan was more complex than Western caricatures of the Terrible. I have long harboured a theory he suffered borderline personality disorder. Yet there is no doubt he was cruel, violent, and sadistic. He practised state violence in a European cultural era of spectacular state violence. Notoriously on 25 July 1570 at Poganaya meadow in Moscow, Ivan, personally and through his executioners, publicly tortured, mutilated and killed 116 of his leading and most senior officials. You can read the details in de Madariaga’s Ivan the Terrible; I will spare you the horror, but note the reflection of Soviet historian, A.A. Zimin (1920-1980),

The Russian capital had seen many horrors in its time. But what happened in Moscow on 25 July, in its cruelty and sadistic refinement, outdid all that had gone before and can perhaps be explained only by the cruel temperament and the sick imagination of Ivan the Terrible.”

Quoted Madariaga, Ivan the Terrible, p. 259

Zimin and the film director Eisenstein would have witnessed in his youth another inexplicable act of cruel, personally directed state violence, Stalin’s terror and the purges of the 1930s. Ivan IV and Stalin stand like giant shadows over Russian history. Unsurprisingly, Ivan’s violent oprichniki has stood as a symbol of state violence even today; for example, in the novel, The Day of the Oprichniki, by the contemporary liberal Russian novelist in exile, Vladimir Sorokin. Sorokin imagines an aged Putin figure brooding over his 21st century oprichniki committing the same acts of cruelty. Smith wrote,

Given what we know of Russian history - the merciless reforms of Peter the Great, the prison camps of nineteenth-century Siberia, above all the terror of Stalin - it might seem obvious that Ivan the Terrible was only one act in a continuous history of violence which continues to define Russia today.

Smith, Russia Anxiety, p. 158

Russia as Terror State.

Does Russia have a continuous and exceptional history of state violence? Is there a bloodline that runs from Ivan IV through Peter I to Stalin and Putin?

The Black Legend of Russian History says, ‘Yes. Russia is culturally totalitarian.’ Indeed, Anne Applebaum has argued that Russia’s entry into modernity itself was prevented by, in Sorokin’s words, the “vicious nature and archaic underpinnings of the Russian state,” and its retrogressive instincts for empire, autocracy, violence and terror.

In contemporary America’s retelling of the Black Legend, central place goes to Stalin’s Terror of the 1930s - the purges, the show trials, the midnight arrests by the ‘KGB’ (the secret police name was then actually the NKVD), the Gulag, the murders, and, in recent decades, the accusation of a Red Famine targeted at Ukrainians, the Holodomor.

This version of the legend has its origins in Cold War American political science and history which invented the ‘totalitarian model’ that equated Nazism and Communism, and the dictators, Hitler and Stalin. Despite the fame and honour bestowed on writers in this tradition - Arendt, Brzezinski, Applebaum and Snyder - the model sucks. Historian Ronald Grigor Suny wrote,

The totalitarian approach turned an apt if not wholly accurate description into a model, complete with predictions of future trajectories, The concept exaggerated similarities and underestimated differences between quite distinct regimes. . . . Even more pernicious were the predictive parallels: since Nazi Germany had acted in an expansionist, aggressive way, it could be expected that another totalitarian regime would also be aggressive and expansionist. Indeed, during the Cold War Western media and governments fostered the notion that the USSR was poised and ready to invade Western Europe. Any concessions to Soviet Communism were labelled ‘appeasement’, a direct analogy to Western negotiations with the Nazis in the 1930s.

Suny, ‘Reading Russia and the Soviet Union in the twentieth century’ The Cambridge History of Russia Volume III: The Twentieth Century (2006)

Suny wrote those words twenty years ago in scholarly optimism that the work of historians who debunked the totalitarian myth would prevail. But over the last two decades, the American liberal empire’s public intellectuals, like Applebaum and Snyder, have marched the zombie army of totalitarianism across its disintegrating Raj. Arendt’s Origins of Totalitarianism (1951) was reanimated in 2016 to explain the new tyrannies of Trump and the Axis of Upheaval, despite the defects of its historical explanation and ignorance of decades of Soviet history since the Cold War début of the book. Robert Conquest’s polemical work on the Terror and the Holodomor and the CIA’s Black Book on Communism were sexed up by Applebaum and Snyder in Red Famine and Bloodlands. The central claim of the Black Book became the standard talking points of the political leaders of the USA’s networked empire, but now attributed to the sub-civilizational Russians and other allegedly authoritarian regimes.

Communist regimes did not just commit criminal acts (all states do on occasion) [ironically stated by the CIA given the USA’s decades of lawlessness]; they were criminal enterprises in their very essence: on principle, so to speak, they all ruled lawlessly by violence and without regard for human life.

Black Book on Communism, quoted Suny, ‘Reading Russia ’ The Cambridge History of Russia Volume III, p. 63

This familiar propaganda presented the Soviet Union, and later Russia or China as inherently Terror States. Reagan’s ‘evil empire’ metamorphosed into Applebaum’s Russian Imperialism. The absence of ideological competition after 1991 did not end the hatred. This line of thinking reached its apotheosis in Snyder’s Bloodlands and his celebration of the extreme ethnonationalist Ukrainian regime. Communism became Stalin’s Russia, and then Putin’s, and was defined as worse than Nazism, indeed culpable for the war that killed 27 million of its citizens. As in the first Cold War fight against Communism, intellectuals were urged to fight tyranny and to deliver salvation for Western civilization, at any price; even if it meant consorting with Wolfsangel wearing anti-semitic neo-Nazis in downtown Lviv.

Paradoxically, Suny wrote that,

in its attempt to judge Soviet killing by the standard of Nazi crimes, The Black Book actually de-historicised Soviet violence. Content and causation were less important than the equation with the colossal, seemingly inexplicable evil that led to the Holocaust.

Suny, ‘Reading Russia ’ The Cambridge History of Russia Volume III, p. 63

Bad history fuelled black legends. And starved the empathetic engagement with the experiences of Stalin’s Terror in the 1930s. Historians and journalists mutated into propagandists and shills for the American empire.

Irritation with such blatant propaganda provoked another kind of bad history. In rebellion against legends of Stalin’s crimes, writers such as Grover Furr have claimed the Terror was an invention of the intelligence agencies of the West, or the Trotskyites or some other scapegoat. These rehabilitations of Stalin - oddly in ghettoes of the left and right - are a counter-cultural response to saturation propaganda of the new totalitarian model. I discussed them indirectly with Sheila Fitzpatrick and Michael Carley in my interviews with them on this period. Like the cranks who deny the reality of the Holocaust, these revisionists confuse complexity of historical events with cleansing of historical guilt. They fail to engage with the tragic experiences of the Russian and Soviet peoples in the twentieth century.

Violence in Russian and Human History

That experience was not exceptional to Russia and not unlike the violence of states in Greater Europe, and indeed in Asia. Sheila Fitzpatrick compares the ‘Cultural Revolution’ in the Soviet Union in the 1920s to 1930s with that in China four decades later. Smith wrote, “What the Russia Anxiety often misses is that Russia’s history of violence is a history of modernity.” (p. 168) But within a century of civil war and violence, the violence and terror unleashed during Stalin and his security chief Yezhov’s Terror was a peak. After unleashing a ‘revolution from above’ to reform industry, agriculture, culture and the party, Stalin turned strangely against his inner circle and party in the mid 1930s to unleash the purges, show trials and executions that were a multi-year version of Ivan the Terrible’s executions at Poganaya meadow. Purity of belief was tested in blood. The Party was purged. Offenders were executed, expelled or imprisoned in the Gulag. The most intense years were between 1936 and 1938, as the threat of Nazi Germany loomed.

Party functionaries, industrial managers and professional people were targeted; ordinary citizens were swept up; orders were sent out to arrest members of particular ethnic groups, such as Poles and Koreans; local branches of the NKVD were given targets and invited to exceed them. In all, 692,000 people were shot, typically on trumped-up charges of espionage, industrial wrecking, ideological transgression or orchestrating far-fetched conspiracies.

Smith, Russia Anxiety, p. 179

The sheer weirdness of this collective violence, mixed with the redemptive cruel re-education of the Gulag, defies simple explanation. Its enigmas have opened the way for simplistic propaganda and asinine conspiracy theories. It was an event too unique to fit into a general model. As Smith wrote,

Although one can draw out the origins and consequences of Stalinist violence, it was strikingly of its time. It had no forerunners in Russian history and it never recurred.”

Smith, Russia Anxiety, p. 182

The best way to view the Terror is as an unique historical tragedy. It is not a symptom of totalitarianism. It is not atrocity propaganda by the CIA, however much its agents may have milked the tragedy for their twisted purposes. It does not show that Russia is built on a history of violence, nor that its people still live under Putin’s shadow of terror today. To compare Putin’s Russia with Stalin’s Terror is to engage in unethical, if highly remunerated, distortions of history. Mark B. Smith argues, indeed, that it is,

impossible to see how Stalinism’s combination of extremist ideology, police-power, state building, social flux, long-term brutalization, popular mobilization, and personal dictatorship in a closed-off society could have any explanatory power for the twenty-first century Russia.”

Smith, Russia Anxiety, p. 182

Such comparisons are political pornography. They fail to engage empathetically with the real historical experience of the Terror. This experience cannot be reduced to the prism of one psychopathic personality. Stalin’s violence had its roots in a historical period of exceptional violence, including the Civil War, as Michael Carley remarked during our interviews. Stalin did not act alone. “Although the mass violence of the Soviet period was a Soviet phenomenon and had no direct causes in the deep history of Russia,” Smith wrote, “it was still shaped by Russian people and their culture.” (Russia Anxiety, pp. 176-177)

The totalitarian model relies on the myth of enslavement to dictatorship; but that is not how the great outbreaks of mass violence of the twentieth century society really worked, whether in Soviet Russia, Nazi Germany, Maoist China, Yugoslavia, Rwanda, or the decades long tour of the USA ‘Rockin’ All Over the World’, that is estimated to have killed 4.5 million people since 2001, not counting Korea, Vietnam, Cambodia, Ukraine, and all the other bombing campaigns, frozen conflicts, regime changes and proxy wars.

Looking squarely and honestly into the faces of terror and state violence is difficult, however, even for the most compassionate souls and disciplined historians. The temptation is to succumb to what Inga Clendinnen, writing in Reading the Holocaust, called the ‘Gorgon effect’ - “the sickening of imagination and curiosity and the draining of the will which afflicts so many of us when we try to look squarely at the persons and processes implicated in the Holocaust.” (Clendinnen, Reading the Holocaust, p. 7)

That effect can paralyse our empathetic imagination in more cases than just the Holocaust, as so many experience today with the ‘live-streamed genocide’ in Gaza. I believe the same effect has gripped writers, propagandists and some historians of the Soviet Terror and Russian state violence in the twentieth century. Totalitarian models, fairy tales of resistance, and conspiracy theories of incrimination are preferred to empathetic engagement with the bewildering reality of the historical experience. Writers as different as Anne Applebaum, Grover Furr, Vladimir Sorokin or the scriptwriters of a thousand absurd Hollywood movies about Russian history betrayed the suffering of victims, distorted the experience of bystanders, and misunderstood the motives of perpetrators. They failed to see how in any historical period, one person could be victim, bystander and perpetrator. They ignored Solzhenitsyn’s dictum that the line between good and evil runs through every individual human heart.

This failure of empathy is tragic, but can be remedied by history and poetic imagination. Towards the end of her book, Clendinnen called to historians to fulfil their special obligation to rescue the voices of the dead from the “vagaries of fashion and political exploitation” in the representation of the Holocaust. Her call can extend to anyone who thinks or writes compassionately about history. Her ethical purpose can be transferred from misrepresentations of the Holocaust to all the black legends of history. Clendinnen wrote,

Humankind saw the face of the Gorgon in the concentration camps [Jeff note: I would add, or the Gulag, or Cambodia or Gaza or the rants of Lyndsey Graham], petrifying the human by its denial of the human both in itself and in its prey. The shadow of the Holocaust has lengthened with the years. In that shadow, none of us is at home in the world, because now we know the fragility of our content. If we are to see the Gorgon sufficiently steadily to destroy it, we cannot afford to be blinded by reverence or abashed into silence or deflected into a search for reassuring myths. We must do more than register guilt, or grief, or anger, or disgust, because neither reverence for those who suffer nor revulsion from those who inflict suffering will help us to overcome its power to paralyse, and to see it clearly.”

Clendinnen, Reading the Holocaust, p. 205

Poetic Defiance of Terror and its Gorgons

Ttwo acts of poetic defiance by victims of Stalin’s Terror show, however, that we can overcome that power to paralyse. Their stories also show the human complexity of the Terror.

In 1937 the five year-old Alexander Askoldov stood in terror, abandoned in his Kiev apartment after the NKVD, the Soviet secret police, had knocked on the door at midnight and arrested his mother, a doctor. His father, a factory manager, had already been arrested. Stalin’s agents leered at the suspicious Comrade Askoldova as she dressed without privacy to be taken away to interrogation, trial, execution or the Gulag. The agents told the mother, ‘We’ll come back for the boy.’ They did not. He wandered into the city, and was taken in by a Jewish family, who cared for him until his grandmother came from Moscow. She would raise him on her own during the war. Years later, after Stalin’s death, Alexander Askoldov returned to Kiev to thank the Jewish family. He discovered they had been executed, not by the NKVD; but by the Nazis and their Ukrainian collaborators at Babyi Yar in 1941.

In 1941, his mother was freed from the camp, and offered a chance to redeem herself serving as a doctor in the army during the Great Fatherland War. She did so but left her son alone, abandoning him this time through choice, if again in extreme circumstances. The wound of abandonment permanently scarred Alexander Askoldov who would go on to become a film director of a single film, The Commissar (1967). It was a searing portrayal of the Civil War (1918-22), during which terror and violence were perpetrated by the state and the masses, the Reds and the Whites, the Russians and the foreign intervention troops alike. It contains a character, Comrade Vavilova, who merges poetically with his memories of the mother who abandoned him to serve the Red Army.

They both wore the uniform of Soviet power during its most violent incarnations, in the Civil War and the Stalin period respectively. Who was the victim and who was the perpetrator? They were fighting for different things. But how do you tell the difference so many decades later?”

Smith, Russia Anxiety, p. 188

The film was made a little too late in the years after Khrushchev’s thaw when artistic experimentation was opened up. Its portrayal of the vindictive violence on both sides of the Civil War and the ambiguous legacy of the Bolshevik Revolution offended the censors. The film was banned. Askoldov was consigned to non-artistic work, stripped of party membership, and sent into internal exile away from Moscow. But he was not executed. He was not sent to a camp. Smith concludes, “In retrospect, it seems less important that the film was banned than that it was made.” (p. 187)

Most importantly of all, Askoldov survived long enough to witness Gorbachev’s perestroika and glasnost. By 1987, Askoldov could share his testimony of the Terror and the all too human tendency to violence, with Soviet, Russian and international audiences. Today, you can watch The Commissar, on YouTube, Комиссар (1967) Драма о войне.

Askoldov’s film was not the only suppressed act of poetic defiance released in the years of the late democratisation of the Soviet Union before its tragic collapse in 1991. Boris Pasternak, Vasily Grossman and Anna Akhmatova were among the suppressed books that were eagerly consumed by Russians, and as eagerly mythologised by Westerners.

Anna Akhmatova’s Requiem had been banned for years and was widely published at last during Gorbachev’s glasnost. It is a poem of the terror, by the terror, and for the people. I included the poem as one of my 100 poems to read during times of war, and next week will post an audio reading of this great poem, since it deserves a post of its own.

Today let me finish my stories of terror, violence and poetic defiance in Russian history, with the extraordinary courage in the composition of Akhmatova’s Requiem. This story may stand as an admonishment of all who compare contemporary conditions in Russia, or the West, with Stalinist totalitarianism; just as the poem itself admonishes all who diminish the manifold suffering of the Terror.

Akhmatova composed her poems during and after the Terror under the assumption of constant surveillance. She knew she could not be published. Worse yet, Akhmatova feared to write the poems down, for herself or anyone else. She saw dangers and spies everywhere. She refused to recite her poems aloud, even in her apartment in Fountain House in St Petersburg, now the site of a wonderful literary museum. She developed a secret practice to communicate her poems to a select group of friends, including Lidia Chukovskaya. They had all learned the Russian custom of memorizing poetry. They used this skill to defy the Stalinist state. Chukovskaya recalled:

Anna Andreevna [Akhmatova], when visiting me, recited parts of ‘‘Requiem’’ also in a whisper, but at home in Fontanny House did not even dare to whisper it; suddenly, in mid-conversation, she would fall silent and, signalling to me with her eyes at the ceiling and walls, she would get a scrap of paper and a pencil; then she would loudly say something very mundane: ‘‘Would you like some tea?’’ or ‘‘You’re very tanned’’, then she would cover the scrap in hurried handwriting and pass it to me. I would read the poems and, having memorized them, would hand them back to her in silence. ‘‘How early autumn came this year,’’ Anna Andreevna would say loudly and, striking a match, would burn the paper over an ashtray.

Quoted in Nancy K. Anderson, The word that causes death’s defeat : poems of memory / Anna Akhmatova; translated, with an introductory biography, critical essays, and commentary (2004), p. 82

It was, dare I say it, a burning archive of memory.

‘‘Eleven people knew Requiem by heart, and not one of them betrayed me,’’ Akhmatova later recalled. Here is its tear-inducing first page of memory, recalling the Yezhovian terror of 1936 to 1938.

As Clendinnen wrote in Reading the Holocaust, “Whatever the virtues of novelists and historians, it is still the poets who say most, and most succinctly.”
