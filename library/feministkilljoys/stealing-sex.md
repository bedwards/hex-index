I was glad to learn that Penguin was releasing some of Andrea Dworkin’s books as [modern classics](https://www.penguin.co.uk/authors/163419/andrea-dworkin). I was glad for many reasons. One was that we would now have *closer to hand* evidence that radical feminists have offered strong critiques of the idea of biological sex.

This paragraph is from Dworkin’s *[Woman Hating](https://www.penguin.co.uk/books/468277/woman-hating-by-dworkin-andrea/9780241735954)*,

*Hormone and chromosome research, attempts to develop new means of human reproduction (life created in, or considerably supported by, the scientist's laboratory), work with transsexuals, and studies of formation of gender identity in children provide basic information which challenges the notion that there are two discrete biological sexes. That information threatens to transform the traditional biology of sex difference into the radical biology of sex similarity. That is not to say there is one sex, but that there are many. The evidence which is germane here is simple. The words “male” and “female,” “man” and “woman,” are used only because as yet there are no others.*

Dworkin’s arguments are important but not exceptional. Feminists from many different intellectual traditions have suggested that the division between the sexes is a social division that is mostly masked as a natural one.

Still, I rather suspect I was foolish to be glad, at least for this reason. The feminists who argue that is sex is real and immutable– sometimes called “gender critical” feminists – have shown over and over again that they are willing to ignore any evidence that disrupts their core claims even if that means ignoring so much feminist history.

Having evidence is not sufficient. We already had evidence.

The argument that feminism depends on accepting biological sex as a “given” has a function. It is how “gender critical” feminists present critiques of sex as foreign to feminism. This is the story they have told and sold. Having more evidence that it is false will not get us very far if the point of the story is not to be true but to hold.

What’s the story? That until postmodernists and queer theorists and trans people came along there was just plain old sex. It was common sense. Not contested.

AND IT HAS BEEN STOLEN!

[Sarah Franklin](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/13505068211065138a) has described “gender critical” feminists as “feminism’s Brexiteers.”

*It remains a* *stunningly accurate description.*

Brexit too was premised on a fantasy of a past when we had control and of a future when we will have control.

Brexit was another best-selling story of stolen identities.

Another disaster.

Brexit too was premised on creating a threat, the others who had taken what was ours and will keep taking it unless we “take back control.”

And what has Brexit really meant? We have had to pay a very high price for way too many lies with the loss of way too many freedoms.

This story of stolen sex is how hostility toward trans and queer people is masked by the very form in which it is enacted. It is how trans and queer are treated as threats to feminism – as if moving toward a more expansive understanding of sex and gender is to move away from feminism.

When “gender critical” feminists identify queer and trans people as threatening feminism by stealing sex, however, the hostility does not always come out.

Nor the loss of freedoms.

Many readers will know about [The Sullivan Review](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/independent-review-of-data-statistics-and-research-on-sex-and-gender) published on 19 March 2025, which took pretty much this narrative form, albeit with some weak qualifications and additions: our data (on sex) has been stolen and we need to take it back, take back control.

Five years earlier, in 2020, Alice Sullivan published [an article](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/13645579.2020.1768346) on sex, gender and the census in which she made the following claim:

“As the UK’s most high-profile population survey, changes to the census are likely to influence other data collection exercises. If the decision to reframe the sex question in terms of self-declared gender identity is replicated in other surveys, we *are likely to lose data on sex more widely*, with detrimental implications for policy-making, research and analysis.”

The article is written in the dry review style you would expect from a social scientist. But then, at the end, something else happens. She claims, with reference to the postmodernists, “they are coming for our questionnaires.”

By making “postmodernists” the threat, Sullivan conveniently avoids discussing the reasons that critiques of science and empiricism have been *internal* to feminism (that feminist focus on the politics of knowledge, the production of reality, how surveys are not neutral devices, etc.).

But my point is not just that critique is made external to feminism.

This statement “they are coming for our questionnaires” does not belong in an academic article. It is emotive and inflammatory.

But it is useful that it is there.

It is the moment the mask comes off.

The point of the story.

The postmodernists, the queer theorists, trans activists, they are the bogey men from your nightmares. They are stealing sex. Our data. Our reality. They don’t believe in reality. Or data. So, they are stealing ours!

In her article, Sullivan also explains how the loss of data on sex has or will come about. In the abstract, she writes that she will be addressing “the difficulties of discussing the collection of data on sex in a climate of policy capture and silencing.” In the conclusion of the article, she again uses that term “policy capture.” She borrows its application from Murray and Hunter Blackburn (independent researchers who are also “gender critical” campaigners) to explain the role of “genderist” organisations who have meetings behind closed doors to influence policy without due process. The evidence of “policy capture” provided by the paper is one example she calls a case study (but is really about a letter written by one of the author’s own colleagues) and an example from her own experience. I should not need to comment here on the inadequacy of this evidence to justify the claims made. It would not take us very long to gather a mountain of evidence of how “gender critical” lobbyists have had meetings with policy makers, some of which have happened behind closed doors, and most of which were not offered to any organisations supportive of trans people.

Any equality campaigns that push for the development of new policies and practices can be called “policy capture.” Anti-feminists can and do call the development of policies against sexual harassment “policy capture.” It is a term typically used to stigmatise equality work as sinister campaigns intended to promote the interests of a specific social group over everyone else rather than as campaigns to redress the historic reasons that some social groups do not participate fully or equally in workplaces or public life. Feminists are repurposing anti-feminist tools to stigmatise campaigns by other minority groups. This is evidence of how such feminists do not view trans people as a minority group fighting for their human rights, equality or dignity in the workplace.

I cite this article because it is evidence. It shows us that by 2020, many years before the Sullivan Review was even commissioned, the author had already reached that conclusion: that sex was being erased because of the corrupting influence of campaigns for trans equality. That is *not* how research is meant to be conducted even when it is based primarily on reviews of existing sources. We begin not with answers but questions. When you already have answered the question, it affects what data is collected and how it is arranged.

The research did involve an open [call for submissions](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/ioe/departments-and-centres/ucl-social-research-institute/research/review-data-statistics-and-research-sex-and-gender#Call%20for%20evidence) in which the following statement was made:

*Do you have an example or examples of UK data collection on sex and/or gender identity which you perceived as inadequate or flawed? If so, we invite you to submit them for the review. We will ask you to provide your examples (ideally including screenshots of any relevant questionnaire items) and some basic supporting information about what the data collection exercise was and why you perceived it to be flawed or inadequate.*

Because the person who initiated that call was herself a “gender critical” feminist who had already framed “gender identity” as “policy capture,” it is clear who is the intended audience. Note how the use of the terms “flawed and inadequate” (used twice) are putting the problem (or the perception there’s a problem) into the question. An open question would be phrased differently, asking about people’s experiences of collecting data on sex and gender. An open question however could only really have been asked by a researcher who was not already a key actor in a movement that has already made “gender identity” a name for a problem.

Sullivan is on the advisory board of [Sex Matters](https://sex-matters.org/). Yes, that anti-trans lobby group that basically claimed in [a letter](https://sex-matters.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/20250428-Letter-to-Sir-James-Mackey-and-Wes-Streeting.pdf) to James Mackey, CEO of the NHS, that transition is pointless and that trans people should not bother to exist now that their campaign to stop trans people from participating in spaces that correspond to their assumed sex has been successful (“*Children under the care of all gender services, both NHS and private, must now be told that they will never be able to access spaces or services for the opposite sex, no matter what legal or medical steps they take now or in the future”).*

The acceptance of the Sullivan review as if it was an “independent” piece of research rather than an ideologically motivated tool produced by a well-funded campaign has been one of many recent disasters for feminism, for justice and human rights, as well as for LGBTQIA+ communities in the UK.

The argument that sex has been stolen is not specific to the review and its back story. Many “gender critical” feminists use such narratives of theft though they are rarely made quite so explicitly – that trans and non-binary people and queer people, even people with blue hair, are stealing their spaces and identities.

But I think it is important how words or terms are used in this story of theft. I noticed back in the late 1980s that “gender” tended to be used more than “sex” on equal opportunities forms. This was before “queer theory” had become part of the theoretical language or “gender identity,” part of our political vocabulary. My own view is that people tend to use “gender” rather than “sex” because it is seen as more polite (sex evokes bodies and acts). That people tend to use these words interchangeably tells us that the distinction between them is not clear. But the “gender critical” campaigners now treat each and every instance of the use of the word *gender* as evidence of a sinister campaign to replace women’s rights with trans rights. Take for example [this exchange](https://x.com/zeno001/status/1188514403886534657) on social media. Gender critical campaigners claimed that by using the word *gender*, the Garden Court Chambers is concerned only with “trans rights,” and not “women’s rights” or “LGB rights.”

Gender itself is treated as a foreign imposition.

Gender as foreign not feminist.

Gender as foreign to LGB, too.

The Sullivan Review states that sex, understood as a biological binary, should be the default on all forms of data collection, M or F. Contrast Sullivan’s 2020 statement about the potential loss of data on sex if it is not the default, with this [statement:](https://docs.google.com/document/d/e/2PACX-1vRXXLr0Nf8OvUg0idwnX3zJJeB-Bz9u_2fBYZyJQF6RkXrk9YXqPO6bFxfNLo8SkPO-53c0ufv0HqV1/pub)

The term “biological sex” has been used to sort all people into one of two groups. However, a strict, binary categorisation is an over-simplification. An individual’s “sex” is in fact made up of a *collection* of characteristics, including external genitalia, secondary sex characteristics, gonads, chromosomes, and hormones, and may be better described as bimodal. Each of these traits can vary beyond two categories and may or may not coincide with other measures of sex within the same individual. Thus, a binary, immutable model of sex is not capable of capturing biological diversity present in human populations, **and as a result it is an unsafe basis for policy**.” (Biology is not binary: a letter from biologists, doctors, and other experts to Bridget Phillipson, Minister for Women and Equalities).

Note I am not being bold. The emphasis was in the original: **an unsafe basis for policy**.

[Kevin Guyan](https://kevinguyan.com/2025/03/19/a-trumpian-approach-to-sex-and-gender-data/), the Director of the Gender + Sexuality Data Lab, notes that the Sullivan review was “premised on an invented past: the belief that – once upon a time – a clear, stable concept of sex existed that everyone agreed on.” He also points to the function of that invention: “Doing social research in the 1950s was easy before pesky queer participants got in the way. To demonstrate this claim, the Review leads with a graph that shows that 100 per cent of surveys held by the UK Data Service from 1946 to 1969 asked about ‘sex’. By the 2020s, less than 20 per cent of surveys asked about ‘sex’. For the Review authors, this steep decline is framed as a ‘loss of data on sex’ rather than the more simple explanation that language used to describe what is understood as ‘sex’ and/or ‘gender’ has changed since the mid-1940s.”

Guyan adds, “The mistake of equating ‘terminology’ with ‘concept’ is a rookie historical error. For example, in [my research on LGBTQ data](https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/queer-data-9781350230729/), if I found that most sexual orientation surveys from the 1990s asked whether the respondent identified as ‘homosexual’ now ask whether the respondent identifies as ‘gay’, I would not conclude that ‘homosexuals’ cease to exist just because the language had changed.”

Feminism has been crucial not just to how sex has been problematised but to the availability of different terms by which people can now describe themselves. That story of *more* gets in the way of a story that sells, of what has been taken away, not added, *but subtracted*.

It is important to remember here that surveys depend on self-identification. Terms used in surveys are likely to change; language changes, as society does. We are in the realm of ideology when changes to the status quo are framed as imposed from the outside or as coming from outsiders (such as “pesky queer participants” to borrow again from Guyan). I remember there was a time that I used to answer the race/ethnicity questions as “other.” Now there are more mixed-race/mixed ethnicity options, I tick a different box. That there are more different boxes can mean there are more different people. But it also reflects changes in social values and consciousness. One way people are not valued is by not counting them, which is not to say that being counted always means being valued.

Some people had to fight to be counted.

Others prefer not to be.

Maybe for some, even many, sex is relatively simple: tick that box, go through that door. But it is not simple for everyone.

And note that sex even when simple is still a task: we are being organised by ticking or entering. Common sense can be a history of how we become used to something, which is why claiming sex is common sense (the Supreme Judgement is often called “a victory for common sense”) is to make a point about history not nature. I will return to this point. And possibly write a book about it.

They claim that it is not anti-trans (or anti-queer) to talk about sex as real. I agree!

But it is not just that sex is treated as real. It is that gender is treated as not real.

And remember, the association of gender with trans people has *stuck.*

Sex then comes to *stand for* or *stand in* for reality.

So, trans people come to be understood not only as alienated from reality, but as *stealing it.*

Stealing *facts*, even.

Take, for example, Kathleen Stock’s [statements](https://www.judiciary.uk/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/miller-v-college-of-police-judgment.pdf) as expert witness at an Employment Tribunal in 2020. She is trying to explain that what some people consider *transphobic*, is, in fact, just stating the facts. She makes her argument by contrasting racist and transphobic speech:

*Where an utterance is perceived to be racist, it usually contains some identifiable pejorative element which explains that perception, so that it is not reasonably interpretable merely as straightforward, non-evaluative description. For instance, racist utterances might involve: a slur, such as the N-word, conventionally expressing contempt; mocking epithets designed to ridicule; or other statements expressing personal disapproval.”*

Stock argues that in contrast

*expressions such as “Trans women aren’t women”: contain no pejorative, expressive, mocking, or disapproving elements.” She adds, “In the mouths of many people, these utterances are intended to convey, and be heard as simple descriptions of observable facts; that is they are intended to be fact-stating and non-evaluative utterances, along the lines of “water boils at 100 degrees” or “pillar boxes in the UK are red.” For many English speakers, “woman” is strictly synonymous with “biologically female” and “man” with “biologically male.” For these speakers, therefore, given the accompanying true belief that trans women are biologically male, to say that “trans women are men” and “trans women aren’t women” is simply to neutrally state facts.*

Stock’s evidence is written with the confidence that comes from a certain style of analytical philosophy, allowing her to make claims about what other people think or do without talking to them. I doubt that she had done any empirical research to justify the claim that for “many English speakers” woman is “strictly synonymous with biologically female.” I am happy to be corrected. I also doubt that “biologically” would be used in quite this way by many people if they were asked the question about how to define *women* (that use of the word “biologically” is rather recent, which is precisely why I argued the Supreme Court’s [argument](https://substack.com/home/post/p-161608720) that they did not need to define the word *biological* was premised not on common use but an assumption of it).

To define words is to pull them out of everyday use. It is probably rather hard for people to say exactly what they mean by *sex* or *gender* without resorting to crass or crude observations. Indeed, it is often social change that leads people to enter into discussions about definition. Usually, feminists lead the way in opening up conversations about the meaning of words because of our commitments to social change. We introduced gender neutral language. And gender inclusive language. Sometimes the terms *we don’t think about* are the ones that shape worlds in problematic ways (including sexist ways).

Putting that issue to one side, my own view is that Stock’s statements about the nature of racist and transphobic speech and the difference between them are all false. Racist statements can take the form of obviously pejorative statements (such as “Paki,” a term that has become more commonly used, appearing on fascist placards alongside old Tory policy platforms like “stop the boats”). Transphobic speech can also be pejorative (such as “trannies” or “men in dresses”). However so much or even most racist speech is not pejorative. It can often be prefaced with statements such as “I am not a racist” or “my best friend is Black” and be followed by an apparently neutral statement of fact (for example about the number of immigrants in a country or that England is red post-boxes and white people).

Racism and transphobia often operate through the logic of stranger danger: rendering a group of others, those who not us or not like us, threats to our existence. Perhaps the hostility towards strangers, those who are out of place, dangerous also, is predicated on what they might steal: *they might take away our red post-boxes, even.*

How far we are now from queer use, what I also sometimes call diversity work, our collective efforts to open institutions up by recognising how they have been restricted as being only for some to use.

That is one way of thinking about what “gender critical” feminists are fighting for: a return to restrictions. The more they succeed, the more freedoms we give up. Hence so many “gender critical” feminists are now on *righteous rampage*s. All puffed up with too many places to go.

Speaking of puff, when Alice Sullivan was in t[he paper](https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/2025/03/30/alice-sullivan-transgender-donald-trump-wes-streeting/), she said she was made uncomfortable by how she shares views with Trump. “As a life-long Leftie, it feels uncomfortable to be put in the position of agreeing with Trump. But the fact is that he is simply saying that there are two sexes and that this matters, for example in prisons and sports. If Donald Trump says that the earth is round, should Leftists claim it is flat just to avoid being on the same side as him? This kind of tribal thinking has been horribly damaging to the Left. The idiotic positions that the Democrats took on these issues helped to gift the election to Trump.”

Of course, Sullivan put herself in that exact position of agreeing with Trump. And Trump’s views on sex and gender are explicitly linked not just to his anti-trans and ant-queer politics but his views on abortion and the rejection of that key feminist concept of bodily autonomy, and thus I would argue, *with how he is a predator/ protector of women*.

Remember: [he will protect women](https://feministkilljoys.substack.com/p/a-noisy-part) “whether they like it or not.”

Protecting women becomes about protecting *sex* not just as a category but as property, whether it belongs to a person or to a space.

The people we need protecting from are assumed to “the others.” The immigrants. The strangers. Those who are not us. Violence is treated as originating with the outsider. That is why, when someone who is “not one of us” is violent, the problem is made one of culture. When it’s one of us, it’s one not us: the problem is made individual, an exception not the norm.

Queer and trans people have historically been stigmatised as “the others,” those who threaten the safety and well-being of children.

Indeed, Sullivan herself wrote on [social media](https://x.com/ProfAliceS/status/1577217249861332992) that paedophilia was “a feature not a bug” of queer theory because of the “queering” of boundaries. Last I heard that the most unsafe place for children was in the traditional, nuclear family. Last I knew the patriarchal idea that children are property was central to the history of the abuse of children.

Yes, queering boundaries might just be safer for children (as might listening to children when they tell us who they understand themselves to be).

I still want to ask: what has led to it becoming acceptable for academics to make such homophobic statements? (And yes, associating queer theory with *paedophilia* is plain old homophobia). I remember giving an annual lecture for [qUCL](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/lgbtq-research/) in 2018 to a packed auditorium (I was speaking on queer use), with so many students and academics who were engaged in and by queer theory. Alice Sullivan was making such a statement about and to her colleagues and students. When we use the term TERF to describe some “gender critical” work (mostly inaccurately, as they are mostly not radical feminists) we are heard as using a slur. But they can associate the body of our work with paedophilia?

**It is not just that the hostility has come out.**

**It comes out because it is not seen as hostility.**

**It comes out because it has been permitted to come out.**

Returning to Trump, we might note as well how his sex = “biological truth” can be linked to his wider attacks on DEI, treating the efforts to counter historic and structural inequalities as restrictions on freedom.

It is not surprising that many “gender critical” feminists in the UK are leading similar attacks on EDI. This [letter](https://lucaf.org/viewdocument.php?id=6) (and [briefing notes](https://lucaf.org/viewdocument.php?id=5)) written against the incorporation of commitments to equality and inclusion into understandings of research environments is just one recent example.

The story of stolen sex quickly becomes one of stolen institutions. Stolen culture.

Yes, they are telling *that* story that EDI is an attack on “academic freedom.”

The anti-EDI position amongst “gender critical” feminists and their allies probably came about because of the critiques of Stonewall’s trans inclusionary policies. In the briefing notes, when reflecting on the composition of the panel tasked with assessing People, Culture and the Environment, they single out two researchers who’ve been critical of trans exclusionary feminism, one who used the term TERF in tweets, the other who is described as having “appeared to attack J.K. Rowling” for writing on a blog, “From government interventions to infamous authors being the poster child for a regressive movement, it is a toxic and challenging landscape.” Perhaps by *academic freedom* they mean not just the freedom to keep doing more and more politically motivated trans exclusionary research, but to stop other people from questioning or criticising or even describing the landscape it has helped to create. That landscape is increasingly hard to disentangle from the caricaturing of EDI itself. One of the founding members of the London Universities Council for Academic Freedom, which drafted both the letter and briefing notes, is John Armstrong, a mathematician from Kings, whose recent “[rant](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A58PFmGLkEk)” against EDI, I discussed in [an earlier post](https://feministkilljoys.substack.com/p/oh-cruel-world).\* In this speech, Armstrong was able to joke about Black history month, about trans people and disabled people, about suicide and self-harm, by channelling the humour into a critique of the “stupidity” of EDI.

The briefing notes describe “decolonisation” and “gender identity theory” as “highly contested frameworks.” They suggest that the merits of “the Athena Swan scheme and the Race Equality Charter, the creation of safe spaces and the inclusion of EDI activities in the promotion process” should all be debated. Of course, if they actually consulted the substantial literature on equality and diversity by feminists written over the past twenty years, they will realise we have been having that debate. That literature would have offered them another way of understanding the history of equality and diversity work in universities. They might have learnt about our collective feminist efforts to embed equality and diversity into the culture of institutions, and how we encountered so much institutional resistance to change. Some of this literature - including my own book, [On Being Included: Racism and Diversity in Institutional Life](https://www.dukeupress.edu/on-being-included) as well as the work of [Kalwant Bhopal](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10734-022-00835-7) -is based on actual research with diversity and equality practitioners. We can develop a better understanding of the difficulties we face in making research environments more open, equal and inclusive if we talk to people who are doing the work.

Many of us have offered critiques of diversity and equality for what they do not do, but without losing sight of why they matter, why we need to make them matter, to the social and educational missions of universities. Feminist academics (especially feminists of colour) have also ended up doing institutional work, joining diversity committees and equality task forces, often without having that work recognised as intellectual as well as political labour. We have had to push for diversity and equality to matter including in promotion cases. We have had to push against the neutrality of terms such as “excellence” and “merit.” We have had to push to show that many of the terms by which research has been valued historically are not only individualistic, but tend to reinforce social hierarchies. We have had to push against cultural norms and educational ideals that have historically led to the conflation of bullying with brilliance.

**That inequalities are bad for research is an old feminist position. Elitism, also.**

All that history, **gone.**

I may return to how that letter recruited some people into a Trumpian-inflected discourse of equality = loss of freedom and excellence in due course. Or I might not. I am rather tired of reading statements signed by people who have the skills to dissect the story rather than buy into it.

Also note in Sullivan’s discomfort that she has become Trump’s ally is a description of any positions other than hers and his as “idiotic.” The idiocy of recognising that some people are not at home in the sex they were assigned or that gender can be more expansive? The idiocy of other people’s dreams of freedom?

I think too of how Kathleen Stock treats Judith Butler’s critique of the naturalness of sex in *[Material Girls](https://www.hachette.co.uk/titles/kathleen-stock/material-girls/9780349726595/)*. Stock suggests that if you view sex as constructed (which remember as a view does not originate with queer theory; it is also a radical feminist view), that there are “not two naturally pre-given, stable sexes” also means saying, “There is no climate change” or “no molecules, atoms or quarks.”

This is just embarrassing (perhaps especially the point about climate change which is after all about the impact of human beings and their labour on the natural world). To say *something* is constructed in a certain way is not to say *everything* is constructed in the same way. To say something is constructed is not to deny that it is material. To say something is constructed is not to deny it exists. It is to point to *how some things come to exist*. When we say a building is constructed, it does not mean we think it will fall down. The materials used for building buildings are used for a reason. Yes, that buildings are constructed has something to do with the nature of our bodies and our needs. But they are still constructed.

Judith Butler has given us many resources to critique that critique of their work. In *[Who’s Afraid of Gender](https://www.penguin.co.uk/books/452776/whos-afraid-of-gender-by-butler-judith/9781802061062)*[,](https://www.penguin.co.uk/books/452776/whos-afraid-of-gender-by-butler-judith/9781802061062) Butler comments on the reliance on common sense reasoning in the anti-gender movement, “there is a pounding on the table that goes along with the insistent repetition of the claim of purely biological differences, as if pounding and repetition makes it so.” Tables are here the recipient of a gesture. Tables are also *enlisted* to demonstrate the realness of many things including categories such as sex. [Kathleen Stock](https://unherd.com/2022/04/five-rules-for-dealing-with-transactivists/) encourages her readers to “set boundaries” by starting with small claims like “tables can’t be chairs” (ignoring anyone who calls you the “furniture police”) before explaining why we need “mutually exclusive names for the two human sexes in order to talk clearly about them.”

It is striking that tables and chairs, which are mostly human artefacts, products of human labour, are used to demonstrate a natural distinction. If anything, tables, as materials, or human artifacts, or as things that can be used (a stone can be a chair if it is large enough to sit on, or a table if it is flat enough to lean on) might give us a rather different view on the facts of the matter. In *[Capital](https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/download/pdf/Capital-Volume-I.pdf)* [](https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/download/pdf/Capital-Volume-I.pdf)Marx noted: “it is as clear as noon-day that man, by his industry, changes the forms of the material furnished by nature in such a way as *to make them useful to him*. The form of wood, for instance, is altered by making a table out of it for all that, the table continues to be that common every-day thing, wood”. The table becomes a thing, alteration as the acquisition of shape, a product of human labour; there’s a hand in the table, however material it remains. Marx also suggests “Every useful thing is a whole composed of many qualities: it can therefore be useful in many ways. The discovery of these ways and hence of the manifold uses of things is the work of history.”

Sex too is the work of history. That’s what makes sex material. Also, handy.
