“From Birzeit University, in the heart of occupied Palestine, we are screaming with Gaza. We need everyone from Palestine, the Arab nations, and the peoples of the world to amplify the cry of Gaza…. Palestinians no longer need to offer proof of the enemy’s savagery – the dead neither provide nor require evidence. The bodies of the living – of the displaced, besieged, sick, wounded, hungry, and thirsty – still resist, driven by a will to live. The genocidal will of the Zionist state, however, has not stopped at starvation; it went further by turning food into a weapon through death traps disguised as ‘aid distribution.’… The shame borne from the world’s failure in the face of Gaza’s starvation must become the shame of shame itself – to erase the disgrace of complicity with genocidal will, and to initiate saving Gaza as a step toward saving our shared humanity, which has suffered greatly from the continuation of this genocide.” Union of Teachers and Employees of Birzeit University, Occupied Palestine, July 24, 2025.*

To hear the wisdom of these words is to scream with Gaza.

Screaming with.

Solidarity is a scream.

A long piercing sound. For however long it takes. The sound of refusal.

Raising not just our voices. Everything.

Until: everyone.

We need all of the world to be “screaming with Gaza.”

We scream more because we are not getting through.

No evidence, enough.

Even the evidence of death.

The dead “do not require nor provide evidence.”

They are evidence. But still: not enough.

The genociders and their enablers smile. Shrug. Their indifference not just callous but calculated. Not indifference really, more glee.

See! The brutality of power for all to see.

The “shame of shame itself,” to hear the wisdom of these words is to commit to action.

To commit to using what we have, our voices, our resources, our materials; whatever is to hand, to be of use, to get behind it, everything behind it, that scream, long and loud and piercing, as long as it takes, saying no to business as usual, combining our forces, doing what has to be done to stop genocide, stop the starving of Gaza, where even “food is a weapon,” where a search for sustenance, however small, to avoid death, risks death.

There are no words for this. It is beyond evil, even.

You might feel powerless. That it is too late.

It is way too late for way too many.

But way too late is going to have to be soon enough.

And so, scream. Do not wait. Hesitate.

Everything you send out: can be that sound.

Nabil Echchaibi wrote of “the scream for Gaza.”

Writing can be screaming.

“Do not be fooled by the silence of print,” he states.

“This writing clamours to be screamed.”

Asking, “What sound should letters make when your fingers feel like stones on the keyboard?”

What sound do these words make?

Heavy fingers. Weighed down by history.

Louder, when said more or said by more.

That was my task in listening to complaints: to make them louder by bringing them together.

Many meanings of the word complaint relate to sound.

In early uses, to complain had the sense of “to cry out” a complaint as a lament, a lament as “wailing, moaning, weeping.”

To complain can be to seep as well as weep.

It is not only humans that are heard as complaining. The creak of a ship has been called complaint, in the sense of “to strain or groan from over-straining.” Complaints can be the sound of being over-stressed or under pressure.

To listen to a complaint is to hear these sounds, these moans, these groans, as speech, as saying something, or doing something.

Sound matters because of how it travels.

The sound of complaint can be sharp and piercing. Or dull and low.

Sound can be involuntary.

One woman was in the middle of a meeting, when she received yet another harassing email. She let out a sound, eehhhhh, that alerted other people to what was going on. It was a queer complaint, a guttural sound.

The sound of frustration. However involuntary, that sound was an action. The eehhhhh tells us that she could not take it anymore.

Nor would she.

Sound can be information. In coming out, how it comes out, we learn what we cannot accept. Will not.

Sound can be the transition from private to public.

It can be how others are alerted: to what happened, that this happened.

Zehay Liva Bocretsion wrote to me about how she turned her complaints about racism into songs, which she sent to cultural institutions such as museums. She explained, “I got the idea because in Danish klagesang (complaint song) carries both the meaning of elegies, the retelling of a tragedy, and a more sarcastic meaning, like someone who is just wailing on about all the things not going their way.” Zehay sings her complaints with a “monotonousness” that “becomes a point in itself,” so she can “express the matter-of-fact ways in which a lot of people try to disregard the complaints I have had.”

We find other ways to send an alert. To make some noise.

Sometimes a scream is a sound we can only make when together.

Some resistance is barely audible.

Because what you would scream about can be what stops you from screaming.

That does happen; we know that happens.

Inaudible does not mean unexpressed.

You have to open your ear to hear.

The barely said.

The strains, the stresses, the cracks, the croaks.

I hear here a poem by Jasmin Kaur, SCREAM.

Scream so that

“another sister will not have to

dry her tears wondering where

in history she lost her voice.”

The point of becoming louder is so she, another sister, the one who comes later or after, will not be left wondering how, also where, also when, she lost her voice.

We need you to know we said no. For tears to flow.

Not to lose our voices in history.

To use our voices to make history.

Banging the pots and pans, making noise, clatter, shatter, the pots emptied of food filled with sound.

So they can hear the horror of empty pans.

Sound made in another way: the snap of a slogan; the repetition of a chant on a demo.

Free Free Palestine.

So many chants, so much violence.

What can you hear?

I hear here the sharp sound of the direct-action group Sisters Uncut setting off 1000 rape alarms to mark the anniversary of the Clapham Common Virgil for Sarah Everard, murdered by a police officer in 2021. The police had beaten and arrested many women on the day.

I hear here the rallying cry of feminists in Argentina, saying Ni una menos (Not one less), a “collective scream against machista violence,” words that spread to other places, other collectives, repeated, echoed, amplified, passing between bodies, travelling across time and also space: transgenerational; transnational.

We might hear anger in that no, that refusal, but also a lament, the sound of grief.

I will always hear the words of Chicana-Palestinian feminist Sarah Ihmoud, as she writes of speaking through grief, sharing with us words shared by Mona Ameen, a young Palestinian scholar in Gaza.

She asks Mona if she has any messages for women and feminists around the world. Mona answers, “Keep posting and posting and posting about us . . . keep us in your prayers.” Ihmoud invites us to break out of “this غصة/ ghassa, this lump in our throat that keeps us from speaking, and to speak loud and courageously into the wind.”

What makes it difficult to speak is why we need to do so.

Posts, prayers, poems, post-its, posters: we need them all.

Every little bit. To create a pile.

We need to shout no now, so we can hear each other over what Ihmoud calls “the noise of complacency.”

Time can be like the wind, a no blown about. We don’t always know when it will be heard. Or how. Flying kites of solidarity, lifting hopes, light enough to be carried.

Movement in the air.

We are louder not just when we are heard together but when we hear together.

When sound moves, sound builds.

A scream, a many; a momentum.

Until:

Free Palestine.
