What if the rooms in which you speak of how institutions are haunted by violent histories are haunted by those same histories?

To be haunted by violence is not just to bury it.

Violence buried is violence repeated.

What if those you are speaking *to* can’t make it into the room in which you are speaking?

Because of those same histories.

No, the title of this post is not a reference to [a song](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Room_Where_It_Happens) from the play, *Hamilton.*

It is about being in the room with violence.

Where the past is present.

In *[Shakespeare, Trauma and Contemporary Performance](https://www.routledge.com/Shakespeare-Trauma-and-Contemporary-Performance/Silverstone/p/book/9781138809123?srsltid=AfmBOoohhekrSMj54GecUGDa-umUBLwQXDuFFBhz4S990f_HEG-TbINJ)*, Catherine Silverstone analyses the programme accompanying *[Henry V](https://www.theguardian.com/stage/2003/may/14/theatre.artsfeatures2)*, (dir. Nicholas Hytner), played at The National Theater in 2003. She describes the programme as paying “homage to a pictorial history of representations of war in its inclusion of images from ancient, medieval and early modern cultures.” She states that “given Hytner’s interest in the play speaking to current events,” it is “striking” that the collection of images in the programme does not “include any of the military action in Afghanistan and Iraq.” However, she notes that the National Archives holds research materials used by the cast for rehearsal. This archive included images from the invasion of Iraq in 2003, which are, like all the other images, held “in clear plastic sleeves,” giving them the quality of “specimens.”

The photographs “by keeping sometimes violent moments open to scrutiny,” “make claims upon the viewer to acknowledge the violence of war.”

What Silverstone’s careful analysis shows is that the materials that might *force* acknowledgement of violence that is happening now are removed from the programme, from the room, the theater, the building.

**And so;**

**What they claim, we might not see.**

**There is violence in the removal of violence.**

It is important to me that any lectures I give (especially on complaint) can be heard live by those who are not in the room. Why? Because many people who have experienced the kinds of institutional violence I describe cannot be in the room because of that experience.

In listening to complaint, I had my ear to so many doors. I was listening to what happened in this room. Or that room.

Of how so many complaints end up in something akin to “clear plastic sleeves.”

Files. Boxes. Closets. Bins. Graves.

Put away.

But the complaints have not gone because of what goes on.

The complaints can be in the air.

**History can be stale air.**

I think of one participant in my study Mia who had to struggle through a meeting to discuss a new policy on bullying because she had been bullied.

A complaint can make it hard to get into the room. Or be in it.

And of Alice who gave her testimony in the same room she had been tone-policed and harassed in a meeting set up to discuss her complaint.

The same room.

And of Stephanie, who when describing being assaulted by a lecturer, he pushed himself on her against his office door, said “*it was an office a bit like this but without any glass.”*

An office a bit like this one. Where we were seated.

The room where it happened.

But no glass, harder to see out.

One section of *No is Not a Lonely Utterance* includes a discussion of research and activism. I share some of what I have learnt from sharing the research.

Some examples from the chapter:

*One time I had a seminar with students the day after I gave a lecture on complaint. The students told me that some of them could not attend the lecture as it was given in the same theater where the university had held a “town meeting” to discuss sexual harassment cases. It would have been triggering for them to go into that room to hear this work. That is how I ended up* not *addressing the people most affected by the forms of institutional power I was critiquing.*

I needed to find another room to share the research.

Even in virtual rooms we can encounter the violence.

*I was contacted by a student after giving a virtual lecture. She apologized for having left early and explained why she did, ‘because she saw the name of her abuser in the chat.’ Think about that: you have to leave a discussion of complaint because the person who abused you is in that discussion.*

*Another time a woman of colour was due to give the land acknowledgement for a virtual talk. I was told at the last minute that she was not able to do so. She contacted me after to explained why she had declined the invitation. She said, ‘I found it disingenuous to do so under emerging and struggling work of the institution to decolonise and to address white supremacy. Equity work is violent, as you know and I have had many undermine my position and work. I wanted to share, not as the burden is yours, but for truth and awareness. Folks listened to your words. Then enacted institutional violence.’*

I ended up speaking *to* institutions about the violences they keep enacting.

And so even: listening to my words was also how they kept enacting that violence.

I share many more instances in the book.

Each, a chance to learn.

It has kept happening. Since. It will happen again.

Too many rooms where it happened.

I know one way of surviving institutions is to create pockets within them to talk not just about the violence but the survival (which is where the joy comes in, and laughter, the world making).

I hope to find you in a pocket some day, in some way.

But mostly when the new book comes out, I will not be speaking in universities.

Because of all of the above.

I will be sharing the work in independent, feminist and queer bookstores.

No spaces are free from the ongoing violence and brutality of the times we are in.

We must name it. Speak of the genocide in Gaza. Speak of transphobia and the onslaught of violence against LGBTQIA+ communities. Speak out. Speak up.

And also: listen.

Give each other a hearing.

A *no* becomes louder not just when we say it together but when we hear it together.

*No* can be how we find others who say it.
