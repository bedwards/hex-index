In a powerful article, Nicky Bandini describes what it is like to be “a transgender person in the UK over the past two weeks,” waking up “daily to discussions on how your life must be made smaller.”

The equalities minister Bridget Phillipson used that word dignity for a promise made by Labour to give trans people “what previous administrations had denied.”

“Is this what ‘dignity’ means in 2025?” Bandini asks.

Or do Labour “not know the meaning of the word”?

Or do we have to wait to find out “what they think that word means”?

I hear her questions, each so sharp, rhetorically.

Yes, you can promise dignity when telling trans women they should use men’s toilets, to put themselves in a place where they risk being harassed or assaulted. We know they can because they did. Yes, you can promise dignity when telling some people they are not who they say they are. We know they can because they did.

Dignity: the worth and the value given to a person and a life, to a people and how they live. When you welcome a decision that harms the people to whom you have promised dignity because it gives you clarity, then you are making it clear what your promises are worth.

Or not worth.

When dignity is that promise, it is stripped of meaning.

When dignity is that promise, it covers over the truth, smiling over a hostile environment.

When I read Bandini’s important words, I was reminded of what happened when I gave a lecture on complaint. Viola, who had shared her story of complaint with me, was in the audience. I knew she was going to be there. I was conscious of what it meant to give back to her the words she had given to me. “It was only after the lecture,” Viola wrote later, “that I realized how undignified these complaint processes are, and how, yes, my dignity was stripped.” She explains, “In my dealings with the union, they had advised me at the time that my dignity at work had been breached, but that word did little then for me, as it felt like another procedural piece of jargon ‒ but when I felt a swell of pride at the lecture, indeed, when I felt a sense of dignity about it all, I realised that this must have been somewhat lost.”

When you have been stripped of dignity, the word dignity too is stripped of meaning, doing nothing, saying nothing. When we share our stories with each other, giving them to each other, we take something of ourselves back. We take something back for ourselves.

To bring complaints together can feel like that, giving each other a sense of dignity, restoring what has been lost, amidst so much hostility.

I wrote No is not a Lonely Utterance amidst so much hostility.

Hostility might not be so obvious unless it is directed against you.

Hostile environments are sometimes masked by the very policies we have against them. It is worth reminding ourselves that “dignity at work” is typically referenced in equality policies.

Dignity at work can be a promise or a pledge, to create a workplace environment where everyone is treated with respect, fairness, and without discrimination, harassment, or bullying.

A policy can be polish, words used because they are emptied of force.

The word diversity too can be a form of polish, a smile covering over the hostility of an environment. Those of us who embody diversity are supposed to smile. A woman of colour was dropped from the diversity committee “for mentioning things to do with race,” as if to bring race up is to bring hostility into the room.

The hostility was already there. How people are stripped not just of dignity but the words that would make sense of that experience.

When dignity is stripped, it is more than the word that has lost its meaning. For a Labour government to turn dignity into “a procedural piece of jargon,” an empty promise, means that it too has been stripped of meaning, stripped of its own history, how it came to be, what it came to do, losing the plot, the very point of being a Labour Party, there to support the workers, those who have to fight for dignity.

Parties, emptied.

Policies, too.

Many decisions by employment tribunals in the UK reference the fact that even when organisations have equality policies, yes, those that make dignity a promise, employers and employees do not seem to know about them. Take, for example, Rose Taylor’s case against Jaguar in 2017 for discrimination in relation to the protected characteristic of gender reassignment. Rose is non-binary. She had raised a complaint with HR after two of her colleagues referred to her as ‘it’. They responded, “Well, what else would you want them to call you?” The company had argued that her identity as non-binary did not fall under the Equality Act protected characteristic of gender reassignment. As part of the ruling, the tribunal stated:

It was very clear [Parliament] intended gender reassignment to be a spectrum moving away from birth sex, and that a person could be at any point on that spectrum . . . it was beyond any doubt that somebody in the situation of the Claimant was (and is) protected by the legislation because they are on that spectrum and they are on a journey which will not be the same in any two cases.

You might assume clarity requires narrowing meanings.

But it can often be achieved by pointing to a range of meanings.

Too often, clarity is conflated with the restriction or narrowing of meaning.

Lawyers Adam Cooke and Oscar Davies noted that Jaguar had pre-existing Equal Opportunities and Dignity at Work policies, but that no one in HR or management “knew they existed.” They concluded that the case was consequently “a prime example of how having a policy in place cannot, in and of itself, absolve an employer of liability when related grievances arise.” The implication of Cooke and Davies’ observation is that some employers use policies to absolve themselves of liability when “related grievances arise.” That might be why organisations respond to grievances by writing new policies. Perhaps they can then point to the policy as evidence they are doing something.

Creating evidence of doing something is not the same thing as doing it.

As one diversity practitioner observed, “Well I think in terms of the policies, people’s views are ‘we’ve got them now so that’s done, it’s finished.’” In her view, this was “even worse than having nothing, that idea in people’s heads that we’ve done race, when we very clearly haven’t done race.”

You can change a policy without changing the culture.

A policy can be how a culture does not change.

Even if a culture is not simply there, some inert thing, it can be experienced as if it is so, at least by those who do not fit. You encounter what the policy has not changed. You experience what does not appear. What appears is not what you experience.

I talked to Stephanie, a cis lesbian academic about the hostility she faced. She said, “They know enough not to express it openly ‒ though sometimes it comes out ‒ because they realize it is not OK.” She gave one example of being “all around a big table,” and then, during an icebreaker, someone said, “This is Larry, and Larry used to be a woman,” followed by laughter. In another instance, during a discussion about a colleague’s son who had someone over to stay, “it was a joke about whether it was a girl or a boy.” Stephanie said, “A colleague turned to look at me in recognition that I would find that difficult.”

Notice how much of this hostility takes the form of jokes about sex, whether someone is a boy or girl or was one. Transphobic and homophobic humour can be hard to tell apart probably because some people find it hard to tell us apart, those who are not cis, not straight.

When Stephanie was introduced as a lesbian head of department by a student, “there was some discussion of that with colleagues, like I had some banner to fly, pushing students to get involved with this.” Even identifying yourself as a lesbian can be heard as raising a flag.

Maybe you are being told that if you are a lesbian, it is better not to say you are.

One consequence of hostility is that some people try not to stand out. I sometimes call this work “institutional passing,” how we minimize the signs of our difference. I first used this term for how many Black and brown people described “softening” their appearances to counter perceptions they might be a problem in the workplace, perhaps by wearing or not wearing certain clothes. One woman of colour manager said that to wear a sari to work would be to “rock the boat.”

I know of many gender non-conforming people, cis and trans, who use dress as a deliberate strategy to try and fit in with the culture of their workplace, not because they do not want to be themselves but to lessen the likelihood of confrontation.

To survive hostile environments we might try to disappear.

Hostile environments can stop us talking about hostile environments.

And so then, when we talk about hostile environments, we are often judged as introducing hostilities that would not otherwise be there.

I have been thinking about how, over a decade ago now in February 2015, I wrote a blog, “You are Oppressing Us,” which questioned the oft-made claim that feminists were being censored by “trans activists.”

A “gender critical” feminist responded back then that “speaking is not the same as pointing a gun.”

Did I suggest or imply that speaking was the same as “pointing a gun”?

Of course not.

We are trying to make explicit the hostility in what was being said.

How the terms being used were loaded.

We heard how one “gender critical” author said that “the problem of transsexualism” is best dealt with by “morally mandating it out of existence.” We heard a “gender critical” feminist at an LGB Alliance conference objecting to the use of the term “gender identity extremist” because she said the term implied being trans was not, in itself, an extremist position. For her, it is extremist to say that trans people exist. When she said this, she was applauded by the audience. We heard a “gender critical” journalist make a claim that the amount of people who transition should be “reduced” as trans people are “a huge problem for a sane world.”

These are not exceptions; aberrations.

When people are stripped of dignity they become numbers, amounts; so many, too many, too much.

So yes, we challenged whether the call for conversation was friendly or whether conversation was what was being called for.

We point to the problem, we become it.

To make the hostility explicit, to cause it.

So many “gender critical” feminists claim not just to be friendly, but supportive of trans rights. One judgement by an Employment Tribunal, which found in support of a “gender critical” feminist, stated that she, the claimant, was supportive of trans rights. What was the evidence? That she said she was supportive of trans rights in one lecture and one tweet.

If the evidence that “gender critical” feminists support trans rights is that they say they support trans rights, then perhaps they say they support trans rights, so it can be used as evidence.

Creating evidence by saying something.

Not the same thing as doing something.

A policy, a smile; how polite.

Some of the most “polite” members of the “gender critical” movement (using their own self-description) have been signatories on the Women’s Human Rights Declaration (WHRC), which calls for the “elimination” of “the practice of transgenderism” as well as the repeal of the Gender Recognition Act.

It is possible to claim you are not hostile to trans rights whilst participating in a movement that has the explicit aim of removing them.

That is why I called the call to dialogue a false positive.

As a result of employment tribunals, gender critical beliefs are now protected. A criterion for a belief to be protected is that it has to be so certain that it would not be amended by any new developments or information. The fact that they “gender critical” beliefs are protected is evidence that they were not, as it were, “up for debate.”

It is trans and queer feminists who are, in fact debating sex and gender. And yes, we are still at it.

This was never about having a debate or a dialogue.

Dialogue was a useful word because of what it obscured. It helped to create the impression that “gender critical” feminists were a minority being censored rather than members of a majority doing what they could to take away the hard-fought-for-rights of a minority. It was about imposing a view, and a very conservative view, of biological sex.

The Supreme Court judgement has embedded that view of biological sex in an interpretation of the Equality Act (2010) whilst avoiding making explicit that that is what it was doing. By this I mean: it avoided making explicit that its use of the biological was a view of the biological. It managed to do this, as I argued in a previous post by a sleight of hand, both importing the word biological into the legislation, and then claiming it does not need to be defined.

Not so clear then.

What is most clarifying about this judgement is how it will be used. Or has been used.

Christopher Clannachan has observed that the judgement by limiting “the practical scope” of a gender recognition process, will lead to trans women having to out themselves as trans “when accessing public and services.” He argues that this limitation cannot “be squared with our international human rights commitments and trans people’s rights to privacy,” but that “in the absence of political will to ensure that trans people’s human rights are upheld, the European court may have to remedy this indignity.”

This absence of political will is an achievement.

The previous and current government did what they could to ensure the absence of political will, giving those involved in the “gender critical” movement (how a trans hostile or anti-trans movement appears from the front), positions of power and influence including in the EHRC. If Lord Sumption quickly clarified on April 18th that the judgement meant that trans women could be lawfully excluded from the single sex spaces not that they were obliged to be, the EHRC quickly ignored him. Six days later, they published their interim guidance, turning could into should: that single sex spaces for women should be restricted to “biological women.”

The FA has since banned trans women from participating in women’s football. And trans women and girls will no longer be able to play competitively in women’s cricket. This is devastating for women and girls. And they are fighting back. As Jon Holmes reports, at least 50 clubs for women and nonbinary players have issued statements thus far. He quotes from an interview with Goal Diggers FC’s Becky Taylor-Gill. She said, “I am a cisgender woman who plays in a team week in, week out, with trans members, and in leagues with other trans players. They [representatives of anti-trans organisations] do not speak for me. I would like to ask how many of them play in leagues at all, as there are a lot of people on Twitter and in the world right now who are saying, ‘this is just to protect women.’ I don’t need your protection.”

But the protectors continue with their glee, so close to hostility. Some of the responses to predictable articles from predictable newspapers about these bans show how the judgement is clearly being used to strip yet more dignity from trans people. They are men and boys! They were taking spaces that would otherwise be for our girls! That is what the judgement is doing. What it gets so wrong. So much of the hostility directed towards trans people takes the form of assertions they are not the sex they say they are; their claims to personhood, fraudulent; that they are lying, even criminal.

Earlier I noted that I wrote No is Not a Lonely Utterance in a hostile environment. I think of the accusation of fraudulence, and how many populations it catches. When politicians described the reform of the welfare system to make it harder to access benefits as “a moral mission,” they label those who need benefits, including disabled people, the poor and the unemployed, fraudsters. Consider too the figure of the bogus asylum seeker. The word bogus derives from counterfeit money, a “spurious coin,” slang for the counterfeit’s apparatus, the machine that makes such coins. It is not just that those who claim asylum are assumed to be bogus, to be passing their way into the nation through fraud, but that every asylum seeker is understood as a singular impress created by a machinery intended to defraud the system.

The effort some people have to make to establish they are not frauds has life consequences: a system becomes a hammer directed against those who are perpetually being rendered dubious because their bodies, their stories, their papers, are not in the right place. That is another way of describing hostile environments, as systems designed to make it harder for people to get what they need.

I said, back then, “There cannot be a dialogue when some at the table are in effect (or intent on) arguing for the elimination of others at the table.”

I would put it differently now. Because this was never about having a dialogue.

By “elimination” I was referring then to what is happening now: not recognising other people as existing or depriving them of what they need to exist.

The goal of this anti-trans movement is to change the law so trans people cannot access the public spaces and services they need to live their lives safely and with dignity. Once those laws are changed, those who are hostile to trans rights can argue that trans people should not exist because they will not have access to the public spaces and services they need to live their lives safely and with dignity.

In case this sounds like hyperbole, this is the exact claim made by transphobic hate group, Sex Matters, in response to a proposed trial for puberty blockers.

Citing both the Supreme Court Judgement and the EHRC interim guidance, they write:

Children under the care of all gender services, both NHS and private, must now be told that they will never be able to access spaces or services for the opposite sex, no matter what legal or medical steps they take now or in the future. Such children will, throughout their lives, need to use either the spaces and services designated for their own sex or an alternative provided for both sexes (mixed-sex or single-user) if available.

This means that the entire premise of treatment with puberty blockers for gender dysphoria has fallen away. The drugs are intended to block gender-distressed children’s natural puberty so that if they persist in cross-sex identification and progress to cross-sex hormones and perhaps surgery, they will “pass” better as the opposite sex. But passing as the opposite sex ceases to be a desirable goal to present to children when they will never be permitted to use spaces or services for the opposite sex, and may in future be restricted in employment in roles such as police officer or nurse which can involve intimate contact with others in situations where sex matters for those others’ human rights. Instead it becomes a major, permanent social problem for those young people, since their future lives and freedoms will be seriously curtailed by being legally barred from using the other sex’s spaces while appearing anomalous in those for their own sex.

This is it. Sometimes, the hostility comes out.

The truth, even. The mask lifts.

They are trying to stop it being possible to transition.

Trying to stop trans people from being.

To stop trans being.

No, you cannot have a dialogue with those who are trying to prevent you from existing.

Whether in intent or by effect.

Conversion therapy under another name.

So yes, we will keep fighting. Keep complaining. Saying no, together.

I think back to how Viola had her dignity restored by having her complaint heard as part of a collective.

That is what we need. To be a collective, hearing from each other.

And that is what is happening.

The biologists. The feminists. The historians. The geographers. Some of us doing our bit in our own way, working as we do, independently.

All of us, speaking back, trying to find a way through the mess, the damage caused by gifting some people the means to strip other people of their rights and their dignity.

None of us will have dignity, let alone humanity, when any of us are deprived of it. It will take a long hard fight to make dignity mean something again.
