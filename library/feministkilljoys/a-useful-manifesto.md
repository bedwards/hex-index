I remember when I first wrote “A Killjoy Manifesto,” one of the two conclusions of my 2017 book, *[Living a Feminist Life](https://www.dukeupress.edu/living-a-feminist-life)*. I had not intended to end the book with a manifesto: the book originally concluded with “lesbian feminism.” I rather suspect it was writing the book whilst fighting against the normalising of sexual harassment at my former institution that led to this change of shape.

The book needed to be snappier especially in its ending, to be more direct not just about the need for action but the nature of action.

Snap to it, Sara!

I wrote the manifesto very quickly; and it came out in the exact shape it has.

I feel like if feminist killjoys had a genre, manifesto would be it. But they don’t: have a genre, that is.

Writing takes a different shape when it comes from urgency.

It is never too urgent to write, but when the situation is urgent, time can slip through your fingers. Time can be what we don’t have.

You let the words out. Your body too. Roam free. If the urgency of the times we are in, the need to say *no to business as usual,* because Israel is conducting a genocide in Gaza **now**, does not stop us from writing, urgency travels into the writing.

And into you.

I always remember [Stuart Hall](https://msuweb.montclair.edu/~furrg/pursuits/hallcultstuds.html) describing Cultural Studies as “deadly serious.” He had been reflecting on the AIDS crisis. Hall notes that it might seem that Cultural Studies is rather pointless when people are dying **now**. Rather than dismissing the feeling that what we are doing is just “ephemeral,” Hall suggests we let ourselves be hit by it. We need to know how “little it registers, how little we've been able to change anything or get anybody to do anything. If you don't feel that as one tension in the work that you are doing, theory has let you off the hook.”

We are not off the hook.

We need to keep sharpening our tools because nothing we can say or do will be enough in the face of so much. An action will not be enough but nor is it nothing.

It is during the times Cultural Studies seems useless, that we need it the most.

Later, I wrote another manifesto.

I called it “A Useful Manifesto,” which is the title of this post. If the manifesto came out of me quickly, it did not come into the world. It was for my book, *[What’s the Use](https://read.dukeupress.edu/books/book/2629/What-s-the-Use-On-the-Uses-of-Use)*. In the end, I decided that book needed to end with *queer use*, which meant making an argument: how we have to work to stop the same old institutions being reserved for the same old people to use.

An argument: how you pick up so many trails.

Also things.

Pick them up, pull them together.

*The book was filled with many used things - old books and old bags as well as well used paths.*

Old books: holders of wisdom. Or at least some of them.

I have been thinking recently about what it means to be learning from Edward Said.

From all he left behind.

I draw on Said in the final chapter of *[No is Not a Lonely Utterance](https://www.penguin.co.uk/books/470363/no-is-not-a-lonely-utterance-by-ahmed-sara/9780241759271)*, when writing about the British empire as an empire of closets: so much violence buried. In [“Zionism from the Standpoint of its Victims,”](https://www2.hawaii.edu/~aoude/ES455B/said/Said_Ch.5.pdf) Said stated, “Imperialism was the theory, colonialism the practice, of changing the uselessly occupied territories of the world, into the useful new versions of European metropolitan society.”

It is clear, I think, and how and why *useless* is a powerful tool in justifying the stealing or occupation of land.

But not just uselessness: also use and used and unused.

Said also describes how Palestine was appropriated by being rendered a place that needed a care-taker, a place that was unappreciated as well as unused. Through close readings of Zionist narratives, he shows how Palestine is rendered “a *whole* territory essentially unused, unappreciated, misunderstood . . . *to be made* useful, appreciated, understandable.” Palestine becomes “an empty and patient territory, awaiting people who show a proper care of it.”

From Said, we learn how *unused is* deeply political, as a frame, as a technique. They declare something is unused or make something unusable (harder or impossible to use) as the ground for justifying an appropriation.

Theft and destruction turned into care.

Why am I sharing this old draft now? It’s not much use. It is not what I would write **now.**

I think I am sharing it as I have no new words, just old teachers. And gratitude for so many lessons.

**A Draft of a Conclusion that Wasn’t**

Sometimes we have to become creative because of a restriction or to fight a restriction. We queer the places in which we reside; queer as where we have been, a tail, a trail. A kitchen table becomes a publishing house. To challenge a history of use is to call for us not to reproduce that history, to break from it or take a break. To take a break is to shake up what has settled.

**Principle 1: Use is finitude**

We begin with finitude: that everything is limited in size or extent or capacity. Everything is something. To recognise something is to recognise that something is only so much. To demand that something be of use in order to be something can be to demand too much. The world can be too easily depleted, the world too must be thought of as something that can only take so much.

We do not start with the expectation that to use something well is to get the most out of something nor with the assumption that what is not of use should not exist (nor do we assume that what is of use should exist) but instead with the effects of use on what is, the wear and tear, the soil and the soiled, the corrosion. We begin with the exhaustion of what has already been. And to begin like this is to ask for other ways of working, to find forms that are more sustainable, to bring life back into forms. At certain points, when it is too much, we break. A break can be a break through.

**Principle 2: Everyone who can do housework should do housework**

To recognise that use is a relationship that requires energy to maintain requires that we recognise and value labour. We might call this labour reproductive labour as well as manual labour: the labour required to reproduce the possibility of existence. So much of this work is around use: the work of making things usable is necessary, including the work of cleaning things, washing things, caring for those who cannot do this work themselves. Everyone who can should clean up after themselves as well as others; everyone who can do the work of maintaining their lives should do that work. Caring for those who cannot do housework is not a burden or a drain on resources, it is a resource, a way of building a world in which we all recipients of care before we come to be.

All those who can, who are able, should be required to do their own housework; they should know what it takes to clean up after themselves and to care for others, to do the hard work, dirty work required to reproduce the possibility of an existence. Organisations also require housework: and too often that work is undervalued and is done by only some (often those who embody diversity), in order to free up the time for others. Revaluing housework is also about challenging the hierarchy between manual and mental labour and showing how products of “the mind,” always depend on manual labour, the physical work of using things that shapes things.

This revaluing of housework is also a revaluing of forms of unpaid work including emotional labour. We would challenge the moral quality given to employment as well as the restriction of our understanding of employment to some kinds of work. We would begin to recognise what is contributed by those who have been deemed unemployed. To value house work would also be about creating more space for workers to have breaks, to recognise labour is also to recognise the need to recover from the exhaustion of labour. Reducing a working day, would be a key aspiration we could take from this history of use, which is the history of how some become too exhausted to challenge the cause of their exhaustion.

**Principle 3: We need to challenge the division of labour and show how through this division utility becomes inequality**

The division of labour does not just lead to hierarchy and injustice. It is a profoundly unjust mechanism for making some useful to others; as well as for teaching some that others are available for their use, to pick up after them. The division of labour is understood as inevitable and progressive within social theory as well as in everyday life; we need to challenge this received wisdom if we are to open up the world, to show the openness of the world. The division of labour is not only about specialisation. It is how people become parts, reduced to function. We need to show how the division is inherited through a formal system of laws and rules as well as informal mechanisms that direct individuals to futures assumed to be waiting for them.

To throw this system into crisis requires rethinking the part/whole relation. It is not that we are not parts. In being part, we are not for.

**Principle 4: For is not before**

We need to question the functionalism that is often at stake by assuming that the design of an object is replicated in all forms of life: that the idea of what something is, or should be, brings that thing into existence. For is not before. For is up to those who have come, by hap, to exist. The questions of what is for, and what it means to be for, need to be kept alive as questions for those who are alive. To keep a question alive is to have a dialogue and an exchange about that question. I return to the importance of questions in principle 9.

Sometimes, it is not clear for whom a world is assembled. A for can be what recedes, when a world has been assembled around you. So even if for is not before, we need to bring *for* into social consciousness, we must use *for* to knock at the door, to show how some come before through *for.*

For is not before: this does not mean being against for or even not being for “for.” So often, we rush over things when we are doing things, because it seems we are concerned with *for*. We pick up the pen, because we are writing something. We rush over the pen, too quickly perhaps, because of what the pen is *for.* But if *for* is what we are heading for, we can also rush over *for* by rushing to for; we can assume the point of *for* is just that it is what we need to reach. What if we linger over *for*? What if we queer *for*, work out, with each other, what we are *for*?

**Principle 5: Not everything in use needs to be preserved**

We would recognise we have different relations to what we use; that to value something can mean for some to use it less, for others, to use it more. We would attune ourselves to corporeal differences: how some might want to take care not to break things; how others might take care because they break things. We would recognise that ethics and politics relates to how we relate to the worlds we build around us; the worlds that are built around us. We would think from the formulation: equality is a bumpy ride. We bump into each other because we are different from each other. Smoothing things over can be: eliminating the signs of difference. Smoothing things over can mean: creation the illusion that things are not damaged.

We might question the idea that preservation is what we should aim for. Some things that are valued need to cease for those for whom they have value. Something can be valued because it comes and goes. The politics of preservation so often means some assume their rights to appropriate what is of use to others, because they assume they alone have the technologies needed to preserve things. The demand for preservation can bring with it violence. Sacred human remains can be preserved in museums, becoming objects, kept apart by artificial means or even by any means. When sacred remains are returned to a people and a place, it means they would be allowed to degrade; degradation as becoming part, as ceasing to be separated; degradation as becoming soil. Degradation is part of life and death: it can be how something matters and becomes matter. Those deemed degraded have a story to tell, a story of having to fight to matter.

The guardians often represent themselves not as the law-makers but the care-takers. They do not always speak the language of mastery. The guardians can speak fluently in the language of happiness and care; they are concerned for the future, the future of fragile persons and of fragile things, they want what is best (for you). They want to preserve what they deem the happiest and most useful. So much and so many are cancelled out by being rendered unhappy; deviation is hard, deviation is made hard. So many paths are not taken out of fear that they would take you away from happiness.

It is possible then: that the story of a cessation, of how something no longer is, or how something is no longer happy, is how we begin again.

**Principle 6: We cannot assume a clear distinction between good and bad use or between affectionate and instrumental use**

The instrumental story of use, when what is used is a tool for the user, is only one story. We can contrast at least two different stories: that of a used, loved pot (Eliot) and a useless foot that would despise itself if it realised it had been no use to the body of which it was a part (Pascal). But I have tried to show that these stories can be parts of the same story, or two ways of telling the same story, even if there is more than one story of use to tell. Indeed, abuses of power are often performed as appeals to affection; those who abuse the power given them by institutions evoke their own affective histories, such that to challenge them would be to refuse affection.

To lose our confidence that we can tell the difference between instrumental and affectionate use requires an ongoing commitment and pedagogy: we have to keep learning by unlearning a history of use, which thickens as necessity, as the ways things are or the way they must be.

**Principle 7: Use is an ongoing commitment to learn about things from things**

To be in a relation is to be in a relation of use (although that is not only the way we can describe relation). To recognise relationality of use is not a moral guarantee of a good relation (see principle 4). But we can think of the maintenance of use as an epistemic commitment. Use requires work. Once we are in a relation to use, there is wear and tear; there is friction.

That we acquire knowledge through use changes what we mean by knowledge. Knowledge is local and embodied: we come to know things by doing things. We come to know bodies, our limits, our tipping points, from those moments it is too much: when we snap, when we can no longer take the weight.

Starting from use is thus a way of doing intellectual as well as physical labour. To start from use is to restart philosophy. Perhaps we rewrite philosophy from the point of view of the philosopher’s servant. In *Willful Subjects* (2014) I mentioned the irony that Kant wrote of how the moral subject should be approached as an end not a means as a subject with means, with a servant. Kant’s servant was named Martin Lampe. The servant has to attend to the needs of the employer, the master; the servant has to look after things; to polish and maintain things; the servant is *for*. Some become for. The word administration comes from service. No wonder administrators have institutional knowledge. Doing house work can be hard, but it can also be how we generate knowledge, *by working with stuff we work out stuff*. Perhaps the means/ends distinction would be messed up, once we think of means as work, as what we have to do to be. There is no end without the means. The means matter.

**Principle 8: The shattering of something is not the end of a use story**

Use requires we rethink how we give value to things. Principle 3 recognises differences in how we use things that we love. But whatever our differences, to value use is to value how things point outwards to others, and how things are shaped by the uses, intended uses, queer uses. Given this, if something breaks and can no longer be used for what it was being used for, that is the end of that specific use but not of that thing. Even if Silas could not use the pot for carrying water, he makes use of the pot by turning it into a memorial. A memorial is useful as a container in which we can deposit our memories.

A shattering can be how something becomes available to others. Because the pot has become stationary, it might even have become useful to more than Silas. The pot might be more useful as a home for an insect because it will not be filled with water. Telling the story of something that shatters is an opening up of use; when one biography of use ends, another begins.

**Principle 9: We need to keep asking: what’s the use?**

To ask “what’s the use?” is to make use into a question. To make use a question is to question the point of a path being taken; it is to consider how use might have thickened into a convention; how use might have become a restriction. We recognise then that political work might involve asking how what usually happens still happens; to intervene usually requires making the usual appear. We might, in aiming not to reproduce what is; in not following the well-trodden path, be understood as causing damage, as shattering things. We know the fragments, however sharp, can be starting points.

The question, “what’s the use,” is a life question. I opened my book with reference to Virginia Woolf’s insistence on asking that question: what’s the use, what’s the point? It is a question she asks to herself as a writer, as a woman writer. I think as writers we must ask this question to our own writing because being less sure about the point might be how we learn about the point, the point of writing, of leaving words behind, of committing words to memory.

I think asking the point *of*, might be an address *to.* To be useful to can be an opening. This is why, despite learning about how the requirement to be useful can be an imposition, we do not give up on useful, let alone on use or utility. The history of use has taught us how elites have distanced themselves from utility; the scholar sets himself apart from the craftsperson. We can refuse such distance. I am keen for knowledge to be useful, because I want the point to be somewhere other than where are or where we have been. I think too of Audre Lorde, who often talked about being useful, useful *to* a community, useful as a way of addressing a world; a multiple plural *to*, a *to* that faces many directions; a *to* that can animate a life, too.

**Principle 10: We need to create a crisis of usage to open up a world**

We would come to recognise how a world can be built to enable some to meet the requirements. We would show how privilege works by enabling some to have their energy saved, their time freed up from the work of what you have to do to be. We would recognise how spaces and worlds are occupied either through claiming of property (as the right to use or not to use space) or through the conditions of use. Not to reproduce an inheritance requires that we make use into a crisis: we squat; we stop the flow of traffic; we strike. To make usage a crisis is to participate in a shared project, a world-dismantling project, a world-building project.
