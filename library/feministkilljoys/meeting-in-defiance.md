By saying “[no is not a lonely utterance](https://www.penguin.co.uk/books/470363/no-is-not-a-lonely-utterance-by-ahmed-sara/9780241759271),” I am not denying that sometimes we are alone when we are hit by violence.

Nor am I denying that sometimes when you speak out you might do so as a lone voice.

When I mean is that by saying *no* you participate in a longer history; *no* as how we know of the violence that remains.

We meet in *no*, and not necessarily in person.

We meet in defiance.

To say *no*, you make a connection, an electric connection.

Right now, we need that connection.

Of course, I learnt this from Audre Lorde.

Lorde tells us she was out driving her car and heard on the news that a white police officer had been acquitted of the murder of a black child, Clifford Glover, in 1973.

History gets repeated. Too many unheard complaints.

Lorde stopped the car, to let it in, the violence of the police in, of white supremacy, in. She stopped the car and a poem, “[Power](https://poets.org/poem/power-audio-only),” came out, with its electric line, not to let our power “lie limp and useless as an unconnected wire.”

In a way, by saying something Lorde said no.

No to the violence, yes.

But also, no to not saying anything.

She refuses to be silent, to let this news, this reality, pass by without complaint.

What Lorde let in, what she gets out, we read.

**No can be how we meet in an action.**

I am thinking of that *no,* now. How we need it.

Still, it is hard not to be overwhelmed.

There is so much to take in. Too much.

*We* assemble, saying *no*, doing it.

We are watching a genocide in Gaza. We are watching as our government continues to [send Israel the weapons](https://www.middleeasteye.net/news/uk-continuing-send-arms-israel-despite-ban-report) they use to murder tens of thousands of Palestinians.

Destroying anything so they can take everything.

It should be unbelievable.

How much more can we watch? How much more suffering?

We cannot look away, we must not.

*No is Not a Lonely Utterance* was sent out for a legal read. Every time I used the word *genocide* for what is happening in Gaza, it was “flagged” for “global sensitivities.”

What globe is this, too sensitive to hear about genocide but not too sensitive to commit it or to stop it being committed?

We are not just watching, we are protesting and marching. We are writing and reading and communicating. Risking “global sensitivities.”

As we must.

But still it goes on.

In this country, we are watching the lives of trans people made more and more difficult. Each new law, new report, written from the same place of perpetual hostility as if trans people should not go anywhere or be anywhere.

Biological sex turned into a mission statement.

That [feminist dystopia](https://feministkilljoys.substack.com/p/meaningless-sex).

We watched as party platforms became fascist placards.

“Stop the Boats.”

We are listening as the Labour party sounds more and more like an extreme right-wing party, as if the best way to defeat our enemies is to become them. Labour, doing what they can to makes lives harder for those who are already struggling to make do, disabled people, the unemployed, anyone who needs access to benefits.

It should be unbelievable.

[Keir Starmer](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2025/may/12/uk-risks-becoming-island-of-strangers-without-more-immigration-curbs-starmer-says): we are becoming an “island of strangers.”

Yes he said that.

Little island, on its own.

A stranger: the one we recognise as a stranger.

The one who does not belong here.

Who is not from here.

Not British. Not white.

Or just plain old *not.*

I am not a stranger because I came from Australia. I am a stranger because my father came from Pakistan.

Just one of many countries, colonised, by this little island.

The stranger: the one who does not look like us.

Whose brownness does not come from being too long under the sun.

When your brownness is seen as a stain you are told not to complain.

Perhaps you stand instead on a platform, “Stop the Boats,” your arm, becoming the rod of the state: yes.

**Those who say yes are more likely to progress.**

The strangers take away what is ours. They take away the condition of not being “an island of strangers,” heard as no, negation, a negative demand, as changing who we are.

Were.

That past that wasn’t.

We are not just watching, we are complaining and protesting and marching. We are writing and reading and communicating.

But still it goes on.

We protest, we communicate because of what goes on.

It is a lot to handle.

That’s why I wrote another book on complaint. Because of what goes on.

I learnt from sharing stories of complaint that the point can be to find other people.

One person wrote to me, “I thought that I had had a unique experience. It’s so therapeutic to know what I experienced is real ‒ that it actually happens to other people . . . that it wasn’t just in my head.”

It helps to know that our complaints are not so singular; that what happens to us also happens to others.

We find out it happened to others. We find others.

It helps to talk to others. One person said at the end of sharing her story of complaint with me, “It helped me to talk to you. It made me feel less alone.”

It helped me to talk to you too. It helped me feel less alone after I left my job and a life I had known.

**If it helps to be heard, it helps to hear.**

**A no becomes louder not just when we say it together but when we hear it together.**

My *no book* (as I now think of it) has a different tone or feel than my earlier book on [complaint](https://www.dukeupress.edu/complaint). I know how I felt in writing it. Full of anger, yes. Full of [grief,](https://open.substack.com/pub/feministkilljoys/p/in-grief-grievance?utm_source=post&comments=true&utm_medium=web) certainly. But also, alive. Full of the energy and electricity that comes from finding the people who see it and say it. [Snap](https://feministkilljoys.com/2017/05/21/snap/).

I am about to visit Istanbul. This is my first visit to Turkey. It means so much to go there, to make connections. To meet up with activists.

It will also be the first time I share material from *No Is Not a Lonely Utterance.* Yes, there is old material in the new material. But it sounds different.

This book is about *sounding different*.

I have collected so many stories of how people use complaints to push and to press for change. There are so many different ways people express their complaints in posters, and post its, and postcards, in prayers and songs and performances.

*I travel not just to share stories but to receive them.*

*Not just to speak complaints but to hear them.*

*To be in solidarity, that to-and-fro.*

*Killjoy solidarity.*

*Solidarity in the face of what we come up against.*

*Solidarity so we can face what we come up against.*

I am grateful to Elif Araz who got in touch with me recently. She gave me permission to share her reflections on what my visit might mean:

*“Sara Ahmed’s visit to Istanbul feels like a small but powerful mark in the ongoing history of feminism in Turkey — a reminder that our complaints, our struggles, our feminist joys matter.*

*As a queer feminist living here, this moment feels like a breath: a space being made, gently and intentionally, for voices like mine. To be seen, to be heard, to be part of a larger feminist lineage — that means everything.*

*And more than that: it’s a chance for us to meet each other. Feminist readers, thinkers, dreamers — from different cities, different walks of life — sharing the same room, the same breath, the same refusal.*

*This is what feminist space-making can do: not just build bridges upward, but stretch them sideways — across lives, across differences, across joys.*

*Feminist history in this country holds many erased beginnings: the first political party was founded by a woman, and one of the earliest international women’s congresses in the Muslim world was held in Istanbul in 1935. We remember, we continue, we gather.*

*We are not just meeting Sara Ahmed. We are meeting each other, in defiance of a system that works to keep us apart.”*

**What a joy not just to meet but to be part of other people’s meetings.**

**To meet as defiance.**

**To meet in defiance.**

***No*** **can be sociable, an expression of solidarity, how we share our refusal to accept things as they are.**

**A no can be how we find others who say no.**
