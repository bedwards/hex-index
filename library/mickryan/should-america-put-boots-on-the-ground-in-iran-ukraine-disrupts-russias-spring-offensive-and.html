I paid tribute to the warriors who fell defending and liberating the village of Moshchun. It is both important and right to honour the heroes who protected the Kyiv region and our entire state. We remember and will always be grateful to our warriors for their heroism and bravery. They achieved the most important thing – they gave our children the chance to live in a free Ukraine. We continue the fight to ensure that this war ends justly. President Zelenskyy, 21 March 2026.

War, as Clausewitz understood it, is a contest of wills prosecuted through the application of force. While many are absorbed by coverage of the war in Iran, the week of 15–22 March reinforced, again, that the will of Ukraine and Russia remain fundamentally unreconciled. Ukrainian forces are imposing genuine operational dilemmas on the Russian military command, on the ground, at sea and in the air, while Moscow’s diplomatic obstruction continues with barely concealed contempt for any settlement short of Ukrainian capitulation.

In the Pacific, China continues its military build-up and persists with their systemic campaign of coercion against different regional nations. It does so at a time when the key security guarantor for Pacific democracies, the United States, is stuck in another Middle East war and absorbed in a campaign of abuse against allies from which it needs assistance.

And in the Middle East, the question on everyone’s lips is this: will Trump declare victory and withdraw from the war, or escalate it through the commitment of U.S. ground troops in one or more different locations in Iran? In this week’s update, I provide a short assessment of the cases for and against American boots on the ground in Iran.

Welcome to this week’s update and The Big Five.

Ukraine

Ukraine’s Southern Operations: Disrupting the Russian Spring Timetable. The most consequential military development of the past few weeks is not what Russia has done. It is what Ukraine has prevented Russia from doing. The Ukrainian counter attacks in the south, initiated in late January, have now liberated more than 400 square kilometres of previously occupied territory in Dnipropetrovsk Oblast, according to General Staff operations chief Major General Oleksandr Komarenko.

Also this week, the Ukrainian commander-in-chief, General Syrsky, described how the tempo of attacks had increased in recent weeks. At the same time, Ukraine has claimed that Russia suffered 4840 casualties over a three-day period. If accurate, this would be one of the highest daily casualty rates for the Russians since the start of the full-scale invasion in 2022.

Ukraine’s counterattacks on the Oleksandrivka and Hulyaipole axes of advance are having cascading effects across the frontline, disrupting Russian preparations for what was intended to be (again) a decisive spring-summer campaign. Russia had hoped that advances toward Hulyaipole would allow it to approach Orikhiv from both east and west simultaneously and therefore bypassing Ukraine’s east-west defensive lines in Zaporizhzhia Oblast without frontal assault. Ukrainian air assault and assault regiment formations have comprehensively disrupted that plan.

The Russian military in Ukraine now faces competing dilemmas. Ukrainian advances in the Oleksandrivka direction threaten the rear of Russian 5th Combined Arms Army elements pushing west from Hulyaipole, potentially compelling them to shift from offensive to defensive posture. Elements of Russia’s operational reserve have been committed to defend against Ukrainian counterattacks. This is a good indicator that Russian forces are burning through reserves earmarked for spring offensive operations.

The price of this Ukrainian operational success, however, is being paid elsewhere. Ukraine’s ground force resources are not infinite. Meduza’s analysis published on March 19 notes bluntly that the redeployment of Ukrainian units to the south has weakened the northern outskirts of Pokrovsk, allowing Russian forces to launch a new offensive push toward Dobropillia. This is the inherent tension of counteroffensive operations in a resource-constrained environment: you cannot be strong everywhere.

Russian Shaping for its Spring Offensive Operations. On the night of 17 March, Russia appears to have launched the opening salvos of its long-anticipated spring-summer offensive. Euromaidan Press reported that a sudden weather change, which included fog and drizzle across the Dobropillia, Pokrovsk, and Hulyaipole regions, triggered pre-infiltrated Russian assault groups that had been deployed in forward positions throughout early March. The assumption of the Russian forces was that fog would suppress Ukrainian drones. It did not.

By the end of 17 March, Ukrainian Unmanned Systems Forces drone units had reportedly killed 292 Russian soldiers and wounded 221 others. Over the following 36 hours, Russian forces suffered an estimated 900 casualties across a 100-kilometre stretch of front without breaking through at any point. The drone operators used ground-based systems that proved capable even in low-visibility conditions. This capability apparently caught Russian planners off guard.

The broader picture of Russian mechanised activity is interesting. An assessment on 20 March by ISW notes that Russian forces are increasingly conducting mechanised assaults in multiple sectors such as the Lyman, Kostiantynivka, and Pokrovsk axes of advance. These are probably reconnaissance-in-force missions ahead of larger Russian spring assaults in the area of its main effort.

The situation around Kostiantynivka is worthy of close attention. Meduza’s analysis describes how Russian forces destroyed a dam near Osykovo on 25 February with a three-tonne guided aerial bomb, flooding a key Ukrainian supply road into the city and forcing Ukrainian units into permanent buildings where they become easier targets. Despite Russian General Staff claims of holding 60 percent of the city, Russian troops remain engaged in urban fighting on its outskirts. Meanwhile, along the Kazennyi Torets valley, a separate Russian grouping has reached the village of Novopavlivka, about 12 kilometres from Druzhkivka, in a push designed to cut off Kostiantynivka from the broader Kramatorsk urban complex.

Ukraine’s Deep Strike Campaign. One of the more significant strategic shifts in the war may currently be playing out not on the ground but in the air over Russia. An analysis in the Kyiv Post this week noted that from February through mid-March 2026, Ukrainian forces conducted at least 110 long-range drone strike missions against Russian targets. More significantly: for the first time in the war, the number of long-range drones Ukraine launched at Russia was on par with — and on some nights exceeded — the number that Russia had launched at Ukraine.

There is an operational logic behind this shift. Ukrainian drone units have been systematically hunting Russian air defence systems, particularly Pantsir, at a pace that Russia’s military-industrial complex cannot match. Ukraine’s SBU Alpha unit claimed to have neutralised approximately half of Russia’s operational Pantsir stockpile in 2025 and early 2026.

The strategic consequences of this aerial campaign are accumulating. Ukraine has continued to hit Russian oil and defence industrial infrastructure, and this has, according to Russia Matters, forced nearly 40 percent of Russia’s oil refining capacity offline at its peak. Ukrainian forces also struck Iskander-M ballistic missile launchers in occupied Crimea and radar stations that support Russian strike operations. This aimed to degrade Russian air defence capabilities and disrupt air defence support to ground forces and their preparations for the spring campaign.

However, Russian drone and missile pressure continues at scale. Each night in the past week the Russians have fired between 100 and 200 drones and missiles at Ukraine. The largest recent attack occurred on the evening of March 13–14. That night, Russia launched a large-scale strike on Ukraine that caused significant civilian casualties in Kyiv. Ukrainian air defences downed 402 drones, one Zircon hypersonic cruise missile, seven Iskander-M ballistic missiles, 25 Kalibr cruise missiles, and 24 Kh-101 cruise missiles in a single engagement.

President Zelensky noted on March 16 that Ukraine is now intercepting Russian drones worth tens of thousands of dollars with systems costing thousands of dollars. The cost asymmetry is a developing strategic advantage for Ukraine, and an important component of any cost imposition strategy against Russia.

Peace Talks: Stumbling Like a Drunk Russian. Peace negotiations this week were defined primarily by Russian intransigence and European diplomatic manoeuvring around the edges. The core dynamic has not changed: Moscow continues to condition any ceasefire on concessions that amount to Ukrainian capitulation.

On 16 March, Russian Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov declared that the EU had “completely discredited itself” in the Ukraine peace process, dismissed Ukraine’s readiness for diplomacy, and stated that Russia would continue pursuing its objectives on the ground. Lavrov rejected French and British plans for a Coalition of the Willing deployment as “an occupation force posing a threat to the Russian Federation.” Lavrov continues to reject any negotiated settlement that fails to concede to all of Russia’s territorial demands. This is a position, unchanged throughout the entire war, that makes any near-term ceasefire functionally impossible on terms Ukraine could accept.

The Financial Times, cited by the Kyiv Independent, reported that Russia has rejected attempts by European nations to play a larger role in peace negotiations. The exclusion of Europe from the trilateral US-Ukraine-Russia framework remains a core Russian negotiating objective. It is designed to isolate Ukraine from its most committed supporters and deal directly with a U.S. administration whose peace priorities, as Carnegie Endowment analysts argued, lean toward resolution over quality.

The territorial question remains an irresolvable centrepiece. Russia demands Ukraine surrender the remainder of Donetsk Oblast and formally renounce claims to occupied Luhansk, Zaporizhzhia, and Kherson oblasts.

Related to these negotiations was Putin’s offer this week to stop sharing intelligence with Iran if America cut off support for Ukraine. While this was undoubtedly a tempting offer for some in the Trump administration, it was rejected (for now). The Russians called this a fake story. But if Trump gets desperate enough in his war against Iran, who knows what might happen in the future in regard to such an offer from Moscow?

Ukraine War Assessment

In the past month, Ukraine has achieved something quite significant in the south. Ukrainian attacks there have disrupted Russian offensive planning, consumed Russian reserve forces, and demonstrated that Ukrainian combined arms operations can impose genuine operational costs. The blocking of Starlink for Russian forces in early February has been a force multiplier.

But there is also a geographic trade-off in these southern operations. Gains in the south have come at some cost to northern Donetsk, and Russian forces retain the initiative on what is Russia’s main effort on the ground: the envelopment of Ukraine’s fortress belt and the remainder of Donetsk.

The Russian spring-summer offensive has been disrupted but hardly stopped. As Magyar, commander of the Ukrainian Unmanned Systems Forces wrote in an update this week, the Russian spring offensive is “partially uncorked.” The 17 March Russian assault that cost 900 casualties without a breakthrough is a data point. But it is very early days in what will be a long spring and summer campaign season on the ground. Russia continues to accumulate forces, improve its drone and electronic warfare capabilities, and demonstrate a competent operational method.

However Russia’s tactics require enormous manpower and generally result in only small gains (when they are successful). It is not sustainable forever. As Lawrence Freedman wrote this week:

The real problem for Russia (and for that matter Ukraine) is the difficulty of mounting a classical military offensive under contemporary conditions. Even if Russia can keep the flow of recruits getting to the front it will struggle to move forward in numbers and with momentum. This is why the next few months will be important. Putin will only change his stubborn negotiating position when he accepts that his forces are stuck.

The fundamental question for 2026 is whether Ukraine can hold its defensive lines in the east while sustaining its momentum in the south, and whether its partners will sustain the material and political will to support that effort it. What this week suggests is that Ukraine is fighting with considerable sophistication, and Russia’s margin for error is narrowing but not yet exhausted.

The Iran War

The Cases For and Against Boots on the Ground

Wars reveal the limits of military forces. Since 28 February, the United States and Israel have prosecuted a military campaign of extraordinary air and naval power against Iran. They have removed large numbers of senior leaders, degraded Iran’s missile arsenal, destroyed its navy and attacked its nuclear capability. The military results are tactically impressive.

And yet the oldest lesson in strategy keeps surfacing: military success in the air and at sea does not automatically translate into political outcomes on the ground. Iran has not been beaten. The question likely being considered in the Pentagon, Congress and the White House is whether ground forces would ensure that the military campaign achieves a decisive political outcome - or whether it would lead to a larger and more difficult American military commitment to the Middle East with uncertain results.

A paper published by the Center for Strategic and International Studies this week, authored by retired Lieutenant General James Dubik, examines this problem. In it, he writes that:

Our operations in Iran are again teaching that war involves more than fighting. Wars must be fought and waged. Fighting is a necessary part of war, and the U.S. military is very good at it. Our military won every tactical engagement in Vietnam, Afghanistan, and Iraq. American citizens have every reason to ask, therefore, if we fight so successfully, why did we lose in Vietnam and Afghanistan and why was success in Iraq so limited?

Dubik’s challenge has very real implications for the ongoing campaign in the Middle East. The new Iran War has exposed the divergence between military organisations fighting wars, and nations waging wars. It has revealed the difference between the synchronised lethality of American air and naval power, and the sustained organisational, political, economic, diplomatic and strategic capacity required to convert military effect into durable political outcomes.

Ground troops are a partial answer to filling the fighting-waging gap. But they are not a cost-free one. To that end, I wanted to undertake a short assessment of the cases for and against American ‘boots on the ground’ in Iran.

The Case for a Ground Interventions. The most compelling argument for a deployment of ground forces is irreversibility. Airstrikes can damage Iran’s nuclear infrastructure, but only forces on the ground can physically dismantle it, verify its destruction, and prevent reconstitution. In a conflict whose genesis lies in nuclear ambiguity, that distinction matters. Physical presence converts temporary effect into a near permanent fact.

A second argument relates to Iran’s internal political system. The regime’s January 2026 massacre of protesters, which was the largest domestic uprising since the Islamic Revolution, revealed again an Iranian regime that rules through terror alone. Ground forces, carefully deployed with a clear mission and working towards a credible political objective, could serve as both catalyst and guarantor for an Iranian population that has already demonstrated its desire for change. The historical parallels are imperfect, and regime change has become a discredited concept in Washington DC. But it is not an irrational or impossible objective.

Third, the Strait of Hormuz problem is unlikely to be resolved from altitude. Iran’s mining campaign has already spiked global oil prices above $110 a barrel. A ground presence along Iran’s southern coastline, coordinated with air and naval operations, could provide a solution to the world’s most consequential energy chokepoint in ways that air power and naval interdiction alone cannot guarantee.

Fourth, the seizure of a significant Iranian oil refinery site, such as Kharg Island, could offer an important negotiating ‘card’ for the Trump administration. It is strategically important for Iran and a site where much of its refined oil is produced for export. It could be an economic ‘sword of Damocles’ held over the head of the Iranian regime. But it would need to be seized with minimal damage to the oil refining infrastructure. And, it goes without saying, seizing ground and holding ground are two very different things.

The Case Against Boots on the Ground. The counterarguments for boots on the ground are strong. Iran is a nation of nearly 90 million people, across 1.6 million square kilometres of mountainous terrain purpose-built for insurgency, with a Basij and IRGC structure designed precisely to wage protracted irregular warfare against a conventional occupier. The U.S. military could win every tactical engagement in Iran. That, as Dubik reminds us in his article, is exactly the problem.

The political architecture is equally unfavourable. It is difficult to articulate a ground campaign’s purpose, or its exit conditions, without a clearly defined political objective. Wars without clear political endpoints become long term occupations. These become the kind of national sore points that hollow out military forces and national treasuries and fracture civil-military trust.

Regionally, escalation risks are significant. Iranian-backed militias are already striking American assets across the Gulf. A ground incursion into Iran would provide these networks with purpose, propaganda, and more vulnerable targets than aircraft carriers and airfields. A war already stretching across Lebanon, Iraq, and the Gulf could metastasise further.

A ground incursion, even something designed to be a time limited raid, could easily become something far larger and of a much longer duration. Air raids represent one level of commitment. A ground incursion could easily expand in scope and duration. It is far easier to commit troops than to extract them. Even if America did not have other compelling military challenges elsewhere, it is not certain that the strategic return on such an investment in the lives of American ground troops is worth it in Iran.

A ground deployment that becomes a longer-term deployment (even if this is not the original intention) is likely to draw in assets from all the U.S. services and continue the consumption of munitions needed to be stockpiled to deter China or provided to Ukraine. Because of the distraction a protracted ground deployment might cause, and the very high likelihood of more civilian casualties, it may also heighten the fracture in America’s relationships with allies in the Middle East, Europe and beyond.

Finally, at home, America’s political seams are showing. A ground deployment, with its inevitable casualties, an open-ended timeline, and inescapable echoes of Baghdad, Fallujah and Kandahar, would further test public and Congressional tolerance in ways that even the most successful air campaign has not.

The Strategic Verdict. The United States faces the same dilemma it has faced in every war since Korea: how to convert tactical dominance and exquisite military technology into strategic and political success. Dubik offers that fighting and waging are different disciplines, and that America has consistently mastered the former while struggling with the latter. Boots on the ground would not automatically resolve that deficit. In a country like Iran, without a coherent American political strategy for the employment of ground forces, they might deepen it.

The advantages of threatening an American ground intervention are real. Not ruling out boots on the ground is good strategy, and can help achieve strategic deception objectives as well as support negotiations over war termination. The advantages of actually committing boots on the ground are also real but more limited. As my quick assessment finds, the disadvantages could be numerous.

Before any order to deploy ground troops is given, the Trump administration should ponder and answer the questions it has until now avoided: What does victory look like? How long is America prepared to stay? Who governs Iran if the regime falls?

Until those questions have credible answers, America’s ground options for Iran – be they special operations raids, Marine landings or the commitment of conventional army forces - are probably best left in the planning room. This is not because America troops on the ground in Iran is inconceivable, but because the conditions that would make their mission strategically sound do not yet exist.

The Pacific

Summit Delayed. The President of the United States announced on 17 March that he would delay his summit with Chinese leader Xi Jinping so he could prioritise the war against Iran. Trump announced that he would delay the summit with Xi by “five or six weeks”. The Trump administration subsequently confirmed that China had agreed to Trump’s request on March 18. No new date for the Trump-Xi summit has been announced. The delay in the Trump-Xi summit will extend the trade dispute between the two countries and delay arms sales to Taiwan.

Japanese PM Visits Washington. This week, the Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi visited Washington DC and met with President Trump. Before the meeting, Japan joined a handful of European nations in issuing a joint statement calling for an “immediate comprehensive moratorium on attacks on civilian infrastructure, including oil and gas installations” and expressing a readiness “to contribute to appropriate efforts to ensure safe passage through the Strait.”

Japan came out of the meeting well, and the Japanese PM escaped the meeting without any Trumpian abuse. That is the measure of success for such meetings now. There was one uncomfortable moment, however. President Trump joked about Pearl Harbour and used the Japanese surprise attack on 7 December 1941 to explain his decision not to notify American allies ahead of strikes on Iran. As Trump stated:

We didn’t tell anybody about it because we wanted surprise…Who knows better about surprise than Japan, okay? Why didn’t you tell me about Pearl Harbor, okay?

PLA Joint Combat Patrols. This week, K. Tristan Tang published his latest update on Chinese combat patrols around Taiwan. The key finding in his report was as following:

Although PLA aircraft sorties fell to zero on 17 of the past 30 days, joint combat readiness patrols have not declined in frequency or scale, suggesting no significant degradation in overall PLA combat readiness.

China-Japan Strategic Competition. The U.S. Intelligence Community’s Annual Threat Assessment released on 18 March had a useful but short section on China-Japan tensions. The assessment noted that:

China–Japan tensions increased significantly in November 2025 following comments made by Japanese Prime Minister Takaichi describing a potential Chinese invasion of Taiwan as a “survival threatening situation” for Japan. In response, China is employing multi-domain coercive pressure that probably will intensify through 2026, aimed both at punishing Japan and deterring other countries from making similar statements about their potential involvement in a Taiwan crisis.

Japan has deployed Type 12 surface-to-ship missiles in Kyushu as a counter-strike capacity against enemy bases. Defense Minister Koizumi announced plans to install missiles on Yonaguni Island (250km from Taiwan) by fiscal 2030. China’s Foreign Ministry Spokesperson Lin Jian stated on 19 March that Takaichi’s remarks “signal Japan’s intention to attempt armed intervention on the Taiwan question, and constitute a threat of force against China.”

2. China’s Cognitive Operations Against Taiwan

The topic of cognitive warfare is an expanding and vigorous debate in defence and national security circles. A range of different articles and reports have been published recently that cover the topic. To this growing literature can be added this new report published by Modern Diplomacy. As the author writes, “there is a form of warfare that leaves no craters, deploys no soldiers, and issues no formal declaration, and yet it may prove more consequential for the future of Taiwan than any military confrontation. Scholars now call it cognitive warfare…It represents the cutting edge of how great powers seek to reshape international order in the 21st century, not through the costly and escalatory use of force, but through the patient, deniable manipulation of what target populations believe.” You can read the full article at this link.
