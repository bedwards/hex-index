The war in Ukraine this week conformed to patterns established early in 2026. Russian forces continued their attempts at generating operational momentum in their spring offensive with only limited success, while Ukrainian defensive operations – particularly drone operations – are hurting the Russians in greater depth behind the frontline. Ukraine and Russia continue their deep aerial strikes, with Russia generating large numbers of Ukrainian civilians killed and wounded, and Ukraine hurting Russian air defence and oil production. Diplomacy has stalled, and that does not appear likely to change while the Iran War absorbs much of the strategic bandwidth of the Trump administration.

In the Iran War, peace talks in Islamabad between U.S. Vice President Vance and Iranian representatives collapsed on 12 April. Trump subsequently declared that he “no longer cared about negotiations” and announced a US naval blockade of Iranian ports. By 16 April, Defence Secretary Hegseth confirmed that more than 10,000 US sailors, marines, and airmen, along with over a dozen warships, were executing the maritime blockade. The U.S. Navy simultaneously began initial operations to re-open the Strait of Hormuz. More recently the New York Times reported that “American military and intelligence officials estimate that, after weeks of war, Iran still has about 40 percent of its arsenal of attack drones and upward of 60 percent of its missile launchers — more than enough to hold shipping in the Strait of Hormuz hostage in the future.” It is hard to see why Iran would give up such leverage cheaply.

The week in the Pacific has seen North Korean ballistic missile launches, further negotiations about Taiwan’s special defence budget, the Balikatan 2026 live-fire exercises and China’s response, Australia’s new defence strategy, and the continued evolution of Japan’s operational footprint in the South China Sea and Philippine Sea.

Ukraine

Ukrainian Long-Range Strike Operations: Denying Russia’s Oil Windfall. This week demonstrated again that Ukraine has transformed its long-range strike campaign into a powerful source of strategic leverage against Putin. The campaign’s geographic scope would have been hard to imagine just a couple of years ago. Its economic effects, measured in denied oil export revenue, are becoming difficult for Russia to absorb. And the timing of the current Ukrainian strike campaign momentum is important – it coincides with Moscow’s windfall from the Iran war-driven oil price surge, and the extension of U.S. sanction relief.

In the past 24 hours, Ukraine hit the Novokuybyshevsk refinery, located 1000 km from Ukraine. It is an important asset for the Russian military and provides a large proportion of the Russian army’s fuel requirements.

Another striking operation (pardon the pun) of the last week came on 10 April, when Ukrainian forces struck two drilling platforms in the northern Caspian Sea - the ice-resistant stationary platforms at the V. Graifer and Yuri Korchagin fields. Ukraine’s General Staff stated the platforms served as fuel supply nodes for the Russian occupation army, making them legitimate military targets.

The week also saw Ukrainian drones hit two oil pumping stations in Volgograd and Krasnodar Krai, an oil depot in Tver, the Cherepovets Azot ammonia plant in the Volga region, the Sterlitamak Petrochemical Plant in Bashkortostan, and the oil export terminal and refinery at Tuapse on the Black Sea. The cumulative damage to Russia’s export infrastructure, in the final days of March and first two weeks of April, appears to be significant. Open source satellite photography showed Primorsk had lost 40 percent of its storage capacity and Ust-Luga 30 percent. Neither port was handling normal oil shipments as of early April, with Finnish maritime officials reporting tanker arrivals had fallen from a weekly average of 40–50 ships to “individual vessels.”

Reuters estimated that Russia had missed out on 40 percent of its potential oil windfall because Ukraine had destroyed the capacity to export at least 2 million barrels per day. In the context of Brent crude trading at $114 a barrel, that is a significant financial problem for Putin.

Russia’s response to the Ukrainian campaign was to issue a threat to Europe. The Russian Defence Ministry published a list of addresses of European companies involved in drone joint production with Ukraine, and former Russian President Dmitry Medvedev clarified that the list amounted to “a list of potential targets for the Russian armed forces.” The warning came after Germany pledged 300 million euros to Ukraine’s long-range strike capability, Norway committed 560 million euros to drone support, and the Netherlands and Belgium added further hundreds of millions.

Related to Ukraine’s robust and evolving long-range strike capability, President Zelenskyy this week marked Arms Makers’ Day on 15 April by releasing a video showcasing 56 types of Ukrainian-built weapons, including 31 types of drones. His deputy defence minister noted that Russia cannot produce enough air defence systems to protect its vast territory. This is evidence that Ukraine’s strike campaign has found a structural gap in Russia’s air defence system that more investment and more drones and long-range missiles can continue to exploit.

Zelenskyy’s Europe Tour. Zelenskyy’s diplomatic tempo during the week took him to Norway, Germany, and Italy in a sweep of alliance meetings. His messaging was consistent: air defence is Ukraine’s highest priority, the Iran war has complicated Ukraine’s position, and Russia does not deserve any easing of international pressure including oil sanctions.

In Germany on 14 April, Zelenskyy and Chancellor Friedrich Merz agreed to strengthen defence cooperation, particularly in drone warfare, with Berlin committing 300 million euros to Ukraine’s long-range strike capability and pledging 5,000 mid-range attack drones for use against Russian battlefield logistics networks and other targets. In Norway on the same day, a joint defence and security cooperation agreement was signed alongside a Norwegian commitment of 560 million euros in drone support.

In Rome on 15 April, Zelenskyy met Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni and announced a “Drone Deal” format. This is a security cooperation model through which Ukraine’s expertise in drone warfare, electronic warfare, and missile defence would be integrated with partner nations’ industrial capacity. Teams from Ukraine and Italy’s Leonardo defence company were tasked with developing a technical plan. Zelenskyy told the joint press conference that Ukrainian drone expertise had become a strategic export, with the Iran war creating new demand for Ukrainian counter-drone knowledge among nations that had found themselves on the receiving end of Iranian-designed systems.

“Air defence is our absolute priority,” Zelenskyy told reporters in Rome. “Almost every day, Russia strikes our cities and communities, our energy facilities, and residential buildings with missiles and drones.” His statement also addressed the peace negotiation deadlock directly: American negotiators, he told German television, appeared to him to be “convinced of Russia’s victory in the war” and he described this as “the only problem” with the current framework.

The previous week Zelenskyy had proposed an Easter ceasefire, offering a trade: Ukraine would stop attacking Russian energy facilities if Russia stopped striking Ukrainian ones. Putin rejected the extension of the 32-hour Easter truce unless Kyiv accepted his terms, an approach that used territorial concessions as the condition. Zelenskyy’s public handling of both the offer and its rejection demonstrated a consistent strategic communication discipline: position Ukraine as the reasonable party, make Moscow’s intransigence visible, and continue building the European coalition that can sustain the war regardless of Washington’s engagement level.

Russian Aerial Attacks. The Russian air campaign against Ukrainian cities during the period under review was not aberrant. It was the continuation of a policy that has now killed more than 15,000 Ukrainian civilians since 2022, according to the United Nations — a policy of deliberate targeting of civilian infrastructure and residential areas that, whatever language Russian officials deploy to describe it, constitutes systematic terror.

The worst attack of the past week occurred on the night of 15–16 April. Russia launched 703 weapons - 659 drones and 44 missiles - against Ukrainian cities in its largest aerial attack in almost two weeks. Ukraine’s air defences performed well under pressure: 636 drones and 31 missiles were shot down or neutralised, a 95 percent intercept rate for drones and 70 percent for missiles. But 26 locations were struck. At least 16 people were killed and more than 100 injured across Kyiv, Odesa, Dnipro, and Zaporizhzhia. In Kyiv, four were killed including a 12-year-old boy. In Odesa, nine were killed and 23 injured when a missile struck a high-rise residential building. In Dnipro, three women were killed and around three dozen injured.

“Such attacks cannot be normalised. These are war crimes that must be stopped and their perpetrators held to account,” Ukrainian Foreign Minister Sybiha said on X.

The Easter ceasefire period provided a revealing window into Russia’s attitude toward negotiated humanitarian pauses. Ukraine’s General Staff recorded 10,721 ceasefire violations over the 32-hour period, including 1,567 artillery attacks, 119 assault actions, and 9,035 short-range kamikaze drone attacks. Russia refrained from long-range missile and Shahed attacks during the truce period, but the volume of shorter-range violations made any characterisation of the truce as genuine a difficult case to argue. Russia’s Defence Ministry accused Ukraine of 1,971 violations. Analysts described the truce as ‘smoke and mirrors’, noting it reflected Kremlin narrative management more than genuine pursuit of peace.

With regards to defending against Russian aerial attacks, during the week the Ukrainian Defence Minister issued a social media post that described how private air defence companies were now being integrated into Ukraine’s national air, missile and drone defence system. There are currently 19 companies contributing, and this number is sure to rise. It is a model for air defence – a public/private partnership – which has not been attempted in western nations before. Nonetheless, it is a fascinating development and another Ukrainian innovation we should watch and learn from.

And in a final interesting development this week, Ukraine’s Unmanned Systems Forces (USF) announced the first shoot down of a Shahed drone by a Ukrainian interceptor drone launched from a mothership drone. The details are contained in the following statement posted on USF social media:

For the first time in the world, an Unmanned Systems Forces interceptor drone launched from an unmanned surface platform has shot down a Shahed UAV. Operators of the naval unmanned systems division within the 412th Nemesis Brigade are carrying out combat missions in the maritime operational zone. This unit achieved the successful interception of a Shahed-type attack drone using an interceptor UAV launched from an unmanned surface vessel. This marks a new level of integration between naval and aerial unmanned capabilities. Using surface drone carriers to deploy interceptor drones expands air defense options and creates an additional layer of protection for Ukrainian cities.

War on the Ground. The ground war during the week was characterised by the familiar rhythm of attritional combat: large numbers of tactical engagements, incremental gains and losses measured in hundreds of metres, and Russia’s continued inability to translate local tactical gains into operationally significant advances.

In the Donetsk Oblast, the most significant development came from the DeepState monitoring group’s assessment on 18 April that Russian forces were solidifying positions in Kalynove as a staging point for an advance toward the logistical hub of Kostiantynivka. Russian drone crews redeployed from the Kursk Oblast operations were reported in the area. Russian forces are now able to operate drones deep enough to reach Kostiantynivka itself - a city of 67,000 pre-war residents that is an important Ukrainian logistics node. ISW assessed that Ukrainian forces made advances in the Kostiantynivka-Druzhkivka tactical area on 12 and 13 April.

On the Sumy front, Ukrainian forces pulled back to prepared lines near Myropilske as Russian forces claimed ground in three villages in the border region. Russian small-group infiltrations continued in northern Sumy Oblast, though a Kremlin-affiliated milblogger cautioned on 13 April that Russian forces “are unable to control large areas of forest” due to frequent Ukrainian counterattacks, and that a near-term seizure of Krasnopillya was “premature.” Zelenskyy warned on 17 April that Russia would likely attempt offensives in Sumy, Kharkiv, and Zaporizhzhia oblasts. This signals that Ukrainian intelligence assessments are seeing Russian ground forces assembling on multiple potential axes of advance.

The broader operational picture however displays a slowdown in Russian territorial gains. DeepState reported that Russian forces captured just 133 square kilometres in March 2026 — the lowest monthly total since June 2024. But as I noted last week that figure should not generate premature optimism. Russia has captured enormous amounts of territory since 2022 and holds approximately 20 percent of Ukraine. However, the latest figures do suggest that the combination of deeper Ukrainian drone operations and increased attacks on the Russians, improved defensive preparations, and the diversion of Russian military attention to southern Ukraine is imposing costs on Russia’s spring offensive tempo.

On the Kharkiv axis of advance, ISW’s 14 April assessment noted that Ukrainian forces continued their mid-range strike campaign against Russian radar systems in Belgorod Oblast. This was part of the larger Ukrainian campaign to degrade Russian early warning and targeting capabilities along the northeastern approaches. This campaign reflects a maturation of Ukrainian operational thinking. Ukraine is actively shaping the sensor and intelligence architecture that supports Russian air defence, artillery and aviation.

Diplomatic Negotiations. The diplomatic situation remained largely paralysed during the past week. The core challenge has not changed: Russia insists Ukraine concede approximately 20 percent of its territory, and Ukraine quite rightly refuses. At the same time, the Iran War has consumed Washington’s diplomatic bandwidth. A fourth round of U.S.-brokered trilateral talks on Ukraine that was due this month was postponed.

The Easter ceasefire was the key diplomatic event of the week. Putin announced the 32-hour truce on 9 April after having rejected Zelenskyy’s identical proposal more than a week earlier. Analysts assessed the truce as ‘smoke and mirrors’ aimed at maintaining soft-power links to traditionalist constituencies via Christian Orthodox symbolism and not as a genuine peace signal. After the truce expired, Russia refused to extend it unless Zelenskyy “accepted its terms.”

Zelenskyy’s response to all of this was to accelerate European industrial integration and drone production agreements and to treat American uncertainty not as a crisis but as a planning assumption that requires mitigation.

Strategic Assessment: Ukraine

The week of 12–19 April 2026 shows how Ukraine’s overall strategy – hurt Russia economically through long range strikes, while hurting them on the ground with enhanced drone and defensive operations, and continuing to build diplomatic support in Europe – is continuing to deny Putin his desired objectives for Ukraine. In the fifth year of the war, it is Ukraine’s forces that appear to be gaining in military effectiveness, not Russia’s. This is destroying Putin’s narrative (influential with the Trump administration) that Russian victory is inevitable. Ukraine is destroying any medium to long term prospect for Russian success.

The diplomatic situation is still concerning however. The Iran war has distorted U.S. diplomatic efforts to resolve the war in Ukraine, and has created space for continued Russian aggression. Zelenskyy’s European tour has been a strategic response to this. He seeks to build the industrial and political relationships that can sustain Ukraine’s war effort. But ultimately, this is not an enduring solution. A durable end to this war requires a change in Putin’s strategic calculus, and nothing indicates that a change is imminent. We must help Ukraine continue to increase the hurt felt by Putin and Russia to change this.

Ukraine’s long-range strike campaign has reached a level of geographic ambition and economic effect that is increasingly difficult for Russia to absorb, even when oil prices are at a high. A series of strikes at 1,000-kilometre ranges are a demonstration of the new era strike system built by Ukrainian industry, western assistance and operational creativity over the last four years. This, combined with the Ukrainian ground forces continued delay of Russian advances during the spring offensive and ongoing tech and tactics innovation, might be the very initial glimmer of turning the corner in this war. Ukraine is acting and performing like it has the strategic momentum in this war, and there is a fair argument that maybe now it does.

The Pacific

North Korea’s Ballistic Missile Launches. The Japanese Prime Minister announced on 19 April that North Korea had launched ballistic missiles. In her social media post, she stated that “at around 06:00 today, what may be multiple ballistic missiles were launched from North Korea. The items that may be ballistic missiles are believed to have already fallen outside Japan’s EEZ, and at present, Japan, the United States, and South Korea are closely coordinating to analyze information.” Both Japanese and South Korean sources reported the launches. The South Koreans have also reported that at least one of the missiles may have been submarine launched.

The Iran War and Its Pacific Reverberations. One of the most important dimensions of the Iran conflict for Indo-Pacific security is the systematic observation of modern war by China’s People’s Liberation Army (PLA). The PLA is a learning organisation, and is absorbing real-time insights on U.S. military capabilities, operational patterns, and strategic and political decision-making from the most significant American combat operation in a decade.

As I observed in an interview with RFE/RL earlier this month, the U.S. military has demonstrated it remains a very powerful organisation, capable of deploying overwhelming force and conducting sustained precision operations at least from the air and from the sea. That is a lesson Xi Jinping needs to absorb before contemplating any future action across the Taiwan Strait.

PLA-affiliated media outlet China Military Bugle published five formal lessons from the conflict for domestic military audiences: the danger of infiltration by adversary intelligence (’the enemy within’); the catastrophic cost of assuming peace during negotiations (’blind faith in peace’); the decisive logic of superior firepower; the paradox facing authoritarian regimes in modern warfare; and the paramount importance of technological self-reliance. That last lesson directly supports China’s growing domestic defence-industrial capacity. China’s military budget grew 7 percent in 2026 to roughly $277 billion, and its planners are rapidly translating wartime observations into capability investment.

The ISW-AEI China and Taiwan Update for 10 April reported that China has provided material support to Iran in ways carefully calibrated, so China remains beneath the threshold of U.S. retaliation. Multiple shipments of sodium perchlorate, a solid missile propellant precursor, arrived in Iran from China, potentially enabling partial reconstitution of Iran’s missile arsenal. More significantly, China’s commercial satellite company MizarVision, in which the Chinese government holds a minority stake, published AI-assisted satellite imagery showing precise locations of US military assets in Middle East bases. This imagery appears to have been employed by Iran to improve the accuracy of its missile and drone strikes.

The Center for International Maritime Security observed that the Hormuz crisis is stress-testing China’s own maritime strategy and exposing gaps. A key vulnerability for China is chokepoint dependency. Just as Iran’s closure of the Strait of Hormuz paralysed oil flows, so too could an adversary impose similar constraints on Chinese maritime trade. The PLA is watching the Hormuz crisis as a possible dress rehearsal for potential scenarios in the Taiwan Strait and South China Sea.

Taiwan’s Defence Budget Crisis and Deterrence. Taiwan enters this week facing a paradox. The external threat from the PLA has never been more clear, but Taiwan’s ability to construct a modern military to deter Chinese threats is being hamstrung by political dysfunction. This is becoming a deterrence failure, and Beijing is watching with satisfaction.

The NT$1.25 trillion (approximately US$40 billion) special defence budget proposed by President Lai Ching-te in November 2025, which was blocked from committee review at least ten times by KMT and TPP legislators, remained in limbo this week. The ISW-AEI April 3 China and Taiwan Update assessed that Taiwan’s main opposition parties were preventing Taiwan from acquiring systems critical to modern warfare, and that the legislative deadlock was damaging military readiness.

On 14 April, Legislative Speaker Han Kuo-yu convened a cross-caucus negotiation on the special defence budget, with the DPP version on the agenda. Two days later, the Taipei Times reported that caucus leaders agreed to move the budget negotiations forward, though the KMT’s substantive alternative — capped at NT$380 billion, roughly a third of the administration’s proposal — remained a stark point of division. U.S. Senator Jim Banks met with President Lai this week and delivered a clear message: “When you pass the special budget in the legislature, that is a signal to China, and to the rest of the world, that Taiwan is serious about peace through strength”.

Japan’s Strategic Transformation Continues. Japan’s defence transformation continues at a pace that would have seemed impossible five years ago. The country is on track to become the world’s third-largest defence spender, and is deploying combat troops to the Philippines for the first time since 1945.

Fiscal Year 2026, which commenced this month, is the fourth year of Japan’s five-year 43-trillion-yen Defence Buildup Program. The defence budget for FY2026 totals 9.04 trillion yen ($58 billion), a record high and the fourth consecutive year of record spending. Japan has already effectively achieved the 2 percent of GDP defence spending target — reaching approximately 11 trillion yen when the supplementary budget is included — a target that was set for 2027. The FY2026 budget allocates over 160 billion yen to jointly develop the next-generation Global Combat Air Programme fighter with Britain and Italy, 30 billion yen for hypersonic guided missiles, and 100 billion yen for the layered ‘Shield’ coastal defence system incorporating aerial, surface, and underwater vehicles.

The most significant operational development of the past week for Japan is its deployment of combat forces to the Philippines for Exercise Balikatan 2026 (more on this exercise below). Japan will deploy approximately 1,400 troops and naval assets, marking the first time Japanese combat personnel have set foot on Philippine soil since the Second World War. The exercise will see Japanese forces fire a missile in a ship-sinking exercise in northwestern Philippine waters facing the South China Sea. Analysts at the South China Morning Post characterised Japan’s participation as ‘a big deal’, reflecting how Tokyo is taking a larger role in regional security groupings that could face off with China in contingencies involving the Luzon Strait and northern Philippines.

Exercise Balikatan – Rehearsing the Pacific Coalition. One of the key features of security developments in the Indo-Pacific is the growing construction of a layered deterrence architecture. This includes a mesh of bilateral and multilateral agreements, military and humanitarian assistance exercises, and capability-sharing arrangements that complement and reinforce the U.S. alliances in the region.

Exercise Balikatan 2026, being held from 20 April to 8 May is a key element of this mesh of Pacific alliances and partnerships. Over 17,000 troops from seven nations — the Philippines, United States, Japan, Australia, Canada, France, and New Zealand — are participating in what will be the largest iteration of the annual exercise. Training activities span locations facing the South China Sea and the Luzon Strait. For Japan, the Reciprocal Access Agreement with the Philippines, which entered into force in September 2025, provides the legal framework for this deployment. For all participating nations, Exercise Balikatan 2026 is partially a demonstration that America’s preoccupation with Iran has not diminished the resolve of its Indo-Pacific partners to maintain deterrence in the Pacific.

Australia’s Self-Reliance Strategy. Australia released its 2026 National Defence Strategy and accompanying Integrated Investment Program on 16 April, committing AU$425 billion over a decade in the most ambitious defence investment plan in the country’s history. As I wrote during the week, the new strategy reflects a slight evolution from its 2024 predecessor: it broadens the definition of national defence to encompass civil preparedness, fuel security, and economic security. The strategy commits an additional AU$14 billion over the next four years and AU$53 billion across the decade, with defence spending projected to rise to 3 percent of GDP in the 2030’s – a slow rise in spending that does not keep pace with the major security changes occurring in the Indo-Pacific.

The 2026 Integrated Investment Program prioritises the following: the nuclear-powered submarine capability under AUKUS; accelerated maritime lethality; expanded long-range strike; integrated air and missile defence; autonomous and uncrewed systems across all domains; counter-UAS for critical infrastructure protection; and resilient multi-orbit satellite communications. Defence Minister Marles emphasised that Australia would focus in 2026 on building greater military self-reliance, while insisting the U.S. alliance remained ‘fundamental’.

But as I and other Australian analysts have noted, the quantity of defence spending will remain in the low 2% range for some years yet, and this is placing increasing pressures on balancing the AUKUS program with retaining a ready Australian Defence Force. The Australian government has little reason for high fives on its new strategy just yet.

Strategic Assessment of Pacific Issues.

The past week has (again) made one thing very clear: the security architecture of the Indo-Pacific is being stress-tested by concurrent crises in ways that are revealing the strengths of different nations and relationships, while also exposing some key vulnerabilities.

On the positive side of things, the U.S. alliance network has demonstrated resilience during a period of American distraction. Japan’s Balikatan deployment, Australia’s NDS announcement, the multilateral presence in Philippine waters are important strategic signals. They represent commitments by nations that have observed, and at least partially absorbed, the lessons of European collective security failure in the early 2020s. These nations appear to have a growing understanding that a failure of deterrence in the Pacific could be much more catastrophic than Europe’s current war and are determined ensure a similar failure is not replicated against China.

The Iran War has exposed the Indo-Pacific’s energy dependence on Middle Eastern supply routes. This is a vulnerability that decades of warning failed to adequately address. China’s systematic learning from Operation Epic Fury may produce a more capable, more informed PLA that will be harder to deter in the 2030s than the force that exists today. The diversion of significant U.S. military assets to the Persian Gulf, including two carrier strike groups, has created a temporary window of reduced conventional deterrence in the Pacific that China has noted.
