The chance is considerable at this moment, and it matters for our every city, for our every Ukrainian community. We are working to ensure that peace for Ukraine is dignified, and to secure a guarantee – a guarantee, above all, that Russia will not return to Ukraine for a third invasion. President Zelenskyy, 14 December 2025

In the past week, we have seen a more joined up approach to the Ukraine peace process from the Trump administration, and a counter-proposal from the Ukrainian President. Ukraine and Russia have traded strikes on oil tankers and commercial cargo ships, and Ukraine conducted a series of successful counter-attacks around Kupiansk.

In the Pacific, a Chinese aircraft carrier strike group conducted operations in the western Pacific off the coast of Japan, Taiwan unveiled the design for its T-Dome missile defence system, and China continued its aerial incursions into Taiwanese and Japanese airspace.

Welcome to my regular update on modern war and strategic competition.

Ukraine

Mr Zelenskyy Goes to Kupiansk. Not long ago, the Russian President claimed that Kupiansk was under Russian control. If it ever was, it isn’t anymore (see frontline update below).

And to show Putin was lying - and demonstrate again to Trump that Putin lies - the Ukrainian president travelled to the front line around Kupiansk this week. There is even proof (see image at the top of the article).

These frontline visits have been a feature of Zelenskyy’s leadership since the beginning of the war. He has travelled all over his country, and visited soldiers in bunkers, trenches, training establishments, headquarters and hospitals.

When Russia commenced its full-scale invasion of his country in February 2022, Zelenskyy stayed put and asked for help.

Compare this with Putin.

In June 2023, when Prigozhin began his short-lived mutiny and advance on Moscow, Putin rabbited out of Moscow. Even though Prigozhin never got within 100km of the Russian capital, the Russian leader preferred to get out of town than stay and lead the counter-mutiny. As far as we know, Putin has never visited Russian frontline positions, probably because he fears his own troops more than the Ukrainians.

Zelenskyy is not a perfect President, and the recent corruption scandal has tainted him. But, no one can deny his courage.

That is something that will never be said about Putin.

Peace Negotiations. This week, Russia rejected Ukrainian proposals for a ceasefire and referendum. Put forward by President Zelenskyy on 12 December, the Kremlin’s spokesman Dmitry Peskov rejected the proposal and noted that they “will not work” for Russia. The Kremlin also rejected a Ukrainian offer this week to establish a demilitarised zone in the Donbas.

Ukraine has put forward a counter proposal. The New York Times described it as follows:

The 20-point Ukrainian plan for ending nearly four years of fighting, unlikely to be accepted by Russia, is part of a Ukrainian attempt to stymie Mr. Trump’s demand, made in his proposal last month, that Ukraine secure peace by relinquishing more land than Russia now occupies.

In a 13 December social media post President Zelenskyy has covered the outlines of the plan sent to America, along with negotiators. It has three elements: security guarantees; economy, reconstruction, and investment; and, communications with Ukraine’s security partners. Zelenskyy described it as follows:

In Germany preparations are underway for the work of our military group focused on the details of security guarantees for Ukraine. Our group is led by Lieutenant General Hnatov; representatives of our army, intelligence services, and security forces are also taking part.

In the United States the group on economy, reconstruction, and investment has already begun its work this morning and will keep working to prepare a good outcome. On our end this group is led by Prime Minister Svyrydenko, Deputy Prime Minister Kachka, and Minister Sobolev with the participation of experts.

The third line of work consists of ongoing online contacts between National Security Advisers and all those brought in by the leaders’ decision. Today, as on almost every day, communication continues between the United States, Ukraine, European countries, and other participants of the Coalition of the Willing. On our side Rustem Umerov moderates these discussions.

Across all areas, our goal is the same – to bring the war to a real conclusion and to define the steps that will make peace dignified for Ukraine, with security and reconstruction – guaranteed.

I guess we will see after the weekend where we are at.

Meanwhile, as part of its ‘peace proposals’, Russia undertook another large overnight strike on cities across Ukraine. In the past 24 hours, Russia attacked targets in Kirovohrad, Mykolaiv, Odesa, Sumy, Kharkiv, Kherson, and Chernihiv regions. 495 drones and missiles were fired at Ukraine, with 430 being shot down by Ukrainian air defences.

The Front Line. The big news this week, and probably some of the best news for Ukraine’s ground war for a while, was the Ukrainian liberation of territory around the city of Kupiansk. In counterattacks executed by Ukraine’s 2nd Khartiia Corps north of Kupiansk, it appears that the Russian infiltrations into the city - and the creation of a Russian corridor into the city - have been cut off. At least for now.

The Institute for the Study of War also reports that around 200 Russian troops might be trapped in the city, surrounded by Ukrainian forces.

This tactical development demonstrates again that Putin is deceiving the Americans (and his own people) about Russian battlefield success. While I am sure that he may not be getting the full, God’s honest truth from his own military, Putin is a willing manipulator of information to perpetuate the ‘inevitable Russian victory narrative’ to shape the views of America’s peace negotiations team.

Beyond this, the Kupiansk operation does tell us a couple of other things.

First, Ukraine still has offensive capacity that can be employed where it identifies Russian weaknesses and where other conditions (such as weather, weaker units, etc) are right for exploitation. This is a good sign.

Second, it is an indication of the capacity of the new Ukrainian Corps organisation. Admittedly, it is a single case study of a corps on the offensive, but it does give some indication of the capacity of a unified corps. The corps involved, the 2nd Khartia Corps is a combined arms unit of the Ukrainian National Guard, and has five combat brigades. It was formed on April 15, 2025, as part of the C2 reforms of the Defense Forces of Ukraine.

Third, again, this shows the fallacy of the transparent battlefield concept. If the battlefield was truly transparent, such offensive actions should not be possible. But seeing lots of things on the battlefield is not the same as understanding them, and transparency is more than a physical construct. The Ukrainian attack again validates that offensive actions are still possible in the modern era that don’t require the horrendous expenditure of human lives like those of the Russians.

Despite this success, it is just a limited success. It is a bright spot in an overall grim frontline where Ukrainians and Russians continue to fight, suffering large casualties for minor territorial gains. We should be careful not to read too much into this week’s successes around Kupiansk, encouraging as it is.

Finally, this week, British intelligence issued one of its periodic updates on Russian operations and casualties. By their estimation, up until 13 December, the Russians had suffered 395,000 casualties during the 2025 calendar year. Even with all his rejections of Trump’s peace proposals in 2025, he is yet to pay a major price. He has however convinced many on the Trump administration that coercing Ukraine into handing over territory and making accommodation about the size of its military is a good idea. Russia, of course, is not being asked to make any accommodations.

Maritime Strikes. Over the past week, Ukraine has continued to strike oil tankers that are part of Russia’s shadow fleet, which are used to ship oil and avoid international sanctions. Given the Russian Navy has basically vacated large parts of the Black Sea, Ukraine is now using its advanced Sea Baby drones to attack Russia’s shadow fleet and up the pressure on Russia’s economy.

Ukraine’s missile and drone strikes against Russia also continue, including a strike this week on the massive Slavneft–YANOS refinery. This is one of the largest in Russia and located about 700 km from Ukraine’s state border. The strike was carried out by the Ukrainian 14th UAS Regiment.

Ukraine has also reportedly hit two Russian cargo vessels in the Caspian Sea.

Russia has responded by hitting commercial ships that are either docked in Ukraine or may be shipping goods to or from Ukraine. Russia has now hit 4 Turkish ships in the past couple of days, including three in the port of Odesa.

The Pacific

Chinese carrier patrols off Japan. This week, the Chinese PLA-N aircraft carrier strike group, which included CNS Liaoning, the Type 055 cruiser CNS Nanchang, Type 052D destroyers CNS Xining and CNS Kaifeng, and Type 901 fast combat support ship CNS Hulunhu, transited northeast through the waters between Okinawa’s main island and Minami-Daito Island. It then sailed north to waters approximately 190 km east of Japan’s Kikai Island.

These Chinese patrols serve several purposes. First, they are training the Chinese navy in the conduct of aircraft carrier strike group operations, including the integration of different ships and high tempo aircraft operations. Second, they serve an operational purpose to provide PLA joint theatre commands experience in commanding high-level expeditionary naval operations.

Finally, they serve obvious political purposes. This includes projecting the will of the Chinese Communist Party, to test the reaction of Japanese military organisations as well as the U.S. administration’s support for Japan, and to cow regional nations.

The Chinese aircraft carrier naval capability provides little utility against the U.S. navy and its fleet of much more capable aircraft carriers and integration of them into joint warfighting plans. But they are excellent coercive tools against every other nation in the region.

As the image below shows, this deployment by the Liaoning carrier strike group is the furtherest north such a task force has ventured since Chinese carriers started operating in the Pacific. It is also closer the Japan’s home islands than other deployments - clearly a political message.

The Chinese continue to stretch their legs. I would not be surprised to see a Chinese aircraft carrier strike group doing a circumnavigation of Australia in the coming years to really stretch the legs of the PLA-N, ascertain the reaction of America given its continued build up of capacity in Australia, and to test the rather rubbery and inconsistent strategic resolve of the Albanese government with regards to China.

Taiwan’s T-Dome. More details about Taiwan’s proposed national air, drone and missile defence system, known as “T-Dome”, became available this week. According to a report in the Taipei Times, the system will be comprised of several layers (see diagram below) and will cost just short of $13 billion U.S. dollars.

T-Dome will contain both indigenous elements. Taiwanese elements include the Chiang Kung ballistic missile interceptor. This system, which is now in production, features a two-stage interceptor and includes an active electronically-scanned array (AESA) radar domestically produced in Taiwan. It is able to engage targets at an altitude of at least 70 kilometres. The War Zone recently had an excellent in-depth look at this missile defence system, which you can read here.

Other domestic elements include the TKIII system (or Sky Bow 3), a single-stage, Mach 7 interceptor for missiles and aircraft; and, the TCII beyond visual range air defence missile. The project will be implemented between now and 2033, according to Minister of National Defense Wellington Koo.

Japan’s “Missile Archipelago”. This article in The Japan Times provides interesting political context, including domestic political issues, on the establishment of an electronic warfare division by the Japanese Self Defence Force on the Japanese island of Yonaguni. The island site just 110 kilometres east of Taiwan, making intelligence collection from facilities on the island a critical element of Japan’s developing counter-strike capacity.

AUSMIN 2025. Finally this week, the annual AUSMIN talks have were conducted in Washington DC. Both Australia and America issued official releases about this year’s meeting and the key outcomes. Key themes were multi-lateral cooperation in the Pacific, economics and the US-Australia military alliance.

The US and Australia committed to work with Japan and the Philippines on the Luzon economic corridor, as well as partnering with PNG to develop communications and transport infrastructure. Cooperation with PNG will also include disaster management. Collaboration on more assured ICT networks in the Pacific is also part of the way forward, and this includes partnering the Japan, Taiwan and NZ to deliver this. Cyber resilience with south East Asian nations will also be a priority.

Economics was a key element of the just-released US National Security Strategy and it would have been a surprise if it wasn’t discussed at AUSMIN. The meeting agreed to rapid execution of the Critical Minerals framework, which includes Japanese investment, and USD600 million in funding from EXIM in the US will be provided for the critical minerals supply chain.

The meeting confirmed that it is now full steam ahead with AUKUS, as announced in the press in the past 24 hours. This includes another one billion dollar payment (US or Oz not specified) to the US soon for expanding the US submarine base.

Work will continue on expanding infrastructure at key Australian air bases to facilitate USAF access. The USMC MRF-D in Darwin will expand in capacity including the pre-positioning of V22 aircraft. This will probably mean an increase in the permanent numbers of Marines in Darwin and elsewhere in Australia. Accompanying this will be new command and control arrangements for the expanding US military presence in Australia with the formation of the “U.S. Oversight and Support Group – Australia”. This is important given the establishment of the new Submarine Rotational Force-West in 2027, and the regular visits and maintenance of U.S. SSNs at HMAS Stirling in Western Australia.

Overall, no real surprises. As I wrote in a post earlier this week:

Australia got what it wanted - American presence and investment in Australian infrastructure and critical minerals as well as no public commentary on Australia’s low defence spending as a % of GDP. America got more money for its submarine enterprise, more real estate in Australia, an enhanced strategic logistics hub, as well as some useful multi-lateral cooperation on security in the Pacific region and some additional munitions production by an ally. Lots of European nations would love this deal!

1. We Can’t Buy Our Way Out

T.X. Hammes is one of America’s foremost thinkers and authors on the future of war, and the impact of new technologies. Over the past decade, he has written extensively about the impact of massed, cheap autonomous systems, and in this piece, he explores the technologies of drone and containerised munitions. Importantly, he argues for changes in mindset and warfighting concepts to shift how the American military thinks about war and warfighting. You can read his piece, for the Stimson Center, at this link.

2. Asia and the U.S. National Defense Authorisation Act

Somewhat balancing what some have viewed as a catastrophic 2025 U.S. national Defense Strategy, the U.S. National Defense Authorisation Act (NDAA) provides more insight into American defense priorities. This analysis, by Zack Cooper from AEI, examines some of the key elements of the NDAA and how they assist America’s allies in Asia. Initiatives include Taiwan’s drone defences, restricting any Trump drawdown of the U.S. presence in Korea and strengthening the U.S.-Philippines alliance. You can read the full article here.

3. Humility and Leadership

Military Review is one of those ‘must read’ professional journals for junior and middle ranking military leaders. Published by the U.S. Army, it is well worth scanning every time a new issue comes out. Its latest edition contains a very interesting piece on the role of humility in leadership. And, to the credit of the authors, they fully explore the potential downsides of humility in military leadership. Overall, this is a thoughtful piece that provides useful insights for developing contemporary military leaders. You can read the full piece at this link.

4. Managing Nuclear Risk in an AI world

If you are anything like me, you are probably terrified by the prospect of AI having anything to do with nuclear command and control systems. Given how awash we all seem to be now with AI-generated slime, why would be put the most dangerous weapons ever created anywhere near this imperfect technology. While some might reason that humans are similarly imperfect (we are), humans at least have an existential interest in preserving the now 80 year long non-use of nuclear weapons. This piece examines how AI might amplify misperceptions by strategic decision-makers, leading to more rapid escalation towards the use of nuclear weapons in a conflict. As the author writes, “the risk is not only that adversaries will misinterpret signals, but that domestic publics will demand responses based on false premises.” A sobering piece from the Bulletin of Atomic Scientists, you can read the full article at this link.

5. Decision-Making and Human-Machine Teaming

One of the areas where human-machine teaming is starting to gain a lot of traction is in decision-support for military personnel. It is a subject that experts in the field have been examining and writing about for some time. The prospect of AI leading to better decision-making is something that most military organisations have at least been pondering for the better part of a decade. In this article, the author explores the integration of AI into military officer training and education. You can read the article, published by Small Wars Journal, at this link.
