It was with some sadness that I departed Ukraine on Tuesday evening. It was good to get back and see my friends in Kyiv and elsewhere. My visit gave me a good opportunity to also catch up on the state of the strategic and tactical adaptation battles that are an inherent part of this war. I published six updates about my visit, which are available here at Futura Doctrina. There will be a couple more articles about the visit to follow in the next week or so.

It has been another huge week in international affairs and the war in Ukraine.

The U.S. government is levying new sanctions on Russia while Sweden is selling up to 150 Gripen fighters to Ukraine. America is also allowing the use of European long-range missiles against Russia, further turning up the heat on Putin.

In the Pacific, China continued its operations around Taiwan while purging senior leaders at home. There were also significant defence and intelligence announcements from Japan and Australia, as well as new naval vessel launches in Singapore and South Korea.

Welcome to my weekly update on modern war and strategic competition.

Ukraine

Summit Delayed. This big news this week was the announcement that the previously announced summit between Trump and Putin to have been held in Budapest had been delayed. Apparently, the U.S. Secretary of State decided that he didn’t like Russian foreign minister Lavrov’s attitude towards the peace process, which was essentially that there is no peace process. Trump stated afterwards that he did not want a wasted meeting.

New U.S. Sanctions on Russia. Strategic whiplash continued this week as the Trump administration announced new sanctions on Russian energy production and exports. As President Trump stated before the sanctions were announced, “Every time I speak to Vladimir, I have good conversations and then they don’t go anywhere.”

Putin was quick to respond. Speaking with journalists after the new American measures were announced, Putin stated that:

The new U.S. sanctions are an attempt to put pressure on Russia. No self-respecting country ever does anything under pressure. Russia is a self-respecting country. [The sanctions are] serious for us and will have certain consequences. But they will not significantly impact our economic well-being.

Additionally, there are reports that the White House is preparing additional sanctions packages if Putin continues to resist negotiations to end the war.

European Missiles Now Permitted Deep inside Russia. The new sanctions were not the only good news from the Trump administration about America’s support for Ukraine this week. It emerged that the U.S. had removed its restrictions on Ukraine’s employment of European long-range missiles like the Storm Shadow.

The Ukrainians duly struck the Bryansk Chemical Plant. The facility produces a large amount of Russia’s gunpowder, explosives and rocket fuel components that are used in the Russian military’s ammunition and missiles.

In another interesting move, the authority for such strikes has been shifted from the Pentagon to SACEUR. This should streamline the decision-process for such strikes. Whether this is an indication of Trump’s displeasure with anyone in the Pentagon is only speculation. But as Michael Weiss notes:

Transferring authorization from Hegseth to Grynkewich is almost the scoop itself. Cuts Elbridge Colby out of the process, and one wonders how and why this decision was taken.

Gripens to Ukraine. This week, President Zelenskyy visited Sweden and signed an agreement that signalled Ukraine’s intentions to purchase over 100 of the aircraft. The first is due to arrive in 2026. As Zelenskyy noted in this social media post on this topic:

These are great aircraft, strong aviation platforms capable of performing a wide range of missions. Everyone can see what threats they help counter.

While no one doubts that these are capable aircraft, when they might be procured and enter service is unclear. And, now, Ukraine must manage a transition from older Soviet aircraft to newer western models, while juggling the introduction into service of at least three western fighters: American F16s, French Mirages and Swedish Gripens. The Ukrainian’s are clever and resourceful, but this will be a steep hill to climb.

New Maritime Drones. Ukraine this week unveiled updated variants of its indigenous uncrewed maritime strike vessels that have been employed to good effect against the Russians in the Black Sea. The new drones have longer range, apparently now able to operate anywhere in the Black Sea. They also carry a heavier weapons load out and employ artificial intelligence for targeting.

More Russian Strategic Air Attacks. Not long after the announcement of the delay to the summit between Putin and Trump, Putin resumed his large-scale bombardment of Ukrainian cities. After a relatively light week of attacks, deadly strikes took place on the evenings of the 23rd, 24th and 25th of October.

Russia has also now deployed rocket-propelled glide bombs which can strike at distances of around 150-180 kilometres from their launching aircraft. Over the past week or so, Russia has used this longer range weapon on multiple occasions. The jet engine not only increases the range of the bombs, it makes intercepting them much more difficult for the Ukrainians who are still working on a systemic solution to this problem. The bombs also have improved electronics which are more resistant to electronic jamming by the Ukrainians. The War Zone features a useful examination of these new weapons, which you can read at this link.

The Pacific

Japan Defence Spending. The new Japanese Prime Minister, Sanae Takaichi, stated this week that she intends to speed up Japan’s journey to 2% of GDP on defence spending. The objective now is to achieve this in the 2025 financial year. This is two years earlier that had been the previous plan. The 2% objective represents a doubling of Japanese defence spending in a short period of time. Given the size of the Japanese economy, this will see the Japanese defence budget leap from 10th in the world to 6th or even 5th in the short term.

In related news, Japan has announced the creation of a new National Intelligence Agency which will oversee and coordinate the operations of collection and analytical agencies across the Japanese government. Chief Cabinet Secretary Minoru Kihara told a press conference Friday he had been directed by the Prime Minister to strengthen the government’s intelligence functions, noting that:

Boosting the country’s intelligence capabilities is a pressing issue in order to protect the national interest and ensure people’s safety as we face the harshest and most complicated security environment since the end of World War II. We want to sort out points of discussion soon.

In a final piece of news from Japan, Russian bombers this week tested Japan’s air interception capabilities. Two Russian Tu-95 bombers, accompanied by two Su-35 fighters, initially flew towards Japan’s Sado Island before turning north.

Coming in the same week at Russia’s incursion into the airspace of Lithuania, there is clearly a full court press by the Russians to test the defences of its Asian and European neighbours as it plans its post-Ukraine military campaigns.

China Purges - Again. In what has become almost as much of a ground hog day event as Trump’s changes about Ukraine, the Chinese Communist Party this week announced the departure (purge) of more senior leaders. Members of the Central Military Committee and the Central Committee were targeted this time. Nine generals were expelled in total according to reports. This included the commanders of the PLA Rocket Force, the commander of the Eastern Theatre Command and the commander of the Peoples Armed Police. These are all pretty important people for any Taiwan contingency - either for the planning and execution of warfighting plans, or for planning any subsequent occupation of the island.

Also included were a couple of political commissars. It is unlikely they will be missed because they were probably much less likable than the political officer in The Hunt for Red October.

The apparent crime of all these PLA officers was to undermine the authority of President Xi. No word on whether they received a gold watch on the way out the door, but it seems improbable.

What do these purges mean for PLA modernisation and warfighting capabilities?

It is hard to judge this accurately unless there is an actual conflict. Most military organisations move around their senior leaders every year or two, so losing a commander is not the big deal that some would have us believe. Military organisations are designed to lose and replace people.

I think the real impact is the chilling effect it could have on the remainder of the institution, and the willingness of other military officers to innovate, take risk or in any way question CCP dogma. And this, of course, is a large part of Xi’s calculus in these purges.

Taiwan Military Exercises. In the past week, Taiwan’s Navy finished its annual Hai Chiang live-fire naval exercises in the waters around Taiwan. This year, the exercises focussed on anti-submarine warfare. Just after the conclusion of the Hai Chiang activity, the Taiwanese Marine Corps executed an amphibious assault exercise near the city of Kaohsiung.

China Not a Threat to Taiwan? During his press conference with Australian Prime Minister Alabanese this week, President Trump described how he did not see China as a threat to Taiwan. He stated that “China doesn’t want to do that. I don’t see that at all with President Xi. I think we’re going to get along very well as it pertains to Taiwan and others.”

Trump then went on to explain that “the United States is the strongest military power in the world by far, it’s not even close. We have the best of everything, and nobody’s going to mess with that.”

Given Trump’s tendency to admire authoritarians, and his continuous misjudgment about Russia’s president over the war in Ukraine, is the government of Taiwan likely to bet the survival of its vibrant and prosperous democracy on such comments? Unlikely.

New Asian Naval Vessels. This week, a couple of interesting new naval vessels were launched in Singapore and South Korea. The Singaporean vessel, the first of a new class of frigate-drone mothership hybrids (or Multi-Role Combat Vessels) for the Singaporean Navy, was launched this week in Singapore. It will be largest vessel in Singapore’s navy.

The second vessel launched this week was the first of a new batch of South Korean KSS-III submarines. This is a very advanced boat, with Lithium Ion batteries for stealthy underwater running. It also features ten vertical launch tubes for ballistic missiles.

Trump and Albo Meeting. Early this week, the Australian Prime Minister (known down under as Albo) met with President Trump in the White House. As White House meetings go, it was a roaring success. As one Australian media outlet noted, the Australian Prime Minister “leaves Washington with everything he came for: a deal on critical minerals, an assurance on AUKUS, and an alliance reaffirmed.”

The two leaders signed an agreement on developing critical minerals projects, with six of these being in Australia. While not the only collaboration that America is undertaking on this topic, Australia will be an important pillar of the cooperation among western nations to break China’s stranglehold on these minerals.

The agreement between the U.S. and Australia on critical minerals and rare earths can be viewed at this link.

The meeting also reaffirmed the AUKUS agreement and that there would be some streamlining of the agreement (what the streamlining is, we don’t yet know). Trump noted that the plan for Australia to acquire U.S. Virginia Class subs is full steam ahead. Now, Australia has to deliver on its part of the deal, which includes significant naval infrastructure to support American and British nuclear submarines in Western Australia, and training more submariners.

1. House of Dynamite

Ok, you got me. This isn’t a book or an article. But it is my choice of the week. This new film by Katheryn Bigelow is the first one that I can remember that got my heart rate up as much as it did. It is spectacularly successive in immersing the viewer into three realistic stories about a nuclear missile bearing down on an American city. As the scriptwriter Noah Oppenheimer notes “It’s too easy to push it out of our minds. It’s too easy to ignore it because it feels abstract. But it’s a calamity that could unfold at any moment.” The film is available to watch on Netflix.

2. Tom’s Review of A House of Dynamite

Complementing my recommended film above, this article from Tom Nichols explores both Katheryn Bigelow’s extraordinary movie as well as the issue of nuclear weapons. As Tom writes in his article, “Americans, along with billions of other people on this planet, once had a healthy fear of nuclear war. They knew, even if they did not dwell on it, that they could wake up and make a cup of coffee, and then, before they had a chance to finish breakfast, they and the civilization they took for granted every day could be extinguished. That fear seems mostly gone now.” This film certainly should prompt the return of some that fear. You can read Tom’s excellent article at this link.

3. The Stagnant Order

In this article for Foreign Affairs, the author argues that the era of large-scale industrial expansion and economic growth of nations is coming to a close. As the author argues, the “era of power transitions is ending. For the first time in centuries, no country is rising fast enough to overturn the global balance.” You can read the full article at this link.

4. Maturing Counter Drone Capability

In this piece, published by the Modern War Institute, the authors propose that “Ukraine’s approach to capability generation can be thought of as a wartime adolescent: fast, clever, and brilliant at learning, but also inconsistent, unstructured, and prone to internal conflict. This is not a criticism—it is a diagnosis.” They then outline a plan to mature the Ukrainian innovation system to ensure a more strategic approach to the development of counter-drone systems. You can read this article here.

5. An AI Cataclysm

In this article, the author explores some of the pitfalls and opportunities with AI. As he notes, “the first application of a technology that mirrors human intelligence is to undermine the cultivation of the real thing.” Given AI is being used more widely in military and national security functions, this piece certainly made me think. I hope it does for you as well. You can read the article at this link.
