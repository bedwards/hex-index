Authoritative PLA texts published since 2022 emphasize enhancing the aggressiveness of the military strategic guidelines, noting that the PLA must take the initiative to seize strategic opportunities and create favorable external conditions. At the strategic level, this means no longer simply defending China’s borders and passively responding to threats but rather actively building and shaping a military posture around China’s periphery that can adequately respond to China’s perceived threats. The implementation of this guidance can be seen in China’s more assertive military posture in the South China Sea and around Taiwan. Report to Congress on Military and Security Developments Involving the People’s Republic of China 2025

On 29 December, just as the summit between President’s Zelenskyy and Trump was finishing at Mar-a-lago, and hundreds of journalists were filing their initial analyses of the meeting, the Peoples Liberation Army Eastern Theatre Command announced that it had commenced exercise "Justice Mission 2025" in the Taiwan Strait.

Similar to other joint exercises conducted in 2025 and 2024, this was an activity to rehearse future military campaigns to “shield” Taiwan from outside military forces that might come to its aid. At the same time, the bottom of the shields in the PLA posters for the exercise are shaped like blades. This is intended by the Chinese to “cut off the delusional dream of Taiwan independence.” Thus, Justice Mission 2025 is intended to project the image of an advanced, capable PLA that can both attack Taiwan while fending off assistance from the military forces of America, Japan and elsewhere.

The exercise is rehearsing a range of military activities and features live fire drills.

The exercise is one of President Xi’s ways of demonstrating his displeasure at the 17 December 2025 announcement by the U.S. government of a massive weapons sale to Taiwan. With a value of US$11.1 billion, this is the largest single U.S. sale to Taiwan in history, and includes highly symbolic weapon systems such as the HIMARS long-range precision strike system.

Finally, we should not discount that Justice Mission 2025 is also designed to message China’s continuing displeasure with Japan. Two of the exercise zones for this PLA activity would (theoretically) permit operations against Japanese military targets on Yonaginajima to the east.

The aim of this article is to provide insights into the new PLA exercise, including locations and objectives, and what the political and military leaders of democracies in Asia and beyond can learn from this joint military exercise.

Exercise Locations

Exercise Justice Mission 2025 has taken place at multiple locations around Taiwan. As the map below demonstrates, these locations correspond to areas where a future Chinese campaign to subjugate Taiwan would focus many of its aerial and naval operations. The northern, eastern and southern zones would correspond to areas where the PLA might conduct “shield” operations to prevent foreign military aide reaching Taiwan. The western and north western zones correspond to transit zones for any aerial and maritime invasion fleets, which would need to be protected by the PLA.

The latest exercise is covering a much larger area than previous exercises going back to 2022. What might we take from this?

First, the PLA is continuing to evolve its operational planning and it is using differing exercise areas over time to assess the optimum locations for any future campaign designed to either quarantine Taiwan or to invade it.

Second, the zones for Justice Mission 2025 are much more interconnected, even though they don’t form a complete, single ring of zones around Taiwan.

Third, the Chinese are messaging the Taiwanese that “the noose is tightening” over time and that the sooner they negotiate a return to the PRC, the better it is for them.

Finally, the PLA is expanding in size and capability. Since 2022, the PLA’s naval fleet allotted to the Eastern and Southern theatre commands has expanded from 2 to 4 cruisers, from 24 to 30 destroyers, from 32 to 39 frigates, and from 31 to 34 attack submarines. The bomber fleet allotted to the Eastern and Southern theatre commands has grown from 250 to 300. Finally, China’s ballistic missile force - operated by the PLA Rocket Force - has also grown significantly in the last three years.

While the maps demonstrate the physical environment of the exercise, the more important exercise “location” is the minds of Taiwanese and foreign observers. While the Taiwanese are quite familiar with decades of CCP coercion, it is unlikely that Xi and his party will ease back on their cognitive warfare against this prosperous, democratic nation that provides an alternative model of governance for the Chinese people.

Foreign audiences for the cognitive warfare associated with Justice Mission 2025 includes politicians, military leaders, business leaders with significant China investments and influential think tank personnel. The key messages that the Chinese are sending are resolve, willingness to sacrifice and unwillingness to negotiate over what the Chinese Communist Party sees as the integral place of Taiwan with the PRC.

Perhaps the most important exercise location is the mind of the U.S. president. The Chinese leader will have been observing just how much Russia’s president is able to manipulate the views of President Trump about the war in Ukraine. Xi may be attempting something similar with this exercise. Xi’s key objective is to project an “inevitable victory” narrative (similar to Putins) and that American intervention would be expensive, bloody and ultimately futile. Xi is also shaping the environment for his 2026 meeting with Trump.

Exercise Objectives

The key military goals of the activity, as released by PLA’s Eastern Theatre Command, include live-fire anti-ship drills at sea and in the airspace north and southwest of Taiwan. This included PLA destroyers, frigates, fighter-bombers, unmanned aerial vehicles, and other forces taking part in the drills. Other operations conducted on the exercise include maritime and aerial targeting operations, simulated strikes against land targets, and live-fire anti-ship missile launches.

The Chinese have also admitted that simulated decapitation activities were a part of the exercise. These operations are designed to kill or capture key political and leadership targets in the early phase of a military campaign in order to degrade decision-making and execution at the political level in Taiwan, and in military operations of the Taiwanese military. National Defense University professor Zhang Chi stated in an interview that “decapitation” is one of the key terms of this exercise, and that the exercise had undertaken simulated strikes on symbolic targets associated with the leaders of the “Taiwan independence” separatist forces.

One of the key lessons the Chinese leadership have learned from the war in Ukraine is to not let enemy leaders live to rally their people and gain foreign support. The CCP cannot afford the emergence of a “Taiwanese Zelenskyy” in the event of a military invasion of the island, and is sure to have multiple contingency plans to prevent this.

The Chinese Coast Guard also announced that they had formed maritime task forces to conduct “comprehensive law-enforcement patrols” in waters surrounding Taiwan and in the areas near the Matsu and Wuqiu islands.

An important higher level objective of this exercise will be to continue developing the skills of the commanders, staff, units and overall command and control of the Eastern Theatre Command. This command is one of the five joint commands established as part of a transformation of the PLA into a more integrated warfighting organisation. These commands operate under the direction of the Central Military Commission, chaired by Xi.

The Chinese theatre commands are responsible for developing and wargaming theatre-specific plans for warfighting, responding to natural disasters and military contingencies, as well as the day-to-day operations focussed on Chinese sovereignty, which includes the kind of coercive military activities conducted daily around Taiwan. As the 2025 Annual Report to Congress on China’s Military and Security Developments issued by the Pentagon notes, these exercises emphasise “the PLA’s ability to coordinate military operations across multiple theatres and domains”.

The Eastern Theatre Command will be just one of the Chinese joint commands that have been training, planning and exercising in order to implement the most recent Chinese military operating concept called Multi-Domain Precision Warfare. As the Pentagon’s 2025 China report notes:

Multi-Domain Precision Warfare (MDPW) is the PLA’s core operational concept, representing an evolution of the PLA’s operational thinking for a potential future conflict with the United States.

The PLA’s Eastern Theatre Command has a variety of military resources to carry out its mission. Its key military formations include three PLA Ground Forces Group Armies (71st, 72nd, and 73rd) which each have around six combat brigades as well as other supporting brigades (aviation, special operations, artillery, engineers). The Eastern Theatre Command also commands the Eastern Theatre Navy, its naval aviation division, coastal defence units and two marine brigades. It also has 13 Air Force fighter and UAV brigades and regiments as well as a Bomber Division (equipped with different H6 variants), and 11 PLA Rocket Force brigades.

These short-notice, large joint exercises also have a range of other objectives for Chinese leaders at the theatre command, PLA and political levels.

First, the announcement was made over a low tempo period for several nations as well as at a time when much of the attention of the U.S. government and others was focused on the talks between Presidents Zelenskyy and Trump. This timing will have been to test the reaction of Taiwanese, Japanese and U.S. reporting and decision-making systems. The Taiwanese, who observed Chinese preparations for the exercise, were able to quickly react and undertake their own response training as the Chinese ramped up Justice Mission 2025. Both sides are certain to be studying each other’s reactions as the exercises progressed.

Second, the tendency of the Chinese to conduct larger and more complex joint exercises over the past few years is aimed at normalising large-scale Chinese military activity around the coasts, and in the air, in the vicinity of Taiwan. This normalisation of military activity, at increasing scale and frequency, complicates the ability of the U.S., Japan and others to respond because they have to make choices, alone and collectively, about when the increasing scale becomes worthy of an intervention. This normalisation is also central to Chinese strategic and operational deception plans for any future military operations against Taiwan.

A third goal for Justice Mission 2025 to is to test and refine China’s strategic messaging about its resolve and its capability to secure its strategic objectives regarding Taiwan. At the CCP’s 2022 White Paper on Taiwan states:

Resolving the Taiwan question and realizing China's complete reunification is a shared aspiration of all the sons and daughters of the Chinese nation. It is indispensable for the realization of China's rejuvenation…Taiwan Is Part of China - This Is an Indisputable Fact.

Exercises such as Justice Mission 2025 serve as just one element of the large Chinese effort to convince the world that they will never give up on seeking to absorb Taiwan. From a military perspective, the PLA is becoming more sophisticated in the timing and manner of announcement of these activities, as well as the kind of media it releases that is related to the exercises. For this exercises, videos and a myriad of posters were released by the PLA as well as other Chinese government agencies.

The Chinese are also analysing the responses of Taiwan and other nations in the information domain. Taiwan, well used to Chinese aggression, issues media releases and useful videos about the Chinese exercises on social media. It also conducted regular briefings about Justice Mission 2025, and Taiwanese responses.

Another objective of Justice Mission 2025 and joint exercises like it is to improve the familiarisation and trust between the force elements drawn from different PLA services. Chinese propaganda videos have shown Chinese Coast Guard and Naval operations, as well as the live firing of long-range missiles. Ultimately, the Eastern Theatre Command will be seeking to improve its joint integration for a real military campaign against Taiwan. As I wrote in a previous article on this topic:

Developing expertise in that single domain requires focus and time. Truly effective modern military institutions need to integrate the operations of forces from across the air, land, sea, space and cyber domains. This joint force is then employed to pose multiple dilemmas for an adversary and eventually, break down their cohesion and overwhelm them. Just putting images of different parts of the PLA joint force in a single video does not mean that the PLA is able to wield them all as an effective and unified joint force.

This integration is not just about inter-service integration. It will also include functional integration. Functions that this will include are air, missile and drone defence, offensive fires, surveillance and reconnaissance and logistics. Each of these are complex in each domain. Multidomain integration is very tough and needs constant rehearsal and learning.

A fifth objective of the Chinese for Justice Mission 2025 will be to provide additional data about the effectiveness of PLA weapon systems. This data can then be used to analyse how individual weapons as well as force structure elements can add to the overall joint campaign that might be executed against Taiwan.

Finally, the most important objective for the Chinese in activities such as Justice Mission 2025 is to hone their understanding of thinking and decision-making of political and military leaders in Taiwan and America. Xi and other senior members of the Chinese Communist Party leadership will in particular be observing the American reaction.

The recent U.S. National Security Strategy released by the Trump administration contained the following passage:

Deterring a conflict over Taiwan, ideally by preserving military overmatch, is a priority. We will also maintain our longstanding declaratory policy on Taiwan, meaning that the United States does not support any unilateral change to the status quo in the Taiwan Strait…We will build a military capable of denying aggression anywhere in the First Island Chain.

Xi and the strategic thinkers who advise him will be watching for how the U.S. president responds to this exercise, what it portends for the future of the China-America relationship and what signals the American military and foreign affairs institutions send about defending Taiwan against Chinese military aggression.

Assessment: PLA Joint Exercises are Learning Opportunities for Taiwan and America

The PLA is clear that emerging technologies are crucial to military modernization, and to building a mutually reinforcing economic and military industrial system. But it is also clear that such technologies on their own are not sufficient for achieving a world-class military. Recent technological progress in the PRC has been impressive, but it is not a panacea. And some warn that overreliance on certain technologies could lead to path dependency that will be difficult to break free from. Arran Hope, New Quality Combat Forces Underpin Military Modernization, 22 December 2025

The Chinese are not the only ones who have an opportunity to learn from large-scale military exercises such as Justice Mission 2025 and its 2025 and 2024 predecessors.

Military and intelligence agencies across the Pacific, especially the U.S. Indo-Pacific Command based in Hawaii, but also in Taiwan, Japan and elsewhere, will be observing the exercise closely with a variety of human and technical resources. In some respects, this latest Chinese exercise is a Christmas gift for intelligence agencies across the Pacific region.

What might be some of the possible insights to be gleaned from this activity?

First, how proficient are the Chinese at planning and executing joint operations? Multi-domain integration, and the conduct of cross domain activities, is a demanding and complex undertaking for any military institution. China has only recently commenced its ‘joint journey’, but is a fast learner. With Multi Domain Precision Warfare as the guiding doctrine for the joint employment of forces, external intelligence agencies will seek to analyse how rapidly the PLA are improving their theatre level joint operations capacity and doctrine.

Crucially, the Chinese ability to achieve surprise will be assessed. Intelligence agencies will be looking for signs of Chinese deception, camouflage and other measures that might be rehearsed in Justice Mission and employed in a real military contingency. In developing this understanding of China joint military capacity, and its ability to generate surprise, it is likely to indicate critical vulnerabilities in the Chinese military system that can be exploited in any future conflict.

Second, how good are basic Chinese military weapons systems ? Many different agencies are likely to conduct collection activities to better understand PLA sensors, weapons, command and control networks and other advanced technologies deployed by the Chinese. Given one of the aims of the PLA large-scale joint exercises will be to test new weapon systems, these exercises can provide a good opportunity for Western intelligence agencies to update their threat libraries based on the latest Chinese equipment, sensors and technologies. For example, Justice Mission 2025 is the first deployment of the new Chinese Type 075/Yushen class LHD, the Hainan, in the waters around Taiwan. Many will be seeking insights into its capabilities at sea.

Third, how is the Chinese military progressing towards “new quality combat forces”? An important element of analysing Chinese equipment and weapons will be an assessment of the PLA’s progress in the development of what it describes as “new quality combat forces”. This concept is the the integration of emerging technologies with military capabilities, and is central to the modernisation of the PLA. As Arran Hope wrote in a recent article for the Jamestown Foundation, “the concept is important to the Party’s attempts to design a national system that fuses economic progress and military strength into an overarching “national strategic system and capabilities.”

As such, the exercise offers the chance for an assessment of how new technologies such as drones, AI supported decision-making, digitised command and control are being integrated into the PLA. It also provides an opportunity to see how these new technologies are forcing change in military structures, doctrine and warfighting concepts. This effort might also assist with assessments about the impact of corruption and removal of senior PLA leaders in the past couple of years.

Fourth, how effective is the command and control between Eastern Theatre Command and the Central Military Commission? Understanding Chinese strategic and operational C2 is crucial because it informs us about how much freedom of action the Commander of the Eastern Theatre Command has, and what kind of operational tempo he might generate with his military forces. This also provides insights into how centralised or otherwise the national to theatre command and control construct is. This can reveal targetable vulnerabilities.

Fifth, how well synchronised is the Eastern Theatre command with other commands such as Southern Theatre Command as well as supporting space, cyber and information commands? There is no way that the Eastern Theatre command could act alone in any military campaign against Taiwan. It would require support from other theatre commands, especially if there is a protracted conflict. Therefore, what are the links between these commands, how good are the working relationships and processes between them, and again, what are the targetable vulnerabilities?

Sixth, what is the current quality of PLA leadership and their personnel overall? PLA exercises provide insights into the quality of the humans in the Chinese military and whether their training and education supports the development and employment of an effective, modern joint force. Key issues that will be watched by external observers of Justice Mission 2025 include how much free play is permitted (that is, how much does Xi trust his generals) and whether there are senior command and leadership changes in the wake of the exercise. These exercises provide valuable insights into the leadership culture as well as the training and education frameworks of key institutions within the PLA.

Seventh, how effective are Chinese military sustainment operations? One of the problems with short military exercises like this is that they rarely rehearse the full array of logistic elements that underpin military campaigns. But any insights into the conduct of joint logistics operations during Justice Shield 2025 will be invaluable because logistics is almost always one of the weakest elements in expeditionary military campaigns. Therefore, intelligence agencies and other observers will be looking for indications of the PLA joint logistics capabilities, how they are deployed and how they are used during the exercise.

Eighth, how good are open source sensors and analysts at tracking Chinese military activity? An array of think tanks and analysts follow developments in the Chinese military system as well as the larger civil-military integration that supports the development of the PLA. They publish images and their findings on social media and on institutional websites. As shown by the war in Ukraine, the open source capabilities offered by think tanks, individuals, private imagery collection companies and corporate intelligence organisations have provided a useful supplement to traditional military intelligence agencies. Over the past decade there has been a growth in a similar open source capability focused on Pacific issues. Both the Chinese and western intelligence agencies are sure to want to build their understanding of this issue.

Finally, are the Chinese learning and improving at large-scale joint operations? Justice Mission 2025 will be analysed alongside previous large-scale joint exercises to assess whether the PLA is actually getting better at planning and executing such activities. As observed during the war in Ukraine, the Russian military has ‘learned how to learn better’ in the past four years, while at the same time nurturing a learning community between Russia, Iran, China and North Korea. As I wrote in a recent report on this topic:

A new adversary learning and adaptation bloc has emerged. While not a formal alliance, China, Russia, Iran and North Korea have developed a mesh of different agreements and strategic partnerships that have allowed these authoritarian regimes to construct a connected knowledge market on 21st century strategic competition and conflict.

Therefore, has China learned how to learn better from its successive large-scale joint exercises and how has this learning improved their military effectiveness at the tactical, operational and strategic levels. This is vital because the learning and adaptation culture of a military institution plays a large role in determining its tactical and strategic success. Understanding the Chinese military adaptation system will be crucial to developing allied war plans.

Justice Mission 2025: Unwelcome, But a Useful Learning Activity

In many respects, these large-scale Chinese joint exercises are a gift to foreign intelligence agencies. Even with the best operational security and deception plans, it is unavoidable that the Chinese will unveil the capability of many different elements of their military institution.

While the aggression of these activities is unwelcome, they do provide Taiwan, America and those who support Taiwan with a wide range of activities to observe, assess and respond to China’s developing theatre-level campaigning and warfighting capacity.

The final, and most important, question that emerges when these PLA Exercises are conducted is this: when might the Chinese decide that the political and military conditions are just right for them to turn one of these joint exercises around Taiwan into the real thing?

Only Xi knows.

Until then, we must watch and learn from these exercises, and use what we have learned to deter Xi from making that fateful decision.
