Dear friends,

In [Letter #95](https://petergray.substack.com/p/95-is-dyslexia-a-school-produced), with the admittedly provocative title “Is Dyslexia a School-Produced Disorder?,”I suggested that dyslexia may often result from pressure in school. I suggested that some students may feel traumatized by school pressure to read, especially to read aloud in front of others, which can lead to a persistent fear and dislike of reading, which can interfere with subsequent learning to read and eventuate in a diagnosis of dyslexia. Continued pressure to read may, for them, be counterproductive. I presented four lines of evidence supporting this theory:

(1) In an online survey of families where a child who had been diagnosed with dyslexia was subsequently removed from school for homeschooling, all ten respondents said, essentially, that their child was traumatized by reading at school, hated the thought of reading, and only began learning to read after an extended period in which pressure to read was completely removed.

(2) Staff members at the Sudbury Valley School, where there is no pressure to read, report that they have never seen a case of genuine inability to learn to read, including among students diagnosed as dyslexic prior to becoming students there.

(3) A large experimental study of the effect of teaching reading and other academic skills to four-year-olds found that such training greatly increased the percentage of students who, by 6th grade, were diagnosed with a learning disorder (including dyslexia). This is consistent with the idea that earlier pressure to read, for children not ready for it and made anxious by it, may be a cause of the ever-increasing rate of diagnosed dyslexia.

(4) Researchers have regularly found a significant correlation between anxiety, especially social anxiety, and dyslexia among schoolchildren, consistent with the idea that children prone to anxiety about performing in front of others may be predisposed to reading problems because of feeling traumatized when reading in front of others in the classroom.

The idea that school-induced anxiety about reading contributes to the prevalence of diagnosed dyslexia is an idea one does not encounter in the voluminous literature on dyslexia. That literature too often leads to the false impression that incidences of dyslexia are solely explained by inherited brain differences. So, I wanted to bring evidence of another cause to readers’ attention. Again, the title of Letter #95 was a question: “Is dyslexia a school-produced disorder?” My answer, as you might have inferred, is neither a clear yes nor a clear no, but is “sometimes” or “partly” or maybe even “often.” Because researchers have ignored this possibility, we don’t know how often.

In comments to Letter #95, some readers noted that I did not acknowledge the experiences of people diagnosed with dyslexia who have long struggled with reading but never felt traumatized by reading experiences in school. For that I apologize. I should have made it clear that I was not writing about everyone’s experience, but only about the experience of what may be a rather large (but unknown) percentage of people diagnosed with dyslexia. One or two other commenters suggested that I must be unaware of the great amounts of research on heritability of dyslexia and fMRI brain correlates of it. In fact, I was quite familiar with such research, having reviewed it several times over the past decade or so. Still, I took the criticism to heart by reading new articles suggested to me by one of those who commented. I don’t see anything in that research countering my anxiety theory. In fact, I see evidence quite consistent with it.

In what follows, I elaborate a bit on what research to date has revealed concerning the nature and causes of what gets diagnosed, in our current culture, as dyslexia.

### How Dyslexia is Diagnosed

The American Psychiatric Association’s Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-V) sets out four criteria that must be met to diagnose dyslexia (Erbeli et al., 2022), all of which are rather vague and open to subjective interpretation. These are:

> 1\. Persistent difficulty (lasting for at least six months) in reading, such as inaccurate or slow and effortful reading.
> 
> 2\. Reading skill is well below average for the person’s age and interferes with academic achievement or daily life.
> 
> 3\. The difficulty began during the school years, even if it did not become a significant problem until adulthood.
> 
> 4\. The difficulty in reading is not better explained by some other disorder.

As Nicholas Benson and his colleagues (2020) have pointed out, based on a national survey of how specific learning disabilities are diagnosed in practice, U.S. children who receive a diagnosis of dyslexia are those of normal or above normal intelligence who, for whatever reason, have not learned to read despite attendance at reading courses in school. Nobody is diagnosed with dyslexia by looking at their brains or their genes.

Although many attempts have been made to find some unitary cognitive difference underlying dyslexia, these have largely failed. For example, some point to differences in ability to identify phoneme difference; some point to specific differences in visual perception; some point to eye movement difficulties; some point to slow cognitive processing speed; etc. All of these find support in some samples of people diagnosed with dyslexia, but none apply to all or, apparently, even to most people diagnosed with dyslexia. In large enough samples, researchers can find statistically significant correlations of all of these and more with diagnosis of dyslexia, but that does not mean that all individuals, or even most individuals, diagnosed with the disorder manifest any one of them. Moreover, in most such studies no attempt is made to determine whether the correlated cognitive or perceptual difference from typical is a cause or result of poor reading ability.

In a major review of genetic literature on dyslexia, Florina Erbeli and her colleagues (2022) concluded that the genetic underpinning for reading ability is normally distributed, in the bell-shaped curve characteristic of most human abilities, and that, in their words, “dyslexia is best viewed as the extreme end of a continuum of reading traits in the general population.” They report further that attempts to identify qualitative differences between those diagnosed with dyslexia and other cases of slow or poor reading have consistently failed. They also point out that any estimate of the prevalence of “actual” dyslexia in the population is arbitrary; it depends on where we choose to place the cut-off point and say that a person’s reading problem is so poor that we will label the person as dyslexic.

Some have suggested that the cut-off could be standardized for schoolchildren by adding to the DSM criteria that the child’s score on standard tests of reading must be more than one standard deviation below the mean for a person of that age. By that definition, based on the evidence that reading ability for schoolchildren is normally distributed, 14% of schoolchildren would have dyslexia. For any normally distributed characteristic, 14% fall more than one standard deviation below the mean. But again, this is a completely arbitrary cutoff, as there is nothing magic about scoring 1.0 standard deviation below the mean as opposed to, say, 0.99 standard deviation below.

### What Do We Know About the Genetic Underpinnings of Dyslexia?

It is common to read that dyslexia is a genetically based disorder. What does that mean? It means that it is, to some degree, heritable. In this sense it is no different from any other variable behavioral human trait. All personality and ability differences among us are to a considerable degree heritable. That doesn’t mean that environment and experience play no role. Again, I’ll use the Erbeli et al. review as a source concerning heritability, as this source was specifically recommended to me by a person critical of Letter #95.

Erbeli and her colleague point out that the concordance rate for monozygotic (identical) twins for dyslexia, according to various studies, is somewhere between 40% and 70%. In other words, in 40 to 70 percent of cases in which one twin “has” dyslexia the genetically identical other also has it. Conversely, of course, this means that in 30 to 60 percent of cases in which one twin “has” dyslexia the genetically identical other does not. This, right off, proves that dyslexia is not solely genetic in origin. The lack of stronger concordance is especially striking, given that identical twins not only share genes but also share many of the same experiences. Clearly there is lots of room here to look for environmental influences.

My observations ([here](https://petergray.substack.com/p/92-the-reading-wars-why-natural-learning)) of learning to read outside of school suggest that some children simply become engaged with reading, interested in it, sooner than others, for reasons known to nobody. If we were to carry this out into my anxiety theory, it seems quite plausible that Twin A became engaged with reading before starting school and, therefore, had more skill at it by the time reading was taught in school than was the case for Twin B. Twin A loved reading lessons in school, but Twin B, not having developed the skill, was nervous about it, became ashamed of his poor performance, and therefore developed the mental block that eventuated in a diagnosis of dyslexia. It is also possible that Twins A and B had different teachers, with quite different ways of introducing reading to the children.

Erbeli and colleagues also point out that attempts to identify specific genes or constellations of genes specifically involved with reading ability, or correlated with dyslexia, have consistently failed. They conclude that, to the degree that dyslexia is heritable, it is polygenetic, that is, predisposed by any of many possible combinations of multiple genes. The genetic foundation for one person’s dyslexia may be entirely different from another person’s. Extending this, I suggest that for one person the genes may have to do with some aspect of auditory perception, for another with some aspect of visual perception, for another with eye movements, and for another with social anxiety (which clearly has a genetic foundation), and so on. These researchers even point out that heritability studies have revealed a significant heritable correlation between “internalizing disorders” (that is, anxiety and depression) and dyslexia. Ah, yet one more bit of evidence that a susceptibility for anxiety may underlie many cases of dyslexia!

My anxiety theory about dyslexia is consistent with any possible genetic underpinning. As I suggested in Letter #95, one child may feel anxious and shamed by reading lessons in school because of genetically predisposed social anxiety, and another may feel so because of a genetically predisposed perceptual, or cognitive, or motor control difference that makes reading difficult for them, so they stumble and feel ashamed. In either case, reading will come easier when the pressure is off.

(Parenthetically, I add, there is one clear genetic marker for increased likelihood of dyslexia diagnosis—the presence of a Y chromosome or absence of a second X. By various estimates, boys are anywhere from 1.5 to 3.3 times more likely than girls to get a diagnosis of dyslexia—Arnett et al., 2017).

### What Do We Know About the Neural Basis of Dyslexia?

Another common statement about dyslexia is that it is a brain-based disorder. Well, of course it is. All behavior, all differences among us in behavior, reside in one way or another in the brain. However, the numerous studies using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) to identify any specific brain regions involved have failed to find such a region. In Letter #95 I referred to a review by Peters and Ansari (2019), which concluded that “all the estimates \[from such brain studies\] are weak and noisy” and another by Thomas (2020) reaching a similar conclusion. To see if recent research might have changed that story, I did another search and read the most recent thorough review of such research I could find, by Martins et al (2025), who come to the same conclusion. Martins and colleagues, like the previous reviewers, concluded that brain differences in dyslexia vary across studies and differences that are found are diffuse, not localized, apparently involving connections among a variety of structures primarily in the left hemisphere.

So, consistent with the heritability studies, the brain studies indicate that a variety of biological differences may underlie different cases of dyslexia. There seems to be no unifying account. Moreover, as I noted in Letter #95, any correlations between fMRI findings and reading ability could stem from an effect of reading on the brain as well as the reverse. The wiring of the brain changes with experience.

### Why Reading is Not Such a Big Problem for Kids in Settings for Self-Directed Education

For kids attending a conventional, graded school, it is very important to learn to read on schedule. By third grade, if not before, much if not most of the learning and testing in school comes by way of the written word. So, if you can’t read, you fall behind in most of your classes. You are in serious trouble. The graded curriculum of schooling is founded on an assumption that all children should be able to learn certain skills, especially reading, at a timetable set by the curriculum (which over decades has moved to ever younger ages), so if someone fails to meet such a timetable, that person must have a “disorder.” The psychiatric establishment complies with this assumption by providing an “official” way of diagnosing the disorder and, *ipso facto*, the school system assumption is made true!

But for kids in schools for Self-Directed Education and for those in unschooling families, learning can occur in all sorts of ways, only some of which depend on reading. Today this is even more true than it was in years past, thanks to the abundance of videos, audiobooks, and programs that transcribe text into sounds. Some non-reading kids in such settings are very much into literature and have “read” many books by listening to the audio versions. Others are outdoor kids, or in other ways physically active, acquiring rich understandings of the world through direct experience. Others are heavily into music or art and developing aspects of the brain involved with these. And many are involved with all of these. At some point, however, they learn to read, in some cases with little difficulty and in other cases with considerable difficulty.

Indeed, by DSM-V criteria, a diagnosis of dyslexia would not be appropriate for kids in Self-Directed Education, regardless of their reading ability or disability. As noted above, one of the criteria is that the poor reading must “interfere with academic or daily life.” But reports from unschooling families and staff at schools for Self-Directed Education indicate that rich academic experiences and satisfying daily life can occur in today’s world without reading. Lack of reading need not hold anyone back, unless they are attending a school that makes reading the only route to what the school counts as learning.

After I published Letter #95, Tomis Parker, a long-time leader in Self-Directed Education, sent me [this](https://www.self-directed.org/tp/dyslexic-kids-wrote-this-article-in-a-school-that-forces-no-one-to-read-or-write/) essay, published by the Alliance for Self-Directed Education, which includes quotations from several children at a center for Self-Directed Education in North Carolina. Although these children embrace the diagnosis of dyslexia, apparently because it helps them connect with others so diagnosed and helps them feel less alone in their condition, they describe how their difficulty with reading does not hold them back. I like the concept of what one calls “ear reading.”

### Concluding Thoughts

I confess that one reason for my long-standing interest in differences among us in learning to read derives from my own experiences. All three of my brothers and my son could read well before they started school, with no deliberate training, which always seemed a bit magical to me. I was a late reader, for whom reading did not come easily. I was always in the slowest reading group at least through third grade. I still recall my embarrassment at being the only kid in my first-grade class who could not recognize his own name when the teacher printed it on the blackboard. “Dyslexia” had not been invented at that time (and I’m glad it hadn’t), and I don’t know if my reading was poor enough to warrant a diagnosis even if it had been invented. But I may well have been in the bottom 14% in reading ability at least through third grade. I hated reading in school, especially when called upon to do so out loud.

I’m still a slow reader, though I have learned, from necessity, how to skim books and articles rapidly to extract the crucial evidence and meanings. And I have always been a terrible speller, a trait that, no surprise, commonly accompanies poor reading. Every day I thank the gods of spellcheck for allowing me to avoid the tedium of trying to find words I can’t spell in a printed dictionary, which characterized the first decades of my career as a student, professor, researcher, and writer.
