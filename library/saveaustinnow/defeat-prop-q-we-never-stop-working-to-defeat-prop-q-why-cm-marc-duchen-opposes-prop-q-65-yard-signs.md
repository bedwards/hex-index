# **If You Love Austin, Vote Against Prop Q**

# *City Hall has better options*

On October 20, Austinites will head to the polls to vote on Proposition Q, the biggest property tax increase in city history.

I urge you to vote against it. Prop Q threatens to permanently raise our tax rate by 20% in a time of financial uncertainty and push scores of our community’s most vulnerable residents out of their homes.

**Here’s the good news: If voters reject Prop Q, the Austin City Council will have an opportunity to reconsider this year’s record-breaking budget and look for ways to reduce spending, an alternative to raising taxes.**

**[Voting Locations](https://causeandconnect.us17.list-manage.com/track/click?u=2e9c7874db8cfa375b50c6ee6&id=289fa7b6ed&e=443e5195af)**

# **Prop Q Worsens Homelessness**

Prop Q could permanently raise property owners’ annual costs by $1,000 when combined with Travis County’s new tax rate, utility hikes, and other new fees. On top of that, City Hall is planning to present voters with a $700 million bond in 2026, which would raise taxes even further.

**Renters aren’t protected from these costs, because landlords will pass them through to their tenants. For every $100 in rent increases, groups like ECHO and Caritas report that the local homeless population will grow by nearly 10%.**

Roughly half of the funds raised by Prop Q would go to third-party contractors that provide homelessness services. That means Prop Q would create a “vicious cycle” of increased homelessness and tax hikes to address the growing problem.

# **Core Services Are Already Safe**

Prop Q pays for things that are “nice to have,” not core city services.

**The city’s base budget already covers the public safety, park maintenance, and other services that Austin needs to maintain our quality of life.**

# **There’s a Better Way**

**If local voters reject Prop Q, my colleagues and I will have an opportunity to reconsider this year’s record-breaking $6.3 billion budget and look for ways to save money.**

To do that, I’ve proposed an efficiency study like the one performed by the City of Houston, which identified more than $120 million in savings. Prop Q would permanently increase Austin’s annual budget by more than $100 million, but our fiscal shortfall this year was $33 million, far lower. I’m confident we can follow Houston’s lead and find the savings to cover that shortfall, avoiding the need for a tax increase.

**I’m voting against Prop Q because it’s the smart, responsible, and compassionate thing to do. I also want to send my colleagues in City Hall a message: It’s time for them to stop overspending and to start building back trust with the community they’ve sworn to serve.**
