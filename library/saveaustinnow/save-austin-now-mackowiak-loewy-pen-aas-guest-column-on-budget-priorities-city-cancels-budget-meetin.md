-   **Mackowiak & Loewy Pen AAS Guest Column on Budget Priorities**
    

Save Austin Now co-chair Mackowiak (Republican) and local business and community leader **Adam Loewy** (Democrat) penned a [guest column](https://www.statesman.com/opinion/columns/your-voice/article/opinion-prop-q-s-defeat-gives-austin-chance-21164678.php) for the Austin American-Statesman on what we think the budget priorities should be after the overwhelming defeat of Prop Q.

# **Prop Q’s defeat gives Austin a chance to refocus on basics**

## Instead of significant new spending, voters want better core services. City Hall should start with what’s broken.

*By **Matt Mackowiak** and **Adam Loewy***

Austin American-Statesman

November 13, 2025

*Austin voters sent a clear message in overwhelmingly rejecting Proposition Q, an ill-considered and unjustified tax hike plan that 109,000 Austinites voted against to deliver a stunning 63%-37% defeat to Mayor **Kirk Watson** and nine of 10 City Council members.*

*Only Council Member **Marc Duchen** had the foresight and the courage to oppose Prop Q. He saw the damage it would do, and diagnosed the deep and undeniable taxpayer distrust.*

*The message taxpayers sent was this: We don’t trust City Hall right now.*

*We recognize that many cities face predictable yet tough budget climates, as temporary federal COVID funding has run out.*

*Faced with a $33 million deficit, the council proposed Prop Q, a 20.2% city property tax hike that would have generated $110 million a year, with $35.5 million [going to address homelessness](https://www.austintexas.gov/page/ballot-propositions). Taxpayers roundly rejected it.*

*Prop Q’s defeat was about credibility. When people no longer trust that City Hall is spending wisely, they won’t hand over more control or more money.*

*Residents see a city budget that now exceeds $6 billion — larger than that of some states. The general fund, the discretionary part of the budget, has risen from about $906 million in 2015 to $1.4 billion this year. That’s a 55% increase over the past decade, during which time the city’s population grew just 10%.*

*Do residents see results that justify that budget? No, they don’t. They deal with slow emergency response times, short-staffed police and EMS units, a misguided proposal to lower staffing shifts for fire, deteriorating roads and soaring housing costs. For a city that prides itself on innovation, Austin often struggles with basic delivery of core services.*

*If City Hall wants to rebuild trust after Prop Q, it should start by [refocusing its budget priorities](https://www.statesman.com/news/article/austin-s-new-budget-plan-trims-social-service-21153741.php).*

***First, restore public safety capacity.** The Austin Police Department is operating well below authorized strength, and morale remains unacceptably low. The same challenges exist in EMS and the fire department. Safe neighborhoods are the foundation for everything else — affordability, community vitality and economic growth. Urgently re-establishing full staffing levels should be priority one. Public safety is the first responsibility of any city.*

***Second, make infrastructure investment a core value, not a campaign slogan.** Voters have repeatedly approved ballot measures for transportation, water and drainage improvements, but they expect projects to be completed on time and on budget. Every major project, including the Project Connect transit expansion, is behind schedule. Austin’s explosive growth demands serious attention to basics: reliable utilities, well-maintained roads and functioning drainage systems.*

***Third, practice fiscal discipline.** Property appraisals continue to rise, pushing effective tax bills higher even when the nominal rate stays flat. City leaders should make it a goal to lower the effective tax rate next year — not just slow its growth. That means setting priorities, pausing non-essential hires, and reviewing every department for savings. An outside audit is urgently necessary, [as Houston just showed](https://www.statesman.com/news/article/austin-houston-taxes-budget-differences-prop-q-21114848.php) by conducting one and avoiding a tax rate election.*

***Fourth, increase transparency.** Taxpayers deserve to see where their dollars go. A public-facing “Austin Budget Dashboard,” similar to those used by other major cities, would allow residents to track spending and performance. Real accountability builds real trust.*

*Prop Q failed because voters are tired of being asked for more without seeing better results. It wasn’t a rejection of reform; it was a demand for focus and responsibility.*

*Austinites love this city. They want it to work well, spend tax dollars wisely and plan ahead. If the mayor and City Council treat this election result as a wake-up call rather than a setback, they can start to rebuild the confidence that’s been lost. We are all watching.*

*Matt Mackowiak, a Republican, is the co-chair of Save Austin Now. Adam Loewy, a Democrat, is a longtime Austin business and philanthropic leader and founder of Loewy Law Firm.*

-   **City Cancels Planned Budget Meeting After Clear Violation of Texas Open Meetings Act**
    

KXAN’s intrepid reporter **Grace Reader** has the [details](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austin-budget-meeting-canceled-after-attorneys-say-city-violated-open-meetings-act/) of a truly embarrassing episode:

# **Austin budget meeting canceled after attorneys say city violated Open Meetings Act**

by: [Grace Reader](https://www.kxan.com/author/grace-reader/)

Updated: Nov 12, 2025 / 09:17 PM CST

*AUSTIN (KXAN) — Austin city council’s first budget meeting since **[Proposition Q failed](https://www.kxan.com/news/your-local-election-hq/proposition-q-austin-residents-vote-on-tax-rate-increase-to-fund-core-services/)** — and since the **[city manager released his proposed budget amendments](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austin-city-manager-releases-revised-budget-after-voters-reject-prop-q/)** — was canceled Wednesday afternoon after attorneys **Bill Aleshire** and **Rick Fine** said that city council may be in violation of the Texas Open Meetings Act (TOMA).*

*The meeting had been previously scheduled for Thursday.*

*“In a letter delivered today, Aleshire and Fine highlighted that the posted notice for the November 13, 2025 City Council meeting fails to comply with the legal requirement of TOMA to include a link to the proposed budget and an updated taxpayer impact statement,” the local attorneys wrote.*

***Jenny LaCoste-Caputo**, deputy director of communications at the city of Austin responded.*

*“The City of Austin believes that the budget work session meeting noticed for November 13, 2025, was posted correctly and appropriately,” LaCoste-Caputo said. “The City Attorney’s Office consulted with outside legal experts prior to posting the agenda and believes it meets all legal requirements. However, our goal with the budget is to restore trust in the City, so we are willing to take additional steps to achieve that goal.”*

*“We will cancel the meeting for tomorrow, November 13, and will re-post an agenda for the Tuesday work session and subsequent meetings related to the budget,” she said.*

*At the meeting, Austin city council was supposed to discuss what could be cut from the **[budget it approved in August](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austin-expected-to-approve-6b-budget-with-tax-rate-election-looming/)** after voters rejected Proposition Q, the city’s tax rate election proposition.*

*With Austinites saying ‘no’ to higher property taxes, the city will need to rework that budget, which it will no longer be able to afford. Over the weekend, **[City Manager T.C. Broadnax released revisions to his July budget](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austin-city-manager-releases-revised-budget-after-voters-reject-prop-q/)** that the city council will work from.*

***What’s the law?***

*A bill that **[became state law in September requires](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/GV/htm/GV.551.htm)** a governmental body to include a copy of the proposed budget “unless the governmental body has made the proposed budget clearly accessible on the home page of the governmental body’s Internet website,” the law reads.*

*It also requires governmental bodies to have a taxpayer impact statement “showing, for the median-valued homestead property, a comparison of the property tax bill in dollars pertaining to the property for the current fiscal year to an estimate of the property tax bill in dollars for the same property for the upcoming fiscal year.”*

*There is no budget document nor a taxpayer impact statement **[attached to the Thursday work session agenda](https://www.austintexas.gov/department/city-council/2025/20251113-spec.htm)** as of Wednesday afternoon.*

*However, the city **[does have the city manager’s proposed base budget and taxpayer impact statement from July on its budget website](https://www.austintexas.gov/page/city-budget)**. It also shows the amended budget recommendations, which detail how the city manager would revise the July budget.*

*Aleshire told KXAN he doesn’t think that meets state law.*

*“This looks really bad for Austin to the legislature … they should be concerned that once again it looks like Austin is defying state law,” Aleshire told KXAN.*

***Let’s talk about how we got here***

*Broadnax released his office’s **[proposed base budget for FY 2026 in July](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austin-fy-2026-budget-releases/)**. The initial budget used a 3.5% tax rate, which would not have required asking voters to approve a rate increase under state law.*

*But some city council members said they **[thought the initial budget cut too much](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austin-city-manager-to-present-2025-2026-budget-proposal-to-council/)** from city services. Mayor Pro Tem Vanessa Fuentes called it “a Band-Aid budget, a budget that has Band-Aid solutions.”*

*The council’s concerns led them to **[consider proposals for a tax rate election](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austin-city-council-signals-a-tax-rate-election-is-coming-but-for-how-much/)**. Those proposals allowed the city council to add services to the July base budget, including: Committing to the **[homelessness office’s full funding proposal](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/city-of-austin-to-give-update-on-new-strategies-funding-to-address-homelessness/)**, adding money to EMS’ budget and **[restoring the Austin Fire Department’s overtime budget](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austin-firefighters-push-back-against-proposed-budgets-staffing-requirement-changes/)**.*

*In August, the council voted to approve a more than $6 billion budget with those additional services added to it, which also set a tax rate high enough it triggered a tax rate election.*

*In November, **[voters turned down that proposition](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austins-city-council-reacts-to-prop-q-defeat-look-ahead-to-reworking-budget/)** therefore nullifying that budget adopted in August.*

*The city of Austin told KXAN previously that with Prop Q being defeated: “The property tax rate will be reduced to the voter-approval tax rate of 52.4017 cents per $100 value. At that tax rate, the City estimates it will collect $109.5 million less revenue than the amount budgeted for Fiscal Year 2025-2026 General Fund revenue, and the typical Austin property owner’s 2026 tax bill would see an annual increase of $104.76 from the previous tax year, based on a median homestead assessed value of $494,803.”*

***What’s in the city manager’s new budget proposal?***

*While the revised budget proposal released over the weekend largely mirrors the one Broadnax produced in July, there were some slight changes, including: Adding funding for continued operation of the city’s Marshalling Yard homeless shelter, more funding and staff for parks maintenance and cuts in several other areas including from youth programs. That’s according to **[city staff](https://services.austintexas.gov/edims/document.cfm?id=462375)**.*

*City of Austin financial staff put together a list of differences between this new budget proposal and the city manager’s July budget proposal. They also highlighted **[differences between the city’s previous fiscal year budget and the new proposal](https://services.austintexas.gov/edims/document.cfm?id=462375)**.*

*“Voters told us that city government can’t be all things to all people. We can’t pick up all the expenses, grants, and needs that other levels of government don’t cover, even if they’re for things we think are important,” Austin Mayor Kirk Watson wrote. “Voters want us to avoid adding to Austin’s unaffordability. Well, as I’ve said this week–message received.”*

***What will be cut, and how much?***

*Now that voters have said “no” to paying more in property taxes in exchange for those additional city services, the Austin city council will need to wrestle with how they’ll plug **[an immediate $33 million budget shortfall](https://www.kxan.com/news/local/austin/austin-city-manager-to-present-2025-2026-budget-proposal-to-council/)**.*

*Had Proposition Q passed, it would have brought roughly $110 million additional to the city annually. Without Austinites agreeing to that funding, the council will now need to re-budget without that $110 million additional that would have been added.*

*Among the cuts Broadnax proposed is $38 million from social services contracts, nearly $6.3 million from the Austin Emergency Medical Services, $5.2 million from Parks and Recreation, $3.7 million from the Municipal Court, $1.3 million from Austin Public Health, and $1 million from the Austin Fire Department. The Homeless Strategy Office will see an increase of $3.7 million, according to the amended budget.*

*City council members are expected to have several work sessions and town halls before ultimately being asked to start debating the budget and vote as early as November 20. The fiscal year began October 1.*

-   **SAVE AUSTIN NOW TO SUPPORT AUSTIN FIRE ASSOCIATION-LED CHARTER AMENDMENT TO GUARANTEE STAFFING LEVEL FOR FIRE DEPARTMENT**
    

The Austin Fire Association (AFA), the labor union for our firefighters, has launched a charter amendment petition effort to guarantee their needed staffing level of 4 firefighters per truck and aims to put in on the May 2026 ballot.

Save Austin Now will actively support this effort.

City leaders proposed reducing the fire staffing level to 3 firefighters per truck earlier this year, and while that unwise and unsafe proposal failed to gain traction, the fire union isn’t taking any chances and is seeking to make permanent the staffing standard they need to save lives and protect their own responding personnel so no future council or Fire Chief resuscitates this indefensible proposal.

You read that right: Our Austin Fire Chief actually supported that staffing reduction.

For Austin Fire Chief **Joel Baker’s** outrageous decision not to deploy AFD personnel to Kerrville before and immediately after the historic flooding, the Fire Union took the almost unprecedented step of approving a ‘no confidence’ vote on July 11. In that vote 93% of AFA members voted ‘no confidence’ in Chief Baker. You can read about that vote [here](https://thedailytexan.com/2025/07/21/austin-firefighters-association-vote-no-confidence-in-fire-chief-joel-baker/).

Their vitally important charter amendment can be found here: [AustinSafetyFirst.com](http://AustinSafetyFirst.com).

As AFA says:

*For years, Austin has followed a national gold standard known as **“Four Firefighter Minimum Staffing.”** It’s backed by science, endorsed by safety experts, and written into Austin law. Why? Because four firefighters on every fire engine means:*

-   *Faster rescues*
    
-   *Safer operations*
    
-   *Better outcomes for you and your family*
    

*Whether it’s a fire, crash, heart attack, or overdose — **FOUR saves lives.***

***We must preserve and secure this Life-Saving Standard***

*This summer the City Manager attempted to repeal the **minimum staffing law** and **cut fire crews down to just three firefighters per engine**. And the Fire Chief TWICE suggested the **closure of critical community fire stations** or the **shuttering of essential rescue and fire truck operations.*** *SAFER AUSTIN will prevent these dangerous cuts to your family’s safety.*
