The Secret Faith: Further Notes on the Religion of Art

An anonymous inquirer wrote into my super-secret Tumblr with the following inquiry based on my assertion last week that “[t[he main and developing lesson of The Invisible College…is that…‘the religion of art’ is actually a real religion”:

John I have to admit your literature-as-the-true-religion thesis appeals to me, but don’t you think it’s a little self-serving—the true religion happens to be the area *I* toil in?

And in anticipation of you saying ‘yes, intentionally so, we need new myths and I need self promotion and we’re mass communicatin here’—fine, but what if you were trying to convince, or at least explain to, a less sympathetic audience?

I replied: I take your point, but I didn’t and don’t mean anybody has to read literature for this religion’s sake, that it’s a literary religion, or that literature itself is a religion or should be worshiped. I mean, following Shelley’s “Defence” and whatever Pound was going on about and my crude impressions of Graves and Frye and Hughes and Bloom and Paglia and Pagels and Doody, that a select body of imaginative literature (minimally the Greek tragedians plus Plato, then Dante, then Shakespeare, then the Romantics and the modernists) is the extant textual record of an actual and by no means exclusively textual religious practice and faith, or overlapping set of practices and faiths, later marginalized by monotheist orthodoxy’s development in the west. We could roughly identify it (at the risk of trying to name the true Tao) first with the Greek mystery cults and then with various gnosticisms, in particular their wider “magical” metaphysics and more diversely populated cosmic order, all this reflected in the panoply of literary archetypes, in literature’s identification of human imagination with God, and in the anti-moralistic and anti-reductionist literary practice of irony. (This is a dim and intuitive apprehension on my part, not anything I’d want to write up more systematically. Also there’s a lot we don’t know—obviously a lot I don’t know but also a lot we don’t know about ancient religion.) So my answer to your cogent objection, again, is that imaginative literature of all textual traditions, and much more than the mainstream religious or philosophical ones, encodes this older religion, which, with the onset of postmodernism (i.e., social changes bearing upon kinship and intercultural relations, new media’s destruction of linear textuality, the popular dissemination of haute nouveau skepticisms from Nietzsche to Freud to quantum physics, even at its most literal the discovery of the gnostic gospels and various occult and eastern revivals) started to become a genuine religion again (in the sense of a social practice) sans literary mediation, even though literary mediation was all the access to it anybody really had in the intervening millennia. (I refer you back to that panicked letter McLuhan wrote to Voeglin about literary traditions as the remnants of mystery cults, “a person feels like an awful sucker to have spent 20 years of study on an art which turns out to be somebody else’s ritual,” which reminds me I still need to read Jane Harrison.) I’m not saying, “Read Ulysses and get religion!” I’m not proselytizing. I am saying to read Ulysses if you want to understand consciously your once and future religion, the religion you probably practice even without knowing it, a religion that has quietly crept even into the official churches by now. (You place a candle on the altar every time you say, “Who am I to judge?”) But it’s a religion whether you read Ulysses or not. The people practicing it most ardently as we speak are borderline illiterates making a vision board or watching anime—or are, you know, Grimes or Sigh Swoon or whomever, no disrespect, I like them both—while erudite academics writing up the latest sociological analysis of Ulysses are in fact theologians of quite another sect. Whether this is a true religion or even a good one is still another question. I could criticize it! I have my Catholic schoolboy side, too. When the aforementioned Voeglin, for example, reads it as partner to or origin of modern totalitarianism in its master-manifester drive to “immanentize the eschaton,” we should heed his warning. But, if monotheist orthodoxies won out over gnosticism and paganism initially for a reason, monotheist orthodoxies and their materialist sequelae haven’t been able to sustain themselves for a reason, too, so this at once archaic and futurist religion is probably what we have to work with.8

Meanwhile, another New Year, another blast of in/out lists about how this will finally be the year when we bury irony and the internet in a common grave. We will veritably and earnestly palpate the turf at long last. Irony, however, is a necessary structure of human consciousness—God save us, anyway, from the sincere!—and the internet the product of that consciousness’s inevitable quest to extend its dominion. I can go with the anti-internet crew this far, however: I think social media is in decline and will continue to decline due both to AI onslaught and to diminishing political returns for all sides. The future of online, at least for cultural elites, will be as a delivery system for offline goods and experiences. For example, I am here online to try to sell you my book and to try to get you to read some other books; as a result of this effort, I have met people in real life I would never otherwise have met, and have experienced other real-world benefits. The key is to avoid getting stuck entirely inside the machine rather than using it to augment non-machinic experience. On the other hand, the old infrastructure of print culture is simply not coming back, and for that reason we will not be able to abandon the online world entirely. I was startled to see a viral polemic this week about how authors can’t sell books on Substack. Not to get all Marty Supreme about it, but I just outsold much of the Big-Five lit-fic mid-list with a book that wouldn’t even exist if it weren’t for Substack. There’s no point in living as if it were 1996. But I also had the indispensable help of an excellent publisher with access to promotional and retail resources I could not have commanded on my own. That’s precisely my point: we want online and off- to be working in concert to advance common ends. This idea, I suspect, not total Luddism, will be increasingly “in” in the coming year.

In the vid, Ian amusingly pushes back against subscribers who aren’t interested in contemporary fiction but only in classics, and who consequently complain that Major Arcana is not like Cormac McCarthy and not as good as Tolstoy. It’s probably more like Cormac McCarthy than it looks on the surface—McCarthy’s gnostic themes are there, as is the ethical investigation of heroism, and a fascination with world-shattering violence—but I grant that the setting and its gender inflection are metropolitan-androgynous rather than pastoral-masculinist, and that the style is accordingly on the recursive, interior, and subjectivist rather than the Biblical-epic side of modernism. Major Arcana also lacks a certain Tolstoyan limpidity, to its detriment, I’m sure. And yet any living culture offers present works in eye-to-eye conversation with past ones. Antiquarianism and presentism are equally dead ends, equivalent forms of kitsch. The watchword is juvenescence. (By the way, DeLillo and Dostoevsky, rather than McCarthy and Tolstoy, might be the more apt stylistic reference points for my work, not that I make a claim for equal merit.)

I borrow the term “The Secret Faith” from anon’s follow-up on the importance of the non-literary arts to the question. Speaking of the non-literary arts, and despite my allusion in footnote 1 above, I didn’t love Marty Supreme. Now that we—I’m not perfectly sure what I mean by “we”—are winning the war for aesthetic vitality, we don’t need to offer tactical praise for every flawed aspirant to liveliness. I certainly experienced an identification with the main character, allowing for differences in our vocation and historical circumstance. (Not that many historical differences, though. I assume every ambitious Emersonized son has had those “get a real job, live a normal life”-type arguments with every skeptical immigrant mother in every period of American history.) Marty’s liveliness is too often forced, purchased at the expense of emotional and thematic coherence. A good two-hour movie lives somewhere inside the two-and-a-half-hour one. Here’s what I would have said in the script meeting. First, excise the dog subplot, which has no organic relation to the main conflict and exists only for the sake of cheap thrills. Replace it with a tighter focus on Marty’s relationship with the actress and this relationship’s conflict with his girl-next-door love interest; a better-developed love triangle would have the added benefit of giving Odessa more to do than just follow him around bathetically. Also, we need a speech from Marty somewhere in the first act about what ping-pong actually means to him beyond an economic opportunity; without this defense of ping-pong for ping-pong’s sake, the first climax, where he finally plays the game in perfect artistic disinterest, lacks sufficient weight. I did appreciate the film’s attempt to severe the drive toward individual achievement—echoed extra-textually in Timothée’s own self-branded quest for greatness—from a cold and cruel disavow of love, and its doubling of this personal theme with a political metatext on the 20th-century Jewish experience in America, as if to say that (American) self-reliance and (Jewish) other-regard may finally be reconciled. (The flashback to the Auschwitz prisoner bringing the honey back to the camp on his physical person is the whole film in nuce.) But better a failed quest for greatness than no quest for greatness at all.
