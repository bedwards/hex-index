The Rock or the Cloud: Major American Novelists and the Affirmations of Form

A reader with the Tumblr username McCarthyian-Faulknerian, no doubt inspired by my thoughts about Pynchon from last week, and perhaps also my qualified defense of “ranking” art on recent Invisible College episodes, asks:

DeLillo, McCarthy, or Roth, and why?4

These three are so different from one another.5 I think of Orwell on the question of Dickens vs. Tolstoy:

But one is no more obliged to choose between them than between a sausage and a rose. Their purposes barely intersect.

But, with apologies to my inquirer’s username, and if I take the game seriously, it comes down to DeLillo vs. Roth. McCarthy’s corpus has a certain artificiality. First he’s Southern Gothic; then he’s writing a Knoxville Ulysses; now he’s the evil laureate of the Southwest; look, he’s gone Hollywood; and so on. It’s all very well done, but is it all of a piece? With McCarthy vis-à-vis Roth and DeLillo, I think of Edmund Wilson on Wharton vis-à-vis James. Ironically, Wilson back in the olden days thought this was a problem that afflicted the female author uniquely, but it turns out even a cowboy may be vulnerable:

Henry James in a virtual vacuum continued to possess and develop his métier. But Mrs. Wharton had no métier in this sense.

Now this was true of Roth and DeLillo as well. Both of them spent the ’70s desperately searching for a métier. But they finally found one, whereas I don’t know that McCarthy ever did. It was strange in those last two novels, The Passenger and Stella Maris, which I enjoyed, to finally hear what sounds like his actual style, dry and sardonic and skeptical, rather than the archaic-apocalyptic.

Now, the temptation is to choose Roth over DeLillo, because Roth’s work has people in it, and DeLillo’s doesn’t exactly. One feels, even in the memory of reading Roth, warmth toward certain characters and situations. Harold Bloom’s comparison of Roth to Shakespeare doesn’t feel as exaggerated when I think back to Eli the Fanatic or Amy Bellette or the Swede, or Zuckerman collecting his mother’s effects after her death, or Sabbath on the beach, or Roth himself in Patrimony tending his dying father, which is Tolstoyan, or the impassioned intricacy of the ideological combatants’ positions in Operation Shylock, which is Dostoevskean, or Coleman Silk “killing” his mother when he decides to pass as white, or the boy’s memories in The Plot Against America, and I could go on, and this is off the top of my head, and I only read most of those books once. I haven’t even mentioned the latter-day Jamesian perfection of the style even when it all has to convey is rage or hatred; and, yes, there are impurities, misogynistic tantrums and extraneous sexual hijinks and an autofictional impulse that becomes cloying, but these are recompensed by all the rest, by the suffering of the figures and the passion of the prose; and yes, Roth should have gotten the Nobel. Bloom reviewing Roth, and this before the greatest works:

But the Rothian difference from Nathanael West and Thomas Pynchon also should be emphasized. Roth paradoxically is still engaged in moral prophecy; he continues to be outraged by the outrageous - in societies, others and himself. There is in him nothing of West’s gnostic preference for the posture of the satanic editor, Shrike, in “Miss Lonelyhearts,” or of Pynchon’s cabalistic doctrine of sado-anarchism. Roth’s negative exuberance is not in the service of a negative theology, but intimates instead a nostalgia for the morality once engendered by the Jewish normative tradition.

[...]

Roth seems prophetic in the biblical tradition. His absolute concern never ceases to be the pain of the relations between children and parents, and between husband and wife, and in him this pain invariably results from the incommensurability between a rigorously moral normative tradition whose expectations rarely can be satisfied, and the reality of the way we live now.

After this, what can I say on behalf of my paesan’, Don DeLillo? Laura Miller, reviewing Underworld long ago, admired his power of symbolic construction, his sheer power as novelistic conceptual artist, but warned us we could not come to him in need of human warmth:

When I think of the novels that strike me as great, the ones that seize me up and carry me along like a river, I think of characters — Jane Eyre, Hester Prynne, Huckleberry Finn, Raskolnikov. People who kidnapped their authors and set them on uncharted paths, people who, when they enter a scene, make me think, “Ah, it’s you.” “Underworld” bleeds as a result of never settling on such a character…

It’s a bit of an imitative-fallacy evasion to reply to this with, “Well, it’s about a world without human warmth,” close kin to the old stand-by, “It’s not bad, it’s a comment on badness.” Artists should be more than recorders; they should, while they’re transfiguring the real anyway, bestir themselves to leave it in better order than they found it. We shouldn’t confuse this mandate with superficial affirmation, mere “uplift,” as if tragedy were not transcendent, and we should confuse it still less with an obligation to make certain moral or political points. The form affirms, not the content.

DeLillo’s form does affirm, however, I persistently argue, rather than parching us for parchedness’s sake. We can make this claim by linking his work to the American romance tradition, seeing his novels as symbolic prose-poems like those of Hawthorne and Melville, whose own characters tend toward allegory rather than realist depth. (Hester Prynne is a partial exception, but only partial. Her psychological depth is the psychological depth of a type: the first modern woman, Protestant-American Madonna. But what do we actually know about her? Less than we know about Klara Sax.) We could point to surprising affinities with other classics, however, ones less bound to nation or culture. DeLillo’s arch dialogue is as stylized, I have always thought, as Austen’s and Wilde’s: the high-artifice wit of a social observer skeptical toward all society, using society as an occasion to play variations on its absurd non-dialogues. And his characterization, as I’ve argued, resembles no one’s more than Woolf’s: in both authors, character is not the bedrock of the Rothian self slamming through the flesh and into the world but is rather a hazy cloud of memory and desire hanging loosely amid an estranged body. I don’t defend this as the modern or postmodern condition, though. Which one of these we prefer probably comes down more than we’d like to admit to our own experience and sensibility rather than a free artistic choice or an unconditioned social observation.

I and my characters tend, I think, more toward the Rothian. But does this allow for DeLillo’s mysticism, his haunted and mordant sense of the numinous, with which I like to infuse my own work? The most “realistic” gesture, it occurs to me, is to thicken and thin your characters depending on their situation, since it seems to me that we are not ourselves equally “all there” at every moment. The perfect novel, if one could write it, would be the perfect synthesis of Roth and DeLillo.

Some further remarks on Cather here in the spirit of the stairway. First, still somewhat awed by my first reading of Death Comes for the Archbishop, I want to elaborate slightly on her Southwestern engagements, since I’ve been remarking on and off in these pages on what I’ve called contemporary culture’s “pull toward the desert,” which implicates even the ending of Major Arcana, but think also of Eddington and One Battle After Another this year. Cather first went to the Southwest in 1912, even before she completed O Pioneers! On that trip she discovered the cliff-dwelling civilization mourned in The Professor’s House. Her point of entry, however, was Winslow, Arizona, thus prompting these poetic lines that have furnished scholars with clear evidence for her lesbianism:

Well, I’m a-standing on a corner
In Winslow, Arizona
Such a fine sight to see
It’s a girl, my Lord
In a flat-bed Ford
Slowin’ down to take a look at me

But more importantly for Invisible College purposes was her sojourn at Mabel Dodge Luhan’s ranch in Taos, New Mexico, an artist’s colony and site of modernism discussed on 2024’s free Invisible College episode on D. H. Lawrence, “Down the Strange Lanes of Hell.” While I’ve compared Cather to Yeats (late-period Yeats) in both her formal restraint and her mythification of people and land, an extended comparison with Lawrence would be even more suggestive, as much for the differences as the similarities. The Taos trip helped to inspire Death Comes for the Archbishop, which everyone should drop everything—except Major Arcana—and read:

Beautiful surroundings, the society of learned men, the charm of noble women, the graces of art, could not make up to him for the loss of those light-hearted mornings of the desert, for that wind that made one a boy again. He had noticed that this peculiar quality in the air of new countries vanished after they were tamed by man and made to bear harvests. Parts of Texas and Kansas that he had first known as open range had since been made into rich farming districts, and the air had quite lost that lightness, that dry aromatic odour. The moisture of plowed land, the heaviness of labour and growth and grain-bearing, utterly destroyed it; one could breathe that only on the bright edges of the world, on the great grass plains or the sage-brush desert.

That air would disappear from the whole earth in time, perhaps; but long after his day. He did not know just when it had become so necessary to him, but he had come back to die in exile for the sake of it. Something soft and wild and free, something that whispered to the ear on the pillow, lightened the heart, softly, softly picked the lock, slid the bolts, and released the prisoned spirit of man into the wind, into the blue and gold, into the morning, into the morning!

And where are all the artist’s colonies today? Why is literally everything still in Brooklyn, no offense to Brooklyn, which I enjoyed visiting? Do they not exist or am I just not in the group chat? Inspired by the Pittsburgh literary history I recounted in the Cather episode, I invite a wealthy patron to consider furnishing the funds to create one here. (After all, when Dreiser read Balzac in the Carnegie Library before he wrote Sister Carrie, he believed Pittsburgh was the American Paris, not quite one of the “bright edges of the world,” but surely one of the neglected centers.) Call to action for my wealthy readers: acquire this $5M mansion in Shadyside—check the pool in the basement!—and I’ll do the rest. What else are you going to spend your money on? Your preferred progressive NGO has just been declared a terrorist organization.

Finally, and speaking of progressivism, I had to laugh when I discovered an epistolary essay by our Republican laureate called “Escapism” from the “red ’30s,” in which she lets the left-wing literati have it with both proverbial barrels. I can’t imagine how mad her Tumblr anons were when they read this:

At the moment, we hear the same cry which went up during the French Revolution: the one really important thing for every individual is his citizenship, his loyalty to a cause—which, of course, always means his loyalty to a party. The composer should be Citizen Beethoven, the painter Citizen Rembrandt, the poet Citizen Shelley, and they should step into line and speed their pen or brush in helping to solve the economic problems which confront society. There have been generous and bold spirits among the artists: Courbet tried to kick down the Vendome Column and got himself exiled, Citizen Shelley stepped into line and drove his pen, but he was not very useful to the reforms which fired his imagination. He was “useful” if you like that word, only as all true poets are, because they refresh and recharge the spirit of those who can read their language.

[…]

But doesn’t the new social restlessness spring from a desire to do away with the exceptional? And isn’t this desire partly the result of thwarted ambitions? Eighteen or twenty years ago there were graduated from our universities a company of unusually promising men, who were also extravagantly ambitious. The world was changing, and they meant to play a conspicuous part in this change: to make a new kind of thought and a new kind of expression; in language, color, form, and sound. They were to bring about a renaissance within a decade or so. Failing in this, they made a career of destroying the past. The only new thing they offered us was contempt for the old. Then began the flood of belittling biography which has poured over us ever since. We were told how shallow had been all the great philosophers, what educated dullards were Goethe, Rousseau, Spinoza, Pascal. Shakespeare and Dante were easily disposed of; the one because he was somebody else, the other because he was a cryptogram and did not at all mean to say what the greatest lines in the Italian language make him say. Able research work was done on the bodily diseases and physical imperfections of Beethoven, Schubert, Hugo Wolff and all the German composers. Not even their teeth were overlooked.

Is this a natural, unprejudiced way to study history? What does it lead to? Nothing very worthy. And what it comes from is less worthy still.

[…]

The revolt against individualism naturally calls artists severely to account, because the artist is of all men the most individual: those who were not have been long forgotten.

And the week after that, what the hell, The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas by Gertrude Stein. Then Nightwood, then Absalom, Absalom!, and so on as before. I have adjusted the schedule accordingly. (I’m sorry for the short notice, but, since it’s nominally a historical survey, I want to keep the book list chronological.) To whet your appetite for Stein’s almost literally infantine form of modernism—I allude not only to Alice’s calling her “baby,” but also to the etymology: Latin, in- + fans = “not speaking”—foe-of-the-pod Ryan Ruby eloquently reflects this week on Stein’s legacy in Bookforum:

SPEAKING OF JOYCE: he was the writer Stein regarded as her main living rival. She envied the seriousness with which he was taken following the publication and trial of Ulysses. Though the comparison was always meant unfavorably, she took petty pleasure when reviewers said his writing reminded them of hers. Toward the end of her life, she told an interviewer: “You see it is the people who generally smell of the museums who are accepted, and it is the new who are not accepted. That is why James Joyce was accepted and I was not. He leaned toward the past, in my work the newness and difference is fundamental.” This may be sour grapes, but it is not entirely untrue. Pound inadvertently confirmed half the point when he told William Gaddis, then the thirty-three-year-old author of The Recognitions, that “Joyce was an ending, not a beginning.”

Stein’s writing, however, has proven to be a beginning, and an immensely rich one at that. The Making of Americans presents a no less radical challenge to the realist novel’s concept of character than Finnegans Wake, written twenty-eight years later; in this respect, it also predates the formal innovations of Samuel Beckett’s The Unnamable and Alain Robbe-Grillet’s Jealousy by over four decades. The appearances of the Stein character in The Making of Americans, who comments in the first person on the writing of the novel, are an early instance of the now widespread technique of incorporating the process of creation into the created work. With its stripped-down diction and its repetitive, paratactic, run-on sentences, The Making of Americans synthesized minimalism and maximalism long before these two tendencies split to form the stylistic poles of American high literature. More than any other writer, including Joyce and Pound, Stein invented high modernism, and—if you take Fredric Jameson’s word for it—postmodernism, too.

I think this is less a beginning than a dead end, obviously—certainly less of a beginning than a museum is, which can always spark new thoughts. It’s more, in its postmodern emptiness, like an airport gate: another instance of the punitive privations of the avant-garde. As with the ressentiment-laden gestures of the political radical Cather observed in footnote 2 above, the privative avant-garde, privative in its very maximalism, here’s a whole lot of nothing!, would forbid splendor and greatness to anyone because it cannot be equally enjoyed by everyone. Ruby quotes Stein’s latest biographer, who admires her: “Stein is less a writer in the conventional sense than a philosopher of language.” Could someone who didn’t admire her have thought of a more damning judgment? God save us from philosophers of language! But the true and usable aspect of Stein’s challenge to realist characterization can be found in Woolf (in modernism) and in DeLillo (in postmodernism), for which see my comments in the main text.

Meanwhile, all the talk on Substack is of Gore Vidal. First is Ross Barkan with a stirring essay on Vidal as his role model as essayist, politician, public figure, and novelist, unclassifiable in his political antinomianism. Pursuant to the latter point, Vincenzo Barney replies to solve the riddle of how Vidalian politics might apply to the present. Everyone has the sense that Vidal’s response to Trump would be somehow unpredictable, since Trump represents at once the triumph of an American vulgarity and authoritarian temperament Vidal consistently disparaged and the revolt of populist elements against the imperium he loathed above all. Neither Ross nor Vincenzo comment in this context on the president’s Myra Breckinridge quality, though this is somewhere near the heart of the matter in my analysis. (“I am a very aesthetic person,” he says. To borrow an old meme: I was working on this story for years, and he just Tweeted it out! My initial response actually did some numbers on Tumblr, which I’d thought was a totally dead platform.) If Vidal could find sympathy in his heart for Timothy McVeigh, then Trump might not be a bridge too far, but it’s genuinely hard to tell; and Vincenzo’s identification of Trump as casus belli for the deep state, though perhaps one they’ve lost control of, has something of the Vidalian paranoia to it. (Whereas the people who think Christopher Hitchens would have liked Trump because he was a “contrarian” are just politically stupid and don’t realize that every contrarian’s contrarianism has an inner logic and necessity of its own. One wonders if such people have ever tried to have an independent thought at all.) Take, for example, these lines about immigration from Vidal’s classic essay “Monotheism and Its Discontents” (“Professor Pendulum” is Vidal’s contemptuous nickname for Arthur Schlesinger, Jr., the type of liberal centrist—the pendulum swings between right and left extremes—he had little time for):

Professor Pendulum is having a nervous breakdown because so many different tribes are being drawn to this sweet land of liberty and, thus far, there is no indication that any of the new arrivals intends ever to read The Age of Jackson. I think the taking in of everyone can probably be overdone. There may not be enough jobs for very many more immigrants, though what prosperity we have ever enjoyed in the past was usually based on slave or near-slave labor.

On the other hand, I think Asians, say, are a plus culturally, and their presence tends to refocus, somewhat, the relentless white versus black war. Where I am as one with friend Pendulum is that the newcomers must grasp certain principles as expressed in the Declaration of Independence and the Bill of Rights. Otherwise, we shall become a racially divided totalitarian state enjoying a Brazilian economy.

Granted these were normal immigration opinions on the left before the now-obsolete “emerging Democratic majority” thesis took hold, but the shot at immigration advocates for upholding a system of “near-slave labor” is a common Dems-are-the-real-racists conservative talking point, for all that Bernie once agreed with it, as is the line about Brazilification. And is there not, overall, if one is not a class reductionist, a Bronze Age Pervert quality to Vidal, hence Vincenzo’s pun on “vital”? I think of Vidal’s appreciation for Cavafy, for example. BAP and Vidal share a common way of life—the erudite cosmopolitan as sex tourist—and a common erotic metaphysic of cultivated Schopenhauerian pessimism, elegiac over the broken dreams of beautiful boys. Anyway, you can see here and here for my thoughts on The City and the Pillar and Myra Breckinridge; the latter essay is one key to the apparently mysterious gender politics of Major Arcana. I still need to read the historical novels, but the last time Substack did Vidal discourse, courtesy of Henry Begler, I snuck in the first 100 pages of Creation around my Invisible College reading, which is just the exquisitely languid and ironic survey of the ancient world you’d want it to be. Maybe I’ll sneak in the next 100 pages soon.
