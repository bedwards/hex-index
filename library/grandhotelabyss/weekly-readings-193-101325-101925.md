### Pulling Rank: Great Books vs. Great Authors in the American Canon

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8rSx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb1d4ae51-51c7-4fab-951e-7421902146c7_575x750.jpeg)

*Charles Wilson Peale, *The Artist in His Museum* (1822)*

Based on last week’s [post](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/weekly-readings-192-100625-101225#footnote-8-175846569),[4](#footnote-4) a reader writes in to inquire, with the shared understanding that ranks and lists are meant to be stimuli to thought, a game or an exercise to clarify values, rather than a stern, serious critical judgment:

> Okay so in your footnotes this week you said Faulkner, James, and Melville for the best American novelists; last week’s IC claimed Cather as one of the five best; so who’s the fifth?

I am tempted to put all the “authors of one book”—or just their single books—into a separate category from the Great American Novelists. Are the Great American Novels not *Uncle Tom’s Cabin*, *Moby-Dick*, *Huckleberry Finn*, *The Great Gatsby*, and *Invisible Man*? *Uncle Tom’s Cabin*, which is not a great novel but a better one than you’ve heard, was the first ever proposed as a “Great American Novel” when the category was formulated by John William De Forest in 1868. The category presupposes a novel that reflects on “the American soul,” as De Forest said, not simply a novel we judge aesthetically meritorious. (I draw from [this book](https://www.google.com/books/edition/The_Dream_of_the_Great_American_Novel/5m-2AgAAQBAJ?hl=en&gbpv=1&printsec=frontcover) and [this podcast](https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/great-american-novel/id1561004056).) Writing one puts a curse on the author’s oeuvre: even writers of the stature of Melville, Twain, and Fitzgerald fail to equal their one greatest work, while the rest of Stowe goes unread, and the rest of Ellison went unwritten. Stowe’s 19th-century female-identified sentimentalism notwithstanding, itself long kept out from the canon despite the admiration from afar of Dickens, Tolstoy, and George Eliot, the category also tends to attract a certain masculinism, with women almost wholly excluded, except as marking the boundary line to the civilization from which the heroes are in flight, in Melville’s, Twain’s, and even to an extent Ellison’s books, while they stand only as a besmirched ideal in Fitzgerald’s.[5](#footnote-5)

“What about the best novels of James, Wharton, Cather, Faulkner, and more?” you inquire. But we cannot even decide what their best novels are, because they each wrote more than one great novel, and this in distinction to Stowe, Melville, and other GAN victims. Thus I propose Great American Novelists get their own category, with the understanding that greatness of oeuvre, not just greatness of a single book, counts at the main criteria. Each has written more than one plausible GAN candidate. James, who first jokingly coined the GAN acronym, proposed Hawthorne’s *House of the Seven Gables* as the true Great American Novel, but many would opt for *The Scarlet Letter*. Which of James’s novels is the greatest? Is it *The Portrait of a Lady* or *The Bostonians* or *The Ambassadors* or *The Wings of the Dove*? In Faulkner we find *The Sound and the Fury*, *Light in August*, *Absalom, Absalom!*, and the neglected *Go Down Moses*. Cather gives us the exquisite elegy *My Ántonia*, but also, on either side of it, the more epic *O Pioneers!* and *Death Comes for the Archbishop*. Morrison offers *Song of Solomon* and *Beloved*, the one a bid for masculine greatness, the other a feminist revision of the same, not to mention the neglected *Paradise*.[6](#footnote-6) The greatest American *novelists*, then, are Hawthorne, James, Faulkner, Cather, and Morrison.

She was, for instance, caught praising Hitler, not in the group chat, but in the *New York Times*. (I see the “Gertrude Stein Groyper” has already been [proposed](https://x.com/CSandbatch/status/1977121727315337594).) I quote from Barbara Will’s *Unlikely Collaboration: Gertrude Stein, Bernard Faÿ, and the Vichy Dilemma* (2011):

> In May 1934, six years after the ill-fated Kellogg-Briand Peace Pact, Gertrude Stein had something new to say about the idea of international peace: “Hitler should have received the Nobel Peace Prize . . . because he is removing all elements of contest and of struggle from Germany. By driving out the Jews and the democratic and Left elements, he is driving out everything that conduces to activity. That means peace.” Delivered bluntly in an interview with the *New York Times Magazine*, Stein’s comments remain unexplained, unapologetic, and elliptical. At the time, and for years afterward, these comments have troubled, baffled, and divided Stein’s readers. On the one hand, they seem to confirm the surprised observations of Jay Laughlin during the same period: that for Stein, as for Bernard Faÿ, Hitler had the features and the promise of a “great man.” Yet for many of Stein’s defenders, the comments about Hitler are clearly ironic and provocative: “a point of black humor” that is, and has been, “easily misread” by literalistic readers.
> 
> Stein probably wanted her audience to respond in both ways. Her “political” pronouncements of the 1930s often verge on the outrageous, deliberately scandalizing her listeners in order to shock them out of their habitual ways of thinking and responding. As with the rest of her writing, Stein sought in her political statements to speak against the grain, to break through clichés, and to undercut ideological pieties. Yet there is also a strong element of conviction and intentionality in what Stein says in such pronouncements, as though she requires—indeed demands—that her words be taken literally.

With this commitment to “outrageous” irony and contrarianism, she would have written *great* “incendiary footnotes” on Substack.

Speaking of right-wing lesbian modernism, of modernists who stand athwart modernity: as a follow-up to our recent [episode](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/the-invisible-college-willa-cathers) on Willa Cather’s *O Pioneers!*, I recommend, courtesy of Henry Oliver, Charlotte Stroud’s eloquent [celebration](https://engelsbergideas.com/notebook/willa-cathers-prescriptions-for-modern-life/) of the centenary of Cather’s later novel, *The Professor’s House*:

> *The Professor’s House* is a book in three parts. The key to understanding its structure, and therefore its meaning, is given in the epigraph: ‘a turquoise set in dull silver’. It is a fittingly suggestive image that refers to a bracelet given by Outland to Rosamond during their courtship, but it also describes the form of the novel. Like setting a bright blue stone within grey metal, Cather’s structural experiment in *The Professor’s House* is the insertion of the *Nouvelle* (the short story) into the *Roman* (the novel). If parts one and three are the *Roman* and, in their depictions of the ‘dull’ present, correspond to the silver – then part two, where we receive Outland’s story, is the turquoise. As fresh air revives a stale room, Tom’s account of his summer on the Blue Mesa takes us out of the grey paralysis of modernity – a world that, in the manner of those Dutch interiors, is ‘overcrowded and stuffy with things’, and with people whose only concerns are acquisitive.

Muting the characteristic modernist political problem presented by Cather’s turquoise “land acknowledgement,” however, Stroud does not explore the exact nature of the dull silver the novel decries in modernity. Please see my own [essay](https://johnpistelli.com/2016/06/08/willa-cather-the-professors-house/) on the novel of about nine years back for more. Further pursuant to our theme of reactionary politics wedded in seeming incongruity to revolutionary aesthetics, I regret more and more not fitting *Their Eyes Were Watching God* into the IC schedule, as I was reminded this week when I saw the rudely acute [comment](https://x.com/jota_dey/status/1978137189708890536) of a right-wing X anon who actually read politics past identity, as rare these days as was a leftist Twitter “blue-check” reading politics past identity circa 2017.

Further mopping-up operations. Geoff Shullenberger contributes a conspective [essay](https://www.compactmag.com/article/the-faith-of-nick-land/) in *Compact* to the impromptu Nick Land *Festschrift*. Perhaps Nick Land has been listening to The Invisible College:

> The old term for a “directed historical process” is providence—a concept Land explored a few years ago in a [series of essays](https://www.compactmag.com/contributor/nick-land/) for this magazine. In a recent dialogue with the Russian philosopher Aleksandr Dugin, he brought it up by way of a passage from Goethe’s *Faust* in which Mephistopheles describes himself as “a portion of that power which always works for evil and always effects the good.” Because Satan is ultimately subordinate to a higher divine will, what might seem to us like the devil’s machinations are always manifestations of a “providential scheme.” Milton’s *Paradise Lost*, as he noted, points to the same conclusion.

I am reminded once again of Bloom’s occulted thesis in *The Western Canon* that said corpus, from Dante to Beckett, is the *real* Gnostic Bible. As Emerson said, we don’t need to search out any esoterica; the strangest books are the ones we already own; we just haven’t read them yet. Proving that the supposed seductions of pop usually just delay one’s inevitable rendezvous with the canonical sources of our controlling ideas, Shullenberger understatedly observes:

> His older account of a “directed historical process” was a mashup of Deleuze and Guattari with *Neuromancer*, *Blade Runner*, and the *Terminator* films. He has now recast it in the terms of Abrahamic religion and English political history.

Meanwhile, I am just catching up to Matt Tyrnauer’s centenary [celebration](https://archive.is/nGgXb) of Gore Vidal’s feuds in *Vanity Fair*, of which the last was with Christopher Hitchens. When we were imagining Vidal’s and Hitchens’s responses to present-day politics in prior posts, I forgot to link to the latter’s leftist quincentennial Columbus Day [celebration](https://web.archive.org/web/20150213011832/http://lists.csbs.utah.edu/pipermail/marxism/2004-April/106467.html) in *The Nation*, probably the most offensive thing he ever wrote by contemporary standards, and written while he was still firmly on the left and from left-wing precepts:

> Even if the matter of who came “first” could be decided, it would be pointless except as a means to devalue the claims of those—some millions of Irish, English, German, Italian, Jewish and other refugee workers—who migrated across the Atlantic many years after at least some of the “natives” migrated across the Aleutian Island chain. How can a sensibility that represents mass emigration and immigration as mere conquest and settler colonialism dare to call itself “progressive”? But those who view the history of North America as a narrative of genocide and slavery are, it seems to me, hopelessly stuck on this reactionary position.
> 
> \[…\]
> 
> As Marx wrote about India, the impact of a more developed society upon a culture (or a series of warring cultures, since there was no such nation as India before the British Empire) can spread aspects of modernity and enlightenment that outlive and transcend the conqueror. This isn’t always true; the British probably left Africa worse off than they found it, and they certainly retarded the whole life of Ireland. But it is sometimes unambiguously the case that a certain coincidence of ideas, technologies, population movements and politico-military victories leaves humanity on a slightly higher plane than it knew before. The transformation of part of the northern part of this continent into “America” inaugurated a nearly boundless epoch of opportunity and innovation, and thus deserves to be celebrated with great vim and gusto, with or without the participation of those who wish they had never been born.

To quote Fred Jameson from the footnote below, “I know I will be misunderstood if I add that I have some sneaking admiration” for this, but, considering the alternative in the footnote above, Willa Cather’s nostalgic elegy over an indigenous culture triumphed over by greedy Jewish interlopers, a vision with which James, Wharton, Eliot, Pound, Hemingway, Fitzgerald, *and even Stein* might have agreed, I do wish people would confront the actual complexity of the question. I’m not so sure about “indigenous” anyway; as the science fiction author said, “We are all aliens, down here on this alien earth.”

You know who might agree with that gnostic dictum? László Krasznahorkai, our new Nobel laureate in literature. I am now 170 pages into his second novel, *The Melancholy of Resistance*, about two-thirds through. (I’ve only been reading its endless unparagraphed sentences on Fridays and Saturdays, when I allow myself to play truant from the Invisible College.) I liked the last 110 pages better than the first 60. In them we meet the novel’s two heroes: the wandering fool Valuska, who in his innocence takes the fact of the heavens as evidence of cosmic oneness and order; and the bedbound scholar Eszter, who for all his sophistication cannot locate this same principle in his beloved art of music, despite his attempt to recover Pythagorean harmonics. Meanwhile, their community collapses into chaos all around them when a traveling circus trailed by a dangerous rabble brings the exhibit of the world’s largest stuffed whale to town, even as Eszter’s evil wife attempts to impose order. (Krasznahorkai’s “adjectival insistence” continues as almost every page bristles with assertion about about how strange, surreal, frightening, and apocalyptic the events described are; I have chosen to find this funny, as a knowing style of camp excess preferable to over-serious minimalist understatement.) Is it too simple to say that the novel represents spirituality and art (Valuska and Eszter, respectively) as legitimate attempts to perceive cosmic unity amid terrestrial chaos, while religion and politics (represented by the circus and Mrs. Eszter, respectively) are a totalitarian imposition, even if one sometimes favored by the swinish multitude? (The whale, meanwhile, might be the mystery itself.) And is this libertarian aestheticism, with which I am in some sympathy, not just what we’d expect given the novel’s historical context? I will have to investigate further. Next week I hope to offer my final dispatch from the Hungarian apocalypse. Maybe I’ll even find a way to quote from what the translator famously called the novel’s “vast black river of type.”

Then there’s this week’s [“feminization”](https://www.compactmag.com/article/the-great-feminization/) debate. This is a crucial but subtle matter, in some respects the only thing worth talking about, but bound to be distorted by cheap polemics. (And if you think tech accelerationism is the only thing worth talking about, please remember, as I am always suggesting, that they’re really the [same](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/n1x-gender-acceleration-a-blackpaper) topic.) My comments will therefore necessarily have to be oblique or suggestive. Somehow for me the topic intersects with a line in this week’s viral Substack [piece](https://avenuesofamericas.substack.com/p/on-adam-kellys-new-sincerity-american) on literary sociology, a line about the hegemony in literary studies of Fredric Jameson, the last of the great male theorists:

> The writings of Fredric Jameson, long seen as the most important living Marxist critic, moved to the center of literary and cultural studies, displacing the poststructuralist theories that had pervaded humanities discourse at the turn of the twenty-first century.

If we allow what we’ve long known to be as shocking as if we’d learned it for the first time, then this casual observation will seem extraordinary given what Jameson, while incomparably brilliant, both represented and believed:

> What Paul DeMan clearly was, however, as the articles testify, can be seen to be a fairly unremarkable specimen of the then conventional high-modernist aesthete, and the apolitical aesthete at that. This is clearly a very different matter from Heidegger (although it seems unquestionable that the twin Heidegger and DeMan “scandals” have been carefully orchestrated to delegitimate Derridean deconstruction). Heidegger may have been “politically naiive,” as they like to say, but he was certainly political, and believed for a time that the Hitlerian seizure of power was a genuine national revolution that would result in a moral and social reconstruction of the nation. As rector of Freiburg University, and in the best reactionary and McCarthyite spirit, he worked at purging the place of its doubtful elements (although one should remember that genuinely radical or leftist “elements” were very scarce in the German university system of the 1920s, compared to the Hollywood of the 1940s or the Federal Republic of the 1970s). His ultimate disappointment with Hitler was shared by a number of people on the revolutionary (anticapitalist) left within National Socialism, who failed for some time to understand Hitler’s pragmatic position as a moderate or centrist or his crucial relationship to big business. I know I will be misunderstood if I add that I have some sneaking admiration for Heidegger’s attempt at political commitment, and find the attempt itself morally and aesthetically preferable to apolitical liberalism (provided its ideals remain unrealized).

And so fashionable were Jameson and a company of quasi-Leninists and related materialists and radicals in academe (e.g., Žižek and Badiou and their followers, Walter Benn Michaels and other neo-socialists, some Foucauldians who’d turned to Carl Schmitt, etc.) when I was doing my graduate coursework almost two decades ago, and so uncouth was my protest on behalf of the (sentimentalist, aestheticist) novel against the hegemony of what I took to be their swaggering love of art’s desublimation back into violence and a formalized terrorism of reason over emotion, that my (feminist, female) advisor asked in the comments on my written exam if I were proposing a “feminization” of literary criticism. (She wrote [the book](https://www.google.com/books/edition/Expert_Modernists_Matricide_and_Modern_C/Z099DAAAQBAJ?hl=en&gbpv=1&dq=lois+cucullu&printsec=frontcover) on modernism as simultaneous anti- and crypto-feminization of culture: the transformation of the Angel in the House to the Androgynous Artist in the Metropole turned Female Expert in the Academy, as discussed in the Invisible College episodes on Woolf and Stein and *passim*.) I don’t remember how I answered. In part this feminization *did* transpire, and the violence, the cruelty of rational protocol, it only partly re-sublimated into the militant sentimentalist institutional avoidance of harm proved bad enough that I wrote [this](https://johnpistelli.com/2019/09/26/benjamin-moser-sontag-her-life-and-work/) in 2019, in many ways my first and last word on feminization in art and academe, even though I retain a strong sense (see [here](https://johnpistelli.com/2022/05/14/virginia-woolf-a-room-of-ones-own/) and [here](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/dave-hickey-the-invisible-dragon)) of why it seemed necessary and attractive in the first place.

On Substack Notes, relatedly, some were discussing, re: the leaked Young Republican group chat vis-à-vis Jay Jones’s leaked texts, whether the casual cruelty now common on the far left was more or less objectionable than the casual cruelty now common on the far right. (Personally, in real life, I’ve known plenty of suburbanite normie Republican voters, mostly Boomers, not innocent of a certain complacent and unsystematic bigotry, but I don’t know any right-wingers who praise Hitler; on the other hand, my personal real-life acquaintance with leftists not only joking but seriously *theorizing* about who “gets the wall” has been much more extensive. This might be taken into account when you read what follows.) I think the left is less objectionable precisely because it is more objectionable. The infinite violence of which their moral universalism renders them capable curiously mitigates the offense; Žižek somewhere once lauded Mao for saying that the nuclear destruction of the planet would mean nothing to the universe as a whole and was therefore (this was implied) no reason to halt the revolution. The right-winger is racist, hating this or that race, which we all know is culpably small-minded, whereas from disappointed idealism the left-winger may become racist against the whole human race, hence the recent attraction to environmentalism, though it fits more, sensibility-wise, on the right. Right-wingers’ cruelty is transgressive; they are almost erotically aware of their ethical violation; for that reason, their cruelty leaves the very category of cruelty intact. Cruel right-wingers know they’re being cruel, and they relish it. But because they know they’re being cruel, one can almost imagine talking them out of it, since one can still say the word “cruelty” to them and be understood. Cruel left-wingers generate so thick an atmosphere of moral righteousness that there’s no convincing them they’re even capable of doing wrong; no matter what they do, it’s done with the best of intentions, and for the good of humanity. The right-winger embraces cruelty *as* cruelty, extols Hitler as the obverse of all good things, as a vast satanic loser, and therefore presents a more obviously hideous moral spectacle than the left-winger’s entirely unwitting reign of terror. The right’s violence is the violence of a knowing sadist, which we reprehend. The left’s violence is the violence of a guileless innocent, whom we pity. This is why one despises the right more, why the right is proscribed and the left indulged everywhere in the west, even though the left’s historical kill count is plausibly higher than the right’s. The right adulates Hitler in his sublime abjection; they are repelled even as they are seduced; this is the basis of the self-awareness and irony in their fetishistic affection. The left, without ever quite being aware that this is how they feel, are charmed by Stalin’s rakish adolescent mischief and by Mao’s fleshy infantile ingenuousness. Mao might’ve been the biggest killer of all, but it’s as if a baby had murdered everyone in a cute paroxysm of pique, a darling little world-obliterating sneeze. I’m not talking about history here, by the way, which is always in any case in dispute, but rather about what our comrades in the seminar room call “the political imaginary.”

But who, you will wonder, is the *sixth*? Perhaps Wharton, whom I’ve excluded from the top five on the semi-arbitrary nationalist grounds that she’s a “French novelist” in sensibility and even conviction. I doubt she can be excluded from the top 10 on this account, however, whether we prefer the tragic romance of *Ethan Frome* or the tragic realism of *The Age of Innocence*. Who else? I count Hemingway as a poet. Ozick, whom I revere, is more an essayist and story-writer, even in her novels; the same for O’Connor re: stories; Baldwin, also an essayist; Vidal, presumably the same. I exclude Nabokov not because he’s a Russian novelist but because, like Wharton, he’s a “French” one. I assume Dreiser is a man of one book since it’s the only book of his I read, but perhaps *Sister Carrie* qualifies as the sixth Great American Novel, unless that honor goes to another overtly naturalist but covertly Romantic social novel I admire, *The Grapes of Wrath*. Never read Dos Passos, haven’t read much Gaddis, dislike DFW. I am unsure how the contemporaneity of Pynchon, DeLillo, and Roth will weather the ages, though I am happy to extol the latter two. McCarthy is also plausible, though his archaism may prove a style of its time no less than contemporaneity. I will be accused of snobbishly excluding the literary masters of popular romance, so please insert here your own case for Raymond Chandler or Philip K. Dick or Ursula K. Le Guin or (my own preferred choice) Samuel R. Delany. It’s probably too soon to say anything about writers under the age of 80—though when it comes to septuagenarians, I have made a case for Bruce Wagner and have admired what I’ve read from Louise Erdrich—and definitely too soon to say anything about writers under 70.

For sheer power of prose, it seems to be that Saul Bellow must also be in the roll call and would probably be my own choice for number six. Like Harriet Beecher Stowe’s, his friends come from overseas. James Wood said Bellow wrote the greatest American prose of the 20th century besides Faulkner. Martin Amis pronounced him the all-time greatest American novelist without qualification or rival (I quote from his collection *The Rub of Time*):

> I hereby trumpet the prediction that Bellow will emerge as the supreme American novelist. There is, hereabouts, no shortage of narrative genius, and it tends, as Bellow tends, toward the visionary—a quality needed for the interpretation of a New World. But when we look to the verbal surface, to the instrument, to the prose, Bellow is sui generis. What should he fear? The melodramatic formularies of Hawthorne? The multitudinous facetiousness of Melville? The murkily iterative menace of Faulkner? No. The only American who gives Bellow any serious trouble is Henry James.

Amis goes on to dismiss James for mannerism of style and for “gentility, fastidiousness, and a lack of warmth, a lack of candor and engagement,” though to me this indicates a lack in Amis’s own “engagement” with The Master. Amis later recanted his dismissal of Melville. In his autofiction *Inside Story*, Amis recounts that when he read the passage above aloud to Bellow on the author’s deathbed, the expiring Bellow briefly revived to exclaim, “Jesus Christ!” But perhaps Bellow might be excluded from the canon on grounds of moral probity. A *Slate* [exposé](https://slate.com/culture/2025/10/saul-bellow-herzog-nobel-book-abuse-domestic-violence.html) this week elaborates on the problem of Bellow as domestic abuser, a conundrum in a moralist like Bellow as it would not be in an aesthete like Nabokov (whom Amis, incidentally, believes to have genuinely been attracted to adolescent girls). (An aesthetic problem, however, does arise: Bellow’s inability to represent women from the inside, not a debility that afflicted even such literary patriarchs as Tolstoy or Joyce, nor such American male precursors and successors as Hawthorne or James or DeLillo, though Melville, Hemingway, and Roth also had problems in this area.) While we now have more detail, the problem was already known, revealed in the biographies; I made a quick observation about it a year ago when [contrasting](https://grandhotelabyss.tumblr.com/post/764633606825148416/perhaps-my-aversion-to-rabbit-run-is-misguided) Updike (a candidate for the top 20 or 30, I tend to imagine, not the top 10) with Bellow—

> The only reason I said you should read \[*Rabbit, Run*\] is because it’s a really, really good book. Maybe a touch shy of being a great one. *Herzog* is more in the vein of wisdom writing, is equally Joycean and eloquent, and is probably the greater novel; that one has counsel for you, pretty good counsel, I think. But the gentlemanly Updike was apparently a lot nicer IRL than the womanizing, wife-beating Bellow, which just goes to show you that you can’t always trust a wisdom writer.

—but further comment will have to wait until the Invisible College episode on *Herzog* later this year. A line from *The Adventures of Augie March* comes to mind, a caution against extreme responses to human frailty—

> But I had the idea also that you don’t take so wide a stand that it makes a human life impossible, nor try to bring together irreconcilables that destroy you, but try out what of human you can live with first.

—and the author’s vicious failure to heed his own good advice (to conclude a quarrel with an assault on the physically weaker party is all extremism in embryo) doesn’t mean we should *also* fail to take it.
