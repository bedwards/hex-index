Duly Noted: Autotheorizing Annotation

Many new readers must be wondering about my footnote praxis in these Weekly Readings. I’m not sure if I’ve ever fully articulated it, beyond a casual or joking aside, to longtime readers—or even to myself. I do not intend by the footnotes a tribute to postmodern or post-postmodern fiction; I did recently release an Invisible College episode on experimental literature’s all-time masterpiece of annotation, Pale Fire, but as for Infinite Jest, the other most classic example, I never finished it. And my footnotes are only slightly meant in tribute to academe, to that way so many academics reserve their most quarrelsome points for the notes, as I often use my footnotes for what some have called “incendiary” political observations.5 No, the footnote aesthetic of Weekly Readings started accidentally, when I noticed how easy Substack’s interface made the creation of footnotes compared to prior blogging platforms. I started playing with this function week by week and gradually noticed that writing something as a footnote—a paratext rather than a text—relieved the pressure of public utterance and therefore allowed me to be more candid, provocative, playful, experimental, or otherwise “unofficial” than I might otherwise have been inclined to be. I’ve been taking this idea further lately by placing barely explained quotations6 and photos7 down there for a true montage effect. The footnotes also allow me to write in a genre I probably would have disallowed myself—as too much the despairing European late-modernist anti-philosopher’s game—if I’d proposed it to myself as such: the post-Romantic-fragment8 extended aphorism à la Schlegel, Nietzsche, Wittgenstein, Adorno, Barthes, and others, cf. in our own literature early Sontag’s favored “notes” structure.9 This permits me the welcome hypocrisy—how much time do we spend seeking permission for our hypocrisies?—of castigating my contemporaries’ slovenly and portentous fragmentariness while indulging my own. In a classic “when a measure becomes a target”-type mistake, I slowly began to plan only these entries’ footnotes and to make the supposed main text more and more off-handed or even trivial. Like every tactic or schtick, this one will wear out its welcome, but I’m not going to make a New Year’s resolution about it. Most problems resolve themselves organically if you let them; we underrate the power of inaction. Perhaps as we go on in this endeavor, I will more and more reserve for the main text, which I suspect many people of no longer reading at all, my most recklessly earnest or insouciantly playful writer-self, while the footnotes will vanish into the irrelevance that always awaits the most strenuously serious of discourses.

On this darkest day of the year, I can’t say nothing about Jacob Savage, can I? I’d rather say nothing since it’s too hard for any side in the dispute to acquire the correct tone. One is torn between wanting to say what one saw inside of academia in the 2010s and not wanting to present oneself (misleadingly) in the victim role, or to over-value what the institutions were like immediately before their misguided efforts to save themselves with social justice. My record is clean: I wrote exactly what I thought under my own name when it was all happening: see here, here, and here, all from summer 2020, and especially here from 2019, not to mention this intervention, plus my review of what may have been the signal literary text of that moment. About my own experience, I won’t tell you more than I already told you—please note, in that post from the antediluvian days of 2023, a link to the first in what is now a triptych of Savage’s viral elegies—though one thing I missed from our intrepid journalist’s comprehensive survey is enough sense of how utterly cynically these actions were undertaken.

(Okay, one funny anecdote: I was very briefly acquainted with a literal DEI chief, neither male nor white, who had a burner account on Twitter circa 2021 to follow Ben Shapiro et al. as psychic relief from what she privately regarded as the unbearable nonsense and hysteria of her day job.)

The problem is not the demographics that were promoted but the ideologies enshrined. Savage runs the numbers, but what I won’t forget is the moral atmosphere. To have watched liberals naively or nihilistically instrumentalizing totalized essentialized hate and its sacrificial scapegoating logic on behalf of their own managerial elitist project in straitened economic circumstances is to understand why Dostoevsky wrote Demons. It’s to understand as well, for example, if I may mention one demon Dostoevsky himself never dodged, and as I was trying to explain just last week, why it’s no coincidence that anti-Semitism became so much more widespread and virulent after more than a decade of this project to “start a war inside society,” rather than to “take all classes, the whole population as it is, and proceed toward a new social truth” (to use Boris Groys’s terms quoted in a post I linked last week). The latter is the true Romantic option, while post-Romantic war-inside-society ideologies like Marxism, fascism, and their sequelae in identity politics are lethal reductive corruptions of the justly utopian Romantic impulse.

(“But,” the good left-liberal reader will object, “conservatives also indulge in hate based on sacrificial scapegoating logic.” Yes, I know. Didn’t your mother ever tell you two wrongs don’t make a right? Or, if you won’t listen to your mother, did you forget what the man said not to do when you battle with monsters?)

Candace Owens’s most recent video (well over two million views as of this writing) exemplifies the blasted battlefield where this war inside society has stranded us, as she couples the apocalyptic victimological tropes of the 2010s ideology allowed to commandeer the institutions—she has been abused, she has been enslaved, she is a survivor—with a different conclusion about the guiltily privileged party responsible for her suffering:

You are such a little worm. […] What you really believe in is Baal-Berith, that’s what your people believe in, you believe that you are contract lords. [...] You have absolutely no values that exist outside of the Talmud. […] Jewish people were the ones who were trading us, Jewish people were in control of the slave trade. […] You are not “chosen” to do shit, except within the Synagogue of Satan, clearly.

Then again, here is part of a sentence I wrote in 2015: “it has always seemed to me that the pejorative use of the racial epithet ‘white’ often attracts the prejudicial associations that attach to ‘gay’ and ‘Jewish’ as well.” My point was not to stigmatize the criticism of inequitable systems, but to advise caution about how that critique may translate culturally. Among people who are not populist conspiracy theorists, or those who think they aren’t, the assumptions under which these policies were devised have not actually been revisited or altered (except tactically: “tell the plebs we just care about ‘affordability’ now…”) by their one-time champions. I don’t see why their partisans should stop defending them when presented with evidence that they’d worked as intended. I admired their candor back when they were surer they were no longer obligated to persuade anyone:

Must history have losers? The record suggests yes. Redistribution is a tricky business. Even simple metaphors for making the world more equitable — leveling a playing field, shifting the balance — can correspond to complex or labor-intensive processes. What freedoms might one have to surrender in order for others to be free? And how to figure it when those freedoms are not symmetrical? […] Some things are zero sum — perhaps more things than one cares to admit. To say that feminism is good for boys, that diversity makes a stronger team, or that collective liberation promises a greater, deeper freedom than the individual freedoms we know is comforting and true enough. But just as true, and significantly less consoling, is the guarantee that some will find the world less comfortable in the process of making it habitable for others.

Do you see what I mean why I say (see footnote 3 above) the left lost something when it lost the spiritual element? If the choice is between two factions advocating different versions of a zero-sum reductionist-materialist society that treats human beings as numerical blocs in absolute quantitative competition, rather than proposing a different philosophical basis for society entirely, then who cares? Or take the retrospectively ironic conclusion of a universally celebrated essay from 10 years ago: “Let us burn this motherfucking system to the ground and build something better.” Well, the system has certainly burned down, but we await the “better” construct.

Anyway, as the poet said, and later regretted saying for the same reason some now regret the excesses of the 2010s, “History to the defeated / May say Alas but cannot help or pardon.” Despite some institutions’ having regarded me as the culpable legatee of epochal oppression, and thus the historical dialectic’s justly designated loser, I was not actually born into the classes that took their access to those institutions for granted, and I therefore always felt ambivalent about them anyway. Crude provocateur Delicious Tacos’s jape is in this case correct. As the mystics say, “Rejection is God’s protection.” To be promoted to officer of a sinking ship is not the honor it might seem.

Savage cannily casts himself in his article’s conclusion as an “ordinary talent” left out of the social rewards once awarded the ordinary; he invites sympathy according to the same logic by which he was excluded in the first place. As I’ve argued several times in this year’s Invisible College, America runs on the Exodus narrative, which means the victim role is always the star turn. But I am temperamentally averse even to victimologies that might advantage me. I endured what some whiskey-sippers are now rather hyperbolically calling the “annihilation” of white men the same way Susan Sontag (see footnote 9 below) endured whatever exclusions she faced in her era, that is, by not regarding myself as an “ordinary talent” and therefore figuring I would be taken care of one way or another in the end. The institutions of art come and go, but art is forever.

(The last time we had this conversation, Savage expressed skepticism at such an approach to the problem.)

A small handful of bureaucrats I never respected may have been rude to me, but this weighs little against the heavy circumstances of war, penury, exile, debility, and/or oppression in which so many great works of art and thought I love were created. So, like Sontag, I am admittedly not filled with a sense of solidaristic responsibility toward my superficial cohorts, nor do I want my work affixed with a group label, still less pitied on group grounds. When I was Christmas shopping in the new Barnes and Noble, I saw a display advertising what we might call “identity politics nonfiction,” and Hillbilly Elegy was on it sure enough, right there among all the famous activists of the other party. Like Sontag, I aspire to a higher shelf in that Borgesian library in the sky—soon, I’m sure, to be unanimously designated “The John P. Pistelli and the Jorge L. Borges Infinite Library of Babel.”

Finally, a reminder about Compact magazine, which some fastidious scribblers argue should neither be read nor written. It brings me no pleasure to be fair to Compact since they gave me what many people are saying is the most unfair review of the century, but we should recall that, included among their mysterious funding sources, was exactly the same one that helps to fund all the outlets our high-dudgeon leftie hacks will write for. I guess we can credit the reductionist materialists with this insight at least: America’s not an infinite library but a 24/7 casino, as I’m sure Chomsky informed Epstein on the plane, and the house always wins.

8

Continuing debates about the “New Romanticism” with Matthew Gasda and Paul Franz at The Republic of Letters.

Brother Matthew begins with the sobering suggestion that “New Romanticism” appeals to writers on this platform because it gives an ersatz and brandedly nostalgic gravitas to what might otherwise appear to be just more monetized online discourse. This is true but somewhat tautological. Artists and thinkers use the mediatic affordances of their moment, and sometimes aggrandize them in the process. What’s the alternative? I think “the internet” is a negative ethical category for Matthew, as it is a positive ethical category for the pro-tech set, whereas it’s not really either for me. I find it more positive than negative, on balance, because there’s never been a medium of wider dissemination for thought and art, even if the very broadness of its address threatens to debase thought and art’s value and therefore invites a compensatory exclusionary gesture like declaring oneself a Romantic to create an air of artificial scarcity. Still, the medium may be the message, but the message is also the message. I agree much more strongly with Matthew’s second and third suggestions: that the New Romanticism offers a way out of our period’s dueling political extremisms (see footnote 5 above) by allowing us to go back before the ossification of left and right, themselves divergent creations of the Romantic moment, into twin totalitarianisms; and, more narrowly and more sweetly, that the New Romanticism represents the coming into full cultural seniority of those of us reared up like an invisible army in the middle-class American suburbs on the eve of the millennium by Harold Bloom.

(I started reading Bloom in the 10th grade, 1998 or so, because I saw Shakespeare and the Invention of the Human in Borders Books. I got the just-published hardcover of How to Read and Why as a graduation gift from my AP English teacher at a ceremony where each academic department in the high school awarded its senior star pupil. All the teachers wrote me nice little messages on the inside covers. I still have the book. Is this an embarrassing revelation? This somehow feels like an embarrassing revelation. I think that’s because even if you were such a person once, such a young man, you were supposed to grow up and get hard and become a real tough leftist, a real take-no-prisoners literary sociologist, a materialist, a guy who ruthlessly slaughtered the part of his soul that apprehended art as a numinous portal, and a guy who, even though he did all that, even though he killed the best part of him, still didn’t get an elite job, but isn’t mad about it like those chuds are, no way, because what do you take him for, a reactionary, a petty bourgeois, some kind of a fucking pussy? And yet, ephebe, why would I wish to be such a person? Like Schiller, like Melville, I hold fast to the dream of my youth.)

Brother Paul, meanwhile, waxes more obliquely on Romanticism old and new as a faith that the cultivated inner spark may persist in an alienated world and may perhaps even someday adequate that world to itself. (Worth recalling here that when some of us first started talking about New Romanticism two years ago, we weren’t thinking of any version of Bloomian high culture but rather of popular online occultism—mind over matter, baby—no matter how occasionally vulgar its form, as for instance in footnote 4 above. Bloom, theorist of gnosticism qua American religion and never quite the elitist his detractors take him for, was of course also intrigued by such things.) Paul concludes by re-staging an old debate between Marshall McLuhan and Norman Mailer. I find myself there slightly more on McLuhan’s side than Mailer’s, which is no surprise, as I do tend to prefer the modernists’ vast impersonal heterocosms to the Romantics’ small personal fragments. (Or do I? It seems odd to say so in a highly fragmentary and highly personal footnote. See, for once, the main text of this post!) I finally accept the Eliotic mandate of “making new wholes” for my work, but I also hope this may be consistent with a fidelity to my own dream. Modernism as a continuation of Romanticism: as participants in the Emersonian oversoul, we may all, though the recessive agency of an organically ordered artifice organizing our affections, share a dream someday. The McLuhanite catch—the Joycean riposte to the Lawrentians—is that the materialization of this possibility will look more like a fully artificial world than either Matthew or Paul might wish to countenance. I’m on the fence about it myself.

The week’s main event in this corner of the internet came when Henry Begler added to his canon of the great 20th-century nonfictioneers and dropped an impressively comprehensive essay on friend-of-the-blog Susan Sontag. I could spend all day quoting my favorite lines (“Under the Sign of Saturn is Sontag at her most majestic: the raven queen of the intellect, black wings spread wide”) or frivolously quibbling with this or that local judgment (I don’t like Illness as Metaphor; I think The Volcano Lover is crucial), but the whole performance mesmerizes, and the conclusion gets it just right:

She wanted to be a major novelist. If that didn’t work out, she wanted to be Eliot or Matthew Arnold—a black-clad gatekeeper at the portcullis of Culture. Yet the way in which her example inspires brings to mind neither of those two, nor does it bring to mind Benjamin or Canetti or Adorno or any of her other European models. Instead it makes me think of Oscar Wilde, to whom she dedicated “Notes on Camp.” Wilde’s literary output was wonderful, but his greatest work of art was his life and his myth. So too with Sontag.

That’s probably a parable for all of us on this platform and the adjacent ones, as per Matthew’s stricture on New-Romanticism-as-opiate-of-the-Substack-intellectual in footnote 8 above. We will need an Influencer as Artist to go with Wilde’s Critic as Artist, and Adorno and Benjamin, each in his own way an icon according to canons of taste Sontag herself taught us, have a place in that story, too. The question for skeptics of this platform and adjacent ones is this: why didn’t the institutions they’re replacing put out anything as fun and must-read as 7000 words from Henry Begler on Susan Sontag this week?
