I will keep this final Weekly Reading post of 2025 as brief as I can, since I released a novelette yesterday and will release a podcast episode[1](#footnote-1) and the the new Invisible College syllabus later this week. While 2025 has obviously been the biggest year so far in my literary “career,”[2](#footnote-2) I will try to remain calm. I got a taste, as the poet said, of the glamorous life.

Reviewing the year in reverse, I notice that Ross Barkan’s new literary journal, *The Metropolitan Review*, was there at the beginning, in the middle, and here at the end. Yesterday, the *Met* released my new novelette, *[The Persephone Complex: A Letter](https://www.metropolitanreview.org/p/the-persephone-complex)*. As I summarized the matter in my too-excitable Substack Note:

I’ve told myself over the years that I was finished writing short stories, that the form itself may even be finished; before *Persephone*, I hadn’t written a story since the 2010s. (See my thoughts on the form and my “collected stories” [here](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/149209371/short-order-collected-stories).) When the *Met*’s fiction editor Django Ellenhorn solicited a story this summer, I didn’t know what to do. I couldn’t say no, though; I spent the last two decades having my submissions rejected, so I’m not going to turn down invitations! I pieced *Persephone* together, therefore, over the course of August, wondering what it meant and where it came from all the while. I submitted it, with its early episode of political assassination and shell-case messaging, the week before Charlie Kirk. Then I figured I’d been onto something. Regular readers and listeners will recognize in *Persephone* motifs from the Invisible College and themes from the New Romanticism discourse (see below). Finally, at the novelette’s conclusion, the *very* end, I played a possibly annoying Nabokovian trick, the kind I would hypocritically deride in another writer, such as Nabokov. Please don’t give it away in *TMR*’s comment section, but the first person to identify the trick in the comments section to *this* post will receive a year’s free subscription to The Invisible College or a free print copy of *Major Arcana* or some other gift commensurate with my esteem for your perceptiveness.

Last month witnessed my academic piece, [“Romantic Truth: Imaginative Authority in the Literary Criticism of René Girard,”](https://muse.jhu.edu/pub/1/article/974691/pdf)[3](#footnote-3) in the distinguished Johns Hopkins journal *Literary Imagination*, anent, of course, James Tussing’s (formerly Secret Squirrel) even more celebrated essay [“The Mysteries of Love: On Alice Munro.”](https://muse.jhu.edu/pub/1/article/974683/pdf) Both are free to read until the New Year. For more on my essay, you can listen to my podcast [episode](https://soundcloud.com/jhupress/4-12-paul-franz-and-john) with editor Paul Franz and read Gnocchic Apocryphon’s [summary](https://gnocchiccodices.substack.com/p/mimesis-imagination-alice-munro) and qualified defense of Girard.[4](#footnote-4)

Then there was *Romanticon*, the new journal at once [conceived and baptized](https://therepublicofletters.substack.com/p/substack-and-its-discontents) by friend of the blog Mary Jane Eyre, an explicit attempt at what has been called “The New Romanticism” of this platform and adjacent ones. With my contribution, [“I Come in Self-Annihilation,”](https://romanticon.substack.com/p/i-come-in-self-annihilation) I attempted two tasks: first, to remove discussions of New Romanticism from historicist pedantry about the *old* Romanticism, since the “New” term is transparently a ploy on our part to advance today’s culture, not to provide academic studies of yesterday’s; and second, to acknowledge critics’ qualms about the potentially dangerous metaphysic and politic of Romanticisms old and new, of Romanticism as reactionary-radical *temperament*. This was my least liked piece of the year, I confess. My concession of the danger was read as an endorsement of the danger, and my evasion of historicist pedantry was met with the expected riposte that, as I had obviously *not* consulted Éclair’s reading of Bürgermeister’s commentary on Astrostratus’s tractate, as I evinced the bad taste to believe that reading Blake or Keats like a normal person gave me any right to say anything about the subject, I was unfit to pronounce. And yet the dimmest manifestation coach on TikTok would remind me that titling the piece “I Come in Self-Annihilation” invited what else but my annihilation. *Be careless what you wish for*, as Bruce Wagner (see below) advises.

Did I mention *The Metropolitan Review*? In April, just before my book came out, I was honored to contribute a centenary essay, [“America the Beautiful,”](https://www.metropolitanreview.org/p/america-the-beautiful) on *The Great Gatsby*. That essay more than any other suggests what I mean *literarily* when I say, *if* I say, New Romanticism. I mean not only the recovery of beauty, but also the redefinition of beauty to mean nothing less than an orderly recreation and therefore an augmentation of the world’s intense complexity. And in February, the day after James Joyce’s birthday and three days before my own, I was pleased to be there at the journal’s debut with a comprehensive introductory essay, [“Dream Warrior,”](https://www.metropolitanreview.org/p/dream-warrior) on the oeuvre of Bruce Wagner, a Romantic visionary among the contemporaries if ever there was one.

Speaking of Bruce, I began 2025 with a big essay for his great publisher, Arcade, [“Literature and Magic: The Marriage of Word and Image,”](https://arcadepublishing.substack.com/p/literature-and-magic-the-marriage) an argument for the spiritual meaning and potential of the multi-medial text in this hybrid world of ours when even or especially the most screenified of us long for the page, the stage, the desert, the mountain, the forest, the ocean, and when picture and word, the image and its breaker, make love and war as never before.[6](#footnote-6)

Finally, 2025 saw the successful second year of [The Invisible College](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/the-invisible-college-introduction), my literature podcast for paid subscribers. We started in the winter with ancient Greek literature from Homer’s Odyssey through the great Athenian playwrights Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides, and Aristophanes to the birth of aesthetic and literary theory in Plato, Aristotle, and Longinus. Then we turned in spring to modern western world literature from Goethe to Borges, encompassing Kleist, Flaubert, Baudelaire, Dostoevsky, Tolstoy, Chekhov, Ibsen, Kafka, and Proust. A summer on Shakespeare and Milton took us radically to the root, if I may redund, of English-language literature and myth, while fall brought us to the modern American novel from James and Twain to Morrison and Pynchon, including Wharton, Cather, Toomer, Stein, Barnes, Ellison, Bellow, Nabokov, and DeLillo.

In any successful course of study, both teacher and student (i.e., podcaster and podlistener) should learn something. Personally, I learned in 2025 that I like French literature, both in itself (Flaubert, Baudelaire, Proust) and in its expat American apprentices (James, Stein, Barnes), more than I’d remembered, since I’ve developed over the years some prejudicial pseudo-conviction that (as Deleuze put it) French literature is “too human” compared to the varied alien intensities of the American, Irish, English, German, Russian. Not so! The French are aliens, too, I am happy to report, which may influence some 2026 choices.

The main and developing lesson of The Invisible College, however, centered in the *Ulysses* episodes of 2024 and the Greek tragedy, Shakespeare, and Toni Morrison episodes of 2025, is that what critics of aestheticism, whether Christian conservative or Marxist materialist, disparage as “the religion of art” is *actually a real religion*, a religion older than (though encompassing everything true in) Christianity and Marxism—and, I speculate, fitter for the future than either. The final episode drops on New Year’s Eve, the 2026 schedule arrives on New Year’s Day, and the first episode of the New Year, on an undisclosed topic, will debut on January 16. Tremendous thanks to everyone who has made this project possible. The adventure continues.

“You’re talking about yourself a lot,” you will say, “but aren’t you supposed to be listing the best books you read this year?” Yes, but I spent too much time rereading old books and their surrounding contexts and commentaries for The Invisible College to make that a very exciting exercise. I *have* been trying to make myself more responsible, since I’ve evidently not devoted myself to enough dying or dead arts, for contemporary cinema. As I’ve said elsewhere, though, and bearing in mind that I still haven’t seen *Sinners*, I found it a very let us say gendered year at the movies, with the capital-M male statements (*Eddington*, *One Battle After Another*, *Bugonia*) and capital-F female statements (*Materialists*, *Die My Love*, *Hamnet*) somewhat canceling each other out. So I will split the difference: I will make the androgynous or nonbinary choice and say that *Sentimental Value*—a sort of “woman’s picture” by a male director—is my movie of the year.
