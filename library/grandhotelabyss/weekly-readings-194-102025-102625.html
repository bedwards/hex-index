High Definition: What Is Literature?

The simplest questions are the hardest to answer. Recently, an anonymous reader wrote in with this: “How do you define literature?” The following is my best3 answer to this unanswerable question:

“Literature” as we know it (creative writing) is a post-Enlightenment historical formation meant to preserve an autonomous, ordered space for psychologically, socially, and spiritually necessary non-rational thought (i.e., the imagination). Literature liberates into social freedom energies once requisitioned as textual commands by the now-vanquished ancien régime and keeps triumphal Enlightenment from rationalizing them out of existence. Literature’s hallmark is a metacognitive awareness exhibited somewhere in the text of language or narrative’s aesthetic (i.e., non-strictly-communicative, connotative, affective, sensuous, etc.) dimension; the emphasis on the aesthetic and the prompt to meta-awareness defunctionalizes the rhetorical function of language, setting the reader free to reflect on the text rather than regarding it as a command. (In this sense, “literature” is a subset of the broader field of “art.” The other arts also participate in imagination but tend to incorporate more sensory experience than literature can.) The concept of “literature” can be applied retrospectively to texts that pre-date but anticipate “literature” from Greek and Biblical poetry through the Renaissance, and can also be applied to non-literary media of all sorts from philosophical treatises to pop song lyrics.4 This definition is more normative than descriptive (it allows one to say, for example, that a bad novel or poem is “not literature,” usually because it lacks the aforesaid aesthetic dimension of language5 or invitation to metacognitive awareness) and in fact implies that merely descriptive definitions, which would dissolve “literature” into “rhetoric” or “discourse,” are esoteric power-plays by various actors who wish to subject the imagination either or both to the pre-modern polities the Enlightenment subdued or/and to unrevised Enlightenment in its rationalist totalization, i.e., either or both to theocracy or/and to technocracy.6

Speaking of everyone’s favorite revolutionary engraver, you probably don’t need me to tell you to read our own Henry Begler’s characteristically thorough essay on Blake in Romanticon, assessing the status of our poet-prophet in popular nonfiction from the ’90s to today. Henry encompasses English authors perhaps somewhat shadowy for Americans but who nonetheless comprise the immediate literary genealogy of the Blakean visionaries who inspired Major Arcana:

[Peter] Ackroyd, one of the last of his kind, was the man for the job: an English man of letters in the truest sense, not a lifelong academic surfacing every so often to offer a book to the public, but an enthusiastic, talented, and staggeringly productive amateur who stands or falls on the strength of his work. His output includes, among other things, biographies of Eliot, Dickens, Shakespeare, and Chaucer, a six-volume History of England, begun in 2011 and finished in 2021, the massive London: The Biography, and sixteen novels as of this writing, many of them requiring no small amount of historical research. He is more like a Victorian sage than a modern writer; one imagines him polishing off a few bottles with Carlyle and Ruskin before going home to dash off another fifty pages of flowing prose.

[…]

Ackroyd had a shadow, a magus who stalked the streets of London: Iain Sinclair, poet, novelist, and psychogeographer, who has made a life out of excavating the secret history of the capital via Sebaldian rambles around its backstreets and forgotten corners. Once, Ackroyd and Sinclair had been colleagues in the same poetic circles—Sinclair a fearsomely modernist disciple of Pound and Olson, Ackroyd charming and gossipy, a Kensington Frank O’Hara—and Ackroyd had borrowed heavily from Sinclair’s poem Lud Heat for his novel Hawksmoor (both of which went on to inform Alan Moore’s From Hell). Both men were chroniclers of England and its myths, but in different forms; Ackroyd smooth and feline on the BBC promoting his majestic biography of Dickens and novels about Milton and John Dee, Sinclair speaking from the gutters for the madmen, murderers, and holy fools. Blake, being somewhere between the two camps, was contested territory.

Sorry if that’s unreadable. Using jargon is the only way to compress. To make it readable, I would have to write a book expanding on each term and concept. But I don’t want to write a book defining literature; I just want to write literature.

I renew the apology here. The day after I posted it, however, someone on Goodreads “liked” my old review of art theorist Boris Groys’s book In the Flow, which you can read here. This fortuitously reminded me that I borrowed my definition of “literature” from Groys’s definition of “art.” (My plagiarism was the concept of “defunctionalization.”) For Groys, “art emerges as the death of design.” By analogy, literature emerges as the death of rhetoric. While both art and literature appear to be dying in our time, Groys, as a kind of wry communist accelerationist, might rather argue, somewhat in the spirit of artificial angel Grimes, that we are all becoming art and literature as artifice envelops the planet.

Or cinema. Like perverted spectators at the proverbial tennis polycule or ballet orgy, we are rooting for Luca Guadagnino here at Grand Hotel Abyss, so much so that I nominated Suspiria as one of the century’s best films so far. But After the Hunt unfortunately misses the mark. It’s a weak solution of Lydia Tár, as Humbert Humbert might have said, and as every reviewer has pointed out already. It attempts the same tricky maneuver as Todd Fields’s film—using a complex, ambiguous character study to grant all contending parties in the woke vs. anti-woke debate their due, thereby ironically discrediting wokeness by proving art superior to politics—and with some of the same materials—primarily, the anti-hero as ultra-successful white woman in a demanding and traditionally male-dominated high-culture profession beset by a rising generation of privileged minorities—but collapses in a tangle of portentous allusions, absurd twists, and strained performances. Like Tár, the film shows the woke to be correct in their moral analysis but disqualified to make one by their lack of taste; the film’s mimesis of good taste, however, is not quite convincing enough to drive home the point.

The governing conceit has potential but is probably too precious to animate a whole film instead of just a skit: as signaled by the credits sequence, this is, content-wise, a Woody Allen film, neurotic intellectuals in erotic extremis, but formally it’s shot and scored more like Alfred Hitchcock, all suspenseful camerawork and startling strings. Guadagnino and Co. intended, I gather, to draw out the menace latent in Allen’s metropolitanism. But that’s a one-off joke, not serious cinema. Anyway, Allen himself rendered a fair bit of After the Hunt thematically redundant in the underrated Irrational Man.

After the Hunt strikes me as a “Kamalacore” film, not in this case one that aimed to support a continuing woke hegemony, but one that anticipated the need to go on criticizing or satirizing that hegemony. To give the filmmakers their due, however, the “five years later” epilogue clearly implies the main narrative’s 2019 or 2020 setting, which renders its portrayal of wokeness duly historical and accounts for the “vibe shift,” something I also tried to do in Major Arcana. There’s no excuse, however, for the too-cute Brechtian gesture at the end, as if our auteur were exclaiming, like Miss Mowcher in Dickens, “Ain’t I volatile?”

Because I don’t think After the Hunt succeeds even in meeting the standard of entertainment, I won’t allow myself to be baited into any philosophical and literary analysis by its obtrusive allusions to the panopticon and Homo Sacer and similar, but I did laugh at “The Future of Jihad Is Female,” and I appreciate the reminder that I still need to read Buddenbrooks.

In the New York Times review, Alissa Wilkinson, while also unimpressed by the aesthetics, does extract the likely philosophical point: an endorsement of intersubjectivity over abstract ethics. I disagree with Wilkinson’s reading of the climax, though. The Euro-sophisticate director of Call Me By Your Name likely thinks the alienist’s riposte to his wife’s confession of age-gapped infatuation is Ameri-naive, not to mention puritanical. We could even read the scene as a pointed reversal of the earlier film’s climax. In Call Me By Your Name Michael Stuhlbarg’s scholar-father warmly bestows his blessing on his son’s ardor; but in After the Hunt, Stuhlbarg’s therapist-husband condemns his wife’s similar youthful longing. In After the Hunt, the failure of intersubjectivity is the husband-therapist’s, not the wife-philosopher’s: he is not listening to her, replacing her authentic testimony with an ethical abstraction. He tells her what is and isn’t right while she’s just trying to tell him what is. This is why she looks away, and this might also be the final import of the Woody Allen allusions: a Manhattan, as it were, on the rocks, all illicit desire chilled for better and for worse by the therapeutic state.

To give a sample of the film’s intellectual qualities, I’ll just tell you that its scene ripping off the master-class in Tár takes place in a philosophy seminar where teacher and student debate an aphorism from Minima Moralia. Not, alas, that book’s most relevant aphorism, given the circumstances: “Every visit to the cinema leaves me, against all my vigilance, stupider and worse.” Better luck next time, amico mio.

Can you go too far in the other direction? I sometimes wonder if our new Nobel laureate László Krasznahorkai has done just that. I’ve reached page 246 of The Melancholy of Resistance. The measured defense of holy fool Valuska and musicological Pythagorean Eszter’s twinned quests for metaphysical unity in a chaotic world I detected earlier in the novel has given way to something else. The mob the circus has incited is now destroying the town. Meanwhile, as Eszter tries to barricade himself inside his house, planning to hide out forever with Valuska, the novel becomes a precise Heideggerean-Levinasian fable (I’ve still never read Levinas; I just remember from grad school that if someone says “face” you’re supposed to reply “Levinas,” and the same goes for “hammer” and “Heidegger”) as the old professor learns from wielding a hammer that there is no higher unity but only

an eternally restless seething mass of events conducting an austere and and eternal dialogue between themselves, every one of the million incidents, each of those million relationships, million but uniform and therefore in one single uniform relationship along with everything else, uniting in a single confluence of conflicting elements, between things that simply by existence resist and those which by virtue of being themselves strive to overcome that resistance.

while his recollection of Valuska’s face teaches him that only love of the concrete other matters, and not “fixed ideas”:

…as far as he was concerned the process whereby the face resolved itself into a smile, or having emerged from a solemn mood brightened up again, simply revealed the fact that there was nothing further to seek in it: the smile was enough, the solemnity and the brightening were sufficient; he understood that the celestial aspect was no longer of real interest to him, that it was the face and the face alone that mattered to him: it was the face of Valuska not Valuska’s vision of the universe that counted.

Valuska, meanwhile, has been taken up into the furious mob, which makes him a target of Mrs. Eszter. This experience has taught him that Eszter was right when the old man used to preach a gospel of total chaos to him, that his holy-foolism has indeed been folly, an epiphany emphasized when he finds a notebook scribbled by one of his fellow members of the psychotic rabble, a notebook written in the exact same torrential style as the novelistic prose that surrounds it, Valuska’s reading and rereading of which doubles as a defense of this style:

…he too started [reading] again in the belief that what didn’t go down properly the first time might be fully absorbed the second time around: most importantly so that he should be able to overcome as yet terribly repulsive aspect of the whole, the essence of which seemed to lie in the impression that every sentence kept recurring; secondly, so that, like a colt accommodating itself to the pace of his mother, he should ties himself as closely as possible tot he headlong rush of the dark, galloping narrative; and lastly, so that he should be able to understand more completely its deeper significance, which was directly specifically at him, and thereby double his strength, so he might be able to join his comrades in ‘the war that was raging outside’.

I will finish the novel before I come to any conclusions. The novel’s vision of “resistance,” and of its ethical demand of patient attention to the other, justifies the impediment to reading that is its style, but I remain skeptical that the standard-issue philosophical skepticism, however crossed by a last-ditch vision of love, demands a style so abrasive. (For the civilizational consequences of this postmodern ethic of skepticism-plus-love, which I have defined as a negative worship of the Hitlerian totalitarianism the novel represents in circus and whale, see footnote 6 below.) When Valuska was caught up in the mob, I thought, “This reminds me of some other novel I like better, but which one?” Then I remembered which one: Portraits and Ashes.

Or the unholy wedding of theology to technocracy that was Nazism. This week it was the Democrats’ turn in the Hitler Seat. Their latest fortunate-son faux-populist showed not the skull beneath the skin but the Totenkopf beneath the t-shirt. (Enough working-class theater already, enough proleface minstrel show. What attracts the would-be arriviste, which every good American is, to Trump is precisely his nouveau riche flaunting of wealth’s flagrant aesthetic. Everybody wants a gold toilet and a bathroom chandelier, not to mention a fabulous ballroom, and nobody wants to look at some slumming trust-funder’s costume of filth.) As the “postwar liberal international order” collapses around us with the disappearance of its Baby-Boomer heirs, all its taboos spring like cartoon skeletons out of their graves, trailing mud and worms in their danse macabre, with Hitler the chief taboo among them, the master signifier holding all other ethical signification in place. Postmodernism, the postwar liberal international order’s legitimating philosophy in its end-of-history phase, had only one commandment: “Don’t be Hitler.” The guidance it offered in this matter, unhelpfully, was that anything you do makes you Hitler, since Hitler’s sin was not so much genocide or conquest as essentialism and positivism, the transformation of reality in the light of what he took to be truth, as if this were not the basic human endowment. It was probably a mistake to institute a wholly negative civilizational telos, a high culture based on a negation (of itself) rather than any truly positive ideals. While this via negativa created a certain dynamism, since nothing could be allowed to stand, since everything ironized itself into everything else, it might have been too unstable to last. Whatever is made taboo is also and necessarily made sacred; a negative ideal is always in danger of flipping into a positive ideal in the absence of any other virtue. (Anti-fascism, anti-sexism, anti-racism, etc.: these are not proper ethical goals because, being mere negatives, they are guaranteed to reverse themselves into the positive. As Freud says, the unconscious cannot understand the word “no.”) The postwar liberal international order was a religion all adversary and no deity, as many have observed, which is why we will need a new religion in the coming years if we are to avoid the reign of sheer diabolism. A wholly uninteresting diabolism, too: I was recently revisiting Hitler’s “thought,” if we can call it that, through this guidebook, and was struck all over again by its worse-than-mediocrity, a grim farrago of cheap jingoistic biologisms, simplistic Darwinism and Malthusianism, and the least interesting passages of Nietzsche. This idiot is our devil-god? And people want to blame Romantic idealism for this? Blame Romantic idealism for the Red Terrors: at least Stalin and Mao had the sheer majesty of the Hegelian system behind them, however corrupted into materialism. Can we see any alliance between these twin terrors, however, these enemy brothers, as some did at midcentury? Someone asked me this on my super-secret Tumblr, and I repost my reply here:

It’s revealing that thinkers very close to the event, thinkers in fact to whom the event had happened, didn’t draw the distinction. That for Arendt the whole socialist-fascist phenomenon, along with the preceding imperialism, fell under the rubric of “totalitarianism,” a scheme of systematized and systematically dehumanized life implied by modern materialism. That for Camus it all, fascism and socialism both, grew logically out of the modern revolutionary’s world-remaking ambition to replace the God he had killed. That for Adorno the whole apparatus, including the postwar “liberal” settlement, was a drive toward the total administration of life implied by the first stirrings of Enlightenment in man’s domination of nature. These are extreme views, thus their relegation to realms of “theory” and belles lettres rather than history. And their extremity is a problem: they leave no image of authority intact, no name for sovereignty that is not “Hitler.” When Bernie says a country needs a border, which is common sense, they call him a “national socialist.” Adorno argues in Minima Moralia that totalitarianism inheres in the structure of time itself, since some one must precede some other; thus one stands in the place the other might have occupied, an eliminationist logic whose rails run to Auschwitz. You are the Hitler of whatever patch of ground you occupy, no matter how small. The maximal claim therefore demands one commit suicide so as not to commit the homicide that is one’s existence. Like many maximal claims, then, it’s probably true, but we can’t live that way. Mussolini and Hitler were not conservatives; they were revolutionaries, inspired by Hegel, Darwin, and Nietzsche. This is why advanced artists, inspired by the same revolutionary ideas, not to mention the vision of the state as work of art, were attracted to them. And when the revolutionaries of the rival party like Lenin and Trotsky spoke of creating a “new man,” neither the concept nor the means would prove so different from what Mussolini and Hitler had in mind; even Stalin and then the Third Worldists after him turned on “the Jews,” proving that this particular ideologeme cannot be assigned to the fascists alone. In the end, then, I accept a version of the “totalitarian” thesis. (The intellectual left reneged on this first, with, for example, Žižek’s book at the opening of this century with its attack on Arendt, and all the rest of their weary ostalgie, so we can assign the intellectual left blame for the recent resurrection of Hitler, if only because they should have known what would accompany their resurrection of Stalin.) And I am as worried as anyone about the totalitarian features of the emerging technocracy, without quite knowing what could be done about it that wouldn’t also risk slipping into the equal and opposite totalization, politics being a realm where opposites become each other almost more than any other. What the totalitarians didn’t understand, in the end, is that all truth is written first in the margin of error.
