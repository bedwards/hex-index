In Fear of K: Approaching the Apocalypse

This year’s Nobel Prize in Literature went to László Krasznahorkai, “the contemporary Hungarian master of apocalypse,” as La Sontag once had it.5 “Total brodernist cultural victory!” proclaimed one social-media post I saw. The same bookstore discussed in my “brodernism” post from a few weeks ago advertised on their Instagram that they had veritable stacks and bales of Krasz for sale, so I went and bought myself a copy of The Melancholy of Resistance, since it seems to be the average Anglophone’s entrée into this apocalyptic6 corpus. (I told the bookstore owner they were sold out on Amazon, so I wouldn’t blame him if he marked up the price, but he graciously declined. A believer in the petite bourgeoisie as a utopian universal class, I support small businesses.) I have now read the first 60 pages of this novel. Unlike Sam Kriss, I will probably go on; unlike Sam Kriss, I’m afraid it’s supposed to be funny. The first reply to Sam Kriss reads, and this is an accurate description of both the text and what I take to be its intended effect:

The whole opening chapter from the perspective of Valuska’s snooty mother is funny—the way she perceives and thinks about the world is funny. When the guy is oggling her and she’s uncomfortable and her bra snaps for no reason and her boobs start bouncing it’s funny. […] His books are full of humor!

Well, there is taste in humor as in everything else. But 60 pages of mean-spirited sophomoric satire on two stupid women (a self-satisfied petite bourgeoise whom Krasz relishes exposing to social chaos and an aggressive commie-type utopian whom Krasz relishes revealing to be the dupe of her own will-to-power), coupled with what Dr. Leavis might call an “adjectival insistence” on how nightmarish and chaotic and anarchic and unreal and evil it all is, is not really my idea of a good time, I have to confess. It’s also not actually hard to read despite lacking paragraph breaks and conventional syntax. But this is aggravating, not mitigating, of the experimental technique. If you have not really challenged my middle-class sense-making but just thrown a merely visual impediment across my eyes, then what’s the point? Just put in a paragraph break before we all go blind!7 Such prose is hard to quote for reviewing purposes, which might forestall a certain kind of critical objection; if they can’t find where your sentences begin or end, then Compact magazine can’t say you have bad grammar! Speaking of grammar, though, I am not totally persuaded by the long-sentence technique either. Krasznahorkai, at least if George Szirtes’s translation is accurate, and I’m sure it is, usually does obey grammatical rules and doesn’t just call a heap of comma splices a “long sentence.” He also creates a sense of breathlessness and urgency by adding clause to clause to clause in a careering rush, even as the syntax also impedes the rush with its unconventionality: a bit of stop-and-start imitative form to evoke the frenzied stasis of his provincial apocalypse. That’s the case for the defense. The prosecution says that half the commas might just as well be periods, and if half the commas might just as well be periods, and if the clauses they punctuate, moreover, contain the usual free-indirect-discourse third-person narration, and they do, then we have the superficial mimicry of an experimental technique barely concealing a conventional, in this case satirical, grammatical and fictional design.8 It’s as if a Jackson Pollock were revealed to be a Magic Eye picture, the spatters resolving themselves into a 3D dolphin when you relax your retinas.9 More apocalyptic would be the clearer description of apocalyptic things and events rather than this vaguely summoned fog of mood. That’s too American, too Anglo, too “concrete,” I’m sure, insufficiently “world literature.” (We love to read in translation because we haven’t read in our own literary tradition and are therefore accustomed to reading words as if they had no historical connotation, no history, no trace of prior usage. Everything we read is vague; everything we read is in translation as far as we’re concerned.) But all these objections aside, I know there’s a whale, if not a dolphin, coming, so I will travel hopefully. (I did see the movie; the movie, which I found church-giggle-inducingly self-serious, like a Scary-Movie-style parody of a European art film, decided me against ever reading Krasznahorkai back when his cult was just getting started in academe in the mid-2000s.) I hope for nothing more than to come back next Sunday to tell you that the next 240 pages of the novel exceeded my every expectation, “changed me as a (so-called) human (so-called) being,” and generally altered my conception of what fiction can do. This, however, is my report for now from the world-literary front, as honest as I can make it. Tell me why I’m wrong in the comments, please!

Some men were born 150 years ago on October 12th, and some books began 40 years ago on October 12th, but Maya Deren died 64 years ago on October 13th, Friday the 13th, to be precise. This week a reader asked me to elaborate on my admiration for her great film, Meshes of the Afternoon, at my super-secret Tumblr. I repost the answer here:

Just as an experience, I enjoy everything about it: the house in Los Angeles, the quality of the afternoon L.A. light, the whole sense of a drowsy siesta, the dreaminess. Plus the comic quality of the repetition: she has a sense of humor, of the ridiculous, about it that makes travesties of her style of experimental short film, whether in Ghost World or in The Ring, to name two end-of-the-century parodies, wholly redundant. Her use of her face and body, the dance of it, as she trained in dance, the poetic association of images, as she wrote her master’s thesis on modernist verse, and her weird uncanny beauty that we can’t look away from. And then, on top of that, it’s about cinema, an early key to what cinema in practice is, cinema as dream-space, as the place you encounter all the heightened versions of yourself you might, as visions of oneself projected in extremis, of the screen as mirror—until you break the mirror and escape identification, scattering the shards of ego on the beach that leads back to the sea as point of origin (cf. her later short, At Land, plus her self-given name as veil of illusion). Finally, a poetics of gender: the phallic desire for mastery (the key) become phallic instrument of murder (the knife), though each also has its feminine use (entry to and tool of the domestic), as she thought the essence of femininity in art was at once patience and process (e.g., pregnancy) as against men’s immediate force and all that pertained to the strictly human (e.g., culture) as against immediate survival needs, as she explains in her documentary on Voudoun—hence finally her possession, perhaps unto her death one year older than I am now, by Maîtresse Erzulie. Unbelievable that there hasn’t been a great novel or film (beyond this very good documentary) based on Deren’s life.

First and last Americans: on the heels of last week’s free episode on Willa Cather’s O Pioneers!, a reader asked me to write a capsule review of Death Comes for the Archbishop on my super-secret Tumblr. I repost the result here:

Cather recalled in On Writing that her formal goal for DCFTA was to elevate almost mythic storytelling over the scene-setting and dramatization that had become prevalent in the novel:

I had all my life wanted to do something in the style of legend, which is absolutely the reverse of dramatic treatment. Since I first saw the Puvis de Chavannes frescoes of the life of Saint Geneviève, I have wished I could try something a little like that in prose; something without accent, with none of the artificial elements of composition.

This artistic interest then intersected with her belief, derived from traveling the Southwest, that “the story of the Catholic Church in that country was the most interesting of all stories.” It was an alternate founding for the United States based on a doubled set of vanished values—those of the pueblo Indians, those of the Church—more honorable, if not more durable, than those of Enlightenment and economic power. All the better for not being durable, given the dominant strain of elegy in Cather’s fiction. Like any modernist working the mythic method, though in her case with Yeatsian gravity rather than Joycean irony, and without the sensuality and fascist ranting of Lawrence’s similarly inspired Plumed Serpent, she produces a new kind of prose epic, as signaled by the novel’s early reference to Father Ferrand as

a man of wide wanderings and notable achievement in the New World, an Odysseus of the Church.

As in her Midwestern fiction, so here: she hails the founders of a new order even though she will not be able to hail the order, unable because it is an order, and therefore deadening to the pioneer spirit she admires. I love the juxtaposition she creates between the beautiful Old World manners of Father Latour and the raw sublime of the landscape; I love the sense of fragile community she evokes between all who share their lives in that part of the world, especially the relation between the two priests, the one scholarly and mental, the other vital and physical; I love the fragmentary form with its many interpolated tales, allowing her to create a sense of epic vastness in so brief a book. The always pungent sexual symbolism, which I’m never quite sure is under her control, often symbolized by the serpent, here most vividly in the evil phallic Buck Scales, with his snake-like head, the terror of male sexuality and sensuality she conveys, also evoked in the legend of the Pecos’s great snake, and the sense of buried passion overall, nowhere communicated with more eerie power than in the motif of “hidden water” culminating in the scene where Father Latour and Jacinto hide in the cave in the mountain, a kind of Indian church, and listen to the river far underground. Cather is on one hand a proto-multiculturalist, as in the last section’s celebration of the Navajo return—

After he was gone, the Bishop turned to Bernard; “My son, I have lived to see two great wrongs righted; I have seen the end of black slavery, and I have seen the Navajos restored to their own country.”

—or in the brief narrative of Manuel Chavez, where Cather compresses Blood Meridian into a few limpid pages; but she also allows herself to express something, too, of what Burroughs so famously said in Naked Lunch—

America is not a young land. It is old and dirty and evil before the settlers, before the Indians. The evil is there waiting

—which grants the novel the metaphysical terror of true epic: monsters ravening at all the edges of the map. To this she counterposes the beauties of all real civilization and, in the story of Father Latour’s kindness to the Southern Protestant family’s suffering servant, distills the essence of Catholicism as it is, at its best, practiced:

Kneeling beside the much enduring bondwoman, he experienced those holy mysteries as he had done in his young manhood. He seemed able to feel all it meant to her to know that there was a Kind Woman in Heaven, though there were such cruel ones on earth. Old people, who have felt blows and toil and known the world’s hard hand, need, even more than children do, a woman’s tenderness. Only a Woman, divine, could know all that a woman can suffer.

[…]

Happily Father Latour bethought him of a little silver medal, with a figure of the Virgin, he had in his pocket. He gave it to her, telling her that it had been blessed by the Holy Father himself. Now she would have a treasure to hide and guard, to adore while her watchers slept. Ah, he thought, for one who cannot read—or think—the Image, the physical form of Love!

The comical out-of-placeness of “Hungarian” in that otherwise sweeping phrase suggests Weltliteratur’s permanent problem of the particular and the universal. What are we really reading when we read a translation? Do we want other worlds or do we want mirrors? What separates a “Hungarian” apocalypse from any other kind? Paprika? (Incidentally, Stein uses “hungarian,” her lowercase, throughout the Autobiography as a metonym for all peripheral Europeans who came to her Parisian salon in quest of modernist centrality or world culture.) Does Krasznahorkai tell me anything I didn’t already learn from Kleist or Kafka, to cite two authors in the brodernist genealogy treated at length in The Invisible College at those links? Brodernism, the revenge of the mutant K-Men on a world that has rejected them, begins with Kleist’s challenge to Goethe’s classicism. The problem with this, as readers of Faust Part Two will recall, is that Goethe’s classicism already contains Kleist’s challenge, and much more besides, so there’s no need to be so melodramatic about our bad moods, cf. my censure in footnote 2 above of Nick Land’s “histrionic rhetorical cruelty.”

The political situation in America is hilariously, unnervingly unstable. Something’s coming, but what? The ongoing division of our political culture into two “we’re going to fucking kill you” factions, of course, with Trump bearing down militarily on the cities and snatching people into vans, while center- and far-left promise together that they’ll murder voters’ children just as soon as they’re finished torturing their own pets and haranguing their own personnel. But on the other hand, the bipartisan establishment, which spent the last 10 years trying various means to take Trump out, is also desperately trying to embrace and therefore control him for whatever global instability they sense or know is upcoming. (Are they like animals before an earthquake or like actors reading from a script? We’ll never know, thus the prevalence of conspiracy theory. All mundane astrologers agree, in any case: world war in 2026. This will be a good test of their vatic capacity. If we’re all still here in 2027, maybe I’ll give rationalism another look.) To wit, Bari Weiss put Hillary Clinton on TV to praise Trump, a video that received fewer views on YouTube than the pirated upload of Nick Fuentes on Red Scare, itself a kind of neo-neocon struggle session to break the youthful broadcaster of his Third-Worldism. Even the Swedish Academy tried to make an overture to the president by giving the Peace Prize to a female Venezuelan Ahmed Chalabi, who’d praised Trump as a “visionary,” thus effectively ratifying from Stockholm the administration’s ever more bellicose regime-change policy. (Some “peace” prize!) Our new Venezuelan laureate understood the meaning of the gesture, dedicating the Prize to Trump, but Trump, all political genius and no political talent, didn’t quite get it at first. They should have given him the Literature Prize, anyway. No president, of this or any other country, should ever get peace prizes; to be president is necessarily to be the avatar of war. We can’t deny, however, that, like Gertrude Stein before him, Trump has both coined innumerable unforgettable comic phrases and introduced a new rhythm into the language. They didn’t give Stein the Nobel either, though, and they’ve called her a fascist, too, so what can you do? (The Swedes prefer unpunctuated gospels of gloom written by the starved prophets of Europe’s periphery to rotund American divas’ impeccably timed one-liners.) Now if I can offer any comfort to the Libs of Substack during this fraught time, I’ll tell you that Bari doesn’t like Trump at all. Don’t ask me how I know. Let’s just say that, to quote Secret Squirrel, Pittsburgh is “the intellectual center of the world,” and you’re therefore never sure what indiscreet conversations you may overhear in its Habermasian coffee houses. (You’ll say I’m a creep, but she was talking very loudly right behind me. I was just trying to read!)

Hugh Kenner brought essentially the same charge against Faulkner. Still, when a correspondent asks me, “Faulkner or Hemingway, and why?” I don’t hesitate to reply Faulkner, which is why the above can’t really be my last work on Krasznahorkai, who might turn out to be Faulknerian in breadth. Faulkner tried everything, and some things worked better than others, but the overall vision that emerges, the tragedy, the characters, yes, the apocalypse, and the humanism that made the apocalypse worth fearing and mourning—all that is practically unequaled in modern literature. Is Faulkner the greatest American novelist? Well, it’s him or James, or Melville just for the one book. In that sense, he’s not even in the same league as Hemingway. I’m not a Hemingway basher, though. Hemingway wasn’t a major novelist, but he was a major stylist. He changed the language, as Faulkner did not, even if he got a lot of it from Stein. Like Stein, he should be classed with the poets. Like a poet, he has a lexicon, a set of images, an ongoing concern with the experiences of the self. Read him (and her) next to Pound, Stevens, Moore, Williams, not next to James or Faulkner, and their genius stands out more. Maybe this is too perverse, but I think Faulkner is a great novelist and Hemingway a great poet. For more see here and here.
