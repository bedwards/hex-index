Inventing the Human: On the Emergence of Humanity from Self-Consciousness

Recently, an anonymous inquirer wrote in to my super-secret Tumblr with a classic imponderable. I answered rather casually—if I’d saved the question for Substack, I’d have felt the need for more formality, whereas I keep the Tumblr as a book of scraps and rags—but it might interest a broader audience anyway, so I repost the whole exchange, unedited, below. (See also the more academic sequel about Claude Lévi-Strauss.4) Anon asked:

Have people ever been radically different, psychologically, or have we always had a fairly recognisable psychology just with different cultural overlays? (Did Shakespeare, Jesus, the Egyptians, or the first homo sapien invent the human?)

I replied: I don’t know. This is unknowable by definition since any answer premised on universalism is vulnerable to the reproach that it has mistaken the different for the similar or the indecipherable for the comprehensible. When we say Jesus or, more to the point, Shakespeare “invented the human,” we mean they helped to inspire or devise the very reflexivity of consciousness that makes us regard the question as unanswerable because it refocuses our attention not on those mysterious others out there whom we wish to know but on the media “in here” through which we know them, i.e., our own psychic faculties and our own language. Then we find that what is most our own is also somewhat opaque to ourselves; we have that within which passeth show. But will everybody “out there” prove as strange upon self-inquiry as we do? Are there degrees of consciousness? Are Fortinbras5 and Caliban made of the same stuff as Hamlet and Prospero?6 Should they be? Should this crippling self-consciousness colonize the earth? An anecdote: Edward Said, the great scourge of imperialism and Zionism, lamented that “we Arabs have no unconscious,” said that psychoanalysis (he was in it for much of his life) should be imported into the Arab world, that the spread of such “theory of mind” would break down the various cultural debilities he saw as rendering the Arabs so vulnerable to western conquest in the first place. Leaving aside the conscious irony of his wish to combat a Jewish particularism born in the atmosphere of German nationalism (Zionism) with a Jewish universalism inspired by the laureate of British imperialism (Freudianism), if we can leave that aside, we observe the greater irony that the only effective resistance to assimilation into a global imperial hegemony will require people to become more and more the same, i.e., reflexively aware enough to reflect critically on both self and other and their mutual interrelation and constitution in the conflict of cultures. If this theory is right, that the universal is a phenomenon we build out of the struggles of the particular rather than its having inertly existed before we began to think about it, just as God may wait for us at the end of time as our technological creation rather than having formed us in the beginning, then we will go on inventing the human more and more each day—the human, defined as that within which passeth show, transmitted now through the undersea fiberoptic cables that weave the globe more and more into a single cultural text in the air around us, where even the parochialisms and bigotries are shared across longitude and latitude, conveyed by the mirror in our hand through which we scrutinize ourselves for evidence of the heart of our mystery: Planet Hamlet.

The question of the physical bookstore arises again and again. One of my earliest substantial posts on this platform, when its center of ideological gravity was a lot more right-wing, was an attempt to articulate what’s so appalling, what is such a betrayal, about the strenuously progressive urban indie bookstore. The problem is not the ideology itself—urban spaces will by their nature be cosmopolitan, liberal, queer, etc.—but the boastful and drubbing overidentification with the most bad-taste and dubious versions of the ideology, ones which the very bookstore owners, if they’re like every other urban progressive I’ve ever spoken with privately, will even concede are stupid. There’s an “own the chuds” element, pointless since the chuds aren’t even there. A more nuanced approach to this question would discriminate among urban bookstores. The problem is really only the indie store that primarily stocks new books. The academic used and rare bookstore—the best type of bookstore—tends to be either free of this ideological debility or to mitigate it by adopting the more properly vanguardist and therefore paradoxically more aesthetically satisfying versions of progressivism (Mao rather than RBG looks down on you from the walls), and, in any case, the academic nature of the stock, encompassing every worldview in the repertoire of human thought, works against aesthetic or epistemic closure. And despite the complaints of conservatives, the corporate chains, or the only one remaining, aren’t so bad. This month, for example, Barnes and Noble returned for the first time in almost two decades to Pittsburgh’s city limits, with a new store in the controversially gentrifying East Liberty neighborhood. (The B&N, part of a new shopping complex, cf. Big Numbers and footnote 6 below, is just up from “AI Avenue” in Steel City’s continued bid to reformulate itself as San Francisco East.) I toured the new store and took special interest in its wares from an ideological perspective after I saw a viral post from a rightist congratulating himself for “going into the liberal bookstore and asking for Jünger, Mishima, and Spengler.” B&N, I noticed, had Jünger (Storm of Steel) and Mishima (more than half a shelf’s worth; I suppose the fujoshi buy them on the rare occasions when they wander, dazed, out of the manga section); they had no Spengler, but they did have Girard, as well as Heidegger, if he even counts (he obviously counts as far right, but he’s been so hyper-canonized and so fully appropriated and thereby neutralized by the left that he doesn’t count as controversial). For lowbrow American conservatism to go with whatever lowbrow woke stuff they also sell (White Fragility etc.), they had all Charlie Kirk’s books stocked with covers facing out, as well as MAGA kitsch books unabashedly on display (Letters to Trump, etc.). In fiction, they also had half-shelves full of Bellow and Roth, with Sabbath’s Theater of all things facing out, for those who fear our beloved midcentury misogynists have been terminated, though I concede Updike was represented only by Rabbit, Run. A lot of Gore Vidal, too, mostly the American historicals, which proves they are probably on Substack reading Begler and Barkan. Speaking of Vidal, to reward B&N’s investment in our city, I decided I’d buy something, so I got the Modern Library edition of Of Human Bondage with Vidal’s introduction, since Maugham is also back in a big way, as evidenced by what else but an essay in the new Literary Imagination. I don’t want to over-celebrate our corporate chain, but there might as well be a place on AI Avenue where an engineer of artificial souls can stroll on the lunch hour and get a crisp new copy of Pride and Prejudice or The Brothers Karamazov. (Dare I say Frankenstein?) No Major Arcana, though: a pretty serious lapse.

Default Friend, writing in propria persona, is in the New York Times, with what I hope she’ll forgive me if I call a Major Arcana-coded and also beautifully written argument about the internet as otherworld:

Adapting begins with seeing the internet for what it actually is — not a drug, nor a set of behaviors, but a place we travel to, with its own geography and customs. It’s not a physical place, but it’s no less real. Anyone who came of age online knows the feeling of crossing that threshold: When you log on, time runs differently, the body slips away, and, as one early inhabitant put it, “the selves that don’t have bodies” step forward.

Our earliest language about the internet seemed to understand its nature best. The central question of cyberspace has always been one of navigation. How do we move through this world while remaining human? What do we bring back from our travels? What bargains do we strike unknowingly? And how do we step back into the world of bodies when part of us would rather remain online?

To survive an enchanted world, an otherworld like the internet, we need the right kinds of stories to help us move through uncertainty and cross thresholds without losing ourselves.

Her appearance in the New York Times might very well exemplify her point about what real-world goods can be obtained from the otherworld, provided we’re careful not to be bamboozled by fairy gold or goblin fruit. It was only a few years ago that Kat and I were going on each other’s podcasts and joking in a rueful “won’t ever happen” way about her taking her rightful place at the Times. What I used to call “the true mainstream in exile” is becoming more and more mainstream (thus uncool or “over,” but that’s the cycle of culture, which goes on whether one participates or just snidely snipes from the margin; a writer, artist, or thinker who’s in it for the whole ride will have to be cool and uncool and then cool again just as a person has to be born, has to die, and must try to be remembered fondly). Relatedly, the Barnes and Noble expansion—see footnote 1 above—is also downstream of internet trends. “Online,” it appears, can potentiate its own antibodies as the mimetic desire for certain kinds of physical experiences and objects. As with the idea of “manifestation” that Kat and I have both written about, we must make sure the astral and physical planes are kept in a tight cybernetic or Hegelian braid as we continuously transform the one into the other and vice versa.

Curiously, or synchronistically, the Goodreads version of my review of Stoppard’s Arcadia—see footnote 5 above—is headed with an epigraph from Alan Moore:

Find me a dead cloud
and a sharp piece of science
I want to see the skeleton
of weather
And let me map
all maps we have mistaken for the world
And learn by heart the timetable of dice
And in our clutching, self-invented steps see
An accidental grace
A choreography

As the aficionado will recall, this poem was “written” by the heroine of Moore’s unfinished masterpiece, Big Numbers. Big Numbers is about an American shopping mall colonizing Moore’s hometown of Northampton—cf. Barnes and Noble’s re-arrival on AI Avenue in urban Pittsburgh in footnote 1 above—investigated through the lens of chaos theory. That both are early-1990s artistic responses by major British artists to chaos theory connects the otherwise dissimilar Big Numbers and Arcadia, hence my epigraph. Big Numbers, undertaken in partial collaboration with Moore’s soon-to-ex-wife and their mutual lover on the eve of the divorce, was also the last book Moore planned before his mid-life-crisis conversion to magic. I am no longer the Moore aficionado I once was, having lost track of his sensibility sometime over the last two decades. Perhaps it was psychically necessary for me to prepare this ferocious kill-your-idols prosecution of Moore before I could write Major Arcana; I don’t know. I just know I’d rather read the rest of Big Numbers, which doesn’t exist, than the rest of Jerusalem, which does, and that Long London doesn’t, at the conceptual level, speak to my interests at the moment. But I should be more grateful than this, should just thank the man for almost a lifetime’s tutelage—as documented here, I read The Killing Joke when I was six years old—should heed Amis’s admonition when Hitchens insulted Bellow: “Don’t cheek your elders.” All these thoughts are occasioned, of course, by Alexander Sorondo’s spectacular Metropolitan Review profile-of-profiles—I have called Sorondo’s genre the “nonfiction novella”—on Alan Moore, sympathetic and skeptical in turn, a worthy tribute to our magus:

Nat Segnit, on assignment from the New Yorker (his editors wanted something about “an English eccentric”), visited Northampton to spend a day with Moore on the occasion of Jerusalem’s release; he was surprised, as his train rolled in, to find that Moore had come out to pick him up at the station — “like my dad if I was visiting from college.”

They’d met a couple weeks prior at the Odditorium, a series of cultural events in Brighton where Moore’d been interviewed onstage by John Higgs. “He was smoking a massive reefer,” Segnit recalls, “as he would tend to do.” So they’d already been acquainted by now and just got right into talking about the book, its themes, Moore’s ideas about death, the fourth dimension, eternalism. All while walking the streets of Northampton. Pausing now and then to examine the sites.

At one point, visiting a stretch of road where Moore and his brother had played as children, they happened upon excavators in the process of tearing it all up.

Moore, with his joint and his long gray beard and his serpent-headed walking stick, stood there watching it happen, another burn mark sizzling through the map of his past.

He began to cry.
