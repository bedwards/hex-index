Auld Lang Mine: 2025 in Review

While 2025 has obviously been the biggest year so far in my literary “career,”2 I will try to remain calm. I got a taste, as the poet said, of the glamorous life.

Reviewing the year in reverse, I notice that Ross Barkan’s new literary journal, The Metropolitan Review, was there at the beginning, in the middle, and here at the end. Yesterday, the Met released my new novelette, The Persephone Complex: A Letter. As I summarized the matter in my too-excitable Substack Note:

I’ve told myself over the years that I was finished writing short stories, that the form itself may even be finished; before Persephone, I hadn’t written a story since the 2010s. (See my thoughts on the form and my “collected stories” here.) When the Met’s fiction editor Django Ellenhorn solicited a story this summer, I didn’t know what to do. I couldn’t say no, though; I spent the last two decades having my submissions rejected, so I’m not going to turn down invitations! I pieced Persephone together, therefore, over the course of August, wondering what it meant and where it came from all the while. I submitted it, with its early episode of political assassination and shell-case messaging, the week before Charlie Kirk. Then I figured I’d been onto something. Regular readers and listeners will recognize in Persephone motifs from the Invisible College and themes from the New Romanticism discourse (see below). Finally, at the novelette’s conclusion, the very end, I played a possibly annoying Nabokovian trick, the kind I would hypocritically deride in another writer, such as Nabokov. Please don’t give it away in TMR’s comment section, but the first person to identify the trick in the comments section to this post will receive a year’s free subscription to The Invisible College or a free print copy of Major Arcana or some other gift commensurate with my esteem for your perceptiveness.

Last month witnessed my academic piece, “Romantic Truth: Imaginative Authority in the Literary Criticism of René Girard,”3 in the distinguished Johns Hopkins journal Literary Imagination, anent, of course, James Tussing’s (formerly Secret Squirrel) even more celebrated essay “The Mysteries of Love: On Alice Munro.” Both are free to read until the New Year. For more on my essay, you can listen to my podcast episode with editor Paul Franz and read Gnocchic Apocryphon’s summary and qualified defense of Girard.4

Then there was Romanticon, the new journal at once conceived and baptized by friend of the blog Mary Jane Eyre, an explicit attempt at what has been called “The New Romanticism” of this platform and adjacent ones. With my contribution, “I Come in Self-Annihilation,” I attempted two tasks: first, to remove discussions of New Romanticism from historicist pedantry about the old Romanticism, since the “New” term is transparently a ploy on our part to advance today’s culture, not to provide academic studies of yesterday’s; and second, to acknowledge critics’ qualms about the potentially dangerous metaphysic and politic of Romanticisms old and new, of Romanticism as reactionary-radical temperament. This was my least liked piece of the year, I confess. My concession of the danger was read as an endorsement of the danger, and my evasion of historicist pedantry was met with the expected riposte that, as I had obviously not consulted Éclair’s reading of Bürgermeister’s commentary on Astrostratus’s tractate, as I evinced the bad taste to believe that reading Blake or Keats like a normal person gave me any right to say anything about the subject, I was unfit to pronounce. And yet the dimmest manifestation coach on TikTok would remind me that titling the piece “I Come in Self-Annihilation” invited what else but my annihilation. Be careless what you wish for, as Bruce Wagner (see below) advises.

The banner event of the year came in the spring, however, with the publication (the day before Shakespeare’s birthday) of my new novel, Major Arcana, a big book about art, magic, love, and death that drove conversation and debate in venues from The Wall Street Journal to the The New Yorker to Compact to (yes!) The Metropolitan Review.5 I promote it every week—it is perhaps distractingly ubiquitous by now as it becomes something of a cult object—so I won’t insist too much here, except, to remind you that you can order the book in all formats (print, ebook, audio) here; you can also find it in print wherever books are sold online. You can buy it directly from Anne Trubek’s distinguished Belt Publishing, too—we receive more of a profit that way—or you might also suggest that your local library or independent bookstore acquire a copy. Please also leave a Goodreads, Amazon, or other rating and review. Thanks to all my readers! If you’re totally new here and want to know more, I will link to three podcast appearances on the book from this year: PILCROW, Eminent Americans, and Beyond the Zero. Finally, the only Weekly Reading post I’ll link today is my memoir of my extraordinary adventures traveling to New York in support of the novel at the height of the controversy its publication occasioned in legacy media.

Did I mention The Metropolitan Review? In April, just before my book came out, I was honored to contribute a centenary essay, “America the Beautiful,” on The Great Gatsby. That essay more than any other suggests what I mean literarily when I say, if I say, New Romanticism. I mean not only the recovery of beauty, but also the redefinition of beauty to mean nothing less than an orderly recreation and therefore an augmentation of the world’s intense complexity. And in February, the day after James Joyce’s birthday and three days before my own, I was pleased to be there at the journal’s debut with a comprehensive introductory essay, “Dream Warrior,” on the oeuvre of Bruce Wagner, a Romantic visionary among the contemporaries if ever there was one.

Speaking of Bruce, I began 2025 with a big essay for his great publisher, Arcade, “Literature and Magic: The Marriage of Word and Image,” an argument for the spiritual meaning and potential of the multi-medial text in this hybrid world of ours when even or especially the most screenified of us long for the page, the stage, the desert, the mountain, the forest, the ocean, and when picture and word, the image and its breaker, make love and war as never before.6

Finally, 2025 saw the successful second year of The Invisible College, my literature podcast for paid subscribers. We started in the winter with ancient Greek literature from Homer’s Odyssey through the great Athenian playwrights Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides, and Aristophanes to the birth of aesthetic and literary theory in Plato, Aristotle, and Longinus. Then we turned in spring to modern western world literature from Goethe to Borges, encompassing Kleist, Flaubert, Baudelaire, Dostoevsky, Tolstoy, Chekhov, Ibsen, Kafka, and Proust. A summer on Shakespeare and Milton took us radically to the root, if I may redund, of English-language literature and myth, while fall brought us to the modern American novel from James and Twain to Morrison and Pynchon, including Wharton, Cather, Toomer, Stein, Barnes, Ellison, Bellow, Nabokov, and DeLillo.

In any successful course of study, both teacher and student (i.e., podcaster and podlistener) should learn something. Personally, I learned in 2025 that I like French literature, both in itself (Flaubert, Baudelaire, Proust) and in its expat American apprentices (James, Stein, Barnes), more than I’d remembered, since I’ve developed over the years some prejudicial pseudo-conviction that (as Deleuze put it) French literature is “too human” compared to the varied alien intensities of the American, Irish, English, German, Russian. Not so! The French are aliens, too, I am happy to report, which may influence some 2026 choices.

The main and developing lesson of The Invisible College, however, centered in the Ulysses episodes of 2024 and the Greek tragedy, Shakespeare, and Toni Morrison episodes of 2025, is that what critics of aestheticism, whether Christian conservative or Marxist materialist, disparage as “the religion of art” is actually a real religion, a religion older than (though encompassing everything true in) Christianity and Marxism—and, I speculate, fitter for the future than either. The final episode drops on New Year’s Eve, the 2026 schedule arrives on New Year’s Day, and the first episode of the New Year, on an undisclosed topic, will debut on January 16. Tremendous thanks to everyone who has made this project possible. The adventure continues.
