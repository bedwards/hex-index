You Do Not Need to Leave: Against Experience, Against Knowledge

A persistent motif of Grand Hotel Abyss: trying to get the balance right when it comes to experience vs. intellect as the raw material for contemporary literature and the arts. (See here and here, for example. I have more and more new readers all the time, so why not repeat myself?) Two recent and prominent posts around Substack, which I will not link to, reminded me of this: the one a manifesto for a newly authentic writing of intense experience, and the other a critical note demanding rigorously historicized global scholarship of any critic positing canons of aesthetic taste.4 How much does the writer need to do? And how much does the writer need to know? I have attempted a perhaps too-nuanced double polemic against the cults of experience and of intellect. (You will say, “That’s because you neither do nor know anything,” which is true, but here you are reading me anyway!)

A viral X post of last week said something like, “Some of you are not interesting enough to write personal essays on Substack.” This is wrong, but it would become right with the addition of a single word: “Some of you are not interesting enough writers to write personal essays on Substack.” If the interest of one’s personal or indeed political life determined literary quality, then Napoleon would have been a better novelist than Stendhal. Abraham Lincoln, who steered the ship of state through civil war, would have written better poetry than Emily Dickinson, who barely crossed the threshold of her house. Zohran would write his own book instead of leaving the task to Ross. Here in what remains for now the “universal homogenous state” people increasingly have similar types of experiences, obviating literature’s reportorial function of publicizing new ways of life. The self-hatred of this slightly immiserated universal middle class, generally the class filling out the ranks of the writers, generates a demand like the one I saw this week for a new literature of raw experience, of blood and grit, of a phoneless scrabble in the dirt, words spat in the gaps of bloody teeth, written up in prose of violent and unstudied terseness. People forget Hemingway was first and foremost a stylist, something of a dandy, better read than Faulkner or Fitzgerald, the macho thing his own version of the Wildean or Nietzschean art of life. He wouldn’t have been America’s greatest 20th-century writer—I like this argument!—if he’d just suffered and scrawled; he almost certainly would have been a better writer if he’d sought out less suffering.

My reproof also holds, however, for the scholars. Mere bookishness isn’t the answer either. While one need not necessarily take things to a Yeatsian extreme of hostility, bearing in mind also that history furnished Yeats a unique opportunity for public action, which we now largely reprehend him for anyway—

The Scholars

Bald heads forgetful of their sins,
Old, learned, respectable bald heads
Edit and annotate the lines
That young men, tossing on their beds,
Rhymed out in love’s despair
To flatter beauty’s ignorant ear.

All shuffle there; all cough in ink;
All wear the carpet with their shoes;
All think what other people think;
All know the man their neighbour knows.
Lord, what would they say
Did their Catullus walk that way?

—I reject a demand that the “cultural construction” of our own canons of taste require us to reconstruct every canon of taste the globe has ever witnessed before we dare to speak of anything at all.5 Taste, or aesthetic standards, emerge immanently from an ongoing dialectic with an open tradition generally considered one’s own.6 A timely shot of sheer exoticism can play a role here, but, as the world becomes more and more one, this possibility wanes anyway, and has always been something of a fraught gesture even when most successful (e.g., Pound’s reception of classical Chinese poetry). An account of how we got here and can get there could plausibly include absolutely everything, but still, you need a pressing reason to tell people to read an untranslatable ancient Sanskrit text offering worship instructions even as they’re arguing about “cultural feminization” without even having read Pride and Prejudice.7 (Please note, as in the broader “literature in translation” question, that I am not arguing against cosmopolitanism; I am arguing against a fetishization of superficial “difference” for its own sake, inspired by the same middle-class self-hatred discussed above. The art of scholarship is keeping the archive accessible and in order; the art of criticism, and of a critical art, is explaining the pertinence of this or that object found on a random stroll in the archive.) Even a working critic, to say nothing of an imaginative artist, is not the same as a scholar, and works best in community with artists themselves against the background of a common problematic, as Celine Nguyen argues in a recent essay refusing the romance of despair:

A collective investment in creating art helps create a lively, varied artistic ecosystem. And criticism, with its public and social qualities, can help that community construct a shared understanding of artistic excellence.

And as for these artists’ and critics’ combinations with the world, I think of what Kafka famously wrote:

You do not need to leave your room. Remain sitting at your table and listen. Do not even listen, simply wait, be quiet, still and solitary. The world will freely offer itself to you to be unmasked, it has no choice, it will roll in ecstasy at your feet.

(Kafka, whose art might have encapsulated the early-20th-century condition best: what did he do or know? A lot less than many lesser artists.) We might leave our room anyway, but we can be good writers, good enough even to write essays on Substack, without leaving either for a cage match in the dirt or for the dim basement of the university library.

Speaking of modernism, Paul Franz writes an essay on D. H. Lawrence’s Birds, Beasts and Flowers for Romanticon. I love that book. Lawrence holds the record for the longest time between when I started reading a writer and when that writer became one of my favorites. I began reading Lawrence in my late teens and didn’t truly realize how great he was until my early 40s. Birds, Beasts, and Flowers, read in my early 30s, was the book that told me I had dismissed him prematurely as some kind of slovenly ranter too offensive by contemporary standards to bother with. I had to hear that voice as pure poetry before I could understand it as a vehicle for high fiction. Reading The Rainbow and especially Women in Love liberated me into the style I was able to use for Major Arcana, in its way a very Lawrentian novel, though I’m sure I’m too “intellectual” and fussy and mannered for our blood-conscious vitalist, who died when he was only a year older than I am now. Paul writes about Lawrence’s use of an imagined censor as interlocutor allowing him to protect his wounded sincerity behind boasts and vaunts—I very consciously deploy this exact technique in these Weekly Readings—and quotes Lawrence thusly:

You tell me I am wrong.
Who are you, who is anybody to tell me I am wrong?
I am not wrong.

It’s funny, because I’m not wrong either! On the question of taste, Sam Kahn writes a welcome argument against the extraordinarily philistine mentality of the literary agent today:

The first page is of inexhaustible importance in convincing a reader to buy a book, and that means that the first page — really the first sentence — has to have the hook, has to keep readers reading. “This is key to reader satisfaction!” Fenton tells us. What falls by the wayside, needless to say, is a great deal — atmosphere, tone, build-up, subtlety, individuality — but no matter. Why is any of that of importance if you can’t move copies of your manuscript? And then it would be very hard to come up with any work of classic literature that fits Fenton’s test. Does Madame Bovary tell you what it’s all about in the first lines? Does Brothers Karamazov hook you right in with that multi-generational history of the Karamazov family? Does Moby Dick have hardly a wasted word?

Obviously if ironically, these formulaic standards can’t even produce competent entertainment, let alone great art. Narrative suspense, like personal happiness, can’t be directly aimed at or engineered, and no reader really wants to be hurried by empty no-word-wasted sentences through yet another iteration of one of the tiny handful of stories in existence. Readers want to be immersed in the highly individual world of a supreme fiction, and I’m sorry to say you have to waste a few words, to risk the seemingly extraneous, to create any sense of that world’s vastness and reality. The fictional world, like the real one it mimics and rivals, has to include a bit of chaos, some of the unexplained, streets visible that we will never walk down, languages we will never speak, events we will never understand. (That’s why it isn’t a matter of “world-building.” This is world-discovery, for writer and reader.) It’s tempting to blame capitalism for the situation Kahn describes, but this isn’t good capitalism either. They’re selling fewer books than they used to, even as they tool them more and more precisely for sale. Capitalism always claimed to allow for the contingent and the individual, as its planned-economy enemies did not, but what we are dealing with, as Mark Fisher used to say, is really “market Stalinism,” totalitarian regimentation under conditions of consolidation in the name of the market. Chance favors the prepared mind, to be sure, but much success, in life as in literature, is a matter of the happy accident.

This is admittedly the controversial Joycean and Ellisonian position—that what may count as one’s own tradition is almost infinitely elastic and that this syncretic Jewgreek-is-Greekjew worldview is compatible with the continued production of great epic art; that art is the alchemical wedding of the particular and the universal—as against various narrower essentialist, particularist, nationalist, activist, etc. views of the matter. For the “all art is political” crew, this position is a premature leap over or under the hard duties imposed by immediate exigency, which is why the Soviets called Joyce’s work “[a] heap of dung, crawling with worms, photographed by a cinema apparatus through a microscope” and why the Black Power activists called Ellison an “Uncle Tom.” Joyce and Ellison, however, understood the correct role of art, and their gemlike flames will therefore continue to burn in human memory, while the idolaters of history, whether worshipers of the evanescent fire or of the brittle stone alone and uncombined, will—ironically—be forgotten as early as tomorrow.

I am reminded of a prescient passage from Bellow, not from Herzog in this case but from Seize the Day:

Every other man spoke a language entirely his own, which he had figured out by private thinking; he had his own ideas and peculiar ways. If you wanted to talk about a glass of water, you had to start back with God creating the heavens and earth; the apple; Abraham; Moses and Jesus; Rome; the Middle Ages; gunpowder; the Revolution; back to Newton; up to Einstein; then war and Lenin and Hitler. After reviewing this and getting it all straight again you could proceed to talk about a glass of water. “I’m fainting, please get me a little water.” You were lucky even then to make yourself understood. And this happened over and over and over with everyone you met. You had to translate and translate, explain and explain, back and forth, and it was the punishment of hell itself not to understand or be understood.
