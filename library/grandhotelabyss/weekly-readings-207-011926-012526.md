### Out of the Maelstrom: How Joyce Avoided Politics

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uzy2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1238679f-8eb9-44d4-a2ef-b19e38ef31d0_1680x2247.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uzy2!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1238679f-8eb9-44d4-a2ef-b19e38ef31d0_1680x2247.jpeg)
*[Vladimir Tatlin and a model of his](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatlin%27s_Tower) *[Monument to the Third International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatlin%27s_Tower)* [(1920)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatlin%27s_Tower)*

An anonymous reader inquires:

> How did Joyce—seemingly alone among the major modernists, and to the point where the others point-blank could not process it—avoid getting sucked to any degree into the feverish-murderous political whirlpool of the early 20th century?[3](#footnote-3)

As one’s greatest virtue and one’s greatest flaw are often two faces of the same quality, so what many have called Joyce’s selfishness and arrogance became an almost self*less* disinterest in ideological warfare, while his ingenious ability to master, manipulate, and parody all intellectual systems prevented him from taking any of them too seriously. His was just too fundamentally playful a temperament to become captured by an ideological system, or to regard any secular ideological system as a religion to which he might convert. His might also have been too cynical a temperament to put any faith in political leaders. He saw almost before anyone else, before anti-, post-, and de-colonialism even existed as such, that these could be forces as oppressive as empire. That all makes him sound too negative or nihilistic, though. The positive side is that he remained faithful to the enlightened non-violent liberalism of Parnell as he understood it, which he transformed into an aesthetic when it was no longer viable as a politics. We might also apply James C. Scott’s [thesis](http://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/james-c-scott-two-cheers-for-anarchism#toc25) that the petite bourgeoisie[4](#footnote-4) is inherently anarchic more than it is (in the Marxist slur) inherently fascistic, and that Joyce spoke for the universal values of that class of his birth (see [here](http://grandhotelabyss.tumblr.com/post/685904501058748416/the-version-of-this-magnificent-awful-essay)). This may displease my further-left and further-right readers, and not to indulge in the always too-simple horseshoe theory, but I note that the other high modernists who came out of the interwar period morally unscathed by our standards, more or less, which includes Proust, Woolf, and Faulkner, had philosophical or artistic roots in 18th- and 19th-century liberal traditions. Mann is an interesting example here. By the time of the Great War, he was rigorously drifting toward a position like Heidegger’s or (in a different context) Pound’s, and I even understand why, his brother’s Voltairean activist temperament (not unlike Stanislaus Joyce’s!) being somewhat shallow and threatening to art; but he arrested this drift, spiritualized the Nietzschean assault on the bourgeois ethic into an artistic irony able to hold extremes in a sustaining tension, and arrived essentially at Joyce’s position. A certain *liberal imperialism* may itself arise from the Joyce-Proust-Mann version of modernism, an idea I struggled with [here](https://johnpistelli.com/2019/06/16/richard-ellmann-james-joyce/) in Joyce’s case, but then—and no, the CIA did not pay me to say this, though they should have—a certain liberal imperialism may still be preferable, since the imperfect is our paradise, to fascism or communism.

I consciously decided to test the strength of what monoculture remained in 2025 by watching almost all the year’s big movies, and I was fascinated to see this week’s yielding of the apparent Hollywood consensus represented by *Sinners*’s record 16 Oscar nominations. I missed this movie in theaters as I was pretty busy last spring, but I finally watched it yesterday and was duly impressed. *Sinners* is, for one thing, and this is what people are responding so enthusiastically to, a high achievement at the intersection of pop and art, thus Coogler’s citation of directors like Coppola and Nolan among his influences. They don’t seem to make such movies as much anymore in the post-monoculture age. *Marty Supreme* attempts a similar feat—and perhaps it’s no coincidence that both films tip their respective hats to the post-monoculture by rooting themselves in specific pre-monoculture mid-20th-century American realities (Jewish New York in the ’50s and Jim Crow Mississippi in the ’30s)—but *Sinners* is more coherently and consistently entertaining, despite its one brief lapse into mindless action-horror movie violence. The reddening palette and more and more rapid cross-cutting as the climax approaches, the use of music as emotional pacing and temporal crossing, the well-paced deployment at intervals of sensuality and brutality, the performances balanced between allegorical archetype and realistic personality—I don’t see how anyone could say it fails to work as a piece of artistry. (As with *Marty Supreme*, though, don’t go into this period piece expecting period dialogue. Isn’t there anyone with a historical “ear”—perhaps someone who reads old books—that Hollywood screenwriters could show their scripts to?) Like the year’s other big movies, e.g., *Hamnet* with Shakespeare and *One Battle After Another* with Pynchon and *Sentimental Value* with Chekhov and Ibsen, this one flatters a certain “cultured” viewer; while Coogler cites only Stephen King among the film’s specifically *literary* influences, one could experience *Sinners* as a crystallization into mass spectacle of all the great themes of 150 years of African-American literature from Chesnutt’s conjure tales forward, and I tend to think, thematically, it’s best read through the lens not of Stephen King but of Ishmael Reed’s *[Mumbo Jumbo](https://johnpistelli.com/2018/05/07/ishmael-reed-mumbo-jumbo/)* and [“Neo-HooDoo Manifesto.”](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9bOXfKuWPDw) Like those radical texts, *Sinners* dismisses Christianity as a moralizing and oppressive imposition on the world’s peoples to rob them of art, here represented by the blues, which alone has the power to materialize individual and collective freedom on the earthly plane. (For a different approach to the same question, see friend-of-the-blog Gnocchic Apocryphon’s [summary](https://gnocchiccodices.substack.com/p/preliminary-notes-on-the-blues-aesthetic) of the Ellison-Murray position; here the blues serves as a bulwark rather than a battering ram for a traditional culture Ellison and Murray saw as capable of genuine democratization.) Vampirism, by contrast, stands allegorically in the film as art’s false friend, a forced rather than voluntary collectivism that menaces the music-making individual with its transpersonal mind and that cedes too much (i.e., the whole of daylight) to dominant culture rather than seeking to transform that culture from the inside out by making it dance. (*Sinners* is a vampire movie with the paranoid texture of a zombie movie; I leave the import of this fact to better scholars of the cultural politics of horror cinema than myself, but I ask you only to recall my fellow Pittsburgher George Romero’s epochal representation of the zombie as tradition’s leaden attack against racial equality.) Vampirism, we might say, is in its replacement of the individual-in-community with the hive-minded horde, the false promise of *politics*, a Reedian reading—consider Reed’s dismissal of Marx as just one more Mosaic lawman—that comes into focus only if we contrast *Sinners* with its Oscar front-runner rival, *One Battle After Another*, and its nostalgia for collective action. Anyway, very good movie. Best picture of the year? Why not?

Blake Smith ventures (as he [allows](https://blakeesmith.substack.com/p/the-silicon-valley-canon)) to techno-Syracuse to try to advise the Silicon Valley prince in [“The Education of the Broligarchy,”](https://colossus.com/article/education-broligarchy-silicon-valley-canon/) a sharp but not entirely antipathetic critique of the Valley’s unofficial literary and philosophical canon. This is worth doing, even worth doing *urgently*, because, as Blake implies in the essay, the tech oligarchy is the most potent political actor in 21st-century America whether anybody likes it or not, the last living heir to the country’s 19th-century frontiersman and 20th-century technocrat spirits, perhaps even (as an upstart class rising against an ossified old elite) a “revolutionary subject” in the same way the old bourgeoisie once was, and therefore in some need of guidance by the free-agent intellectual with access to knowledges and affects more subtle than those contained in the last century’s popular fictions or self-help philosophies. (That these intellectuals—me, for example—should be fairly remunerated for these services goes without saying.)

> The Valley’s canon may offer many useful mental resources for young men entering a competitive world in which they may win great power. But it offers far less orientation by which such men, as they grow older, can learn to occupy new roles beyond the anxious quests of adolescence. Those who have won Silicon Valley’s competitions for status often seem to revert, in the full glare of publicity, to teenage behaviors that would have embarrassed or ruined them in the early, upward phases of their careers. Individually and collectively, Silicon Valley’s elites are stepping into roles characteristic of middle-age: positions of stewardship, responsibility, and authority for which their lives, cultures, and canon have left them unprepared.
> 
> In other eras, elites in such a situation would seek the company of cultured specialists who could offer advice, along with the pleasures of encountering new perspectives that might enlarge and mature their own points of view. The nomad conquerors arriving from the steppe, or the Roman generals having annexed the East, surrounded themselves with literati, philosophers, priests, and courtesans out of a combination of respect, contempt, and curiosity. Much of ancient political wisdom concerns a proper balancing among the varying human types that fill a court, no one of which ought to monopolize a ruler’s attention. Or throughout American history, when the cultural segregation of the sexes did much the same work of ensuring a variety of human types, the wives and daughters and fruity sons of plutocrats created the museums, art collections, and social reform movements that complemented the tycoons’ one-sided achievements, softening their edges and palliating their harms. Our contemporary culture offers little in the way of such variety or complementarity of human types, and it must be said that Silicon Valley elites have exercised often risibly poor taste in selecting candidates to fill the roles that traditionally supplemented and advised raw power. If the Bay Area’s equivalents of a learned courtesan and philosopher-poet are Aella and Curtis Yarvin, then a nation that depends on Silicon Valley to do what politicians cannot is in serious trouble.

The purpose of reading the best books is not to adopt the values transmitted by or the information transmitted in any one of them but to develop a mind wide and flexible enough to range among alternatives, imagine possibilities, and come to properly complex conclusions. Because the little details of books (even my own) always slip my mind—I would have failed Nabokov’s class at Cornell—I am inordinately fond of Pound’s line that culture is what’s left when you forget everything you’ve read. Blake hopes for the tech class’s acculturation into the more disciplined thinking modeled by a more rigorous set of texts. Blake’s critique of a prematurely politicized tech class—a tech class that tried to act before it could think—rhymes, I believe, with this caution from Paul Franz’s [“Conjectures”](https://ashesandsparks.substack.com/p/conjectures-f33)—

> Self-government (i.e., democracy) in the literal sense implies the capability to restrain oneself. Activist-ism, in contrast, is anti-government: not in the deregulative sense, but in the sense that it recognizes no legitimate limitation of its own action; the only limit it recognizes is the force of the opposing party, which it seeks to push as far as it can. Mainstream progressive politics of the 2020s were “anti-government” in this sense: in that they externalized the sources of limitation of their projects. (And thereby disavowed them.) Hence the spuriousness of its “radicalism.” Activistism never sees itself as in charge; it always sees itself as making demands form those who are truly in charge. An authentic political movement must be self-limiting, self-defining, self-governing.

—though how it comports with the more delirious occult aesthetics commended popularly by *Sinners* and its literary precursors in footnote 1 above, which set themselves far more decisively against both tradition and any traditional sense of the political, remains to be seen. I myself am torn between these antinomies, which cut through every major period of art, thought, and politics.

I understood anon to be *lauding* Joyce for his abstention, but in the school where they sent me he was generally judged to be at fault for not allowing himself to be pulled into the maelstrom; the radical intelligentsia from the Soviet Writers’ Congress in Joyce’s own lifetime to the Marxist critics of today have tended to regard him as culpably aloof from the political realities his omni-parodic prose registers without protesting. Not for nothing is the head vampire in *Sinners* (see footnote 1 above) an Irishman pleading his own people’s case as a ground for internationalist cooperation; but Joyce, like Coogler, upholds individual-communal art against collectivist politics as the only true emancipation. See this overly long footnote [disquisition](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/weekly-readings-185-081825-082425#footnote-7-171774372), which culminates in a defense of Joyce’s apolitics, for more. The paradox, evoked [here](https://substack.com/@secretsquirrel2/note/c-204517679) by James Tussing, is that art can only be useful when allowed to be useless, can only be revolutionary if it’s not asked to be political at all, can only do its work if it’s permitted to do nothing—rather like the aristocratic class Blake Smith describes at the link in footnote 2, who need leisure to accomplish the labor of art, and just as the act of writing an essay or a novel will look (when observed) much like staring for hours out the window or into empty space.

Along with Madeline Cash’s *Lost Lambs*, reviewed [here](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/184838241/zoom-patrol-lost-lambs-and-the-empire-of-cuteness) last week, another book I plucked from the new shelf at the library was *Toni at Random*, a study by Dana A. Williams of Morrison’s longtime editorship in the 1970s and ’80s at Random House. The book has much to say, obliquely, about the questions incited by Madeline Cash’s controversial publicity blitz, or, to put it generally, about the relation of art to commerce. On the one hand, Morrison was always marching into her bosses’ offices on behalf of difficult writers like Leon Forrest or Toni Cade Bambara to remind them that Random House published *Ulysses* and had a legacy to protect of disseminating serious and challenging art even at a loss, that the leaders of an independent firm should regard it as their responsibility to use their resources to advance culture. On the other hand, Morrison was *also* always pleading with her serious and challenging authors to accommodate themselves to the reality of the paying customer’s understandably straitened resources and attention span. She had to insist to more than one recalcitrant *artiste* on the need to participate in publicity and all else that pertained to the marketing of a book. This frustrated, ironical letter to poet Barbara Chase-Riboud captures the conflict:

> I don’t understand what you are saying about holding a firm line between the work and the publicity. I hope you are right that people who like the work will “do things” for it without being asked—that would relieve us entirely of doing anything at all other than manufacturing it—but it is probably not a good idea for us to take that risk. We have to think of all sorts of anonymous people walking into a book store and wanting to buy the book for some reason—one reason I can give them is that they have heard or read about it....I must also try to get booksellers to put in \[*sic*\] on their shelves and they will do that for one of two reasons: Random \[House\] says so or they *too* have heard about it. So. What is that but publicity?...This is a commercial house historically unenchanted with 500 slim volumes of profound poetry that languish in stockrooms.

The balance between art and commerce has shifted some 40-50 year later, with major publishers seeming to be more exclusively devoted to the bottom line than in Morrison’s day but with unprecedented affordances for the entrepreneurial writer to self-market serious work, even to traditional publishers themselves. Some people, no matter the circumstances, will dream of a work that offers secret gnosis held back by initiates from the vulgar (“Not a book for normies, at all”), while others will always wish for the perfect alchemical wedding of the absolute to the multitude in a work that is simultaneously the best art and the most popular art, the best picture of the year.
