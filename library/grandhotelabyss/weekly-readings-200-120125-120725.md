### Passing Through Nature to Eternity: *Hamnet* and the End of Cinema

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lRaP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fae59c282-44be-480a-8cb9-a3ad8301e215_544x358.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lRaP!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fae59c282-44be-480a-8cb9-a3ad8301e215_544x358.png)
*Jean-Luc Godard, *Weekend* (1967)*

When the ghost cried, “List, list, O, list,” he wasn’t asking Hamlet for his top 10 films of the 21st century, but this exercise—which we all did back in [June](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/weekly-readings-177-062325-062925#footnote-5-167036076)[3](#footnote-3) but are apparently doing all over [again](https://www.worldofreel.com/blog/2025/12/2/tarantino)—undeniably has something of the mournful and the haunted about it. Given a bad year at the box office, critical dissatisfaction with even the most ambitious releases, and a Netflix-Warner deal that threatens to end the theater experience forever, talk of cinema’s death is (do I [dare](https://www.notebook.bdmcclay.com/p/please-dont-say-things-are-increasingly) say?) *increasing*.

Films are still being *ex*creased as we speak, however, and Chloé Zhao’s pseudo-Shakespearean tearjerker *Hamnet* makes a good candidate for the end of cinema, the final film, not on Mao-period Godardian[4](#footnote-4) grounds that art must give way to revolution but rather because its lachrymose finale, in which audience and actors merge in shared grief, recalls in an elegiac key cinema’s [fabled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%27Arriv%C3%A9e_d%27un_train_en_gare_de_La_Ciotat#Contemporary_reaction) birth in the *fin-de-siècle* audience’s panicked excitement at a train rushing toward the screen. We have long dreamed of breaking the proverbial fourth wall to live life as art, art as life, and the cinema screen has accordingly shattered like the godhead into those millions or billions of glass portals between art and life we now carry in our hands.

Cinema has been dying for a long time, though. Friend-of-the-blog Susan Sontag, for example, wrote its obituary in her essay, [“A Century of Cinema.”](https://southerncrossreview.org/43/sontag-cinema.htm) (She designated this century’s terminal date as her own moment of writing in 1995—cultural critics always think their era apocalyptic—and its inaugural date the apocryphal year the Lumière brothers screened the train film in 1895.) Our critic lamented three whole decades ago the inevitable end of the moviegoing experience as domestic and portable screens, continuous with the viewer’s distracting environment, replace the theater’s heterocosmic separation from everyday life, not to mention commercialism’s ongoing triumph over artistic experiment and integrity. More importantly, however, she attributes the death of the form to the waning of its devotees’ faith in its centrality—

> Perhaps it is not cinema that has ended but only cinephilia—the name of the very specific kind of love that cinema inspired. Each art breeds its fanatics. The love that cinema inspired, however, was special. It was born of the conviction that cinema was an art unlike any other: quintessentially modern; distinctively accessible; poetic and mysterious and erotic and moral—all at the same time. Cinema had apostles. (It was like religion.) Cinema was a crusade. For cinephiles, the movies encapsulated everything. Cinema was both the book of art and the book of life.

—as if the cinema were like an ever-emptier church dotted sparsely with ever-older parishioners. But this very fate has now befallen *all* the arts. With the leveling and dispersion of every artform onscreen, neither the old nor the new artforms can possess the all-importance once enjoyed in successive centuries by the play, the novel, or the film. Even the seemingly freshest modes go quickly *out* of mode in the feed’s or the scroll’s infinite relay of immediacy. Everyone knows the novel and the film and the album are dead, but any sullenly pre-empted child will tell you that it’s *also* now too late to become a YouTuber or a TikToker or a streamer, that podcasts have had their day, and as for that fossil known as Substack, well, even *The New Yorker* has [arrived](https://newyorker.substack.com/).

The reply to this advanced fatalism—I’ve made it myself—is that, *actually*, the internet has given a second life to all the once-obsolete forms by allowing their “fandoms” to gather and keep their flames alive. This is true, and I have benefited from it as a novelist myself, however belated the novel may seem. Yet the feed and the scroll, not the novel or the film, remains the central artwork of our time, the *Gesamtkunstwerk* enclosing us all, visible, as Boris Groys has [theorized](https://johnpistelli.com/2017/07/01/boris-groys-in-the-flow/), not to any earthly audience but only to the eye of God, with the novel and the film allowed minor niches (Goodreads, Letterbox) in its cathedral, like once-regal pagan deities requisitioned as divine functionaries for the empire’s new creed.

But I set out to say something about *Hamnet*. I am mystified by the near-unanimity of its reception, considering its many dubious features, not least its vapid portrait of Shakespeare and its risible manhandling of *Hamlet*. The film requires “Shakespeare” as a selling point, a brand name, but it portrays Shakespeare so generically that it could have been about any writer at all, the less original and inventive the better. That Shakespeare’s tragedy *Hamlet* has nothing in particular to do with the death of Shakespeare’s son Hamnet—no surprise, since the emotions expressed in fiction tend to be very remotely related to their biographical origin, as the flower bears little resemblance to the root—and that we can’t imagine *Hamnet*’s picturesquely tortured Shakespeare producing *Titus Andronicus* or *Troilus and Cressida* or sonnets for Platonic boys and venereal ladies: all this erects a high barrier to appreciation for this particular viewer, even allowing the commonplace that each age creates its own Bard.

At the end of 2025, we seem to want Shakespeare to stand for art as individual and collective therapy, art as a way to process and heal trauma. If Godard hadn’t died, *Hamnet* would have killed him; the late Tom Stoppard, whose own cinematic Shakespeare glittered with its author’s wit, wouldn’t have cared for it either. Now, as more the Romantic emotionalist than the avant-garde Godard or the neo-classical Stoppard ever were, I can accept Zhao’s idea in the absolute abstract. “Therapy” (and here I mean Freud in relation to Shakespeare) stole its central insights from art in the first place: that there’s relief to be gained from the ordered externalization of the emotions and knowledge to be gleaned from the emotions’ objectivization as discourse.

(In company with a long line of modern artists, however, I have [objected](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/107416737/never-again-psychology-why-writers-would-rather-x-than-go-to-therapy) to psychology’s forced recruitment of these insights to a project of scientistic over-simplification and expert-class professional aggrandizement. Poetry’s will to create new wholes and syntheses—*poiesis* = bringing forth[5](#footnote-5)—might be the opposite of diagnosis’s cutting abstraction—*dia-* = apart, *\-gnosis* = knowledge, thus “to know a thing apart from the whole”—and therapy’s original sin is Freud’s use of poetry to diagnose the poet, in a medical reprise of the contemporaneous juridicial procedure where Wilde’s prosecutors called his literature to witness against his erotic transgression.[6](#footnote-6) I was sorry to see, in the profile linked below, that Zhao herself has been seeking a “diagnosis,” a physician’s reductive label for the eccentricity of her personality, though her further remark on her eccentricity’s fitness for the age is apt. This desire to be *labeled* and so *reduced*—as if Freud’s diagnosis of Hamlet with the Oedipus Complex were not a culpably weak misreading of the play and the person it stages—is the true spiritual sickness of our time.[7](#footnote-7) Our labels will one day seem as limited as the Renaissance’s humoral theory, except that “melancholia,” for example, has more to recommend it as a trope, poetically speaking, than “depression”; the former allows us to picture an inner inundation of blackness, a workable *substance*, *materia poetica*, whereas the latter evokes only an agentless and external weight, the better to nominate the physician, and *not* the patient, as the only possible relief. Psychology is less art’s [“near enemy”](https://moonandmouth.substack.com/p/magick-a-near-enemy-of-art) than—to borrow a Shakespearean and Freudian figure—its murderously usurping offspring, at once regicide and parricide; whereas I am, let’s say, a loyalist or restorationist of the old regime.[8](#footnote-8))

So the vision of art at *Hamnet*’s heart—encoded in its affectively anachronistic line of rather mawkish dialogue, “Keep your heart open”—is a much degenerated copy of something genuinely present in Shakespeare, *vide* [“the invention of the human”](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/180181090/inventing-the-human-on-the-emergence-of-humanity-from-self-consciousness) where “the human” means the modern psyche, but must we *really* accept the reduction?[9](#footnote-9) Artists have all sorts of motives, high and low and in-between, God and money and power, but even the most piercing cry for love comes from desires more complex and ambivalent[10](#footnote-10) than a need to unrepress grief or confess regret.

Which isn’t to say there’s *nothing* in the film. If it had continued down the road it started on, if it kept being the story it started being and forgot its obligation to become an Oscar-season tearjerker, whether it advanced toward a Tarkovsky-type destination, all memory and phenomenology, or a Ken Russell one, an elemental and daemonic spectacle, I’m sure I would have liked it better. A woman who vibrates with the landscape, mistaken for a witch, an emissary of nature affined to the inner landscape of the artist as against the selfishly burgeoning burghers and their pious wives of the bourgeois civilization building its prison bars around witch and artist both: this is a promising premise, New Romanticism with a vengeance. (*Deviled Hamnet*, we might say. There’s not a pun, incidentally, in the whole movie; you might not like puns, but Shakespeare did.)

And if it’s also a feminist exoneration of Anne Hathaway from the old charge that she of the second-best bed was to poetry what Xanthippe was to philosophy, then why not? Not quite feminist, though. Zhao, as far as I can tell from [profiles](https://www.vanityfair.com/hollywood/story/chloe-zhao-dream-weaver), is less a feminist than a complementarian, a celebrant or elegist of the feminine rather than one advocating its exposure as myth and consequent egalitarian abolition:

> “Every decision Chloé makes as a filmmaker comes from her feelings and not her thoughts,” Spielberg tells me in an email. “Like Agnes in *Hamnet,*” a mystic at one with the woods of Stratford-upon-Avon, “I have always had a feeling that Chloé is a creation of the earth itself.”
> 
> \[…\]
> 
> Nurturing *Hamnet*’s cast and crew—facing what she’d long avoided— shifted her outlook. Behind the scenes, Zhao urged her players to “gather around the mother” for inspiration—not her, necessarily, but a higher feminine power.
> 
> \[…\]
> 
> Much has been made of Zhao’s “female gaze,” but she’s relying now on her woman’s intuition. Despite her male-dominated industry and our male-dominated political moment, Zhao senses a “feminine consciousness” bubbling up all around us, a new era of enlightenment full of compassion and heart. You know the shift is happening, she says, when the resistance rises to suppress it. Zhao speaks so confidently, I feel a rare ripple of hope: “Let’s be ready.”

Her exaltedly recessional art is almost enough to render *Hamnet* refreshing in a year whose capital-M major cinematic statements (*[Eddington](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/168992363/masks-of-power-for-and-against-eddington)*, *[One Battle After Another](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/weekly-readings-190-092225-092825#footnote-1-174682513)*, *[Bugonia](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/weekly-readings-195-102725-110225#footnote-5-177262782)*) seemed to me to cancel each other out. After all that overbearingly epic ironism of auteurs trying to match our moment absurdity for absurdity—the author of *Major Arcana* has a lot of nerve complaining about this!—I *was* happy to see someone just point a camera at a leaf.

Accepting Zhao’s complementarian premise for a moment, though it may be more aligned to our conservative era than she would wish to acknowledge, *Hamnet*’s cinematic double and opposite in cinema’s final year might be the underrated and under-performing *Die My Love*. Lynne Ramsay’s surreal psychoscape of postpartum breakdown might seem to refute Zhao’s plea for maternal *jouissance*, just as Jennifer Lawrence’s central performance offers much more menace than Jessie Buckley’s Oscar-frontrunning but slightly one-note turn as grieving mystic. But the films share the central conceit of woman in/as forest. (*Hamnet*, curiously, gives us Shakespeare’s own mother as something of a villain. That she shared her maiden name with the redemptive green world of *As You Like It*’s Forest of Arden is arguably even more relevant to the film’s concerns, it seems to me, than Hamnet’s similarity to Hamlet.) Both also offer an olive branch in the gender war, as Ramsay [insisted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Die_My_Love#Development) her film was a “bonkers, crazy love story,” thus not a stigmatization of the male for inciting the heroine’s spiral, while *Hamnet*’s rapprochement between husband and wife comes when she finally sympathizes with Shakespeare’s own authentic articulation of parental grief. She comprehends and accepts that the theater is for him what the forest is for her. And if we don’t want to wall off the female from the male filmmakers, we might triangulate *Hamnet* and *Die My Love* with Joachim Trier’s *Sentimental Value*, which likewise concludes in a theatrical-cinematic representation of a past tragedy that, we are to understand, forestalls a future one, as father and daughter reconcile in their shared art as they cannot do in life. (*Sentimental Value* also has a intertext in the western dramatic canon: *The Seagull*, Chekhov’s rewriting of *Hamlet*.)

I heard snickers as well as sniffles in the packed theater as *Hamnet* arrived at its conclusion. (The final music cue in all its thudding [obviousness](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rVN1B-tUpgs) did not help; Zhao should have played Rihanna *in the film*, as she did when the filming wrapped.) But did Joyce not write of “laughtears,” and is anything more natural than laughing and crying over the deceased’s foibles at a wake? If *Hamnet* is cinema’s wake, and theater’s before that, father and son laid in a common grave while women stand weeping and laughing, so much the better. The rest is—well, you know the rest.

DeLillo’s staccato and montage style, with all its repetitions and alliterations and elisions, may be the fullest expression in late-20th-century literature of his friend Gordon Lish’s ideas about [“consecution”](https://numerocinqmagazine.com/2013/02/04/the-consecution-of-gordon-lish-an-essay-on-form-and-influence-jason-lucarelli/) in fictional prose, which Lish derived from all that semiotic theory DeLillo claims not to have read, but is DeLillo’s style not also a translation of the Godardian jump-cut from the moving image to the printed word?

[7](#footnote-anchor-7)

Just as I was finishing this, a new *Romanticon* [essay](https://romanticon.substack.com/p/high-and-halcyon-region-of-magic) by Bethany Tabor flew into my inbox to solve the riddle of why Mabel Dodge Luhan keeps flitting in and out of so much of my Invisible College research—Lawrence, Cather, Stein, Barnes—and why our culture seems pulled more and more toward not *Hamnet*’s forest but Luhan’s desert. Whether forest or desert or digital world, however, the point is that to put your self at the disposal of a conscious network of which you’re a central node is its own form of Romantic genius:

> Born in 1879 to a wealthy family in Buffalo, New York, she is often referred to as *just* a socialite and given little credit for the shifts in the cultural gestalt she initiated. Her life is not composed of monumental achievements that can be succinctly listed out as one might do with an author or artist; rather, Mabel’s life story reveals a *process* of elevating the collective consciousness. With her penetrating cognizance of the world around her, the cadre of innovative artists and thinkers that she formed into a network, and her constant search for meaning to fill a void left by industrialized modernity, Mabel broke open a new reality and invited some of the most influential people of the twentieth century into it, leaving a profound and lasting effect on American culture.
