### To a Person: Perspective in Fiction

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8aQc!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3282f9dc-63f6-45b3-adbd-257ef08c8722_1027x1497.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8aQc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3282f9dc-63f6-45b3-adbd-257ef08c8722_1027x1497.jpeg)
*[Seated Maitreya (2nd century)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Buddhist_art)*

An anonymous inquirer recently wrote to my super-secret Tumblr to ask the following question:

> Best/most interesting novelistic uses of the first and third person?

I answered: Everyone has imitated but no one has outdone *Don Quixote*’s use of first *or* third person in its intricate metafictional metatextual labyrinth. After that, Austen deserves credit for her systematic creation of free indirect style, especially in *Emma*, a true revolution of the third person. Finally, the Faulkner of *As I Lay Dying* out-modernisted his fellow modernists[5](#footnote-5) with his first person as direct present-tense straight-from-the-psyche stream of consciousness, his narrators escaping from Cervantes’s parodies and Austen’s ironies to speak for themselves. That’s all high-canon, but I always come back to a [graphic novel](http://johnpistelli.com/2016/04/20/peter-milligan-and-duncan-fegredo-enigma/) I read in middle school, whose seemingly opinionated and idiosyncratic third-person narrator turns out, on the final page, to be a lizard the magical hero has cruelly endowed with consciousness and a knowledge of the story. I like when the narrator isn’t who you think it is (Morrison’s *Jazz*, e.g., narrated, as far as I can tell, by the book *qua* book). Who’s really narrating *Major Arcana*, for example?[6](#footnote-6)

[1](#footnote-anchor-1)

While you were all greeting *The New Yorker* and *The New York Review of Books* like the primates gathered around the monolith in *2001: A Space Odyssey* (no offense), I was noticing that JF Martel of *Weird Studies* podcast fame has arrived on Substack. I wrote about his excellent breakout book *Reclaiming Art in the Age of Artifice* [here](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/j-f-martel-reclaiming-art-in-the). It was published by a small New Age press 10 years ago, when I discovered it using my trusty method of taking a Situationist *dérive* through a large library (bookstores and neighborhoods studded with Little Free Libraries also work), but it’s since been republished by a Big 5 and can now be purchased off the shelf in Barnes and Noble thanks to the timely intervention of Donna Tartt. Martel’s first post on Substack is a Christmas [essay](https://impofthepossible.substack.com/p/later-on-well-conspire-as-we-dream\) not on *2001: A Space Odyssey* but rather on the psychosexual odyssey that is *Eyes Wide Shut*. I’ve long thought the parapolitical reading of this film in the Age of Epstein has crowded out a more persuasive interpretation: it’s a portrait of paranoid interiority, “an inside story,” a Decadent salute from the 20th century’s *fin de siècle* to the 19th’s. (*Eyes Wide Shut* was poorly received upon its sensational release in 1999—I went to see it on opening weekend the summer before my last year of high school in the company of my father; when we left the small suburban theater, he remarked, “I need Harold Bloom to explain that to me”—and the first big critical defense before [Tim Kreider](http://www.visual-memory.co.uk/amk/doc/0096.html) incited the broadly parapolitical interpretation came from [Lee Siegel](https://scrapsfromtheloft.com/movies/eyes-wide-shut-critics-failed-see-kubricks-last-film/) in *Harper’s* mounting the case for the inner life.) Martel, however, unites both readings, psychological and political, in a metaphysical frame that reinterprets the Christmas story itself, the film’s basis, as the first operation in an attempt to break humanity out of the Black Iron Prison, the latter figured here through the imposition that is the Biblically proscribed census:

> To leave it at that, however, would be to settle for a too-earthly interpretation of a film that attains to the cosmic by way of its cinematic rendering. Indeed, the above summary passes over what’s most striking about *Eyes Wide Shut*, namely its dreamlike construction. Everything unfolds as if in a dream; every scene bathes in the oneiric night-light we experience only while asleep, and which the traditional aesthetics of Christmas—coloured lights glowing in the dark—seem intent on reproducing. This is a film about the reality of dream and the dream of reality. It matters that Bill’s wanderings in the night-world are mirrored by Alice’s interior journey; that while he attends the orgy, she is dreaming of one. The unlikeliness of the coincidence cues us to the synchronistic logic that holds the film together. The conspiracy isn’t just social; it’s metaphysical, going all the way up and down.

Kubrick avoided physicians—presumably as census-takers of the individual’s body politic, cf. Ash del Greco’s remarks on the subject in *Major Arcana*—which should be borne in mind when considering the protagonist’s profession in *Eyes Wide Shut*. Despite the rumor of his assassination by an elite cabal like that in the film, this iatrophobic aversion is probably what hastened Kubrick’s death of natural causes, nor is this uncommon among the artistic set as poet and physician, like poet and priest, confront each other in fraternal enmity over which is the rightful heir of the poet-physician-priest shaman’s once integral legacy (“He fears the lancet of my art as I fear that of his”). And as for the psychologists, I thought I was hard on them, but Joyce Carol Oates [outdid](https://x.com/JoyceCarolOates/status/1997699047138726319) me; relatedly, Rachel Aviv, who once [tried](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2023/11/27/joyce-carol-oates-profile?utm_social-type=owned&utm_source=twitter&utm_brand=tny&utm_medium=social&mbid=social_twitter) to psychologize and pathologize Oates’s prodigious fiction-making, [revealed](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2025/12/15/oliver-sacks-put-himself-into-his-case-studies-what-was-the-cost) this week that at least one celebrated advocate for a science of the mind manufactured a number of fictions himself. (Has anything claimed by professionals anywhere in the vicinity of “mind science” ever turned out to be true?) I think of the 2020-2022 period, when the apologists of the expert class [informed](https://johnpistelli.com/2021/08/03/benjamin-bratton-the-revenge-of-the-real-politics-for-a-post-pandemic-world/) us of our “responsibility to be counted.” The consequent populist revolt against the expert class, while abstractly merited, poses its own problems, however, as spiraling ungrounded paranoia narrows on its usual target. This is not a political point in the mundane left-vs.-right way but a *meta*\-political point in JF’s cosmic sense. (New readers might consult my attempt at a brief [explanation](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/138983193/a-war-inside-society-why-im-not-a-marxist) of “Why I Am Not a Marxist” from a two years ago.) That “anti-Semitism is the socialism of fools,” for example, is finally a point against socialism. It implies that a pogrom-structure of instrumentalized blame and resentment becomes legitimate once you put the right target in place of the wrong one. As long as you’re slaughtering the kulaks and not the Jews, you’re fine. But that’s bad ethics *and* bad politics: no matter how many bodies we pile up, if we don’t alter both soul and structure, starting with our own, we will end up back where we started in The Revolution’s customary 360-degree turn from tyranny to tyranny. The materialist dialectic—to stop them from counting and killing us, we’ll count and kill them first, and this will somehow bring in utopia—was never going to work. Which is the point of Morrison’s *Paradise*, as it is also the point of Milton’s *Paradise Lost*, as it is also why—see footnote 2 below—the Romantics who came between Milton and Morrison reposed their Platonic faith in divine frenzy and eros as the only way to fulfill *truly* the (laudable) Enlightenment dream of universal liberation.

[2](#footnote-anchor-2)

Andrew Kuiper at *Romanticon* [hails](https://romanticon.substack.com/p/diotimas-wild-children) Diotima and provides, with more scholarly detail about the history of philosophy than a mere belletrist like myself could ever marshal, a reading of Romanticism as a Platonic fulfillment of Enlightenment:

> Many influential readers of Plato take the myths, or his use of lyric and mysteric language, as sops for the masses, the effeminate supports needed to prop up the souls who are incapable of manly dialectic. Even worse, the myths are sometimes read as helpful deceptions, making it easier to control the masses. Luckily, the Romantics did not read Plato with the perverse instincts of an unreconstructed Hobbesian. They read him as someone open to divinity through imagination and as someone who claimed that his philosophical quest was given him by the god of poetry and oracles, Apollo himself. It should be no surprise then, when we read in the *System-Fragment* that myths are helpful for the masses for the same reason that they are helpful for the philosopher; namely, the sensuous must be united with the conceptual. Imagination and intellect cannot be divorced. It is this conviction that produced the famous statement from three students in a *Lutheran theological seminary*: “Monotheism of the reason and the heart, polytheism of art and the imagination, that is what we need!”

The importance of the Eleusinian Mysteries to this story will resonate with matriculates in my Invisible College. I don’t think I connected the Mysteries to the *Symposium* in my Plato [episode](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/the-invisible-college-platos-ion)—my lack of Greek might have doomed me there—but I hope I made up for it in reporting, with help from Ted Hughes, that Shakespeare staged the Mysteries right in the middle of *[The Tempest](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/the-invisible-college-william-shakespeares-e5e)*. (Again, I liked *Hamnet*, but I would have liked it more had it taken notice of such things if it was going to give Shakespeare’s wife “second sight” and the like.) See also my own [essay](https://johnpistelli.com/2020/07/22/friedrich-holderlin-hyperion/) on Hölderlin’s beautiful Romantic novel *Hyperion*. Even before Plato, we recall that [Aeschylus](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/the-invisible-college-aeschyluss) was born in Eleusis, and that he received his theatrical commission from Dionysus in a dream. And even after Shakespeare and the Romantics, what is that *pietà* on the final page of Morrison’s *Paradise* if not a re-appearance of Persephone/Mary cradling her son-daughter Iacchus/Jesus? What if we can draw a straight line from Aeschylus through William Shakespeare to Toni Morrison? Why not, instead of a paranoid reading of the canon, which understands the cultural tradition to be our jailer in a prison of illusion, we favor a *pronoid* reading, one in which our artists, whether they knew it or not, wanted to liberate us this whole time?

RIP John Carey. Obviously, as a disciple of modernism, I had my differences with the author of *The Intellectuals and the Masses* (see [here](https://grandhotelabyss.tumblr.com/post/190029418047/the-intellectuals-and-the-masses-was-the-most) and [here](https://grandhotelabyss.tumblr.com/post/658496488716042241/john-carey-the-intellectuals-and-the-masses)). The late Oxford scholar represented a brand of English common-sense thinking hostile both to the Continental and to the American brands of cultural extremism (have you decided what to buy Jane Austen for her birthday yet, anon?), and perforce to homegrown exponents of such intensity like Woolf and Lawrence, yet he wasn’t at all wrong to note these extremisms’ social and political dangers despite their aesthetic appeal. I like this quotation from an [obituary](https://www.thetimes.com/uk/obituaries/article/john-carey-obituary-literary-critic-mxjvmfxml?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=AWEtsqd52I_Mgs_wqkAG9Yudae2xaLk3jr3OSjpPvSMhvP1xtwaOjBXy01Gv&gaa_ts=693ed3b2&gaa_sig=2LdDBSIvAAmAEXW3mmfNVYr20W5ZdsXTqGIEi9lHZmLjY3tUC-n_3Aq8dmD0cftNpUhX3cGuTzpUbUCMdTpaeg%3D%3D), which gives a sense of his bracing critical deportment:

> Carey was no respecter of reputations, even at Nobel prize level. Reviewing a biography of Yeats, he wrote: “Was he really all that intelligent? He was substandard at school. He never learned to spell … His gullibility was seemingly fathomless. Mysticism and magic occupied much of his waking and sleeping life. In his relations with Maud Gonne he comes across as a credulous simpleton.”

I am *also* fathomlessly gullible! Anyway, I want to recommend *in memoriam* Carey’s 2005 book *What Good Are the Arts?* There, after commonsensically dispatching claim after claim after claim for the “religion of art,” to which I remain (see footnote 2 above for its full significance) an adherent, Carey turns around and argues for the superiority of literature to the other arts because its ideological pluralism, critical use of language, and indistinctness of ultimate reference (is *Hamlet* about princes? Danes? sons? men? everybody?) can create a supple and flexible intellect in students and readers that will serve them well in the fractious 21st century. He also humanely commends the pursuit and study of art less for a generally emancipatory social end than for its granting the pleasures of self-directed achievement to all individuals:

> My more hopeful answer to the question ‘What good are the arts?’ comes at the end of Chapter 5 in the section on the arts in prison. There is evidence that active participation in artwork can engender redemptive self-respect in those who feel excluded from society. This may be the result of gaining admittance to an activity that enjoys social and cultural prestige. But it seems also to reflect the fact that standards of achievement in art are internal and self-judged, and allow for a sense of personal fulfillment that may be difficult to gain in standard academic subjects.

[6](#footnote-anchor-6)

Not exactly a rhetorical question. The most recent negative review of *Major Arcana* on Goodreads grasped its import, but the reviewer nonetheless found my implication too cute or self-satisfied and instructed me to go fuck myself. My desire for every novel I write to be my last, my consistent impulse to write a novel and then declare I only want to write plays or movies or comic books from now on, stems from my impatience with the point-of-view problem, the same problem that so exercised proto-modernists like Flaubert and Chekhov and James (and, for our purposes here, Austen), to which they developed the systematic solution of the consistently restricted third-person perspective and its accompanying free indirect discourse. But free indirect discourse, in granting drama to narrative by allowing characters their “voice” even in the third person, traps the characters in the ineffable web of smug irony generated by the author’s knowing “exposure” of their limited thoughts. There’s a reason I like Chekhov’s plays better than his stories. Giving the third-person narrator itself or themself or her-or-whatever-self a “voice,” as I did in *Major Arcana*, is one solution, but I doubt I should do it over and over again. I don’t actually prefer the visual or the spectacular; this is not what I mean when I say I want to write a play or a movie or a comic book; I just increasingly want the characters to speak for themselves so I don’t have to whisper their secret thoughts to you as if I were a spy. True literature’s requisite irony will emerge as of itself from their dramatic interaction without my laughing behind my hand in the audience’s general direction at the characters’ ignorance—the reliable if subliminal affective consequence of free indirect discourse. “Poor little dears, they just don’t know.” Yet singly first-person novels too often, even at their best, come off as strained stunt-like rhetorical performances (*Lolita*, etc.), and that is if they do not become dull autobiographical testimony. While we’re New Romanticizing, we should revive the genre of the Romantic visionary closet drama *à la* *Faust*, *Manfred*, or *Prometheus Unbound*. This is an under-explored ambition among the modernist novelists, as witness the Joyce of “Circe,” the Toomer of “Kabnis*,”* the Faulkner *As I Lay Dying*, the Woolf of *The Waves*. (Bruce Wagner’s “oral history” in *ROAR*, recently [praised](https://substack.com/@agoodhardstare/note/c-185568159) by Henry Begler, is the most striking contemporary example; others might include the Jeannette Winterson of *Art and Lies* and the George Saunders of *Lincoln in the Bardo*.) Back in October, Paul included this among his [“Conjectures”](https://ashesandsparks.substack.com/p/conjectures):

> Few cultural syntheses are more stunning than Gandharan Buddhist art: Greek modeling, combined with a contemplative inwardness utterly alien to Greek art. There is nothing really comparable, except perhaps—*La Gioconda*. Conjecture: three-dimensional, sculptural modeling can be adopted by seemingly any artistic tradition, whereas post-Albertine perspective can only mean destruction for the non-perspectival art that attempts to incorporate it. This suggests that there is, in fact, a deep asymmetry between positive and negative three-dimensional space—that they stem from entirely different principles. On the one hand, presence; on the other, a hegemonic nullity, leveling all things—and condemning as inept whatever imperfectly assimilates itself to that nihilistic leveling.

It came to me instantly: free indirect discourse is the literary equivalent of perspective’s nullifying, leveling realism of a mathematicized empty space—I am using “realism” as a synonym for “three-dimensional…modeling”—while the literary equivalent of a positive or “presencing” sculptural realism is *speech in the first person*.
