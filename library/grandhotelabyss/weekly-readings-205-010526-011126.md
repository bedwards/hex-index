### Higher Love: On Fiction and/as Feeling

[

**Buddenbrooks* Vintage International edition cover (1994), detail*

An anonymous inquirer recently asked:

> Do you think it is indicative of naivete or bad taste to primarily want to be moved when reading fiction? I feel like I almost always prefer works that touch me emotionally, and they usually fall within a pretty narrow range: “a surprising but eloquent affirmation of love”. I’m afraid that is incredibly corny. I also feel like this tendency blind me to other aspects of the work; even when a book is fundamentally unsentimental, its lack of sentimentality may add to the poignancy when I read it.

I replied: No, your priorities are in order, and I am the same. Fiction is not an intellectual exercise, not a joke. The development of taste in the west as constituted over the course of the 20th century requires the “student” to learn to arrive at the affirmation of love only after traversing a labyrinth of irony, irony either in the form of the medium’s becoming self-conscious or the content’s being treated from a distanced and de-familiarizing perspective, because the earnest affirmation of love had been debased by commerce and propaganda, by Hallmark and by Stalin. Yet many of the famously “difficult” 20th century works, the ones at the very apogee of the old model of modernist taste, whether *Ulysses* or *Lolita* or *Gravity’s Rainbow*, retain the affirmation of love as their final significance, and are fully as moving as anything permitted to be earnest in the prior century, e.g., Austen or Dickens or Tolstoy or Dostoevsky, when we realize this. *Madame Bovary* is the model, as you suggest (”its lack of sentimentality may add to the poignancy when I read it”), a work more thoroughly and scabrously ironic than what followed, but a work secretly in injured love with the world and its inhabitants behind its disaffected armor. The vaunted “new sincerity” of the 21st-century novel, its inability or refusal to sustain an ironic perspective, its finally flagrant sentimentalism, in *The Corrections* or *Cloud Atlas* or *Lincoln in the Bardo* or *Major Arcana*, plausibly signifies civilizational regression. Yet love was *always* the point.

Then someone pursued the initial question deeper into the realm of love, and I repost it here to invite my own readers to leave examples in the comments:

> Speaking of love, is there any great (imaginative) literature but Austen that’s centrally about romantic relationships and especially their *development* between individuals? Not as a symbolic prize of maturity (C. Brontë), or vivification of myth (E. Brontë), or the effects of some state of mind (Mme. Bovary). Course there are relationships in plenty of great novels, easy thing to hang a plot on. But surprisingly few ‘classics’ coming to mind have it as even a major strand, let alone core focus

I replied: It’s a good question. A lot of examples that come to mind—*David Copperfield*, *Middlemarch*, *The Portrait of a Lady*—are counter-examples, books about realizing you’ve married the Wrong One. As for the Right One, I suppose “happiness writes white.” The happiest marriages in Shakespeare are the Macbeths and Gertrude and Claudius. (There’s something attractive about a “partners in crime” approach, if not so literally as that.) Curiously *Mrs. Dalloway* and *To the Lighthouse* come close, as Woolf, not (always) stooping to satire, tries to imagine for herself what actually made those Victorian marriages work, when they did. (Speaking of, you might be under-selling the *bildung* that goes on between both parties in *Jane Eyre*, even if Brontë does resort to symbolism.) *Women in Love* also comes to mind, with Ursula and Birkin, who theorize the ideality of their own marriage. Sethe and Paul D. in *Beloved* maybe. I hear good things in this respect about *Mating*, but I haven’t read it. The closest thing to what you’re asking for among highly canonical works might actually be *Ulysses*, where it is, if not the core focus, definitely a major strand. (Ella writes well about it [here](http://ellaschmidt.substack.com/p/modern-wife).)

Finally, as a follow-up, another anonymous inquirer made the following observation—

> As a fellow closet sentimentalist disguised as modernist, my biggest problem *A Man Called Ove* and Colleen Hoover is their hallmark structures prevented their siege the successful extraction of my tears. My highest praise for *The Magic Mountain* (The Good Soldier chapter in particular) was it extracted them successfully, even if it does revert back to some ironic distance towards the end.

—prompting me to this reply: Yes, language loses its ability to move us directly once the tricks of manipulation have been identified. Audiovisual media, particularly photographic, particularly enhanced my music, are different. Music has a more direct access to the emotions than any other art, while photographic media can fool the eye into thinking it witnesses unredacted reality. This creates an almost physiological response in even the experienced viewer; those laughing in the theater at the end of *Hamnet* had to discharge the stimulated affect as well as those who were crying, as if smoke had gotten in their faces and made them cough, sneeze, and weep. To move to a different genre of narrative and feeling, even the most jaded spectator will jump at a jump scare, precisely why the jump scare is now such a cheap and debased effect (The exception here is comedy; we don’t laugh at what’s not funny in any medium; comedy, more than any other mode, addresses itself primarily to the intellect, which is probably why it doesn’t travel well. We are far more similar in our feelings than in our thoughts. Proving the rule is the comic pratfall or the most crassly sexual or scatological humor, appreciated by anyone or everyone because of the subject matter’s common nature.) After we’ve been initiated into the experience of being moved by a Mann, a Joyce, a Flaubert, we can’t go back, or I can’t, to being moved by Hoover, who is far more direct and unadorned in language than the 18th- and 19th-century sentimentalists, writing in a style the schoolchildren of Samuel Richardson’s or of Harriet Beecher Stowe’s day would have found simplistic, bereft of any *rhetoric* of feeling, even simple-seeming ones like the grave parataxis of Biblical narrative or the thundering repetitions of the preacher, because a fuller complement of stylistic techniques in language functioned for them as the music accompanying a video functions for us.

Alexander points out the novel’s performative contradiction in its flaunting of the characters’ intellect to undermine the ethical pretension of that intellect, somewhat in the manner of a tradition going back to Sterne or Rabelais or somebody like that:

> I argued a lot with *Major Arcana* but not in a bad way. There were passages in which the characters are expounding, at length, with brilliant thought-provoking ideas and I would peck a note into my kindle, “Too explicit,” because I’ve long believed that you cannot write a gripping or propulsive or real-seeming novel if you’ve got a cast of perfectly erudite characters who can articulate their thoughts in quick, reliable, effortless paragraphs.
> 
> \[…\]
> 
> If you could take a ten-year-old child, and instantly upload a library’s worth of knowledge into that child’s brain, would they somehow cease to be a child? If only in the abstract sense that you’d have “ended their childhood,” or “ruined it,” by opening their eyes to whichever branches of reason and heartbreak and broken hope by which we measure maturity?
> 
> The place where *Major Arcana* shows its heart is that I think, if you were to ask it that question with a Oija board or something, it’d say no. That youth is about learning things, warehousing knowledge, and then maturity’s about being shown, through experience, that you somehow don’t know anything.

I confess I like novels in which erudite characters articulately discuss ideas. I am impatient, more and more impatient as I get older, with an author who seems to manipulate taciturn puppets, a fictional style with special prestige in American literature due to the status of Hemingway and his code of action. (Hemingway’s dirty little secret, or one of them, is that he was better read than the swooningly Keatsian Fitzgerald or the thunderingly Shakespearean Faulkner, even though their work “feels” more literary than his does. He probably even read Rabelais. It was a *very* large iceberg.) In his 30th-anniversary preface to *Invisible Man*, Ellison writes, speaking for me on the *highest* frequency,

> Not that many worthy individuals aren’t in fact inarticulate, but that there were, and are, enough exceptions in real life to provide the perceptive novelist with models. *And even if they did not exist it would be necessary, both in the interests of fictional expressiveness and as examples of human possibility, to invent them.* Henry James had taught us much with his hyperconscious, “Super subtle fry,” characters who embodied in their own cultured, upper-class way the American virtues of conscience and consciousness. Such ideal creatures were unlikely to turn up in the world I inhabited, but one never knew because so much in this society is unnoticed and unrecorded. On the other hand, I felt that one of the ever-present challenges facing the American novelist was that of endowing his inarticulate characters, scenes and social processes with eloquence. For it is by such attempts that he fulfills his social responsibility as an American artist. (My emphasis.)

Some of my characters are smarter than I am—a paradox I am convinced is possible—such as Ash del Greco. But an author who so endows his characters with erudition is not the same as an erudite author. There are people on this website who are spectacularly learned—I think of Julianne Werlin or James Tussing—but I’m not one of them. My “learning,” like Borges’s, is [“literally encyclopedic.”](https://grandhotelabyss.tumblr.com/post/90355782877/literally-encyclopedic) Similarly, in his recent *The Master of Contradictions: Thomas Mann and the Making of* The Magic Mountain, Morten Høi Jensen quotes a letter from the author, often himself thought stupendously learned, to one of his academic explicators, and this is also what I’d say on my own behalf, not I’m at the same altitude as the German master, and not that I would, if I were speaking in my own voice, perform so blatant a “my greatest flaw is that I’m *too* good” type of maneuver:

> Perhaps I am too good at display, the ‘show window,’ at the manipulation of esoteric material. Perhaps I have always acquired knowledge solely for a purpose, for the moment, and in order to play with it.

Taking a hint from *[After the Hunt](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/weekly-readings-194-102025-102625#footnote-4-176602620)*, I finally read Mann’s first novel *Buddenbrooks* this week. Is there any greater relief than when one trepidatiously picks up a monumental classic and discovers that it’s pure pleasure, a fantastically entertaining has-it-all pageant whose almost 800 pages one can sail swiftly through in eight days? That Mann wrote *Buddenbrooks* at the age of 25—I doubt I’ll be able to pull off anything comparable at 55 or 65—puts to rest forever all those tiresome platitudes I am always mocking about how much [“experience”](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/141791621/war-and-peas-what-kind-of-experience-does-the-writer-require) the writer needs. Mann even disproves my concession to the “experience” crew that we are likelier to get lyric than epic or dramatic genius from those who have not yet reached the middle of the walk. The ingenious epic *Buddenbrooks* is like the work of a wickeder Tolstoy: everything is seen in absolute detail from an Olympian height, though here the distance works in service to the aesthete’s irony rather than to the moralist’s wisdom. This is to say that it’s perfect.

(I speak of aesthetic perfection. Ideologically, Mann had still of course to outgrow the late-19th-century naturalist logic of “degeneration” that informs his narrative design. The novel’s final pages lament the crushing of humanistic lightness by the Prussian militarization of learning and therefore foretell all that was to come in the subsequent German decades, all that would make a “degeneration” framing of collective development humanistically untenable, as those who most ardently attempted to arrest cultural degeneration ended up destroying all culture.)

Mann, torn from the first between antinomies of bourgeois moderation and bohemian frenzy, northern reserve and southern passion, the *paterfamilias*’s care and the pederast’s lust, the solid citizen’s duty and the avant-garde artist’s anarchy, at once celebrates and execrates his vocation:

> He had once again felt how painful beauty truly is, how it plunged you into shame and yearning despair and at the same time gnawed away at your courage and fitness for daily life.

This is what Adam Kirsch was [saying](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/2026/01/reading-crisis-solution-literature-personal-passion/685461/) last week, which is why we must read the article and not just the headline or a misleading excerpt. *The Atlantic* is unreliable, to be sure, but Kirsch is a serious critic; the author of *Rocket and Lightship* deserves better than knee-jerk mockery on the TL. Anyway, Balzac praised *The Charterhouse of Parma* for containing a novel in miniature on almost every page, and so we might say of *Buddenbrooks*. I especially appreciated the disastrous and abortive modernist *Künstlerroman* that concludes the novel—I was really pulling for little Hanno to make good like his *confrères* Marcel and Stephen, but alas!—and I wondered why some contemporary heterosexual female novelist has not yet regaled us with the 800-page version of Hanno’s tragic boys’-love-manga romance with Kai. If you need an idea for a book, you can have that one for free. Call it *Hanno’s Hands*. TikTok will eat it up.

But art enables as well as disabling human connection, as Mann might have seen had his circumstances not forced him to make art both a monster big enough to frighten the bourgeoisie with and a closet big enough to hide all his forbidden desires in. To wit, I had three separate conversations with strangers incited by my reading of *Buddenbrooks* in public this week, an experience I haven’t enjoyed with any book in many, many years, probably not since I was reading *The Portrait of a Lady* on a campus bench in college, and a passing fellow student said to me, “I feel bad for you, that’s really boring.” I recount these conversations here:

—In the library, an older woman, made up in bright blue eyeshadow, wrapped in a bright red black-striped scarf, topped with a bright red black-brimmed fedora, and carrying a dull brown briefcase stopped in front of me and said, “What are you reading?” I held up the book to show her the cover, the 1990s Vintage International edition. She repeated the novel’s subtitle to herself: *The Decline of a Family*. “‘The decline of a family.’ Let me tell you,” she said. She told me too quietly and too quickly for me to get it all, something about how she didn’t know her mother, and how she was trying to counteract the “voodoo” her other family members were sending her way but didn’t want to harm them exactly. “What do you think I should do?” she asked me in earnest. A couple of years ago, I would have had no advice for her at all, but luckily I absorbed much of the New Age to write *Major Arcana* and was able to channel the wisdom of 100 influencers her way: “Just stay centered in yourself and know you’re a good person,” I suggested, drawing an imaginary line down the middle of my body with one finger. “Whatever wrongs they have done to you will come back around to them. You don’t have to do a thing.” She thanked me and went on her way.

—In a café, the next day, a man with a gruff been-in-jail demeanor, unshaven cheeks, sweatpants, passed my table, a carrier full of purple smoothies in his grip, looked down at the book and said, “Hey, I read that. It’s really depressing. I didn’t even read the last page. I got to it and just said, ‘I don’t need that.’” (I see what he means; I myself averted my eyes here and there in the last quarter. But I wouldn’t have wanted to miss the last page’s cyclical return to the first page, as Antonie is scolded on the threshold of old age, just as she was in her early childhood, for misapprehending her religion.) “Yes,” I said to him with about 200 pages to go. “Everybody’s already starting to die.”

—Back in the library, just as I was finishing the book, a man about my own age in a puffy winter coat and a sports team bobble hat (I forget which sport and which team, not a local one) stopped and struck up a stern conversation about the novel and about Mann’s work in general without any preamble. For five tense, tight minutes, we had what I can only describe as a lightning round of literary chess that began with Nabokov’s critique of Mann and worked backward from there through Conrad, Dostoevsky, Tolstoy, Flaubert, and Stendhal, matching each other reference for reference, quotation for quotation, critique for critique (“Nabokov’s full of hot air,” he said), until he seemed satisfied that I had mastered, more or less, the curriculum of modern fiction. “Nice talking to you,” I posited (*was* it?) as he walked away. We never exchanged a word of personal information. Not even our names.
