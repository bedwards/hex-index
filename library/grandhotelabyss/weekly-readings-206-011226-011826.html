Zoom Patrol: Lost Lambs and the Empire of Cuteness

Three years ago, upon the publication of her debut collection Earth Angel, we heard that Madeline Cash was aligned with “serious protofascism.” I wrote about the matter in passing here and then reviewed Earth Angel alongside Honor Levy’s My First Book here. Now, upon the publication of Cash’s debut novel Lost Lambs, we hear that she’s some kind of industry plant. (No links. You either know what I’m talking about or you don’t, but I don’t want to fight with named and notable personages on this increasingly and enervatingly Twitter-like website.) I enjoyed Earth Angel enough to want to read Lost Lambs in a vague and non-urgent way. I even enjoyed Earth Angel enough to wonder why Cash of all people attracts these controversies, these accusations that Peter Thiel or the publishing monopolies have foisted her upon us, since she came up through the writing ranks in plain view as Forever magazine editor, and since she has a sentence-to-sentence lyrical-comic talent that leaps off the page.

As the Substack controversy raged I saw Lost Lambs on the new books shelf at the library two days ago and thought, what the hell, I’ll read it in the interstices of The Magic Mountain. And you can read it in those interstices; you can read the whole 320-page novel in the time it takes to read about 50 pages of The Magic Mountain. This is partly owing to the lamentable thinness of almost all contemporary fictional prose, its spare and (one can’t help but observe) televisual1 dialogue-and-description having replaced the intricate psychological analyses and philosophical disputations of mature-period Mann. But early-period Mann, the precocious Mann of Buddenbrooks, wrote a rapid and seriocomic family saga, too, which is exactly what Lost Lambs is, and so we must not hold the latter novel’s velocity, its brio, against it.

Cash makes the spareness of her descriptions and the archness of her repartee work with a chiseled wit that reaches back before not only television, and before not only her and her Gen-Z cohort’s most immediate literary influence—the Didion-DeLillo-Adler-Ellis axis of minimalist deadpan cultural critique in late-20th-century American writing—but all the way to more classic comic writers like Wilde or Austen. As I suggested in comparing modes and media last week, it’s almost certainly easier to make an audience cry than to make it laugh,2 but Cash will, if you share her skewed sense of humor, have you LOLing and IJBOLing:

Her family saga’s deft and brisk handling of the Flynn family’s comic archetypes—the midlife-crisis-afflicted parents lamenting their rebel youth, the dangerously sexually active older sister, the attention-starved and awkward middle sister, and the cutely intrepid girl genius youngest sister—is also effective enough to disguise their familiarity (Pride and Prejudice in partial reverse but also and more straightforwardly The Brady Bunch with a soupçon of The Simpsons) and to renew their interest.

Those are the “wonderful gargoyles,” to borrow Orwell’s architectural metaphor for our greatest comic novelist, but what about the architecture? (Orwell said Dickens’s architecture was rotten.) Like Dickens, Cash adjoins her family saga to a social and political critique. Earlier critics accused her of being funded by Peter Thiel, but Peter Thiel, more or less, is the villain of Lost Lambs. For it transpires that the “tech millionaire” Paul Alabaster who rules the Flynn family’s little coastal town is up to something nigh vampiric, which the family must overcome its internal dating-and-divorce divisions to defeat. Alabaster’s surname, moreover, links his villainy to race, though the novel never seriously addresses this idea, while we’re told that his family has held power almost since the country’s founding, whereas most wealthy tech bros form part of an upstart new class. (Dickens also had resort to fairy-tale simplicities, but I can’t help but observe that as a journalist he would have more sharply discriminated his political observations.)

Udith Dematagoda,3 in considering the monopolistic publishing company’s publicity blitzkrieg on Cash’s behalf, implicitly renews the leftist charge that the U.S. imperium, as militant and would-be unipolar adversary of eastern communism, is the formal successor to the Third Reich. But this isn’t a secret. Cash’s own novel positions a fascist form of capitalism as its bloodsucking villain. America’s—or Anglo-America’s—ruling class rules by selling its subjects image after image of their own grievance in a perpetual and processual self-legitimation-via-autocritique (see my essay on The Scarlet Letter and my theory of “subaltern empire”).

Not that Cash’s novel doesn’t occasionally threaten to get out of ideological hand in a belated Dimes Squarish manner. For most of its length, until the Dickensian-sentimental ending when the family recomposes itself as “something modern, but also traditional in a sense” (note the sitcom reference),4 a fierce left-conservative polemic seems ever on the verge of coalescing. Yes, capitalism and patriarchy threaten the novel’s three sisters, sexually and otherwise, but did their mother’s abdication of her maternal duty not strand them in such danger in the first place? It looks that way for most of the book. We are almost in hot political water here: not only right-wing anti-capitalism, but also a right-wing heterofatalism of the kind Didion used to essay (see here and here). At the end of Lost Lambs, the Flynn matriarch, who began the novel wanting to abandon her family to become an artist, must learn to see herself “as an artist simply by being female, doing what women had always done: creating life,” though Cash mutes the point’s conservatism by putting in the mouth of the mother’s lesbian lover in the fiction’s final insubstantial pageant of Brady-Bunch-blended Modern Family.5

Lost Lambs is a mixed achievement, then, if consistently hilarious. (I could say, if I were being severe, that it’s a bagatelle, a divertimento, a jeu d’esprit, an operetta, or some other romance-language term denoting levity and connoting frivolity, but it’s only severe criticism, or only frivolous art, if we take the comic too lightly.) When Cash lists the novel’s influences in an interview, one name stands out to me:

I love, and endeavored to write, a systems novel, a maximalist novel, a book that makes us examine our societal or political systems. So I read a lot of those, like Something Happened by Joseph Heller, Underworld by Don DeLillo, Absalom, Absalom! By Faulkner. Jonathan Franzen is a huge inspiration/ north star, particularly The Corrections. The Last Samurai by Helen Dewitt and The Virgin Suicides (book and film). The art of Henry Darger, particularly Vivvienne Girls. Movies with bumbling criminals like Burn After Reading and Lock Stock and Two Smoking Barrels. I love Martin McDonagh.

Not DeLillo or Franzen, and certainly not Faulkner, but rather Darger is Lost Lambs’ master spirit. That outsider artist’s homespun epic of a war between beleaguered and be-lingam’d little Christian girls and their brutal male oppressors anticipates Lost Lambs when it’s at its best, and Lost Lambs is at its best, its most lightsomely nightmarish-surreal, when most involved with its girl genius Harper and her Dargeresque fight against the vicious world.

The 21st century’s greatest revolution in aesthetics—however anticipated by 19th- and 20th-century currents like, well, the novels of Charles Dickens or (Darger’s main inspiration) Harriet Beecher Stowe—is the full admission of cuteness into the halls of high art. This signals our own childishness, no doubt. The 19th was the century of the child, the 20th the century of the adolescent, but the 21st is the century of eternal youth, of the kidult, and its attendant cuteness-unto-death. This comes at an ethical and political cost, I’m sure. At the outset of our cute century, Kitty Hauser gave us this critique at the intersection of Marx and Nietzsche—

On the contrary, according to Kinsella, cute style betrays a lack of confidence in the very notion of the individual, and cannot muster the energy and optimism necessary for rebellion. It is a soft revolt. It seems that becoming an adult is not an attractive option to these burikko (‘fake children’) when it is associated with the responsibilities and obligations of work and family. This is a generation of ‘freeters’ (the word comes from ‘free arbeiter’) who have rejected the stringent work patterns of their parents, even when they are available, as they often are not in the current economic climate. Acting and dressing like children represents their refusal of the adult world: as Kinsella writes, cute style ‘idolises the pre-social’. Cute is a kind of rebellion, then, but its retreat to the imagery of childhood indicates that there is no alternative to the adult world except a deliberate regression to this one remaining realm of freedom. Seen in this way, cute style is bleak: it allows no looking forward to a future, either for individuals or for society. In this sense it is far darker than punk, which had an energy and rage that promised action, if not social change. Cute disguises its pessimism and political inertia as winsomeness.

—and just a few years ago I wrote (scroll down to the part about Goodnight Punpun) this.6 Darger himself was once thought a pedophile before more sensitive critics apprehended that his phallic maidens were not his object of desire but were rather his own self-image. (A certain kind of stern psychoanalyst will judge the simplicity of this assertion naive and would speak, perhaps, of autogynopedophilia. But why be unpleasant? It’s Sunday morning.) Per Hauser’s essay subtitled “Style in Japan,” Japan’s conquest of global youth culture has arguably inaugurated the reign of cute, with that subdued nation’s disavowal of its earlier imperial aggression in an exercise of kawaii (Sianne Ngai famously dubbed cuteness “aestheticized powerlessness”).

But then there are Dickens and Stowe with their tiny Tims and Topsies, and there is the fact, which I haven’t mentioned yet, that Lost Lambs is, like Stowe’s and Dickens’s and Darger’s, a Christian novel, just as its title is a Christian figuration. If the last line of the paratext is in fact the last line of the text, then Lost Lambs ends with Cash’s giving her ultimate acknowledgement to “G*d.” And so we are left, since it was not the fair motormaids of Japan but rather Christ himself who enjoined us to suffer the little children, with the familiar paradox of a Christian empire, of an authoritative art that disowns its own authority, of power that presents itself to its subjects as powerlessness. At that impasse I am afraid we must remain until the shepherd returns for us, if ever he does, on this pious Sabbath morn.

Must the sentimental always be a pejorative? I remember taking satisfaction as a ’90s teen when Shakespeare in Love usurped what was then seen as Saving Private Ryan’s assured Oscar. The latter film, I thought, had a radical opening that it proceeded to dissolve in schmaltzy bromides, while the former had the integrity of its sentiment through and through. A similar upset of Hamnet against One Battle After Another would satisfy me in the same way—again the front-runner betrays its fierce proem in the mawkish balance—though Hamnet’s sentiment is rather more flagrant than was Stoppard et al.’s witty love story. (See my reviews of the two films here and here.) I was startled to see Hamnet director Chloé Zhao speak of Jung and the “Black Madonna” as informing her approach to Shakespeare, which puts her in line with last summer’s Invisible College reading of the Bard; and yet her description of our poet as “the ultimate IP” leaves a sourer taste in the mouth. This conversion of literature into IP, and not so much the ideology involved, is what offends about the umpteenth Wide Sargasso Sea retread, this time a female-centric rewriting of Moby-Dick. Why not use archetypes rather than brands? But Dante re-wrote Virgil, we will be told. Yes, but he didn’t exploit Virgil as IP. (Though here there might be something to say about what Christian and feminist re-writings of the classics have in common. Christianity and feminism, as high universalist moralisms, may in fact injure art, which is duty bound to explore all those proscribed passions and insights that moralist universalism must leave out of its picture of the universal, as the universe has not obviously promised us that it would conduct itself morally.)
