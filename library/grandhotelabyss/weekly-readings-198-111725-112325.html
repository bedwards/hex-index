Mountain, Sea, Desert: On New Romantic Fiction

Someone wrote in to my super-secret Tumblr with the following question:

Not that it’s anything as serious as this, but if you had to name 3-5 essential works that anyone should have to read to join your substack New Romantic movement, what would it be?

Just to clarify, I believe it started as Ted Gioia’s and Ross Barkan’s New Romantic movement, and then transferred to the editors of Romanticon. I promoted the narrower term “Romantic Realism”—my deployment of this phrase has now been discussed in a master’s thesis; hyperstition works, élèves!—in conjunction with Major Arcana to try to shift dominant literary fiction away from slim slice-of-life novels written in benumbed translationese and back/forward to a vaster conception of the novel as world-encompassing the world-transforming. With that in mind, I will first open up the question to anyone else who wants to answer it on behalf of “New Romanticism” at large and second answer on my own behalf with five novels constellated around the “Romantic Realist” aesthetic of Major Arcana. (I left off the near-contemporary authors I listed here as influences—Don DeLillo, Toni Morrison, and Cynthia Ozick—so as not to repeat myself.)3 

Thomas Mann, The Magic Mountain
I read this eight years before I wrote Major Arcana, but it became and remains my model of a novel that combines thought and feeling, philosophy and sensuality, epic grandeur and cutting irony: everything, in short, romanticisms and realisms and everything in between, a total vision of life in all its sublime self-contradiction.
D. H. Lawrence, The Rainbow and Women in Love 
I read these a year before I wrote Major Arcana and, despite Lawrence’s misgiving about Mann, it renewed the lesson, and added a further precept: that fastidiousness of style can be less potent than visionary rapture captured in language. And furthermore: a novel can take the form of scripture or myth and still be a novel, which Lawrence and I both learned first, to be fair, from the Brontë sisters.
Iris Murdoch, The Sea, the Sea
I read this two years before I wrote Major Arcana, desultorily, just because I figured, “I should probably read some more Iris Murdoch,” and found it absolutely revelatory, a piece of anti-magical magic, a clear fable not swamped but heightened by its intricacy of detail, a latter-day Tempest, an out-Nabokoving of Nabokov, perhaps my favorite English novel after modernism.
Roberto Bolaño, 2666 
I read this while writing Major Arcana, encouraged by the heroism of its darkness, the rigor of its irony, the energy of its invention, its remarkably un-cloying compassion, its stunningly unprogrammatic “romantic anarchism,” its odd combination of lightness and vastness, gravity and speed. 
Bruce Wagner, ROAR: American Master, The Oral Biography of Roger Orr 
I read this after writing Major Arcana and believe it had a magically retrocausal influence—both books are influenced in any case by Balzac’s Séraphîta—one of the best American novels of this century, its form deepening from gimmick to entrancement, its pun-laden jokes harrowing into legend, its parody actually an elegy for the American century, its protagonist a brother-sister under the skin to my own Simon Magnus.

American Canto: On New Reactionary Poetry

A scandal in poetry: the literary journal Spectra has published a poem by the neoreactionary philosopher recently re-appellated in The Hollywood Reporter as Curtis Jarvin—I will call him “Jarvin” hereafter for the sake of ostranenie—followed by very 2018ish recriminations from the leftist majority in the literary world about “platforming fascists” and how fascists can’t create good art and so on. Now historically, the leftist position on this general question has been, first, that, in the waning days of class society, fascists will make better art than liberals (because liberals are politically unserious) and better art than leftists (because leftists are too busy changing the material infrastructure to bother with art)—

There are those who imagine that to criticize a writer’s politics is necessarily to impugn his or her art. With Yeats and the other great modernists, almost the contrary is true. What helped to produce much of the major art of the early twentieth century was political reaction. It is not accidental that Conrad, Yeats, Eliot, Pound and Lawrence were all on the radical right, or that Heidegger is among the most original of modern philosophers. The radical right finds conventional middle-class society supremely distasteful, and confronts it with a critique far more searching and fundamental, if also a good deal more wrong-headed, than anything a liberal realism can muster. In the absence of a revolutionary aesthetic, it is the great reactionaries who stand askew to a degraded present, invoking spiritual values whose political implications are occasionally odious, but whose artistic depth and intricacy resonate far beyond the workaday decencies of an E. M. Forster.4 Yeats is the supremely fine poet he is, not despite his politics but in some measure because of them—a truth which offers little comfort to either liberal aesthete or reductive leftist. (Terry Eagleton, “The Archaic Avant-Garde,” Heathcliff and the Great Hunger)

—and, second, that, come the day of red revolution, liberals and fascists will be dumped in a common grave:

Step forward: we hear
That you are a good man.
You cannot be bought, but the lightning
Which strikes the house, also
Cannot be bought.
You hold to what you said.
But what did you say?
You are honest, you say your opinion.
Which opinion?
You are brave.
Against whom?
You are wise.
For whom?
You do not consider your personal advantages.
Whose advantages do you consider then?
You are a good friend.
Are you also a good friend of the good people?
Hear us then: we know.
You are our enemy. This is why we shall
Now put you in front of a wall. But in consideration of your merits and good qualities
We shall put you in front of a good wall and shoot you
With a good bullet from a good gun and bury you
With a good shovel in the good earth.
(Bertolt Brecht, “The Interrogation of the Good” )

And yet, perhaps because we are not in the waning days of class society, unless Trump’s Oval Office rendezvous with Mamdani means more than I think it does, the interest Jarvin’s poem holds for us is not primarily aesthetic. I could say the imagery blurs, the rhythm stumbles, the diction wavers, but, still, contemporary poetry offers no agreed-upon canon of taste by which we might judge any individual poem—I discuss this in my essay on Louise Glück, who, for all her brilliance of invention and reverence for the canon, still consciously sacrificed sound on the altar of sense, knowing we no longer expect our bards to sound like anything at all—except perhaps the apophatic and fundamentally ethical postmodern poetic advocated by Ben Lerner’s Hatred of Poetry, according to which the poem must testify to its own failure as a poem lest it posture destructively as a complete description of the real, in which case we might revise Jarvin’s faults, his tasteless “LOL,” for example, into virtues. (And even the “good man” Lerner was last seen here producing “right-wing egirl poetry” akin to Jarvin’s.) No, the interest, unsurprisingly, is political. Politically, though not poetically, we might compare Jarvin’s poem, an ode to the poet’s newborn son, to its most obvious intertext in the fascist canon, Yeats’s “A Prayer for My Daughter.” (Like Jarvin’s son, Yeats’s daughter was born late in the poet’s life.) Celebrating “custom” and “ceremony,” Yeats wishes for his daughter an aristocratic medieval lady’s life of perfect feminine cortoisie, in pointed contrast to the ideological ugliness of a modern beauty like his bitterly beloved Maud Gonne: 

In courtesy I’d have her chiefly learned;
Hearts are not had as a gift but hearts are earned
By those that are not entirely beautiful;
Yet many, that have played the fool
For beauty’s very self, has charm made wise,
And many a poor man that has roved,
Loved and thought himself beloved,
From a glad kindness cannot take his eyes.

[...]

An intellectual hatred is the worst,
So let her think opinions are accursed.
Have I not seen the loveliest woman born
Out of the mouth of Plenty’s horn,
Because of her opinionated mind
Barter that horn and every good
By quiet natures understood
For an old bellows full of angry wind?

In other words, Yeats attempts to assert the authority of the past over his child, an authority he identifies with his right as a father. This rhymes with Adorno’s ultimate definition of fascism as temporality itself, which I’ve recently discussed: whatever comes first deserves to rule. Priority is sovereignty, thus the medieval should guide the modern and the parent the child. Jarvin’s poem, to the extent that it may move us, moves us for its concession of just the opposite point. For this poem, time, embodied in the infant, is revolution. 

The clouded newborn eyes
Of our alien overlord,
Bluing to his mother’s glass,
Care nothing for the past,
Our “beautiful oak door.”

The one who will burn your “beautiful oak door”—this alludes to the most quoted passage in the anti-immigrant cult novel The Camp of the Saints—is not an immigrant, not an interloper, not an invader, not a stranger, not an Other, but your very own offspring. With this oedipal precept in mind, and with the poem’s vision of the child born late in the father’s life standing at the father’s deathbed, we could go so far as to read the poem as the poet’s political palinode, his concession that his politics have never been anything other than a dying animal’s compensatory fantasy. Is this—

The family is a nation;
All politics is loyalty;
All realities are false.

—not a syllogism, deriving from the family-nation equation the inevitability of disloyalty due to incompatible realities in which each political constituency lives? If there were perfect loyalty—as in Yeats’s imagined medieval world of master and man, itself inspired by the same types of Victorian fantasist (Ruskin, Carlyle) who influenced Jarvin—then there would be no need for politics. Politics, qua sublimated warfare, is when factions with dueling interests, and therefore no obligation to mutual loyalty, negotiate. When they cannot resolve their differences, war resumes. To become a father at all, then, is not to become a sovereign patriarch, the lord of the manor, but rather to risk becoming that “good man” Brecht’s revolutionary, here recast as the mewling infant, will put up against the revolutionary wall and shoot someday soon. According to this poem, then, revolution inheres in the structure of personal and political temporality; Carlyle and Yeats were wrong to posit primordial social unity; Marx and Freud were right to insist on dialectical division in both polis and nursery. Q.E.D., not to mention R.I.P. reactions neo- and paleo-. Leftists should be sending thank-you cards to Spectra! Joking aside, please have a little more faith in art’s holy work of irony (I draw from the Marxists’ own canon: “Irony…is the highest freedom that can be achieved in a world without God”) to make whatever corrections need be made in the margin of whatever you call fascist poetry. The alternative is a desire for purification, itself the very nucleus of fascist desire, as another canceled poet once told us.

In conclusion, for reasons that now elude me, I wrote a villanelle in the voice of a neoreactionary in 2014, when I was first reading the likes of Land and Yarvin, to return him his real name, and thought their ideas might someday be influential. I once posted it to my super-secret Tumblr, but never on Substack. I reproduce it here only for the irony of its juxtaposition to Yarvin’s poem. In 2014, I seem to have taken for granted, from the slightly sarcastic title forward, that a neoreactionary would write neo-formal verse, just as I took for granted that a neoreactionary would support Eastern European nationalism against Russian imperialism (see line 13); neither of these suppositions has proved true. Line 5, by the way, cryptically alludes to the same news story that animated my ferocious novelette “Right Between the Eyes.” The Anna Karina allusion is meant to evoke the ubiquity of her image on Tumblr in the period. I could go on annotating, but I don’t want to bore you. So, without further ado, see who “wore it better,” “it” being “fascist poetry,” painfully sincere in Yarvin’s case, archly ironic in mine:

THE NEW FORMALISM

The prep-school boys sing Ave Stalina;
Our new scholar-gypsies pound on their drums;
And all the girls, they think they’re Anna Karina.

They would have jeered as she said her Ave Marias
Who leak false drunkards’ tears on the grave of a nun:
Prep-school boys singing Ave Stalina.

They weep for the ruins of old Tenochtitlan—
Not blood-sated fields where their skirts come from,
All of those girls who think they’re Anna Karina.

To pious Aeneas, God’s command was “Destina!”
But the children of empire just carp at the scrum:
The prep-school boys sing Ave Stalina.

Hoist that black flag, O men of Crimea!
Show our snide-eyed schoolboys here how it’s done—
And all the girls who think they’re Anna Karina!

Will honor return to us—our exiled Perdita?
For now the mockers’ jihad, double-tongue hum:
Prep-school boys singing Ave Stalina
And all the girls thinking they’re Anna Karina.

I actually wonder when was the last time an issue of a peer-reviewed journal in literary studies generated this kind of suspense. Maybe it’s not comparing like-to-like to reach for an example from analog times, but maybe when Derrida’s long unreadable response to the de Man affair dropped in Critical Inquiry? (I don’t know; that happened when I was six years old; I’m judging from the David Lehman book on de Man.) I suppose a beloved theorist turning out to have been a Nazi and a beloved fiction writer turning out to have been a pedophile’s accomplice both inspire a certain rubber-necking, that desire to gaze on corpses Socrates attributed to the basest part of the soul, and yet the soul’s highest organ of discernment legitimately requires and treasures an articulate response to these dire revelations, and James Tussing is more fun to read than Derrida (see footnote 2 below). On this topic, though, let us not dismiss Frère Jacques too hastily. (I tried my best to discuss him here.) I’ve had one eye this week on a long and winding debate on this platform about Continental vs. analytic philosophy and related matters. I, who have never knowingly read a work of analytic philosophy, unless Wittgenstein counts, and surely the whole point of Wittgenstein is that he doesn’t really count, am unqualified to weigh in, but the spillover to debates about the decline of academic literary studies did catch my eye, here for instance. I first just want to insist upon the distinction made by Sigrid Nunez re: Sontag quoted here between snobbery—always bad, a caste mentality, judging someone based on origin, appearance, or other superficies—and elitism—often good, a form of universal reason, judging on performance and accomplishment. I am generally averse to a cynical folk-pragmatism conflating snobbery with elitism and declaring that all difficult thought or art evinces its difficulty only as a visible index of snobbish exclusionism. Second, I renew a point I made here years ago about the effective charisma of difficulty in philosophy, literature, and art. Often, difficulty creates a mystique that makes a writer or text more popular, not less, because everyone wants in on the secret. It seems to me that we have lost this wisdom, even though English departments went into decline after they stopped emphasizing difficult modernist literature or high theory and promoted pop-culture studies instead, and literary fiction went into decline after publishers became averse to anything written above a hook-’em-on-the-first page see-Spot-run style. I hope the fact that I often and vocally find writers like Derrida and Nabokov incredibly annoying grants me some leeway in making this point, as I am not a partisan of experimentation for its own sake or to the exclusion of other pertinent philosophical or literary values. As a practical matter of classroom teaching, it’s true that one never wants to insult the students. I make the cynical concession that one usually has to insult someone, however, to keep a restive crowd on one’s side. I advise the teacher, then, to rally the students as sharers in the difficult text’s unfolding secret, with judicious reference to a menacing “they” outside the classroom, not so much a “they” who wouldn’t get it as a “they” who doesn’t want “us” to know. Much as I may regret stooping to these Trumpian tricks over the years—I was so melodramatic when I taught large lecture courses: I would start talking, and then suddenly stop and say, “I’m not sure I should be saying this,” and then ask them if they were sure they wanted me to go on, and then theatrically close the blinds or open the door and look both ways down the hall, that kind of thing—the fact is that if you’re speaking to more than about three people, you’ll have to use a bit of demagogy just to feel secure in your person, which doesn’t of course mean they won’t hang you on a meat hook in the piazza someday.

James’s essay is the Munro chapter of Literature and Evil we didn’t know we needed. I confess I read a handful of Munro stories when she won the Nobel and dismissed her as a dull Chekhovian, all flavorless and patronizing free indirect discourse about cramped provincials, just as Christian Lorentzen claimed. Jonathan Franzen’s statement that her fiction was about “people, people, people” didn’t help; one “people” is enough, I thought; fiction also needs style and ideas; for “people, people, people” I can just go outside. But James emphasizes the sheer darkness of Munro’s vision: not just a meditation, though it’s also that, on the “every woman adores a fascist” problem, but a general investigation of the amorality of desire as epitomized in the selva oscura of that extraordinary story “Vandals,” which the essay convinced me to read. James compares Munro to the “great male narcissists” Bellow and Updike and Roth, but pronounces her at once less sentimental and more economical; he compares her to her countrywoman Atwood, whose signal dystopia appears naive in comparison to Munro’s realism. For apparent colorlessness of prose wedded to frightening pitilessness of vision, we might have to point to Coetzee or Ishiguro, and hope their personal lives furnish less literal proof of this vision than Munro’s did.

I reproduce here, since I haven’t quoted it on Substack since 2022, my favorite passage on this general problem of literature and evil (it may refer also to Tussing on Munro) from Geoffrey Galt Harpham’s One of Us: The Mastery of Joseph Conrad:

It is often suggested that a writer’s greatness is independent of his or her unworthy prejudices or deplorable opinions, that literary achievement is a different kind of thing from personal virtue. [Fredric] Jameson rejects such a notion as a mark of a naive liberalism that distinguishes between the primary or authentic artistic work and the contingent details of one’s political convictions. A decidedly nonliberal response to the problem represented by the scoundrel genius would affirm that greatness is inseparable from certain kinds of reprehensibility. A glance at the canon does not disconfirm this hypothesis. Dickens is (found by many to be) sentimental and cheap, George Eliot depressingly high-minded, Hardy clumsy and provincial, Wilde indifferent to suffering other than his own, Joyce inhuman and elitist, Woolf snobbish, D. H. Lawrence obsessed with the phallus, T. S. Eliot cold and academic, Pound bigoted—to name a few. But couldn’t one name them all? Can one imagine a figure of canonical stature—other than, perhaps, Jane Austen and the immensely tantalizing figure of Shakespeare—whose work does not suggest to some the mind of a bounder, a boor, an egocentric, a domestic bully, an ingrate, a sexist, a hectoring puritan, a shrew, a fraud? Isn’t this element of reprehensibility what E. M. Forster, for example, is missing, and why his reputation never breaks the surface of true greatness? Isn’t the problem that, with his sympathetic attachment to women, to the socially and economically stressed, and to India, Forster is simply too enlightened to engage the darker regions of the cultural psyche, and his appeal suffers as a consequence from a certain shallowness? Isn’t—to take the case of Shakespeare—the picturesque image of the immensely prolific playwright-actor living and working among gifted and well-compensated people so unsuited to the company of the canon that many believe the man from Stratford couldn’t have written those plays? Isn’t this image also responsible, for those who believe in his authorship, for the singular position he holds not exactly in but atop the canon? As a man, Shakespeare may be surcanonical, but his work is canonical because, in addition to such happy writerly virtues as intellectual acuity, verbal accomplishment, and fertility of invention, it displays an immense range of imaginative sympathy that extends from the virtuous and the heroic all the way down to evil, despair, perversion, impotence, waste, cowardice, misogyny, and anti-Semitism.

As a canonical figure—the canonical figure—Conrad exemplifies the unsorted mix of attributes, the ambivalence proper to our notion of eminence. Modern, western culture does not revere those who write solely from within the culture, reproducing only narcissistic images of of its best moments or most admirable traits. In Exiles and Emigres, [Terry] Eagleton points out that, at least in terms of the English canon, the weightiest writers are socially marginal people when they are not actually foreigners like Conrad. In British literary modernity, those who, like Bennett, Galsworthy, or Wells, take an identifiably English point of view on life in England follow the very recipe for minor literature. The perspective provided by the outside has this advantage, that it not only confirms the centrality of one’s own culture as an object worthy of attention, but also gratifies a distinctively modern appetite for self-criticism by calling the legitimacy or normativity of the culture’s prevailing customs and attitudes into question. This fairly obvious and conventional point actually entails another, far more problematic one—that the dominant culture of modernity is eagerly receptive to representations of the transgressive or disavowed impulses it has supposedly overcome. Literature is the site of this return of modernity’s repressed, and one of the common threads running through canonical literature is an invitation to identify with that which has been stigmatized as morally or socially unacceptable. Jameson takes this point to an extreme, declaring that “all the works of class history…have all had a vested interest in and a functional relationship to social formations based on violence and exploitation; and that…the restoration of the meaning of the greatest cultural monuments cannot be separated from a passionate and partisan assessment of everything that is oppressive in them and knows complicity with privilege and class domination, stained with the guilt not merely of culture in particular but of History itself as one long nightmare” (299). I would argue instead that “the greatest” monuments of modern culture expand, enlarge, or (better yet) widen the mind by soliciting a full and uncensored act of identification with both the forces of enlightenment and with those extremities of thought, feeling, and action that have been sacrificed to enlightenment.

As I pointed out the last time I quoted this passage, though, Forster makes a poor example of pure goodness, though Eagleton and Harpham both treat him as such. I said then, and I repeat here: Forster served as the secretary to the Maharaja of Dewas Senior in India in 1921. The monarch gifted the novelist the palace barber-boy to bugger, and the sexually repressed author of A Passage to India, with no little liberal guilt and colonial shame, duly availed himself. So in the annals of depravity we call the canon, even the gentle Forster may take his place.
