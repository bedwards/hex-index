### Zoom Patrol: *Lost Lambs* and the Empire of Cuteness

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kNi1!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5722de09-e9dd-4ae0-b08c-7903090dcece_2024x1092.png)

*Henry Darger via Jessica Yu’s *[In the Realms of the Unreal](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sRlvDKcDvsI)* (2004)*

Three years ago, upon the publication of her debut collection *Earth Angel*, we heard that Madeline Cash was aligned with [“serious protofascism.”](https://www.versobooks.com/blogs/news/the-right-wing-avant-garde-in-american-fiction) I wrote about the matter in passing [here](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/135566886/untimely-meditation-independent-american-literature-from-destruction-to-affirmation-2013-2023) and then reviewed *Earth Angel* alongside Honor Levy’s *My First Book* [here](https://marsreview.org/p/john-pistelli-on-debuts-from-honor). Now, upon the publication of Cash’s debut novel *Lost Lambs*, we hear that she’s some kind of industry plant. (No links. You either know what I’m talking about or you don’t, but I don’t want to fight with named and notable personages on this increasingly and enervatingly Twitter-like website.) I enjoyed *Earth Angel* enough to want to read *Lost Lambs* in a vague and non-urgent way. I even enjoyed *Earth Angel* enough to wonder why Cash of all people attracts these controversies, these accusations that Peter Thiel or the publishing monopolies have foisted her upon us, since she came up through the writing ranks in plain view as *Forever* magazine editor, and since she has a sentence-to-sentence lyrical-comic talent that leaps off the page.

As the Substack controversy raged I saw *Lost Lambs* on the new books shelf at the library two days ago and thought, what the hell, I’ll read it in the interstices of *The Magic Mountain*. And you *can* read it in those interstices; you can read the whole 320-page novel in the time it takes to read about 50 pages of *The Magic Mountain*. This is partly owing to the lamentable thinness of almost all contemporary fictional prose, its spare and (one can’t help but observe) televisual[1](#footnote-1) dialogue-and-description having replaced the intricate psychological analyses and philosophical disputations of mature-period Mann. But early-period Mann, the precocious Mann of *Buddenbrooks*, wrote a rapid and seriocomic family saga, too, which is exactly what *Lost Lambs* is, and so we must not hold the latter novel’s velocity, its *brio*, against it.

Cash makes the spareness of her descriptions and the archness of her repartee work with a chiseled wit that reaches back before not only television, and before not only her and her Gen-Z cohort’s most immediate literary influence—the Didion-DeLillo-Adler-Ellis axis of minimalist deadpan cultural critique in late-20th-century American writing—but all the way to more classic comic writers like Wilde or Austen. As I suggested in comparing modes and media [last week](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/184027342/higher-love-on-fiction-andas-feeling), it’s almost certainly easier to make an audience cry than to make it laugh,[2](#footnote-2) but Cash will, if you share her skewed sense of humor, have you LOLing and IJBOLing:

Her family saga’s deft and brisk handling of the Flynn family’s comic archetypes—the midlife-crisis-afflicted parents lamenting their rebel youth, the dangerously sexually active older sister, the attention-starved and awkward middle sister, and the cutely intrepid girl genius youngest sister—is also effective enough to disguise their familiarity (*Pride and Prejudice* in partial reverse but also and more straightforwardly *The Brady Bunch* with a *soupçon* of *The Simpsons*) and to renew their interest.

Those are the “wonderful gargoyles,” to borrow Orwell’s architectural metaphor for our greatest comic novelist, but what about the architecture? (Orwell said Dickens’s architecture was rotten.) Like Dickens, Cash adjoins her family saga to a social and political critique. Earlier critics accused her of being funded by Peter Thiel, but Peter Thiel, more or less, is the villain of *Lost Lambs*. For it transpires that the “tech millionaire” Paul Alabaster who rules the Flynn family’s little coastal town is up to something nigh vampiric, which the family must overcome its internal dating-and-divorce divisions to defeat. Alabaster’s surname, moreover, links his villainy to race, though the novel never seriously addresses this idea, while we’re told that his family has held power almost since the country’s founding, whereas most wealthy tech bros form part of an upstart new class. (Dickens also had resort to fairy-tale simplicities, but I can’t help but observe that as a journalist he would have more sharply discriminated his political observations.)

Udith Dematagoda,[3](#footnote-3) in [considering](https://substack.com/@udithdematagoda2/note/c-200091404) the monopolistic publishing company’s publicity *blitzkrieg* on Cash’s behalf, implicitly renews the leftist charge that the U.S. imperium, as militant and would-be unipolar adversary of eastern communism, is the formal successor to the Third Reich. But this isn’t a secret. Cash’s own novel positions a fascist form of capitalism as its bloodsucking villain. America’s—or Anglo-America’s—ruling class rules by selling its subjects image after image of their own grievance in a perpetual and processual self-legitimation-via-autocritique (see my [essay](https://johnpistelli.com/2017/04/06/nathaniel-hawthorne-the-scarlet-letter/) on *The Scarlet Letter* and my theory of [“subaltern empire”](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/122305255/subaltern-empire-reviewing-the-politics-and-aesthetics-of-postmodernism)).

Not that Cash’s novel doesn’t occasionally threaten to get out of ideological hand in a belated Dimes Squarish manner. For most of its length, until the Dickensian-sentimental ending when the family recomposes itself as “something modern, but also traditional in a sense” (note the sitcom reference),[4](#footnote-4) a fierce left-conservative polemic seems ever on the verge of coalescing. Yes, capitalism and patriarchy threaten the novel’s three sisters, sexually and otherwise, but did their mother’s abdication of her maternal duty not strand them in such danger in the first place? It looks that way for most of the book. We are almost in hot political water here: not only right-wing anti-capitalism, but also a right-wing heterofatalism of the kind Didion used to essay (see [here](https://johnpistelli.com/2019/06/03/joan-didion-play-it-as-it-lays/) and [here](https://johnpistelli.com/2020/01/14/joan-didion-a-book-of-common-prayer/)). At the end of *Lost Lambs*, the Flynn matriarch, who began the novel wanting to abandon her family to become an artist, must learn to see herself “as an artist simply by being female, doing what women had always done: creating life,” though Cash mutes the point’s conservatism by putting in the mouth of the mother’s lesbian lover in the fiction’s final insubstantial pageant of *Brady-Bunch*\-blended *Modern Family*.[5](#footnote-5)

*Lost Lambs* is a mixed achievement, then, if consistently hilarious. (I could say, if I were being severe, that it’s a *bagatelle*, a *divertimento*, a *jeu d’esprit*, an operetta, or some other romance-language term denoting levity and connoting frivolity, but it’s only severe criticism, or only frivolous art, if we take the comic too lightly.) When Cash lists the novel’s influences in an [interview](https://lithub.com/madeline-cash-on-writing-an-absurdist-systems-novel/), one name stands out to me:

> I love, and endeavored to write, a systems novel, a maximalist novel, a book that makes us examine our societal or political systems. So I read a lot of those, like *Something Happened* by Joseph Heller, *Underworld* by Don DeLillo, *Absalom, Absalom!* By Faulkner. Jonathan Franzen is a huge inspiration/ north star, particularly *The Corrections*. *The Last Samurai* by Helen Dewitt and *The Virgin Suicides* (book and film). The art of Henry Darger, particularly *Vivvienne Girls*. Movies with bumbling criminals like *Burn After Reading* and *Lock Stock and Two Smoking Barrels*. I love Martin McDonagh.

Not DeLillo or Franzen, and certainly not Faulkner, but rather Darger is *Lost Lambs*’ master spirit. That outsider artist’s homespun epic of a war between beleaguered and be-lingam’d little Christian girls and their brutal male oppressors anticipates *Lost Lambs* when it’s at its best, and *Lost Lambs* is at its best, its most lightsomely nightmarish-surreal, when most involved with its girl genius Harper and her Dargeresque fight against the vicious world.

The 21st century’s greatest revolution in aesthetics—however anticipated by 19th- and 20th-century currents like, well, the novels of Charles Dickens or (Darger’s main inspiration) Harriet Beecher Stowe—is the full admission of cuteness into the halls of high art. This signals our own childishness, no doubt. The 19th was the century of the child, the 20th the century of the adolescent, but the 21st is the century of eternal youth, of the kidult, and its attendant cuteness-unto-death. This comes at an ethical and political cost, I’m sure. At the outset of our cute century, Kitty Hauser gave us [this](https://www.lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v26/n08/kitty-hauser/cute) critique at the intersection of Marx and Nietzsche—

> On the contrary, according to Kinsella, cute style betrays a lack of confidence in the very notion of the individual, and cannot muster the energy and optimism necessary for rebellion. It is a soft revolt. It seems that becoming an adult is not an attractive option to these *burikko* (‘fake children’) when it is associated with the responsibilities and obligations of work and family. This is a generation of ‘freeters’ (the word comes from ‘free arbeiter’) who have rejected the stringent work patterns of their parents, even when they are available, as they often are not in the current economic climate. Acting and dressing like children represents their refusal of the adult world: as Kinsella writes, cute style ‘idolises the pre-social’. Cute is a kind of rebellion, then, but its retreat to the imagery of childhood indicates that there is no alternative to the adult world except a deliberate regression to this one remaining realm of freedom. Seen in this way, cute style is bleak: it allows no looking forward to a future, either for individuals or for society. In this sense it is far darker than punk, which had an energy and rage that promised action, if not social change. Cute disguises its pessimism and political inertia as winsomeness.

—and just a few years ago I wrote (scroll down to the part about *Goodnight Punpun*) [this](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/october-books).[6](#footnote-6) Darger himself was once thought a pedophile before more sensitive critics apprehended that his phallic maidens were not his object of desire but were rather his own self-image. (A certain kind of stern psychoanalyst will judge the simplicity of this assertion naive and would speak, perhaps, of autogynopedophilia. But why be unpleasant? It’s Sunday morning.) Per Hauser’s essay subtitled “Style in Japan,” Japan’s conquest of global youth culture has arguably inaugurated the reign of cute, with that subdued nation’s disavowal of its earlier imperial aggression in an exercise of *kawaii* (Sianne Ngai famously dubbed cuteness “aestheticized powerlessness”).

But then there *are* Dickens and Stowe with their tiny Tims and Topsies, and there is the fact, which I haven’t mentioned yet, that *Lost Lambs* is, like Stowe’s and Dickens’s and Darger’s, a Christian novel, just as its title is a Christian figuration. If the last line of the paratext is in fact the last line of the text, then *Lost Lambs* ends with Cash’s giving her ultimate acknowledgement to “G\*d.” And so we are left, since it was not the fair motormaids of Japan but rather Christ himself who enjoined us to suffer the little children, with the familiar paradox of a Christian empire, of an authoritative art that disowns its own authority, of power that presents itself to its subjects as powerlessness. At that impasse I am afraid we must remain until the shepherd returns for us, if ever he does, on this pious Sabbath morn.

[1](#footnote-anchor-1)

There’s *too* much to say about Mo Diggs’s *Met* blockbuster [“Stop the Stream”](https://www.metropolitanreview.org/p/stop-the-stream) from this week, a comprehensive genealogy of our absolute online engulfment by the televisual dream-life. For one thing, speaking as a child of the ’90s, the piece made me reflect on how much *Beavis and Butthead* and *MST3K* formed my sense of humor and *The Real World* my sense of (if not drama then) melodrama. For another, how could I possible disagree with the concluding call to make literature the counterculture of our time? But I want to single out this quotation as most relevant to my reading of *Lost Lambs*:

> Funny that all these moments he lists seem to recall childhood because I believe, paradoxically, this extreme media immersion is meant to return them to a state most of us experienced in childhood, a state also felt by household pets, when a parent, caregiver or “legal guardian’s” voice acts as a balm, regardless of the words being said. The pets and the children can’t understand what is being said: the pure sound is enough. This extreme media immersion is extreme regression.

[2](#footnote-anchor-2)

Must the sentimental always be a pejorative? I remember taking satisfaction as a ’90s teen when *Shakespeare in Love* usurped what was then seen as *Saving Private Ryan*’s assured Oscar. The latter film, I thought, had a radical opening that it proceeded to dissolve in schmaltzy bromides, while the former had the integrity of its sentiment through and through. A similar upset of *Hamnet* against *One Battle After Another* would satisfy me in the same way—again the front-runner betrays its fierce proem in the mawkish balance—though *Hamnet*’s sentiment is rather more flagrant than was Stoppard et al.’s witty love story. (See my reviews of the two films [here](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/weekly-readings-190-092225-092825#footnote-1-174682513) and [here](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/i/180623524/passing-through-nature-to-eternity-hamnet-and-the-end-of-cinema).) I was startled to see *Hamnet* director Chloé Zhao [speak](https://www.ft.com/content/d37000bd-11dd-4500-a49b-bb737778bae1) of Jung and the “Black Madonna” as informing her approach to Shakespeare, which puts her in line with last summer’s Invisible College reading of the Bard; and yet her [description](https://www.the-independent.com/arts-entertainment/films/features/hamnet-overrated-jessie-buckley-golden-globes-b2898187.html) of our poet as “the ultimate IP” leaves a sourer taste in the mouth. This conversion of literature into IP, and not so much the ideology involved, is what offends about the umpteenth *Wide Sargasso Sea* retread, this time a female-centric [rewriting](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/01/14/books/review/call-me-ishmaelle-xiaolu-guo.html) of *Moby-Dick*. Why not use archetypes rather than brands? But Dante re-wrote Virgil, we will be told. Yes, but he didn’t exploit Virgil as IP. (Though here there might be something to say about what Christian and feminist re-writings of the classics have in common. Christianity and feminism, as high universalist moralisms, may in fact injure art, which is duty bound to explore all those proscribed passions and insights that moralist universalism must leave out of its picture of the universal, as the universe has not obviously promised us that it would conduct itself morally.) As for the observation, made by some, that reviewer and novelist William Giraldi went easy on this one-dick-less *Dick*, which I concede I haven’t read, please see my defense of his earlier and more controversial procedure [here](https://raintaxi.com/hold-the-dark/).

[3](#footnote-anchor-3)

Critics strangely speak of *Lost Lambs* as an “internet novel,” but the internet is really not its focus at all. For a truly uncompromising and independent internet novel, see Udith’s own *Agonist*, which I wrote about [here](https://grandhotelabyss.substack.com/p/book-review-uh-dematagoda-agonist).

[4](#footnote-anchor-4)

Cash has made statements in several interviews implying fairly heavy editorial involvement in the novel’s final shape. While the narrator’s mischievous vibe-shift tone occasionally tweaks the proverbial sensitivity reader (“Catherine had switched on the television to *The Real Housewives of Baghdad*. Fatmi and Saadia were at it again”), I wonder if Cash’s editors had a hand in these ideological shifts. That is one reason I would be wary of operating on the Big Five level. All novelists can use an edit of their prose, but nobody alters the essential meaning of my work but me.

[5](#footnote-anchor-5)

The same accusation can be made, and has been made, of course, against *Major Arcana*. There was a brief period when novelists felt free *not* to affirm but rather to ironize in their narratives’ concluding pages—even a onetime big bestseller like *Buddenbrooks* leaves us chastened and abraded—but those days are gone.
