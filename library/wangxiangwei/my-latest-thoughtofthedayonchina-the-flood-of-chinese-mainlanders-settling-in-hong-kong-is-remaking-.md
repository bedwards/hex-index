Hong Kong is bouncing back, especially in finance, defying predictions of decline after the 2019 unrest and the 2020 national security law. The Hong Kong stock exchange has surged to the top of this year’s global rankings as the world’s largest initial public offering venue, with over 300 companies queuing up to list their shares.  
  
This resurgence signals resilience, but it also ignites a deeper debate: what kind of Hong Kong is emerging?

Concern has swirled around the recent influx of mainland Chinese and whether their arrival will erode the city’s social fabric, hastening its slide into “just another Chinese city”. These worries surfaced vividly in my conversations with academics who hail from the mainland and foreign journalists revisiting after years away.  
  
Such concerns, while understandable, are misplaced. The catalyst for this unease lies in the government’s post-2020 talent schemes to address the exodus of locals and foreign residents.  
  
Since 2022, the government’s talent schemes have drawn extraordinary interest, as evidenced by the half a million applications, more than 330,000 approvals and over 220,000 arrivals, mostly from the mainland.  
  
The impact is impossible to miss. Stroll through the financial centre of Central, and the crisp cadence of Mandarin arguably rivals Cantonese as the lingua franca, spilling beyond tourist traps into boardrooms and back alleys.  
  
This wave is reshaping everyday life in subtle but seismic ways. As Hong Kong’s retail and dining sectors reel from pandemic scars, mainland chains are seemingly replacing the cha chaan teng of yore. Boutiques hawk mainland liquor like Mao-tai.  
  
Even the roads hum with change: Chinese electric vehicles, from sleek Maxus vans to luxurious Zeekrs, are supplanting status symbols like the Toyota Alphard, offering comparable luxury and comfort at lower prices.  
  
Some read these changes as omens: will more Mandarin, more mainland brands and more “mainland-style” habits accelerate Hong Kong’s transformation into “just another Chinese city”?  
  
Even small anecdotes become touchpoints, particularly among mainlanders who arrived decades ago and have long woven themselves into the city’s tapestry.  
  
At a recent gathering, an academic from the mainland and a veteran of American and Hong Kong academia lamented what they perceived as a surge of impatient drivers, though it’s unclear if this stems from new arrivals or contagious frustration among locals.  
  
Historically, Hong Kong has always grown through migration from the mainland. Waves of Cantonese-speaking farmers and fishermen from Guangdong province and well-capitalised entrepreneurs from Shanghai and Zhejiang province brought capital, skill and ambition to turbocharge the post-war boom.  
  
They were not absorbed into a vacuum; they were socialised in and empowered by Hong Kong’s institutions. The result was neither a replica of the cities they left nor a static preservation of colonial-era life. It was Hong Kong: a hybrid culture made distinct not by its cuisine, accents and licence plates, but by its operating system of capitalism, the rule of law and cosmopolitanism.  
  
The current cohort demands the same lens: they represent the mainland’s crème de la crème – highly skilled professionals, scientists and entrepreneurs. They are drawn to Hong Kong’s crystalline incentives of lower taxes, world-class schools and the city’s unapologetic capitalism. Far from imposing a mainland mould, they’re voting with their feet for Hong Kong’s distinct DNA.  
  
Here’s the interesting twist: these newcomers voice some of the sharpest alarms about the city morphing into a mainland clone due to local erosion. Over the past year, in candid chats with newly arrived professionals, I’ve heard a chorus of unease aimed at Hong Kong’s political class. They decry the creeping adoption of mainland jargon and performative deference to Beijing.  
  
Consider the bottled water scandal in which a local couple allegedly misled the government into awarding a contract to supply dubiously sourced mainland bottled water to public offices. The incident dented confidence in the government and prompted a public apology by a senior official.  
  
That is precisely the kind of lapse that worries new and old Hong Kong believers – not because the water was from the mainland, but because trust in the system was breached.  
  
One refrain from new friends echoes with haunting clarity: “We moved to Hong Kong precisely because it’s a capitalist city, much different from the mainland. If it starts looking and acting like just another Chinese city, what’s the point of being here?”  
  
That question goes to the heart of the matter. The real test for Hong Kong is not whether more Mandarin is heard on Queen’s Road or whether a spicy hotpot chain displaces a neighbourhood diner. Those are superficial markers that ebb and flow with consumer preferences and demographics.  
  
The test is whether the city’s institutional core stays intact: common law, clean and competitive public procurement, open and rules-based markets and a government that communicates in the pragmatic, accountable language that has earned investors’ trust for decades.  
  
By that measure, the biggest risks do not come from newcomers. They come from complacency or mimicry within the local establishment.  
  
Clarity of purpose is key. Beijing’s call for deeper integration does not require Hong Kong to blur the lines that make it useful to the nation and the world.  
  
The city must distinguish between cosmetic and institutional change. Changes in restaurant rosters and car models are cosmetic. The institutional core – capitalist norms, rule of law and outward-facing openness – is what has always made Hong Kong tick. Protect that core, and the new arrivals will strengthen the city rather than dilute it. Neglect it, and even those who came will ask, fairly, why they came at all.
