In an interview on the podcast, “Perspectives with Wenchi Yu”, I share my personal and professional insights across my life and career, Hong Kong’s unique role as a capitalist city in a vast socialist country, and my 26 years at South China Morning Post, Asia’s leading English-language newspaper. Wenchi is a thought leader on Asia, with her career Her career spanning multiple sectors, including the U.S. Department of State, U.S. Congress, and Goldman Sachs.

Here is the summary of my one-hour long podcast:

#### Growing Up in the Rustbelt Province of Jilin

Wang describes his childhood in Jilin province, part of China’s northeastern rustbelt, as marked by economic hardship and industrial decline. He notes that after the founding of the People’s Republic of China in 1949, the region saw significant development of heavy industries over the first 20-30 years, much like the U.S. rustbelt states. However, the economy later suffered greatly. The area’s proximity to the Soviet Union shaped early education, with Russian being the dominant foreign language during periods of good Sino-Soviet relations. By the time Wang grew up, relations had soured, and China was beginning to open up to the wider world. His family was poor, and their most valuable possession was a shortwave transistor radio, which became a gateway to outside information.

#### First Exposure to English by Listening to “Enemy Radio”

Wang’s introduction to English started in a modest school with around 50 students, where he was the only one truly engaged with the subject. His teacher, originally trained in Russian but retrained in English as China opened up, took a personal interest in him and provided extra tutoring, igniting his passion. One day, tuning into the family radio, he discovered the “English 900” program on Voice of America (VOA), hosted by a woman with a sweet Beijing accent who had immigrated to the U.S. This, along with BBC’s “Follow Me” program aired on Chinese state TV, influenced generations born in the 1950s-1970s. “English 900” was originally designed to teach English in Taiwan and later adapted for mainland listeners.

Unbeknownst to him at the time, listening to VOA was illegal in China from the 1950s to the 1980s, labeled as “listening to enemy radio” and punishable by imprisonment. He draws a parallel to today’s restrictions, like using VPNs to access foreign news, which many still do despite the risks. This experience propelled him to study as an English major at Beijing Foreign Studies University (then the Beijing Institute of Foreign Languages), China’s premier institution for training diplomats and international affairs specialists.

#### His Journalism Years

Wang’s journalism career began during college, fueled by access to international magazines like *Time*, *Newsweek*, *The Economist*, and *BusinessWeek*, which his university library subscribed to with special permission. He devoured them daily, admiring their concise and elegant writing style, which sparked his interest in news reporting. After undergraduate studies, he pursued journalism at the graduate school of the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, where he learned American journalism principles from a visiting professor, Kenneth Starck (former dean at the University of Iowa). Upon graduation, he joined *China Daily*.

In 1991, he won a scholarship for an international diploma course at Cardiff University in the UK—his first time abroad. This coincided with Deng Xiaoping’s southern tour and China’s economic boom. He interned at *The Guardian*, *Financial Times*, *The Economist* and EIU, and secured a temporary role at the BBC World Service’s Chinese section. He contributed stories to *The Economist* (on China’s aviation opening and equity markets) and *The Guardian* (on stock markets).

In 1993, he moved to Hong Kong, starting at a Chinese-language news magazine (*Yazhou Zhoukan*, the Chinese edition of *Asiaweek* by Time Warner). By June 1996, he joined the South China Morning Post (SCMP) as a China business reporter. In 2000, he took charge of China coverage; in 2012, he became editor-in-chief under the previous owner. After Alibaba acquired SCMP in early 2016, he stepped down voluntarily to focus on family in Beijing but stayed as an editorial advisor until October 2022.

From 2000 to 2022, he expanded SCMP’s China desk, shifting from print to digital focus. Online readership evolved from 90% Hong Kong-based in 2012 to over 90% international today (30% U.S.-based, with sources from Five Eyes countries and Southeast Asia). As the first mainland Chinese to lead a mainstream English-language newsroom in Hong Kong/Asia, he faced a predominantly British management initially. Founded in 1903 by British interests, SCMP was once a colonial mouthpiece with pre-1997 China coverage influenced by foreign media leanings, often fixated on the “four T’s”: trade, Tibet, Tiananmen, and Taiwan.

His mission was to deliver insider, balanced, and in-depth reporting on China, broadening coverage to politics, society, people, and policies like “Go West.” Post-1997 handover, SCMP could hire Hong Kong correspondents (previously barred by Beijing). He recruited more mainland Chinese reporters, though they couldn’t get full accreditation due to restrictions. He navigated pressures from both Beijing (harassment and bans for critical stories) and Hong Kong/the West (accusations of being “soft on China”). His guiding principle: follow conscience and pursue credible stories.

He engaged with stakeholders from Chinese, Hong Kong, and U.S. officials. Under Alibaba, editorial independence remained intact with no interference, allowing controversial stories to run. Post-2019 National Security Law, political scoops became harder due to China’s emphasis on loyalty, discipline, and secrecy, plus rampant online rumors. SCMP continues breaking stories with a unique Hong Kong/Chinese perspective (not state propaganda) and is banned in mainland China without a VPN.

Now teaching journalism, Wang aims to train reporters on covering China’s rise, repay his opportunities, and bridge global understanding of China, countering misperceptions from poor propaganda efforts.

#### His Views on Hong Kong and SCMP

Wang sees Hong Kong as a vital “superconnector” and capitalist enclave bridging China and the world under “one country, two systems.” It must preserve its openness, rule of law, efficiency, and global ties to support China’s modernization and attract international capital to Asia. Despite challenges, Hong Kong should proactively demonstrate its unique value to Beijing rather than passively conceding its future, ensuring distinct identity and prosperity.

On SCMP, he positions it as distinct from Western media by offering a fuller, insider view of China from a Hong Kong/Chinese lens—balanced, not propagandistic—for audiences seeking deeper understanding. Pre-2019, Hong Kong’s media spectrum included pro-Beijing, mainstream (like SCMP), and anti-Beijing outlets. Post-2019 protests and National Security Law, the landscape narrowed: some outlets closed (e.g., *Apple Daily*, *Stand News*), others went into exile, fostering self-censorship. He likens the law to the “constricting golden headband” from *Journey to the West*—a tool to curb rebellion (e.g., 2019 protests with U.S. flags) without destroying Hong Kong’s essence, applied sparingly to avoid economic harm.

Fundamentals like capitalism and rule of law endure, but political perceptions lean toward “one country, one system,” exacerbated by enforcement actions like bounties on exiles. Fact-based reporting should persist, as pledged by officials, since free information flow is essential for Hong Kong’s financial hub status, alongside free movement of goods, people, and capital. Hong Kong must balance national security with growth, integrate with the mainland while maintaining international appeal, and host unique events (e.g., Bitcoin Asia with Eric Trump) impossible elsewhere in China. Pre-2019 criticism of the Communist Party, as allowed by Deng Xiaoping, should continue for better governance.

Wang is cautiously optimistic: China’s post-Deng opening unleashed irreversible aspirations for better lives, and Hong Kong can advance and play up its unique strength by proactively influencing Beijing’s thinking on how Hong Kong should move forward.
