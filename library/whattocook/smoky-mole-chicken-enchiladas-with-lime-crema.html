Some recipes come to me in a flash. I have a vision, jot down a draft, test it, tweak it, and voila. Other recipes, this one included, take longer. Much longer! I have had this mole-inspired sauce nailed down for more than a year, but haven’t been able to figure out what meal to make with it. In the fall, I was thinking we’d cook a mole chili using this sauce as our base. Before that, I was picturing a taco situation. But I really wanted this sauce to be the star of the show!

Finally, a few weeks ago, I landed on the recipe we have here — chicken and veg skillet enchiladas doused in this smoky mole sauce, baked to golden perfection, and finished off with a quickie lime crema — and am happy to report that it hits ALL the right notes.

The sauce has a smoky heat and richness, and is a “greater than the sum of its parts” situation. You’re not tasting one specific ingredient, like the peanut butter or chocolate. Instead, they all meld together to form a cohesive, rich, absolutely delicious flavor that’s nicely juxtaposed by the light chicken and veggie filling and soft tortilla. And the lime crema drizzle adds a bright, cooling factor to round things out.

It’s likely that almost everything you need to make this mole is already in your pantry. Chocolate chips, peanut butter, tomato paste, sesame seeds, and a handful of spices. These are the kinds of ingredients that live in the back of your cabinet just waiting for their chance to shine!

Mole is basically a whole universe of sauces that hail from Mexico. The version most people think of is dark and velvety with chocolate and chiles (both of which are included in today’s sauce!), but there are also bright green moles thick with herbs, and brick-red ones built on dried chiles and nuts.

Traditional mole is one of the most complex, labor-intensive sauces in Mexican cuisine. It’s a labor of love that can take days to make and typically involves toasting and grinding dozens of ingredients. It is truly an art form, and if you ever have the opportunity to taste a traditionally-made mole, please do not pass it up.

This is not that.

This is a 30-second blender mole that captures the soul of the sauce on a Tuesday night when you have 45 minutes to cook dinner and a serious craving. Consider it a love letter to the flavors of traditional mole, not a replacement for the real thing, and after one bite, I think you’ll agree it earns its place!

More enchilada options for you to try next: 30-minute al pastor enchiladas, which tap into the succulent spit-roasted flavors of al pastor in just half an hour. And for when you can’t bring yourself to roll up an enchilada, make this cheesy enchilada bake, where you just smoosh tortillas down into filling to get the same flavor with less effort.

Serves 4 to 6

Cook time: 45 minutes

Tools:

  • Blender

  • Chef’s knife

  • Cutting board

  • Large (ideally 12-inch) oven-safe skillet

  • Small bowl

Ingredients:

Mole sauce:

  • 3 cups low-sodium chicken broth

  • 2 to 4 chipotle peppers from a can of chipotles in adobo sauce (start with 2 and taste before adding more)

  • Heaping 1/3 cup semi-sweet chocolate chips

  • Heaping 1/3 cup tomato paste

  • 3 tablespoons roasted sesame seeds (or 1 tablespoon tahini)

  • 3 tablespoons creamy peanut butter

  • 1 tablespoon chili powder

  • 2 teaspoons cinnamon

  • 1 1/2 teaspoons garlic powder

  • 1 1/2 teaspoons kosher salt

  • 1/2 teaspoon cumin

Enchilada filling:

  • 1 tablespoon olive oil

  • 1/2 small white onion, thinly sliced

  • 1 bell pepper (red, yellow, or orange), thinly sliced

  • 1 1/2 pounds boneless, skinless chicken breasts, thinly sliced into bite-sized (1/2-inch thick) strips

  • Kosher salt

  • 8 to 10 large (roughly 10-inch) tortillas (I like flour because they’re easier to roll, but corn works, too)

  • 2 cups shredded Monterey Jack or Mexican blend cheese (one 8-ounce bag)

Lime crema:

  • 1/2 cup sour cream

  • Zest and juice of 1 lime

  • Water, as needed to reach drizzling consistency

  • Roasted sesame seeds, for garnish

In a blender, add 3 cups low-sodium chicken broth, 2 chipotle peppers from a can of chipotles in adobo sauce, a heaping 1/3 cup semi-sweet chocolate chips, a heaping 1/3 cup tomato paste, 3 tablespoons roasted sesame seeds, 3 tablespoons creamy peanut butter, 1 tablespoon chili powder, 2 teaspoons cinnamon, 1 1/2 teaspoons garlic powder, 1 1/2 teaspoons kosher salt, and 1/2 teaspoon cumin. Blend on high until totally smooth, 30 seconds to 1 minute (or until all the chocolate chips are blended). Taste and add 1 to 2 more chipotle peppers if you want more heat. Set aside.

Preheat the oven to 375°F.

Heat 1 tablespoon olive oil in a 12-inch oven-safe skillet (like cast-iron) over medium-high heat. Add 1/2 a thinly sliced white onion and 1 thinly sliced bell pepper and cook, stirring occasionally, until softened and lightly golden, about 5 minutes.

Add 1 1/2 pounds thinly sliced chicken breasts to the pan with the veggies. Season with 1/2 teaspoon kosher salt and cook, stirring occasionally, until the chicken is fully cooked through, 5 to 6 minutes.

Add 1 cup of the mole sauce and stir so the chicken and vegetables are nicely coated.

Turn the stove off, leaving the chicken mixture in the skillet. Put a plate on the counter beside the stove, and the bag of shredded cheese beside the plate. Hold a tortilla in your hand, and use a spoon to scoop in about 1/2 cup of the enchilada filling. Sprinkle a little shredded cheese on top, roll it up, and place it seam-side down on the plate. Repeat with the remaining tortillas, placing them seam-side down on the plate and stacking them on top of each other as needed until all the filling is used. If your tortillas aren’t super fresh and are cracking (or if you are using corn tortillas), microwave them in a damp paper towel for 10-second increments to make them more pliable.

Spread about 1/2 cup mole sauce across the bottom of the now-empty skillet (no need to wipe it out — it’s OK if there are some chicken/veggie bits in there), then transfer the enchiladas to the skillet, snuggling them in tightly, seam-side down.

Pour the remaining mole sauce down the center of the enchiladas and around the edges of the skillet. Sprinkle the remaining cheese over top. Bake until the cheese is melted and golden and the sauce is bubbling, 15 to 20 minutes.

While the enchiladas bake, make the lime crema: In a small bowl, whisk together 1/2 cup sour cream, the zest and juice of 1 lime, and enough water to thin it to a drizzleable consistency (start with 1 teaspoon and add more a little at a time until it’s perfect).

Let the enchiladas cool for a few minutes. Use a spatula to gently cut around and lift each enchilada onto plates, then drizzle with lime crema, sprinkle with sesame seeds, and serve.

  • What does “heaping” mean? It means you should not level off the top of the 1/3 cup — there will be a little mound.

  • Save time: While you’re shopping, ask if the butcher has availability to thinly slice your chicken — say you want it “fajita style” in 1/2-inch strips. They are usually happy to oblige if they aren’t too busy!

  • Reduce the spice: If you want all the chipotle flavor with less heat, remove the seeds before you add the peppers to the blender.

  • Riff/what to cook when you really don’t feel like cooking/save time: Make a rice bowl! Get a pot of white rice cooking (or use microwaveable frozen rice) and, as it cooks, cut up and sauté the vegetables and chicken as directed above. Or you could use ground chicken or another ground meat and cook it with diced veggies. Once those are cooking, make the mole sauce in the blender. Add the sauce into the pan with the chicken and veggies and serve the chicken mole on top of rice with the lime crema and extra mole. Takes about 20 minutes!

  • Love your leftovers/double the sauce: This recipe makes about 4 cups of mole sauce — possibly more than you need, though I use it all because I like my enchiladas saucy. It’s also easy to double if you want to make two batches and freeze one. The extra is incredible on roasted vegetables, baked eggs with cotija and cilantro on top (you could swap the tomato sauce in this recipe for mole!), or rice bowls. Store it in an airtight container and use within a week, or freeze it and thaw overnight in the fridge next time an enchilada craving hits. You could also freeze whole enchiladas if you have some leftover (or eat them within 3 days stored in the fridge). And FYI that you can freeze the rest of your chipotle peppers in adobo.

  • Bulk it up: Cook a pot of rice and a pot of black beans (save a little mole sauce to stir into the beans for flavor!) right after you put the enchiladas in the oven. They’ll be ready by the time the enchiladas come out.

  • Gimme some greens: You can add some chopped cilantro and/or spinach or chopped kale or Swiss chard to the enchilada filling. Kale and Swiss chard will take longer to wilt than spinach.

  • Feeding kids: Stick to 2 chipotle peppers to keep the spice level down. If you’re worried that that will still be too spicy for your kids, you can omit the seeds from the peppers, or use only 1 pepper. Another option: Add extra crema on top to diffuse the heat. My 3-year-old devoured this, and my 7-year-old was fine with it until he found the onions inside. Other ideas, depending on your kid: Sub a can of drained and rinsed black beans for the onions and peppers if that’d make it more likely that they’d be into them. Or you can fish out some chicken and onions for their enchiladas and use the bell pepper in the adults’ version only. Or make cheese quesadillas while your enchiladas bake!

  • Make ahead: Each component can be made ahead all the way up to assembly. I tested assembling the whole skillet and storing it covered in the fridge for a day before baking, and it worked well!

  • Vegetarian: Use 1 to 1 1/2 pounds thinly sliced mushrooms instead of the chicken. Sauté them after the other veg have softened. Or add a can of drained and rinsed black beans — add them after the veggies along with the cup of mole sauce. And/or double the veggies to bulk it up! Also use veggie broth instead of chicken.

  • Dairy-free: Omit the cheese and use dairy-free sour cream. If you’re omitting the cheese, I highly recommend prepping the skillet of rolled enchiladas ahead of time — when they sit in the sauce they soften and turn deliciously creamy! Also, buy DF chocolate chips.

  • Gluten-free: Use corn tortillas or the rice bowl riff. Be sure to warm your corn tortillas in a damp paper towel in the microwave before you try to roll them or they will rip. If your corn tortillas are on the smaller side, use less filling for each. Also double check that your chicken broth is GF.

  • Nut + seed free: Blend 1/2 a corn tortilla (this acts as a thickener), 1 extra tablespoon chocolate chips, and 1 teaspoon olive oil into the sauce. See below for more subs if you just need to cut one of them.

  • Chicken broth: Low-sodium chicken broth, low-sodium chicken or veggie stock, or 3 cups water + 3 teaspoons reduced-sodium Better Than Bouillon.

  • Chipotle peppers in adobo: 1 to 2 teaspoons chipotle powder or smoked paprika plus a dash of cayenne. You could also use canned green chiles (a tablespoon or 2) plus some smoked paprika.

  • Semi-sweet chocolate chips: Bittersweet baking chocolate, Mexican chocolate, 3 tablespoons unsweetened cocoa powder plus a pinch of sugar, or dark chocolate (though that’d give the sauce a more intense flavor).

  • Tomato paste: Crushed tomato or tomato sauce, but cut back a little on the broth so it’s not too liquidy.

  • Roasted sesame seeds: 1 tablespoon tahini or 1/2 corn tortilla. Or if you only have raw seeds, toast them in a dry pan over medium heat until golden and fragrant, 2 to 3 minutes.

  • Creamy peanut butter: Sunbutter (good for nut allergy) or almond or cashew butter

  • Chili powder: Ancho chili powder, taco seasoning, or a mix of cumin, paprika, and oregano

  • Cinnamon: Pumpkin pie spice, allspice (only use 1/2 teaspoon because it’s more intense)

  • Garlic powder: 2 to 3 cloves fresh garlic, 2 to 3 cubes thawed frozen garlic, or onion powder

  • Cumin: Ground coriander, taco seasoning, or increase the chili powder a little

  • Olive oil: EVOO or a neutral cooking oil like canola or grapeseed

  • White onion: Any other color onion, shallots, or 1 teaspoon onion powder if you’re in a bind.

  • Bell pepper: Poblano pepper is great but brings a little extra heat. Chopped zucchini or mushrooms would also be tasty!

  • Chicken: You can use chicken thighs if that’s what you have. Or thinly sliced steak or mushrooms. You could also swap the fajita-style sliced chicken for ground chicken, turkey, pork, or beef (if using beef, drain fat as needed after browning).

  • Flour tortillas: They roll easiest, but corn tortillas work, too. If using corn tortillas, wrap the tortillas in a damp paper towel and microwave in 10-second intervals, until pliable. Don’t skip this step or they’ll rip! Or see the “riff” note above if you want to skip tortillas and make a rice bowl instead.

  • Shredded cheese: Pretty much any shredded cheese will do. Or omit.

  • Sour cream: Greek yogurt

  • Lime: Skip making crema if you don’t have a lime and just dollop sour cream on top.
