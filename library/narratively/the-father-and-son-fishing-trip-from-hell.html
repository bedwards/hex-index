Get ready, readers—this one’s a banger. Evan Lubofsky previously penned an incredible longform feature for WIRED, “A Son Is Rescued at Sea. But What Happened to His Mother?” which was turned into the recent #1 Netflix movie “The Carman Family Deaths.” He has a real knack for finding odd news items and uncovering the full human story behind them. This one is as cinematic, exciting and terrifying as it gets.

“I’m gonna kill you motherfuckers and leave you to drown!”

Fishing captain Mark Bailey is on the top deck bellowing death threats into the stifling Sarasota sky. His five frightened charter passengers cower inside the cabin below. They know Bailey’s been snorting coke and nursing a bottle of Captain Morgan’s since morning, upending what was supposed to be an idyllic fathers-and-sons fishing trip. Now he’s got a 9mm gun. Nothing’s stopping him from climbing down from the fly deck, kicking in the cabin door and having himself a round robin.

The cabin’s a sauna. The terrified passengers keep the windows shut to drown out the sound of the boat’s twin diesels. They need to listen intently for footsteps coming down the ladder. They crouch down to keep from view and whisper quietly as darkness falls over the Atlantic.

Captain Bailey guns the throttle of the Double Marker deeper into the Gulf until the 47-foot blue and white charter boat shrinks to a lone spot on the horizon, beyond cell range. Everyone holed up in the cabin realizes that they’re no longer passengers aboard a fishing charter.

They’re hostages.

Twelve hours earlier.

Normally, Chris Giuffre Jr. likes to sleep in on Sundays. At least when there are no early house showings or open houses to run to. But today, he springs out of bed at 5 a.m. with the sting of the alarm. He throws on jeans and a white V-neck and tells his young wife, Fernanda, that he’ll see her around 7 p.m. He feels lucky that, with the baby and all, she’s given him the green light to spend the day fishing.

He meets his dad downstairs. Chris Giuffre Sr. is in his mid-50s with a square-jawed face, soft features set under neatly brushed-back dark hair and an easy smile.

They gather their backpacks and fishing gear, climb into Chris Sr.’s gray Toyota pickup and roll through the manicured maze of cul-de-sacs and mirror-like ponds dotting their posh neighborhood. The morning sky begins to blush as they head northwest toward Sarasota Bay.

It’s June 2, 2019. The forecast is mostly sunny, low 90s, and Chris Jr., who is 23 with a medium athletic build, dark hair and the suave look of a boy-band singer, is ready to let loose. He, Fernanda and their 8-month-old daughter are staying with his parents while saving for a place of their own. Despite living in the same house, he and his dad don’t spend a lot of non-working time together due to his new-father responsibilities and their frenetic pace working in Gulf Coast real estate development.

To Chris Sr., the trip is about more than fishing. It’s a chance to simply be a father to his young adult son — for a beat anyway — before life’s demands come charging back up the hill.

“We were just really looking forward to catching some fish, taking some photos and bringing home some stories,” Chris Jr. says later.

They have no idea just how dark of a story they’re heading into.

Low tide creeps through Sarasota Bay. Pelicans huddle on algae-coated pilings as the smell of musty fish hangs in the morning air at Marina Jack’s charter fleet pier, where the Double Marker is moored. The Giuffres carry their gear and provisions onto the boat as the captain climbs down from the fly deck to greet them.

Captain Mark Bailey, 36, has the signature look of a sport fisherman: long dirty-blond hair flowing beneath a baseball cap worn backward, Florida-man scruff, mirrored sunglasses and board shorts. His first mate joins the group as Bailey runs through the plan for the day. They’ll stop to get some bait before heading 50 or so miles offshore into the Gulf of Mexico to find their target species, red snapper.

Chris Sr. eyes the captain closely. Something’s off with this guy, he thinks. Bailey’s cordial enough, but he doesn’t appear to be the laid-back, fun-loving guy people around the marina say he is. He’s in some kind of fog. Maybe he tied one on last night and is trying to shake it off.

After the meet-and-greet, Bailey and his first mate climb back up to the fly deck to get rolling as Chris Jr. and his dad traipse into the cabin with the three other passengers on the charter.

Carlo Lopeparo, who booked the trip, is the link between the two families on board. Carlo subcontracts for Chris Sr., sweating his days out installing drywall inside the homes that Chris Sr. develops and sells from his air-conditioned offices. Thirty-five years old, Carlo has a shaved head and massive arms inked shoulder to wrist.

Along for the ride with Carlo is his 38-year-old half-brother, Jason Rialmo Sr., who also works in the trades and who recently got out of prison after serving a 10-year stint for weapons charges.

Lastly, there’s Jason’s 15-year old son, who everyone just calls “Junior.” A BMX-obsessed teen, he’s still trying to make up for all the years without his dad. He hasn’t forgotten the day he came home from elementary school to a wrecked living room after the cops busted in and wrestled Jason Sr. into custody.

The party of five settles into the cabin. They crack open a few breakfast beers and kick back as the blinding-white condos collaring Sarasota Bay fade into the distance.

By mid-morning, the alcohol is flowing throughout the Double Marker — especially on the top deck. Bailey, whose chief responsibility as a charter boat captain is to ensure the safety of his passengers, has already demolished a handful of beers and half a bottle of rum.

Carlo, who bought the booze, sees that the supply is running low. He asks Junior to do him a solid — climb up to the top deck and grab a beer from the stash up on the captain’s deck.

Junior, who’s wearing a bright red T-shirt, matching snapback cap and thick Cuban link chain, doesn’t think twice. He’s fearless as he scales the narrow ladder, hoists his lanky frame onto the top deck and reaches into the bucket. He hears the captain tell him to put the beer back but shrugs it off, figuring he’s joking. He climbs back down to the main deck.

Suddenly, Bailey flies down the ladder in a fury, grabs hold of Junior, pushes him and starts choking him with his chain. Then he rips the Cuban link off his neck and moves to the side of the boat, threatening to drop it into the ocean.

“Dude, give me his chain!” Carlo yells as he approaches Bailey. “What are you doing?!”

He grabs Bailey’s shoulder and tries to push him away from the boat’s edge. Bailey concedes and hands the chain back to Carlo.

Carlo goes into the cabin, where Jason Sr. is taking a nap, to let him know the captain’s assaulting his son. With his shaved head, bodybuilder’s physique and the letters “IDGAF” inked down his right bicep, Jason Sr. is the right guy to settle a score. But there’s one problem: He’s on probation, and going off on Bailey could earn him a trip straight back to the clink. He decides to sacrifice his own pride and stand down.

Chris Jr., a self-proclaimed hater of bullies, realizes that Junior’s dad can’t risk getting into an altercation. He steps in and yells at Bailey: “Dude, what the hell are you doing?”

“The kid’s been trying me and looking at me weird!” Bailey fires back.

“If Junior’s insulting you,” Chris Jr. says, “we’ll take him inside and talk to him. You don’t have to take things into your own hands.”

“I’m NOT going to be disrespected on my own fucking boat!” Bailey declares.

Chris Jr. puts his arm around Junior, shuffles him into the cabin, and blocks the door as Bailey tries to follow the kid inside. He tells the captain, “I’m not letting you go into the cabin. Just go up and do your job!”

“Well, this is my fucking boat and I can go into the cabin whenever I want to!” Bailey barks.

The shouting match continues until the first mate, Devin Kissell, steps in to mediate. Kissell, a stocky 6-foot-3 guy with a crew cut, picked up some conflict resolution skills in the Navy. He tries to de-escalate the situation and reminds everyone that they’re here to fish, not throw punches.

Bailey continues to seethe, but the intervention works. He heads back up to the fly deck, grabs the throttle, and guns the Double Marker farther into the open ocean.

“Do NOT BOOK with this guy.”

The warning on Tripadvisor hadn’t deterred Carlo from reserving the Double Marker. Nor had the one that said “Keep clear of this one,” or any of the other negative reviews of Bailey’s charter service. It had been operational since 2016, when, after studying graphic design at Northeastern University in Boston, Bailey had dipped his toe into the world of offshore fishing and decided to head south to make a living at sea. For a while, his business appeared to thrive. Bailey was pulling in around $100,000 and was able to buy into another, more established operation within Marina Jack’s charter fleet and upgrade to the Double Marker, a larger and more expensive fishing vessel.

Along the way, he became an experienced captain with a “pirate persona” who knew how to find the fish — a skill that earned him a healthy respect within the local sportfishing community and several fishing tournament titles. Kissell later says, “He taught me a lot about fishing, tricks that you learn that only professionals know. He’d go out of his way to help you out.”

On land, however, things for Bailey were headed in a downward spiral. A darker side of him had emerged as his dependence on alcohol and drugs grew. Kissell, who’d become friendly with him, became concerned.

“When we were hanging out and he would start to get out of hand, I’d try to pull him in a different direction,” Kissell recalls. “He had gotten to a point where that side of him was outweighing him.”

In September 2018, Bailey was arrested for trespassing at the apartment complex of an ex-girlfriend. Less than a month later, he was arrested for trespassing again, this time at the home of a different woman he’d been involved with. In a police statement, the woman wrote, “He cause enough distress as it is that now me and my 16 years old daughter live in fear in our own place.”

Then in 2019, Bailey was charged with driving on the wrong side of the road, acting erratically and resisting arrest. Meanwhile, his charter boat license with the Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission — a state requirement on for-hire vessels — had expired.

Bailey appeared to be drowning in a world of drugs, drama and bad decision-making. “He was definitely spiraling,” Kissel later says.

Yet somehow, he continued running offshore charter trips. And recreational anglers kept trusting him with their lives.

On the Double Marker, the passengers try to put the incident between Bailey and Junior behind them. They re-shift their focus to fishing, and by mid-afternoon, catch their limit of red snapper. Kissell reconfigures the fishing gear with different tackle and bait in case they end up angling for larger fish. He heads back upstairs to check in with Bailey and returns a few minutes later with a message for Chris Jr.: “The captain wants to talk to you.”

Chris Jr. suspects that Bailey’s temperature has cooled and he probably just wants to apologize. Everyone’s hotheaded now and then, he thinks.

He takes a deep breath, climbs up to the fly deck and parks himself next to Bailey on a cushion to the left of the helm. “Hey capt, what’s going on?” he asks.

Bailey pipes up. “People can’t be telling me what to do on my own fucking boat,” he says as he gazes out at the ocean. “This is my property, which is an extension of my house. He’s a disrespectful piece of shit. And you’re just an asshole.”

So much for the apology.

Then, he turns and looks Chris Jr. in the eye. “I’ve got a 9mm on deck. I could shoot all of you guys and dump you over the side,” he says. “No one will care.”

Chris Jr.’s stomach tightens. “No, I’m pretty sure a couple of people will care if that happens, bro,” he says. “That’s murder.”

Bailey then warns Chris Jr. that, as an alternate plan, he could have some “friends” waiting at the dock for when they get back. The threat doesn’t sound entirely vaporous.

Chris Jr. has heard enough. He stands up and takes a few strides toward the ladder to climb back down, but not before Bailey gets the last word in.

“Yeah. Get the fuck off my fly deck.”

Chris Jr. is done. He’s not touching another fishing pole. He sits down next to his dad at the back of the boat and stares blankly into the frothy Gulf waters surrounding them. The nightmare has to end, he thinks, but he figures the Double Marker is still at least 40 miles offshore.

From where the Giuffres are sitting, they have a clear view of Bailey up at the helm. He’s now oscillating between sipping rum and snorting lines of cocaine. That’s when the real terror begins. Suddenly, Bailey grabs hold of his 9mm handgun, looks out at Chris Jr. and his dad and begins firing in their direction.

PAP-PAP-PAP! PAP!!

Chris Sr. sees the flashpoint of the gun explode from the stern as four shots blaze just a few feet above his head. He flails his arms in wild disbelief while Chris Jr. hits the deck and starts crawling toward the cabin to take cover. Junior, who’s at the stern, sees the gun flashes as well. He, too, bolts for the cabin.

All five passengers quickly reassemble inside the enclosed space to take refuge. They have no weapons, no means of calling for help and no law enforcement anywhere near them. Daylight is fading fast.

At 6 p.m., Tracie Giuffre, Chris Sr.’s wife, is waiting on the lanai out back for him to get home. They have 7 p.m. dinner reservations with their friends, the McMullens, and Chris Sr. hasn’t responded to any of her texts. It’s not like him to be late. Maybe the guys are just having a good catch day, she hopes. But as dinnertime approaches, panic sets in. She calls Jennifer McMullen and gives her the heads up that she and Chris will likely run late.

“They’re not even responding,” Tracie tells her. “They should be within range. Should I call the police?”

“No, you have to call the Coast Guard.”

Tracie hangs up and steps inside the house. She calls out to Chris’ wife, Fernanda, who is upstairs with the baby. “Have you heard anything from Christopher?” she yells.

Fernanda, too, has been trying to reach her husband for hours. “No. I haven’t heard from him — at all!” she says, exasperated.

On the Double Marker, the sweltering cabin offers some protection for the hostages, but all that stands between them and a potentially deadly situation is the five-rung ladder connecting the two decks. They need a plan, and fast.

Staging an ambush seems like the most obvious option. It would be five against one (or two, depending on whose side First Mate Kissell is on). The problem is how to get to Bailey without first coming into his direct line of sight. Using the ladder won’t work. So, quietly, the group begins to strategize. If there’s a way to access the top deck without being seen, maybe they can sneak up behind Bailey and take him out.

Doing that would mean two of the hostages would have to quietly exit the cabin, slip onto the main deck, hop up onto the side railing and try to scale the front of the bow. Then, one of them would need to incapacitate Bailey from behind while the other takes control of the boat.

It seems promising at first, but the Double Marker is too small for that much stealth. Chris Sr. thinks it through and decides they’re not going up there.

The group begins hunting around the cabin for blunt objects — anything that they can swing or use to puncture if Bailey comes down. Jason Sr. spots a fire extinguisher stowed just below one of the benches. He grabs it and tucks it in between his knees as he crouches back down. Chris Jr. finds a second extinguisher. Chris Sr. rummages through one of the cabin drawers and grabs the sharpest thing he can find: a rusty screwdriver.

Chris Sr. calmly takes charge. “I’ll be the first line of defense,” he announces.

Nobody argues.

With the makeshift weapons in hand, they sit and wait for Bailey to descend. “Everyone needs to stay calm and do nothing to aggravate things,” Chris Sr. tells the group. “If Bailey does come down with gun in hand, I’ll bull rush him with the screwdriver head on, tackle him and try to get both of us overboard.”

Realizing that he’ll likely die doing so, he level-sets with Chris Jr., “Look, one of us is going to have to sacrifice. It’s a life-or-death situation for sure. I’ll probably get shot.” It’s no surprise to Chris Jr. that his dad — his hero — would make that sacrifice for him.

Even if he does take a bullet, Chris Sr. thinks there’s still a chance he can wrestle Bailey over the side. If not, the plan is for Jason Sr. to knock the captain out with the fire extinguisher. “Once the immediate threat is gone, we’ll coerce the first mate to guide us all back safely to shore,” Chris Sr. tells the group.

An hour passes. The cabin’s temperature climbs to 103 degrees Fahrenheit as the hostages sit trapped inside. There’s nothing left to drink — the soda cans and water bottles are all empty. In fragile solidarity, they open up to one another about their fears.

Chris Jr., who should be home by now helping Fernanda with the baby, has taken Bailey’s threats to heart. “Maybe he’ll kill us, dump us over the water between Florida and Cuba and go dock and hide in Cuba for a while.”

Chris Sr. isn’t thinking about Cuba. He’s thinking about the consequences for his family if he dies at sea. Tracie would be anguished, but she could accept losing him if it meant her son gets out of this alive. Since making the decision to sacrifice his own life, Chris Sr. feels an odd sense of relief. His fear is gone.

Jason Sr. cannot say the same. He’s terrified, but not for his safety. He’s scheduled to have an early evening check-in with his parole officer, and is worried about the consequences of missing it. “I hope my parole officer understands what’s going on,” he says with a hint of vulnerability in his voice. “You guys are going to have to tell him.” He gets up briefly and paces nervously about the cabin as if it’s just another prison cell, saying, “What the fuck, what the fuck, what the fuck…”

Junior is more bewildered than scared, as if it’s all straight out of an action movie he’s watching with his teenage friends. “Oh my god dude, how could this even be happening, like holy shit man, I can’t even believe this!” he bursts out in this high-pitched twangy drawl. The outbursts offer some comic relief, but the others shush him so they can listen for movements up top.

Finally, after hours of waiting for something to happen, they hear movement on the fly deck. Then, footsteps thud down the ladder.

Everyone’s frozen in place. Chris Sr. tiptoes over to the inward-swinging cabin door, ready for action. Through one of the cabin windows, an ankle comes into view — a thick ankle.

It’s First Mate Kissell. He needs to grab something from a cabinet. Everyone stands down. Chris Jr. silently hopes Kissell isn’t retrieving more ammo for the captain.

As Kissell rummages through the cabinet, he tells the group, “I don’t know what’s wrong with him. I don’t know what’s going on. I’m just trying to keep him calm.”

Chris Jr. cries out, “Take us fucking home!!”

“Yep, no problem,” Kissell replies. “We’re going to be heading home right away.” He leaves the cabin and goes back upstairs.

After cruising through the now-pitch-black Gulf for about an hour, Chris suddenly feels something peculiar. His body is leaning at a slight angle. Which doesn’t make sense since the seas are calm. The tilt is subtle, but enough to prompt him to launch the Compass app on his phone, which doesn’t require cell reception.

He points the virtual compass dead straight. Then he watches in horror as its tics begin rotating around the screen.

“This dude is out of his fucking mind,” Chris thinks to himself.

Bailey has been keeping the radius wide enough, and turns incremental enough, to trick his hostages into thinking they’re actually headed back to shore.

They’re not.

When Kissell returns to the cabin a while later, Chris Jr. loses his shit. “Dude, we’re doing fucking circles in the middle of the ocean — what the hell is going on? Do you think we’re stupid or something?”

“The captain says that the left motor on the boat is not working at full thrust properly,” Kissell replies calmly. “So, he’s trying to maneuver us to see if he can get it kicked out.”

Chris isn’t buying it. He’s not sure whose side Kissell is on. Perhaps he’ll tell the captain that the group is aware that they’re not heading home. Maybe that will be Bailey’s cue to head down and kill them, Chris thinks.

It’s now around 10 p.m. As the Double Marker churns through the Gulf, there are still no landmarks in sight or any signs of civilization. But somehow, Chris Jr.’s phone unexpectedly catches the edge of a cellular network. It blows up with dozens of missed calls and texts, mostly from Fernanda. He calls and she answers immediately, shouting, “Chris, what the hell is going on?”

Tracie, meanwhile, is on the line with the Coast Guard reporting the overdue vessel. The officer hears panic in her voice and suggests she drive to Marina Jack’s to see if Chris Sr.’s truck is still there. She’s about to leave the house when a text pops up on her phone. It’s Chris Jr. He’s telling her that the captain they went out with is acting crazy and they’re still way offshore.

Tracie relays the message to the officer, who tells her to have Chris Jr. dial 911 so the Sarasota Police dispatch center can track the boat via his phone’s GPS.

Moments later, Chris Jr. calls for help. A female dispatcher with a slight southern drawl answers. “911. What’s the location of your emergency?”

“I’m on a charter boat right now,” Chris Jr. says. “I just talked to the Coast Guard. They said for me to call you guys to get you to request to locate my GPS.”

“Are you a vessel in distress?”

“Yes, it’s just that the captain is, like, high as a kite. He’s got a gun on board and shot it multiple times.”

“Stay on the line with me,” she says in a protocolary tone as she bangs away furiously on a keyboard. “I want you to stay separated from him. Are they aware you’ve called the police?”

“No,” Chris Jr. says.

The dispatcher sends the Double Marker’s coordinates to the Coast Guard. It’s now only about five miles offshore. Maybe Bailey’s running out of fuel from his joyride through the Gulf. Or maybe he just needed time to sober up before coming back in.

Coast Guard officers, meanwhile, are gathering intel on Bailey. One of them talks to a local charter captain who says Bailey’s had a history of substance abuse and has spent some time in rehab in the last eight months.

Back on the water, a small crew boards a Coast Guard response boat to intercept the Double Marker. The vessel is roughly the length of a humpback whale and has an aluminum pilot house topped with an array of radio and navigation system antennas.

The crew suspects that Bailey will head through Longboat Pass north of Sarasota Bay, but an updated phone trace from the 911 dispatcher places him in Big Sarasota Pass a dozen miles to the south, directly across the twinkling lights of the marina. The crew navigates through an intercoastal waterway and positions the boat below the John Ringling Bridge for the stakeout.

About an hour later, Tracie calls the Coast Guard station again for an update. As she talks to the officer, he interrupts the call. “Hang on a second,” he says.

A colleague at the station is trying to tell him something.

Against a muffled background, Tracie hears the officer say to his colleague, “What?? There’s a gun?”

Tracie gasps. The officer hangs up.

The 911 dispatcher, meanwhile, is still on the line with Chris Jr. She continues to transmit his GPS coordinates to the Coast Guard as the Double Marker keeps moving. “Let me know if you see any channel markers,” she says. Chris Jr. looks out and doesn’t see any.

“Are you currently safe and out of danger?” she asks.

“I don’t think I’m in danger at the moment,” he says tentatively. But he tells her he’s sickly worried about the threat Bailey made of having some guys waiting for them back at the pier.

“I just don’t want to have any people waiting while we’re walking back to our cars,” he says. “It’s a really weird fucking scenario.”

“They’re trained. They’re not gonna put you in any danger,” the dispatcher tries to assure him.

The response boat waits for the Double Marker underneath the bridge as a fleet of police cruisers pull out of the Sarasota Police Department downtown and head straight for Marina Jack’s. When they arrive, a few officers go right to Bailey’s slip and take cover behind another fishing boat. Another positions himself behind the fuel docks in case Bailey decides to moor the boat there.

Back under the bridge, the Coast Guard crew finally spots a fishing boat entering the pass. As it nears, they see a shirtless guy with long blond hair up on the fly bridge working the throttles. It’s Bailey. Before maneuvering the response boat in his wake, the crew decides to go rogue and turn off the vessel lights to avoid the chances of spooking him back into the Gulf.

They keep their distance as they shadow the Double Marker furtively through the black water of Sarasota Bay all the way to Marina Jack’s pier. Bailey approaches a slip, bumps the gears into reverse and backs the boat in, as if he’s just coming in from a normal day at sea. As if nothing ever happened at all.

“Looks like all the units have arrived,” the dispatcher tells Chris Jr.

“They’re tying off the boat right now,” he reports back.

“Don’t approach anybody,” she warns him, as a symphony of cell phone alerts and boat chimes ring in the background. Chris relays her message to the group. “Hey, just stay calm.” A few of the guys are already trying to exit the cabin. “Hey, sit down. Just come over here,” he instructs them.

The hostages wait inside the cabin as a dozen Sarasota police officers and Coast Guard agents, with guns drawn, wait on the concrete dock. It’s 1 a.m. — nearly 18 hours after the charter had gotten underway.

Finally, it’s showtime. Bailey climbs down from the fly deck, exits the boat and surrenders. Officers handcuff him and stuff him inside one of the cruisers. He has “watery bloodshot eyes and slurred speech,” notes one of the police officers.

From inside the cruiser, Bailey kicks the rear door. He looks up at an officer and laughs. Then he puts his hands on the back door window to block the door from being closed until a few other officers come over to contain him. He’s brought to the Sarasota County Jail and arrested.

The hostages, meanwhile, wait around on the dock until Federal Coast Guard agents arrive from Tampa. They give their statements and, finally, are free to go.

Chris and his dad walk cautiously through the marina parking lot, climb into the pickup, lock the doors and drive straight back to the house where the entire Giuffre family has gathered for their return. It’s 6:30 a.m. The sun’s coming up as they arrive at what looks like a vigil being held in the kitchen. Family members sip coffee and embrace one another, trying to shake off the horror of what’s happened. The baby is asleep upstairs, and Fernanda greets Chris Jr. as soon as he walks in. They hold each other for 10 minutes straight without saying a word.

Needless to say, that’s the last time any of the five hostages go fishing with Captain Mark Bailey. In the weeks following the incident, his charter captain’s license is revoked, his boat gets the boot from Marina Jack’s and the Feds begin building a criminal case against him. Meanwhile, the news media plasters his menacing mugshot everywhere with headlines like, “Blow, booze, guns, and hostages: Florida charter boat captain arrested.” (First Mate Kissell was never accused of any wrongdoing.)

Several months later, Bailey is found dead in his apartment.

The life-or-death hostage situation on the Double Marker becomes a hot topic on fishing forums like thehulltruth.com, where commenters brazenly chime in on what they would have done.

“I would have climbed up that tower and went Chuck Norris on his azz,” writes one. “My foot would have been so far up his ass you’d smell my feet on his breath.” Another posted: “I could have disarmed him with a fishing pole from 30 feet away, once cast, the weight of the sinker would have wrapped around the gun and I would have just jerked it out of his hand, as the gun flew through the air, I would have caught it and then been in control of it. All in under 3 seconds.”

The guys who were actually on the boat are more concerned with putting the whole thing behind them.

When the Giuffres look back on that day, they’re reminded of how delicate a situation they were in. Chris Sr. says that if Bailey had come down the ladder again, or if any of them had gone up, “it would have been a much different result.” Calmer heads prevailed, and having lived through the experience has “made everything good that much better.”

Chris Jr. is still amazed that “something as simple as a fishing trip could be your last day.” Today, he spends as much time as he can with his growing family and tries to keep conflicts with Fernanda and others as short-lived as possible. “Nothing’s that serious,” he says.

And he still hits the Gulf to fish once in a while. A few of his buddies have boats, but since that day, he hasn’t stepped foot on a fishing charter.

“Just don’t really want to risk it,” he says.
