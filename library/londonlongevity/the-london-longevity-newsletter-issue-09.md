## **RESEARCH SPOTLIGHT** 🧬

-   **Are we aging because of cancer resistance?**  
    The evolution of cancer and ageing: a history of constraint - A new review traces the evolutionary tug-of-war between aging and cancer, showing how these two seemingly opposite processes are deeply intertwined. While cancer stems from runaway cell growth, aging reflects cell loss and decline. Because cancer threatens survival even in youth, natural selection prioritizes tumor resistance over late-life maintenance. The catch is that cancer-suppressing mechanisms like telomere shortening and cellular senescence limit cell proliferation, protect against tumours early, but drive tissue degeneration later. In this view, longevity itself may be constrained by the evolutionary need to keep cancer in check, making aging, at least partly, the price we pay for tumour resistance. **[Read More](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41568-025-00861-4)**
    

-   **Brain editing might now be closer to reality**
    
    Human genome editing has been working for disease causing mutations in the liver, blood, and eye, but the brain has so far been considered a difficult target. A flurry of breakthroughs in mice is bringing the dream of genome editing in the brain closer than ever. Researchers have used advanced CRISPR-inspired technologies like prime editing and base editing to correct mutations associated with devastating neurological disorders like alternating hemiplegia of childhood, Huntington’s disease, epilepsy and intellectual disabilities. In one study, prime editing fixed the AHC mutation across nearly half of the mouse cortex, easing seizures, improving cognitive and motor function, and extending lifespans. Other edited mice showed restored social behaviors and neural connectivity. While challenges such as targeting the brain’s defenses remain, these results in mice hint that human trials may be closer than expected. **[Read More](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-02578-8)**

-   **Could iron be a factor in driving brain aging?**  
    A new study identifies ferritin light chain 1 (FTL1), an iron-associated protein, as a surprising pro-aging factor in the brain. Researchers found that FTL1 accumulates in hippocampal neurons of old mice, with levels tightly linked to cognitive decline. When young mice were engineered to mimic this FTL1 buildup, their brains showed altered iron oxidation, synaptic changes, and premature cognitive aging. Crucially, silencing FTL1 in the hippocampi of old mice reversed synaptic dysfunction and improved memory. Further RNA-seq analysis revealed disruptions in energy metabolism, and supplementing NADH partially rescued the damage. The findings position FTL1 as a potential molecular driver of age-related cognitive impairment and a potential new target for brain rejuvenation. **[Read More](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43587-025-00940-z)**

-   **Do different brain cells age on their own clocks?**  
    By analyzing nearly 74,000 single nuclei from human prefrontal cortex tissue spanning ages 18–94, researchers have built **cell-type-specific transcriptomic aging clocks** for the brain. These clocks revealed distinct aging signatures. Microglia in older brains showed heightened inflammatory gene activity, while other cell types followed different molecular aging trajectories. The models not only tracked chronological age with high accuracy but also held up in independent datasets. Strikingly, individuals with Alzheimer’s disease or schizophrenia showed accelerated aging in certain cell types, pointing to altered brain-specific aging pathways in disease. The work highlights how zooming into cell-specific clocks could unravel why some neurons and glia are more vulnerable and open up new avenues for neurodegeneration research. **[Read More](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40878446/)**
