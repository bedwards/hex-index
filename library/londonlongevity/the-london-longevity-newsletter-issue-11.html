RESEARCH SPOTLIGHT 🧬

  • Huntington’s disease treated for first time using gene therapy
    A landmark early trial shows that a single dose of a gene therapy can slow Huntington’s disease progression by up to 75% over three years, marking the first treatment to alter the course of this fatal neurodegenerative disorder. Developed by uniQure, the therapy delivers a viral vector carrying microRNA into the striatum, where it silences the mutant huntingtin gene and lowers toxic protein levels. Participants tolerated the procedure well, though the required brain surgery remains invasive. The promising results, if replicated, could usher in a new era for gene-based neurology, where permanent molecular “muzzles” replace lifelong symptom management, but questions of durability, safety, and access linger as costs are expected to exceed $1 million per patient currently.   • Can ageing clocks guide cancer immunotherapy?
    A new review connects cellular senescence, the stress-induced, permanent cell-cycle arrest that shapes both tumour suppression and progression, with the rise of ageing clocks that quantify biological rather than chronological age. The authors trace how senescent cells, through the p53/p21 and p16/Rb axes and their pro-inflammatory secretome (SASP), can remodel the tumor microenvironment, alternately activating immune surveillance or promoting immune escape. They outline how epigenetic, transcriptomic, proteomic, and metabolomic ageing clocks capture these molecular shifts across tissues, refining our understanding of biological aging and cancer susceptibility. Integrating these metrics into oncology could personalise immunotherapy for older patients, identifying when senescent stromal niches foster resistance and when senolytics or clock-guided interventions might restore immune competence. The review closes with the challenges of multi-omics standardisation and the ethical frontiers of measuring biological age, proposing that ageing clocks may become a unifying framework linking geroscience to precision cancer care. Read More

  • Is ageing an unfinished wound?
    This perspective reimagines ageing as a chronic, never-resolving tissue injury. Drawing parallels between the inflammatory phase of wound healing and the cellular hallmarks of ageing such as inflammation, immune infiltration, lipid accumulation, and senescence, the author argues that aged organs remain trapped in an early “damage response” state. Molecular and morphological overlaps between injury and ageing support this view, from elevated IL-6 and TNF-α signaling to extracellular matrix remodeling and fibrosis. Intriguingly, interventions that extend lifespan, like mTOR inhibition or caloric restriction, also delay healing, suggesting shared regulatory circuits. The framework proposes that aging could be alleviated either by suppressing maladaptive injury responses or by inducing true regeneration, reframing the biology of ageing as a failure of tissues to transition from inflammation to repair.
