Last week, when I listed a few favorite new books, a reader asked if I had any more suggestions and I offered up an old list published almost twenty years ago. I promised a longer list….

This is hardly comprehensive. Since I’m in Italy I can’t even go through my bookshelves. So off the top of my head and in no particular order these are a few of the many, many food books that mean a lot to me.

Please let me know which books are important to you.

**[The Jemima Code: Two Centuries of African American Cookbooks](https://www.oah.org/tah/other-content/the-jemima-code-an-interview-with-toni-tipton-martin/#:~:text=Throughout%20much%20of%20the%20nineteenth,kitchen%20instincts%3B%20%5Bthe%20code%5D).** Toni Tipton-Martin has the most amazing cookbook collection. Here she presents the books in chronological order and virtually rewrites the history of Black cooks in America.

**[The Third Plate.](https://www.amazon.com/Third-Plate-Field-Notes-Future/dp/0143127152)** I think Dan Barber has the most interesting food mind of anyone writing - or cooking - today. Essential reading.

**[Our Lady of Perpetual Hunger](https://www.amazon.com/Our-Lady-Perpetual-Hunger-Memoir/dp/0525560947) by Lisa Donovan.** “Stop letting men tell your story,” the indomitable Diana Kennedy once told Lisa Donovan. So Lisa took on the male-dominated kitchen - and triumphed. Her bad-ass memoir is right up there with Blood, Bones and Butter.

**[Hungry](https://www.amazon.com/Hungry-Eating-Road-Tripping-Risking-Greatest/dp/1524759643) by Jeff Gordinier**. One of our finest food writers finds himself caught up in the crazy orbit of one of the world’s most fascinating chefs. Whatever you think you know about Rene Redzepi - this will tell you so much more.

**[Dirt](https://www.amazon.com/Dirt-Adventures-Training-Looking-Cooking/dp/0307455807/ref=pd_lpo_d_sccl_1/142-4141586-2305826?pd_rd_w=SaJ2w&content-id=amzn1.sym.4c8c52db-06f8-4e42-8e56-912796f2ea6c&pf_rd_p=4c8c52db-06f8-4e42-8e56-912796f2ea6c&pf_rd_r=GFC6GFPBCN9NB2PGEZJH&pd_rd_wg=99Mh4&pd_rd_r=2381272e-e44e-48d5-9ff2-bbe8cf5d4fb6&pd_rd_i=0307455807&psc=1) by Bill Buford.** Bill has such an interesting mind…. I love everything he writes. But his description of working in the restaurants of Lyon makes you understand the origin of all the bad behavior in professional kitchens.

**[Everything is Under Control](https://www.amazon.com/Everything-Under-Control-Memoir-Recipes/dp/0374150141).** In beautiful prose Phyllis Grant (poet and granddaughter of the painter Richard Diebenkorn), reimagines the memoir. And the recipes are fantastic.

**[What’s Good?](https://www.amazon.com/Whats-Good-Memoir-Fourteen-Ingredients/dp/1419747622)** [](https://www.amazon.com/Whats-Good-Memoir-Fourteen-Ingredients/dp/1419747622)Peter Hoffman has always cooked to his own inner music. The opposite of the bad boys chefs, he was probably the first New York chef to start every day at the Union Square farmers’ market.

**[We Are Each Other’s Harvest](https://www.amazon.com/Are-Each-Others-Harvest-Celebrating/dp/006293256X)** by Natalie Baszile. The author of Queen Sugar celebrates African American farmers.

**[Invitation to a Banquet](https://www.amazon.com/Invitation-Banquet-Story-Chinese-Food/dp/B0CLWG3BS4/ref=sr_1_1?adgrpid=190238690070&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.XqQ5WFtfcQ7pVm0Yk23QHx5PnfRkBDj_0thHupUMLgvGjHj071QN20LucGBJIEps.hWbgZM7FYRibcjQBGG0nzhetfx083TDlDTrNcWW2piI&dib_tag=se&hvadid=779578491108&hvdev=c&hvexpln=0&hvlocint=9004077&hvlocphy=9203410&hvnetw=g&hvocijid=8635037718166199574--&hvqmt=e&hvrand=8635037718166199574&hvtargid=aud-2443140925601%3Akwd-2410273708956&hydadcr=16211_13842734_8120&keywords=dunlop+invitation+to+a+banquet&mcid=ae798f89313f33c593efa8e299956d65&qid=1763053898&sr=8-1)** by Fuschia Dunlop. A book about the history of Chinese food by our best contemporary Chinese cookbook writer. I’ve admired all of Fuschia’s work, but she’s outdone herself here. [Here’s a link to](http://www.gourmet.com.s3-website-us-east-1.amazonaws.com/magazine/2000s/2005/08/frenchlaundry.html) my favorite of the many articles Fuchsia wrote for Gourmet.

**[Fast Food Nation](https://www.amazon.com/Fast-Food-Nation-Dark-All-American/dp/0547750331)** [](https://www.amazon.com/Fast-Food-Nation-Dark-All-American/dp/0547750331)by Eric Schlosser. The subtitle is “the dark side of the All-American meal” which pretty much says it all. Written 25 years ago, this was a wake-up call to the nation.

**[The Omnivore’s Dilemma](https://www.amazon.com/Omnivores-Dilemma-Natural-History-Meals/dp/0143038583/ref=sr_1_1?crid=36S6E2KI0NOW4&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.SB2jaEtAGjI1YOKVeXxyCiCqXruWTF0fLQahGFRO66fuPmW9YPXRot9UpFYA6MD3OIHr2hpEkBBaZIleOvuwMshC_0YfhBMIaVIrhB_rPNLnYCMa5JDppn-MYOUHo52gtdn3xei2qXZ70lYuv6QCvoYsIC7_2nklgICi3E3hbjJ5HCwFnYqOpCPdpqOvCpWQ6ocPU_OgEkgXt49lhk2W0E4U9o9IONoSbca-BCEu1pQ.7No_cdXgGTR4q9kUEZ92n8IwQdZfiQvFPYR8fX7T3cM&dib_tag=se&keywords=The+Omnivore%27s+dilemma&qid=1763103865&s=books&sprefix=the+omnivore%27s+dilemma%2Cstripbooks%2C219&sr=1-1)** by Michael Pollan. The most important, and most influential book about food of our era.

**[Tomatoland.](https://www.amazon.com/Tomatoland-Industrial-Agriculture-Destroyed-Alluring/dp/1449423450)** Barry Estabrook’s seminal book about what agribusiness has wrought. Barry, like Michael Pollan, is one of the very few writers who can turn a serious political food issue into a page turner.

**[The Dream Land: Chasing Water and Dust Across California](https://www.amazon.com/Dreamt-Land-Chasing-Across-California/dp/1101875208)** by Mark Arax. A shocking, anguished and beautifully written book about the insanity of American agricultural practices. Published in 2019, it has become even more relevant with the passing of time.

**[Animal, Vegetable, Miracle](https://www.amazon.com/Animal-Vegetable-Miracle-Tenth-Anniversary/dp/0062653059/ref=asc_df_0062653059/?tag=hyprod-20&linkCode=df0&hvadid=692875362841&hvpos=&hvnetw=g&hvrand=17457419753131039124&hvpone=&hvptwo=&hvqmt=&hvdev=c&hvdvcmdl=&hvlocint=&hvlocphy=9060351&hvtargid=pla-2281435177818&psc=1&mcid=8dd69f31036a3321af825a35cdf50e21&hvocijid=17457419753131039124-0062653059-&hvexpln=73) by Barbara Kingsolver.** The extraordinary novelist decided that she and her family were going to spend a year raising their food or getting it from neighbors. This eloquent book is the result.

**[Provence 1970](https://www.amazon.com/Provence-1970-M-F-K-Reinvention-American/dp/0307718352) by Luke Barr.** MFK Fisher’s great-nephew offers up an intimate look at the time when Fisher, James Beard, Richard Olney, Julia Child, Simone Beck and Judith Jones all ended up in the same place - and changed the way America eats. It’s a wonderful read.

**[The Gourmand’s Way](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=the+gourmands%27+way&adgrpid=186984563672&hvadid=779793880754&hvdev=c&hvexpln=0&hvlocint=9004077&hvlocphy=9203410&hvnetw=g&hvocijid=11058623656232889633--&hvqmt=e&hvrand=11058623656232889633&hvtargid=aud-2443140925601%3Akwd-392487244268&hydadcr=22537_13531293_8187&mcid=8e2a41dd71e33e17a4f86a3ab54caf63&tag=googhydr-20&ref=pd_sl_6pihy9p42o_e)** Justin Spring gives us a slightly different look at a very similar subject. I reviewed the book for the New York Times [here](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/11/22/books/review/gourmands-way-justin-spring-julia-child.html), and while I don’t always agree with Spring’s take on the various writers, I promise you will never look at Julia Child or Richard Olney in quite the same way.

**[My Place at the Table](https://www.amazon.com/My-Place-Table-Recipe-Delicious/dp/1328588831)** by Alexander Lobrano. Alec’s deeply honest memoir is a more contemporary look at an American in France. This one tells how a shy gay man from Connecticut ended up as the restaurant critic for a French newspaper.

**[The Oysters of Locmariaquer](https://www.amazon.com/Oysters-Locmariaquer-Eleanor-Clark/dp/0060887427).** Eleanor Clark was ahead of her time in 1964 when she considered the Belon oyster in this elegant mingling of history, science, nature and sociology. It won the National Book Award.

**[Mastering the Art of Soviet Cooking:](https://www.amazon.com/Mastering-Art-Soviet-Cooking-Longing/dp/0307886816) A Memoir of Food and Longing** by Anya Von Bremzen. A memoir, in recipes, of living in the Soviet Union and coming to America. Totally original and completely wonderful; it’s a book I find myself thinking about all the time.

**[Blood, Bones and Butter](https://www.amazon.com/Blood-Bones-Butter-audiobook/dp/B004Q3EZBC/ref=sr_1_1?adgrpid=186409712877&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.qsZ2KhZKcGjVyiFZmP-lMAO-tv3DVz-1hRP_E7ixC7mXcojGCMiaTHnTgR75UwWb5JikclMj8_-2YP7lnPdrwL6xNoJM0Pjp-DVnQUJer2tYg5Int6LHfjNqEBet2Q7r0pSzCY2TjbDXdIjvW3l2btE6x6G-KrOfSgz5voJLBEzPQCZ6EX9LqpNI86aC9wp8.vGrk51sefz0gQ_8oXYtW-DLJgmcxlwnaLwTViqZYHhs&dib_tag=se&hvadid=779551046781&hvdev=c&hvexpln=0&hvlocint=9004077&hvlocphy=9203410&hvnetw=g&hvocijid=6746710068294352029--&hvqmt=e&hvrand=6746710068294352029&hvtargid=aud-2443140925601%3Akwd-299398841413&hydadcr=22122_13323353_12426&keywords=blood+bones+and+butter&mcid=b6468853ccfb3cec9461e08c1d83769e&qid=1762790262&sr=8-1) by** Gabrielle Hamilton. In my opinion this is the best of the many, many wonderful chef memoirs out there. Gabrielle has a new memoir that’s just been released, **[Next of Kin](https://www.amazon.com/Next-Kin-Memoir-Gabrielle-Hamilton/dp/0399590099).** I haven’t read it yet, but I’m certainly looking forward to that.

**[Thirty-Two Yolks](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=32+yolks+by+eric+ripert&adgrpid=187208824020&hvadid=779625726882&hvdev=c&hvexpln=0&hvlocint=9004077&hvlocphy=9203410&hvnetw=g&hvocijid=12239218725397414388--&hvqmt=e&hvrand=12239218725397414388&hvtargid=aud-2443140925601%3Akwd-390712064090&hydadcr=22563_13730660_8252&mcid=a7efa5ab10ea3a6480524d662aafd362&tag=googhydr-20&ref=pd_sl_9o3ql84lmg_e)** by Eric Ripert. Tony Bourdain called this book “heartbreaking, horrifying, poignant and inspiring.” It’s all of that - and also a great introduction to our most beloved chef.

**[Eat Like a Fish](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=eat+like+a+fish&adgrpid=185689537665&hvadid=779717938487&hvdev=c&hvexpln=0&hvlocint=9004077&hvlocphy=9203410&hvnetw=g&hvocijid=14079814735023148985--&hvqmt=e&hvrand=14079814735023148985&hvtargid=aud-2443140925601%3Akwd-697653150850&hydadcr=16154_13517808_9060&mcid=9b63b8aab9263d07be5f113c522641f2&tag=googhydr-20&ref=pd_sl_3wpfcos0jm_e) by** Bren Smith. Smith was born in Newfoundland and became a commercial fisherman while still in his teens. He lived hard and loved it. Then he became a restorative ocean farmer, set up a nonprofit to teach others how to do it and won the $2 million Food Planet Prize for initiatives revolutionizing food production. Part memoir, part manifesto, this is an inspiring book.

**[Cod, A Biography of a Fish that Changed the World](https://www.amazon.com/Cod-Biography-Fish-Changed-World/dp/0140275010/ref=sr_1_1?adgrpid=186568886779&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.Gh5nTn3wO5b_LAfjzRn5U9lJwkufLVU0UMqUlA72_SfK9CZfFv_Yo5JIa1t5PK1ROioSC4_8SfVdIaJF1aZmmHWXQ0BwbuOWFchRKU9Y38KpHPdL3qQLZxZ4gQhER5w6SLPyn74b7IJ651MNKrwZzeCyap6VFBG7jBA29j3zl1cCerf_sgOBltlqlKGuj7e7iS48--6u99STHoqshRCRi7ghQDbf5q9gPPK0AicJbG8.y9Ry3wommKgZTsGI2iLTGnO_yPHaGkmzFI-DNZRz1uQ&dib_tag=se&hvadid=779649665681&hvdev=c&hvexpln=0&hvlocint=9004077&hvlocphy=9203410&hvnetw=g&hvocijid=2488827073084530567--&hvqmt=e&hvrand=2488827073084530567&hvtargid=aud-2443140925601%3Akwd-323948759434&hydadcr=3172_13534105_11286&keywords=cod+kurlansky&mcid=f841d2762252389ca805ee3917e74f9e&qid=1763058504&s=books&sr=1-1)**. How do you choose a single book by the prolific Mark Kurlansky? He’s written definitive treatises on dozens of subjects - and has still more books in the works. This is a particular favorite.

**[Four Fish](https://www.amazon.com/Four-Fish-Future-Last-Wild/dp/014311946X/ref=pd_bxgy_d_sccl_1/132-1341512-8472846?pd_rd_w=Ox3E6&content-id=amzn1.sym.dcf559c6-d374-405e-a13e-133e852d81e1&pf_rd_p=dcf559c6-d374-405e-a13e-133e852d81e1&pf_rd_r=YYVMYGZHH6FXVDEH3MPM&pd_rd_wg=spzDk&pd_rd_r=1715143a-cd3b-416a-847d-013e3c8e5e05&pd_rd_i=014311946X&psc=1)** by Paul Greenberg. Speaking of fish….If you want to understand what’s been happening in the American seafood industry you need to read this book.

**[Feed the Resistance](https://www.amazon.com/Feed-Resistance-Recipes-Getting-Involved/dp/1452168385/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&qid=1513693004&sr=8-1&keywords=feed+the+resistance)** by Julia Turshen is the perfect present for people who understand that cooking is a political act. A handbook for social activism, this little book is also filled with easy and unusual recipes you really want to make.

There’s never been a book quite like Michael Twitty’s **[The Cooking Gene.](https://www.amazon.com/Cooking-Gene-Journey-American-Culinary/dp/0062379291/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&qid=1513690868&sr=8-1&keywords=the+cooking+gene)** History belongs to those who choose to write it and it’s high time African-American cooks set the record straight. Here Twitty chronicles his own ancestral food journey with remarkable results.

**[Unforgettable](https://www.amazon.com/Unforgettable-Flavors-Paula-Wolferts-Renegade/dp/1538729881/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&qid=1513690994&sr=8-1&keywords=unforgettable+paula+wolfert)**. Paula Wolfert has the best palate of anyone I’ve ever met. She’s been one of America’s most influential cooks and has rarely received the attention she deserves. Her greatest recipes are here, along with a loving biography of one of our most fearless, adventurous and intrepid cooks.

**[Offal Good](https://www.amazon.com/Offal-Good-Cooking-Heart-Guts/dp/0770435122)** by Chris Cosentino. I’m not generally a fan of chef’s cookbook, but here is an unusual book written straight from the heart. Chris has always been a fan of the fifth quarter - the unloved, and mostly unused parts of animals. This guide to cooking heart, tripe, oxtails, tongue, kidneys and the like would be a great gift for an adventurous cook.

**[The Alice B. Toklas Cookbook](https://www.amazon.com/Alice-B-Toklas-Cook-Book/dp/0063043807).** Written in 1954 and still stunning. There’s never been anything quite like this book which has a chapter called “Murder in the Kitchen.” (I should probably mention that MFK Fisher wrote the original introduction to the book and I added another to the latest edition.)

**[Vibration Cooking](https://ugapress.org/book/9780820337395/vibration-cooking/)**[,](https://ugapress.org/book/9780820337395/vibration-cooking/) **[or The Travel Notes of a Geechee Girl](https://ugapress.org/book/9780820337395/vibration-cooking/)** [](https://ugapress.org/book/9780820337395/vibration-cooking/)by Vertamae Smart- Grosvenor. I bought this book when it came out in 1970 and read it cover to cover in one sitting. It’s one of my favorite books, and it makes me sad that it isn’t better known.

**[The Kitchen Diaries](https://www.abebooks.com/servlet/BookDetailsPL?bi=31448093320&dest=usa&ref_=ps_ggl_18382194370&cm_mmc=ggl-_-US_Shopp_Trade0to10-_-product_id=COM9780007241156USED-_-keyword=&gclid=CjwKCAjw3624BhBAEiwAkxgTOtIfBeWxjgSG4-LR4WtAlyFitV4vpMBiNt-I8xiUau_VpRRCUielFxoCOnUQAvD_BwE) by Nigel Slater.** I don’t think anybody has ever made food sound more delicious. When Slater describes a beet it comes alive on the page and you want to run out and eat as many beets as you possibly can.

***Food Novels

Speaking of books… I’ve been reading an advance copy of Luke Barr’s fascinating new book, [The Secret History of French Cooking](https://www.amazon.com/Secret-History-French-Cooking-Outlaw/dp/1524744735), and I’m learning much I didn’t know. About the rise and fall of *nouvelle cuisine,* about Paul Bocuse, about the extreme misogyny of the French kitchen - and about the shocking, long-hidden collaborationist past of France’s most powerful restaurant critic Robert Courtine (who wrote for Le Monde).

I was amused to discover that the name Yanou Collart kept popping up in the book. I’ve had a few encounters with Yanou, so I looked through my files to see what I’d saved and found this from 1986.

[Collart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yanou_Collart) is a remarkable creature: an actress, she was also a proto-foodie who turned herself into a public relations person for famous chefs. This worked because Yanou knew absolutely everyone. I remember the meal as a delicious star-studded production. To my delight I found myself sitting down with Barbra Streisand, Bette Midler and Danny Kaye…to name just a few. Here’s [my article](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1986-07-27-ca-1327-story.html) about the dinner.

I think the table favors were Hermes bracelets. Can that be right? I remember it mostly because I had never seen one before, and when I offered it to a friend she replied, “Do you have any idea what that thing is worth?”
