The One Pseudo-Argument

To their credit, the Fox News article (written by Madison Colombo) did attempt something resembling an intellectual response by citing the academic whose research my video draws from. Professor Kyna Hamill, they noted, has “repeatedly said her work is being misrepresented.” The implication is clear: even the scholar behind the research disagrees with people like me. Case closed. Sing your Christmas carols in peace.

There is just one problem. The quote they cite comes from a 2017 Boston Herald article, and if you actually read that article (which Fox’s writers apparently did not expect anyone to do, and probably didn’t do themselves), you discover something extremely ironic. Hamill was not responding to people like me. She was responding to conservatives, specifically to a Breitbart article that carried almost the exact same headline as Fox’s current coverage: claiming she had declared “Jingle Bells” a racist song that should be shunned.

For more context, Hamill faced severe backlash after her research was first published, receiving hate mail, online harassment, and threats serious enough that she filed a report with university police. In her first interview after the attacks, she told CBC News that her work had been “absolutely misreported or reported very irresponsibly” by right-leaning outlets, including Fox News and Breitbart. She was not walking back her findings. She was defending herself against the exact same bad-faith smear campaign that Fox is now running against me.

In other words, Fox News just cited an interview in which Hamill defended herself against conservative misrepresentation of her research, and then used it to further misrepresent her research. They weaponized her defense against one right-wing smear to launch another right-wing smear.

What Hamill Actually Said

Fox’s article quotes Hamill saying, “I never said it was racist now,” and “At no point have I ever made a claim on what people should or should not sing at Christmas.” These quotes are accurate, but they are deeply misleading without the context Fox conveniently omitted.

In that same Boston Herald article—in the very same interview—Hamill also said this: “In 1857 when it was performed in blackface, that is white men blackening up with burnt cork on their faces, it would have been racist. This performance tradition is historical fact and continued in the U.S. until the 1930s as an amateur entertainment.”

She doubled down on her research and confirmed the blackface origins as “historical fact.” She did not backtrack; she clarified. And there is a difference, one that Fox either failed to grasp or deliberately obscured.

To make it crystal clear: Hamill’s position, and mine, is straightforward: the song has racist origins in blackface minstrelsy. This is documented, factual, and not a matter of opinion. What people choose to do with that information, whether they keep singing the song or not, is up to them. Neither Hamill nor I have ever prescribed what should happen to the modern carol. We simply reported where it came from.

What I Actually Said

Watch my video again or read my article. You will not find me calling the modern song itself racist, and you will not find me demanding it be banned or shunned. What you will find, however, is a history lesson. James Lord Pierpont wrote minstrel songs, “One Horse Open Sleigh” debuted in blackface at a minstrel hall, the song’s themes aligned with racist comedic routines of the era, and Pierpont later fought for the Confederacy. It’s all laid out in peer-reviewed research.

Pierpont’s other songs, titles like “The Colored Coquette” and “Kitty Crow,” used slurs and dialect to caricature Black Americans, and his career in minstrelsy is a matter of public record, preserved in primary source documents. But because conservatives cannot dispute these facts, they invented a straw man. They claimed I said the modern song is racist and that I want to cancel Christmas carols, manufacturing an argument I never made so they would have something to attack.
