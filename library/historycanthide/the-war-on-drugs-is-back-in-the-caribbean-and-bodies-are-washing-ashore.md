The first body washed up on a beach in Trinidad with burn marks across its face and limbs torn off by an explosion. Days later, another corpse appeared nearby. It was so mangled that villagers couldn’t recognize the face, and its right leg was gone. Vultures circled overhead as locals tried to make sense of what they were seeing.

No one knows who these men were, and no government has claimed them. Trinidad’s forensic center couldn’t perform autopsies on bodies too decomposed to identify. Funeral homes near the capital now store the corpses, unclaimed and unnamed, while officials refuse to investigate further.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth posts grainy drone footage of boats exploding into flames on social media like highlight reels, which they claim counts as proof.

Trinidad’s beaches have become dumping grounds for America’s undeclared war.

### The Current U.S. Military Campaign

The U.S. has deployed an aircraft carrier, guided-missile destroyers, amphibious assault ships, drones, and thousands of troops to the region, in what amounts to the largest American military deployment in Latin America in decades. The strikes have killed people in clusters, from two to fourteen in each operation.

Trump announced the first strike on September 2, claiming the boat belonged to Tren de Aragua, a Venezuelan gang he designated as a terrorist organization. He posted footage of the explosion on Truth Social with the caption: “Let this serve as notice to anybody even thinking about bringing drugs into the United States of America. BEWARE!” That strike killed eleven people.

The Pentagon later admitted in congressional briefings that they couldn’t definitively prove the victims belonged to Tren de Aragua or confirm where the boat was headed. CNN reported the vessel appeared to have turned around before the military hit it, but the strikes continued anyway.

In October, the administration told Congress that the U.S. now considers itself in “armed conflict” with drug cartels, labeling those killed “unlawful combatants.” The designation is identical to what the government used for terrorism suspects at Guantanamo Bay. It allows the military to kill people without trial, presenting evidence in court, or seeking Congressional approval.

International law experts say these strikes violate the U.N. Convention on the Law of the Sea, which prohibits countries from attacking vessels in international waters except in cases of immediate self-defense. Professor Michael Becker of Trinity College Dublin told BBC Verify that describing people as narco-terrorists “does not transform them into lawful military targets. The US is not engaged in an armed conflict with Venezuela or the Tren de Aragua criminal organisation.”

The United Nations human rights chief called on the U.S. to halt the attacks and “prevent the extrajudicial killing of people aboard these boats.” Colombia’s president accused America of murdering a Colombian fisherman in one of the strikes. Trump responded by cutting off all aid to Colombia.

Trinidad’s Prime Minister Kamla Persad-Bissessar has taken the opposite approach, fully backing the strikes. “I much prefer seeing drug and gun traffickers blown to pieces than seeing hundreds of our citizens murdered each year because of drug-fueled gang violence,” she told reporters. She’s refused to use state resources to search for victims or investigate the bodies washing up on her country’s beaches, so the corpses pile up in funeral homes as the U.S. military continues bombing boats at sea.

### The War on Drugs as Justification for Military Action

The U.S. has been using drug enforcement to justify military action in Latin America for over 40 years. President George H.W. Bush launched Operation Just Cause in 1989, invading Panama with 27,000 troops under the stated goal of arresting Manuel Noriega on drug trafficking charges. Noriega had spent years on the CIA payroll before Bush decided he needed to be removed. The invasion killed between 300 and 3,000 Panamanian civilians, though the U.S. never bothered establishing an accurate count. Bush declared victory in the War on Drugs while Panamanians lived under occupation.

The Reagan administration had already set this precedent in Nicaragua during the 1980s, arming and funding the Contras while publicly framing the support as necessary to combat drug trafficking. The CIA helped the Contras smuggle cocaine into the U.S. to fund their operations, as investigative journalist Gary Webb documented in his “Dark Alliance” series. When Webb’s reporting proved accurate, the U.S. government destroyed his career and credibility.

“Plan Colombia” was an initiative in 2000: a $10 billion U.S. aid package that officials marketed as an effort to combat drug cartels. The money primarily funded the Colombian military’s war against leftist guerrillas. Coca production didn’t decline but simply relocated to other countries, while the U.S. achieved its actual objectives of expanded military presence in South America and a friendly right-wing government in Bogotá.

The strategy repeats itself consistently. The U.S. identifies a Latin American government it wants to pressure or remove from power, then claims that government facilitates drug trafficking. Military assets deploy under the banner of drug enforcement while serving broader geopolitical goals like securing resources or projecting American power throughout the region.

Venezuela has been in Washington’s crosshairs for years now, and the Trump administration has stopped pretending otherwise. A $50 million reward hangs over President Nicolás Maduro’s head for information leading to his arrest. The CIA now operates covertly inside the country, and a naval blockade disguised as anti-drug enforcement surrounds Venezuelan waters.

Venezuela isn’t even a major cocaine producer. Colombia, Peru, and Bolivia all dwarf its output. Venezuela does, however, hold the world’s largest proven oil reserves and maintains a government the U.S. wants gone. The drugs provide cover for regime change.

### The Grenada Model for Caribbean Intervention

President Reagan ordered the invasion of Grenada in October 1983, sending troops to a tiny Caribbean island nation with a population of 91,000. The official justification centered on rescuing American medical students supposedly endangered by a Marxist coup. Reagan actually wanted to overthrow Grenada’s leftist government and prevent what he called a “Soviet-Cuban colony” from establishing itself in the Caribbean.

Nearly 8,000 American troops invaded an island defended by roughly 1,500 people, including Cuban construction workers building an airport. The U.S. military won within four days, losing 19 American soldiers while killing at least 45 Grenadian civilians.

Reagan called it a “rescue mission,” but the United Nations General Assembly voted 108-9 to condemn it as a violation of international law. The U.S. vetoed a similar resolution in the Security Council and declared victory anyway.

Grenada proved we don’t need to call something a war to wage one. Congressional approval becomes unnecessary if we frame military action as a rescue operation or counterterrorism measure. We deploy overwhelming force and win quickly, and international criticism arrives too late to matter because our troops have already secured the objective.

The current boat strikes are happening in the same Caribbean waters where the U.S. invaded Grenada 42 years ago. The U.S. is counting on the same outcome as Grenada: that by the time the international community organizes a response, the mission will already be accomplished.

### The Precedent These Strikes Establish

If the U.S. can kill dozens of people at sea based on “classified intelligence”, other countries will adopt the same approach. China could bomb fishing boats in the South China Sea while claiming they smuggle fentanyl precursors. Russia could strike vessels in the Black Sea under allegations of arms trafficking to Ukraine. Any government with a navy and a convenient justification could begin executing people in international waters without trial. International law erodes gradually through moments like this one, when a powerful country decides the rules don’t apply and other nations prove too afraid or too complicit to object.

The two unidentified bodies in Trinidad demonstrate how this erosion works in practice. The U.S. kills people based on secret intelligence, refuses to identify victims, and leaves a small Caribbean nation to handle the corpses. Trinidad’s government won’t investigate because their officials fear crossing Trump. Venezuela can’t investigate because the military destroys the boats in international waters, and the families of the dead have nowhere to turn, assuming they even know their relatives are missing.

The War on Drugs has always functioned as a weapon. At home, it feeds mass incarceration targeting poor communities. Abroad, it justifies military intervention in Latin American countries. Sixty-seven people are dead, but we don’t know their names, whether they were trafficking drugs, or if they posed any genuine threat. We only know the U.S. military killed them based on intelligence the government refuses to share.

Villagers in Trinidad have reached the conclusion their government won’t acknowledge. These men died in a war. The real question is whether anyone with power will name it as such.
