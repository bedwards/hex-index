That’s it. That’s all she had to say.

She’s a grieving widow, and Charlie was a father to young children. His family deserves peace and respect, not rumors circulating online. On its face, that’s a reasonable request. Families deserve space to mourn without strangers dissecting their tragedy for content.

But here’s what Erika Kirk has never addressed: the years her husband spent weaving conspiracies about the deaths of Black Americans. He never extended that courtesy to several grieving families, and he helped make lies canon for millions of his followers. **Through it all, she stood by him.**

Conspiracies for me, it seems, but not for thee.

## The George Floyd Lie

On May 25, 2020, Minneapolis police officer Derek Chauvin killed George Floyd by kneeling on his neck for approximately nine minutes while Floyd pleaded that he couldn’t breathe. Bystander video captured the entire encounter. A jury later convicted Chauvin of second-degree unintentional murder, third-degree murder, and second-degree manslaughter. He also pleaded guilty to federal civil rights violations.

*Derek Chauvin, as he was killing George Floyd*

Two separate autopsies ruled Floyd’s death a homicide. The Hennepin County medical examiner concluded Floyd “experienced a cardiopulmonary arrest while being restrained by law enforcement officers.” The cause of death was “cardiopulmonary arrest complicating law enforcement subdual, restraint, and neck compression.” A second autopsy, commissioned by Floyd’s family, found he died of asphyxiation.

Neither autopsy said Floyd died of a drug overdose.

But that didn’t stop Charlie Kirk and his ideological allies from constructing an alternate reality. Candace Owens, once a Turning Point USA employee and the main target of Erika’s demands, became one of the main amplifiers of the overdose myth. Then-Fox News host Tucker Carlson told millions of viewers that Floyd “almost certainly died of a drug overdose.” The narrative spread like wildfire across right-wing media, despite being directly contradicted by the medical examiner’s own findings.

The medical examiner did note fentanyl in Floyd’s system. He also said, explicitly: “I am not saying this killed him.” But Charlie Kirk ran with the lie anyway. He platformed the overdose narrative, amplified it across his massive social media following, and helped transform a debunked conspiracy into gospel for millions of conservatives. Neither Erika nor Charlie asked anyone to “stop.”

Five years later, this lie remains central to calls for Chauvin’s pardon.

## The Breonna Taylor Smear

Breonna Taylor was a 26-year-old emergency room technician working at two Louisville hospitals when plainclothes police officers broke down her apartment door at 12:40 a.m. on March 13, 2020. They were executing a search warrant as part of a narcotics investigation. Her boyfriend, Kenneth Walker, fired one shot, believing intruders were breaking in. Police fired 32 rounds in response. Taylor was struck six times and died in her hallway.

No drugs were found in her apartment.

*EMT Breonna Taylor*

But the conservative media ecosystem, the one Kirk helped build, worked overtime to paint Taylor as complicit in her own death. Social media posts claimed she was “knee deep in criminal/drug dealing activities.” Others said she was “running drugs” for her ex-boyfriend. A viral meme listed supposed “truths” about Taylor that painted her as a criminal who deserved suspicion, if not what happened to her.

The facts tell a different story. Taylor had no drug offenses on her record. Neither did her sister, who lived with her. The detective who wrote the search warrant affidavit, Joshua Jaynes, was later fired for including inaccurate information. He claimed he’d verified through a postal inspector that Taylor’s ex-boyfriend was receiving packages at her address. The postal inspector said the opposite was true.

## She Was Right There

This is not solely about Charlie Kirk. Throughout his career, building Turning Point USA into a conservative powerhouse, Erika was right beside him.

“Who is more conservative and why?” Erika once read from a fan question during an appearance on The Charlie Kirk Show.

“Erika,” Charlie answered immediately. “By far. Not even close. I am a moderate compared to Erika.”

She laughed and said having children made her even more conservative, “which I didn’t think was possible.”

So when Charlie Kirk platformed lies about George Floyd’s death, Erika was there. When his organization’s associates smeared Breonna Taylor’s memory, Erika was there. When Turning Point USA became a machine for manufacturing the kind of disinformation that now haunts her own family, Erika was there, apparently more committed to the cause than her husband.

Now she leads the organization, unanimously elected CEO and Chair of the Board. The statement announcing her appointment promised they would “not surrender or kneel before evil.” They would make Charlie’s work “more powerful and enduring than ever before.”

## Why Canonization Failed

When Senator John McCain died in 2018, even his political opponents could acknowledge certain universal values he represented. During his 2008 presidential campaign, McCain famously corrected a supporter who called Barack Obama “an Arab,” taking the microphone to say Obama was “a decent family man” and that the woman had nothing to fear from him as president. You could disagree with McCain on policy and still recognize he drew lines around basic human dignity.

*McCain was a Republican conservative that led by example*

Charlie Kirk never drew those lines. His movement never espoused universal moral values that transcended partisan identity. Instead, it trafficked in division and selective outrage. Black families grieving their dead were met with conspiracy theories and character assassination.

Erika Kirk is entitled to her grief. Every widow deserves that. But she is not entitled to a selective memory about what her husband built and what she helped him build. The families of George Floyd and Breonna Taylor never got a viral interview asking the world to stop lying about their loved ones. They got [memes](https://historycanthide.substack.com/p/white-supremacists-built-an-internet) and pundits calling their children drug dealers and criminals.

“Stop”, Erika Kirk says. Some families have been saying that for years. She never listened.
