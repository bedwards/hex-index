I recently traveled to El Salvador to study Latin American history firsthand. The timing felt urgent. The US is once again intervening across the region, and back home, attacks against Central American migrants have intensified with almost no acknowledgment of *why* those migrants came here in the first place. Politicians talk about “invasion” and “infestation” while conveniently ignoring the decades of American foreign policy that destabilized entire nations and sent millions fleeing north.

So I went looking for answers. I found them in a forest, from a man with a machete.

Rafael Hernandez, known locally as Don Rafa, is 63 years old and serves as head park ranger at El Salvador’s Cinquera Forest. He has an easy laugh and spends his days clearing hiking trails for tourists. He looks like someone’s favorite uncle.

He also spent over a decade as a guerrilla commander fighting a US-funded military that dropped bombs on his village half a dozen times every day.

Here are seven things I learned from his testimony and my further research.

## 1\. The US hates left-wing leaders and loves right-wing dictators.

Rafael grew up in a country that functioned like a medieval fiefdom. A tiny oligarchy, often called “The Fourteen Families,” controlled virtually all arable land while the peasants who worked it earned pennies. His neighbors labored six days a week on someone else’s soil and still couldn’t afford doctors or schools for their children.

This arrangement had American blessing. Throughout the 1980s, Washington funneled roughly $1 million a day to El Salvador’s military government, eventually totaling around $6 billion. The official reason was to stop Communism. The practical effect was protecting coffee exports and the plantation owners who profited from them.

The pattern throughout the Cold War era (and the time shortly before and thereafter) was consistent: any leader who threatened to redistribute wealth downward was labeled a Communist threat. Any dictator who slaughtered people to keep wealth flowing upward was treated as a partner in stability. When Farabundo Martí suggested land should belong to those who farmed it, Washington branded him a dangerous radical. When General Maximiliano Hernández Martínez killed 30,000 indigenous people in 1932 to crush a peasant uprising, Washington hardly blinked because the coffee kept shipping and the plantation owners kept profiting.

This repeated globally. In Guatemala (1954), the CIA overthrew Jacobo Árbenz after land reform threatened United Fruit Company—the Dulles brothers who orchestrated it were former partners at United Fruit's law firm. In Chile (1973), the US spent $8 million destabilizing Allende's elected government before Pinochet's coup killed 3,000+ and tortured 30,000; Kissinger told the dictator, "You did a great service to the West." In Nicaragua, Washington backed the Somoza dynasty for 43 years, then organized the Contras from Somoza's former death squads. Reagan called them "the moral equal of the Founding Fathers." In Indonesia (1965-66), the US supported Suharto's massacre of up to one million suspected communists.

## 2\. Funding weapons was bad, but providing education was even deadlier.

In 1946, the United States opened a military training facility in the Panama Canal Zone, later renamed the School of the Americas, to train Latin American officers in “counterinsurgency”: intelligence gathering, interrogation, and suppressing popular uprisings. Over 57,700 officers passed through. Wherever graduates rose to power, mass graves followed. Roberto Viola (Argentina) was sentenced for the “Dirty War” that disappeared thousands. Hugo Banzer (Bolivia) had a regime that killed and exiled labor leaders. Graduates toppled governments, commanded death squads, and oversaw torture chambers across the hemisphere.

*Location 2 of the School of the Americas (in Fort Benning, Georgia)*

Nowhere was the impact more devastating than El Salvador. When the UN investigated human rights violations during the civil war, they identified 69 officers responsible for the worst abuses—48 were School of the Americas graduates. The school’s proudest Salvadoran creation, the Atlácatl Battalion, massacred over 800 villagers at El Mozote in 1981, most of them children. Eight years later, the same unit executed six Jesuit priests, their housekeeper, and her teenage daughter. Of the 26 officers implicated, 19 had trained in Panama.

In 1996, under pressure, the Pentagon released the school’s training manuals. They confirmed what critics alleged: the curriculum explicitly advocated extrajudicial execution, torture, and targeting civilians.

## 3\. Global solidarity between guerrilla fighters helped win wars.

Rafael learned survival from people who had already beaten American firepower.

By the mid-1980s, US-supplied aircraft were bombing the Cinquera region multiple times daily. Even a thin trail of cooking smoke could bring death from the sky. So Rafael and his comrades adopted a technique passed down from Vietnamese resistance fighters: underground stoves with buried exhaust channels running for dozens of meters underground. The soil absorbed and scattered the smoke before it could rise high enough to betray their position.

The guerrillas also studied helicopter vulnerabilities. Armed only with rifles, they learned precisely where to aim. Within months, they had knocked six aircraft out of the sky, sending a clear message: superior technology doesn’t guarantee victory.

## 4\. Counterterrorism and counterinsurgency are just polite words for massacres.

Counterinsurgency doctrine rests on a simple premise: guerrilla fighters cannot survive without civilian support. Insurgents depend on local populations for food, shelter, intelligence, and recruits. The logical conclusion is chilling and straightforward: if you cannot catch the fish, you drain the sea. This wasn’t a rogue philosophy invented by brutal local commanders. It was taught at Fort Benning, refined by American strategists, and applied systematically across El Salvador’s countryside.

In May 1980, Salvadoran troops launched an operation along the Sumpul River at the Honduran border. Hundreds of fleeing civilians, many of them women and children, found themselves trapped between Salvadoran forces advancing from behind and Honduran soldiers blocking escape across the river. The Salvadoran troops opened fire anyway. Bodies floated downstream for days. Estimates of the dead range from 300 to 600 people.

Throughout the early 1980s, the military systematically depopulated rural areas suspected of supporting guerrillas. Troops burned crops, slaughtered livestock, cut off food supplies to entire regions, and carried out repeated massacres in areas like Cinquera. By the mid-1980s, approximately one million Salvadorans—roughly 20 percent of the country’s population—had been driven from their homes.

## 6\. American citizens had (and have) a role to play.

The American war machine has always had opponents at home.

While Washington shipped billions in aid southward, American citizens organized resistance. The Sanctuary movement constructed an underground network of over 400 churches, synagogues, and meeting houses to shelter Central American refugees whom the government refused to recognize as legitimate asylum seekers. Groups like CISPES staged dramatic protests, hurling red paint at congressional offices and placing symbolic coffins on politicians’ lawns. The FBI surveilled and harassed them, but they kept pushing.

This domestic pressure turned El Salvador into a political liability. It didn’t stop the war, but it prevented the kind of full-scale ground invasion that had devastated Vietnam. Public outrage forced Congress to attach human rights conditions to aid packages, even if the Reagan administration found ways to certify compliance despite overwhelming evidence to the contrary.

The lesson remains relevant: the military-industrial complex provides the weapons, but organized citizens can jam the gears.

## 7\. Memory is the only thing the US couldn’t privatize.

After the 1992 peace accords, the UN Truth Commission spent months documenting atrocities and naming names. Their 1993 report confirmed what Salvadorans already knew: the overwhelming majority of human rights violations had been committed by government forces and affiliated death squads. The commission identified specific officers and recommended prosecutions. Five days after the report’s release, El Salvador’s legislature passed a sweeping amnesty law granting immunity to everyone involved. The killers walked free. The architects of state terror kept their pensions. For the United States, which had spent $4.5 billion sustaining the Salvadoran military, the amnesty offered a convenient resolution—no trials that might expose the extent of American involvement.

But survivors kept talking. They remembered the bodies dumped at Puerta del Diablo, a scenic overlook that death squads had transformed into a disposal site. They remembered neighbors who vanished into military trucks and never returned. They remembered the smell of burning villages and the sound of American-made helicopters at dawn. When forensic teams finally exhumed El Mozote in the 1990s, it was survivor testimony that led them to the graves. The state spent a decade denying the massacre occurred. The people who lived through it never stopped telling the truth.

Rafael now guides tourists through the same mountains where he once trained guerrilla recruits and dodged government bombs. Sometimes former government soldiers take his tour. When they hear his story, a few approach him afterward to apologize. He doesn’t dwell on revenge—decades of peace have convinced him that reconciliation is possible. But he also doesn’t let anyone forget. Every tour is an act of memory in a country that was told to move on. The United States could fund the weapons and train the killers. What they couldn’t purchase was the silence of those who survived.
