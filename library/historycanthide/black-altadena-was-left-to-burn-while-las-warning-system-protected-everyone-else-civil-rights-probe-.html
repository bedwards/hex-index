The Eaton Fire moved through West Altadena on a January night in 2025 and left the historically Black neighborhood in ash. Months later, survivors were still asking the same question: why hadn't anyone warned them? In February 2026, California Attorney General Rob Bonta opened a formal civil rights investigation into the Los Angeles County Fire Department to find out. His office wanted to know whether race, age, and disability had determined who got evacuated, who got resources, and who got left behind when the fire came through the San Gabriel foothills.

The fire burned over 14,000 acres and destroyed more than 9,000 structures. At least 19 people died. Nearly all of them were Black elders in West Altadena. East Altadena received evacuation alerts within roughly an hour of ignition. West Altadena waited almost nine hours. According to an analysis of LA County fire truck locations, the majority of crews stayed east of Lake Avenue, the town’s unofficial racial dividing line, even as flames consumed the west side. At one point during the blaze, a single fire truck was assigned to West Altadena while over a hundred were deployed elsewhere.

This was a policy decision with a body count.

Right now, Black fire survivors in Altadena are doing what Black communities have always had to do: organize, document, and demand accountability that should have never required demanding.

I’m working to make sure the history behind this moment is part of the public record, because without that context, this story risks being remembered as a tragic accident rather than a predictable outcome.

“A Place for White People Only”

To understand what happened on the night of January 7, 2025, you have to go back about a hundred years.

Altadena in the early twentieth century was a professional enclave connected to Los Angeles by electric railway. It was also explicitly white. Local homeowners associations, with names like the Great Northwest Improvement Association and the West Altadena Improvement Association, coordinated campaigns to get racial covenants written directly into property deeds. One 1919 notice sent to property owners read plainly: “We want our section of Pasadena and Altadena to be a place for white people only.” By the end of World War II, most properties in Altadena carried racially restrictive language. The 1950 census recorded no Black residents at all.

The federal government reinforced this geography through redlining. During the 1930s, federal assessors graded West Altadena as “declining” and “hazardous,” warning of what they called a “threat of subversive racial infiltration.” This made it nearly impossible for Black families to secure mortgages there, and it concentrated them in the western lots that white residents had already decided were worth less.

When the U.S. Supreme Court struck down racially restrictive covenants in 1948, and civil rights legislation made housing discrimination officially illegal in the 1960s, Black families began moving into West Altadena in earnest. Many were displaced from Pasadena itself, pushed out by the construction of Interstate 210, which was routed through Black and Latino neighborhoods in the name of urban renewal. By 1970, roughly one-third of Altadena’s population was Black, and about 70% of Black households there owned their homes, nearly double LA County’s Black homeownership rate at the time.

These were not transient residents. These were people who had built generational wealth in a specific community after decades of being told they couldn’t.

The catch, as it almost always is, was the fine print of what they were actually being allowed to own. West Altadena lots were smaller. The homes were older, built with more flammable materials. The neighborhood sat closer to the wildland-urban interface, nearer to the canyons where fire travels fastest. Decades of redlining had determined who would live in the most combustible section of a fire-prone hillside town.

When the Climate Crisis Arrives, It Uses Your ZIP Code

A February 2025 report from the UCLA Ralph J. Bunche Center for African American Studies found that about 48% of Black-owned homes in Altadena sustained major damage or total destruction in the Eaton Fire, compared to 37% of homes owned by Asian, Latino, or white Altadenans. Sixty-one percent of Black households in Altadena were entirely inside the fire perimeter.

UCLA researcher Paul Ong noted that the fire’s path moved down the western part of Altadena, where African Americans have historically been concentrated. That path followed the geography that racist housing policy built.

Hotter, drier conditions and intensifying Santa Ana winds have made the kind of fire that consumed West Altadena an accelerating pattern. When you stack a century of racist housing policy on top of a climate crisis, what you get is Black elders living on smaller lots, in older homes, in the most fire-exposed corridors of a city that markets itself as climate-resilient, dying at higher rates while the sirens screamed somewhere else.

LA Can Move Fast, Just Not for Everyone

LA County has an emergency alert system. It works. East Altadena received warnings within an hour. The Palisades, wealthier and whiter, received substantial firefighting resources quickly. The technology existed. The infrastructure existed. What failed was a set of institutional decisions about whose lives were assumed to be mobile, whose communities were assumed to be worth the rapid response, and whose blocks could wait until 3:30 in the morning.

This is not unprecedented for Los Angeles. Past heat waves concentrated cooling center resources in wealthier neighborhoods while Black and brown communities self-organized. COVID-19 testing sites were underbuilt in communities of color relative to the Westside. The pattern here isn’t chaos. It’s consistency.

Gina Clayton-Johnson, founder of the Black feminist organizing group Essie Justice Group, lost her own home, her parents’ home, and her children’s school in the Eaton Fire. She was not evacuated. She received no warning, no text, no call. She found out her house had burned down from the news. Her parents are in their seventies. She called her aunt in Atlanta.

Clayton-Johnson and groups like Altadena for Accountability have spent the months since doing grief circles and legal strategy sessions simultaneously, building wellness support networks and press conferences in the same breath. They rang the alarm. They produced the evidence. They pressed Bonta’s office until it finally acted. Without that sustained organizing, Attorney General Bonta said plainly, the investigation would not have been opened.

What Real Accountability Actually Looks Like

Bonta’s investigation is operating under a disparate impact framework, which means prosecutors don’t have to prove that any dispatcher or fire captain harbored racial animus. They only have to demonstrate that the system produced racially unequal outcomes. That is a meaningful legal tool, and it is exactly the kind of tool that the Trump administration moved to block at the federal level last year.

The investigation could result in findings that LA County violated state civil rights and disability laws. It could prompt federal litigation. Civil rights attorneys and survivors are already publicly discussing lawsuits against county agencies.

Real accountability, in this case, means mandatory and publicly accessible alert logs broken down by neighborhood, race, age, and disability status. It means binding reforms to emergency protocols that prioritize historically excluded communities in evacuation planning, nt as an afterthought. It means direct compensation and rebuilding support for Black Altadenans whose generational wealth, built despite every institutional obstacle, was destroyed in a single night.

For decades, Black families in West Altadena were told that homeownership there was the reward for surviving Los Angeles’s racism. The Eaton Fire revealed exactly how thin that promise was the moment the emergency system had to choose which ZIP codes got saved first.

We Now Know the Sirens Never Came

A few miles from West Altadena, Pasadena hosts the Rose Parade every New Year’s Day, floats and marching bands rolling through streets lined with the kind of crowd that buys bleacher seats weeks in advance. LA markets itself relentlessly as a city of resilience, of reinvention, of sun and possibility.

In West Altadena, on the night of January 7, 2025, Black elders waited nine hours for a warning that many never received. Some found out their homes were burning because a neighbor knocked or a relative called. Some didn’t get out at all.

The Eaton Fire is the predictable and documented outcome of a century of racist housing policy, unequal infrastructure investment, and emergency systems designed, whether intentionally or through habit, to treat Black communities as expendable in moments of crisis.

Essie Justice Group, Altadena for Accountability, and the survivors doing this work are insisting that the official record reflect who was left behind, who organized to demand answers, and who fought to make civil rights law mean something when the hills catch fire.

And if California is finally willing to say that a wildfire response can constitute civil rights discrimination, then we have to sit with the harder question: how many past “natural disasters” were actually uncounted civil rights crimes, and how many more will we accept before we admit that who gets saved has always been a political choice?
