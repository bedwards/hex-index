Last month, I descended into the tomb of boy-King Tutankhamun in the Valley of the Kings in Luxor, Egypt. The air was thick and hot as I approached his mummified remains, which had been preserved for over 3,000 years.

In a way, this amazing feat of preservation wasn’t a rarity. The ancient Egyptians mummified hundreds of thousands of their dead across millennia, perfecting a process that defies time itself. Given this vast scale of mummification—from pharaohs to priests to wealthy merchants—you’d expect Egypt’s tombs to overflow with preserved remains. Yet today, tombs across the country sit conspicuously empty. One of the shocking reasons is that, for nearly 700 years, Europeans ground up Egyptian mummies and ate them.

The Translation Error That Started It All

The mummy-eating craze began with a translation error. In the 11th century, Arabic medical texts praised “mumia,” a rare bitumen substance that seeped from a single Persian mountainside. This black, waxy petroleum product did have legitimate uses like waterproofing ships and treating wounds. But when European translators encountered the word, they confused it with the similar-looking resin coating of Egyptian mummies.

One Italian translator, Gerard of Cremona, declared mumia was created when “the liquid of the dead, mixed with the aloes, is transformed.” Another scholar insisted it formed when spices used in mummification mixed with bodily fluids. These misunderstandings launched a centuries-long medical delusion that would empty Egypt’s tombs.

By the 12th century, Crusaders returning from the Middle East brought tales of this miraculous “mummy medicine.” Shrewd merchants in Alexandria quickly realized they were sitting on a goldmine. Why bother extracting rare bitumen when thousands of resin-coated corpses lay beneath the sand? They began raiding tombs, breaking bodies apart, and shipping the pieces to European apothecaries. The medieval mind performed remarkable gymnastics to justify this practice. Somehow, consuming ancient Egyptian corpses was medicine, not cannibalism.

The Mummy Medicine Boom

The scale of this trade defies comprehension. By the 16th century, every well-stocked European apothecary sold powdered mummy. The 1618 London Pharmacopoeia, the official catalog of approved medicines, listed “mummy” among its essential remedies, describing the taste as “somewhat acrid and bitterish.” Physicians prescribed it for everything: headaches, heart attacks, plague, paralysis, even bruising. Mix it with wine for seizures. Blend it with rhubarb for internal bleeding. Dissolve it in goat milk for ulcers. King Francis I of France carried a personal supply of mummy powder mixed with rhubarb wherever he traveled. King Charles II of England had his own signature tincture, “The King’s Drops,” featuring powdered human skull in alcohol.

The demand quickly outstripped supply, spawning a grotesque secondary market. When genuine ancient mummies grew scarce, entrepreneurs got creative. Some merchants discovered it was easier to manufacture fresh “mummies” than dig up old ones. They purchased corpses of executed criminals, plague victims, and enslaved people, dried them out, coated them in resin, and passed them off as ancient Egyptian royalty. In 1564, French physician Guy de la Fontaine witnessed the process firsthand in Alexandria, watching merchants prepare fresh corpses for export. One dealer confessed they could produce forty “ancient” mummies in a single batch.

Fresh Corpses and Domestic Production

Even more disturbing was the evolution of corpse medicine beyond Egyptian sources. The influential 16th-century Swiss physician Paracelsus argued that fresh corpses worked better than ancient ones. The ideal source? A healthy young man, preferably 24 years old, red-headed, who died suddenly.

Executioners began selling vials of fresh blood from the scaffold. Epileptics would pay to drink it warm, straight from the condemned. Human fat was harvested for wound treatments. Powdered skull became the go-to cure for headaches because, naturally, skull treats skull ailments. German physicians even published recipes for preparing human marmalade from blood.

The Colonial Hypocrisy

What makes this medical cannibalism particularly nauseating is the staggering hypocrisy it revealed. While Europeans were literally eating human remains by the boatload, they simultaneously used accusations of cannibalism to justify colonizing the Americas. Christopher Columbus initially described indigenous peoples as peaceful and friendly. But once he needed slaves instead of gold, these same people suddenly became “cannibals” who deserved subjugation.

The Spanish Crown had specifically instructed that indigenous peoples be treated with respect unless they were cannibals, in which case “all bets were off.” How convenient that everyone Columbus encountered transformed into a cannibal overnight.

The French essayist Michel de Montaigne spotted this hypocrisy in 1580, writing that Brazilian cannibalism was no worse than Europe’s medicinal version, and both were preferable to the religious wars tearing Europe apart. But his voice was drowned out by the clink of coins in apothecary tills.

The Underground Trade

Egyptian authorities tried to stop the grave robbing. In 1428, Cairo officials arrested and tortured mummy smugglers who confessed to boiling bodies and selling the oil that rose to the surface. But money talked. John Sanderson, an English merchant visiting in 1586, simply paid the right bribes and sailed home with 600 pounds of “divers heads, hands, arms, and feete.” For every pound of authentic ancient remains, there were probably two pounds of fabricated “mummies” created specifically for European consumption.

The practice peaked in the 18th century but persisted well into the modern era. A German pharmaceutical catalog from 1908, less than 120 years ago, advertised “Genuine Egyptian mummy as long as the supply lasts, 17 marks 50 per kilogram.” Artists ground up mummies to create “Mummy Brown” pigment, a rich color that likely appears in famous paintings, including Delacroix’s “Liberty Leading the People.” The last tube was manufactured in 1964.

Beyond medicine and art, Europeans found endless uses for Egyptian remains. Mummy unwrapping parties became Victorian entertainment, where wealthy guests would watch ancient bodies be stripped of their bandages like some macabre striptease. There are accounts of mummies being used as fertilizer, their linen wrappings turned into paper, their bodies burned as locomotive fuel. True or exaggerated, these stories capture how Europeans viewed Egyptian remains: not as human bodies deserving respect, but as raw materials for consumption.

The Cost of Consumption

Standing before Tutankhamun’s preserved body, I couldn’t help but think he survived only because Howard Carter found him in 1922, after the mummy-eating craze had mostly ended. His tomb was one of the few never ransacked by ancient grave robbers or modern corpse merchants.

We’ll never know how much knowledge disappeared into European stomachs. Every mummy destroyed was a library burned, a story erased, a connection to the past severed. The irony is inescapable. The same cultures that labeled indigenous peoples as barbaric cannibals were seasoning their wine with human remains. The difference wasn’t the act itself but who got to write the history. Cannibalism, it turns out, was never really about what you ate. It was about who got to define civilization and who became medicine.

The next time you see an Egyptian artifact in a Western museum, remember this: we’re lucky it wasn’t edible.
