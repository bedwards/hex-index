“The Colored People Are in Great Danger.”

That’s the headline that introduced America to the Snallygaster in 1909. Not a centuries-old German legend or an ancient Appalachian folklore, but a newspaper article published by the Middletown Valley Register in Western Maryland. This article was deliberately crafted to terrify Black residents with a fictional dragon that, according to the paper, “will not harm a white person, but lives mainly on colored men.”

White newspaper editors invented a monster and published stories specifically designed to control where Black people could safely travel. For decades, it worked. Understanding how it worked reveals something chilling about how white supremacy operated beyond the violence we’re taught about in history class.

How the Monster Was Created

Retired librarian Susan Fair made a disturbing discovery while researching her book “Mysteries & Lore of Western Maryland.” She was looking for the Snallygaster’s ancient origins and found that the creature doesn’t appear in “South Mountain Magic,” the definitive 1882 collection of Western Maryland folklore. No German immigrants told stories about it, and no Indigenous legends mention anything resembling it. The Snallygaster simply didn’t exist before 1909.

By 1909, Black communities in Western Maryland had been gaining economic independence and political voice despite Jim Crow restrictions. Black men could vote, Black families owned property, and Black workers competed for jobs. White power structures needed new mechanisms to maintain control, especially in rural areas where Black populations were smaller but increasingly visible in public life. The Snallygaster emerged as one such tool.

Northern newspapers had been reporting sightings of the “Jersey Devil,” and the Valley Register staff saw an opportunity. They could capitalize on cryptid hysteria while reinforcing the racial boundaries that kept Western Maryland’s Black population contained.

That first article in 1909 describes the Snallygaster killing a Black man named Bill Gifferson, swooping him up from a country road and “piercing his jugular vein with its needle-like bill.” The monster was given specific hunting patterns, specific geographic ranges, and one very specific dietary preference. The dragon had one eye, steel-hook talons, and tentacles sprouting from its mouth, but its most important feature was that it hunted Black people exclusively. White people, the paper assured readers, were perfectly safe.

The article was published in the newspaper of record, telling Black residents exactly where they couldn’t safely go. This was a manufactured threat designed to control Black movement and behavior through fear.

The 1932 Revival and Voter Suppression

The Snallygaster disappeared from newspapers for two decades after its initial appearance. Then, in 1932, the Valley Register resurrected it for a very specific purpose. “Strange Monster Mystifies and Alarms People Living in South Mountain Section,” the headline announced. But this time, the article made the political calculation explicit. The beast was “an omen of ill for colored voters who deserted the Republican party in the Presidential election and voted for Roosevelt.”

Black voters were beginning their historic shift from the Republican Party of Lincoln to the Democratic Party of Franklin Roosevelt during the New Deal realignment. White power structures in Maryland couldn’t accept this, so they brought back their dragon.

The threat was direct. If you’re a Black person who votes Democrat, the monster will find you. It’s supernatural intimidation as an alternative to the Klan’s physical violence, a way to control Black political power without burning crosses or orchestrating lynch mobs. The monster could be everywhere and nowhere, an omnipresent threat that required no actual violence to be effective.

One reader, R. Austin Stine, actually called out the paper in a letter to the editor. “Anything that may have the affect of creating fear in the heart of any man, be he white or black, in my humble opinion should be condemned.” But the damage was done. The message had been sent to every Black voter in Western Maryland.

Other Maryland papers picked up the story, including The Baltimore Sun. A rival paper, The Hagerstown Morning Herald, eventually “killed” the beast by reporting it drowned in a vat of moonshine, complete with a crudely edited photo of Prohibition agents and a cartoon dragon. Even in death, the Snallygaster was a joke to white editors, but the fear it generated in Black communities was very real.

From Terror Weapon to Tourist Attraction

Within a few decades, the Snallygaster had been relegated to “quaint folklore.” The name went on racing sloops. The creature appeared in roundups of local mythology. And in recent years, with the resurgence of cryptid culture, the Snallygaster has become marketable through beer festivals, ice cream flavors, and a traveling miniature museum run by enthusiasts who describe it as having “kitschy punk aesthetic.” Sarah Cooper, who operates the American Snallygaster Museum, repeats the sanitized origin story about German immigrants worried about the “schneller geist” or “quick spirit.” The version she tells is clean and European, erasing everything that actually happened.

This is how America buries its racial violence. We don’t destroy the evidence, we rebrand it. The same thing happened with sundown town sirens, mammy kitchenware, and Gone with the Wind nostalgia. We keep the aesthetic, strip out the violence, and sell it back as “heritage.” And when someone like Susan Fair does the research and reveals what these things actually meant, we act shocked that anyone would bring politics into our harmless fun.

Rural Jim Crow’s Different Tactics

The Snallygaster reveals something crucial about how Jim Crow operated in rural America. We learn about urban redlining, segregated buses, and lunch counters, but in places like Western Maryland, the mechanisms of control looked different.

Small-town newspapers wielded enormous power. They could manufacture threats, disseminate them as “news,” and maintain racial boundaries without the organized violence required in cities. These papers didn’t need to report on actual lynchings when they could invent a monster that served the same purpose: keeping Black people out of certain areas, away from certain opportunities, and in line politically. The Snallygaster was a complement to the very real physical violence happening elsewhere.

Across the rural South and border states, white communities found creative ways to enforce segregation that didn’t require the spectacle of mob violence. The Snallygaster is one example that survived in print, but how many other monsters were whispered into existence and never documented?

The real horror isn’t a dragon with tentacles. It’s that white America was so invested in maintaining segregation that newspapers invented supernatural threats to enforce it. And it’s that we’ve now forgotten this completely, turning those threats into merchandise for people who think cryptids are neat.

As Susan Fair put it, “The real monster in America is racism.”
