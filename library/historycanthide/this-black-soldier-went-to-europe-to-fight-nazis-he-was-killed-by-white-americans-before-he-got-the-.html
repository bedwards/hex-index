Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth has been busy. In the past year, he’s stripped diversity training from military academies, removed videos honoring the Tuskegee Airmen and Women Airforce Service Pilots from basic training, and celebrated a recent court victory allowing the Pentagon to ban transgender service members. “No more pronouns. No more climate change obsession. No more dudes in dresses,” he declared in September. “We’re done with that s**t.”

Hegseth frames this as restoring “military lethality” after decades of “woke” decay. But here’s what he won’t tell you: the U.S. military has historically been ahead of American society on integration, not behind it. President Truman desegregated the armed forces in 1948, six years before Brown v. Board of Education. The military opened combat roles to women, ended “Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell,” and built one of the most diverse institutions in the country, not because generals were bleeding-heart liberals, but because they understood a simple truth: you can’t have a unified fighting force if your troops are fighting each other.

Hegseth’s revisionism erases that history. It also erases what happens when the military allows prejudice to fester unchecked. For that lesson, we need to go back to a small English village in 1943, where a Black soldier named William Crossland went to fight Nazis and was killed by white Americans instead.

Jim Crow Crosses the Atlantic

In 1943, nearly two million American servicemen were stationed in or passed through Britain. About 150,000 of them were Black, serving in segregated units led by white officers. Most were relegated to non-combat roles: driving trucks, loading supplies, building infrastructure. The dangerous work with none of the glory.

The U.S. Army didn’t just bring its soldiers to England. It brought its racial hierarchy too. Military authorities pressured British pubs and restaurants to separate the races, to extend Jim Crow beyond American borders. They issued guidance suggesting that white and Black troops should frequent different establishments on different nights.

The British, for the most part, weren’t having it. After four years of war and German bombing, they welcomed any help they could get. In a country that was almost entirely white, there was no tradition of American-style segregation (though this is not meant to downplay the gruesome racism across the Commonwealth). Black soldiers could socialize openly in British pubs, where they were not just tolerated but warmly received. Local women danced with them. English soldiers drank alongside men they saw as allies against fascism.

This did not sit well with certain white American servicemen, who had brought their prejudices overseas and expected the English to comply.

“Black Troops Only”

The men of the 1511th Quartermaster Truck Regiment were stationed at Adams Hall in Bamber Bridge, a village of about 6,800 people near Preston in Lancashire. Their job was delivering war material to airbases throughout the region. In their off-hours, they frequented the village’s three pubs, where they’d built friendly relationships with locals.

When white American military police attempted to establish a color bar in the village, the residents of Bamber Bridge refused. According to accounts that have circulated for decades, pub landlords responded by hanging signs reading “Black Troops Only” outside their establishments. The village, it seemed, wouldn’t tolerate racial discrimination, even when the U.S. military demanded it.

The warmth between Black soldiers and British civilians created resentment among some white troops, particularly the 234th Military Police Company. The MPs were disgruntled about the popularity of Black soldiers among local English women. That resentment would boil over on the night of June 24, 1943.

A Bottle, a Uniform, and a War Within a War

The trouble started, as it often does, with something trivial. It was just after 10 pm, closing time at Ye Olde Hob Inn, a 400-year-old thatched-roof pub. The barmaid had stopped serving. Several Black soldiers from the 1511th were still inside, along with British soldiers and civilians.

Two white Military Police officers, Privates Windsor and Ridgeway, arrived claiming they’d received reports of trouble. They attempted to arrest Private Eugene Nunn, accusing him of being improperly dressed and lacking a pass. Nunn refused. A crowd formed around the policemen, with British soldiers and civilians verbally defending the Black troops.

“Everyone was saying, ‘Leave him alone. He just wants a drink. It’s a hot day,’” recalled Valerie Fell, whose family ran the pub. She was only two years old in 1943, but her mother told her the story many times. “People just didn’t understand this viciousness.”

When Private Adams of the 1511th advanced holding a bottle, MP Windsor drew his gun. A sergeant temporarily calmed the situation, and the MPs left. But as their jeep pulled away, someone threw a bottle, shattering the windshield.

The MPs returned with reinforcements. They intercepted Black soldiers walking back to Adams Hall. A fight broke out, with bottles and cobblestones flying. An MP fired his weapon, wounding Private Adams in the neck. Back at Adams Hall, rumors spread: Black soldiers had been shot in the back. Up to 200 Black soldiers gathered, confronting their white superiors. The unit’s sole Black officer, a second lieutenant, managed to temporarily calm the crowd by promising that senior officers would hear their grievances.

Then, around midnight, approximately a dozen MPs arrived in a makeshift armored vehicle mounted with a machine gun.

The Black soldiers knew what that meant. They seized roughly two-thirds of the rifles stored at Adams Hall and set up defensive positions. Some stayed in the camp. Others believed they were defending against an armed assault. Another group took more direct action.

What followed was a firefight that stretched across Bamber Bridge into the early morning hours. British residents reported hearing gunfire until 3 am. According to witnesses, Black soldiers went door to door, warning villagers to stay inside, protecting the people who had shown them kindness.

By dawn, Private William Crossland lay dead. He was 25 years old, a former railroad worker from the United States who had crossed an ocean to fight fascism. The Army claimed he was caught in crossfire, but said he was shot in the back. His niece, Nancy Croslan Adkins, told the Associated Press in 2023 that she was never informed about the circumstances of her uncle’s death. She has called for a new investigation.

Mutiny

In the aftermath, the military moved swiftly, but not against the MPs who had arrived with a machine gun to confront unarmed men. The blame fell on the Black soldiers.

Thirty-five members of the 1511th were charged with mutiny, seizing arms, rioting, firing upon officers and MPs, ignoring orders, and failing to disperse. Twenty-eight were convicted. Sentences ranged from three months to fifteen years in prison, combined with dishonorable discharges and loss of pay.

The court-martial president made an immediate plea for clemency, acknowledging the “appalling lack of discipline” at the camp and poor leadership by white officers who had failed to perform their duties. Higher authorities agreed, and sentences were reduced. Within a year, fifteen men were restored to duty. The longest anyone served was thirteen months. The white MPs who arrived with a machine gun were never disciplined.

General Ira Eaker, commander of the Eighth Army Air Force, attributed significant blame to the racist MPs and the white officers of the 1511th. In response, all field-grade officers of Black units were replaced. Junior officers were “weeded out.” The Army introduced racially mixed MP patrols and improved leave arrangements.

These were real reforms, born from crisis. The military learned, however imperfectly, that you cannot maintain order through oppression. That prejudice undermines readiness. That a house divided against itself cannot fight a war.

The Long Road Home

Something else happened to the Black soldiers who survived, who returned home after experiencing life without Jim Crow. Many of them became leaders in the Civil Rights Movement. They had seen another way, been welcomed as equals in English pubs. They knew American apartheid was a choice, not an inevitability.

Bamber Bridge remembers. The village installed a memorial plaque outside Ye Olde Hob Inn in 2022. Locals hold commemorations as a point of pride, honoring their stand against American segregation. Clinton Smith, head of the Black history group in nearby Preston, helped revive interest in the story in the 1980s when he discovered bullet holes in the side of a bank and started asking questions.

“This story just can’t be allowed to wither on the vine,” Smith said. “As much as it’s withered, we’re just now trying to rejuvenate it whilst maintaining the accuracy.”

What Hegseth Won’t Tell You

Pete Hegseth wants you to believe that diversity initiatives are making the military weak. That acknowledging the contributions of the Tuskegee Airmen is “woke.” That the path to lethality runs through erasing the history of how integration actually made the armed forces stronger.

The Battle of Bamber Bridge tells a different story. It shows what happens when prejudice goes unchecked: fratricide, court-martials, a dead soldier who never got to fight the enemy he crossed an ocean to face. It also shows what reform looks like. After Bamber Bridge, the Eighth Army Air Force removed racist officers, integrated MP patrols, and addressed grievances that had been festering for years.

The military has spent eight decades learning that you cannot build cohesion through exclusion. Hegseth is trying to unlearn that lesson.

William Crossland deserved better. So do the service members Hegseth is targeting today.
