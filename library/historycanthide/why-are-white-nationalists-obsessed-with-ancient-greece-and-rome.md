Why do so many men “[think about the Roman Empire](https://www.forbes.com/sites/danidiplacido/2023/09/21/tiktoks-roman-empire-meme-explained/)”? And why does that meme matter more than we realize?

In this live conversation, I sat down with Professor Curtis Dozier, a classicist at Vassar College and author of the forthcoming book *[The White Pedestal: How White Nationalists Use Ancient Greece and Rome to Justify Hate](https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300272734/the-white-pedestal/)* (Yale University Press, January 6, 2026). What started as Professor Dozier’s attempt to debunk historical distortions by extremists became a much more uncomfortable discovery: **there is plenty of material within ancient texts that genuinely supports white nationalist talking points.**

## Table of Contents

### 1\. Introduction: Meet Professor Curtis Dozier (00:00:00 – 00:07:00)

-   Professor Curtis Dozier teaches at Vassar College and specializes in how ancient Greece and Rome have been used throughout history
    

-   His upcoming book, *The White Pedestal*, examines how white nationalist movements draw on classical antiquity to justify their ideology
    
-   Professor Dozier’s academic journey began with Latin literature and expanded into studying how extremist groups deploy ancient texts
    
-   When he first started this research, he expected to find obvious historical distortions by white nationalists
    
-   Instead, he discovered that many white nationalists understand ancient texts quite well because there is genuine material in those sources that supports their politics
    
-   This uncomfortable realization forms the core argument of his book
    

\---

### 2\. Why Does Everyone Think About the Roman Empire? (00:07:00 – 00:13:00)

-   The viral meme about men constantly thinking about the Roman Empire reveals something important about our culture
    
-   Professor Dozier maintains a Google alert for mentions of the Roman Empire and shares examples from the past week: comparisons to American decline, Argentina’s economy, the Kansas City Chiefs, and even an Adult Swim cartoon
    
-   References to ancient Rome and Greece saturate our culture, making these civilizations fertile ground for white nationalists
    
-   White nationalists know that audiences already take ancient history seriously, so invoking it lends credibility to their ideas
    
-   Rome is typically invoked as a warning about civilizational collapse
    
-   Greece is typically invoked as a golden age of democracy and philosophy to aspire toward
    

\---

### 3\. What is “Western Civilization” Really? (00:13:00 – 00:23:00)

-   “Western Civilization” operates on three layers: political, historical, and geographical
    
-   Politically, it bundles together ideals like liberty, rule of law, human rights, and scientific rationality
    
-   Historically, it claims ancient Greece and Rome as the origin of these values
    
-   Geographically, it maps onto Europe, the United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand, but this geography is incoherent (Australia is further west than places we call “the East”)
    
-   These are either majority-white nations or places colonized by white Europeans
    
-   The term functions as a euphemism that justifies a global order where white-majority nations hold wealth and power
    
-   It attributes white dominance to superior culture rather than to colonialism, genocide, and resource extraction
    
-   The phrase has Cold War origins but remains politically relevant today, used by figures in the Trump administration and enshrined in Florida education law
    

\---

### 4\. Race, Ancestry, and the Racialization of Ancient Peoples (00:23:00 – 00:38:00)

-   Words like “ancestry,” “heritage,” and “inheritance” claim a biological connection between modern white people and ancient Greeks and Romans
    
-   When Stephen Miller says “our lineage hails back to Athens and Rome,” he implies white people today share the intelligence and creativity of ancient civilizations because of race
    
-   Race itself is a social construct invented during European colonization to categorize people for enslavement and genocide
    
-   Africans from completely different cultures, religions, and regions were grouped together as one “race” to justify their enslavement
    
-   Black Americans have responded by claiming connections to ancient Egypt, but the context is completely different: it is a reaction to being told you have no history worth celebrating
    
-   When Black people make claims about ancient Egypt, they face intense scrutiny and mockery, while white claims about Western Civilization go largely unquestioned
    
-   Both represent “racialization,” but one is a tool of oppression and the other is a response to it
    

\---

### 5\. Why Does Cleopatra’s Race Matter So Much? (00:38:00 – 00:43:00)

-   The controversy over casting Black actress Adele James as Cleopatra in a Netflix documentary illustrates how deeply people are invested in the racial identity of ancient figures
    
-   When a Black actor is cast, there are death threats and even government condemnation from Egypt’s Ministry of Culture
    
-   Critics never complain about Cleopatra being too tall, too short, or too old; the outrage is entirely about race
    
-   This reveals an assumption that ancient people were white and that depicting them otherwise threatens the narrative of Western Civilization as white inheritance
    
-   This defensiveness exists because if racial continuity is questioned, people might have to confront the violence and exploitation that actually produced today’s global inequalities
    
-   Adele James is not even particularly dark-skinned, showing that any hint of Blackness is enough to provoke backlash
    

\---

### 6\. Sparta at the Capitol: January 6th and White Nationalist Classicism (00:43:00 – 00:52:00)

-   Spartan imagery appeared at the January 6th Capitol insurrection alongside Confederate flags and nooses
    
-   Rioters wore Spartan-style helmets and flew flags reading “Molon Labe” (”Come and Take Them”), a phrase from the Battle of Thermopylae
    
-   These rioters, whether or not they have read ancient texts, draw on a long tradition of glorifying Sparta as a model of masculine valor
    
-   Ancient writers like Plutarch and Xenophon promoted a fantasy version of Sparta as a society dedicated to creating unstoppable warriors
    
-   The Capitol rioters saw themselves as modern Spartans making a heroic last stand against a threat to their civilization
    
-   Goebbels himself once said that visiting Sparta felt like being in a German city
    
-   This is not a major distortion of history; the ancient sources themselves glorify Sparta in ways that lend themselves to fascist politics
    

\---

### 7\. The Intellectual Right and the Great Replacement Theory (00:49:00 – 00:56:00)

-   The “intellectual right” consists of educated figures with PhDs and fluency in multiple languages who use ancient texts to promote white nationalist politics
    
-   Renaud Camus, a French literary figure and gay rights advocate, coined “The Great Replacement” theory claiming immigration will destroy white European identity
    
-   The French version of his book opens with a discussion of a Platonic dialogue to give his arguments intellectual prestige (he moved this to page 140 in the English version)
    
-   Tucker Carlson later promoted the Great Replacement to millions of Americans
    
-   Surveys showed around 70% of Republicans accept some version of this theory
    
-   Belief in the Great Replacement was the number one predictor of participation in the January 6th riot
    
-   The classical reference drops away as ideas spread, but the intellectual foundation helps them gain initial traction
    
-   Very smart people are figuring out how to make racist ideas respectable by connecting them to ancient Greece and Rome
    

\---

### 8\. Solutions: Expanding Our View of History (00:56:00 – 01:05:00)

-   History is not just Western European empires; ancient Greece and Rome receive outsized attention because of how our educational systems were built, not because they are inherently more important
    
-   Learn what non-white scholars and thinkers have done with classical material
    
-   W.E.B. Du Bois, Toni Morrison, Phillis Wheatley, Anna Julia Cooper, William Sanders Scarborough (the first African-American classical scholar), and Charles Chesnutt all engaged with ancient Greece and Rome
    
-   These Black intellectuals used classical knowledge to critique white supremacy rather than reinforce it; they understood how white people weaponized antiquity
    
-   Toni Morrison studied classics at Howard and used the ancient world in her novels as a critique of white supremacist politics, not as a source of inspiration
    
-   Edward Said’s *Orientalism* traces how Europe constructed “the East” as inferior starting from ancient Greek sources like Aeschylus’s *The Persians*
    
-   The Asian and Asian-American Classical Caucus is doing new work on Asian receptions of classics
    
-   Expanding who we learn from changes what we lea
