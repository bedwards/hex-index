Last week, Sydney Sweeney broke her silence in an exclusive interview with People magazine. After months of deflection, declining engagement, and watching four consecutive films tank, the actress finally addressed the controversy surrounding her American Eagle “Great Jeans” campaign. “I was honestly surprised by the reaction,” Sweeney told the outlet. “I don’t support the views some people chose to connect to the campaign. Many have assigned motives and labels to me that just aren’t true.”

The statement was carefully worded, strategically vague, and arrived suspiciously close to the premiere of her upcoming thriller The Housemaid. And if you’re wondering whether this means cancel culture is officially back, the answer is yes.

The Art of Saying Nothing

Let’s be clear about what Sweeney actually said, and more importantly, what she didn’t. At no point in her People interview did she denounce white supremacy. She never acknowledged that Donald Trump praised her campaign. She didn’t address the fact that right-wing commentators weaponized her image as proof that “anti-woke” marketing sells. Instead, she offered the kind of statement a crisis PR team drafts at 2 a.m.

“Anyone who knows me knows that I’m always trying to bring people together. I’m against hate and divisiveness,” Sweeney said—a lovely sentiment that means absolutely nothing when the President of the United States has publicly endorsed your advertising campaign as a victory against diversity initiatives.

This wasn’t Sweeney’s first opportunity to clarify her position. In a November GQ interview, when asked directly about criticism that “in this political climate, white people shouldn’t joke about genetic superiority,” she responded: “I think that when I have an issue that I want to speak about, people will hear.” The implication was clear: this wasn’t an issue she wanted to speak about. Apparently, three consecutive box office disappointments changed her calculus.

The Precedent She Ignored

Here’s what makes Sweeney’s months of silence so conspicuous: other celebrities faced with similar situations handled them very differently.

When Olivia Rodrigo discovered her music was being played on government social media accounts, she didn’t wait for her album sales to dip before responding. She made her position unambiguous. Sabrina Carpenter did the same when right-wing figures attempted to use her songs similarly. These two pop stars had had an infamous beef for years, yet they agreed that it’s worth standing up to an inhumane, racist president, no matter the cost. Neither artist wrote songs intended as political statements, but they understood that silence is its own kind of statement.

Then there’s KATSEYE, the multinational girl group whose Gap campaign became the inadvertent counterpoint to Sweeney’s controversy. The group, featuring members from the Philippines, South Korea, Switzerland, and the United States, didn’t just release a diverse ad. They explicitly centered their public identity around multicultural celebration and have been vocal supporters of trans rights and inclusion. Their North American tour sold out. Gap reported record-breaking engagement. The contrast couldn’t be sharper.

Sweeney had every opportunity to follow this template. A single Instagram story denouncing white supremacist interpretations of her campaign would have cost her nothing except, perhaps, the adoration of her newfound MAGA fanbase. She chose differently.

The Marketplace Spoke

Now, let’s talk about what “cancel culture” actually means in this context, because the term gets thrown around so carelessly.

Cancel culture, in its purest form, is societal pushback. It’s consumers deciding they don’t want to support someone whose values conflict with their own. It’s the free market doing exactly what free market enthusiasts claim to celebrate: letting people vote with their wallets.

What happened to Sweeney was that people didn’t show up. Christy, her boxing biopic, opened to $1.3 million on over 2,000 screens, one of the worst wide-release openings in recent memory. Eden, Americana, and Echo Valley fizzled before that. Four films, four disappointments, and a suddenly very motivated actress ready to talk about unity and healing.

As my friend Joel Bervell noted on social media after Jimmy Kimmel was taken off air, there’s a meaningful distinction here between what we should condemn (censorship) and what we should wield (cancel culture): “The difference between cancel culture and this instance is that cancel culture comes from people (societal pushback), while this directive [the cancellation of Jimmy Kimmel’s show] comes from the government, which is state-sanctioned authoritarianism.”

Sweeney wasn’t targeted by any government entity. She wasn’t blacklisted by studios or banned from platforms. She made choices, those choices had consequences, she refused to apologize, and people stopped supporting her.

That’s cancel culture in its purest sense. And I’m a fan of it.

What Comes Next

Sweeney’s People interview reads less like a genuine reckoning and more like a strategic repositioning. “I hope this new year brings more focus on what connects us instead of what divides us.” Whether audiences accept this olive branch remains to be seen. The Housemaid opens December 19th, and its box office performance will tell us whether Sweeney’s belated attempt at bridge-building resonates or arrives too late to matter.

But here’s the larger lesson, one that extends far beyond one actress and one jeans ad: in a political climate where “good genes” rhetoric has been used to justify forced sterilizations, immigration crackdowns, and the dismantling of diversity programs, neutrality isn’t neutral. And when you become a symbol for something, you have two choices: reject it clearly or own it completely.
