Earlier this year, I visited Yale for the African American Cultural Center’s 55th anniversary. While I was there, I wandered into an exhibition at the Schwarzman Center called [“Shining Light on Truth: Black Lives at Yale & in New Haven.”](https://schwarzman.yale.edu/shining-light-truth-black-lives-yale-new-haven) It promised to illuminate “ongoing research that recovers the essential role of Black people throughout Yale and New Haven history.”

What I found was a story I had never heard. Not in any history class. Not in any documentary. Not once in my years of attending the school or as [student body president](https://www.essence.com/news/kahlil-greene-yale-first-black-student-body-president/).

In 1831, a coalition of Black leaders and white abolitionists came within inches of building the nation’s first college for Black men. They chose New Haven, Connecticut, raised the money, and purchased the land. And then 700 white men, many of them Yale alumni, gathered in a sweltering hall and voted the dream into oblivion.

That decision helped shape the legal architecture of American white supremacy for the next two centuries.

## “Friendly, Pious, Generous, and Humane”

The plan was audacious. In June 1831, Black leaders from across the Northeast gathered at the First Annual Convention of the People of Color in Philadelphia. Delegates from fourteen cities, including New Haven’s own Bias Stanley and John Creed, endorsed a proposal to build a college that would offer Black men access to classical education, the mechanic arts, agriculture, and “all those things which make men happy.”

*1852 map of New Haven, CT*

The site? New Haven, Connecticut. The reasons, as recorded in the convention’s official proceedings, read almost like satire today. Delegates praised the city’s “healthy and beautiful” setting, its “friendly, pious, generous and humane” inhabitants, and its laws that “protect all without regard to complexion.”

They had already secured $1,000 from New York philanthropist Arthur Tappan and identified a building: the former Steamboat Hotel on Water Street. Their governance structure was a board of seven trustees, four of them Black. Everything was ready to go.

And then Nat Turner happened.

## 700 to 4

In late August 1831, an enslaved man named Nat Turner led a rebellion in Southampton County, Virginia. Somewhere between 55 and 65 white people were killed before the uprising was violently suppressed. The news reached Connecticut in early September.

*Depiction of Nat Turner’s rebellion*

Within days, New Haven’s white leadership mobilized. Mayor Dennis Kimberly, a Yale graduate, called an emergency town meeting for Saturday, September 10, 1831. The stated purpose was to consider “a scheme said to be in progress for the establishment in this city of a college for the education of colored youth.”

Hundreds of white men crammed into a 3,000 square foot room in the newly built State House.

The resolution they passed had two parts. The first declared that “the immediate emancipation of slaves” and the founding of a Black college was “an unwarrantable and dangerous interference with the internal concerns of other states.” In other words, New Haven’s white leadership formally voted to protect slavery.

The second part was more local and more lethal. It declared that a Black college would be “incompatible with the prosperity, if not the existence,” of Yale and other schools. The reasoning was blunt: Yale’s student body included “sons of southern planters.”

The final vote was 700 against. Four in favor.

One of those four was John Warner Barber, a New Haven engraver and abolitionist who kept a copy of a pamphlet about the college proposal. In his own handwriting, he scrawled on the cover: “To be carefully preserved.” He knew the story would be buried. He was right.

## The Mob and the Law

The town meeting was just the beginning. In the weeks that followed, white rage spilled into the streets. Rioters attacked the New Haven home of Arthur Tappan, a Black-owned house on Mount Pleasant was “leveled to the ground,” and a Black man was knocked down in the street. Stones were thrown at the homes of white abolitionists.

Southern newspapers cheered. A Georgia paper praised New Haven for acting “promptly and vigorously in opposition to the ill-judged and unjustifiable enthusiasm of misguided zealots.” A Richmond paper called it “an admirable lesson.” A Mississippi paper noted that New Haven’s response would be “duly appreciated by the people of the South.”

Then, in 1833, the Connecticut legislature passed what became known as the Black Law, making it illegal to educate Black students from out of state. The law was aimed directly at Prudence Crandall, a white teacher who had opened an academy for Black women in Canterbury, Connecticut. Crandall was arrested and her case went to trial. The presiding judge was David Daggett, a Yale alumnus, former faculty member, and one of the four men who spoke against the Black college at the 1831 town meeting. Daggett ruled that Black people were not citizens under the Constitution and therefore had no legal standing.

Twenty-four years later, Chief Justice Roger Taney cited Connecticut’s Black Law and Daggett’s reasoning in Dred Scott v. Sandford, the 1857 Supreme Court decision that declared Black Americans “had no rights which the white man was bound to respect.”

The ripple effects of one town meeting in New Haven reached the highest court in the land.

## A Highway Where a Legacy Should Stand

The site where the college would have been built, at the intersection of Water Street and what are now Interstate 91 and Interstate 95, is today just asphalt and exhaust. No acknowledgment that this was once the proposed home of something that could have changed American history.

Imagine if that college had opened in 1832. Imagine two centuries of Black doctors, lawyers, teachers, and engineers graduating from an institution founded before the Civil War. Imagine the generational wealth, the intellectual tradition, the community infrastructure.

Instead, New Haven protected Yale’s revenue streams and the comfort of Southern slaveholders. The city sent a message that echoed through legislatures and courtrooms for decades: Black advancement was a threat to white prosperity, and white prosperity would be protected by any means necessary.

The “Shining Light on Truth” exhibition at the Schwarzman Center is a start. It puts names and faces to the people who fought for the college and to those who destroyed it.

History isn’t just what happened, it’s also what was prevented. The 1831 Black college proposal was stopped by 700 men who believed Yale’s reputation mattered more than Black freedom. Because of them, people drive over the site of what could have been America’s first HBCU every single day …

And they don’t even know it.
