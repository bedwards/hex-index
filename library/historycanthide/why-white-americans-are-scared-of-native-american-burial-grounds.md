We’ve told this story so many times that the “Indian burial ground” has become a punchline. Stephen King used it in *Pet Sematary* and *The Shining*, and *Poltergeist* and *Amityville Horror* relied on it. In 2011, *The Onion* released a satirical video claiming the U.S. economy was failing because the nation was “built on ancient Indian burial grounds.” The joke worked because everyone immediately got the reference. The trope is embedded in our cultural DNA, but we didn’t inherit it from Indigenous cultures. We invented it, and what it reveals about white America is more disturbing than any ghost story.

### How the Burial Ground Trope Became Horror’s Default

Stephen King perfected the Indigenous burial ground trope in 1983 with *Pet Sematary*, which introduced readers to a cursed Micmac burial ground that resurrects the dead as violent, soulless versions of themselves. It became one of the bestselling horror novels ever published. King had already used the device in *The Shining* three years earlier, where the Overlook Hotel is built on a Native American burial ground, with recurring Indigenous imagery throughout the film.

*Poltergeist* (1982) played with audience expectations by revealing the haunted subdivision was built not on an Indian burial ground, but on a white cemetery where a developer moved the headstones. The fact that the film needed to explicitly deny the Indigenous connection shows how dominant the trope had become.

By the 1990s and 2000s, the burial ground backstory appeared everywhere. *Buffy the Vampire Slayer*, *The Simpsons*, *South Park*, *Family Guy*, and *Parks and Recreation* all referenced it. Ghost hunting reality shows like *Ghost Adventures* routinely blame “demonic entities” on disturbed Indigenous graves. The narrative is that Indigenous dead are angry, vengeful, and dangerous, while white dead rest peacefully in their well-maintained cemeteries.

In 1977, Jay Anson’s *The Amityville Horror* claimed the infamous house was built on a Shinnecock “enclosure for the sick, mad, and dying,” land infested with demons. Two years later, celebrity ghost hunter Hans Holzer investigated and announced that an Indigenous chief’s ghost had possessed Ronald DeFeo Jr., making him murder his entire family. The Shinnecocks never lived in that area. There was never a burial ground there, and no archaeological evidence or oral history exists to back this false claim. But the story legitimized Holzer’s career and sold millions of books and movie tickets.

### Actual Desecration of Native Burial Sites

While Americans were inventing stories about cursed burial grounds, we were actually destroying real ones without facing any consequences, supernatural or otherwise. In February 2020, the Trump administration began blasting through Monument Hill at Arizona’s Organ Pipe Cactus National Monument to build a border wall. The site is a sacred burial ground for Apache warriors. The administration bypassed the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act using federal waivers, and ignored all consultation with the Tohono O’odham Nation, whose land and members exist on both sides of the border.

Between 1915 and 1990, approximately 3,000 ancestral Native Hawaiians were systematically removed from the Mokapu Peninsula sand dunes to make way for a Marine Corps base. The University of Hawaii and the Bishop Museum coordinated the “excavation,” a clinical word for grave robbery. Most Native Hawaiians never even knew this was happening.

In 1988, developers archaeologically removed approximately 1,100 ancestral Native Hawaiian remains to build the Ritz-Carlton Hotel at Honokahua, Maui. The mass desecration only stopped because of vociferous protests, and the hotel was relocated after the public outcry.

For over a century, museums in the United States and Europe collected Native American skulls and bones in the name of “science.” The American Museum of Natural History, Field Museum, University of Pennsylvania, Smithsonian, Harvard, Yale, and Dartmouth all participated. These “artifacts” were stolen from their resting places and displayed as “specimens” in museums and classrooms.

It wasn’t until 1990 that the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA) was passed, finally requiring institutions to inventory and return ancestral remains. By then, more than 5,000 Native Hawaiian ancestors alone had been desecrated, their remains scattered across museums worldwide. The Hawaiian organization Hui Malama I Na Kupuna O Hawai’i Nei has since repatriated approximately 5,607 individuals from 26 institutions across multiple countries.

### Projection and the Psychology of Settler Guilt

The “haunted burial ground” narrative transforms Indigenous people from victims into aggressors. It’s a psychological tactic that lets us feel afraid instead of ashamed. The narrative also places Indigenous people firmly in the past. The phrase “ancient Indian burial ground” appears constantly, as if Native Americans existed only in some distant, pre-colonial time. Meanwhile, living Indigenous communities still fight for sovereignty, land rights, and basic recognition everyday. This is the “vanishing Indian” myth embedded in horror. Native people become frozen in time, relevant only as exotic and supernatural forces.

We apply totally different standards to our own dead. White American cemeteries are legally protected, culturally sacred, and treated with the utmost respect. Disturbing them is unthinkable. But Native burial grounds are routinely bulldozed for highways, pipelines, shopping malls, luxury hotels, and border walls. The selective application of “respect for the dead” tells you whose lives and deaths we actually value.

### Why We Need Indigenous People to Be Threats

The Indigenous burial ground obsession reveals something uncomfortable about the American psyche. This nation was built on stolen land through violence and broken treaties. Every town, highway, and suburb exists because someone else was displaced, and that displacement didn’t happen peacefully.

The burial ground narrative lets us acknowledge this history while completely avoiding its implications—we get to feel scared instead of responsible. We get to cast ourselves as victims instead of beneficiaries, and focus on imaginary hauntings instead of the very real, ongoing harms to Native communities.

We’ve built an entire horror genre around our inability to reckon with what this country did to Native Americans. But the truth is, the ghosts aren’t angry or coming for us. They’re not even there. What haunts America is our refusal to acknowledge what happened to them and what continues to happen to their descendants. We’re haunted by our own guilt, projected onto the very people we displaced.

So this Halloween, when you watch a horror movie about a cursed burial ground, consider why we continue to cast Indigenous people as threats. Consider what we’re so afraid they might claim. The answer, of course, is everything.
