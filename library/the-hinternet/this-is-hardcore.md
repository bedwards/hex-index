### 1.

Unreliable sources will tell you that the French word for “fox” is *renard*. But this is in truth only a recent imposition (over the past 800 years or so). The correct French word is *goupil*, while *renard*, though written today in lowercase, is really a proper name, of Germanic origin. *Reinhard* or *Reinhardt* in turn has sometimes been glossed in popular etymology as “Pure of Heart”, though the truth is you just can’t get *hardt* from *Herz*, and the real meaning is something more like “strong in counsel”*.* It is however difficult not to read the name from the point of view of modern German, as a pairing of two attributes, *rein* and *hart*: “pure-hard”, or perhaps “so hardcore”. And curiously, given that the English *core* derives ultimately from the Latin *cor*, we seem to have arrived back where we started.

The fox anyhow is supposed to be, in medieval Europe as in Indigenous North America and elsewhere, the consummate trickster. While this is a character profile that we may naturally associate with hard-heartedness, it is much more difficult to link it with *pure*\-heartedness, unless perhaps we draw inspiration from Kierkegaard and conceive such purity as belonging to any being who *wills only one thing*. Renart, the protagonist of Europe’s greatest animal epic —the medieval template for *Tom and Jerry* and *Itchy and Scratchy* and basically everything else in that venerable mode of imagining, through anthropomorphism, a comic and ultraviolent fun-house-mirror world parallel to our own— is plainly pure-hearted in at least this sense. He wills, namely, nothing but *harm* to those around him (perhaps with the exception of his dear wife and friend, the vixen Hermeline), and nothing but ill-gotten gains for himself.

This might be Kierkegaard’s fox but it is not Tolstoy’s or Isaiah Berlin’s: Renart does not waste his time sniffing around, now at this, now at that, but thoroughly digs in, like Tolstoy’s hedgehog, to the one thing that matters to him: pure malice.

Just how malicious is Renart? His worst deed, surely, is…

…the rape of Hersent the she-wolf, wife of Ysengrin. When he is finished he goes and roughs up her wolf-pups, calling them bastards, pissing on their heads, leaving them in tears \[*Les loviax a lessiez plorant*\]. He particularly enjoys seeing his enemies get skinned, as when he tricks Brun the bear into sticking his head into a hollow tree-trunk in the hope of extracting honey, or when he convinces King Noble the lion that Ysengrin is mocking the clergy by wearing a monk’s hood: in fact the “hood” is the wolf’s natural fur, which only looks like a garment because the hide has already been stripped from the rest of Ysengrin’s body and caused to be made into a blanket for King Noble.

Early cartoons, you will know, loved a good skinning almost as much as Renart does: all those animals, veins and muscles exposed, stomping around angrily, holding their own hide in their hands, or getting even nuder and “[dancing around in their bones](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iYyQF223vuE)”, as Lee Morse sang in 1928, at roughly the same time these animation tropes were first emerging. But it’s really not so bad to lose your skin, as animals in these mirror-worlds display a remarkable capacity for regeneration of lost parts. Brun and Ysengrin both recover the entirety of their outer layers, just as Wile E. Coyote recovers from every fall, and just as every disgusting bump on the head, typically appearing after a strike with a mallet, sometimes accompanied by fluttering bluebirds, will eventually detumesce so that the animal who bore it can return to his usual schemes. Brun even returns from the dead at least once, though this may be less a reflection of the mirror-world’s peculiar metaphysics of death, than of the *Roman de Renart*’s palimpsest quality, having been successively modified, and spun out along more than one “branch”, between the 11th and 14th centuries.

### 2.

Much of the plot of the *Roman de Renart* portrays the aftermath at court of Renart’s deeds, Noble’s well-meaning but inefficacious attempt to adjudicate Renart’s case, and various other animal-courtiers pleas both for and against him. Grimbert the badger, who is somehow also Renart’s cousin, is the closest our protagonist has to an ally. Chantecler the cock, having lost a few of his brood to Renart’s gruesome coop raids, is disconsolate, and presses for death. I confess I don’t know much about medieval law and jurisprudence, but I still get a strong sense that the *Roman*’s depiction of these legal proceedings rather distorts the historical truth. Again and again, Renart is given a chance to defend himself, and each time he is able to get out of what appears to be an imminent hanging — by agreeing to go off to fight in the Holy Land, by promising to travel to some far-off kingdom, made up on the spot, from which he will bring back the invented foreign king’s daughter to marry Noble (in an earlier scene Noble is already married, which we learn only because Renart at one point determines to rape Noble’s wife).

The mission to bring back a foreign princess turns out to be among the most delirious episodes in the entire work. Hermeline informs Renart that in Spain one may learn the art of *nigromance*, which appears to be a deformation of “necromancy”, but in context means something closer to “black magic”. Renart crosses the Pyrenees and enters a mythical land, the popular French image of which had largely been shaped by the figure of the Andalusian philosopher, who knew how to penetrate nature’s secrets and turn them to his own ends. Renart just happens to be captured by the very same magus Hermeline had sent him to find, whose underlings would like to kill him for food right away, but who determines it would be better to fatten the fox up first. This buys Renart some time, which he uses to study the magus’s art, and this involves the use of a Brazen Head, much like the one described by the alchemist Roger Bacon in 13th-century Oxford — a sort of genie-in-a-bottle conceit, but also, obviously, a clear precursor of ChatGPT and similar demonic devices.

Renart visits this entity in secret and has it generate for him a whole troop of exotic animals, some luminescent, some with 40 heads. Among them is a lioness, which he brings back to King Noble, along with the rest of this new zoo crew, to present to the lion-monarch as his bride. The king is ravished, and ready to submit to any request she makes. She calls on him to organize a sort of acrobatic competition between her gang of fantastical creatures and the king’s own animal courtiers, to a few of whom we have already been introduced. It starts well, but then Brun makes a bad landing and shits himself. Brichemer the stag —a *Hart* in German— shits himself too. The contest is a total bust, but then the lioness, under Renart’s nigromantic power, spots Ysengrin, mistakes the fur of his head and neck for a monk’s cowl, and tells King Noble that he will never have her until that impious wolf has the garment torn off. So Noble commands it, which affords us another opportunity to see Ysengrin stomping around wearing nothing but his slick wet meatsuit.

### 3.

The kinship relations, social ontology, and taxonomy of these animals are, to say the least, only vaguely defined. Cousinage in particular is a flexible notion — at one point Renart tries to convince even Chantecler —a bird— that the two of them are cousins. Marriage across species boundaries does happen, and if it is disapproved no one says so explicitly. Like Goofy the dog in the Walt Disney universe, who himself is sometimes depicted as having a dog, the animals at Noble’s court can often be found riding horseback. And at the same time as Renart is on trial for his natural carnivorism, sumptuous dinners of game are often served at court.

Sometimes the actions described are so specific to the anatomy of human bodies —such as being hanged, or straddling a horse, or indeed having sex, in the particular manner this is depicted in the *Roman*— that it is as if the presumptive author of the oldest layer of the palimpsest, Pierre de Saint-Cloud (fluorit c. 1175-1180), has momentarily forgotten that his characters are only anthropomorphs, and not proper *anthropoi*. There are also humans in the world of the *Roman*, always peasants, who often interact on equal terms, indeed on speaking terms, with the animals. And there are in turn animals that are “just animals”, not anthropomorphs, such as the ones that are served at meals. Curiously, equids seem seldom to rise above the “mere animal”, and when at one point a donkey begins to speak to the peasant it serves, this is represented somewhat as a supernatural occurrence, very much comparable to the portrayal of talking horses in Siberian Turkic oral epic (where, incidentally, the fox is *not* a trickster).[1](#footnote-1)

Saint-Cloud has written a satire of 12th-century northern French courtly and clerical culture, not a zoology treatise, nor even a bestiarium of the sort you might have seen from Albertus Magnus in roughly the same period. If you are attentive you can indeed discern much of the real historical world in this inverted fantastical one. The geographical imaginaire is well defined — in addition to Andalusia and the Holy Land, we find scattered mentions of Constantinople, from where Noble’s most trusted advisor, an unnamed and very learnèd camel who speaks in pseudo-Latin, ultimately arrives. There are more frequent allusions to Rome, and whenever a given idea or fashion or bit of gossip needs to be represented as widespread, our author will describe it as being spoken of “from here to Puglia”, “from here to Sicily”.

The most vivid representation of medieval reality in the *Roman* comes across in conflict over the *droit de chasse*, the right of the hunt that was so central to the system of seigneurial privilege, and that prevented ordinary peasants from game-hunting on the grounds of their feudal lord. This becomes an issue when Liétard the peasant curses his bull Rougel for being lazy. He says he would like for some wild animal to come and devour him. Brun the bear overhears this, and appears to tell Liétard that by rights he must now forfeit his bull. Liétard stalls for time, which for some reason always works in the world of the *Roman*, asking Brun to return the following day. Renart shows up and pretends to be Liétard’s friend, and together they devise a scheme to trap Brun and to kill him. When Brun returns, Liétard pretends that the Seigneur’s hunters are near, and offers to hide him.

> Bien li semble qu’eschapez ert  
> Des veneors, mes sa mort quert.
> 
> He thinks he’s escaped the hunters  
> But it’s his death that’s coming to get him.

Liétard convinces Brun to crawl into a hole, and to close his eyes. Then:

> Quant il l’ot mout bien avisé  
> Son coup a loi d’ome sené,  
> Qui de faillir se doutoit trop,  
> Sor la teste jete son cop,  
> Fiert et refiert de tel aïr  
> Qu’il en a fait le sanc saillir.  
> Tel coup li donne de rechief  
> Que tot li a brisié le chief.
> 
> After carefully preparing his blow,  
> Concerned not to miss it,  
> He strikes him on the head.  
> He hits and hits again,  
> With such violence  
> As to make the blood gush forth.  
> He delivers another blow so strong  
> That it utterly shatters his skull.

And so Brun dies, though he’ll be regenerated by the time of Renart’s trial. But this is really just the beginning of Liétard’s troubles, as now he and his wife Brunmatin have to figure out how to hide all this contraband bear-meat without the seigneur’s men catching a literal whiff of it. Renart comes back and offers, duplicitously, to “help”, and it is at this point that the donkey pipes up too. They devise a plan, whose details I had trouble following, in which the donkey plays dead for some reason. Brunmatin attaches herself to him with a strap, but something causes the donkey to dart, and he drags her with him, and shits on her as he goes. And Renart watches, the distal cause of it all, neither gleeful nor yet afraid of the torments of hell that, as everyone keeps warning, he’s bringing down on himself more ineluctably with every malicious deed. He doesn’t ever laugh, but somehow I picture him squinting, like that one meme of the dachshund, I believe it is, where you just know it has been up to no good, and which may, if I’m recalling correctly, have been said to bear some resemblance to Vladimir Putin.

The explanation of Brun’s perpetual pratfalls, in turn, has much to do with earlier power-plays in European history, vividly recounted by Michel Pastoureau in his *[The Bear: History of a Fallen King](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674047822)* (2011). Why *is* an African animal, extinct in Europe since the Paleolithic, installed as monarch in the world envisioned by Saint-Cloud and his successors? Why, from the late Middle Ages onwards, does the lion figure so consistently in heraldic tradition, while the bear is only ever further marginalized? Pastoureau’s argument is surprising. He says there was a real imperative for ecclesiastical power to stamp out the surviving remnants of the pre-Christian bear cult, still a very real presence in the Middle Ages, as when Viking raiders put on the bear-sark and went *berserk*, and as even today, when villagers in the Pyrenees continue the tradition, at festival time, of transforming themselves into bears in a similar fashion. Unless you live out your life wedged deep in the mountains, however, it has been a long time indeed since the bear enjoyed any official public reverence on this old continent. The *Roman de Renart* did much to relegate the bear to the status he has held throughout the entire modern period — that of court buffoon, of unicyclist in chains, held in turn, often, by men from the margins of Europe who seem, by depriving the bear of its natural-born wildness, to have absorbed that wildness into themselves.

### 4.

What now, may we ask, *is* all this? Of course the *Roman de Renart* is a satire of human affairs, but for my part I’m more interested in the cognitive dimensions of the work than the political and social dimensions. What is it, I want to know, about the human representation of non-human nature, particularly animal nature, that enables stories like these to make immediate sense to us? And if this is a satire of distinctly medieval European social institutions, why is so much of it so perfectly continuous with Walt Disney and Warner Brothers?

Claude Lévi-Strauss famously wrote that animals are “good to think with”. And they are: a society really can do a good deal of the work of world-ordering through the symbolic roles attributed to non-human species. Structuralism was however averse to thinking historically, which is why the poststructuralists felt so audacious in coming along and insisting on studying “genealogies” instead of “structures” — both of which are a bunch of stupid nonsense, admittedly, if you are, say, [Bentham’s Bulldog](https://benthams.substack.com/p/how-continental-philosophers-argue). But if you are an actual intellectual you can easily appreciate why this move had to be made when and where it was made.[2](#footnote-2) And when you look at Saint-Cloud’s manner of “thinking with animals”, it is difficult to fail to notice not only its fundamental similarity to our own, but also the fundamental rupture that had already occurred well before the Middle Ages, and that places the medievals in a world very much like our own, and very much unlike that of the Amazonian groups Lévi-Strauss was attending to, and indeed very much unlike that of the pre-agricultural peoples of prehistoric Europe.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Zd06!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F597b03b9-3939-4b0d-b69f-448ed953004c_946x1452.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Zd06!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F597b03b9-3939-4b0d-b69f-448ed953004c_946x1452.png)

Nowhere in fact does this rupture come across more vividly than in the institution of the *droit de chasse*. Agriculture, on a common understanding, is supposed to have replaced subsistence-level hunting in consequence of its superiority, which is generally seen as connected to the fact that we were now able more efficiently to store up food surpluses for times of shortage. But what was the real fate of hunting after agriculture? It did not go away, but only transformed into a privilege for the elite, and a crime for the people who now provided the labor to maintain the agricultural economy from year to year. For these people —and it *is* these people that the earliest articulations of the stories of Renart et al. were meant to entertain— animals can no longer serve as totems, or as quasi-divine sociocosmic Others, but only, so to speak, as a variety of distorted humans, as *us* through the looking glass, and hardly in our best light. The medievals, like us, were already alienated from animals, and this alienation is a precondition of the animals’ role as vehicles of satire, rather than what they are known to be in many mythopoetic traditions of pre-agricultural and non-sedentist cultures throughout the world: embodiments of all the power and wisdom and order of nature.

I’m an agricultural fellow myself, of sorts —my earliest aspirations were to be the king of the mechanical bull, which I rode while wearing a pinned-on number at an all-ages country-western roadhouse somewhere in the Central Valley of California—, and I *love* to see animals getting their heads stuck in logs, getting skinned alive, getting blown up by Acme dynamite. G. E. Lessing for his part was focusing on a very different sort of animal tales —mostly Aesop and La Fontaine—, when he wrote *On the Use of Animals in Fables* in 1759. For Lessing the use of animals in place of humans is not primarily for our amusement, but serves to make a fable’s universal truths clearer by simplifying human motives. Such fables thus have the power to illustrate general moral laws through specific cases, and there is in them an implicit analogy between animal behavior and human reasoning, with the result that the sort of instruction intended might be thought of as the translation into an entertainment medium of a moral philosophy grounded in natural law.

But trickster tales really don’t have much to teach, other than that the world is a neverending cycle of deceit, comeuppance, and revenge. Renart and his companions are getting theirs, for good or for ill. Page after page, almost a millennium after Renart’s creation, a reader can still find himself thinking, with sincere awe: I can’t believe it. That motherfucker is at it *again*. Not all medieval literature feels nearly so familiar and immediate.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MdeA!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbf9eca61-acc8-463b-8b79-a227f26c96d9_940x1446.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MdeA!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbf9eca61-acc8-463b-8b79-a227f26c96d9_940x1446.png)

The editing and plot mechanics of the *Roman* plainly belong to another world, and bear significant traces of orality — the same events will often be recounted three or four times, the first time as they “actually” happen, and the next times as new characters arrive who need to be briefed on what *has* happened. But the characters, and the relations between them, belong entirely to our age, or at least to an age I have lived through, an age of alienation from animals, which enables them to come forward not as gods, and rarely even —*pace* Lessing— as moral exemplars, but mostly only as exaggerations of human perversity.

### 5.

Among the greatest works of 20th-century art I would include, without hesitation, the first Walt Disney animated short, in 1928, featuring [Steamboat Willie](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I5pG1wbRKOg). If you don’t take my judgment seriously, take if from Sergeï Eïsensteïn, who in 1940 wrote of early Disney that its output represents “a triumph over all fetters, over everything that binds”.

When I watch Steamboat Willie I am myself still filled with awe, but also with a sense of loss too great for words. I am reminded that as recently as my own childhood the rural scenes and gestures depicted in that animated short were features of everyday reality — one still knew how to measure up the value of a cow at a glance, or how to discern the signs of sickness in poultry. The most direct descendant of Renart in American mass entertainment is likely Woody Woodpecker — who even looks like a fox (perhaps a cousin?). Willie for his part, ostensibly a mouse, albeit one in charge of several species of domestic animals, is nonetheless able to channel with undiminished intensity the pure insouciant tricksterish malice of his medieval vulpine ancestor. Does he have any thought at all of the innate moral worth of the animals he is playing like musical instruments, squeezing and contorting into unnatural shapes entirely for his own amusement? Is it because they are “only” animals that it is acceptable to do this? If so, again, what is *he*?

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tw3A!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa6c987cd-1290-436a-9f9a-fc160f26fcc8_942x1440.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tw3A!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa6c987cd-1290-436a-9f9a-fc160f26fcc8_942x1440.png)

Somewhere in Stanley Cavell there is passing mention of this peculiarity about cartoons — that in their true nature (a nature that is concealed in many of cartooning’s evolutionary branches, notably manga), they *cannot* depict human beings. This rule would seem to be proven rather than disproven by those apparent exceptions coming from the golden age of animation, such as Betty Boop and Popeye, who in truth are so grotesque in their bodily forms as barely to count as human at all. This cartoon-historical point is in turn made explicit when, in a rare *Simpsons* scene, Bart briefly hallucinates a nightmare world in which his family members are all richly drawn, anatomically correct *Homo sapiens*. The Simpsons are allowed to be people, but in order to be “normal” people, by cartoon logic, they *have* to be stubby, glabrous, yellow, three-fingered approximations of people.

*The Simpsons*, we may agree, is “meta” in many respects, and one of these lies in the fact that the tradition extending from the *Roman de Renart* through Hanna-Barbera, of depicting malicious animals scheming against one another, can now only appear as a show within a show. You might recall the episode where Marge goes on a campaign against violent cartoons, and forces the local TV station to pull *Itchy and Scratchy*. Krusty the Clown, who hosts the children’s show featuring this cartoon, is forced to offer some strange Eastern European play of avant-garde geometric forms that briefly, if abstractly, take on the appearance of a cat and mouse. “What the hell was that?!” he blurts out to the gathered children. And what that was, in addition to being a lovely satire of the great experimental work of Soviet Bloc animators, was a vision of the future — a world where we are by now so removed from the agricultural setting that the world of perverted animals satirically acting out our malicious fantasies has finally come to appear to belong to another time, just as the hunter’s world of quasi-divine animals already belonged to another time for the early audiences at recitations of the *Roman de Renart*.

Today we are mostly entertained by flickering lights, content that scrolls by so fast we barely know who is doing what to whom. As for representations of violence, today we plainly have a preference for seeing it done by men, and if you are yourself a man, and a young one, chances are you are seeing it done from the perspective of a first-person shooter game. I am not just being a nostalgic child of the 20th century when I say I long to return to a world where bulldogs get hit over the head with mallets, only to find a strange excrescence shooting up in the stricken spot, with two small hairs growing out of it as out of a mole, only to look up and to sees stars orbiting the mound, as it begins throbbing and pulsing out, in Morse code, the letters that spell “O-U-C-H”.

I love it when stuff like that happens. Having seen so much of it early in life, a deep part of me continues to believe that that is how the world actually works. As Eïsensteïn understood, it really is a pure expression of freedom, transmitted through a new organic and spontaneous art-form that can only properly have animals as its subjects.

### 6.

But allow me to finish with a few of the *Roman*’s other proper names of note. One is Maupertuis, the name of Renart’s fortified castle, resistant to all siege, where our hero periodically retreats after his violent sprees. There are several variations on the name —Maleperduis, Maupertuus, and even the roughly Englished “Malperdy”—, and though they appear to have something to do with *millepertuis*, St. John’s wort, it is far more likely that the term means, simply, “difficult passage”. Maupertuis is also, of course, the name of Pierre Louis Moreau de Maupertuis, the great French mathematician and materialist, who wrote the *Vénus physique* in 1745, attempting to account for human racial diversity in materialist terms. This coincidence would probably not be worth commenting on, if not for the fact that one of Renart’s own sons, briefly introduced in a scene that takes place within the walls of Maupertuis, is named, as it happens, Malebranche.

This is of course the name of the great French philosopher Nicolas Malebranche, author of *À la recherché de la vérité* (1676), and like “Maupertuis” it begins with a morpheme suggestive of something bad, arduous, or unfortunate. What is unfortunate in this case is the “branch” or “stem” that little Malebranche represents. A branch can, as we have already seen, be conceived by the philologist as a chain of successive additions to a manuscript tradition, such as the one that has yielded up the *Roman de Renart*. It can also be conceived, by the genealogist, as a line of descent: that little fox cub is the *bad seed*, brother, born of his hardcore dad, and he will pass his bad seed down in turn, almost to the present day… only for the lineage to die out, we may imagine, in a glorious fight-cloud, from which fists and feet appear here and there, and nail-studded planks and hairballs and chickens and God knows what fly out inexplicably, sometime, give or take a few years, circa 1975.
