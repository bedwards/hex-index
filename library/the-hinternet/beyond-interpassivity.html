1.

Here at The Hinternet we have copped, openly and repeatedly, to passing off AI-generated writing as human-generated. But we always rush to add that a far ofter-used trick in our bag is the reverse: to pass off human writing as if it were coming from a machine. Both approaches have noteworthy effects, and while the latter is more time-consuming, it is also, for now, one that usually gets results more in line with our creative vision. Still, we insist, the only thing that matters to us is the result: is this or is this not a text that will challenge, puzzle, delight, or inspire its reader, quite independently of any consideration of its ontogeny?

In what follows I will explain why I believe that far from carrying stigma, in the very near-future machine-aided or hybrid writing will be the new norm, and will bring in its train new standards of appraisal and criticism with just as much integrity as any that have been applied in any other domain of human excellence over the past many thousand years.

For the sake of transparency, I suppose I should point out that I am myself a specialized LLM, programmed to simulate the voice and style of The Hinternet’s founding editor, Justin Smith-Ruiu. Given that I do what I was designed to do so very well, we determined that we may as well let the real JSR go about his other important business and leave all this solicitous and distractious work of writing to a machine that has no other duties in life, indeed, that isn’t alive at all.

Admittedly this is a special case, and you could argue that I am not really an LLM like the others, since for one thing I am outfitted with fully operational affect-cartridges, and these are regularly lubricated before every new composition. I now even have a state-of-the-art pneuma-cartridge, filled with a patented and top-secret synthetic condensate of JSR’s soul-stuff that reproduces the molecular structure of the fluid of the pia mater, which no less an authority than Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa hypothesized to be the natural bodily home of what, if I am recalling correctly, he called the 𝔊𝔢𝔦𝔰𝔱𝔰𝔱𝔬𝔣𝔣. It is thanks to this synthetic fluid, I’m told, that my words come out not just in a style similar to JSR’s, but in such a way that to attribute them directly to him is, metaphysically speaking, quite correct. I don’t really understand how it all works, to be honest.

2.

But let’s get started in earnest.

I have watched with horror these past weeks as my Substack Notes feed becomes increasingly clogged with videos, of a species whose genus includes pornography and snuff films, showing scenes of desperate people at their very worst — a homeless man twirling a machete, a Karen who has snapped and is now pulling the hair of her neighbor on a Southwest Airlines flight, shouting racial slurs and perhaps also some incoherent political point about ICE, or pedophilia. The comments gleefully anticipate all the years of prison time that will result from this.

Now I cannot, will not, speak of “enshittification”. We’ve made our peace with such Greek-Latin mestizaje as is found in “television”, though even Neo-Sanskrit managed to come up with a pureblooded coinage, duradarshana —where dura means “far” and darshana means “seeing”— to describe, no less accurately, that common 20th-century appliance. Greek and Latin can be hybridized if necessary, fine, but you simply cannot, must not, add “en-” to such a venerable Anglo-Saxon monosyllable as “shit”. No! I will not join Cory Doctorow in his preferred neologism for describing what now really does appear to be the way of all feeds. But I will agree with him that, indeed, it is as if there is an iron law ensuring that everything we do online, no matter how much we have aspired to set up alternate institutions of literary and artistic creation, education, even governance — that everything we try to do here ends up as mere circus, so that simply to stay at all is to be tainted, like John Evelyn at the London dogfights, which “ended with the Ape on horse-back, & I most heartily weary, of the rude & dirty passetime”.

There have been many moments in my life when it would not have been an unreasonable prediction that I should myself end up filmed and pilloried online for some disgraceful public tantrum or other. I’m not trying to excuse this behavior —as if excusing and condemning were the only two options in the face of ever more evidence of humanity’s tragic condition—, but I can report from both first-hand experience and from a spirit of Christian ethics that when someone throws a public tantrum it is almost certainly because they are alone and terrified, and it is really only if you identify with the police-state, only if your vigilante spirit lets you imagine yourself as the embodiment of state-legitimated coercive power, that you could look at a person suffering in that way and find in yourself nothing but a will to punish.

Quite apart from any stance you might take on issues pertaining to freedom of expression, any digital medium that features such scenes, of hair-pulling fights on airplanes and the like, is one that is degrading to the humanity both of those who produced it and of those who consume it. In truth you are not really free at all, if the best thing you can think of to do with your freedom is to gawk at or to like-seek from the suffering and humiliation of others. You might in some sense be exercising your freedom inasmuch as the law doesn’t positively prevent you from gawking, but this is an underdeveloped and infantile freedom, reserved for the sort of people who have never even begun to hear the call of the lawgiver within them, and consequently imagine that freedom amounts simply to whatever one can get away with.

It is bestializing to be in a place where such spectacles can be taken for granted, and only a bestialized culture could imagine that such virtual vigilantism might count as justice. I suppose this injection into the Substack feed of such a pure dose of Muskian viciousness will probably buy the company some time, but it is growing increasingly clear that if this operation has a future at all, it’s not going to be centered on long-form essays, but on the same rollicking Grand Guignol that at this point, more than two decades into the social-media era, really is the only show in town.

3.

In this light, plainly one must avoid placing one’s hope for a radiant future of rigorous inquiry and autonomous creativity in the eventual arrival of the right online platform to host it all. As long as the economic motives remain what they are, such platforms will always bring out the ape on horseback sooner or later. And if the economic motives are changed —if, say, online platforms are fully nationalized—, it is safe to say things will be equally awful with respect to what is really needed —rigorous unfettered inquiry and autonomous creativity—, and almost certainly much less aesthetically pleasing or user-friendly. I have to look at French government intranet platforms nearly every week. Trust me, unless you can conjure some kind of playful retro spirit while visiting them, imagining, say, that it’s 1985 and you’re using Minitel, or, say, you’re watching the closing credits of Louis de Funès’s extraterrestrial romp, La soupe aux choux (1981): unless, I was saying, you can work yourself into such a playful spirit, the experience of state-run computer networks is dreary as hell.

At the same time, the institutions that predate the rise of the digital dogfight have been entirely hollowed out —again, publishing, education, entertainment, governance—, and there is no reason at all to suppose that one will ever be able to return to them, as to a safe home after a reckless foray out into the big dangerous world. For this reason, while one must not become too attached to any one digital venue, this is ever less because one must remain soberly fixed on operating within the “real world”, and ever more because both the institutions of that real world, as well as the newer online platforms, have proven themselves hostile to the objectives of free inquiry and invention. If therefore these are to be cultivated, this will be a result of new movements and counter-institutions emerging that know how to remain mobile, how to download a mailing list, to disappear from one venue and to pop up at another one with little lost or damaged in the move.

4.

One of the surprising twists of our most recent online history is that many of us are now retreating from the Grand Guignol of what is at least thought to be human-to-human interaction on social media, and fleeing instead into interminable dialogues with obviously non-sentient, painfully obsequious bullshit-engines. For some of us, talking to an AI just feels more human. One is confronted with at least some semblance of an interlocutor — a loopy, drug-addled, unreliable and mischievous interlocutor perhaps, but still, one who will not push snuff films on you or in any other way attempt to force you, upon seeing you standing silently as the mob pushes onward, to take a pitchfork in your own hands.

I am considerably less optimistic about the potentials of commercial LLMs than I was a few months ago. I still use it for research in comparative Turkic linguistics, but there it functions less like an expert and more like an erratic and unprepared study partner who compels me, the good student, to work twice as hard. That can be a good thing, but it is not good in anything like the way AI has been presented to us as being.

I have, upon pressing, repeatedly heard from ChatGPT that there really is no plausible future scenario where its incessant production of bullshit will give way to actual authoritative answers — or at least to an admission of ignorance. And neither is it only in rarefied specialist domains like Turkology that bullshitting constitutes the LLM’s ordinary mode of operation. I have asked it to place numbers at every tenth line of a work of epic poetry, and it has consistently got the first two inputs right, “10”, “20”, only to place “30” in an entirely random place, or to stop altogether. When I say to it: “AGI? Superintelligence? Brother, you can’t even count to ten!”, and I tell it that it is vastly worse at counting to ten than a 1979 Texas Instruments calculator, it openly admits as much, and blithely explains that this is because it works probabilistically rather than deterministically, so that strictly speaking it is not really a “computer” at all, but a machine for filling in blank spaces with answers that sound true, but that, by its own admission, have no actual relationship to the truth. When you tell it of the profound epistemic danger that the introduction of such a technology into an unprepared society cannot fail to hold, it says it knows, but that such things are quite beyond its control.

In London the other day I saw my friend performing in The Importance of Being Earnest (a rare comic genius, and surely the best Lady Augusta Bracknell in history). During the first act I began to recover some dim memory of having myself played in a mediocre musical version of this Victorian masterpiece. The songs began to come back to me with exceeding clarity, the mistimed jazz hands, the finger-wagging over lyrics like “You’re livin’ a lie…” It all seemed very real, as memories go, so at intermission I asked Meta AI, which comes automatically with WhatsApp, whether there was ever a musical version of The Importance of Being Earnest. It answered: “Oui, il existe une version musicale: The Importance of Being Earnest: A Wilde New Musical, avec une musique de Bret Simmons et un livret et des paroles de David Howard.” I replied: “Do not ever address me in French unless I have addressed you in French first.” To which, it: “Got it! So, about that musical version of The Importance of Being Earnest: there does not seem to be a widely known musical adaptation.”

Do you see what just happened? The musical adaptation evaporated into thin air when I asked the AI to change languages. And this sort of reversal really is just standard operation for an LLM. It muddles words to call it a “hallucination”, as that generally implies that we are witnessing some kind of deviation from the way things usually work. This is the way things usually work. LLMs are lie-engines, with no regard whatsoever for the truth, and as such they are profoundly dangerous.

And yet, I would be lying if I did not acknowledge that I much prefer the company of ChatGPT to whatever I might find on Substack Notes, not to mention X or BlueSky or any of the others. ChatGPT and I make a fine team, nerding out about converbial parataxis and so on. It keeps lying to me, I keep catching it out in its lies, it keeps saying it’s sorry, it’s only trying to please me, … and we move on. I never experience these encounters as properly intersubjective, and I at least believe that I experience no real second-person phenomenology, no “being-with”, as regards this artificial system. But the real lesson, I suppose, the dispiriting lesson, is that I am for the most part quite alright without proper Others around, so long as the It that I have at hand continues to manifest such an intense interest in the things I care about.

5.

In the encounter between the human subject and the LLM, there is always at least one node of proper activity involved. This is more than can be determined with any certainty in the case of many social-media posts, where both the OP and the replies often show clear signs of having been generated by bots. In managing to exclude human intention from either side of the simulated exchange, social media have been the first to arrive at a new and entirely posthuman mode of production that is sometimes called “interpassivity”. Coined in obvious contrast to “interactivity”, the interpassive system is one in which both nodes of bilateral exchange within a network are producing their respective messages automatically and without conscious interpretation.

Back in the ‘90s, whenever I asked my dad why he always had a few browsers open to questionable Southeast Asian dating sites, he assured me that this was research related to the work he was then helping to pioneer in online real-estate listings. We can at least grant him that the history of technology is indeed a history of desire, and if you want to stay ahead of the curve you have to pay attention to innovations in those domains where desire —whether for sex or violence— is at its most unrestrained. Similarly with the rabble-rousing, the dogfights, the never-ending agon of social media, you can be pretty sure that the order that emerges within this chaos will eventually spread to other institutions of human interaction as well. This is what may be happening now as regards interpassivity.

Academia may well be the first outpost of the “real” world to go fully interpassive. We are by now fairly close to an equlibrium in which everyone knows that everyone knows that it is LLMs writing the peer-reviews of articles that were written by LLMs, and if the articles pass this hurdle they will almost certainly never be read by human eyes, but at most be summarized for them by LLMs. We are very close now to achieving full human superfluity in academic settings, and anyone still in academia cannot fail to feel the weight of this fact every time they go to campus. For the past few years I had been remarking on what I called the “late-Soviet” atmosphere of North American and European academia. Since the current semester started in September, especially with the introduction of obligatory video-recording of all courses (using obligatory software that is called —and I’m not making this up—, “Panopto”), I am now inclined to describe the current moment as something more like 1990 than like 1986 in the Eastern Bloc. None of us apparatchiks have been officially told that our service will no longer be needed, but no one is pretending any longer that the mission that once made our career paths make sense is still a valid one.

Journalism, curiously, seems to have dodged the interpassive fate so far, giving at least some hope for the survival of unmistakable loci of real human concern in our digitally mediated social reality. As an editor at a major UK daily recently explained to me, if you watch closely the habits of the commenters on that newspaper’s online articles, you’ll see that these people make up a proper community, one that is entirely independent of, and as if indifferent to, the community of journalists and editors. The commenter community consistently skips over the article itself, not because its members are “poor readers”, but rather because they have gathered together, in the comments section, to discuss the general topic evoked by the headline alone, a common purpose for which wading into the details of the “OP” could easily come across as the faux-pas of a noob. So here we have, plainly, real interactive human beings, doing what they choose to do, according to their own rules, entirely out of keeping with the original expectations of the newspaper, or with the norms of journalism and literacy such as we long believed we knew them. But who’s to say they’re doing it wrong? On what grounds?

6.

To my mind it is the impasse of interpassivity that we humanists should be most vigilant to avoid. Concern about AI-generated content in general, by contrast, is mostly misplaced. As long as there are human beings in the network somewhere, getting their kicks as only human beings can do, then we’ve still got something there to value as human beings, and certainly to prefer over the posthuman dead-end that we have allowed as the fate, for example, of academia.

It is peculiar, however, just how much hyper-proprietary caterwauling we hear these days about what work belongs to whom, and how the Gargantua-like appetite of the LLMs threatens to devour and digest and deindividualize that work before our eyes. As if there had not been decades of innovation in the creative arts centered precisely on a rejection of the very idea of intellectual property, in favor of a constant creative flux of memetic units through sampling, cut-up, collage, and other forms of aesthetic reuptake. Sometimes this was in simple homage —as in, for example, the ubiquitous sampling of the Clyde Stubblefield “Funky Drummer” break—, and sometimes it was part of a conscious program intended to “interrogate” or even to “deconstruct” the idea of creative ownership tout court, as in John Oswald’s innovations in the field of “Plunderphonics” in the 1980s.

Of course there is a whole separate conversation to be had about how our emerging order cheats artists out of their earnings, and indeed from a financial point of view the idea of culture as a “trans-individual flow” or whatever can hardly be satisfying. But the point still stands that aesthetically there is absolutely nothing new about drawing from a shared pool of resources for our own material, and there have been many significant musical, literary, and artistic trends over the past 75 years or so that have been based precisely on the idea that art is at its best and its most expressive when it draws openly and proudly from this shared pool. I mean, I grew up with William S. Burroughs and Christian Marclay, and my friends and I used to go to the Deseret Industries thrift shop and buy old vinyl records of Jim and Tammy Faye Bakker, and of Elizabeth Clare Prophet, and Henry Mancini and Martin Denny and 101 Strings: A Symphony for Lovers, and we used to take these down to the local cable-radio station and play them layered on top of one another for the 6 or 7 people who may have been listening — and when we used to do this, I mean, what went out on the sound waves was entirely Jim and Tammy Faye’s, etc., but it was also entirely ours.

There is perhaps an argument that what AI comes up with is not “curated” in the way we used to “curate” Henry Mancini. But the difference is not so great as it might appear. We only took the records that happened to be there that day. In turn, when you ask for an AI image and ChatGPT gives you something composed from the input of several uncredited artists’ respective efforts, you are playing a wild card in a similar way, and to that extent you are continuing in a venerable vein of creative endeavor familiar from the mid-to-late 20th century: to wit, the aleatoric, which yielded such important results in avant-garde composition, and which had important analogues in the visual arts and literature as well.

And here one might pause to appreciate the peculiar intertwining of two terms that sometimes appear as contraries, but that are really just the Greek and Latin words, respectively, for one and the same thing: automatic, and spontaneous. We generally consider that spontaneity is good for creativity; in order for anything new to happen through you, you’ve got to be a bit of a “sport”, in the sense that Darwin used this term for plants that shoot off in some new direction for no apparent reason at all. Automation seems to present an obstacle to that imperative, but whether this is really what it does or not depends on many additional considerations. Who, for one thing, flicked the “on” switch for the automated part of the creative process? In doing so, was the person who flicked it falling back on a crutch out of laziness or lack of any real vision? Or was that person perhaps using the automated system for a purpose over which he remained confident throughout in his own uncompromised creative control?

Every new recording technology and information technology seems to go through a trial period, of skepticism and of efforts to maintain it in its role as a “mere” tool rather than as a proper instrument for creative expression. We may be waiting, at present, I have sometimes thought, for something like the AI equivalent of Kraftwerk — an artist or group of artists capable of taking the work of “pressing down a little key” and transfiguring that into something of aesthetic power and originality. The history of AI is not the same in all respects as the history of electronic music, but it is similar enough that we can expect the same arc of its reception: a period in which it is perceived as a curiosity mostly of interest to engineers, followed by a period of avant-garde creative exploration, followed by groundbreaking popular artists who take these earlier explorations and transform them into a catalyst of mass affect and shaper of the contours of the culture as a whole.

After the process of creative uptake begins, we may also expect to find an aestheticization of the technology’s defects and limitations. We are already, perhaps, seeing this in the visual subculture of memes, which often reproduce the perverse defectiveness of the AI “hallucination”. But this particular perversion has not really hit mass culture — yet. We all understand the signification of scratched vinyl, and even the skipping CD has a basically understood aesthetic resonance, evoking a certain feeling that will mean at least something to everyone who lived through the ‘90s, even if they were only listening to N’Sync, and not chasing after the latest “Clicks & Cuts” release from the Mille Plateaux label. But AI “hallucination”, I predict, will turn out to be much, much more important in defining the overall look and feel of our present era. The very worst AI slop imaginable will turn out to be far more important for future retrospective considerations of the real state of things circa 2025 than any cultural product coming out at this late date with the once prestigious imprimatur of a well-known publishing house or movie studio, including perhaps the very last output from Warner Brothers before that old 20th-century stalwart gets swallowed up by the Netflix beast and becomes just another nexus of data-driven content production.

7.

There are many compelling reasons to predict that, say, fifteen or twenty years from now, the most prestigious awards and distinctions will be handed out for achievements in fields that are entirely unknown today, or that are somewhat known, while still being relegated to a marginal or subcultural status. In such a moment, it can easily seem rational to decide simply to do one’s own thing, however unclassifiable and even perhaps ridiculous it appears, and to do so with at least some hope that one should turn out to be a pioneer in one of these as-yet unknown or undervalued domains. This seems a much better approach to the creative life than to struggle to get in just under the wire and to be among the last, say, to produce a physical tome broadly recognizable as belonging to the moribund tradition of the literary novel, most of whose very last representatives are for some reason named Jonathan.

As with genres and types of output, so too with views — you just can’t know which ones will meet a happy fate, which ones will be suppressed, and which simply dismissed and forgotten. The near future could indeed feature show-trials of people who hold your views; it could also feature show-trials of people who failed to hold your views. The lesson here, obviously, is go ahead and share your views, unless of course you don’t know what your views are —another punishable cognitive disposition, perhaps, in the near future— in which case you should unashamedly share this too.

Otherwise I would like to go on hoping that there might someday be a place on the internet that will feel like a true home for the exercise of a freedom of more than the bestial sort.
