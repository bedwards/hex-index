Under the pen name of Mary Cadwalladr I often write about music from the 1950s and ‘60s, when I was not yet even a Necco wafer in my father’s glove compartment. I assume, when I write in this vein, that I am saying many things that must cause older people, with actual historical experience, to roll their eyes (though one of my greatest accomplishments last year was to have turned Greil Marcus on to the raw genius of Brenda Lee). I in turn roll my eyes at the things many younger people write about the music of the 1980s and ‘90s — but not when it’s Hinternet Associate Editor Sam Jennings doing the writing. We disagree on some substantial points concerning what the music of that period can “really” tell us about culture and history, and of all the artists he treats below Cat Power is the only one I’ve ever cared much about (ok, Janet Jackson too, I suppose, who was my first truly intense crush in her turn as Willis’s girlfriend on Diff’rent Strokes). But this is all productive disagreement, and never the sort to induce eye-rolling. Sam and I agree, fundamentally, that music criticism must not be merely an ornament of intellectual and material history, but is rather the clavis for understanding all of it — who we are and how we ended up here. Sam has promised eventually to supplement this masterful survol of the 1990s in music with a treatment of Tag Team’s “Whoomp! (There It Is)” (1993), for which we are all of course very eager. So play it all, again, Sam. —JSR

“Ahhhhh, the 1990s. The last of the great decades.” —Comment on a Gin Blossoms video, YouTube, 2 months ago

This will be the first in a series of on what I’m calling “The Metaphysics of Music.” Though in the course of these occasional pieces—covering certain eras and genres, even particular musicians—I’ll be playing pretty fast and loose with the term. In the most general sense, I’ll be dealing with meaning—and meaning is a tricky thing in music. By saying this, I don’t intend to dwell on the “content” or the “text” of the music in question, rather on what you could call the “signified.” My preoccupations will be its internal logic (form), and its external context (history/style/genre). Since music is a happening in time, its greater “meaning” doesn’t just lie in what it models for us. It’s not really a “representation” of anything except itself.

This—or something like it—is what Walter Pater meant when he claimed that all art aspires to the condition of music. Music doesn’t “point” to anything. It doesn’t allegorize anything. It is at its best, meaning itself. And so if we want to find the meaning of music—its deeper metaphysics—we must look far beyond the text, far beyond the surface, search it out in the crevices. In the tiniest, vague filigrees of sound and tradition and technology, we have to consider what choices, what artistic decisions, contingencies, and pressures contributed to the creation of it, and what it signified the moment it arrived before an audience in a particular time in history. It’s a chase—and it’s never easy. But it’s always, always fun.

The central theme of the 1990s is that it is an ending. At the end, all things are tried, all combinations are considered, and all new technology rushes outwards past the ability of anyone to foresee where it might go. Perversely, the sense of an ending is also the only thing capable of sustaining the feeling of infinity. And so, even as much of it attempts to stall for time, it’s always plunging vertiginously toward the end of the decade, the century, the millennium. There’s something ghostly and shrouded about so much of the popular music of the 1990s, just as there is something golden, hazy, and photographic about its warmest, sunniest music. It’s hard to escape the feeling, listening to certain tracks (The Smashing Pumpkins’ “1979” and Pavement’s “Gold Soundz” come to mind immediately) that the music was already nostalgic for its own time, in a way distinct from any other decade. Even at its most pleasant, much of the decade’s popular music carries with it a subtle, mournful sense that these are the last good times—that “the last of the great decades” is soon to come to an end.

Popular music in the Nineties most often pulls in two directions: interior, reflected by various production trends, the increasing use of headphones, and the return of a popular drug culture (beyond the poor man’s crack/rich man’s quaalude of the Eighties, that is); and environmental, perfectly engineered for specific spaces, whether that’s the street, the club, a car radio, or an MTV broadcast. There’s also an uneasy axis at work: on the one hand, underground/subcultural and on the other, mainstream/masscultural. In the Nineties, the barrier between the two became briefly slimmer, and many artists crossed it very quickly, in ways that have never really been possible since—Nirvana being the paradigmatic example.

Though I’ll be adapting each of these pieces to the theme in question—when it comes to the Nineties, there is no more appropriate form than a simple list. After all, this was the high era of musical taxonomy: countdowns, music magazines, chart obsessions, and of course the massive impact of music television. Gen X lived through its lists, its hipsters compiling the first internet databases, its young crate diggers obsessively cataloguing and evaluating the contents of the record shops and video stores. The Nineties was a time of haut material culture—a last great romance of physical objects. To make my list, I’ve tried to find nine songs and their music videos which, to me, perfectly represent some aspect of the decade, and yet aren’t always found on such lists, whenever people compile them these days. Hopefully it works. Let’s see…

1. Lush, “Sweetness and Light”

The Nineties begin here—in the autumn of 1990, with a single from a hyped-up London band that would be nearly forgotten by the end of the decade. There were precedents for this kind of rock music: Siouxsie Sioux; the sound of the Cocteau Twin’s Robin Guthrie’s guitar; The Stone Roses; Isn’t Anything. Throughout the whole of the Eighties the firmament of punk had turned strange and arty in the underground, and in America, and in the U.K., had spiraled out in all directions and grown weirder away from the eyes of the average consumer. This subterranean world of bands and beautiful freaks would explode into brief mass-consciousness in the States a year later, but in England things were already beginning: Madchester; trip-hop; drum n’ bass; rave; Aphex Twin. Drugs and dance music and punk leftovers were pushing these weirdos further towards the mainstream, and a pop avant garde was coming close to the public again.

Yet something about this music was already different. Gone were the glitzy dance pop and cavernous Gothicisms of the last decade. Gone were the New Romantics, and the punks they’d driven out. Here was guitar music, yes, but it sounded like a cathedral. Hidden beneath the churning drums and the wash of guitars, the harmony’s not far from a British folk song, or the same modes of so many English chants and hymns. It’s discretely medieval in its moorings, toying with mixolydian and dorian modes, its pedal bass never leaving that droning low “E.” The guitars chime and the girls’ twin voices ping off each other like church bells, ushering in the decade, calling it towards a new sense of loveliness, dreaminess—to bliss. Only watch the video: at 20 seconds, as the drums kick in, it’s just Miki Berenyi and her extraordinary hair against the white glow behind her, her face bent down in concentration over the driving rhythm she strums with all downstrokes. I would nominate this moment—even over Kurt Cobain’s blonde mop and that famous pep rally from hell—as the moment the decade kicks off. Whatever Lush were peddling, it wasn’t angst, or alienation, or anti-establishment rage, or disaffected cool. It was psychedelia and spiritualism—a scaffolding of powerful, electrical rock music cradling a keening search for…well, sweetness and light. Is it a retreat from the world into a sonic cocoon? Is it a blasting outwards into broadcastable, danceable rhythm and groove? It is neither. And it’s almost both. It’s a promise of sensual bliss in the hands of a new kind of rock music—one which the decade, like the band itself, never really achieved. Still, the influence is ongoing.

2. The Pharcyde, “Passin’ Me By”

A mournful hip-hop love song, yet with all the cadence and insouciance of a playground game. West Coast rap in 1991 was a world of guns and crack and after-school specials; Boyz N’ the Hood; Menace II Society. Back East, hip-hop kids had Afro-Centrism and streetwear, jazz and soul samples, conscious rappers like Digable Planets, A Tribe Called Quest, De La Soul, The Fugees, Queen Latifah. But in Watts, in Compton, and all through South Central L.A., the images and horrors that fueled a nation-wide racist panic, which would eventually end up filling the prisons of America to bursting, were images beamed straight from the same world of this black-and-white Pharcyde video. So they called these guys “alternative hip-hop”, because they seemed funny, and rapped about girls and kush more than drive-bys or 8-balls.

In “Passin’ Me By” each emcee takes a verse to describe—almost innocently—a particular boyhood heartbreak, not a fashionable topic for rap songs in any era. In its first decade-and-a-half, rap was resolutely outside music, social music, meant to be experienced in the street, on the block, from a speaker. It was public. But by the Nineties, hip-hop could also be head music, and so it is in “Passin’ Me By.” This is insular, minor-key music that promotes introspection, private listening—thinking, as well as feeling. Yes, it would work just as well coming from a car or a boombox, but (like the Native Tongues in NYC and OutKast in Atlanta), it reflects the music’s growing interest in its own vocabularies and tones, a fresh self-reflexivity and self-knowledge that stands behind so much of the best, longest-lasting rap of the decade. There’s also a sublime thread of effortless Afro-surrealism throughout the video, as the group dangle upside and drift through the city—images in a clear continuum with Spike Lee’s trademark double-dolly shot, with The Pharcyde’s own legendary backmasked video for “Drop”, and even the vaulting elegance of NBA players, all the way to Kendrick Lamar floating through his video for “Alright” in 2015. A sense of how strange it really would be to float, to be weightless, defying the impossible gravity of life in America: that it would not be merely freeing but dizzying, and anyways only an unreal dream.

3. Sneaker Pimps, “6 Underground (Nellee Hooper Mix)”

Perhaps enough time has passed that trip-hop is no longer a joke genre but the most-Nineties genre of all. Tricky and Massive Attack and Portishead and DJ Shadow each produced such unmistakable sounds that the genre practically belongs to them alone. Still, the genre was never more accessible or infernally catchy as it was here, in Sneaker Pimps’ only real hit. Like all great trip-hop, this is music for the hazy hours past midnight—junkie, shadowy music, with almost no light in it. And yet its loping drum sample and acoustic guitar and thin mezzo croon (here courtesy of Kelli Ali), flipped sunny side up, would only reveal just how much basic DNA its kind of shadow pop shared with all manner of “happier” music of the period. This precise kind of midtempo drum loop is the backbone of Nineties pop music—inevitable in everything from Boom Bap to R&B, and even plenty of lo-fi indie and alternative rock. It lends much of the era’s music its sense of a leisurely, unhurried present—of music as pastime, stretching out the experience of heard sound in a way that noisier and more frenetic pop music would start chasing out by the end of the next decade. When Zoomers who never lived through it get all nostalgic for the Nineties, I suspect it’s just this sense of infinite, unrushed ease that they long for most of all.

In trip-hop, the groove fused with James Bond themes, slowed sample breakbeats, hints of sci-fi dystopia—a music finding its equivalent in moody future-noirs like Gattaca, Dark City, Strange Days, and of course The Matrix. Trip-hop defined the darker, blearier side of the Nineties, the side that was approaching the end of the millennium with numb, drugged-out weariness, trying desperately to mute that ever-growing Y2K anxiety. It was music for a sleepwalking world. Not for nothing does the video resemble the washed-out junk chic of a Gregg Araki movie. Araki called his kids the Doom Generation, after all, and this is doom music. Though of course, for the Nineties, the apocalypse could only ever be sexy, fashionable, and above all cool. The Nineties was the last time cool really mattered in culture—the last time subcultures had functional gatekeepers and whole industries stood ready to raid their closets for the next hip trend. Listen to just a few seconds of a song like “6 Underground” and that entire world is conjured in an instant, as fresh as if it had been pried from the insides of some hermetically-sealed time capsule.

4. Cat Power, “Cross Bones Style”

Speaking of apocalyptic, is there anything more uneasy and bleakly beautiful than early Chan Marshall? It’s as much as anti-music video as a real video, as much non-song as song: for 3 ½ minutes the same drum loop, the same guitar line barely hold together as they go on and on outside time, and the ultimate indie recluse known as Cat Power sings the searing line that says it best: “Come child/In a cross bones style/Come child, come and rescue me/Cause you have seen some/Unbelievable things.” The song was written and recorded along with several other Moon Pix songs in a single night, in her Carolina farmhouse, after waking from a hallucinatory nightmare. And it sounds it—sounds like a radio transmission from some indie rock Revelation. Only being crooned by one of the rarest and most original voices of the last decades of popular music.

Set the video next to Madonna’s “Lucky Star” video from nearly 15 years before it (which it parodies), and you’ll get a sense of the veritable ocean of time between the Eighties and Nineties proper. The fun and the glamour are gone; the humor isn’t. The dead-faced women doing their stiff, disaffected dance routine, Power prancing exaggeratedly with her electric guitar, or squatting with a microphone, the American flag draped over her amp—all of it is a mockery of the format, while somehow serving as a perfectly hypnotizing and brilliant instance of it nevertheless. This is “underground” music unbothered by its own subterranean milieu, totally absorbed in its own creative impulse, uninterested in the broader world of pop or fashion or collected cool. And yet there’s none of the sectarian zeal or punk fervor of the Eighties underground: by the Nineties the subcultural was becoming omnivorous, and rock was becoming anything it wanted to be, even as the radio formats and the MTV shows adapted to accommodate a whole new wave of “alternative” music. Only now can we see and understand the depth and idiosyncrasies of those artists—like Cat Power—that were already so unfettered, they couldn’t care less.

5. Guided By Voices, “I Am a Scientist”

Of all indie rockers, Bob Pollard surely taught the ultimate Gen-X lesson: get so good at not caring, your Zen shittiness somehow horseshoes its way into perfection anyways. And he certainly wrote enough catchy rock miniatures to teach the lesson dozens of times over. Only in the Nineties could a weirdo power pop band, recording its dingy, fuzzy lo-fi bullshit in some mythological Midwest basement, receive glowing reviews from Rolling Stone, and sum up an entire era of don’t-give-a-fuck slacker music with the lines: “I am a lost soul/I shoot myself with rock n’ roll/The hole I dig is bottomless/But nothing else can set me free.” It’s the paradox of so much indie music in the 1990s: droll affect, studied irony, total contempt for all the studied poses of the rock stars of yesteryear; and yet still somehow beneath it, the old rock fans’ earnest love for tunes and hooks and leg-kicking fun.

In this sense, Guided By Voices were the era’s sublime idiot-savants: within their impenetrable sound-world, all that obscurity, all that degraded tape sound and vaguely nonsensical lyrics, were more punk than punk—necessary aesthetically because only that could represent the pure joyful, sugary sound of an impeccable rock song, properly transmuted and beamed out to the lonely rock kids of America. In the world of GBV, the pop tradition within American rock music is the Godhead, basically; it’s the final point of it all; to prove it, they’ll blast right past sense, right past content, and on past the fuzz and the crackle and distortion to deliver a mainline hit of 2 minutes twenty seconds pure uncut rock n’roll anthem, no impurities, nothing but the thing itself, which rock stars and poets and posers the world over will never understand—because the secret belongs to that group of lo-fi weirdos in some mythological Midwest basement, and them alone. Consider it like the Dao: “Everything works out right/Everything fades from sight/Because that’s alright with me.”

6. Tori Amos, “Winter”

“New Sincerity” this, “post-irony” that—no artists in the history of mankind were ever so uncool as Nineties confessional songwriters. For an era steeped in disillusionment, numbness, and irony, the Nineties also overflowed with songwriters so sincere it hurt. For all its obvious feminist importance in the industry, Sarah McLachlan’s Lilith Fair featured more than its fair share of such types. By the next decade it had become more of a punch-line than a movement. Yet unlike nearly every other serious female singer songwriter of the era, Tori Amos never appeared at Lilith Fair—despite making the exact music which on the surface would seem to exemplify every cliche that set came to stand for. Everything about Amos’ aching, baroque, and ridiculously dramatic piano numbers should be cringe, awkward, maudlin—but again and again, she saves every moment simply by being so unblinkingly confident and idiosyncratic it doesn’t matter. “Cool” is not a category in Amos’ world—only sublime, radical self-assurance. There’s no calculation, no care for appearances whatsoever. To surrender to Amos’ unashamedly theatrical sensibility is to encounter what real pop freedom looks like, and she’s here to help us get over each of our hang-ups as we do.

Only consider the way she looks at the camera, in the breakthrough video for “Winter”, a look that practically screams, “You think I don’t get how strange and mannered I am? I’m here and I’m daring you to ask who this chick thinks she is, cause the answer is just gonna be whoever and whatever I want to be.” Only look at the way she sits at the piano. It’s the same way she’d sit at her concerts: turned outwards, body open and facing the crowd, looking at everyone like she’d fuck them all if she could, her whole pose one grand, magnificent dare. Though it’s what she fills that space with, ultimately, once she’s carved it out that places her almost beyond comparison—her unflinchingly intimate biography, and impossibly flowery words, and that easy virtuosity. Tori was the singer-songwriter of the Nineties, bar none.

7. Saint Etienne, “Only Love Can Break Your Heart”

The original is a major-key, winsome, folk waltz—one of the all-time classic Neil Young tunes, simple and plain-spoken as can be, so obvious and inevitable it sounds like it’s probably been around since the first time someone picked up a steel-string guitar. How it ever got transmuted into this minor-key, dubby St. Etienne number is a mystery to me. And yet it happened—and it resulted in the greatest cover song of the decade, a version so good I nearly forget who wrote it, whenever I listen to it again. Moira Lambert’s plaintive, simple lines are as unaffected and direct as the original, yet the way the band stretches out all those disembodied echoes of her singing, “I was always thinking…” again and again over the samples and breakbeats of the middle section, forms one of the apotheoses of club music in the decade.

It’s fascinating that so many of these great Nineties music videos—despite arising in a deliberately tacky, colorful, and often psychedelic decade—were made on the street, in grainy black and white. The video for “Only Love Can Break Your Heart” even subverts its own monochrome, with sudden rushes of color, or zooming footage of flowers, like something from a Stan Brakhage short. What does all this signify? Something quick and bright puncturing the grey London clouds? The temporary disaffection of the young? The way they might take and chop up any Boomer classic they wanted? Spinning it into whatever kind of droll bop they might possibly imagine—genre-hopping through house and dub and riddim and twee Sixties throwbacks in one leap? Probably all of the above. Remember: at the end, all things are tried, all combinations are considered. Musical omnivorousness is the great virtue of all Nineties pop—a gift we’ve still not exhausted.

8. Janet Jackson - “Got Til It’s Gone”

There’s no arguing that the Nineties weren’t a golden age for black expression. Even today the most popular black music is so commodified and warped by big money parasites, you don’t have to look very far to see the artists themselves evincing an enormous nostalgia for a time period when black music had finally built itself a stage big enough that there was at least some padding from such voracious commercialism. And despite there being an obvious racist bent to naming so much of the music “urban” to begin with, there really was powerfully “urban” music from the Eighties onward—music which stemmed from the brilliance of black musicians for creating music that reflected the open city life of the late twentieth century. Motown is city music, and so is Philadelphia Soul, Fusion Jazz, funk, and disco. Hip-hop is the city music nonpareil. Jazz is usually city music, though much blues is not, unless you go for certain kinds of electrified blues, Chicago blues especially, which definitely is.

But—partially due to the evolving production standards of the Nineties—you started to get head music out of it just the same, and this translated quickly to R&B and dance music. TLC’s “Creep” is like this, and so is Missy Elliot’s “Supa Dupa Fly” and the best of the Soulquarians. There’s something intimate, private, and endlessly creative bubbling up even underneath the biggest sing-along lines and hooks. And few R&B classics are as easily hypnotic and moody and svelte than this perfect song from Janet’s The Velvet Rope. The Joni Mitchell sample, the Q-Tip feature, the electric piano all communicate that Janet is getting “deep,” reflective, melancholic about her own conciousness—the video evinces much the same. And yet it’s real, and it’s playful, and it sounds great. Someone once said a good groove extends to infinity. “Got Til’ its Gone” could run for four minutes or four hours or forever—it would still sound like its groove only just got started.

9. My Bloody Valentine, “Soon”

So in the end, we travel back to the beginning of the decade, and back to shoegaze, and the most groundbreaking music of the entire era. Thirty-four years on, we still haven’t quite caught up. With “Soon,” the closing track of My Bloody Valentine’s greatest album (which Brian Eno himself praised as the “vaguest pop music ever made”) every still-vital sound of the end of the century—rock, pop, house, hip-hop, trance, electronic, ambient—is fused past the point at which it could ever be disentangled again. It claims for rock one ultimate musical metaphysic, which popular music—with its reliance on lyric, articulable emotion, story, or ballad—generally obscures. That is: the status of music as pure sound and duration and sense. Something unlike language or image, because it is itself the signified, rather than a signifier. A symbol for nothing except its own duration.

My Bloody Valentine’s Loveless was surely the last time any popular music felt this far ahead of its time. Even today it’s like that—like future music from the amniotic sac: somehow both biological and technological. Or like the waiting room for heaven, if that waiting room was also the huge pulsing ventricle of a womb the size of the universe. It’s impossibly big, with massive blistering walls of sound as loud as air raid sirens—yet at the same time soft as pillows. When Kevin Shields himself fell asleep listening to an early rough mix, he gave himself tinnitus. I can think of no better introduction to the music than that anecdote: Loveless is the kind of music that can damage your ears, while you fall asleep to it. And even if “Soon” is the most apparently conventional song on the record (though to say “conventional” here surely stretches the term more than it can take), it still feels a thousand miles away from anything else in the decade. It’s the promise of a new kind of musical consciousness; it’s the end of an entire era. Listen to it loud enough, and you will disappear—you will disappear and you will grasp why the sense of infinity and the sense of an ending are the same. It all begins and ends there— with the band, the album, the promise, the century, and the millennium.
