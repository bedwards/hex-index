Hello!

Since I started writing on Substack in October of 2023, this newsletter has unintentionally become a chronicle of the Texas Book Festival, a whole weekend of writers talking about books that I attend every November. (You can read my previous TBF posts here and here.)

This year, most of the panels I attended were in the Texas Capitol or the poetry tent, which was right across the street from the governor’s mansion. On Sunday, at a panel featuring Texas poet Cecily Parks and California poet Rachel Richardson, we had to strain to hear over the howling of a brown dog behind Governor Abbott’s fence. “I’d like to ask him about his home life,” said Richardson.

The TBF is where you sometimes get to meet and ask questions of your favorite authors. I met Miriam Toews at a memoir panel and walked through the Capitol with her to the signing tent. In the Capitol—where I have been a child on a field trip, a mom, a tourist, a state employee, and a protestor—I walked with Miriam into the center of the rotunda and told her, Look up! and pointed to the star at the top with the letters T-E-X-A-S around it. That was sort of a dream come true.

And now that I have taken the super special employee tour all the way to the top of the rotunda, I got to tell her about how dirty it is up there.

The TBF is also where middle-aged women eat trail mix out of tote bags so that they don’t have to stop going to panels. On Saturday night, eating my first hot meal of the day, I didn’t want to answer when my husband asked if I had any takeaways. It wasn’t a “takeaway” kind of event, I thought, as I ate fried chicken with my fingers. The next morning, however, I woke up thinking about his question, and an interesting theme presented itself.

I don’t remember what the question Donika Kelly was answering when she talked about how she decides whether to publish a poem. It was Saturday morning and she was sitting at a dais in the basement of the Capitol, beside fellow poets Tiana Clark and Amanda Johnston. (Johnson, the moderator, is also the founder of Torch Literary Arts, a nonprofit that supports Black women writers.) Kelly writes poems all the time, she said, and once it’s written the poem “doesn’t need to do anything except be a record of a practice.” But when she feels like a poem wants to become art, that’s when she revises it to share with an audience. She asks, is this poem asking anything of another person?

I was still thinking about Kelly’s words that afternoon when Carrie Fountain interviewed Ada Limón in the Capitol auditorium. The mark of a great poet, said either Fountain or Limón (I can’t remember), is that the reader feels, encountering a poem, as if the reader herself had written it.

I liked the relationship between Kelly’s question—is this poem asking anything of another person— and a reader’s feeling that a poem reflects their own mind so well, they might have written it. There’s a nice reciprocity to the comment: an illustration of how poetry connects reader and writer.

Ada Limón spent three years as the U.S. poet laureate, during which she witnessed the transition from the Biden to the Trump administration. As poet laureate, Limón was appointed by and worked closely with Carla Hayden, the first woman and first African American librarian of Congress, whom Trump fired in May. “She was fired for no reason other than that she is a Black woman,” said Limón. They made up a story that she was giving inappropriate books to children, but the Library of Congress is not a lending library, so the story made no sense.

Limón had started a program to install poetry on picnic tables at National Parks. The Trump administration paused the programs. They had all the money, all the people, the tables were ready to be shipped, and everything just stopped. Limón had invited young poets to the White House as part of the National Student Poets Program. That stopped, too. The part of the White House where Dr. Jill Biden and Limón hosted poetry readings with young Americans has now been destroyed.

When Limón became poet laureate, she thought her job would be to bring poetry to the masses. Turns out, it was the other way around. She traveled to cities and towns across the country, and people came up and pressed poems into her hands or told her about the poetry group that met in a coffee shop on Sundays or invited her to their local poetry festivals. She realized that there were people writing poetry everywhere, all the time. These “secret poems” had changed her thinking about the purpose of poetry. She used to think that a poem was finished when it found an audience. That the poem found its highest purpose when it was shared with a reader. She doesn’t feel that way anymore.

Poetry, she explained, is about the act of noticing yourself and your senses in the world. If you make a practice of noticing, the practice becomes reciprocal: at first you notice in order to write, but eventually you write in order to notice.

At this point, I think we all know the things that prevent us from noticing. Limón referred to one of these distractions as her “telephone,” and everyone laughed. When you put down your “telephone” to notice what’s going on around you, she said, you will notice that you have feelings. Some of these feelings can be big. You need a place to put them. Poetry is a container. It is where you can put the feelings that emerge from the things you see, hear, taste, smell, touch and feel when you pay attention.

Sometimes the poem just needs to be written. As Donika Kelly said, even if it never leaves your notebook, it exists as a document of your practice of paying attention. The act of writing the poem is an act of resistance. You are resisting all the powerful forces in this world that would like to direct your attention elsewhere, towards abstractions, lies, and persuasive partial truths, so that they can control your body, take away your rights, and learn more about your habits in order to sell you more stuff.

When the poem is about something that you need another person to see, hear, taste, smell, touch or feel, that’s when it wants to be shared.1 In this way, the act of reading and writing poetry becomes an act of mutual resistance. And the act of loving poetry in public—whether you’re online or at a reading in a bookstore or coffee shop, or at a poetry slam in a dark theater with sticky floors, or in the bowels of the government of the State of Texas, or standing in a tent across from the governor’s inexplicably outraged dog—your public love of poetry becomes an amplified act of community resistance.

I love that she kept saying “telephone.”
