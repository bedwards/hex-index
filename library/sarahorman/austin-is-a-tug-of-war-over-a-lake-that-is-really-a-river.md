[Hello](https://www.reddit.com/r/NoStupidQuestions/comments/8n1atu/why_is_it_called_tug_of_war_and_not_tug_war_or/)!

I want to tell you about my hometown. Austin is divided into north and south by a body of water that we call a lake but is actually a dammed river. The river’s name is Colorado—not the Colorado River that flows through the Grand Canyon, but a [smaller one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colorado_River_\(Texas\)) with a unique [history](https://www.kut.org/energy-environment/2024-04-24/not-that-colorado-the-muddy-winding-story-of-how-the-texas-colorado-river-got-its-name). Perhaps we call it a lake to shield our local water from the inferiority complex that might result from being compared to its bigger riparian cousin. That would be very in character: perish the thought that anything in Texas could actually be smaller.

I grew up in south Austin, which in the 1980s was home to bikers, plumbers, and rugged Texan hippies, not the hipsters, high-rise condos and slick corporate bars that crowd neighborhoods like SoCo and SoLa today. They were called South Congress and South Lamar back then; there were no cute neighborhood abbreviations. Today I google “Sola” and the second AI search result asks “where do millionaires in Austin hang out?” (Where do they *not* hang out is the better question.)

In the ‘80s, the south Austin “Bubbas” prided themselves on being more laidback and working class than their north Austin counterparts, the “Yuppies.” It was this playful rivalry that prompted a formative event of my childhood: the Great North vs. South Austin Tug-of-War. A fundraiser organized by young state lawmaker Charlie Gandy, the Tug of Honor brought crowds of Austinites to the shores of the river to try to pull each other into the water. On the north shore, the Yuppies ate quiche, drank chardonnay, and braved the river mud in their white Keds, khaki shorts, and tucked-in Izods, while the Bubbas on the south side drank Lone Star and ate hot dogs and BBQ in their cutoff jeans and muscle shirts. The Bubbas outweighed the Yuppies by many beer bellies, which explains why they always won. As the guy in the handmade Bubba t-shirt explains in this [video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_yYJ2mj983c), “Gucci tennis shoes don’t work good wet.”

As the guy in the handmade Bubba t-shirt explains, “Gucci tennis shoes don’t work good wet.”

After a few years, the Tug of Honor reached its fundraising goal and the event disappeared into obscurity, which I suppose means the defeated Yuppies had the last laugh. The Tug of Honor (also known as “The Great Jerk Off”) was an annual event for a few years, but when I moved back to Austin to raise a family in my thirties, no one seemed to remember it anymore. I had to look up footage of the event on YouTube to assure myself that it was real. I wondered if I’d invented it as a metaphor for my childhood.

The tug-of-war was real, but the north-south rivalry had become inextricably linked with my sense that certain problems in my life could be traced back to my family’s move from south to north Austin in 1983. The tug-of-war symbolized a fall from grace—the idea that life became less colorful when we moved from the funky south to the bland newness of the north. And because I am a storyteller, this personal origin story merged with another myth that I told myself about my hometown. Around here, it is often said that you fall in love with the version of Austin that you first knew. In my case, I walked the earth for an embarrassingly long time with the faulty notion that *my* Austin, the Austin of my childhood, was an economically and racially diverse utopia, segregated only by homegrown categories like “Bubbas” and “Yuppies,” Deep Eddy vs. Barton Springs, Whole Foods vs. Central Market, Dan’s vs. Fran’s. Fascinated as I was by these indicators of what a sociologist would call *marginal differentiation*, and protected by my own membership in the dominant (white, middle class, straight, Christian, H-E-B shopping) group, I missed what was staring me right in the face: the homogeneity of Austin and all its quirky subcategories. I grew up convinced that my hometown was an oasis in a country mired in racial strife. Certainly, I believed, we were nothing like Chicago, the other city that loomed in the background of my childhood, where my father was born on a different South Side, in a different time.

Around here, it is often said that you fall in love with the version of Austin that you first knew.

I stopped believing in my mythical Austin, but it took years of experience, of living in other places, and of talking to people who are different from me. It helps that I returned to Austin with a different identity than the one I left with. As a church-going child of Christian parents growing up here, the first time I met a Jewish girl in elementary school I told her she must be mistaken about her own identity. I knew for a fact that Jews lived in Israel because my orange Childcraft Encyclopedia had two pages on Judaism in which I had studied photos of bearded men at the Wailing Wall in Jerusalem. No, no, I told this poor girl, you must be wrong.

When I moved back to Austin after fourteen years away, I had converted to Judaism. I now have Jewish children who attend Austin schools, making it entirely possible that my son or daughter could be on the other side of that regrettable exchange. I didn’t want my children to grow up with the same myth about Austin, and in a certain way, they couldn’t grow up with that myth because their identity separates them from the norm.

Of course, none of my personal transformations come without a reading list. For many years the only thing I’d read about race and Austin was an essay in the 2011 collection, *[Nurtureshock](https://bookshop.org/beta-search?keywords=nurtureshock),* by Po Bronson and Ashley Merryman, in which sociologists studied white liberal parents in Austin who, like the majority of white parents, refused to speak directly about race with their children. This *Newsweek* [article](https://www.newsweek.com/even-babies-discriminate-nurtureshock-excerpt-79233) summarizes the results of the study: “In this supposed race-free vacuum being created by parents, kids were left to improvise their own conclusions—many of which would be abhorrent to their parents.”

## *The Slip* by Lucas Schaefer is the novel about Austin that I have been waiting for.

Nathaniel Rothstein, a husky 16-year-old Jewish kid from Massachusetts, moves to Austin to spend a summer with his Uncle Bob, a retired UT professor, and disappears. In Lucas Schaefer’s *[The Slip](https://bookshop.org/p/books/the-slip-a-novel-lucas-schaefer/135feeac603c3903?utm_source=google&utm_medium=pmax&utm_campaign=16243454879&utm_content=&utm_term={searchterm}&gad_source=1&gad_campaignid=16243514117&gbraid=0AAAAACfld40Fyb8zFV8d_f3qrm_abJLAh&gclid=Cj0KCQjw0Y3HBhCxARIsAN7931WkpNtcwFuspdBohsSG3C-eKwBoLY-49_2wzbpPoTDBpNymDTUBKygaAkEzEALw_wcB),* Austin in the late ‘90s, and the sweaty melting pot of a local boxing gym, are the ideal setting for Nathaniel to shed both literal weight and the baggage of his former identity.

In this passage, Nathaniel contemplates meeting David, his Haitian boss at a volunteer job:

> When Bob had told him he’d be volunteering for a family friend, it never occurred to Nathaniel that the friend wouldn’t be white. He’d driven to Shoal Creek that morning in his uncle’s spare Volvo already dreading the prospect of spending the day having to say actual words to actual people; seeing his boss was a Black guy made him even more nervous than before. None of his mom’s friends or the kids who passed for his friends were Black. Black people had always seemed . . . a little intimidating?
> 
> He was pretty sure this thought wasn’t racist. After all, these were *good* qualities, traits he would’ve killed for. At mostly white Newton South, it seemed a widely established fact that Black guys possessed a particular kind of cool. All year, “It’s All About the Benjamins” had blared from the open windows of the shiny SUVs in the senior lot. Many of the most popular guys wore looks trickled down from hip-hop fashion—backwards caps and work boots—while the boys who were still more into sports than girls hadn’t yet traded out the jerseys of their smooth-pitted youths: Andy Goldenberger masquerading as Ty Law, Chicago-born Eli Finkel forever #23. It wasn’t just the white kids either. Big Jared Tang, who owned his girth in a way Nathaniel never could, often lumbered the halls in baggy jeans, a gold chain, and an outsized T-shirt emblazoned with a photograph of the recently deceased Notorious B.I.G.

We also get to read David’s perspective as he graduates from the “snarling bigotry of one dying generation” to “more cloying forms of white supremacy” among his white clientele at the Shoal Creek Home for the Aged: “This was where Austin’s moneyed liberals hid their most embarrassing relations: Red Scared grand-thises and sodomy-fixated great-thats and slobbering uncles who’d lost control of their tongues but not their checkbooks, lest they miss their monthly three dollars to the John Birch Society.”

Through the story of Nathaniel and David, Schaefer says things about Austin, teenage boys, and race in America that I have been thinking for years. But that’s not all. Schaefer takes on a dizzying array of perspectives in this book, including, in one of my favorite chapters, the well-meaning Jewish mothers who want to talk about race. *The Slip* features an ensemble cast of characters navigating racial, religious, sexual and gender identities against the backdrop of Austin in the 1990s and early 2000s, a time when my hometown was as fiercely proud of its so-called “weirdness,” as invested in being the “blueberry in the tomato soup” of Texas, and as insistent on calling the smaller Colorado River a Town Lake as we have always been.

*Lucas Schaefer’s The Slip is the novel about Austin that I’ve been waiting for.*

I devoured all 484 pages of *The Slip* while I was on a trip to Chicago with my family. While reading, I texted multiple people to tell them they had to read this book: several of them have done so, and all reports so far are favorable. (I’m not the only one who thinks this is a great novel: *The Slip,* which is Schaefer’s debut, is a finalist for the 2025 Kirkus Prize and a USA Today bestseller.) I finished *The Slip* months ago, but its characters have not left my brain. Just yesterday, I saw three or four white-haired Uncle Bobs standing around the meat counter at Central Market. Spend any time around ACL this weekend, and I think you’ll encounter a few Nathaniel Rothsteins as well.

Austin being still small enough for the occasional slacker magic trick, I was fortunate enough to have a personal connection to Lucas Schaefer and could text him as soon as I finished reading. On the evening of October 30, we will be in conversation for a reading and Q&A at [Bambino Book Club](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/bambino-book-club-presents-reading-and-qa-with-lucas-schaefer-tickets-1740098554619?aff=oddtdtcreator).
