[Hello](https://apod.nasa.gov/apod/)!

From time to time, I interview writers about my two favorite topics: reading and writing. This week I’m excited to bring you my discussion with [Emily Hunt Kivel](https://emilyhuntkivel.com/), whose debut novel *[Dwelling](https://bookshop.org/p/books/dwelling-emily-hunt-kivel/21720332?utm_source=google&utm_medium=cpc&utm_campaign=dsa_nonbrand&utm_content={adgroupname}&utm_term=aud-1721779758375:dsa-19959388920&gad_source=1&gad_campaignid=12440232635&gbraid=0AAAAACfld42cnSpuYyN_jme0KzD467JKI&gclid=CjwKCAjwy7HEBhBJEiwA5hQNoo0KGM0oqL77apqvyhoDY2tNluUJbq96wPOBAQ2dcfrOnIeZONRhuBoCxcUQAvD_BwE)* comes out this week from Farrar, Straus and Giroux. Just this morning, Jo Hamya in the *New York Times* [book review](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/08/03/books/review/emily-hunt-kivel-dwelling.html) called *Dwelling* a “beautifully radical debut” and “the most fun I’ve had reading in years.”

*Dwelling* is a fairy tale set in a housing crisis, after all the landlords in New York City simultaneously evict their tenants, including Evie, a 20-something graphic designer and orphan. In many ways, Evie’s world sounds very familiar:

> The actions had all been engineered to seem so gradual, Evie thought, even banal. One subtle slight after another. Nothing shocking, per se. And yet here they were, on the day that the city’s final and most radical amendment had gone into place and everyone was out, on the street, looking up at their soon-to-be-sanitized streets and feeling tricked. By their landlords. By the city. By their mothers and fathers. By their ancestors. By Aha!: Apartments and Homes Anywhere. By God.

Ejected onto the street along with all her neighbors, their furniture, and the “innards of an Italian restaurant—tables, chairs, and a lot of dried pasta,” Evie develops a plan to move to the small town of Gulluck, Texas, where she has a distant cousin.

Evie’s priorities are simple: first find a home and a job, then rescue her sister, Elena, who has been institutionalized and who Evie dreams is “locked somewhere in a tower, crying to get out, pounding against windows.” When the only place Evie can afford to live in Gulluck turns out to be a house in the shape of a giant shoe and she meets a magical locksmith, all her priorities come together to inspire a quest worthy of the Brothers Grimm.

I am a big fan of Emily’s writing. Years before we met in Austin, I added her story “[The Juggler’s Wife](https://www.theparisreview.org/fiction/7566/the-jugglers-wife-emily-hunt-kivel)” to my list of favorite short stories (you can read about my reading lists [here](https://sarahorman.substack.com/p/5-ways-to-read-like-a-writer-b985e8c1e557)), so I was delighted to learn that she is brilliant, charming, and funny in person as well as on the page. Our discussion about Texas, Marxism, fairy tales, and footwear began over iced coffees at a cafe in East Austin and continued in a shared Google doc.

*Emily, “eerier than expected.”*

**The term “debut novel” always reminds me of the word “debutante” in a way that I find funny, and you are very funny, so I’m going to just go with it. So,** ***Dwelling*** **is your debutante novel, and it has already generated some significant buzz. You are being interviewed by the** ***New Yorker’*****s Rachel Monroe at [First Light Books this Tuesday in Austin](https://www.firstlightaustin.com/events/emily-hunt-kivel-dwelling), an appearance at [Emma Straub’s Books are Magic in New York on August 14](https://www.booksaremagic.net/events/2788320250814), and your face on the cover of** ***Publishers Weekly*** **magazine. How are you feeling about all of this?**

This is a funny question. I think I feel like a debutante only in the fashion of Leonora Carrington’s story *The Debutante*, wherein a young woman switches place with a hyena and the hyena ends up eating the literal faces off a couple people at her coming-out-into-society ball. I do feel like that kind of debutante! When I was young adult and teen—probably because I was small and friendly and had a curious mid-Atlantic accent—I often found myself having to perform for people in this very debutante way. I would be trotted out to give speeches at donor dinners and to sing for my parents’ friends, and the effect was never quite what anyone wanted. It was always a little off and weirder than they hoped. I think my presence is eerier than expected.

In all seriousness, I am deeply touched to see people and institutions like PW getting behind *Dwelling*. My assumption and hope is that it means certain people have genuinely engaged with the writing and the world, that the book has found those who actively yearn for a certain degree of madness and newness in their lives. My greatest wish is that *Dwelling* encourages people to reimagine their world and what their reading lives can be, and I think any “buzzy” response probably shows that some people really feel that desire, too.

**When we met, you described** ***Dwelling*** **as basically “failed flash fiction”-- you were trying to write flash, but you just kept going. I love that story because it reminds me of a quote that I cannot find, and which may not exist at all, but the gist is that someone said something like: “If you want to learn how to dance, you just fall and keep falling.” I’m glad you kept falling. Do you ever wonder what the flash fiction version of** ***Dwelling*** **would have been like? Is there another Evie story waiting to be told?**

I think Evie’s story is one of self-discovery and adventure, and formally I don’t know if flash fiction can accommodate that (and vice versa). Perhaps in the hands of another writer it might, but not in mine. Ultimately, I think the experience of writing *Dwelling* was an exercise in actually letting something come into existence without imposing rules and structure that were arbitrary at that nascent a stage. I’m not saying vision isn’t important—it’s hugely important, probably the most important—but I’ve found personally that a story usually tells you what form it wants to take only after you’ve begun writing it. And then, it’s an author’s job to listen and become illuminated by it. To get inspired and critical in shaping that idea into something real. The art throws down the gauntlet, and as the writer you can choose to accept it or not.

I like that potentially fake quote about dancing as falling. I think it rings true for me in that falling isn’t failure exactly, it’s rather surrendering yourself to mystery (gravity).

**You told me that you wanted to write a fairy tale rather than a novel based on a fairy tale. I’m curious what that distinction means to you. As you were writing** ***Dwelling,*** **were there any particular choices that steered you away from a traditional novel toward fairy tale? Or vice versa?**

At their core, so many fairy tales have been about social institutions and societal values breaking down. That’s meant that for most fairy tales with a woman protagonist, the story’s propulsion is about inheritance. You’re young and vulnerable, you can’t earn money, you’ll never own property…so if your father remarries a new woman who doesn’t want to share the wealth, are you totally screwed? There is a fear and scarcity mindset permeating so many fairy tales—even Hansel and Gretel are initially dumped into the woods by their parents because there’s a famine and they present two too many mouths to feed—that feel very germane to the precarity and scarcity of 2025, where people are buying more houses than they can physically live in as financial investments.

Initially, I didn’t have it in my head that I wanted to write a fairy tale. I knew that I wanted to write something that followed a quest—whether that be for self-discovery or to rescue a damsel in distress, I actually wasn’t sure. And I knew more than anything that I wanted to immerse myself in a literary world that was the opposite of much of the maudlin, boring contemporary fiction I’d been reading then. This was in 2021 or 2022.

And eventually, I found the images that came to mind—I’m a fairly visual writer—were informed by folk tale, fairy tale, and myth. And once I conceived of Gulluck as a sort of unassuming portal into a different world, the form coalesced. I wanted to create my own mythology.

**In** ***Dwelling,*** **every single renter in New York City is evicted en masse in order to renovate the city, because the mayor has decided that he only likes “tourists and rich people.” This event was inspired by the real-life idea of “renoviction,” which happens when a landlord evicts a tenant in order to conduct a major renovation. How has it felt for you to watch the housing crisis evolve after writing this novel? (If you feel like weighing in, I’d love to hear what you think about this [essay](https://www.publicbooks.org/autofiction-writers-of-the-world-unite/?utm_source=substack&utm_medium=email) arguing that young novelists are writing auto-fiction about how “lack of affordable housing shapes and reshapes us.”)**

When I conceived of the mass evictions in *Dwelling*, it felt like a satire. A culmination of our then-current, unsustainable practices pushed to the extreme. And then almost immediately after I finished the first draft, renovictions started devouring Los Angeles. My good friend, Chelsea Kirk, actually wrote a great piece on this for the [](https://www.latimes.com/opinion/story/2023-05-18/mass-evictions-barrington-plaza-renters-decarbonization-protections)*[LA Times](https://www.latimes.com/opinion/story/2023-05-18/mass-evictions-barrington-plaza-renters-decarbonization-protections)*[.](https://www.latimes.com/opinion/story/2023-05-18/mass-evictions-barrington-plaza-renters-decarbonization-protections) The essay you shared is getting at something very true. Housing—and the literal state of our home—doesn’t only shape our lives, it genuinely shapes our worldviews and, thus, our tastes and preferences. I suppose *Dwelling* is a ”real-estate novel.” Evie does live a life “characterised by scarcity and insecurity,” per Goddard. In *Dwelling*, she diverges completely, rather than remain to bash her head repeatedly against a hard and uncaring system.

Although, I definitely don’t go as easy on writers today as the essay does. I tend to think novels are becoming more fragmented simply because of the internet and reduced attention span, not because of other external anxieties. And I tend to think age- (and power-) gap relationships follow an ancient sort of logic and so am not as attracted to modern takes on them.

**I’m at the beach with my family as I write this, and just now, as I was walking on the sand I was so grateful that I brought my Tevas. They are the perfect shoes for this activity. In** ***Dwelling,*** **you explore the ability to make a pair of shoes that is exactly what the wearer needs as a kind of superpower. Where did that idea come from?**

Another thing I’ve noticed in fairy and folk tales is the obsession with direction. The prince in Rapunzel becomes blinded and wanders aimlessly, misdirected, to the desert. Hansel and Gretel make a trail of breadcrumbs to help them retrace their steps but birds end up eating them (the crumbs) anyway. Red Riding Hood strays from her path and gets lost in the woods. And the obsession with shoes! Little Thumb’s seven-league boots, Cinderella’s slippers, even Hans Christian Anderson’s famous red shoes (which are rather a harbinger of death and which laid the foundation for one of [my favorite movies of all time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Red_Shoes_\(1948_film\))). Dorothy’s own red shoes that take her home! Everyone is lost and looking for direction, and it’s usually for the direction home. I think we still are.

Evie’s skill is interesting to me because by being able to make fantastic shoes—even ones that take you wherever you need to go—she’s not only a superb craftswoman, but she’s creating a resource that’s about purpose and community, rather than money. The shoes can’t make you rich or famous or even successful, but they can point you in the direction of life. They can orient you towards the living.

So I guess the shoes show a commitment to the task, a dedication to life that is actually kind of radical. So you walking around in your Tevas—it’s a radical act of presence, Sarah!

Funnily enough, I have been notorious among my friends for wearing the most ridiculously uncomfortable shoes as possible. In the dead of winter, in snow, on unsanitary streets. Maybe I’m oriented toward death. But I notice I’m starting to break from that.

**As a graphic designer in NYC, Evie’s work in the beginning of** ***Dwelling*** **is what Karl Marx would call alienated labor: she doesn’t control the means of production or her output; she’s basically coerced to work in order to pay rent for a shitty apartment; and performing that alienated labor has, in Marx’s terms, estranged her from herself and her fellow humans. We could read Evie’s character arc as a search for meaningful, creative work that is free of coercion and can liberate her and Elena from their disenfranchised statuses. Does that resonate with you? As a fiction writer, how do you relate to “good people with good precise jobs”?**

It resonates so deeply. I’ve said that Evie’s job, like many of our jobs both “creative” and not, basically puts her at a remove from her work. And often, when we’re removed from our work, we’re removed from ourselves. It’s impossible to see yourself in the production of your work, either because your role is vague and meaningless or because the production is so complex and globalized that its machinations are overwhelming to decipher.

This is similar to what Frederic Jameson called “cognitive mapping”—a person’s ability to cognitively place themselves in their larger environment. I really don’t think we’re able to do that right now, because much of what we do and use is a) nonphysical, b) produced in a factory, or c) filtered through algorithms, online dialogue, or corporate interest, and probably all three. So by Evie working as a shoemaker and living in a house that looks like a shoe, she’s sort of forced into a concrete self. Finally she can map her own daily existence within her surroundings.

I’m not saying that the only “good precise jobs” are as craftspeople. Nor do I wholly subscribe to the idea that you need to make money doing something fulfilling. I think most work is basically bullshit and it’s a major balancing act to not let it destroy our lives. But I do think that dedicating oneself to the making of something—including art—can allow you to come into your own existence more fully.

**Not every literary novelist uses humor on every single page. Is that something that comes naturally to you?**

It often does. It certainly did with *Dwelling*. I think the world is so broken and absurd that sometimes the only way I can authentically interact with it without crying is through humor. And I didn’t want *Dwelling* to be a soul-searching meditation on a theme. I wanted it to be expansive: to open up the aperture of what we even think a home could be. I find humor to be incredibly expansive. It’s a generous way to write. And I think the story really values generosity.

**I love Texas in your novel. Honestly, it broke my heart a little bit, in a good way. The town of Gulluck and its oddball residents reminded me of** ***Pee-Wee’s Big Adventure*** **and David Byrne’s** ***[True Stories](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5MIHT9d25wY)*****– a fantastical, light-hearted version of my home state that is not how most people perceive this place. What does Texas mean to Evie?**

Both of those were recently playing at Austin Film Society! It’s funny, I saw *True Stories* for the first time in theaters well after *Dwelling* was scheduled to come out, but I felt a really kindred spirit too. Watching it, I kept thinking, “Oh, there it is.” Although Evie’s story can be seen as anti-capitalist, there’s also something weirdly American about it to me. There’s something of a frontiersman quality in her. She is arriving in a place that has open space and room to dwell, and she’s sort of etching her name in the sand. It’s kind of old-fashioned in some ways, this idea of self-determination.

So to Evie, Texas is a place where there’s less noise. I recently met these two chefs who are opening a restaurant in Alpine, right near Marfa. They had lived in northern California for many years, like me, but had to move because opening a restaurant in California was simply too expensive and restrictive, not only in terms of real estate because of all the licensure requirements and limitations.

Obviously, I don’t want to idealize this state and I don’t think *Dwelling* does either. But there is a certain lawlessness and a profoundly bizarre spirit to this land, and they persist in spite of lawmakers’ best efforts to quelch them.
