Let's now turn to the plan itself and outline its provisions. While integrating the European amendments as we go, we should also keep in mind that everything we are discussing remains at a high level of abstraction. The agreement opens with a confirmation of Ukraine's sovereignity and a non-aggression pact to be signed by Russia, Ukraine, and Europe.

Here Europe proposes an amendment expanding the pact to include NATO as well. In the original draft, Russia pledges not to attack neighboring states and in exchange NATO would halt further enlargement, a provision the Ephree removed entirely. The United States, meanwhile, positions itself as a mediator between Russia and NATO, a role Europe struck out, replacing it with more neutral language. Point five states that Ukraine is to receive reliable security guarantees.

This is worth pausing on because it is one of the most crucial elements of the entire plan. Barak David of Axios obtained the more concrete provisions. The guarantees are meant to resemble article 5 of NATO, but according to legal experts, the similarity is largely cosmetic. In practice, the guarantees would not obligate anyone to take action.

They remain discretionary and lack binding force. Therefore, the E3 response replaced the word reliable with robust, a shift meant to imply a stronger, more binding commitment from NATO. Moving forward, Ukraine was expected to reduce its armed forces to 600,000 troops in peace time. In the European version, this figure is raised to 800,000.

78 and 9 concern NATO. Ukraine would be required to enshrine in its constitution that it will not join the alliance. Europe alters the spirit of this clause, stating instead that Ukraine's accession depends solely on NATO consensus, something that at present does not exist. In point 8, the US draft requires NATO to pledge that it will not send troops to Ukraine.

The Europeans clarify that NATO won't deploy troops permanently, but they allow for a rotational presence. Point 9 states in a strikingly vague manner that quote, "European fighter jets will be stationed in Poland, a line viewed with considerable concern in Warso, which interprets it as a veiled attempt to restrict NATO troops on Polish soil while permitting only air assets. The E3 keeps that point and modifies only one word, replacing European with NATO. Continuing through the document, the US plan stipulates that if either side breaks the agreement, consequences will follow.

Sanctions on Russia would be reinstated and a military response might be considered. Washington also seeks compensation for providing its security guarantees. The E3, however, explicitly adds that US guarantees must mirror article 5. Ukraine is to remain on its path toward European Union membership, a point to which the Europeans raise no objections.

Its reconstruction would be coordinated in part through a new Ukrainian development fund. Russia, meanwhile, is to be reintegrated into the global economy, including a phased lifting of sanctions and cooperation with the United States in energy, natural resources, and AI. Moscow would return to the G8, a provision left largely unchanged by the Europeans. The original US draft states that of the $300 billion in frozen Russian assets, 100 billion should be redirected to Ukraine's reconstruction with 50% of the profits going to the United States.

Europe would contribute another $100 billion while the remaining $200 billion would be invested in a joint American Russian quote investment vehicle.
