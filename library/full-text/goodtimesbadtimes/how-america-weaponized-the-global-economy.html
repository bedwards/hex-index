There are five keys to controlling the world. So believed Admiral John Fischer, the architect of the Royal Navy's modernization on the eve of the First World War. Fisher identified those five keys as the Dover Strait, the Strait of Giblartar, the Strait of Malaca, the Souz Canal, and the Cape of Good Hope. His logic was simple.

To rule the seas, the Royal Navy did not need to dominate every ocean basin. It merely had to control a handful of critical choke points. The narrow arteries through which global trade must flow. Control over such a chokepoint acts as a force multiplier.

Command a tiny dot on the map and you gain leverage over entire regions. You can constrain adversaries, block access to major shipping routes, and shape the balance of power far beyond the immediate geography. History has repeatedly vindicated Fisher's feces. Consider Russia, a continental giant whose maritime ambitions were frustrated for centuries by two such choke points, the Bosphorus and the Dardinels.

Despite fighting 12 wars over several hundred years, these traits ultimately remained in Turkish hands, effectively sealing off Russia's path to sustained southern maritime expansion. The 21st century has not repealed these old laws of geography, but the center of great power competition has unmistakably shifted from naval dominance toward economic and infrastructural control. Globalization driven by the digital and financial revolutions has created an entirely new class of critical choke points. They are no longer narrow waterways or canals.

Instead, they are strategic nodes embedded within the dense global network of finance, telecommunications, logistics, and advanced technology. The hidden junctions where power now concentrates and leverage is exercised. Modern choke points are invisible to the naked eye. Yet their impact on national sovereigntity and economic stability is greater than ever.

These are the nodes where global flows of capital, data, and critical technological components converge. They include the US dollar which underpins around 90% of global foreign exchange transactions. They include the financial plumbing, chips, and swift that enables crossber settlements. and they include control over the key technologies required to produce advanced semiconductors, the foundation of modern computing and AI.

Contrary to the popular narrative of the 1990s, they believe that globalization and the internet would decentralize power and democratize international relations, reality has moved in the opposite direction. Instead of dispersion, globalization has produced an unprecedented concentration of the world's critical choke points in the hands of the United States and its allies. That gives Washington a level of power Admiral Fiser could only have dreamed of. With a single presidential signature, the United States can cut Iran off from the global financial system, freeze their reserves of the Russian central bank, or deny China access to critical technologies.

America holds what is effectively an economic nuclear weapon. And as recent years have shown, Washington does not hesitate to use it. More than that, it is systematically refining and expanding this arsenal. But turning the global economy into a battlefield comes with a deep structural consequences.

It increasingly erodess the very infrastructure on which American power itself is built. We are now witnessing the early stages of a process in which US rivals, above all, China and Russia, but also countries across the global south are scrambling to develop alternatives. Their goal is not only to bypass US controlled choke points, but to construct parallel insulated economic systems of their own. Before our eyes, the foundation of the postwar economic order is beginning to crack.

The era of naive integration, the belief that trade civilizes, that the globalization is the only path forward is fading into history. The tools once meant to bind the world together are now being used to pull it apart. Welcome to the age of economic wars. Wars whose origins, rules, and objectives are mapped out by Edward Fishman in his new book, Chokepoint.

A former US State Department official, Fishman offers not academic theory, but a rare insider's view of how the American colossus operates as it fights to preserve its hegemony. As one of the architects of sanctions against Russia, he takes us inside Washington's decision rooms and show how the United States systematically weaponizes the choke points of the 21st century. The true foundation of American power in the 21st century is not aircraft carriers or nuclear warheads. It is the US dollar.

Its role reaches far beyond that of America's national currency. The dollar functions as the world's default medium of settlement and trade. To understand how this came to be, we need to go back to the Bretonwoods Conference of 1944. There the postwar financial order was created.

The dollar became the anchor currency for international transactions and was pecked to gold at a fixed rate of $35 per ounce. This system delivered the stability needed for Europe's reconstruction, but it also tethered US credibility to its physical gold reserves. When President Richard Nixon ended dollar gold convertability in 1971, effectively collapsing the Bretonwood system, the dollar's global role did not weaken, it expanded. The key was the petrod dollar system born from an agreement negotiated in 1974 between US Treasury Secretary William Simon and Saudi Arabia.

In exchange for American security guarantees and advanced weapons supplies, Riyad committed to pricing oil sales exclusively in US dollars. This created a closed capital loop. Any country seeking to purchase energy first had to acquire dollars and surplus revenues. The so-called petro dollars were then reinvested into US government debt, [music] primarily treasury bonds.

The result was permanent structural global demand for the dollar, effectively insulating it from fluctuations in the performance of the US economy itself. Today, dollar dominance is even more overwhelming than during the Cold War. Although the US share of global GDP has fallen to roughly 25%, the dollar is involved in nearly 90% of all foreign exchange transactions in a market that now clears more than $7 trillion per day. Even deals that appear unrelated to the United States, for example, Brazilian soybeans sold to China or Polish wheat shipped to Egypt move through the American financial system.

The dollar has become as essential to global trade as oxygen. But this dependence creates a modern choke point, one that can easily be weaponized in an era of economic warfare. To understand how the dollar became a weapon, we first need to examine how international trade transactions actually work and how the global clearing system operates. Modern banking operates on a correspondent banking system.

When, for example, an Indian refinery pays an Angolan supplier in US dollars, the standard currency for international trade, the transaction does not involve physical cash. Money moves as electronic entries on bank accounts. For a bank in Mumbai to transfer dollars to a bank in Lwanda, the payment must pass through the clearing infrastructure of the currency's issuer. In the case of the dollar, that means US-based systems such as chips or fed wire.

As a result, even transactions between two entirely non-American entities still for a fraction of a second quote touch the US financial system. That fleeting contact creates legal jurisdiction for the US government. This gives Washington not only unmatched visibility into global money flows and financial networks, but more importantly the perfect conditions for economic coercion. conditions the United States has proved more than willing to exploit.

Over the past two decades, starting with the Patriot Act, after the September 11 attacks, the White House has built a vast arsenal of economic weapons based on the dollar's dominance. The most powerful tool in the US Treasury's arsenal is secondary sanctions. first deployed against Iran. Traditional sanctions were often ineffective because third countries routinely ignored them and continue trading with sanctioned actors.

Secondary sanctions solved that problem. Their defining feature is extr territorial reach. They prohibit business with sanctioned entities such as Iranian firms, not only for American companies, but for any foreign entity that uses the dollar or relies on US financial infrastructure. The penalty for violation is exclusion for the US clearing system.

For most banks, a financial death sentence. The power of secondary sanctions is best illustrated by the case of Instax. After the US withdrew from the Iran nuclear deal in 2018, the European Union sought to preserve lucrative contracts secured by European firms in Iran. To bypass US pressure, Europe established inst mechanism designed to facilitate trade with Tehran outside the swift system and settle transactions in euros.

But bankers in Paris, Frankfurt, and London soon concluded that the profits from Iranian trade were far too small to justify a confrontation with the US Treasury and the potential loss of access to the dollar. The result was telling. Over 4 years of operation, Instax processed exactly one transaction, the purchase of medicines, which was already exempt from US sanctions. In 2023, Instax was shut down.

In effect, European firms lost Iranian contracts valued at more than 40 billion euros, sacrificed under direct pressure from Washington. American pressure tools are not always as blunt as secondary sanctions. Sometimes Washington relies on more subtle but equally dangerous instruments. A prime example are the sectoral sanctions imposed on Russia after the annexation of Crimea in 2014.

Instead of cutting Russian companies off from the dollar entirely, sectoral sanctions restricted Moscow's access to specific critical services. Most notably, they prohibited the extension of new long-term financing to Russian entities with maturities exceeding 30 days and in some cases 90 days. This was a particularly surgical strike. Major Russian state corporations operated on a model of constant debt rollover in western financial markets, paying off old loans by issuing new ones in New York and London.

Sectoral sanctions severed this access to cheap capital and forced Moscow to drain its own foreign exchange reserves. In recent years, as rivalry between the United States and China has intensified, the center of gravity in economic warfare has shifted steadily from finance towards technology. And here too, the United States controls the key choke point. The semiconductor industry offers a textbook example.

While the world's most advanced lithography machines essential for cheap production are built by the Dutch company ASML and manufacturing itself largely takes place at Taiwan's DSMC. The process still hinges on a critical American component. The software used to design integrated circuits, so-called EDA tools, is monopolized by two US firms, Cadence and Synopsis. Moreover, even ASML's machines are packed with American components.

Exploiting this unique supply chain architecture, the US Department of Commerce deployed the foreign direct product rule, FDPR, a regulation that dramatically extends American jurisdiction. Under FDPR, any product manufactured anywhere in the world falls under US control if its production involves even a single element of American technology or software. Because cadence and synopsis tools are industry standards, FDPR effectively grants Washington a veto over advanced semiconductor exports to China, even when those chips are produced not in the United States, but in Taiwan. Crucially, American use of FDPR has accelerated rapidly.

In 2020, it was applied only to a single firm, Huawei. By 2022, it had been expanded to entire sectors of the Russian and Bellarusian economies. In an era of accelerating technological competition, FDPR has become a powerful weapon, a new kind of iron curtain used to cut America's rivals off from the key technologies of the 21st century. America's arsenal of economic weapons packs enormous destructive power and can inflict real damage on the economies of US adversaries.

But it is not a miracle weapon capable of winning every confrontation on its own. Military history teaches a consistent lesson. Every new offensive strategy eventually provokes a defensive counter strategy. The same dynamic now applies to economic warfare.

States targeted by US pressure are now learning how to evade sanctions, adapt their economies, and construct alternative systems that, though less efficient, allow them to survive confrontation with Washington. The earliest testing ground for this strategy was Iran, patient zero of modern economic warfare. between 2012 and 2015 coordinated US and European pressure, cutting Theran off from swift and global oil markets, crippled Iran's economy and forced the Islamic Republic to sign the JCPOA nuclear agreement. But when President Trump withdrew from the deal in 2018 and launched a renewed campaign to break Iran economically, the effort failed spectacularly.

Yes, Iran's economy was battered. A currency crisis erupted. Waves of anti-government protests followed. But Tehran did not capitulate.

Instead, Iran partially circumvented sanctions and today exports oil at record levels, around 2 million barrels per day. How was that possible? First, Iran changed its customer. Accepting the loss of European buyers, Tehran now sells almost exclusively to China, but not to large state firms like Cync, which have global exposure and fear secondary sanctions.

Their purchases go instead to the so-called teapot refineries. Hundreds of small independent plants concentrated mainly in Shandong province. These companies hold no overseas assets, do not use the US financial system, and operate entirely within China's domestic market, making them effectively immune to the reach of the US Treasury. Payment mechanisms also bypass the dollar entirely.

Transactions are settled in Chinese yuan via barter agreements or through halal tight networks. Together, Iran and China have created a closed commodity financial loop almost completely opaque to Western regulators. Iran's export revival would not have been possible without parallel logistics, a model later copied by Russia after 2022 through the creation of the so-called shadow fleet. The G7 price cap on Russian oil sought to exploit a choke point in the maritime insurance market.

More than 90% of the global tanker fleet is insured by a small cluster of companies based in London. Under the price cap mechanism, Western shipping or insurance services were prohibited for Russian oil shipments sold above $60 per barrel. In theory, this was an elegant solution, draining Kremlin revenues without triggering a global supply shock or physical shortages. In practice, Moscow quickly bypassed the system by mobilizing hundreds of aging tankers operating entirely outside Western insurance markets.

These vessels sail with transponders switched off, change flags, rename ships, conduct open sea shipto- ship transfers, and even blend Russian crude with oil from other sources, all to conceal true origin. The results of this confrontation are mixed. The price cap has undeniably raised Moscow's operational costs. At the same time, it has proven porous.

Russia continues to sell its oil at market prices far above the G7 ceiling, demonstrating that control over a financial choke point loses its power when an adversary is determined to build parallel infrastructure. And here lies the greatest threat to American coercive leverage. The more countries are swept into the US sanctions regime, the stronger their incentives to cooperate become. What we are now witnessing is the consolidation of an informal anti-western block.

What some US strategists labeled the new axis of evil. States isolated from Western markets and technology, especially Iran, Russia, and China are building their own closed economic circuits. The clearest example is the deepening partnership between Russia and Iran. Before 2022, Moscow maintained relations with Tehran, but largely avoided violating US sanctions imposed on Iran.

After Russia fell under a sanctions regime similar to Iran's following the invasion of Ukraine, cooperation intensified rapidly. The two countries linked portions of their banking systems and accelerated work on the international north south transport corridor designed to bypass western controlled routes. Within this emerging block, China plays the role of the elder brother. Chinese exports to Russia surged, filling the void left by western companies.

Beijing now supplies everything from cars and smartphones to machine tools, semiconductors, and dual use components critical to Russia's defense industry. Although major Chinese banks officially avoid running a foul of US sanctions, smaller financial institutions, barter mechanisms, and localized settlement systems have enabled continued trade with both Russia and Iran. The most dangerous long-term consequence of America's heavy reliance on sanctions is the acceleration of efforts to construct systems outside the dollar framework. Since 2022, interest has grown in alternative settlement currencies, especially the UA for oil trade and in payment systems independent of the United States.

China is promoting SIPs as a swift alternative. Russia has expanded its domestic SPFs network. For now, these systems handle only a fraction of global payment volume and do not seriously challenge the dollar's dominance, but the trend is unmistakable. Most significant is the development of central bank digital currencies, including China's digital yuan.

These allow direct wallet towallet transactions, bypassing correspondent banking entirely. and with it the American choke points that currently anchor US power. If such technologies gain global traction, the financial infrastructure that underpins American dominance today may eventually become obsolete. The future, however, is likely measured in decades, not years.

Many observers view America's weaponization of strategic choke points as chaotic or impulsive, driven by short-term political interests. That is a mistake. The process has a far broader geopolitical logic. The goal is not simply to wound individual rivals, but to correct the trajectory of globalization itself.

For decades, the United States was globalization's chief architect and driving [music] force. Today, Washington increasingly recognizes that globalization in its original form is no longer sustainable, at least not without undermining America's hegemonic position. US rivals, above all, China leveraged open global markets for rapid economic growth, which in turn fueled military expansion and rising political influence. The globalization designed to strengthen American leadership has paradoxically produced a new strategic colossus, the People's Republic of China.

Faced with this reality, Washington is using the choke points it still controls in an attempt to re-engineer globalization to block China, Russia, Iran, and other rivals from a meaningful participation in the global economic system. Whether this strategy can succeed is highly uncertain as US pressure intensifies. Its adversaries are closing ranks and constructing alternatives. Worse still, the weaponization of choke points does collateral damage to America's European allies who are growing increasingly uneasy about the long-term future of transatlantic relations.

The age of economic warfare is pushing the world toward fragmentation. But the final outcome remains open. One scenario is a reinforced iron curtain separating hostile economies from the global system. Another is more ironic.

The very system that elevated the United States to superpower status may now become the force that undermines it.
