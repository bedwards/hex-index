To understand what Poland is building, we should first look at the structure the entire system rests on. This provides the foundation for assessing the state of the Polish military today and where it is headed by 2045. Until recently, Poland fielded three divisions.

The backbone of the force consists of three longest established divisions. the 11th Armored Cavalry Division, the 12th Mechanized Division, and the 16th Mechanized Division. In 2018, Poland added the 18th Mechanized Division, a priority frontline formation where the newest heavy equipment, including Abram's tanks, is being deployed. Two more divisions began forming in 2023.

The first Legends Division and the Eighth Home Army Division, both expected to reach full readiness in the early 2030s. And the expansion goes further. Poland is also planning a specialurpose division built around elite airborne and air mobility units. Its role will focus on deep strike capabilities, combining mass drone operations with cruise and ballistic missiles able to reach more than 1,000 km inside enemy territory.

This reflects a broader doctrinal shift. Traditionally, Poland relied on deterrence by denial. the ability to defend its territory effectively enough to discourage an attack. The new goal moves a step further.

[music] Deterrence by punishment, the capability to impose significant cost deep inside an adversaries territory. In this planned structure, each division is expected to field four brigades. Each brigade four battalions. Each battalion equipped with 58 modern armored platforms, tanks, infantry fighting vehicles, or armored personal carriers.

With this structural overview in place, we can now turn to the individual branches of the Polish armed forces. Let's start with the biggest one, the land [music] forces. The scale of change in Poland's land forces is massive and deliberate. Warso openly argues that it is building the largest and most powerful land army in the EU.

If procurement targets are met, Poland's armored vehicle inventory would exceed that of most EU countries combined. The transformation began after 2022. Before the war, Poland relied heavily on aging Soviet era T72 and PT91 tanks along with Leopart 2S. Most of the T72 and PT91 fleet around 400 vehicles was transferred to Ukraine just after refurbishment along with spare parts and ammunition.

It was a major contribution to Ukraine's war effort, but it also created an argent capability gap at home. That gap is now being filled with modern equipment. Poland has already received 116 M1 A1 Abrams tanks [music] which are planned to be gradually modernized to the latest version. At the same time, deliveries of 250 advanced M1 A2 SCP V3 Abrams among the most capable tanks in the world are nearing completion.

These vehicles are being deployed primarily with the 18th mechanized division. In parallel, Poland is introducing South Korea's K2GP Black Panther tanks. Around 180 vehicles are already in service with additional deliveries underway. From 2028, Poland expects to begin receiving the enhanced K2PL variant with plans for large-scale local production that could eventually reach up to 800 units.

This would mark a return to tank manufacturing in Poland and a long-term investment in the country's defense industry. However, executive contracts for the bulk of K2PL production are still pending, and the timeline extends deep into the 1930s, making it vulnerable to budget pressures or shifting priorities. Both the Abrams and K2 programs include full logistical support, specialized engineering vehicles, and active protection systems designed to defend against anti-tank missiles and drones. These new fleets are being complemented by more than 200 Leopart 2 tanks, roughly half of them Leopart 2PL in modernized versions.

Altogether, Poland already operates around 522 modern or near modern main battle tanks. one of the largest and most advanced armored forces in Europe, but still aims to have three times that amount. Poland also secured large stocks of modern tank ammunition, tens of thousands of rounds secured from the United States, South Korea, and Germany. Domestic production is also underway.

Let's note that these munitions are significantly more advanced than those used by Russia, which means Polish tanks can achieve the same effects with fewer shots. In modern warfare, raw quantities alone do not tell the full story. But while the armed forces are rapidly strengthening, the situation in mechanized infantry remains far more challenging. The mechanized infantry situation illustrates one of the central challenges of Poland's modernization.

The gap between ambitious plans and current reality. As one joke circulating among Polish soldiers puts it, the most common infantry fighting vehicle in the Polish army today is a Yel truck. The main reason once again is support for Ukraine. Poland transferred hundreds of aging BMP1 infantry fighting vehicles.

It is estimated that at least 400 operational vehicles were delivered. As with the tanks, platforms that could not be repaired were scrapped, but only after all usable parts had been removed. The total number of armored vehicles lost as a result of support for Ukraine may have reached as many as 800 vehicles. That is equivalent of the equipment of roughly 14 mechanized battalions.

Very few of these vehicles remain in Polish service today. And even before the war, their technical condition was already poor. Replacing them has proven slow. The long- awaited Borsuk infantry fighting vehicle has only recently entered initial service with just over a 100 ordered so far.

Full scale production is expected to ramp up later this decade, but the overall requirement is estimated at around 1,500 vehicles, including specialized variants. At current timelines, this effort will extend well into the 2030s. Additional demand will come from a planned heavy infantry fighting vehicle program called Ratel, suggesting total requirements of up to 2,000 platforms as Poland expands to six divisions. In the meantime, part of the gap is being filled by the wheeled APC Kate Orosoma.

Several hundred are already in service with additional deliveries planned. These vehicles are being upgraded with modern domestically produced remote controlled turret ZSSW30 systems improving their combat effectiveness while the next generation of tracked vehicles is still coming online. Missile and artillery forces is another area where the Polish military has undergone massive modernization in recent years. So extensive that it has sparked controversy.

Poland currently operates about 180 rocket artillery systems with plans to expand this number to over 300. Earth defense is a topic that deserves a separate discussion. Formally, these systems are split between the land forces and the air force. But the scale of investment, costs, and operational challenges make them a critical pillar of Poland's military transformation. The top layer of the system is Visa, built around eight Patriot batteries acquired in two phases.

In total, 64 launchers will protect key cities and military bases against aircraft and missile attacks. a role whose importance is demonstrated daily by the war in Ukraine. The first two batteries are already operational while the remaining units are entering production with full capability expected around 2040. The system will include advanced LTADS AESA radars currently fielded only by the United States capable of detecting both small drones and high-speed missiles.

At the heart of the architecture is the IBCS battle management system which integrates sensors and launchers into a single network. This allows the system to select the most appropriate interceptor for each threat. An important feature given the high cost of advanced PAC free MSE missiles. Lower cost interceptors from the CAM family used in the NAV system are expected to serve as the so-called affordable.

IBCS is an American system, but Poland is actually ahead of the US in its implementation. The second layer, NARF, will provide short range protection for critical infrastructure and frontline units. The program includes 23 batteries, each with six launchers, a total of 138 launchers capable of firing 1,14 CAM eriles in one salvo. Production of missiles and key components is being localized in Poland with deliveries planned between 2027 and 2035.

Experience from Ukraine has led to an additional layer pitus. This program upgrades existing very short range air defense units by adding CAM launchers and replacing older radars with modern ones. In total, 21 batteries will add another 42 launchers and 336 readyto fire missiles. All units are expected to enter service by 2029.

Taken together and without counting portable systems such as Poprat or Puron, Poland's layered air defense when finished could launch exactly 2,464 interceptors simultaneously. one of the largest capabilities of its kind in Europe and even in the world. The system is also being expanded to address the growing drone threat. Under the recently approved SAND program worth roughly $4 billion, the Army will field 18 additional counter drone batteries.

Combining radars, electronic warfare, and a mix of kinetic and non-kinetic deliveries are planned within the next 2 years. But a modern military is not just tanks, missiles or aircraft. It also depends on something far more basic. What the soldier actually wears and carries.

For years, Poland's program covering personal equipment such as rifles, optics, body armor, clothing, boots, and communication systems was widely criticized for poor quality and slow modernization. The criticism was simple. While billions were being spent on major weapon systems, soldiers often lacked a proper pair of boots. The situation is improving, though many observers still argue the pace is too slow.

Another rapidly growing component is the cyber defense forces. While they do not involve large procurement programs, this branch has been expanding quickly and regularly performs well in NATO cyber exercises. A key initiative is Cyber Legion, launched in 2025, which builds a civilian reserve of IT specialists who support military operations part-time. The program has proven highly successful, attracting more than 2,000 experts, far exceeding initial expectations.

Still, all the fancy stuff would be worthless without a proper space reconnaissance. That's why Warso is trying to establish a presence in space, too. Poland's operations in space domain is organized under the Argus system, the geospatial intelligence and satellite services agency. For years, Poland lacked long range reconnaissance capabilities, but now that gap is closing.

Within the next few years, the country is expected to develop some of the most advanced space-based reconnaissance capabilities in Europe. Argus serves as a central hub integrating data from multiple sources including commercial providers and international partnerships with countries such as France and [music] Italy. A key element is cooperation with ICI, a Polish Finnish company operating a large constellation of radar satellites SAR with its operational center in Warso. This will allow Poland to control its own radar satellite constellation and determine when and where imaging is conducted. Further capabilities will come from the globe program which includes the plant acquisition of highresolution optical satellites from Bbas. In addition, Poland has already placed the PAS satellite constellation in orbit developed by Creotech in cooperation with the Military University of Technology. Its follow-up, the Microlope constellation, will consist of four satellites equipped for optical and infrared reconnaissance with deployment expected by 2027. Taken together with other programs, Poland is likely to operate at least 12 to 15 military satellites within the next few years. And this brings us to the end of the story of Poland's military modernization.

It is a complex and risky process, but also a necessary one. Rapid expansion can strain training pipelines, burden logistics chains, and create maintenance challenges, particularly when operating equipment for multiple countries, as it is with tanks, American Abrams, South Korean K2s, German Leoports, with different supply chains [music] and technical requirements. No military is perfect and none ever will be. A transformation on this scale is rare, even by global standards, and it inevitably involves trade-offs between cost, capability, and industrial capacity.

Factories cannot be built overnight, and advanced systems take years to develop and deliver.
