The central axis of the new American strategy is the concept of national interest. As the document states plainly, the purpose of foreign policy is the protection of core national interests. That is the sole focus of this strategy. The Trump administration delivers a sharp indictment of the foreign policy pursued for decades by America's liberal elites.

Those elites, it argues, convinced citizens that promoting democracy abroad and fighting costly distant wars, particularly in the Middle East, served America's national interest. According to Trump, this was a profound falsehood. It led to the overextension of the American Empire and the gradual depletion of its resources. Trump locates the roots of America's crisis, above all in the promotion of globalism and free trade, and in the excessive entanglement of US foreign policy with international institutions such as the United Nations.

[music] The new strategy argues it was the mechanisms of free trade that led to the dismantling of America's industrial base, the erosion of the middle class, and the growing dependence of the United States on supply chains controlled by tribals. Globalization is no longer presented as an opportunity, but as a vehicle for transferring wealth out of the United States, primarily to China. A similar reassessment applies to international organizations which were originally intended to serve as a force multipliers and reinforce America's global dominance. In practice, however, they have proven to be instruments that constrain Washington and limit its ability to conduct a flexible foreign policy.

The Trump administration seeks a clean break from the legacy of its predecessors, an end to liberal interventionism, and the fundamental redefinition of what American national interest actually means. Where Trump's predecessors defined national interest as the defense of the liberal world order, Trump defines it far more narrowly, as the security of the United States itself. The entire new strategy is subordinated to this single priority. Crucially, however, security is understood in very broad terms.

It does not refer solely to physical protection against military threats such as a hostile invasion of US territory. It also includes protection against hostile trade practices, foreign influence operations, drug cartels, and trafficking networks, mass immigration, and even the defense of civilizational identity. This allows us to identify three core components of US security, military, economic, and civilizational. Defined this way, security carries profound implications for American foreign In Donald Trump's world, a trade deficit is not merely a macroeconomic indicator whose significance depends on other variables.

It is a symptom of strategic weakness comparable to the loss of territory. A trade deficit represents a transfer of American national power to its rivals and one that must be corrected as quickly as possible. In Trump's worldview, re-industrialization of the United States ceases to be merely an economic objective and becomes above all a matter of national security. America must be able to produce everything from steel and ammunition to the most advanced semiconductor chips in order to shield itself from economic coercion by states that control critical supply chains.

This uncompromising approach and the broader securitization of policy is clearly reflected in Trump's pressure to strengthen the US energy sector. The new strategy fully rejects the climate policies of recent decades, dismissing both climate change and the net zero agenda as a quote harmful ideology that has benefited only America's adversaries. As a result, traditional fossil fuels, oil, gas, and coal, along with nuclear energy, regain their status as strategic resources meant to provide cheap and reliable power for America's re-industrializing economy. At the same time, exports of US energy resources, particularly LNG, are elevated to the level of strategic lever.

Energy exports are intended to secure American energy dominance. displace rival powers from key markets and bind the economies of partner states, especially in Europe, more tightly to the United States. The new American strategy also places enormous emphasis on what it defines as the country's civilizational security. The Trump administration argues that if the American empire is to endure, it must be strong, not only militarily and economically, but also spiritually.

Trump seeks to protect what he calls the strength of the American spirit through several channels, foremost among them a decisive break with mass immigration, not only illegal, but mass immigration as such, which in his view has overwhelmed the United States. The president argues that mass immigration has strained national resources, increased crime, weakened social cohesion, distorted labor markets, and undermined national security. The new strategy, therefore, treats mass migration as a form of external invasion. Border protection and the prevention of further migration waves are elevated to the rank of absolute priorities.

Trump also identifies a threat to America's civilizational strength in radical forms of social engineering and ideological activism, including woke ideology and DEI programs, diversity, equity, and inclusion. In his view, this current hollow out society from within, penetrating every sphere of American life, from schools and workplaces to the military and politics, and leaving the nation vulnerable to what the strategy describes as cultural subversion, a weakness that America's adversaries can readily exploit. In this sense, the new strategy also signals a conservative cultural counterrevolution grounded in the defense of traditional values. For Trump, the overriding priority is the security of the United States, a security he's prepared to guarantee at any cost.

There are no fixed [music] rules. The ends justify the means. In the new strategy, this approach is described as flexible realism. Trump seeks a foreign policy guided exclusively by American national interest, not by the promotion of democratic values or human rights.

US diplomats are expected to negotiate, strike deals, and strengthen the empire, not to deliver more lectures about the superiority of the democratic world over other systems. Under the new strategy, the United States is ready to cooperate with any state, whether a democracy, a military dictatorship, or an absolute monarchy. The political system of a given country is of no importance to Trump. What matters and matters alone is whether cooperation serves the national interest of the United States.

Trump therefore embraces pure transactionalism. In his worldview, political and military alliances are not rooted in shared values, but function as business contracts with clearly defined terms, valid only as long as they serve America's interests. Unlike in previous decades, America's partners are no longer those who share liberal values, but those who are solvent and above all useful to the United States. The most striking expression of this transactional approach is Trump's attitude toward NATO.

During his first term, he repeatedly criticized alliance members for relying on the American security umbrella while failing to develop their own defense capabilities. As a result of this pressure and accelerated by the war in Ukraine, European NATO members gradually increased defense spending. At the NATO summit in the hog in June 2025, they went so far as to agree to allocate 5% of GDP to defense. The new US strategy acknowledges this more balanced burden sharing as a positive development, but concludes that it is still insufficient.

Trump wants America's allies to assume even greater responsibility for their own security. European NATO members are expected to take primary responsibility for the defense of the continent, freeing up US military resources for use in other regions of the world. Trump's objective is therefore no longer limited to a more balanced sharing of responsibilities, burden sharing, but extends to a far more ambitious goal, burden shifting, onto America's allies. Washington appears to recognize that this approach could generate serious tensions within NATO.

To manage this risk, the strategy proposes the creation of what it calls burden sharing networks, groupings of states willing to assume greater responsibility for the defense of specific regions. Within these networks, the United States would act primarily as a coordinator rather than the principal guarantor and finaniier of security. To incentivize allies to take on the role of regional security providers, Washington offers a menu of benefits. States prepared to shoulder greater responsibility can expect preferential trade terms with the United States easier access to American weapon systems and privileged entry into US technological ecosystems.

These policies carry potentially far-reaching consequences. NATO increasingly shifts from an alliance of shared values toward a paid security service. At the same time, there is a growing risk of dividing [music] NATO members into two tiers with only Washington's top performers enjoying preferential treatment and taxes. The new strategy identifies five regions for which it outlines a more detailed vision of future US foreign policy.

the Western Hemisphere, the Indopacific, Europe, the Middle East, and Africa. With regard to the Western Hemisphere, traditionally regarded by Washington as it exclusive sphere of influence, the new strategy announces a return to first principles, the Monroe Doctrine of 1823. In its original form, the Monroe Doctrine declared that the American continents were no longer open to colonization or political interference by European powers and that any such attempt would be treated as a hostile act against the United States. The new strategy updates this doctrine through what it terms the Trump correlary.

Under this revised framework, contemporary external powers, above all China, but also Russia and Thran, are to be pushed out of the Western Hemisphere. These states are not to maintain military bases in the region, nor to control ports or other critical infrastructure. Excessive economic cooperation between them and countries of the Western Hemisphere is also viewed with suspicion. Venezuela under President Nicolas Maduro has effectively become a testing ground for the implementation of the Trump corollary.

US strategy toward the Western Hemisphere is encapsulated in the slogan enlist and expand. Washington seeks to enlist its old friends to advance American objectives in the region, particularly migration control and the fight against drug trafficking. At the same time, the United States plans a broad diplomatic and economic offensive designed to systematically displace competitors and draw additional countries into the US orbit. The threats America faces in the Western Hemisphere, however, are not limited to competition with China or Russia.

The strategy also highlights asymmetric and hybrid threats, most notably mass migration and drug trafficking into the United States. Trump argues that if the US economy is to thrive, it must also successfully compete for economic and political influence in this region.

On the economic front, the new strategy rejects the long-standing dogma that trade with China would eventually lead to political liberalization. Washington now seeks to reduce its own economic exposure to China while simultaneously leveraging its partners to exert pressure on Beijing. Specifically, the United States intends to push Europe, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and others to adopt policies that would force China to curb its aggressive export-driven model and redirect production toward its domestic market. The front line in competition with China is also being extended to the global south.

The strategy correctly observes that China has tied many developing countries to itself through infrastructure loans. Washington plans a counter offensive. It aims to mobilize massive financial assets held by partners in Europe and Asia estimated at $7 trillion in order to offer poorer countries an alternative to Chinese capital. Washington's logic is explicit.

The long-term preservation of economic and technological primacy is the most reliable way to avoid a kinetic war with China. This implies sustained pressure on allies to cut China off from advanced technologies, including semiconductors, artificial intelligence, and quantum computing. To that end, the United States seeks to build an informal technological alliance in which access to American capital markets and innovation is offered as a reward for political alignment and the economic isolation of Beijing. On the military side, the new strategy underscores the necessity of defending Taiwan against Chinese aggression.

For the Trump administration, Taiwan's strategic value lies not only in its dominance of semiconductor manufacturing, but above all in its role in maintaining control over the first island chain. The ark stretching from Japan through Taiwan to the Philippines. This chain forms a natural barrier preventing China's navy from breaking out into the deep waters of the Pacific. Should Taiwan fall, the entire American strategy toward the region would collapse, opening a direct path for China toward Guam and Hawaii.

At the same time, the United States expects its Asian allies, Japan and South Korea, to assume greater responsibility for the defense of the region. Once again, the new strategy returns to the issue of burden sharing. Asian allies are expected to expand their conventional defense capabilities and actively reinforce the first island chain. Moreover, the price of the American security umbrella is rising.

Washington now demands not only higher defense spending, but also broader access for the US Navy to allied ports and military bases. The arrangement is straightforward. Allies are to build a wall along the first island chain, while the United States provides the strategic depth and the striking power necessary to prevail in a confrontation with China should war prove unavoidable.
