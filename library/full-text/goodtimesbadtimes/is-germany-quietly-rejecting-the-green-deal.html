I don't know of any other country that has made the energy transition as difficult and as expensive as Germany has. We set ourselves a goal that now needs to be corrected. But quite simply, we no longer have sufficient generation capacity.

Admitting this may be the most powerful symbol of the scale of the failure so far. A failure that becomes even clearer when we look at the data. Electricity consumption in Germany peaked in 2007 at 625 terowatt hours. Throughout the second half of the 20th century, Europe and other developing regions broadly assumed that GDP growth would translate into a proportional rise in electricity demand.

Germany's energy transition was meant to break this link, delivering major savings through reduced grid losses and steadily improving energy efficiency. The transition however was accompanied by the relocation of energyintensive industries beyond Germany's borders. After 2010, new chemical, metological, and cement plants were built primarily in Asia, especially in China. This process was already described in a 2015 report by the handles research institute titled future scenarios for energyintensive industries in Germany and Austria.

The report noted the trend with concern. Yet, as its authors observed at the time, quote, "The shift from an industrial society to a service-based one has over recent decades generally been welcomed as an unequivocally positive development toward a quote clean economy and was clearly visible across much of the Western world. It was therefore no surprise that by 2015 electricity demand in Germany had already fallen to 588 terowatt hours and continued to decline. 10 years later it stands below 500 terowatt hours.

Therefore over two decades electricity consumption in Germany has dropped by around 20%. A similar decline can be observed in per capita electricity use. But lower consumption did not mean that German power plants were struggling with excess capacity or that the energy market was facing over supply. Quite the opposite.

As recently as 2010, energy companies delivered 632 terowatt hours of electricity to customers. By 2017, that figure had actually risen to 652 terowatt hours. Then came a sharp collapse down to just 432 terowatt hours in 2024. That is a decline of nearly 35%.

As Chancellor Mertz himself noted, the shutdown of nuclear power plants turned out to be a serious strategic mistake. Their capacity was not replaced neither by renewable energy sources nor by any other alternatives. As a result, Germany has shifted from being a net exporter of electricity to becoming the largest net importer of power in the European Union. In 2024, Germany imported a total of 67 terowatt hours of electricity while exporting only 35 terowatt hours.

And as a final almost symbolic data point, according to federal statistics, electricity production per capita in Germany stood at nearly 9,000 kwatt hours annually in 2008. By 2024, it had fallen to just 5,500 kilowatt hours, a drop of nearly 40%. In other words, before our very eyes, the largest economy in the European Union and its most powerful member state has subjugated itself to a voluntary process of delectrification. Worse still, this has happened at precisely the moment when China, which has emerged as the EU's primary economic and strategic competitor, has nearly doubled its electricity production over the past decade.

One might say the operation, the energy transition was a success. The patient Germany died. Not entirely perhaps. Chancellor Mert's reflection is a positive sign, but Berlin now faces a challenge of historic proportions.

The number one problem, competitiveness. The competitiveness of German industry has reached an alarmingly low level reported tag spiel in November 2025 citing a study by the Munich based EO Institute for Economic Research. According to EO, the assessment of German industrial competitiveness is now the worst in the history of the survey. In October, 37% of companies reported a decline in competitiveness relative to countries outside the European Union.

More than 10 percentage points higher than just 6 months earlier. Germany's position is also deteriorating within the EU itself with 21% of respondents reporting a loss of competitiveness against other member states. As Klaus Volabe, head of research at Epho, emphasized, German industrial competitiveness has fallen to a new historic low, clearly reflecting the depth of the country's structural problems. The decline spans nearly all sectors, but it is particularly severe in energyintensive industries.

51% of chemical firms, 47% of electronics and optics manufacturers, and 40% of mechanical engineering companies report significant losses. As a result, industrial output has now been shrinking for the fourth consecutive year. According to Federation of German Industries, BDI, industrial production is expected to fall by around 2% in 2025. Since 2019, nearly 272,000 industrial jobs have been eliminated.

120,000 of them by September 2025 alone, including 49,000 in the automotive sector. Not a single industrial branch recorded net job growth during this period, confirming a slow by persistent contraction of the entire sector. But the final months of 2025 resembled a true route. Tyson Group began eliminating 11,000 of the 27,000 jobs at its steel making subsidiary.

So far, two steel plants, GZA Kirishin in Germany and Eber in France have been shut down, both relaying on electricity intensive blast furnace processes. Meanwhile, the French chemical plant DAO has announced plans to close parts of its facilities in Skoau and to shut down its entire plant in Berlin. And this is only a small fragment of a much longer list of companies slowly drifting toward bankruptcy or relocating production away from Germany to entirely different regions of the world. Quote, "Taxes, energy costs, and labor costs in Germany are so high that production here is simply no longer profitable for many companies," said Olivier Xander, president of the Gazamp Metal Employers Association, summarizing 2025.

Quoting Zandel Derspiggel added that Gazamp Metal expects a loss of tens of thousands of jobs in the metal working and electrical engineering sectors in 2026, blaming the excessively high production costs born by German firms. The situation is no different in the automotive and chemical industries and only marginally better in the industrial equipment sector. Meanwhile, cars, automotive parts, machinery, chemicals, pharmaceuticals, and electrical equipment remain the core pillars of German exports. For a quarter of a century, exports have been the primary engine of Germany's economic model, driving growth and sustaining prosperity.

The scale is impressive. In 2025, Germany exported goods worth 1.6 trillion. China, now firmly established as the factory of the world, can boast exports of roughly €3.4 trillion euros, but Germany's economy remains a major force on a global scale. The energy trap Germany has fallen into combined with the erosion of industrial competitiveness is producing increasingly tangible consequences. These effects are no longer limited to those tied directly to industry. They are beginning to be felt even more acutely by ordinary middle inome Germans as well. This is happening because the foundations of the welfare state are being steadily eroded.

Those foundations rest largely on local governments which shoulder responsibility for a vast share of public services. Municipalities finance from their own budgets, kindergartens, schools, infrastructure, social care, and the operation of asylum centers, including benefits for asylum seekers. Until recently, the quality of these services was widely regarded as exemplary. But over the past 3 years, problems have been escalating rapidly.

At a press conference in Berlin on January 5th this year, Ralph Spigler, president of the German Association of Towns and Municipalities, declared bluntly, quote, "The situation is dramatic. Many cities and municipalities no longer know how to finance their expenditures. Construction projects already have to be halted. Funding for cultural and social activities is also being suspended out of necessity." As local officials told journalists, data from federal statistical office showed that the budget deficit of German municipalities is rising year after year.

In 2023, it stood at around 7.5 billion. In 2024, it jumped to 24 billion. In 2025, it is expected to exceed €30 billion. At the same time, municipal spending has reached roughly €400 billion and is likely to rise further in the coming years due to the growing number of elderly citizens requiring care.

As a result, local governments have begun demanding that the federal government assume onethird of municipal expenditures related to social services. Shortly thereafter, Deutsche illustrated these dynamics in a report on the municipality of Visak in Ben Wenberg. The story offers a vivid example of how Germany's energy trap and the loss of industrial competitiveness translate into everyday life. With just 7,700 inhabitants, Visak, located near Stogart, was long considered the wealthiest municipality in Germany.

Porsche operates a major development center there, paying substantial local taxes. In 2009, those revenues amounted to €20,000 per resident. At the time, anyone who wanted to build a home in a visak and had children received a €10,000 construction subsidy per child from the municipality. Quote, "A fourstory library was built, a concert grand piano costing €100,000 was purchased, and subsidies for private music lessons were granted," Deutsche reports.

Then it adds, "Hard times have arrived. The German automotive industry is mired in the deepest crisis in its history. At Porsche alone, profits fell by around 96% in 2025. As a result, bankruptcy now looms over Visa.

Germans, however, are entirely unaccustomed to the idea that their state might simply stop providing services they take for granted, especially at the moment when living costs are rising, household incomes are stagnating or falling, and mass layoffs are spreading. Taken together, this creates a textbook combustible mix fueling social frustration. And such frustration sooner or later inevitably translates into radical shifts on the political stage.
