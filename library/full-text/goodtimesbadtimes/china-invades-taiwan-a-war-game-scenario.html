Beijing could sue for peace and withdraw its forces from Taiwan, a move that would likely end Xiinping's rule and throw the PRC into internal chaos. Alternatively, Beijing might escalate further, for example, by using tactical nuclear strikes against Taiwan.

Such a decision would prompt the US to respond in kind with tactical nuclear strikes on Chinese footholds in Taiwan, [music] risking an uncontrolled spiral of nuclear escalation. Regardless of what China decides, the United States, though winning the war of attrition, may also consider escalation. Hawks in Washington could push to expand the conflict with deeper strikes on the Chinese mainland, seeing in it a chance to topple the government of the People's Republic. Yet such a move would once again bring the world to the brink of nuclear confrontation.

And so our scenario ends in a state of strategic stalemate. The Chinese invasion continues while the Americans methodically destroy PLA warships and aircraft, providing steady support to Taiwan's defenders on the ground. At sea, the US Navy enforces a distant blockade. The Malaka, Sunda, and the Indonesian and Philippine traits are all closed to Chinese shipping.

While China still holds enough stockpiles to fight for months and maintains overland access through Russia, the maritime blockade strikes at the very heart of its economy. As a result, the long-term outlook for China's amphibious campaign grows increasingly bleak. So, what strategic lessons can we draw from this scenario? First, logistics decides the outcome of war.

The results of our war game, like many others, clearly show that a war over Taiwan would above all be a battle of logistics. China lacks a large enough fleet of amphibious ships to sustain an invasion of Taiwan. As a result, the PLA must rely heavily on a vast civilian transport fleet. These vessels, however, are unarmored and make for relatively easy targets.

The entire US and allied defensive strategy centers on destroying that transport fleet to starve Chinese beach heads on the island. This makes America's cost of war comparatively low, but only as long as it has enough long range anti-hship munitions. US submarines play a vital role, but air power is equally decisive. The biggest bottleneck for the US is its limited stock of specialized LAR missiles, roughly 450 in total.

Second base vulnerability is America's critical weakness. About 90% of US air losses occur on the ground. In the first hours of the conflict, US air bases in Japan and Guam lack sufficient hardened shelters, making them highly vulnerable to Chinese missile strikes. This weakness has been known for years, yet little has been done to fix it.

In the past decade, the US has built only two hardened aircraft shelters within a thousand nautical mile radius of Taiwan, far too few to protect its forward deployed assets. Third, Japan is the unsinkable aircraft carrier. Japan's role in the conflict is absolutely crucial. Without it, defending Taiwan would be nearly impossible.

Bases like Kadina on Okinawa, just 600 kilometers from Taiwan, allow US aircraft to fly multiple sorties per day. Without them, the Air Force would have to operate from Guam, 2,700 km away, a logistical nightmare. That would also force the US to deploy its aircraft carriers from day one, almost guaranteeing the lost of at least one of them. It is also worth noting that Japanese bases suffer from the same lack of hardened shelters, leaving them highly exposed to Chinese missile strikes.

Fourth, Taiwan's geography favors the defender. Our war game shows that even a successful amphibious landing is only the beginning of the PLA's problems. Taiwan's coastline offers very few beaches suitable for large-scale landings, and the terrain beyond them, rivers, cities, and mountains, heavily favors the defender and slows any advance. Despite initial successes capturing beach heads, ports, and airfields, the PLA offensive quickly bogged down.

Chinese forces became trapped in brutal urban combat in New Taipei and Tai Chung. Taiwanese defenders skillfully used the terrain to their advantage. for instance, blowing up bridges over the Tamsui River to create natural defensive lines. Fifth, Taiwan must be fully committed to its defense.

It may sound obvious, but it is easy to overlook. Taiwan's survival depends on its determination to fight from day one. The ROC military must wage a relentless defense, constantly harassing PLA beach heads, striking key ports and airfields under Chinese control and launching local counterattacks whenever possible. Without active and organized resistance on the ground, US strikes on Chinese logistics would have little real effect.

Taiwan possesses the resources, terrain, and tactics to inflict heavy losses on the PLA, but morale and the will to fight are just as critical. Sixth, the Taiwan conflict could escalate to nuclear war very quickly. Unlike Ukraine, there is no proxy model possible in Taiwan's case. The United States would be directly engaged in combat from the very first days of the war.

Beijing understands this, which is why most war games suggest that the PLA would immediately strike US bases in Japan and Guam at the outset. Such a scenario could easily push both sides beyond rational calculation once strikes and counter strikes begin. The conflict could spiral very rapidly into uncontrolled nuclear escalation. Seventh, the war for Taiwan is a catastrophe without winners.

Even in scenarios where Taiwan ultimately survives, it is a peric victory achieved at enormous cost for all sides. The United States and its allies lost dozens of ships, hundreds of aircraft, and tens [music] of thousands of troops, weakening their global standing for years to come. Taiwan, though it preserves its autonomy, suffers catastrophic economic and infrastructural damage. China, meanwhile, endures devastating losses, its navy decimated, its amphibious core destroyed, and tens of thousands of soldiers killed or captured, all of which could shake the stability of Communist Party rule.

Worse still, a conflict over Taiwan, the world's leading producer of advanced semiconductors, would trigger unprecedented global supply chain disruptions, unleashing an economic crisis of historic scale. Eight. For China, an invasion of Taiwan may be too costly. Strategic patience as an alternative.

Most war games end the same way with a Chinese defeat. Even under the optimistic assumptions used in our scenario, after a month of fighting, China finds itself in a stalemate with massive losses, especially in its amphibious fleet. A failed invasion could also destabilize China internally with unpredictable consequences. Beijing likely understands that a full-scale invasion may not be the best way to bring Taiwan under its control.

Safer alternatives could include a naval blockade or limited operations against nearby island chains such as Skinmen or the Pescadors. These limited moves would put Washington in a difficult position, forcing the US to fire the first shot and not even in direct defense of Taiwan itself. That would be extremely hard to justify to the American public. Perhaps then China's best option is to do what it does best.

Wait. Historically, China has excelled at strategic patience. In the case of Taiwan, time could work in Beijing's favor. The Trump administration has drifted toward isolationism, viewing many alliances as burdens rather than assets and seeking to redefine them, including the US relationship with Taiwan.

While America remains dependent on Taiwanese semiconductors today, Washington is working to end that reliance by investing in domestic chip production. If that trend continues, future US administrations may begin to reassess the costbenefit balance of defending Taiwan. Over time, patience could become Beijing's greatest weapon, achieving unification not through invasion, but through persistence and shifting global realities.
