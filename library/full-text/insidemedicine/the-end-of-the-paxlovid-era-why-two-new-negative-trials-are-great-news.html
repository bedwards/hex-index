Much will be said about the PANORAMIC and CanTreatCOVID trials, which were finally published as a single paper in the New England Journal of Medicine (NEJM) last week. The big takeaway will be that in two major randomized controlled trials conducted in the United Kingdom and Canada, Paxlovid failed to decrease hospitalizations or deaths in adults with risk factors for severe Covid-19 outcomes.

Let me state this clearly. This is historically good news.

Yes, the fact that two major studies conducted in 2022-2024 failed to show this benefit for Paxlovid is cause for celebration.

To understand why, we can’t bury the real lede here—and I want you to think about this carefully: the studies randomly divided 4,038 higher-risk adults (age ≥50 years or ≥18 years with coexisting conditions, ~98% of whom were vaccinated) with newly diagnosed Covid-19 either to receive Paxlovid or not (i.e., to receive usual care). There were zero deaths from any cause in the study, regardless of which group the patients were in. Zero.

As I typed that, the hair on my arms literally stood up. I’m actually getting a little choked up here, friends. So, join me in reading that paragraph again, because those words should make your heart rejoice. Covid-19 is no longer the massive mortality threat it once was. It’s nowhere near the hospitalization threat either. In the new paper, only 1 in 130 needed to be hospitalized (0.77%). In contrast, in the blockbuster 2022 EPIC-HR study—the one that showed Paxlovid decreased deaths and hospitalizations by 89%—the rate of death or hospitalization in the placebo group was 6.53%, or 1 in 15 people. That’s an 88% reduction in risk. In other words, time (and what comes with it) has reduced the risk of Covid-19 as much as Paxlovid ever did. Time does not need to be prescribed. And it’s free. Or, to quote Maya Angelou: our passages have been paid. We’re simply living in a better world than the one where Paxlovid was life-saving.

Aside: Comparing papers like these can be a little dicey because the study populations are different, to varying degrees. In this case, the average age of PANORAMIC and CanTreatCOVID participants was older (average=55 years) than EPIC-HR (average= 46 years). On the other hand, it’s important to note that a great many extremely high-risk patients had already died of Covid-19 by 2022-2024. That means that in the future, a drug like Paxlovid could, paradoxically, become more useful than it is today.

How did we get here? Largely thanks to immunity—safely gained from vaccines, or far less safely gained from infections. It’s also possible that newer variants cause less severe disease, but that idea remains unconfirmed and its contribution is difficult to quantify.

Does Paxlovid work anymore? (Answer: if it does, the effect is quite small.)

Is this study the end of Paxlovid? Hard to say. It certainly indicates that most of the people historically recommended to receive the drug no longer benefit from it as they once would have. No less than Dr. Anthony Fauci and Clifford Lane wrote an editorial accompanying the research in NEJM to this effect.

Keep in mind that the original trial, EPIC-HR, was conducted in people who had neither been infected nor vaccinated. But since then, virtually every piece of high-quality data on Paxlovid has been disappointing, including these studies. Even Pfizer-sponsored studies have found:

  • Paxlovid does not improve Covid-19 symptoms (EPIC-SR).

  • Paxlovid does not decrease Covid-19 transmission (although as I’ve written before, there’s a glimmer of hope here. In the meantime, we technically have Ensitrelvir, the one FDA-approved treatment that decreases infections after exposure.)

  • Longer courses of Paxlovid don’t help Covid-19 patients with immune compromise clear the virus much faster (though for those with severe immune compromise, there might have been a small benefit).

Findings from some important studies not funded by Pfizer also warrant attention:

  • Paxlovid does not help patients with Long Covid feel better. (Yale study, Stanford study).

  • It’s not known whether Paxlovid taken during the acute phase of a Covid-19 illness decreases downstream Long Covid diagnoses. However, the best study I’ve found on this failed to point toward that benefit. It’s still possible that Paxlovid might prevent Long Covid, though—and the one thing the PANORAMIC trial shows is that Paxlovid recipients did have lower viral loads. This is fascinating in itself. It suggests that the mechanism of the drug remains intact, despite changes to the virus, and despite our immunity. It’s just that a lower viral load after a week or two did not affect hospitalization rates.

There will be bargaining.

Almost immediately after the new studies came out last week, I saw experts online engage in Five Stages of Grief-type bargaining, clinging to the idea that Paxlovid remains important. I can’t say I was surprised. There’s a group of people who are very attached to Paxlovid, including some readers of this newsletter. I suspect this is because the drug was found to be highly effective in reducing hospitalizations and deaths at a time of the pandemic when we desperately needed good news. These days, clinging to Paxlovid is less scientific than nostalgic.

The lingering pro-Paxlovid arguments primarily boil down to four sentiments:

1. “Paxlovid was shown to improve symptoms.” (This is probably wrong.)

It’s true that PANORAMIC participants who were randomly chosen to receive Paxlovid recovered a bit earlier than those who did not receive it. However, there are two problems. First, the study was not blinded, and there was no placebo. That means that patients in the Paxlovid group knew they were getting “the real thing,” and those in the control group knew they were getting nothing (i.e., “usual care”). It’s not uncommon for studies like this to find a benefit for symptoms. Wondering what would have happened if the study had been blinded, and included a placebo? We already know. In EPIC-SR, Pfizer conducted a double-blinded study of precisely that. The result? No difference in symptoms. This is a great example of what to do when two studies have conflicting results: you abide by the better one—in this case the one with blinding and a placebo.

Notwithstanding the placebo effect, the measured difference in symptom relief in PANORAMIC was modest. Even if it’s real, we’d have to give 8 or 9 patients Paxlovid for one person to have a somewhat shorter recovery time.

2. “The trial was underpowered.” (It wasn’t.)

I saw a couple of comments online complaining that the new study was simply not a big enough study to show Paxlovid’s benefit. My friends, this is an insane thing to believe. The new NEJM paper contains two studies, one of which was the PANORAMIC trial. First, that study alone was 50% larger than EPIC-HR (the big 2022 study showing Paxlovid was effective in the pre-immune era.) Second, when the investigators noticed that the rate of hospitalizations was lower than anticipated, they checked to make sure that their study size would still be able to find meaningful differences. So, the authors did their due diligence here on what’s called “power calculations.”

But also, I want to circle back to the very first point I made today. If a study of thousands of patients was too small to show Paxlovid’s benefit, that tells us more than anything! In PANORAMIC, there were more hospitalizations in the Paxlovid group, but the difference was statistical noise. But even ignoring that, given the hospitalization rates in this study population (again, a high-risk group), a study of, say, 30,000-50,000 people could possibly show some modest benefit. The problem there is that the fraction of patients benefiting would be so small, that it would cost many hundreds of thousands of dollars worth of Paxlovid to save a single hospitalization. That’s well above economic thresholds we use when considering what treatments make sense to cover. Back in 2024, Dr. Gideon Meyerowitz-Katz and I published an editorial in the medical journal Clinical Infectious Diseases in which we said something that might’ve been a wee bit unpopular. We basically said that in the year 2024, Paxlovid was probably not worth the cost to health systems for the recommended populations. That seems almost certain, now.

This is exactly the kind of story that gets misunderstood in real time. If you value deeper breakdowns of studies like this, upgrade here to support the work.

The question remains: who should still get Paxlovid? That brings us to the 3rd pro-Paxlovid argument…

Paxlovid and the immunocompromised.

3. “What about Paxlovid for patients with varying degrees of immune compromise?” (This is nuanced.)

Indeed, if there’s one group that still might benefit from Paxlovid, it’s those with immune compromise. But even there, Paxlovid seems to have fizzled. This new paper included hundreds of Covid-19 patients with weakened immune systems (including inherited conditions, acquired conditions, and immune compromise due to treatments like chemotherapy), plus 18 organ transplant recipients who take powerful immunosuppressants. This is our highest-risk cohort, I’d venture. (And remember: zero deaths.) In the PANORAMIC study, 3 out of 296 (~1%) of immunocompromised patients were hospitalized, and there was no statistical difference between the Paxlovid group and the control group.

If you’d told me last year that PANORAMIC would show such a low hospitalization rate in immunocompromised patients with Covid-19, I’d have been dancing on the rooftops.

That said, until we get better data, people with severely compromised immune systems should be vaccinated frequently, and consider using monoclonal antibodies between doses, to minimize their Covid-19 risks. And, yes, they should perhaps still receive Paxlovid. (We’d need a bigger study devoted to immunocompromised patients before we can confidently declare otherwise.) But it behooves us to notice that the data in support of Paxlovid, even for immunocompromised patients, are growing thin (and for them 10-15-day courses may be necessary).

Finally…

4. “What about Long Covid?” (This is aspirational, at best.)

As above, the data Paxlovid for Long Covid prevention are somewhere between non-existent and inadequately weak. Remember, in science, the default assumption is humility—that is, that the “null hypothesis” (i.e., no effect) must be the default. To conclude otherwise would require good science on the topic. We simply don’t have that, at least not yet. (More on that later.)

What’s the harm?

Certainly the downsides of Paxlovid are either minimal or modest—which is good. The main issues are the side effects. But in the absence of significant benefits, those side effects should begin to predominate as your main considerations.

The other problem is Covid-19 rebound due to Paxlovid. Remember that? Rebound is when Covid-19 initially gets better but then worse. While data are mixed, I’m reasonably convinced that Paxlovid causes rebound far more often than Pfizer initially acknowledged (due mostly to how poorly they studied it). It’s also plausible that 10-15-day courses decrease the incidence of Paxlovid-mediated rebound. Pfizer studied this in immunocompromised patients and found that rebound was probably less common in those who took longer courses, though technically the statistics left this uncertain.

The billion-dollar delay.

Pfizer sold billions of dollars worth of Paxlovid in 2025, largely on the supposition that the drug decreases hospitalization or death in high-risk patients. (The CDC continues to recommend the drug for patients with a long list of co-existing conditions.)

But this latest paper is very late to arrive, I must say.

Now, it’s important to say that PANORAMIC and CanTreatCOVID were not funded by Pfizer. So, I don’t think the researchers dragged their feet. Nevertheless, these studies were long overdue. The PANORAMIC group finished collecting its Paxlovid data over two years ago (March 2024). Meanwhile, CanTreatCOVID recruitment wrapped in September 2024. (Aside: I don’t know why these studies were combined into one paper, but it might have slowed things down somewhat.) Regardless, a two-year delay between the end of a massive study like PANORAMIC and a publication in NEJM is noteworthy.

So where did the delays come from? Several places. We know for sure that the paper we are now reading was first submitted to the New England Journal of Medicine sometime in early 2025. (This I determined by looking at the dates of the author disclosure forms, which are typically required after a paper has completed the first round of peer reviews and has entered the revision stage.) The earliest signed forms are dated May, 2025. So, it’s likely that NEJM had this paper in April of 2025 at the very latest, and possibly weeks-to-months before that.

What on Earth took a year after that? Revisions, sure. To some extent, that’s normal. Indeed, we can surmise from other disclosure forms that this paper was sent back for at least once more round of revisions in the summer of 2025 (some of which required new authors to join the studies, thus the later dates on the disclosure forms.)

But then in September of 2025, the manuscript was posted on a University of Cambridge preprint server. At that time, the researchers appeared confident about their NEJM prospects; they listed the Massachusetts Medical Society as the paper’s publisher. (The Massachusetts Medical Society has no important publications other than NEJM). But then the preprint was pulled. That’s unusual. I tried to find the full paper online, but could not. But the abstract remained on the Internet (if you dug around). Here’s a screenshot of that abstract, dated September 2025:

The upshot is that the main outcomes in this abstract are the same as those we read in last week’s NEJM paper.

What happened between September and now? We don’t know. Earlier this year, I’d heard that the study was coming out in April, but I never got an explanation explaining the delay. But, the revision process in top-tier journals rarely takes this long. Once the editors decide they want a paper to be revised for possible publication, they require rapid turnaround. So, this all proceeded rather glacially.

Now, I don’t think that NEJM purposely slow-walked this for overtly nefarious reasons. However, if the study had been positive for Paxlovid and it had been the first study to show positive results (neither of which are true), a nearly identical paper would likely have been published within weeks of submission, instead of taking over a year.

What next?

Now that Paxlovid is no longer the miracle drug it once was, what happens? Will the CDC change its recommendations based on now obsolete data? If it does, many well-meaning, pro-science people won’t believe it. That’s because Secretary Kennedy’s HHS has debased the CDC’s reputation on Covid-19 analysis so dramatically that even if the agency were to revert to its higher pre-Kennedy standards, it’s likely that many medical professionals would incorrectly dismiss the change.

Moreover, I’d normally use this moment to declare that we need better Covid-19 antivirals. To an extent, that’s true. But, as above, the severe event rate (i.e., hospitalizations and deaths) in this paper was so low that it’s difficult to imagine any new antiviral will be able to show such a benefit for the foreseeable future. Why? Because it would take a study so massive, that nobody will be able to do it, just logistically or economically.

So, the next big antiviral against Covid-19 will likely never be shown to prevent deaths or hospitalizations—which, as I opened with, is actually good news. It’s possible, however, that an antiviral could be shown to decrease the duration of illness, which would be nice. It’s also possible, both ethically and plausibly, to study Paxlovid’s efficacy (or that of other antivirals) for decreasing Long Covid, especially given this new study. As far as I know, there is one such study of Paxlovid in process.

The one thing that could change this? A new Covid-19 variant. If we suddenly returned to higher rates of severe outcomes, we could indeed test new antivirals. But at this point, the likelihood of that occurring seems mercifully low. Therefore, it’s likely we won’t get high quality data on severe outcomes for any anti-coronavirus drugs like Paxlovid until something along the lines of SARS-CoV-3 appears.

So, when people ask me when we’ll get something better than Paxlovid, my answer is, perhaps surprising: Hopefully never.
