What is a big box store exactly? Maybe you haven't heard the term, so let's define it now. It's a retailer that has a large store, typically more than 50,000 ft of space. They look like, well, big boxes, hence the name.

Big box stores take up a lot of space, and most of that space is dedicated to parking. It's not uncommon for the parking lot to be two to three times the size of the building itself.

There are lots of downsides to that much asphalt. The parking lot is a large imperous surface. But large parking lots are also a convenience that draws customers, which is really why they exist in the first place. Retailers want you to know that there's no barrier to coming to the store. You will always have a parking space waiting for you. Even better, it's a free parking space.

So then, what can city planners do to reduce the impact of big box stores in their communities?

Let's start at the parking lot. Repealing parking minimums is a great step. A city might go even further and set parking maximums, forcing store developers to consider even less parking than they would find optimal. Smaller parking lots will reduce the urban heat island effect and make destinations closer together, theoretically making places more walkable and bikable.

Of course, that also depends on whether or not the roads have good bicycle and pedestrian infrastructure. Zoning codes can also require big box stores to include additional amenities such as bike parking. They can be required as a number per store area, like one per 2500 square ft, or as a percentage of car parking. Strip malls in my town, for example, are required to include bicycle parking equal to 20% of car spaces.

Another nice amenity is electric vehicle charging. Retailers are installing them in their parking lots without local governments telling them to do so because they are finding that charges attract customers and those customers tend to have higher incomes and spend more. Some companies are leasing out their parking spaces to establish charging companies like EVgo and Charge America, while other retailers are doing it in-house and turning the parking lot into a new profit center. Finally, trees can provide shade in a hot parking lot, improving customer comfort and reducing the urban heat island effect.

Many cities require parking lots to include trees. The challenge of these ordinances is that they only succeed if retailers actually maintain the trees. They need water and enough space with uncompacted soil to allow for root growth. They also require pruning.

Companies that don't do this end up with dead trees and no shade. What if, like some of these parking lot trees, the big box stores themselves die? Like I mentioned in the intro, sometimes entire chains go out of business. This can mean that some high-erforming locations get shut down.

Well, some communities have accountability clauses that make the developer have a plan for the reuse of the building before they're granted a permit to build the big box store in the first place.
