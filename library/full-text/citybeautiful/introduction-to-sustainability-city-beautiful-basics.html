Climate action plans or caps are a set of policies that cities can adopt to advance their sustainability goals through direct action. The cap can specify which climate related issues are most likely to affect the city. For coastal cities, this may be sea level rise while for cities in the south it might be extreme heat. Next, some caps have a carbon inventory where the sources of greenhouse gases within the city are listed and the amounts are calculated.

This gives the plan a baseline amount of carbon emissions and lets the city set a reduction target. Next comes the meat of the plan. the goals, strategies, and implementing actions. A goal may be to cut transportation emissions by 20%, lower emissions for buildings or create more biodiversity.

The strategies will be actions the city can take to actually reach those goals, and the implementing actions are ways the city can do it. Here's a good example. The Houston climate action plan acknowledges that Houston is a car- centric city with 94% of trips made by car. One of their goals is to reduce vehicle miles traveled to 20% cap per capita by 2050.

Their main strategies to achieve this goal are to implement integrated multimmodal transportation systems and build and retrofit complete transit oriented neighborhoods. The implementation actions to carry out these strategies are more specific. For the first goal, they include coordinate regional transit operations and payment systems, prioritize funding mechanisms towards multimmoal transportation, and expand the use of microobility devices. These are like rentable e- scooters.

You can see that next to each implementation action, the cap lists the time frame, whether or not it's a new policy or financial support will be needed and whether the action will be community or city-led. This type of organizational structure, zooming in from the big picture goal of reducing emissions to more specific strategies to even more specific implementing actions is helpful to both the public to understand how their city's becoming more sustainable and for the city to meaningfully change. Breaking down climate change response into bite-size actions helps ensure that things will really get done. The last section of many caps is called monitoring.

Here, the city explains how they'll keep track of the implementation and find out whether or not they're actually working. In Houston's case, city planners will need to measure that every year they hit their VMT reduction targets of 20% by 2050. Monitoring is often where some caps fall short. It's easy to propose changes, but harder to track their progress and change the midcourse.

It can especially be true for smaller cities who may have the may not have the resources to keep track of every single strategy.
