In the Gospel of Mark, Jesus makes a striking promise. He tells his followers that those who believe in him will perform miracles by using my name. They will cast out demons. They will speak in new tongues.

They will pick up snakes. And if they drink any deadly thing, it will not hurt them. They will lay their hands on the sick and they will recover. A few of these miraculous signs have become central ritual practices for Pentecostal and charismatic Christians.

Speaking in tongues and healing and exorcistic rituals are still practiced in Pentecostal and charismatic worship services all over the world. But what about picking up snakes? Well, that one never really caught on, except for a handful of Pentecostal churches in the Appalachian Mountains of the US. They handle live venomous snakes during worship as a demonstration of faith, believing that if the Bible says true believers will pick up snakes, then doing so is a test and testimony, physical proof that the Holy Spirit truly dwells within them.

Today in the US there are only about a hundred small independent Christian churches where handling venomous snakes is a common practice. These independent churches typically identify with two denominations. The church of God and the church of God prophecy which are denominations within the broader umbrella of Pentecostal Christianity and charismatic Christianity. Now these are very large branches of Christianity.

Pentecostal Christianity includes big global denominations such as the Assemblies of God and the church of God in Christ. both of which have millions of members, but neither of them recognize these snake handling churches. Pentecostal leaders have disavowed snake handling for decades, and they do not consider it an acceptable form of worship. It's also illegal in every single state except for West Virginia.

Snake handling churches are spread throughout Appalachia, often located in the mountain towns of West Virginia, Kentucky, and Tennessee. There's also a few in the southeast, as far south as central Florida, Georgia, and Alabama. But in general, snake handling churches are very regional in the Appalachian Mountains. Now, these services are not all about snakes.

They look a lot like any other fundamentalist church you'd find in the US. A typical service involves worship music and a preacher standing up and speaking a sermon based on a scriptural passage. But like many other Pentecostal and charismatic services, they also involve these charismatic gifts of the spirit. According to research conducted by scholars Ralph Hood and W.

Paul Williamson, who spent 15 years conducting ethnographic research in snake handling churches. Sometimes a full sermon may last only a minute or two before members start demonstrating these charismatic practices, possibly speaking in tongues or shaking with a feeling of being filled with the spirit. The preacher might jump back in at any moment, still preaching while people are shouting, dancing, and speaking in tongues. Sometimes snakes don't make an appearance at all, but often during the worship service, suddenly a group of men will retrieve a special wooden box that contains venomous snakes and bring it to the front of the church.

A worshipper then might pick one up and hold it, testifying to the experience that they're having in the moment of God moving on them and protecting them from the danger. In these churches, snake handling is evidence that a congregant has complete faith in God and the Bible as the word of God. It's also proof they've been blessed by the Holy Spirit with the Spirit's presence in their body. This presence which is felt physically by the believer is a sign or a manifestation that the Holy Spirit has inhabited their body and is keeping them safe from a potential snake bite.

Now for decades scholars have debated whether someone practicing charismatic worship like speaking in tongues or handling snakes requires a disassociative trans state. In the 1970s the anthropologist Felicitus Goodman argued that it does require a trance. Later, the linguist William Samarind pushed back hard, arguing that speaking in tongues is a learned social behavior that does not require trans states. Later studies tried to settle the question with experimental data, measuring heart rates, breathing, and brain waves.

But they found no consistent physiological differences between speaking in tongues and ordinary speech. In my video on speaking in tongues, I cite the scholar Nicholas Harkness, who argues that translike glosselia is actually the rarer form of the practice. Hood and Williamson argue for a middle ground position. They argue we're seeing some sort of culturally learned, physiologically real state of focused disassociation, guided by social cues, ritual, and belief.

And worshippers know how to enter, deepen, and exit these states. Hood and Williamson report that snake handling churches actually draw a line between handling by faith, as in simply out of obedience to scripture, and handling by anointing, which is described as a feeling of the Holy Spirit physically moving upon them. And a few studies have tried to measure what's going on physiologically during this anointing state. In the 1990s, the snake handling pastor Lon Pac volunteered for an EEG study while attempting to feel the anointing, and his brain activity changed, moving from calm, meditative patterns to highly focused and aroused patterns, similar to what we see in hypnosis.

Another study in the9s actually analyzed blood samples drawn from snake handling worshippers after the service. And it found huge spikes in adrenaline, endorphins, and cortisol, suggesting that what worshippers called the anointing is accompanied by a full body chemical rush that blunts fear and pain. But this doesn't mean that handlers lose awareness. Even in the midst of the anointing, many stay focused and engaged with the people around them, preaching, testifying, and responding to the congregation.

Hood and Williamson transcribed extemporaneous sermons during snake handling services. One worshipper said, "The Bible says they shall take up serpents." "Honey, it means what it says and says what it means. It won't change for me. It won't change for you.

Amen. Thank God. The word of God is right." Another handler tells those around him that handling the snakes is a test which determines their salvation. These signs shall follow them that believe.

And if you believe it, boys, you're going to do it. And if you don't do it, I believe it with all my heart that you'll die and go to hell. Here the handler is referencing that passage from Mark chapter 16. For him, obedience to scripture is non-negotiable.

And handling snakes is evidence of true belief, proof that one is living out the literal word of God. And many speak with a combination of grandiosity and stark reality about the mortal danger that they're facing when they pick up a snake, saying things like, "There's death in that box." Or, "It's like you to take up death in your arms and just hold it there and not worry about it." And sometimes people are bitten. In one recording, while a handler was being attacked, he said, "Through these serpent bites, he'll take care of us. Don't worry about me.

Don't cry over me when I leave this world. Rejoice. I'm going to be with Jesus." In cases when someone is bitten, the service quickly shifts from ecstatic worship to divine healing. All of the prayers are focused on the bitten member, asking God to take care of the individual and bring them through the pain and venom.

They lay their hands on the attacked person and often use folk healing remedies to clean and bandage the wound. Hood and Williamson write, "In over 15 years of continuing field research and attendance at hundreds of services, we have witnessed no more than 25 snake bites. In each of these cases, those who are bitten always receive special concern from other believers in terms of immediate prayers and support as well as continued personal contact in the days that followed. Now, contrary to what you might assume, those who are bitten are not treated as failures of faith, as if they didn't have enough belief.

It's quite the opposite. Surviving a bite often grants them quiet respect within the community as someone who has proven their willingness to suffer and even die for their faith. The researchers report that news of a serious bite spreads quickly through the close-knit network of serpent handling churches where it becomes both a subject of collective concern and a reaffirmation of the risks. They write, "Serpent handlers are not naive about the dangers inherent in their practice." They understand full well and will be the first to point out that the passage of Mark 16 provides no guarantees that the serpents will not bite, only that believers of Jesus will take them up.

So where did this practice come from? The snake handling tradition grew out of a wave of revivalism that swept through American Christian communities in the early 19th century. It was part of a broader shift toward a more evangelical and emotionally charged style of worship across the eastern US, especially in Appalachia and the South. Methodists and Baptists gathered for huge outdoor revivals.

And at these meetings, worship could get ecstatic and physical. This was the second great awakening in American religious history. And it gave birth to what became known as the holiness movement. Emerging mostly out of Methodism during this revival era.

The holiness movement taught that believers could live free from sin through a profound personal experience of sanctification, a spiritual transformation. They called a second blessing of the Holy Spirit. Once you had this second blessing, you were no longer sinful by nature. Some holiness believers taught that this was followed by a third work of grace, sometimes called spirit baptism.

Many holiness Pentecostals believe that baptism in the Holy Spirit produced visible miraculous signs that proved the spirit's presence. In the early 1900s, a preacher named Charles Fox Parm began teaching that the indisputable proof of spirit baptism was the ability to speak in tongues. and his teachings soon spread through revival networks across the country, preaching this new doctrine of spirit baptism through tongues. At these revivals, people spoke in tongues, fell to the ground, slain in the spirit, danced, shook, and cried out as they felt the power of the Holy Spirit moving through their bodies.

Faith healings and spontaneous conversion became hallmarks of the movement. Early Pentecostals also turned to Mark 16 as a kind of checklist for authentic signs of baptism in the Holy Spirit. And this is where snake handling enters the picture. Catching and handling snakes was already a common folk practice in the Appalachin region at the time, but it likely didn't have any religious significance.

But news reports started popping up in the early 1900s of church congregations handling snakes as part of their worship. Like an article from the Fort Worth Star Telegram in 1913 that mentions a certain reverend dying from a snake bite during a revival. These sporadic cases suggest that by the 1910s, snake handling was emerging organically among radical holiness Pentecostal circles. In the summer of 1914, a fiery illiterate preacher named George Hensley held a revival in Cleveland, Tennessee, the heart of the Church of God movement.

Hensley was not a theologian. He was a former moonshiner turned holiness preacher, fueled by the conviction that the promises of Mark 16 were intended to be taken literally. And on September 12th, 1914, an anonymous note appeared in the Church of God magazine. Brother George Hensley is conducting a revival at the tabernacle in Cleveland, Tennessee.

The power has been falling and souls crying out to God. Twice during the meeting, serpents have been handled by the saints. Newspapers soon picked up the story and hencely became a regional sensation. For many, his revival offered living proof that the full list of signs promised to believers in Mark chapter 16 were being fulfilled in their own time.

Even the overseer of the Church of God denomination, AJ Tomlinson, attended one of these meetings and reported what he saw. On Friday night, it was there. Somebody had brought in a fierce rattlesnake pilot. The power of God was demonstrated successfully, however, and several handled it and no one was injured by it.

Some were bitten, but with no damage to them. The sensation was so great that outsiders got enthused and wanted to test the matter further. In these early days, many accepted snake handling as a miraculous sign. And some churches even started practicing that other sign promised in Mark 16.

If one were to drink poison and be a true believer, they would not be hurt. And that is still sometimes the case as some churches in Appalachia will provide a small container of stricken at the altar, a highly toxic, bitter tasting chemical compound most often used as a pesticide. But by the 1930s, leaders in Pentecostal denominations started calling snake handling and poison drinking into question, arguing it was dangerous and unnecessary. They argued that practicing the other three signs was what was biblically expected.

So why drop the snakes? See, Pentecostalism was growing. Their churches were getting bigger, more organized, and a lot more visible both to other Christians and to the press. And Pentecostals were already caricatured and mocked, having a reputation for what was viewed as a chaotic and outofc control style of worship, which many outsiders saw as undignified and ungodly.

And as more people converted, church leaders became interested in mainstreaming, making their congregations look a bit more respectable to outsiders. That meant toning down some of the more controversial practices, whether it was serpent handling or speaking in tongues. To explain this process, scholars of religion often point to the sociologist of religion, Max Vber, in his idea of the routinization of charisma. Vber argued that when a movement first starts out, it's fueled by intense charismatic excitement and the authority of charismatic leaders.

But as it grows and becomes more organized, that raw energy gets tamed into routines, rules, and institutions. In other words, the more radical edges get smoothed out so the movement can survive and gain legitimacy. But for the denominations who stuck with the practice, the Church of God and the Church of God prophecy, it was critical. It was the only way to read the Bible correctly to ensure that you're committed to the belief that the Bible is 100% the inherent word of God and is literally true.

According to Church of God overseer AJ Tomlinson, the church stands for the whole Bible rightly divided and it is wrongly dividing to cut out the tongues and serpents. Now, snake handling has always been controversial, but it's also been outlawed in most of the US. In 1940, the state of Kentucky became the first to outlaw snake handling for religious purposes. And you might be wondering, well, shouldn't it be protected under the First Amendment, which guarantees freedom of religion in the US?

But the courts have answered with a resounding no. Well, except in West Virginia, where it is still legal. Hood and Williamson, right? The courts have ruled that states may regulate religious behaviors as opposed to beliefs if they have an overriding interest.

Protecting believers from being bitten, maimed, or killed seems a sufficient overriding interest for many states. However, laws against serpent handling have seldom been effective. So, how dangerous is snake handling really? I mean, it's dangerous.

It's really, really dangerous. Do I need to say this? Let's quote the ethnographers. They mention a few churches where you can't help but notice a missing finger or paralyzed limb among the believers who have been maimed in the practice of their faith.

Bites do happen. Remember that these scholars saw about 25 bites over the course of 15 years of study. The data shows that the handlers almost always survive, but people have died. Scholars have compiled at least 89 people who have died handling snakes at these churches.

And this is almost certainly an underestimate considering most of this practice went unrecorded in the early decades of its existence. So why aren't there as many snake handling deaths as you might expect? One reason is how much venom a snake can release. Most snakes used in these services belong to the pitfer family, species native to the region, like timber rattlesnakes, cane brakes, copperheads, and cotton mounds.

An adult copper head is about 2 to 3 ft long and carries around 40 to 70 millig of venom. In a single bite, it injects about a/4 to 3/4 of that. And for a human adult, the lethal dose is closer to 100 mg. So a single snake bite is generally not strong enough to actually kill most adults.

Also, according to herpatologists at the Kentucky Reptile Zoo, another reason is that the snakes used in these services are often kept in poor conditions. Stressed and undernourished snakes are less likely to bite, and even when they do, they often can't inject much venom. Though today, calling 911 is more accepted than it used to be. Some scholars have reported that younger pastors increasingly view that avoiding medical attention is outdated.

The deaths of several prominent handlers have spurred reflection, and many now argue that the Bible technically commands serpent handling and not necessarily rejecting emergency aid. Snake handling churches in the US operate quietly, according to their own rules and often against the law. And even though only a dozen congregations still practice it, the tradition has become disproportionately visible in the public imagination. Usually after tragedy, when a pastor or worshipper dies from a bite, news cameras arrive and the story circulates once again.

In 2013, National Geographic aired Snake Salvation, a 16-part series following Kentucky pastor Jaime Coots and his congregation. A year later, Coots died from a snake bite for which he refused medical attention. It's something they live out through their bodies, through bodily habits, sensations, gestures, and movements that make faith feel tangible and meaningful in everyday life. In snake handling churches, this embodiment is most vividly experienced in what I mentioned earlier, the anointing, a moment when the Holy Spirit is said to move on a person. Snake handling believers describe it in very physical terms about feeling the Holy Spirit's presence take hold of the body. Handlers describe warmth spreading through their limbs, trembling hands, or an electric rush from the head to the toe.
