And this trope is so common that anthropologists actually have a name for it, the relic theory. Relic theory treats sacred groves as survivals, living remnants of prehistoric forests.

And this is sometimes woven into an environmentalist ideology that imagines an earlier stage of human history when humans and nature were in perfect balance. And this way of thinking has deep roots. One of its most influential architects was the Scottish anthropologist James Frasier, whose massive and wildly speculative book, The Golden Bow, shaped how generations of scholars and storytellers imagined what he called primitive religion. Frraasier stitched together classical texts, colonial era ethnography, and secondhand travel accounts to sketch a grand evolutionary history of religion, and sacred grove sat at the center of that story.

Frraasier opens the golden bow with the sacred grove of Diana at Nemi in Italy where according to ancient Roman sources a priest king guarded the forest and ruled only until he himself was killed by a challenger. For Frraasier, the grove was a remnant from an ancient world where ritual violence, kingship, fertility, and nature were all bound up together into a single moral system. Frraasier helped cement sacred groves as icons of an integrated social, political, and ecological order. A pre-social world before bureaucracy, before capitalism, and before industrial extraction fractured those relationships.

And that idea turned out to have real staying power. Which brings us to our first case study, sacred groves in Tanzania. Across much of Africa, communities preserve forest patches as ritual sites tied to ancestry and kinship.

These groves are especially common in the long transition zone between forest and savannah stretching from West Africa to southern Africa. In the 1800s and early 1900s, European ecologists assumed that these groves were relics, scraps of vast ancient forests that had been slowly destroyed by human settlement. But that assumption actually turned out to be backwards. Research from the 20th century onward showed that many African sacred groves were actually made by people.

Communities planted and protected them as they settled in new regions. In some cases, groves even grew up over former villages despite local traditions describing them as older than human settlement. In other regions, sites only became sacred much later, sometimes in response to colonial rule. Although often well-meaning and motivated by real concerns for environmental protection, anthropologists like Michael Sheridan have argued that treating these groves as relics makes African societies look static or frozen in time.

But in reality, sacred groves grew alongside living communities shaped by migration, politics, and ritual. And you can see this clearly in Sheridan's research in the North Par mountains in Tanzania, where hundreds of groves are scattered across a heavily farmed landscape. There are two main kinds of sacred groves here, anduongi are small groves, often an acre or less, tied to a single lineage. Historically, families displayed the skulls of their ancestors in niches around these groves, and you could visit these groves to speak with them.

Because these groves are tied to lineage and ancestors, the trees themselves mark how long a family has occupied that land. In a very literal sense, roots ancestry in a particular place. This is how long my family has been here. Just look at this grove.

Mhu, by contrast, are much larger and far more powerful. These forests are linked not with one family, but with the ancestors of a ruling clan. Young men are initiated there, and access is tightly controlled, and violations are considered extremely dangerous. According to Sheridan's interviews, performing a curse ritual inside an Numu is sometimes described as tantamount to attempted murder.

Local tradition often describes these groves as remnants of ancient forests, but historical records and oral history suggest they're much more recent. From around the 13th century onward, clans established ritual sites, sometimes under a single tree to legitimize their ancestry and political authority. Prohibitions against cutting or disturbing them allowed the vegetation to grow unchecked. And over generations, as families expanded and new ritual sites were formed, these places gradually thickened into patches of dense forest.

The result was a landscape of scattered groves, the product of long-term ritual protection and land use patterns. Though, because they're protected, they often preserve rare indigenous species and high biodiversity. And because these groves are bound up with land rights and ritual authority, outside intervention has real political consequences. That became clear in the 1990s when the Tanzanian government launched the Tanzania Forestry Action Plan, which gave elders the legal authority to find trespassers.

And it actually worked. It strengthened local control and reduced illegal logging. But the program also tried to improve conservation by introducing new indigenous tree species into the groves. And that's where things fell apart.

From a conservationist perspective, planting more trees sounds great. But think of it through the eyes of the grove caretakers. Planting trees was political. Remember, these groves are bound up with your ancestral right to that land.
