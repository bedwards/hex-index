On Good Friday in 1962, the scholar of religion Houston Smith said he encountered God for the first time. Smith joined an experiment held in the basement of Boston University's Marsh Chapel along with a group of fellow seminarians and religious studies scholars. He ingested some psilocybin, the psychedelic compound found in so-called magic mushrooms. The group then listened to the Good Friday service going on in the church upstairs, and many had a profound religious experience.

Smith said he had already believed in God for as long as I can remember. But until the Good Friday experiment, I had no direct personal encounter with God. But his experience on psilocybin was the most powerful cosmic homecoming I have ever experienced. Houston Smith's experience was not unique.

For thousands of years, humans have been taking psychedelic substances to induce so-called mystical experiences. Many people report feeling a sense of sacredness, transcendence, and awe. Others describe traveling to other domains of experience or encountering supernatural beings like gods or spirits. Phenomena classically considered to be religious or mystical.

When psychedelics are used in religious and spiritual contexts, they're often referred to as enthogens. The term enthogen derives from Greek, literally meaning something like to become divine within or generating God within. More simply, it could just mean inspired or full of God. It was coined in the 1970s as a way to avoid the negative associations with terms like hallucinogen or psychedelic.

In other words, enthogens are psychedelics by another name, one that emphasizes their religious properties. The Marsh Chapel experiment was a product of its time. It was designed as part of a larger initiative at Harvard University as research on psychedelics was ramping up in the 1960s. This ended with the Controlled Substances Act of 1970, which placed heavy restrictions on psychedelics and made them more difficult to study.

However, in the past couple of decades, psychedelic research has been making a comeback, shifting the conversation from the counterculture of the 1960s to mainstream discussions about mental health, therapy, and especially religion. This research gives us a better understanding of how psychedelics are believed to be a way to access spiritual or supernatural realms, agents, or experiences. Humans all over the world have been using psychedelics for rituals for thousands of years. Initiates of the ancient Greek mystery religion called the Elusinian mysteries ritually ingested a drink called kickion, which may have had psychedelic effects with some archaeologists arguing it was partly composed of a fungi containing LSD like alkyoids.

Archaeological evidence for the use of a cactus containing measculine called peyote dates back 5,000 years in Meso America and using psychedelic mushrooms in religious ceremonies was also widespread. In 2008, archaeologists in Bolivia discovered a 1,000-year-old ritual bundle that contained traces of psilocybin and DMT. The bundle is believed to have belonged to a ritual specialist of the pre-Inca Tijuanaku civilization. Psychedelics are still used in religious rituals today.

One example is the use of peyote in the Native American church or the NAC, which was founded in the late 19th century. Members of the NAC ingest peyote as a sacrament, and they view it as both a powerful herbal medicine and an omnisient spiritual entity. The first three are all naturally occurring substances. Measculine is found in several species of cacti in the Americas. Psilocybin is found in more than 200 species of fungi and DMT is found in a variety of plants and animals. LSD, on the other hand, is not a naturally occurring substance.

Psilocybin and DMT, on the other hand, need to be metabolized in particular ways. Soon after psilocybin is ingested, the body metabolizes it, stripping it of a phosphate molecule and turning it into psilicin.

So technically psilocybin itself is not psychoactive until it's converted into psilicin. For DMT, when ingested on its own, it's quickly broken down by enzymes in the stomach, leading to a very short-lived psychedelic experience. However, most people who ingest DMT do so by drinking the aforementioned iawasa. Iaska is made from the bark of the iawaska vine and the leaves of the chakruna shrub.

The leaves contain DMT which as I said are quickly broken down by the stomach. But the Iwasa vine contains what are called MAOIs or monoamine oxidase inhibitors which inhibit the enzymes that break down DMT. In other words, it takes much longer for the body to metabolize the psychedelic compound when it's ingested along with MAOIs. The combination of the plants in the Iwasa brew thus allow the effects of DMT to be longerlasting.

All four of these induce heightened mental activity, including visual and auditory hallucinations. Bill Richards, a psychologist who engaged in psychedelic studies as both a researcher and a participant, describes his experience like this. To my utter amazement, I soon discovered in my visual field the emergence of an exquisitely beautiful, multi-dimensional network of intricate neon-like geometric patterns. My awareness was flooded with love, beauty, and peace beyond anything I ever had known or wildly imagined to be possible.

Awe, glory, and gratitude were the only words that remained relevant. Scientists describe experiences like this as the result of brain activity. The common psychedelic molecules that we've been talking about so far are all serotonic, which means that they operate like the neurotransmitter serotonin in the brain. They bind to a specific subtype of serotonin receptor called the 5HT2A receptor.

Activating this receptor causes the subjective experiences associated with psychedelics, including hallucinations and reports of feelings of bliss. But for people on a psychedelic trip, they're not thinking about the 5HT2A receptor. Notice the terms that Bill Richards used. Awe, glory, religiousy sounding terms, right?

And scientists often do label these experiences as mystical type experiences. Now, personally, I think mystical is a weird term for scientists to use. It's usually associated with religious experiences like encountering supernatural beings. And I did notice in my research that a lot of these scientists were very quick to say that they're not making any claim about the existence or non-existence of the supernatural whenever they call a psychedelic experience mystical.

And it's because it's such a loaded term that many scholars of religion and psychedelics have argued it's not a useful term at all to describe these experiences. Different cultures and religious traditions interpret mystical experiences in various ways. So, it's difficult to create an objective standard for what qualifies as mystical in the first place. In recent years, scholars have argued in favor of generic academic terminology that can be applied across cultures like psychedelicinduced non-ordinary states of consciousness, which I know does not roll off the tongue like the word mystical.

But it is fair to ask, why have scientists been so quick to label these non-ordinary states of consciousness as mystical in the first place? And it's because these experiences often closely resemble those described in some religious texts and rituals. feelings of transcendence and unity as well as visions of supernatural beings. So, let's start with the emotional component.

A profound feeling of connectedness. A profound feeling of connection or unity is one of the most commonly reported feelings by people on a psychedelic trip. Consider a study conducted on a group of patients with clinical depression who were interviewed 6 months after a high dose of psilocybin. The researchers found that connection was one of the strongest themes reported by the participants and many of them described this feeling in religious terms.

Even patients who had previously considered themselves not to be spiritual or religious. One patient described feeling the presence of God. I've always thought that he was a man because of the way I was raised reading the Bible, but it felt like a female energy. Other patients saw Hindu gods and at one point during the peak, one patient felt themselves becoming the god Shiva.

Others felt a connection to a nondescript supernatural force or a feeling of merging with the universe. One patient wrote, "I was everybody, unity, one life with six billion faces. I was the one asking for love and giving love. I was swimming in the sea and the sea was me." Another said, "Like Google Earth, I had zoomed out.

I was absolutely connected to myself, to every living thing, to the universe." In another study, the researcher Leo Roseman and his colleagues studied the presence of mystical type experiences in a study on using psilocybin for treatment resistant depression. To measure mystical experience, the team used an altered states of consciousness questionnaire. The Roseman team used the questionnaire to try to measure, among other things, the presence of a feeling of oceanic boundlessness. This term originates in a conversation between Sigman Freud and the French intellectual and mystic Roma Rolland.

Now, Freud considered religion to be an illusion, but Roland argued that Freud failed to appreciate the true source of religious sentiments, a subjective feeling of something limitless, unbounded, as if it were oceanic. This questionnaire lists different types of subdimensions of oceanic boundlessness, a state of blissfulness, insightfulness, and disembodiment. The study ultimately found that in 20 patients with treatment resistant depression, a psilocybin induced experience of oceanic boundlessness predicted positive clinical outcomes. Some scholars described this as the unitive experience of psychedelics and it's closely related to another commonly reported religiousy sounding experience, a sense of awe.

Now the concept of awe has fascinated philosophers and religious study scholars for years. Back in the 1800s, thinkers like Friedrich Schlimmacher and William James described it as a deep emotional response that people feel when they encounter something that is greater than themselves and beyond rational comprehension. Many argued that awe was the defining feature of religious experience. The psychologist Peter Hendris defines awe as an emotion in the upper reaches of pleasure and on the border of fear that emerges from two feelings, a sense of vastness and accommodation.

Accommodation refers to the need to adjust mental structures so as to integrate a new experience, sometimes involving fear or disorientation. I think this is partially why visionary texts like the book of Revelation are frequently attributed to psychedelic trips because these texts are describing a sense of awe, a mix of amazement and fear, a sense of vastness as the small prophet encounters the huggeeness of God. Accommodation is at play, too, as the prophet attempts to adjust and integrate the new experience that they're witnessing. That's not to say these visions are reporting on an actual psychedelic experience.

As we'll explore in a future video, they probably are not. But the similar themes may explain why scientists have been so quick to label these psychedelic experiences as mystical in their research. These experiences of awe and unity are said to cause people to question their own world view. Which leads us to the next religious dimension of psychedelics, their ability to alter a person's belief in the supernatural.

A study conducted by a team of researchers led by Christopher Timberman investigated how psychedelic drugs affect people's metaphysical beliefs, specifically regarding reality, consciousness, and free will. The study recruited 866 participants who were planning to attend a ceremony involving some sort of psychedelic substance. People were asked to complete a survey that measured their metaphysical beliefs at different points. One week before the experience to establish a baseline, and then one day, 4 weeks, and 6 months after.

The survey assessed levels of belief in strict philosophical materialism, the idea that everything is purely physical, as well as belief in dualism, the mind and body are separate, fatalism, the belief that events are predetermined, and pansychism, the idea that mind or consciousness is a fundamental aspect of all things. So, for example, the survey asked questions like, "Do you think the physical world is an illusion generated by the mind?" Or another question, "Do you believe reality is entirely constituted by physical objects and processes?" After everyone took their trip, the researchers found that psychedelic experiences tended to shift the participants belief away from strict materialism. They showed an increased belief in the existence of non-physical supernatural realms and a unifying principle beyond scientific explanation. They also found that these changes in belief lasted up to 6 months after the psychedelic trip and the effect was really strong for first timers.

This suggests that firsttime psychedelic experiences can be particularly life-changing when it comes to beliefs about the nature of reality. The researchers offered a few reasons for why psychedelics may induce changes in metaphysical beliefs. Though one interesting theory they mentioned is called the reebus model, standing for relaxed beliefs under psychedelics. According to this model, psychedelics make our deeply held beliefs and assumptions more flexible or plastic.

Essentially, they reduce the mental rigidity of these beliefs, making them easier to change. Our certainty goes down, allowing for our minds to become more open to new perspectives and ways of thinking. In the case of the study, the changes in belief were not random, but tended to move in a particular direction. Specifically, people shifted away from strict materialistic views and became more inclined toward ideas that acknowledge non-physical aspects of reality.

Interestingly, while people became less certain about materialistic views, others who already held pans-sychic or dualistic beliefs became more confident in their beliefs. So, while psychedelics may make some beliefs more flexible, the changes do seem to push in one direction toward more supernaturalistic perspectives. Yet, perhaps the most striking way that these experiences take on a mystical dimension is through what they call entity encounters. when people report meeting beings that they describe as gods, spirits, or other supernatural or otherworldly agents.

For example, one study from 2020 surveyed over 200 people who reported encountering some sort of entity after taking DMT. Interestingly, the most common labels that people use to describe these entities are not really specific gods, but more generic labels like being, guide, spirit, alien, or helper. A smaller number of people chose words like angel or elf or some sort of religious figure. A handful described their encounters using words like gnome, monster, or dead person.

The researchers found that the experiences were rated as among the most meaningful, spiritual, and psychologically insightful lifetime experiences with persisting positive changes in life satisfaction, purpose, and meaning attributed to the experiences. Another study of over 4,000 people compared two types of encounters. People who claim to have encountered God without the influence of drugs and people who say they did with the help of psychedelics. Both groups reported similar sensory phenomena, hearing voices, seeing visions, or just a feeling of some deep profound emotion.

The differences were striking, though. For example, the researchers found that people in the non-drug group were much more likely to say they encountered a personality, God, or some sort of emissary of God, like an angel. 41% of the non-drug group say they encountered God, versus only 18% in the psychedelic group. Those in the psychedelic group were much more likely to describe their encounter in less personal words like encountering an ultimate reality.

Now, science is still scratching the surface when it comes to understanding antheogens and religion, but it's already providing insights into many of the states of consciousness that are classically associated with religion, a feeling of awe, beliefs in the supernatural, and encounters with other worldly beings. A growing number of researchers have also begun to reflect on the ethical questions raised by scientific work on these substances, especially questions of cultural ownership and knowledge production. Take psilocybin as it's become the focus of a multi-billion dollar pharmaceutical and wellness industry. It has also generated hundreds of patents.

Critics have argued that this commercialization often bypasses indigenous communities whose long-standing ritual knowledge helped bring these substances to the attention of Western researchers, but who rarely share in the economic or legal benefits. More broadly, some scholars argue that western science tends to narrow its focus to clinical outcomes, treating psychedelics primarily as therapeutic or medical tools while sidelining the cultural, ritual, and spiritual frameworks in which these substances have traditionally been understood. And this gets us back to what makes anthogens so compelling in the first place. They sit at an unusual crossroads between the material and the subjective, science and religion.

On one hand, psychedelics are physical substances, molecules that interact with serotonin receptors and alter brain activity in ways that we can measure. On the other hand, the experiences they produce are described in profoundly mystical terms. Unity, awe, spiritual transformation, and encounters with what many people experience as divine entities. It's this tension between chemistry and mysticism that makes enthogens such a rich object of study and why they continue to challenge how we think about religion, experience, and the boundaries of science itself.
