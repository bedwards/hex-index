Rapture talk. If you scroll through Tik Tok in late September of 2025, you might have stumbled upon some people convinced that September 23, 2025 would be the day of the rapture. A largely evangelical belief that Jesus will return and sweep true believers up into heaven prior to the actual end of the world. Under the rapture hashtag, people were posting everything from serious prep videos to ironic tips for how to look cool while being raptured.

The date itself came from a South African man named Joshua Ma who said Jesus appeared to him in a dream back in 2018 and told him he'd return on September 23rd and 24th, 2025. Oddly enough, Jesus supposedly also mentioned that there would be no World Cup in 2026, which I guess could be proof that Jesus really doesn't care all that much about soccer. From there, the idea exploded on Tik Tok. Some people seemed genuinely convinced, others were in it for the memes.

But since you're watching this after the big day, I think it's safe to assume that the apocalypse got delayed once again. Or maybe we're just all left behind. But I've got a prediction of my own. Even though his prophecy did not come true, he probably won't lose many followers.

In fact, I would bet money on that prediction. Why do I think that? It's very reasonable to expect a religious movement to fade away when a prophecy fails. You'd think witnessing your prophet being wrong would be the ultimate letdown and also an embarrassing letdown.

Outsiders could mock you for believing in a prediction that did not come true and it would be easier to just quietly slip away and disassociate from a group that suddenly looks naive and irrational. From the outside, that seems to be the obvious outcome. Failed prophecy equals failed movement. But one of the most counterintuitive findings in the sociology of religion is that most groups not only survived prophecy failure but often come out stronger.

Consider the Baptist preacher William Miller who in the 1840s predicted that Christ would return in 1843 and then again in 1844 when the predicted day came and went with no second coming. The event became known as the great disappointment. But rather than collapsing entirely, many of his followers regrouped and reinterpreted the prophecy. Some eventually formed the 7th Day Adventist Church, which now has millions of members around the world.

We see similar patterns with the Jehovah's Witnesses, who predicted several specific dates for the end of the world, including in 1914, 1925, and 1975, none of which came true. And yet, the group persists by reinterpreting those dates and adjusting their theology. In Japan, the Omshin Rinko's movement's prophecies of a global apocalypse never materialized. But Splinter Group survived even after the main group's violent collapse.

And that's why I'm pretty confident that the South African preacher will continue with his following. Because the central finding of decades of sociological research is that when prophecies fail, religious groups adapt. Sounds simple, but they adapt by employing specific predictable strategies. The sociologist of religion, Lauren Dawson, one of the leading scholars of prophecy failure, has identified several adaptational strategies that maximize a group's chance of surviving a prophetic failure.

So, take notes if you're planning on becoming a failed prophet. Among the most important strategies is rationalization. As the sociologist Brian Wilson wrote, "For people whose lives have become dominated by one powerful expectation and whose activities are dictated by what that belief requires, abandonment of faith because of disappointment about a date would usually be too traumatic an experience to contemplate. Reinterpretation is demanded." In a study published in the late 90s, Dawson noticed that when a prophecy fails, the groups that outlive the failure deploy several types of rationalization.

First, spiritualization. Groups spiritualize the prophecy. While they were expecting an empirically verifiable event in the here and now, they say the event actually did happen, but in a spiritual sense, maybe in our hearts and minds. One of the most explicit examples of a group rationalizing a prophetic failure by spiritually reinterpreting the outcome is Ichien Nomia, a Japanese new religious movement that draws ideas from Shinto and Buddhism that arose after World War II.

On June 14th, 1974, the leader prophesied that an apocalyptic earthquake would strike Japan 4 days later. On June 18th, at 8:00 a.m. sharp, a very precise prediction. But when the earthquake never materialized, the leader of the movement claimed that God had transferred the cataclysm to my own body.

In other words, he pulled a Jesus Christ maneuver and took on all the pain and burden of humanity onto himself to avert the disaster. Thus, the apocalypse was spiritually reinterpreted and the movement continues to this day. Another form of rationalization is what sociologists call the test of faith. In this framing, the failed prophecy isn't treated as a mistake at all, but a divinely intended trial to separate the weak from the true believers.

The logic goes that only those who remain steadfast in the face of disappointment are worthy to carry on. This interpretation can take a lot of forms depending on the group's theology, history, and leadership. One striking case is the Pentecostal Christian movement called the Church of the True Word. After spending 42 days huddled in a bomb shelter for an apocalypse that never arrived, the members emerged celebrating.

Their leader reframed the anti-limax as proof that they had passed the test. Their willingness to persevere and endure had pleased God, and their faithfulness stood as an example to the world. The third form is human error. The group announces that they simply interpreted the signs of the prophecy incorrectly.

This might lead to a new date being set. For example, the late evangelical radio broadcaster Harold Camping predicted that the rapture would occur on May 21st, 2011. But when that date came and went without incident, he recalculated it to October 11th instead, which he also got wrong. As it turns out though, he kept a loyal following to his death despite his multiple failed prophecies.

And finally, the fourth form of rationalization is blaming others. The leader blames the followers or sometimes blames outsiders for somehow foiling the prophecy. This happened among Jehovah's Witnesses when a prophecy that Jesus Christ's millennial reign would start in 1975 failed to materialize. In a speech in the following year to a group of Canadian Jehovah's Witnesses, the Watchtower Society's president blamed the followers, pointing to the audience and saying, "Do you know why nothing happened in 1975?

It was because you expected something to happen." But clever rationalizations alone do not guarantee survival. A group also needs the right social conditions to sustain them. Lauren Dawson points to four sociological conditions that consistently shape whether a movement weathers the storm or splinters apart. First is what he calls the prophetic millu, which is academic speak for the cultural and religious environment that primes a community to expect, tolerate, and reinterpret prophecy.

For example, Dawson has found that the scope and sophistication of a group's ideological system can affect a group's response to prophecy failure. If a very specific prophecy is anchored in a much broader and much more sophisticated worldview with a communal mission and set of beliefs, a single failed prophecy is very unlikely to derail the whole system. Part of the reason why I think Michaela will not lose his following is because his ideas about the rapture are rooted in dispensationalism. The theological system that undergurs so much of modern evangelical ideas about the end times.

Dispensationalism divides human history into distinct eras or dispensations in which God relates to humanity in different ways. It weaves together biblical prophecy, Israel's role in God's plan, the rapture, the tribulation, and Christ's millennial reign into a sweeping narrative of cosmic history. The ideology lends itself to trying to read modern political events through its lens, giving new fuel and energy to it all the time. Because it's such a broad, complex, and flexible framework, a single failed prophecy doesn't necessarily topple the whole system.

Another factor shaping how groups respond to failed prophecy is the nature and extent of the activities that members put into preparing for the prophecy. As Dawson writes, "The more extreme the investments of time, energy, and resources asked of followers, the greater the likelihood of severe disappointment in the face of failure." Now, in some cases, intense preparation can bind people together in anticipation, but it can also backfire if the investment leaves people depleted or unable to recover after the disappointment. For example, let's consider the case study of the church universal and triumphant, which is a new age new religious movement. Under the leadership of Elizabeth Cla Prophet, members prepared extensively for a predicted nuclear war with the Soviet Union in March 1990.

They even constructed an elaborate multi-million dollar bomb shelter in Montana. On March 14th, hundreds of members gathered underground with food, fuel, and supplies, bracing for the worldending catastrophe. When nothing happened, the group was left reeling and largely disintegrated. Followers had sold their homes to move into the bunkers.

But when nothing happened, those extreme sacrifices quickly became liabilities. One way groups soften that blow is through what Dawson calls ritual framing. Rituals, whether staged before or after a prophecy day, provide a powerful structure for transforming disappointment into meaning. Anthropologists note that rituals do a lot for communities.

They can create altered states of consciousness, intensify group identity and solidarity, and channel a group's emotions. A striking case comes from the Unarians, a UFO-based religion that was found in the 1950s. In the late 1970s and early 1980s, Yunarian leaders predicted that a fleet of spaceships carrying advanced space brothers would land on Earth and usher in a new age of enlightenment. When the predicted landings never materialized, rather than abandoned their expectations, the group ritualized the failure by transforming it into a repeatable psycho drama that they called the Isis Osiris cycle.

A psycho drama is a kind of structured roleplay where people act out imagined or remembered events as if they were happening in real time. In the Unarians case, members reenacted cosmic dramas of betrayal, destruction, and redemption, casting themselves as actors in internal story that stretched across lifetimes and planets. Even for those who only watched, participation was said to have a healing effect, confirming the deeper truth of the prophecy. In this way, the Unarians didn't simply explain away the failure.

They enacted it. They ritualized it. The missed UFO landing became a sacred drama to be relived, reinterpreted, and embodied through ritual. Which leads us to Dawson's next factor, a decisive response from leadership.

What often proves critical to survival is how quickly and convincingly leaders step in once a prophecy fails. The group's leaders must not waver. They need to provide a clear explanation and chart a path forward. This happened following that failed prophecy in 1975 for Jehovah's Witnesses when leadership reinterpreted the outcome, reassured the faithful, and kept the movement on course.

When the church of the true word emerged from that bunker, the leader almost immediately held a prayer service telling her followers it was a test of faith. Did you have victory? The Lord has brought the people closer to him. There is not division.

There's a fellowship here and we are the holiness people. But other groups show what happens when that kind of leadership falters. Which brings us back to the church universal and triumphant that new age religion we mentioned earlier. Sociologists Lauren Dawson and Bradley Wel argue that the collapse of CUT was not inevitable.

Yes, it was shaped by how much members had sacrificed. Remember, many of them sold all their stuff to go into those bunkers. But it was also shaped by how poorly their leaders responded. Elizabeth Clare Prophet did not offer a swift and persuasive rationalization.

She hesitated and retreated into her inner circle and provided only vague reinterpretations. Without a clear narrative to anchor them, members grew disillusioned and most abandoned the movement. The lesson is clear. Decisive leadership can help a group survive prophecy failure in that critical window of disappointment.

Leaders who can swiftly redirect their followers with a plausible explanation can preserve and even strengthen their movement. Leaders who hesitate or fail to communicate effectively can preside over rapid collapse. Even so, the church universal and triumphant never fully disappeared. It lost the bulk of its membership, but it does continue to this day in a much smaller form, though with less apocalypticism and a return to new age spirituality.

The last and maybe the most decisive factor is in-group social support. A lone believer rarely has the resilience to weather a failed prophecy. The ridicule and doubt are simply too overwhelming. But in a tightlyknit community, the collective bonds act as a kind of buffer, making even the most far-fetched rationalizations feel plausible.

Dawson stresses that it's not about sheer raw numbers, as in the number of people who happen to belong to the group is really not a determining factor for whether or not it will survive prophecy failure. What matters more is the density of connections within the group. How often members interact, how many overlapping social ties they share, and how much of their daily life is bound up with the community. A large but tightlyk knit community can weather disappointment more effectively than a small but loosely organized membership.

So overall, rationalizations provide the script. They offer ready-made explanations like it was a test of faith or it was fulfilled spiritually. But those explanations only take hold if there's a stage for them to be performed. And that stage is the social conditions of the group itself.

Without strong in-group bonds, decisive leadership, or ritual framing, members drift away because there's no supportive network to reinforce the rationalization. In that context, even the sting of a failed prophecy can be transformed into a moment of renewed solidarity. Okay, let's take all of these pieces, rationalization, prophetic millia, leadership, social support, and see how they play out in a real world case study. One of the most fascinating examples and one that Dawson himself analyzes is the story of Lubavich hidetic Judaism and some members messianic hopes for their rabbi manelen.

Habad Lubavichidism is a branch of heredi Judaism, sometimes called ultraorththodox Judaism. The movement was founded in the late 18th century and it traces a line of seven rebbases or spiritual leaders. The seventh and most recent reb was Manakahim Mendel Schneerson who led the movement from 1950 until his death in 1994. In his Judaism, the figure of the Reb stands at the very center of religious life.

The Reba is not simply a teacher or scholar, but is believed to be at Sadik, a supremely righteous individual whose spiritual authority extends into every corner of a follower's life. For the Lubava Chasadedim, Schneersonen embodied this role completely. He was a scholar, miracle worker, and trusted adviser. Dawson interviewed one man who said, "Life without the Reba is unthinkable.

Everything on this earth is dependent on him. Every major decision on issues of health, business, education, and marriage was made by writing to him or later by faxing or emailing him. Every week, thousands would gather at his home at 770 Eastern Parkway in Brooklyn, often simply called 770. By the late 1980s, many Lubava Chased began to believe he was not just a holy man, but the long awaited Messiah, Moshiach.

Now, he himself never explicitly claimed the title. But his teachings, his mystical interpretations of current events and the messianic songs chanted at his gatherings created an atmosphere of expectation among many of his followers that he was indeed the Moshiach. But when Schneerson suffered a series of strokes and then died in 1994, it looked like a prophecy failure on a massive scale. If he was the Moshiach, how could he die?

For outsiders, the obvious conclusion was that the messianic hopes would collapse. And yet, that's not what happened. What did happen was a fracture within Lubavich itself. Some followers, often called the messianists, insisted that Schneersen was still the Messiah.

Many others, called anti- messianists, rejected these interpretations. While exact numbers are difficult to pin down, by all accounts, the anti- messianist faction is the majority within Kabal Lubavichidism today. The mainstream Kabad leadership who runs the formal institutions actively discourage messianic declarations but messianist factions still exist particularly in Brooklyn where you can see signs proclaiming Schneersonen as the Moshiach. So how does this messianist faction continue to this day?

Well Dawson and his co-author Simon Dayne argue it's because we see all of the psychological and sociological factors that we've identified. First, there's a strong prophetic millure. Generations of Lubavich's teaching had primed members to see their leader as a sadic, a supremely righteous person. This enabled some Messianist followers to rationalize his death in various ways.

Some said he didn't really die. Others said he would be resurrected and some insisted that the Reb is Moshiach and he is indeed dead, but his departure from the physical world only deepened his presence among the living. Still others said he was in some form of occultation, like some sort of coma. One man Dawson interviewed put it this way.

For me, the question as to whether or not the Rebet underwent a physical death is not so important. What is important is that we continue to follow his teachings about the Messiah and redemption. I personally believe that he is sleeping like he is in a coma. When someone is in a coma, they are still alive.

For sadikim, the term death does not mean that they lose their physical body. The rebbit was buried. He is in his grave, but his body is intact. This is not unusual for Sadi.

Here we see the rationalization that Dawson calls spiritualization. attempts to reinterpret his death as something else, something greater. A fringe group within this Messianist movement even suggested that he might be God. In a 1996 issue of a messianist Lubavich magazine, an author wrote, "The Rebba is in essence the being of God enclosed in a body.

He is by nature omnisient and omnipotent, and all material and spiritual blessings flow from the Reb. The Reba is in fact the boss over nature. He delivers a symphony of countless harmonized details of particular divine providence and has an effect past, present and future. So who is our God?

The Reb Moshiach. That's who. This of course is a scandalous idea to most Jews, even to many Messianist Lubavish Jews. Regardless, it is one example of spiritualizing a failed prophecy.

Next, the preparations and leadership response set the Messianist movement up for success. Leading up to the death of Schneersonen, there were not financial sacrifices like selling your homes to move into a bunker. Instead, they were taking part in massive outreach programs. Throughout the 1980s, Schneersen launched a big we want the Moshiach campaign.

This involved placing advertisements in Jewish newspapers, sending emissaries around the world, and urging Lubavich Jews to make the coming of the Messiah the centerpiece of their lives, encouraging followers to see their everyday religious duties as concrete steps toward hastening redemption. In effect, the campaign created a worldwide movement of highly motivated members whose activism gave them both a sense of purpose and a shared mission. So when the Reba died in 1994, the Messianist movement did not collapse. They already had a framework of outreach and daily practice to channel their disappointment into renewed commitment.

The leadership was also decisive. Though Schneerson left no successor, his inner circle quickly reframed his death. Within days, mass gatherings in Brooklyn taught followers to honor the Reba, not by grieving passively, but by redoubling outreach efforts. Dawson writes, "A week after Schneerson's death, for example, a day-long teaching was held in Brooklyn.

Speaker after speaker drove home the same message to the missionary core of the Lubavich. There was little doubt in this case what people should do. They were turned to their task, reaffirming who they were before their disappointment could raise any really damaging doubts about the overall validity of their commitments. And finally, the in-group support was overwhelming.

Lubavich hidism is a famously tight-knit community where nearly every aspect of life is shaped by religious practice and the Reb's guidance. Members share schools, summer camps, matchmaking networks, businesses, and recreational spaces that all orbit around kabad labavich identity. Daily rhythms are infused with study, prayer, and outreach so that social life, family life, and religious life are woven into one hole. That kind of totalizing social environment means that when doubts arise, members are not weighing one isolated belief.

They are weighing their place in an entire world of meaning and belonging. So the messianist movement survives to this day within Lubavich communities. Older theories about prophecy failure like those developed by Leon Festinger back in the 1960s focused a lot on the cognitive dissonance model, focused narrowly on the psychology of belief and the discomfort of being proven wrong. But newer research like what we've covered here shows that survival depends on something broader.

The cultural resources, leadership, rituals, and social networks that help people reinterpret failure and carry on. Outsiders tend to overestimate how central a prophecy is to day-to-day religion. From the outside, it might look like everything hinges on that prediction. But belonging to a religious community is so, so much more than a prophecy.

Religious communities offer friendships, family ties, marriage and dating prospects, and a framework for making sense of suffering and hope. In many cases, these social rewards outweigh the embarrassment of a failed prediction. In fact, in these settings, even when the outside world mocks a failed prophecy, that scorn can actually drive members closely together, confirming their identity as a chosen remnant who see what others cannot. So when we see yet another failed prophecy, whether on Tik Tok, in a Brooklyn synagogue, or in a Montana bomb shelter, we should not just ask, "How could they still believe?" A better question is, "What keeps them together?" Because in the end, prophecy is not only about predicting the future.

It's about building communities in the present. And those communities can prove remarkably resilient even when the world stubbornly refuses to end.
