The average person spends between 6 and 9 hours a night sleeping. What we do remember though is weird, vivid, strange emotional experiences that don't always make sense when we wake up. And that weirdness, that emotional, symbolic, and sometimes surreal element makes dreams fertile ground for religion. In his book, Dreaming in the World's Religions, the psychologist Kelly Balkley makes a bold claim. Dreams have played a creative role in virtually all of the world's religious and spiritual traditions.

Now, I'm always skeptical whenever anyone makes a universal claim about anything, and especially religion, but it's hard to argue with him when you look at the history of religion. In many cultures, dreams are seen as a bridge to communicate with ancestors or supernatural beings. One of the most famous examples is the Christmas story from the Gospel of Matthew. In this story, Joseph, the earthly father of Jesus, receives critical guidance through a series of dreams.

An angel appears in a dream and tells him to take Mary and the young Jesus and flee to Egypt to escape King Herod. Later on, another dream gives him the allclear to head back home. These weren't just personal messages for Joseph. They were moments of divine intervention.

And throughout Christian history, mystics, saints, and theologians have talked about dreams as moments of divine revelation. Other religious communities ritualize dreaming. In Tibetan Buddhism, for example, there's a practice called dream yoga, an advanced technique in the Vadriana tradition that treats dreams as something like a training ground for enlightenment. It involves lucid dreaming, the kind of dream where you suddenly realize midream that you're dreaming.

Practitioners use this awareness to explore the nature of reality even while asleep. In many indigenous cultures of the Great Plains, dreams play a central role in rights of passage. Like the vision quest, sometimes called crying for a vision, young people journey alone to sacred places in nature, fasting and praying for several days, and crying out to the spirits for a vision, seeking insight into their purpose in life, their role in the community, and how they can best serve their people. Dreams have also played a major role in the rise of African independent churches or AIC's.

These were Christian movements that emerged during the colonial and postcolonial times, often as a form of religious resistance, rejecting the control of Western missionaries and blending Christianity with local African cultural traditions. These stories and practices are part of a widespread pattern. Dreams playing vital roles in religious imagination across the globe.

But this also raises the question, why? Why do so many religious communities across vastly different times and places treat dreams as religiously significant? Despite their differences, many cultures see dreams as something more than random mental noise or mundane brain activity. They are treated as meaningful, even sacred.

This cross-cultural pattern has fascinated both scientists and scholars of religion. Is there something universal about the way humans experience dreams that make them religiously significant? As it turns out, one of the earliest academic theories about the origin of religion ties it directly to dreams. The Victorian anthropologist Edward Tyler proposed that people naturally took note of their dreams, like meeting strange figures, seeing long dead relatives, or encountering bizarre creatures, and used that experience as evidence of a soul or spirit.

As in for Tyler, these dream experiences were the raw material for the idea of spirits and ultimately the foundation of religion itself. But Tyler was the classic armchair anthropologist, drawing broad conclusions about other cultures without conducting any firstirhand fieldwork himself. He instead relied on the biased data of European explorers, missionaries, and colonial administrators, forming ideas from a colonial worldview that regarded non-European beliefs as primitive. According to him, the belief in spirits was rooted in a primitive failure to distinguish between dreams and reality.

Tyler's account was what evolutionary scientists now call a just so story. a speculative theory that lacks empirical support and can't really be tested. The sociologist Emil Durkheim did not buy Tyler's theory. Durkheim argued that dreams are just too fleeting and too inconsistent to serve as the foundation of structured religious thought.

In other words, he thought dreams were too vague to inspire the kind of enduring and powerful ideas and practices that shape entire societies. Back in 1912, Durkheim throws some serious scholarly shade against Tyler when he says, "What is the dream's place in our life?" quite a small place it seems since it leaves such vague impressions in the memory and so quickly fades far from providing religion with its fundamental idea. Durkheim argued that dreams don't give rise to religious beliefs. It's the other way around.

Our dreams are shaped by what we already believe. If you grow up in a culture where dreams are thought to be a way for ancestors to give guidance, you're more likely to have those kinds of dreams and to interpret them that way once you wake up. Now, while the debate between Tyler and Durkheim is a classic battle in the study of religion, the field that really shaped modern dream research was psychology, specifically depth psychology, the study of the unconscious mind. Two giants in this territory were Sigman Freud and Carl Jung.

Freud called dreams the royal road to the unconscious. He believed that dreams are manifestations of repressed desires, fears, and conflicts, stuff that's too upsetting for our mind to confront directly when we're awake. The weird fragmented nature of dreams is because our mind is disguising those truths in symbolic form. Freud's colleague Carl Jung agreed that dreams express the unconscious.

But he took it a step further, arguing that dreams also tap into what he called the collective unconscious, a shared reservoir of experiences and symbolic archetypes common to all human beings that transcends individual experience. He wrote that no individual is a blank slate but influenced by inherited presuppositions. The collective unconsciousness comprises in itself the psychic life of our ancestors right back to the earliest beginnings. He argued that these archetypes like the hero, the caregiver or the magician are reflected in our dreams and explain why some religious symbols are common across cultures.

By the late 20th century, the psychoanalytical view of dreams started facing serious challenges from large-scale empirical dream analysis. Psychologists began collecting gigantic data sets of dream reports from people from different cultures, ages, and backgrounds. Two pioneers in this field were Calvin Hall and Robert Vandercastle, who developed one of the most influential systems for analyzing these reports, known as the Hall Vandercastle system, which is still widely used today. They gathered over 10,000 recorded dreams and created a detailed coding system to track patterns in dream themes.

They focused on things like aggression, friendliness, sexuality, and success or failure. Unlike Tyler's subjective armchair approach, which relied on secondhand accounts, Hall and Vandiccastle used quantitative methods to identify consistent patterns and trends. What they found was fascinating. Dream content often varies based on factors like gender, age, and culture, suggesting that our social environment shapes what we dream about.

The second big shift came with the rise of new neuroiming technology. Tools like electronphilography or EEG which measures electrical activity in the brain and functional magnetic resonance imaging or fMRI which tracks changes in blood flow. These enabled scientists to actually observe the brain in action during sleep opening up all kinds of new opportunities for empirical research on dreams and helping researchers understand which parts of the brain are active during different stages of dreaming. EEG was critically vital in helping scientists map the different stages of sleep and showed that one stage in particular, rapid eye movement sleep or REM, is where most vivid dreaming happens.

fMRI added more detail, showing which areas of the brain are more or less active by tracking changes in blood flow in specific parts of the brain instead of general electrical signals. These studies found that the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, the part of your brain responsible for logic, reasoning, and self-control, is less active during REM. Meanwhile, the lyic system, which processes emotions like fear and excitement, stays highly active. That combo might explain why dreams feel so surreal and emotionally intense.

Areas responsible for visual processing are also active, contributing to the vivid imagery we see in our dreams. Together, these discoveries led researchers to shift away from death psychologyy's emphasis on hidden traumas and the unconscious to understand dreams as a product of the same brain systems we use when we're awake, just operating a bit differently. So, let's return to Tyler and Durkheim's original debate. Do dreams shape religious beliefs or is it the other way around?

Dr. Anne Taves, a leading figure in the cognitive science of religion, offers a 21st century perspective on the debate. In her book, Religious Experience Reconsidered. Taves shifts the question away from asking what makes a dream religious and instead asks, "How do people come to see dreams as meaningful in the first place?" Taves studied dreams alongside other forms of extraordinary or special experiences that people often interpret through religious lenses like visions and transances.

And unlike earlier scholars who tried to categorize or decode dreams as symbols or archetypes, she was more interested in context, understanding the social and cognitive processes that lead individuals and communities to interpret a dream as religiously significant. Whether a dream becomes a sacred encounter or just another interesting anecdote depends a lot on how you and the people around you understand and interpret it. This lines up with Durkheim's view. Dreams don't automatically produce religious beliefs.

They become meaningful when filtered through the categories and concepts that individuals and their communities already carry with them. One example comes from the work of the anthropologist Dr. Katie Glasin, who studied dream interpretation among the Barty, an Aboriginal group in Australia. For the party, dreams can be a source of ritual innovation, as in new songs, new dances, or other practices are sometimes received in dreams from ancestors.

But these are not just accepted automatically. Instead, the dream and its message go through a collective interpretation process. The community gets together and decides whether it's a truly legit message from the ancestors. Dr.

Glass can connected this to broader research on how dreaming can spark creativity. Many artists, for example, credit dreams for inspiring their work. But in Barty society, that creativity is balanced with the stability of collective processing. Dream interpretation is a communal process, one that allows space for new ideas, but only if those new ideas are meaningfully interpreted and socially validated.

This brings us back to Anne Taves in her argument about meaning-making. Instead of assuming that dreams are inherently religious, she asks, "How do people decide they're religious?" The Barty example illustrates this perfectly. Someone might dream of an ancestor introducing a new ritual, but that dream doesn't automatically become sacred truth. It's brought to the community where it's discussed, debated, and interpreted.

And that's the key insight. Dreams are not private experiences. They gain meaning through the cultural and social frameworks around them. This approach gives us a more flexible way to think about religious dreams, not as universal symbols or hardwired experiences, but as moments shaped by both personal insight and collective tradition and culture.

In recent years, researchers in the cognitive science of religion have started to take a new interest in dreaming. And many of them use what's called the epidemiological approach, which is all about understanding how certain cultural ideas stick and spread based on how our minds naturally work. The idea is that some concepts are just more sticky, more cognitively contagious. They're easier to remember, share, and believe because they align with how our brains process information.

So, here we're diving into the theory called content affordance relevance for dream divination or card. It's a framework proposed by Dr. Andreas Nordon to explain why certain kinds of dream content tend to be culturally amplified particularly in the practice of onromancy the use of dreams for divination. Nordan's study draws on interviews conducted with Hindu Nepali informants in Nepal and he wanted to understand which kinds of dreams people found meaningful and why.

Over the course of 61 interviews, he asked participants not just what they dreamed but how they interpreted those dreams and how dreams were used in religious and social life. From these interviews, Nordan identified four features that tend to make dreams more culturally significant. Threatening content, lack of self- agency, bizarre or counterintuitive elements, and supernatural agents. Let's start with threatening content.

Nordan argues that dreams involving fear or nightmare elements are more likely to stick in memory and get shared because they provoke strong emotional reactions. Nightmarish content like being attacked or killed also feeds into the second feature, the omission of self- agency. This refers to dreams where you feel like you're not in control. Things just happen to you, often feeling like they're caused by some outside force.

Consider how dreams work. Your brain creates a kind of simulated hallucinatory world and then casts you as a character in that world. In a lot of dreams, you're the main agent behind the action in the dream. Like, you are the one flying around or walking around.

You talk to people. You explore the environment. But in low agency dreams, events unfold without your control. If there's another main character who is controlling the action, they often take on strange or threatening qualities.

Dr. Nordon suggests that the lack of self- agency in some dreams may be one reason why so many religious communities treat dreams as encounters with the divine. He argues the same neurological processes that produce low agency dreams also makes it easier for our brains to generate and retain ideas about supernatural agents. The third feature of the card model is bizarre and counterintuitive content.

It goes without saying that dreams often contain strange or impossible scenarios that defy our everyday expectations. To explain why these kinds of dreams might be more culturally sticky, Dr. Nordon draws on a concept from the cognitive science of religion called minimal counterintuitiveness or MCI. The basic idea is that when something breaks only a few of our intuitive expectations, it becomes easier to remember.

For example, the Hindu people he interviewed often described deities in counterintuitive ways. One person recalled a dream in which the god Bwan appeared as a half statue and half man offering advice. According to MCI theory, this is exactly the kind of dream that sticks. It's just weird enough to be memorable, but not so incoherent that it's forgotten.

Most of our dreams fade quickly, but the ones that violate the rules of reality in just the right way, like a god merging with statues, tend to linger. And when a dream lingers, we're more likely to share it with others, reinforcing its significance within a religious tradition. The fourth and final feature in the card model is supernatural agent imagery. These are dreams that feature gods, spirits, ancestors, or other powerful beings.

According to Dr. Nordon, this kind of content makes a dream especially compelling because it feels like it's coming from beyond the self, connected to something bigger and more mysterious and more authoritative. So to sum it up, the card model suggests that certain features of dream content like threats, a loss of control, minimally counterintuitive imagery and encounters with supernatural beings make dreams more memorable, more likely to be shared, and thus more likely to evolve into religious narratives. And while Nordan's study is based on a small sample within a very specific cultural context, it echoes something that we've seen throughout this episode from Durkheim to Taves.

Dreams are not just personal experiences. They're shaped by the way our minds work and by the social world we live in. And parts of the card model have been supported by other research, especially the focus on low agency and counterintuitive content. In a paper titled dreams as a source of supernatural agent concepts, the neuroscientist Patrick McNamara and the aforementioned psychologist Kelly Balkley argue that dreams provide the raw experiential material for imagining gods and spirits.

Not because we're hallucinating, but because the brain, especially during REM sleep, naturally constructs narratives where we have low agency and other figures take on a central role. As we've already mentioned, during REM sleep, the prefrontal cortex is downregulated while the lyic system is very active. Magnimar and Bulkley argue this makes it harder to feel like we're in control of what's happening and why other figures in our dream seem to act with their own intention as if they're independent beings. When something else in the dream takes control, whether it's a monster chasing you, a deceased ancestor offering advice, or a glowing figure, our brains naturally attribute agency to it.

We experience it not just as a part of our imagination, but as a real intentional presence. The neuroscience of sleep also helps to explain the kinds of characters that tend to appear. Drawing on dream databases, Magnamar and others have found that REM sleep is associated with higher levels of aggression and emotional intensity. While nonREM dreams tend to be calmer and less bizarre, he suggests that when characters show up during dreams, those that occur during non-REM sleep are more likely to be friendly and benevolent, while those that show up during REM sleep may be less friendly and even demonic.

Now, in some ways, you might notice that this does echo Edward Tyler's old idea that dreams gave rise to early beliefs and spirits. But rather than proposing an origin of religion, Magnamar and Bulkley frame dreams as an ongoing cognitive mechanism, an ongoing process that continually reinforced supernatural agent concepts throughout history and across cultures. But of course, not every dream character becomes a god or spirit. Most dreams are full of ordinary people doing ordinary things.

Even in cultures where dreams are considered a possible source of religious experience, not all dreams are thought to be visitations from an unseen world. And that was exactly Durkheim's point over a hundred years ago. Any theory of dreaming and religion needs to consider Durkheim's social challenge and explain why some dreams become important religious narratives or life-changing personal experiences when the vast vast majority of them are forgotten by breakfast. So, these cognitive theories offer intriguing frameworks for understanding why certain dreams gain cultural significance, but it's important to recognize their limitations.

Nordan's card model or Magnamaru's hypothesis about supernatural agents and REM sleep are still largely theoretical models based on correlational evidence rather than definitive causal links. Many of their claims rest on inferences drawn from cognitive patterns and cultural analyses, raising the possibility that they're modern just so stories in the spirit of Edward Tyler, giving us some plausible narratives without sufficient empirical testing. Now, of course, they are much more sophisticated than Tyler and they're working with much better data like those massive dream databases. But they risk flattening religion down to universal cognitive patterns.

The key takeaway is that dreams don't exist in a vacuum. They are bioultural phenomena where our culture and our brain come together and create vivid weird experiences that we can choose to ignore or interpret.
