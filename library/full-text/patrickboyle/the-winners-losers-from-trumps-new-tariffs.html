On February 20th, the US Supreme Court delivered a major legal setback to the cornerstone of Donald Trump's economic strategy by striking down about 2thirds of the tariffs that the administration had imposed. Specifically, the court ruled that the liberation day duties imposed under the International Economic Powers Act or IEPA were illegal. This was not a huge surprise to anyone who had been paying attention. Before reaching the highest court in the land, these duties had been ruled unlawful by the Court of International Trade and by a federal appeals court.

The legal issue isn't that the president can never impose a tariff. It's that the power to tax belongs to Congress under article one of the Constitution. And they've only delegated that power to the executive through very specific limited laws. The Supreme Court essentially ruled that the act, which doesn't even contain the word tariff, was never intended to be a blank check for trade policy.

Because investors had spent months watching this slow motion car crash in the lower courts, there was no major market spike when the verdict finally landed. In fact, the outcome was so predictable that even the family of one of the men helping run the policy was seemingly profiting from it. So, how did a tiny wine importer convince six Supreme Court justices to dismantle a cornerstone of the Trump administration's trade policy?

Well, they simply pointed out that the president was using the wrong tool for the job. The administration's legal case for a lot of the tariffs rested on the International Emergency Economic Powers Act. They argued that because the president had declared a national emergency over trade deficits and fentinel, which are questionable national emergencies to begin with, he was allowed to regulate imports however he saw fit, which in his mind meant applying tariffs. The problem, as VOS selections pointed out, is that the word tariff doesn't even appear in that law.

It was designed in the 1970s to allow a president to freeze the bank accounts of rogue states or stop people from shipping high-tech weaponry to terrorists. It was never meant to be a general taxing authority that bypasses the people who actually write tax laws, Congress. This brought the court's conservative majority face to face with a legal principle that they've spent years championing, the major questions doctrine. It's based on an idea famously described by the late Justice Scalia who said that Congress does not hide elephants in mouse holes.

The idea is that if Congress had actually intended to give the president the power to unilaterally tax the American people to the tune of hundreds of billions of dollars, they wouldn't have hidden that power out of sight in an unrelated law about national emergencies. As Chief Justice Roberts noted in his opinion, the Constitution gave the power to tax to Congress alone, you can't just find a mouse hole in a 50-year-old statute and shove a 10-tonon tariff elephant into it. One of the most compelling arguments made in the case was that if a president was allowed to use emergency powers this way, he could essentially impose tariffs on the American people whenever he wants at whatever level he wants and for as long as he wants just by declaring an unreable emergency. During oral arguments, a hypothetical was raised that should give pause to even the most ardent tariff supporter.

What's to stop a future Democratic administration from declaring a climate emergency and slapping massive taxes on every gas-p powered vehicle or diesel truck that crosses the border? If the court had allowed these tariffs to stand, it would have been a radical rewrite of the US Constitution. This is the same logic that the court used to strike down President Obama's clean power plan and President Biden's attempt at student law forgiveness. In those cases, the conservative majority argued that you can't use vague old laws to push through massive policies that Congress never voted for.

Whether you're a fan of the current president's trade policy or not, the court's ruling is a reminder that sticking to the Constitution is a safety feature for everyone. It ensures that the person you don't like who will inevitably be elected in the future can't use these same shortcuts to do things that you find even more objectionable. While the legal battle was playing out in Washington, the actual economic results of the tariffs were starting to show up in the data and they were unimpressive. A massive driver of this gap was the AI boom. To build the infrastructure for the ongoing AI revolution, US firms had to import a record $3.4 trillion worth of computer parts and equipment. Since most of these high-tech components weren't hit by the initial tariffs, no import duty was charged. Had these components been tariffed, the money would have likely flowed out anyway.

The government would have simply taxed the big tech firms importing them, which might have slowed the construction a bit or reduced the profits of US big tech. We also saw a massive stockpiling effect. Fearing that the tariff man was about to close the gates, American businesses rushed to import as much as they could in early 2025 to get ahead of any new taxes. The US consumers stepped up their purchases, too, to buy the stuff that they wanted before the prices went up.

The administration claimed a small victory when the deficit appeared to dip slightly later in the year, but economists pointed out that this wasn't because the tariffs were working. It was simply the result of firms drawing down the massive inventories they had accumulated in anticipation of the new duties. Now, if you listen to the consensus story on Wall Street, tariffs are supposed to be dollar positive. The logic is that the tariffs lower the demand for imports, which means fewer dollars are being swapped for foreign currencies like the yen or the euro.

With less supply of dollars slloshing around abroad, the value of the greenback should go up. But a funny thing happened on the way to the trade war. Since the implementation of these tariffs, the dollar hasn't soared. It's actually weakened significantly, falling by roughly 10% over the last year.

This decline makes imported goods even more expensive than the tariffs alone as it now takes more dollars to buy the same Japanese machine parts or European wine. For US savers, this has been particularly painful. They've effectively seen their global wealth decline by double digits over the last year just by holding the world's reserve currency. This has led a number of analysts to wonder if the famous dollar smile theory is finally broken.

The idea was famously put forth by the economist Steven Jen during his time at Morgan Stanley. He argued that the dollar thrives in two extreme scenarios. It rises when the US economy is booming as investors want to move their money to where the action is. And it also rises when the world is in a crisis as everyone rushes to the safety of the US treasuries.

We saw this happen in the global financial crisis. Investors are now worried that if the US is deliberately causing the economic problems, its safe haven status no longer makes sense. While the administration is confident in this new pivot, legal experts are already pointing out some massive holes in the hull of plan B. Analysts at the KO Institute, a conservative think tank, have noticed that section 122 was never intended to be a tool for general trade policy.

It was written in the early 1970s as a direct response to the Nixon shock of 1971, the moment that the US effectively ended the gold standard. At that time, the world was still struggling to figure out if it could return to a system of fixed exchange rates. In that old school era, a country could actually run out of gold or foreign reserves to pay its debts, a true balance of payments crisis. But in the modern world of floating exchange rates, that problem has largely vanished.

As the economist Milton Freriedman famously argued, a system of floating exchange rates completely eliminates the balance of payments problem because the currency price fluctuates to prevent such a crisis. Because the US dollar now floats freely on the open market, the administration is once again conflating two very different things. a trade deficit, which is just us buying more stuff than we sell, and a payments crisis, which is us being unable to pay our bills. To the Kato Institute, using section 122 to fix a trade deficit is like trying to use a law designed for emergency bankruptcy to justify a spending spree.

While this might well be challenged in court, most analysts expect that the cases would take much longer than 150 days to settle. It's also likely that the administration would get more wiggle room from the courts on these economic definitions than they did on purely constitutional ones, as that's what the Supreme Court cares the most about.
