These teams could launch from aircraft carriers or enter the country through Iran's long coastline and mountainous borders.

Once inside Iran, there is no shortage of possible targets. Perhaps the most pressing objective is securing the stockpile of highlyenriched uranium. Iran is believed to possess around 400 kg of uranium enriched to roughly 60%. No one knows the precise location.

A raid to seize or neutralize that material could set back Iran's nuclear program. But there is a problem. The operation would take time, and time is exactly what special forces usually try to avoid.

Extracting or diluting the material would require troops to remain on site long enough for Iranian forces to respond. So, it would be a high-risk, highreward operation. Other missions would be faster, but also less decisive. Special forces could strike research facilities tied to Iran's nuclear program or sabotage factories connected to missile and drone production.

These raids might slow Iran's military programs, but they wouldn't eliminate them. Iran's industrial networks are widely dispersed, and destroying one facility rarely shuts down the entire system. Assassinations of senior military commanders are another possibility. But Iran has a decentralized defense doctrine in which the IRGC is divided into 31 units each commanded by a brigadier general.

The units operate autonomously without approval from the central command in Thran. So removing individual leaders would cause disruption, but it wouldn't stop the country from launching missiles or drones. There is also the possibility of working with opposition groups inside Iran. Special forces might attempt to organize Kurdish militias or support underground networks in major cities.

However, building an insurgency is not something that happens overnight. It takes years of preparation and depends on local political conditions that outsiders cannot easily control. Even if any of these operations succeeded tactically, they would carry political risk at home. The capture or death of American special operations troops would quickly become a national story.

Washington still remembers the failed 1980 hostage rescue mission in Iran. Operation Desert Claw, which collapsed in the desert and helped doom Jimmy Carter's presidency. Any administration considering covert operations inside Iran knows how quickly a failed raid can turn into a political crisis. If Washington wants to escalate the war without committing to a full-scale invasion, the next step would likely involve limited territorial operations.

Instead of trying to push deep into Iran, the United States could attempt to seize specific targets along the country's coastline or on nearby islands. Doing so today would expose landing forces to drones, anti-ship missiles, coastal defenses, and artillery. The final option, and the most dramatic one, would be a full-scale invasion, one aimed either at dismantling Iran's conventional military forces or bringing down the Islamic Republic altogether. Neighboring countries would have to host American troops and allow supply lines to run through their territory. Yet few governments appear to have an appetite for total war. Then there is the political challenge at home. A war of this scale would require massive funding and long-term commitment.

Only a major economic or military blow from Iran would be enough to push public opinion toward a ground war.
