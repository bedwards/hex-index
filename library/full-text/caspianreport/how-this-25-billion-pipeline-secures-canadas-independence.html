Oil and gas make up 5% of Canada's economy and it's one of the few growing industries in the country.

Nearly all crude oil comes from Alberta in western Canada where the oil sands pump out millions of barrels of thick tarlike oil every day. However, instead of going to buyers around the world, most of that oil is sent across the border to the United States, mainly to the Midwest. For a long time, Canada was okay with this. The United States is a massive market with an unrelenting appetite for energy.

So both countries came to rely on one another. However, in recent times, this interdependency has become increasingly detrimental to Canada's interests and not just because of Trump and his tariffs. Any nation that bets on one buyer gambles with its future. And operating in a single market has pushed down the price of Canadian crude oil.

It's lower than it should be. Meanwhile, much of the global demand for oil is increasingly coming from Asia. Canada has been trying to sell more oil there for years. The problem, however, is that Alberta is landlocked.

It is hundreds of kilometers away from either coast, making it difficult to ship oil overseas. The original Trans Mountain pipeline, built in 1953, tackled this issue. It became the only means of transporting oil from Alberta to the Pacific coast for overseas shipments. Nevertheless, in the decades following, Canada's oil industry grew faster than its pipelines could handle.

Exports to the United States increased accordingly, but a surplus of crude oil accumulated. In the last 10 years, for instance, oil production went from 3.7 million barrels a day in 2014 to well over 5 million in 2024. There was plenty of room to reach new markets. That is if the capacity of the Trans Mountain pipeline could be expanded.

So, in 2013, energy giant Kinder Morgan proposed to upgrade the pipeline. At first, the expansion project was estimated to cost roughly $4 billion, but the price tag grew substantially over the years due to delays caused by protests from environmental and indigenous groups. In 2018, private investors eventually backed down, deeming the project too risky. Right then and there, the government stepped in and bought the pipeline from Kinder Morgan.

The Trudeau administration had a grand strategic objective. Rely less on the United States and sell oil overseas for better prices. Canada immediately began expanding its infrastructure. This included nearly 200 km of reactivated pipeline, 19 new storage tanks, and three new shipping docks at the Westrich Marine Terminal near Vancouver.

Altogether, the expansion tripled Canada's export capacity from 300,000 to 900,000 barrels of oil a day, just as Trudeau set his sights on markets in Japan, China, and India. Finally completed in May 2024, the expansion promises big returns. Estimates by the federal government say it will generate 73 billion in Canadian dollars for the producers over 20 years while increasing government revenues by 46 billion in Canadian dollars. For context, this converts to 51 billion and 32 billion in American dollars.

For Trudeau, the project's completion comes as a relief. His government was slammed by environmental campaigners for buying the pipeline in the first place. In 2020, protests escalated when indigenous groups and activists set up rail blockades across Canada, which disrupted freight and passenger train services for weeks. The standoff put immense pressure on the government and forced emergency negotiations.

These disruptions became symbolic of the broader movement against fossil fuel expansion. They reinforced the argument that increasing oil and gas production would hamper Canada's efforts to cut greenhouse gas emissions and reach net zero by 2050. It goes without saying that building pipelines in Canada has become increasingly complicated, and the Trans Mountain expansion may well be the last of its kind. Pipelines carve paths that politics cannot erase.

While the Trans Mountain expansion has reduced Canada's interdependence, the United States is and will remain Canada's biggest customer. But that's not all. Canada also depends on the United States for domestic distribution. To clarify, Alberta has built a massive network of pipelines to send its oil to refineries and ports in both Canada and the United States.

However, most of these pipelines either pass through or go straight to the United States. Even Albertan oil designated for Eastern Canada. So, it works like this. The oil used in Ontario and Quebec travels through a pipeline that runs from Canada into the United States and then comes back into Canada.

And so Canada is dependent not only on the US single market for its exports, but also on domestic distribution. This is a bad hand to have with Trump in office. The only way Canada can overcome this is by building more pipelines to its coasts and reaching out to new buyers. Two previously cancelled plans, Energy East and Northern Gateway, keep coming up again.

Now, the idea is to transport Albertan oil directly to the Pacific and Atlantic coasts and then export it around the world. Energy East is a 4600 km pipeline project that aims to transport more than a million barrels of oil a day from Alberta and Saskatchewan to refineries and export terminals in eastern Canada primarily in New Brunswick. Meanwhile, Northern Gateway, 1173 kilometers in length, looks to transport about half a million barrels of Albertan oil per day to a marine terminal in Kitimat, British Columbia for export to Asian markets. If implemented, these two projects, much like the Trans Mountain expansion, would strengthen Canada's sovereignty, giving it much more negotiating room with a protectionist America.

However, Canada still faces considerable internal opposition. Energy East and Northern Gateway were called down for many reasons, including a lack of federal government support, push back from Quebec and Ontario, and environmental concerns. So, it's been a challenge to get everyone on board, but Trump's rhetoric has changed the narrative. Now that Canada might face a trade war, the political discourse is open to more profound internal reflection.

What was once unthinkable is now a matter of negotiation. If Canadian lawmakers can gather the needed consensus, they could build energy east and northern gateway and strengthen the country's long-term resilience. As of now, though, it's all about impact. Suppose the standoff with Trump escalates and Canada starts having lines at gas stations, shortages, and other problems.

In that case, Canadian lawmakers might decide that the security of supply is more important than some of the reasons they oppose those pipelines. However, the biggest barrier is who would pay for these pipelines since the costs would be astronomical. While Northern Gateway is roughly the same length as the Trans Mountain expansion and thus would cost a similar $25 billion, Eastern Energy is four times the length and so likely four times the price. What's more, the companies involved in these two cancelled pipelines, Nbridge for Northern Gateway and South Bow for Energy East, have no interest in restarting them.

Granted, if no private company steps in, the federal government could, like it did with the Trans Mountain expansion. However, the only way the government can justify such expensive, controversial projects, is if there is nationwide approval for them. If Canada decides to do this as a nation, it has the power to make it happen. But it's going to take a lot of lawmakers working in union to find common ground.

It's been a long time since Canada has done that. With Trump in office, however, there is an opportunity to bring Canadians together.
