China is home to the world's largest rare earth deposits. Some of the major mining sites include Bayan Obo in Inner Mongolia, Myaning in Sichuan, Wishan in Shandong, and several more in Djang Xi province. From these mines, ore is shipped to refineries in London, where contaminants are removed. The refined rare earths are then sent to magnet factories near Gonzo.

Those same magnets end up in everything from wind turbines to electric vehicles, including models made by BYD and Tesla. However, long before China, it was America that stood as the world's biggest rare earth producer. For decades, most of the global supply came from the Mountain Pass mine in California. But in the 1980s, the US began to lose its edge.

Stricter environmental regulations and higher operational expenses started to weigh on US mining companies. Processing rare earths became increasingly more costly, making domestic production less attractive. As a result, the industry was pushed overseas, often with the blessing of US lawmakers. By 1996, America had outsourced so much that Congress shut down the US Bureau of Mines, the government's leading research agency on the issue.

Meanwhile, as the US stepped back, China picked up the slack and learned the craft. It accepted the environmental burden and channeled massive resources into developing its rare earth industry. Low labor costs and lacks environmental standards gave Beijing the advantage it needed to expand its hold on the market. By the 2000s, new technologies had sharply lowered production costs.

At the same time, China used export incentives and tax breaks to push down global prices, thereby undercutting international competitors and discouraging investment in alternative supply chains. The result was dominance. By 2005, its share of global production peaked at 98% and although that figure has since declined, Beijing's monopoly over rare earth supply chains remains overwhelming. Numerous state-backed companies have attempted to enter the industry, but the barriers remain immense.

Beijing influences prices to drive competitors out before they can even deliver their first shipment. And this dominance isn't just economic, it's political. In 2010, China halted rare earth exports to Japan following a sovereignty dispute in the East China Sea. The embargo sent shock waves through global supply chains, forcing America, Japan, Australia, and others to reassess their dependence on Chinese rare earths.

After the White House raised tariffs on Chinese goods to 145%, Beijing retaliated by imposing export controls on seven types of rare earths and magnets. Rare earths accounted for only a tiny share of China's exports to the US. So the move caused little economic pain at home, but provoked an outcry from American manufacturers.

So immediately thereafter, two rounds of high-level negotiations followed which led to a 90-day truce between Washington and Beijing. The point is rare earths have become one of China's most powerful bargaining chips.
