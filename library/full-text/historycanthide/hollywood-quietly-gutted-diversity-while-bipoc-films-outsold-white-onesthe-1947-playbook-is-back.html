The Myth of the Market

Before we get to the history, let’s address the excuse you’re going to hear. Somebody at a studio, or on cable news, or in your mentions, will say that this is simply the market correcting itself. Audiences got “fatigued.” Diverse films “underperformed.” It was never political, just business.

The data says otherwise. Films with casts between 41 and 50 percent BIPOC, which roughly mirrors the actual U.S. population, generated the highest median global box office of any cast diversity interval in 2025. BIPOC moviegoers bought the majority of opening weekend tickets for 11 of the top 20 films.

Horror, the most profitable genre by return on investment, is disproportionately driven by Black and Latino audiences. Ryan Coogler’s “Sinners” made history as the most Oscar-nominated film of all time and grossed $368 million. The market is not rejecting diversity. The market keeps paying for it. Studio executives are choosing to ignore the receipt.

So if this isn’t about profit, what is it about?

“To Divert the Loyalty of the Screen”

In 1944, a group of some of Hollywood’s most powerful figures formed an organization called the Motion Picture Alliance for the Preservation of American Ideals. The founding membership included Walt Disney, director King Vidor, Sam Wood, and Leo McCarey. John Wayne and Gary Cooper were among its prominent supporters.

Their statement of principles read, in part, that they pledged “to fight, with every means at our organised command, any effort of any group or individual, to divert the loyalty of the screen from the free America that gave it birth.” They wanted to dedicate their work to “the presentation of the American scene, its standards and its freedoms, its beliefs and its ideals.”

Underneath the patriotic language was a precise enforcement mechanism. The Alliance defined what counted as un-American in Hollywood, and it was a list that should sound familiar: leftist ideas, union organizing, any critique of law enforcement or nationalist mythology, anything that challenged white, Christian, patriarchal norms. What they called “subversion,” we might recognize today as the early stirrings of what their successors would eventually brand as “woke.”

Walt Disney was not just a name on the letterhead. According to Neal Gabler’s biography, Disney brought armed guards to his studio when cartoonists attempted to form a union. He fired organizers, cut wages, and testified enthusiastically before the House Un-American Activities Committee about what he described as communist infiltration of the industry. He viewed the Screen Actors Guild itself as a communist front. His anti-union ferocity was matched only by his willingness to name names.

The Alliance was also performing a crucial political service: linking, in the public imagination, the words “Hollywood” and “Red.” Producer Walter Wanger noted in 1944 that the Alliance had accomplished this “without proof.” The evidence was never really the point. The point was the association.

What “Preservation” Actually Preserved

The pipeline from the Alliance to the Hollywood Blacklist was short and well-maintained. By 1947, the House Un-American Activities Committee was subpoenaing writers, directors, and actors, demanding they answer for their political beliefs and deliver up the names of colleagues. Those who cooperated became “friendly witnesses.” Those who refused became the Hollywood Ten.

The Ten were primarily screenwriters. They refused to answer on First Amendment grounds, were cited for contempt of Congress, and were subsequently jailed for sentences of up to a year. Five of them were under studio contract at the time and were fired within weeks. The blacklist grew from there. According to research by Stanford professor Hayagreeva Rao, artists not directly named by HUAC saw their chances of employment drop by 13 percent simply for having previously worked with someone who was. Actors faced a 20 percent employment drop if they had collaborated with blacklisted writers. Even Oscar winners were not protected.

The chilling effect was the actual product. You did not need to jail every dissident. You only needed to make the industry understand which topics were off-limits: class struggle, police brutality, labor power, anti-fascism, racial critique. Studios responded by turning toward what they called patriotic, “American” fare. Most of it was poor art and box-office poison. They made it anyway, because the goal was never to make money. The goal was to demonstrate loyalty.

Screenwriter Dalton Trumbo kept writing under a pseudonym. His script for “The Brave One” won the Academy Award in 1956, credited to a man named Robert Rich. The award went unclaimed for nearly two decades. The industry understood what it was doing and did it regardless.

The New Alliance Has Better Branding

Eighty years later, the language has been updated. The Motion Picture Alliance pledged to protect “American ideals.” Today’s version talks about “merit,” “audience fatigue,” and the threat of “DEI propaganda.” The mechanism is functionally the same: create a political threat environment, attach that threat to specific kinds of stories and storytellers, and let executives do the rest.

Under Trump’s second term, federal agencies have cut funding for diversity-related research, banned the word “diversity” in numerous government contexts, and positioned anti-DEI politics as a mainstream governing philosophy. Right-wing media cycles have reliably targeted films with queer characters, Black-led stories, and anything that could be branded as ideologically suspect. Congressional figures have raised the specter of hearings over “ideological indoctrination” in entertainment, consciously echoing HUAC’s posture while updating the vocabulary.

Studios have not needed a formal blacklist. They have something more efficient: the reasonable fear of becoming the next culture-war target. The result is a soft blacklist, operating through greenlights that don’t happen, budgets that don’t materialize, and diversity departments that get quietly gutted. Darnell Hunt, co-founder of the Hollywood Diversity Report, put it plainly: “as the country experiences a powerful blowback against diversity, the studios have relapsed into a colorblind complacency.” He warned that more downward trends are almost guaranteed, not just for diversity and representation, but for the industry’s financial survival.

Only 10.1 percent of top theatrical films in 2025 were directed by women, the lowest share since 2018. BIPOC directors stood at 22 percent, still less than half what proportional representation would require. White men alone directed nearly three-quarters of all top films. White men alone wrote nearly 72 percent of all scripts.

The Receipts

The UCLA report is not a lament. It is documentation. It shows, with thirteen years of consistent data, that the industry knows exactly what it is doing and keeps doing it anyway. Diverse casts travel better internationally. They generate stronger opening weekends. They anchor the horror and animation genres that deliver the highest returns on investment. The audience is not white, male, and suburban. BIPOC 18-to-34-year-olds made up the largest audience share for 18 of the top 20 films of 2025.

Hollywood is not confused about who its audience is. It has chosen, under political pressure, to keep producing for an imagined one.

The original Alliance’s members described themselves as defenders of freedom. They were, in practice, building an infrastructure for ideological surveillance and professional punishment inside the most powerful storytelling industry in the world. What they called “the American scene” was always a selective edit: certain people, certain stories, certain futures were permissible. The rest were threats.

The new version is less dramatic. There are no congressional hearings yet, no contempt charges, no jailed screenwriters. There are just fewer greenlights. Fewer slots at wide release. Fewer names in the credits. Progress that, in the words of the UCLA researchers, looks like a rocking horse. Moving constantly. Going nowhere.
