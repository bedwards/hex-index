On April 6, 2026, a voice came through the feed from 215,049 miles away. Calm. Precise. Professional.

“Good morning, Houston, from inside Integrity.”

That voice belonged to Victor Glover: U.S. Navy captain, veteran test pilot, NASA astronaut, and the pilot of Artemis II, the first crewed mission to orbit the moon in over fifty years. A few hours later, his spacecraft swung behind the far side of the moon, cutting off all communication with Earth for about forty minutes. Four human beings were farther from home than any people in recorded history, with a Black man at the controls.

According to NASA mission updates, the Orion spacecraft reached a maximum distance of 252,756 miles from Earth, surpassing the record set by Apollo 13 in 1970. Victor Glover, born in Pomona, California, raised on his grandfather’s stories about being barred from flying in the Air Force because he was Black, is now the first Black astronaut to travel into deep space. He is the pilot.

That detail matters more than it might seem. Because the agency that handed him those controls spent decades making sure no one who looked like him ever got close to them.

The Pattern Starts Before NASA Existed

Before we get to mission patches and press conferences, we need to go back to 1804.

York was an enslaved Black man owned by William Clark. When Meriwether Lewis invited Clark to co-lead the Corps of Discovery, Clark ordered York to come along. York didn’t have a choice. He crossed the same rivers, climbed the same mountains, nearly drowned in the same rapids, and traveled more than 4,000 miles through terrain no American expedition had ever mapped.

According to historian Craig Fehrman, who spent five years researching newly uncovered documents about the expedition, York was one of only two men chosen to operate the corps’ single whipsaw when they built their winter quarters. Clark later praised York’s ability to manage the boats in an unpublished letter Fehrman uncovered. York was among a small group trusted to navigate the most treacherous river rapids on the entire journey. During councils with Arikara, Mandan, and Hidatsa leaders, the corps’ journals record that the Native nations called York “the most marvelous” thing about the expedition.

When the captains asked every member of the corps to vote on where to build their final winter quarters, they asked York too. His vote is recorded in Clark’s elk-skin journal.

When the expedition returned home, the federal government paid $274.57 for York’s labor during the journey, a sum similar to what enlisted privates received. That money went to William Clark. York went back to bondage.

The U.S. has always loved Black courage when it serves national glory. It has been considerably less enthusiastic about Black citizenship, Black wages, or Black credit.

That pattern held for the next two centuries.

NASA’s Whites-Only Era

When NASA launched in 1958, the agency quickly became the crown jewel of American Cold War mythology. The space race was framed as a contest between freedom and tyranny, democracy and oppression. The astronauts were clean-cut, crew-cut, and almost uniformly white. The face of American “freedom” in space looked nothing like the Black Americans who were, at that exact moment, being fire-hosed in Birmingham and beaten on the Edmund Pettus Bridge.

Beginning in World War II, Black women with college degrees were recruited to work as “human computers” at Langley Memorial Aeronautical Laboratory, performing complex mathematical calculations by hand in a segregated unit called the West Area Computers. According to Scientific American, Katherine Johnson’s trajectory math was so trusted that John Glenn reportedly refused to fly his 1962 orbital mission until she personally verified the numbers the electronic computers had produced. Dorothy Vaughan became the first Black supervisor at NACA in 1949. Mary Jackson became an engineer. All three did the math that sent white men into history.

They could not eat in the same cafeteria as the engineers whose careers their calculations were holding up. It took a 2016 film for most Americans to learn their names — more than fifty years after the fact. NASA did not simply forget them. It just never made room for them in the story it was telling about itself.

Gil Scott-Heron noticed. In 1970, as NASA celebrated the Apollo program’s triumphs, Scott-Heron released “Whitey on the Moon,” a spoken-word poem addressing the gap between the space program’s billion-dollar ambitions and the poverty grinding down Black communities at home. As reported in Space.com’s coverage of Glover’s mission, Glover himself has said he listens to that track every Monday, a deliberate reminder of the world he carries with him into space.

Scott-Heron was not wrong. The same year Apollo 13 set the distance record that Artemis II just broke, NASA’s astronaut corps was entirely white. The agency had not hired a single Black astronaut.

That changed, slowly and partially, in 1978. According to reporting from BlackAmericaWeb.com, Guion Bluford, Frederick Gregory, and Ronald McNair joined NASA’s astronaut ranks that year. Bluford became the first African American in space in 1983, aboard the Space Shuttle Challenger. But the door that opened in 1978 did so late, and even then, it did not stay fully open.

In 2018, NASA abruptly pulled Dr. Jeanette Epps from a long-duration ISS mission without explanation. As reported by BlackAmericaWeb.com, Epps had been on track to become the first African American astronaut on an extended ISS expedition. Her brother publicly called the decision a product of racism and misogyny inside the agency. NASA offered no clarifying statement beyond calling it a personnel matter. Epps, a former CIA intelligence officer with a doctorate in aerospace engineering, was replaced by her backup and moved to a desk in Houston.

NASA, to put it plainly, has a history.

The First Black Astronaut on a Long-Duration Mission

Victor Glover already broke one barrier before Artemis II. According to POCIT’s coverage of the mission, Glover became the first Black person to serve on an International Space Station expedition in 2020, spending six months on the ISS as part of Crew Dragon’s first operational mission.

He was born in 1976 and inspired, at least in part, by a grandfather who was told he could not fly because of his race. He earned his wings anyway, logged over 3,500 flight hours across more than 40 aircraft during his military career, and joined NASA’s astronaut class in 2013.

Now, in 2026, he is piloting the spacecraft that carried humans farther from Earth than they have ever been. According to CBS News’ live coverage of the mission, Glover said a quick prayer when the crew went behind the moon and lost contact with Earth, then got back to work. When his wife Dionna was confirmed to be watching from Houston’s mission control gallery, he said simply: “Hey, babe. I love you. From the moon.”

What This Moment Means — And Doesn’t

There are people in Washington right now working to dismantle diversity, equity, and inclusion programs across the federal government. The argument, in its most charitable form, goes something like this: race-conscious policies are unnecessary because excellence is colorblind.

The history of York, of Julius Montgomery (the first Black technical professional at Cape Canaveral, who faced a colleague line that refused to shake his hand), of Guion Bluford, of Jeanette Epps, of Victor Glover’s own grandfather being told he couldn’t fly — that history is the answer to that argument. Excellence was never the barrier. Race was.

Glover is not a symbol deployed to prove a point. He is a Navy captain who earned his seat. But the fact that his seat was denied to generations of Black pilots and engineers before him is not incidental to his story. It is the story.

In 1970, Gil Scott-Heron was asking who the space program was actually for. In 2026, a Black man is the pilot. That is real, hard-won, undeniable progress. It does not mean the work is finished. The people Scott-Heron was speaking for—still navigating medical debt, housing insecurity, underfunded schools, and a justice system that too often treats Black lives as expendable—they are still waiting on a different kind of mission.

But Victor Glover looked out the window of Integrity and watched an eclipse that, in his words, “humans probably have not evolved to see.” He saw the Earth as a bright sphere hanging in darkness. He said, “Trust me, you are special in all of this emptiness.”

We changed who pilots the spacecraft. Now let’s change what people come home to.
