To establish what counts as a series of subtle wins for America's largest passenger rail purveyor, it's important to establish Amtrak's real mandate. To the general public, it's most likely that Amtrak is understood as a vaguely government-run service that is supposed to turn a profit but never actually does.

That's close to right, and it's close to what you'll get from the top few lines of the Wikipedia article, that it's a quasi public corporation that's managed as a for-profit organization. But this lacks some important nuance. Take the idea of forprofit for example. In its founding legislation, the 1970 Rail Passenger Service Act, there was very limited flexibility in the language as it stated that the National Passenger Rail Corporation shall be a for-profit corporation.

Ever since, the idea has been closely associated with the service. At the time, this was a bold stance, but perhaps one that felt viable given the fact that the service would be inheriting all its rolling stock from the floundering private passenger rail sector, and it'd be borrowing rail access from the freight companies, a decision that still haunts the corporation's meager on time percentage. In service by 1971, Amtrak by the end of 1972 was reporting a revenue of $160 million and an operating expense of $310 million. It was costing nearly double the year's revenue to keep passenger rail alive.

Now, early Amtrak and its sponsor, Congress, understood that it would take some capital investment upfront to get rolling, but quickly it also began to recognize that running Amtrak for profit was probably beyond the pale. In 1978, a small edit within the Rail Passenger Service Act acknowledged as much. With the addition of four words that the corporation shall be operated and managed as a for-profit corporation, Amtrak's mandate from Congress quietly became more flexible. It was a subtle concession that saving a deeply unprofitable and thus cratering private passenger rail system in the US was not exactly going to be a money maker for the US government.

Now, turning a profit was not a necessity or an expectation, but operating in a similarly ruthless bottom lineoriented manner of a for-profit was. This difference matters, but this difference is also easy to overlook. A common talking point on Amtrak is that in its 50 plus years of existence, the service is yet to turn a profit. What gets left out here is that since 1978, it hasn't explicitly been mandated to.

The myth that Amtrak exists to turn an eventual profit is not only an oversimplification, but also an offiscation of the services guiding purpose outlined in 1970. As the top of the act outlines, the National Passenger Rail Corporation's purpose was to procure something that the private sector no longer could, an efficient, modern, interconnected passenger rail system for the good of the country. For 50 plus years, then Amtrak has not failed a mandate to turn a profit, but more accurately has failed to procure a quality intercity passenger rail system largely on account of the myth that it's supposed to turn a profit. And yet, misunderstood, mocked and maligned, politicized, and subsequently villainized, perpetually hamstrung by ebbing and flowing support, Amtrak, within its narrow confines, is fulfilling its mandates better than ever.

For all rail passengers along the southern portion of the Northeast Corridor, the BNP tunnel connecting Baltimore Penn Station and BWI has been a speed bump. First, the approach curve from the southwest is prohibitively tight, requiring a slowdown before even entering.

Then on the northeast end, the entrance curve hits a comparatively sharp 8°, requiring passenger trains to slow all the way down to 30 m or 50 km/h. This slowdown creates bottlenecks as 150 trains pass over these two sets of tracks each day. And across the 1.4 m or 2.3 km, the tunnel has a vertical grade of 1.34°, which is the steepest along the northeast corridor and ensures the train stays slow throughout it. Not only is it an operational disaster, infrastructure experts have called it a literal disaster waiting to happen.

It's exceptionally cramped. It's a single point of failure for the multiple passenger systems moving through it. It's nearly a century and a half old. And currently, it requires millions in maintenance every year to ensure the water seeping into the ancient structure doesn't roted out.

It's tunnels like this that all but ensure passenger rail inefficiencies across the Northeast.
