What is now considered good used to just be the norm. The earliest days of the American longhaul trucking industry better paralleled the stage coach industry than trucking of today.

West of the Mississippi, there were no highways, routes were indirect, and service stations were few and far between. The roads were rural and the trucks were rudimentary, traveling at speeds that topped out at 40 mph and experiencing constant malfunctions like tire blowouts and engines overheating. As World War I loomed, the movement of military supplies became critical to American participation, and railroads couldn't handle it alone. So industry advancements occurred quickly.

Fifth wheels were introduced to trucks which standardized their size. Diesel engines ushered in a new era of efficiency with 25 to 30% more miles per gallon and pneumatic tires decreased damage to both the trucks and the goods they carried. By 1920, there were a million trucks on America's roads. As technology evolved and infrastructure improved, the industry around it did too, and the government needed to catch up.

To do this, the Interstate Commerce Act was amended with the Motor Carrier Act of 1935, largely spearheaded by the railroad industry, which was losing business to what they considered an unregulated transportation market in trucking. Key pieces of the legislation standardized trucking rates and required companies to have certificates. Companies previously in operation, like Alco, were grandfathered in, though, and automatically awarded a certificate, making it very difficult for any new carriers to enter the market. They had to prove there was a need not being met to even be considered.

The United Parcel Service, which started in Seattle in 1907 with deliveries on foot and bicycle, ultimately benefited from this legislation. When the company was founded, it specialized in retail deliveries exclusively in Seattle and eventually in Oakland, California. But as it expanded to here and here and here, introducing consolidated carrier shipping, it eventually fell into the jurisdiction of the ICC. So when the 1935 act was introduced, UPS had a unique grasp on several key markets in the United States and it held on to that tightly over the decades when others couldn't directly compete.

Additionally, rape bureaus were set up to standardize regional transport costs and to restrict what a truck could actually carry, paralleling similar regulations in the rail industry. Truckers were told what they could carry, where they could carry it, and how much they could charge for that service. So this farmer in Florida, for example, was allowed to transport their own fruits and vegetables to Cincinnati, Ohio, but could not carry alcohol from a company in Covington, Ohio back to Florida because he wasn't permitted to take loads for hire and as a produce carrier was not allowed to transport alcohol. What started as a way to control the market and even the playing field to the benefit of both drivers and companies ultimately made trucking more expensive for the consumer.

Freight rates were an estimated one-third to half higher due to the regulations. Frustrations mounted, but then everything changed in the era of deregulation. The Motor Carrier Act of 1980 represented an almost full repeal of all regulations set more than four decades earlier. Now truckers could drive where they wanted, transporting what they wanted and charging more or less for it, and new carriers could enter the market with essentially unfettered access.

And a new era in American trucking was born. Workers flocked to the industry. The number of truckers nearly doubled between 1977 and 1996 from 1.1 million to 1.9 million. The floodgates opened on the industry, and while it was a wash in labor and employment options, the sharp increase in competition led to lower wages and a loss in union power. Though consumers and companies benefited from cheaper prices and an explosive growth market, it all came at a cost to one party in particular, the drivers. In this flooded, lightly regulated post 1980 reality, there exists a fundamental and unshakable paradox. Trucking has never been so in demand in the US, and yet it has never been such a bad job.
