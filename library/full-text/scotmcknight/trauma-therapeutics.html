An outline of my message at Kerygma in Nashville last Friday. Kerygma is a gathering of mostly women seeking to know more about the Bible and to know God. My topic, trauma therapeutics, reflects the book I wrote with Adrienne Gibson called Traumatized Church. A few hours after this talk Adrienne and I led a workshop on trauma in the church.

Many in this setting are suffering from trauma.

Trauma can be broken into five terms: trauma is a response to an event or an experience; trauma overwhelms our coping systems; and trauma affects even our identity, our security, and our safety. Trauma is storied in the body and trauma has a very good memory. It is your God-designed body’s natural way to protect itself and to find safety.

All it takes is for someone to say something, do something, or just use the word “trauma” and some people are sent into the trauma zone all over again.

I don’t know what your trauma may be, or what the trauma of someone you love is, but today I want you to know that the Gospels tell us this: Jesus specializes in trauma therapeutics. Therapeutics is from the Greek term for healing.

In what follows we will go through Mt 8–9 four times in order to catch four major themes.

Jesus sees trauma

Each of these was a physical impairment; some of them became a disability in that the person’s social status opportunities were restricted. Jesus’s healings then were both physical miracles and social liberations.

A leper: 8.2. a man with scaly-skin; not Hansen’s disease; but it was a physical impairment and a disability.
A centurion’s young slave: 8.5. paralysis of some sort.

The term παραλυτικός, which is not a modern scientific diagnosis that (today) describes permanent, temporary, or periodic as well as complete or partial impairment of not being able either to use, or a severe restriction of, one’s limb or limbs. Causes include injury or a medical condition. Having said that, the Lkn–Johannine statements that the man was ill (Lk 7.2 [‘being in a bad way’]; John 4.46 [‘weakening’] with a ‘fever’; 4.52), suggests ‘paralysed’ describes the near–death experience

Fevering: 8.14
Demonized: 8.16
Sick: 8.16
Fearful: 8.26
Demonized: 8.28
Paralyzed: 9.2
Death: 9.18
The bleeding woman (zavah): 9.20
The sightless: 9.27
The demonized speechless: 9.32

Each of the persons in these stories was experiencing trauma: something had happened that was so beyond their coping powers that they were suffering, their identity was shaken, their sense of safety was threatened. And, they were driven to find Jesus because they heard he could help.

Like you.

Perhaps a disease or a diagnosis that is too much for you.
Perhaps a child, a spouse, a friend, a parent, a sibling with a disease or a diagnosis that is too much for you.
Perhaps a spouse … a boss… a pastor … a neighbor… a friend of your child.
Perhaps a loss of income…
Perhaps you have had to leave a church you formerly loved. That was safe for you but now is not.

Jesus sees you because he is empathic, empathetic, sympathetic, and compassionate.

Roman Krznaric, an expert on empathy, defines it like this: “Empathy is the art of stepping imaginatively into the shoes of another person, understanding their feelings and perspectives, and using that understanding to guide your actions” (Empathy, x).

I learned the emotion of empathy as an 11-year-paper boy. An elderly man and woman on my route had to scratch around a coin purse to find enough coins to pay for the paper. I did not know such poverty; my body was moved into empathy; my imagination of what life was like was 3D. So sorry was I that I punched “paid” when I came to their home, and for as long as I remember I delivered their paper at my own expense. (This is a very shortened version of a deep memory of mine.)

Jesus’s imagination has super powers because his imagination comes from his omniscience. So his empathy is total. Jesus feels your feelings. Sympathy is to feel sorry for those who are hurting; empathy is the imaginative entrance into what others feel; and compassion combines both and then moves to do something about it.

Before we move on, one more word: to be a disciple of Jesus entails discipling our emotions. So that we can say with Andrew Crislip, “To be a Christian is to feel like a Christian” (Emotions, 3).

Our first point then is that Jesus sees you and your trauma, and that he feels your trauma. There is no distance between Jesus and you. He conquered that distance. Belinda Bauman says the “incarnation is the fullest expression of the empathy of God” (Brave Souls, 42).

Jesus sees the culture codes that shackle people in the trauma zone

A noticeable theme in Mt 8–9 is that Jesus comes into conflict with a series of social codes. I mention three.

8:29: spirit world codes. The redemptive work of Jesus meant going to battle with the devil and his demon underlings. Jesus’s work of redemption cannot be reduced to the individual’s spiritual relationship with God. No, the Gospels reveal a Jesus who goes into battle with the “captain of sinners” because “salvation is principally a work of deliverance, of redemption, of liberation” (Philip Ziegler, God’s Adversary, 105, 108).

9.20: body codes. The woman had been bleeding for twelve years. An impairment; probably a disability; and she could not go to the temple that entire time. But the text makes immediately clear that she could mix in society.

9:23–24: nature codes: “they laughed at him”. They laughed because death is death. Death is irreversible. The dead decay. But not with Jesus. For Jesus, death is sleep.

You may think the culture codes of our day are against you.

Because you are a woman with a calling that transcends someone’s culture codes.
Because your gender restricts you from the social groups where the decisions are made, where leadership roles are decided, and where power is gained.
Because your body blocks others from seeing you.
Because you are understood to be too young or too old to do this or that.
Because you have teenagers.
Because you don’t have enough money to join the group.
Because you’re emotional.
Because you work when the meetings are held.
Because your trauma is not hidden and makes others uncomfortable.

Let this be said: these culture codes restrict and even traumatize people.

Jesus proclaims kingdom redemption and liberation from codes that shackle people from liberation.

Jesus sees your trauma and where your faith wants to be

Hagar is who I’m thinking about. She was a slave to Sarai, Abram’s wife. Sarai suffered through the downsides of no children. She gives Hagar to Abram as a wife and then suggests to Abram to “go in to my slave” Hagar (Gen 16.2). Abram “listened” to Sarai. The birth of Hagar’s child was named Ishmael (“God hears”). Sarai then despised Hagar. She blamed both Abram and Hagar. Abram reminded Sarai that Hagar was in her power. She abused Hagar. Hagar ran away with her son, Ishmael.
Then the “angel of the Lord” found Hagar and promised her that the Lord would bless her with innumerable descendants. The angel also informed her that Ishmael would be at odds with “everyone.” Then Hagar uttered this word about God. She named God El-Roi, “God Seeing Me” (Goldingay). Then she utters, “Did I really see here, after he was seeing me?”
These are the words of someone who has experienced the profound presence of the God who sees us, who knows us, who can then go on seeing because they have been seen.

Trust matters. Trusting Jesus unshackles the trusting person. Trusting opens us to Jesus and to his kingdom redemption. Trusting is risky. Trusting grows over time. Trust opens a person both to their trauma and to the possibilities of it being healed.
Teresa Morgan: Trust is the action of putting something … in someone else’s hands … on the basis … that the other will respond positively…” (Trust in Atonement, 12).
Those redeemed by Jesus in these Gospels stories did not get healed because they had perfect or even mature faith. They got healed because they turned to Jesus and had the courage to look into his eyes for a new future.

8.2: “if you are willing”: wondering by believing
8:23–26: fearful but believing (little faith)
9.2-3: the faith of your friends
9:18: the faith of a parent
9:21, 22: physical faith: “If I only touch his cloak, I will be healed”
9:27, 28, 29: verbal faith: “have mercy on us, Son of David”; “do you believe?”; “Yes, Lord.”

You may think you don’t have enough faith; or that your faith has failed you; or that your faith is burned out and burned up; or that you don’t have the energy to trust again.

Jesus sees not enough faith, failed faith, burned and burned up faith, and a lack of energy as faith that is ready to trust all over again.

Jesus is willing to redeem your trauma

The magic of the Gospel stories is that those who turn to Jesus and have the courage to look into his eyes are unleashed to trust him. Trusting Jesus transforms us. Again, neither their trust nor ours is perfect or mature. It is simply and merely faith, or trust. Risky and scary and real and never perfect.

So, trust him…

… Even if you are wondering: 8.2 (“If you are willing”):
… Even if you have to question who he is: 8.27: “What kind of man is this?”
… Even in your faith, firm or not: 9:28

The operative moment in these two chapters occurs in the first passage. When the scaly-skinned man said, “Lord, if you are willing, you can make me clean,” Jesus said to him, “I am willing” (8.3).

The issue is not Jesus. He says to you and to me, “I am willing. Are you?” Do we have the courage to look into his eyes with trust that he can become for us the agent of trauma therapeutics? His eyes are facing you.

Here is the witness of the Gospels in our two chapters:

8:13: “Go! Let it be done.”
8:15: “He touched her hand and the fever left her, and she got up…”
8:16: “he drove out the spirits with a word
8:26: “he rebuked the winds and the waves, and it was completely calm.”
9:22: “Take heart, daughter, your faith has healed you.”
9:25: He “took the girl by the hand, and she got up.”
9:29: “He touched their eyes… and their sight was restored”; the touch of Jesus breaks the devil’s grip.
9.33: “the man who was speechless spoke”

By the powers of his empathy and compassion (8.17): his role. He identified with us, empathized with us, sympathized with us, and in his great love, compassion, and grace he can heal us.

Pope Leo said “The touch of compassion is the first medicine.”

I close with words from Hebrews, chapter two, verses fourteen to eighteen, which reveals a pure act of divine empathy:

For this reason he had to be made like them, fully human in every way, in order that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in service to God, and that he might make atonement for the sins of the people. Because he himself suffered when he was tempted, he is able to help those who are being tempted.

Jesus sees you because he’s been where you are.
