**wRC+ is the single most useful number for evaluating a hitter.** Not batting average. Not RBI. Not OPS, though OPS is at least asking the right questions. wRC+, Weighted Runs Created Plus, measures how much offensive value a hitter produces per plate appearance compared to the rest of the league, after adjusting for the ballpark he plays in and the run environment of the current era.

The number on the back of a baseball card lies to you. A .285 batting average sounds impressive. It might be. Or the hitter might play half his games in Coors Field in a season where the entire league was hitting .290. The raw number doesn’t know that. It doesn’t care.

wRC+ does. It is one number. It is ballpark adjusted, era adjusted, and every offensive action is accounted for. That’s why it’s the anchor stat here, and that’s why it’s worth understanding before we get into the season.

What Does wRC+ Actually Measure?

wRC+ measures how many runs a hitter creates per plate appearance, compared to the league average, with adjustments for ballpark and era baked in. The scale is designed to be intuitive:

That’s it. A 130 wRC+ means the hitter is 30% better than the average MLB hitter at creating runs. A 70 wRC+ means he’s 30% worse. The number travels across ballparks, across seasons, and across eras with the adjustments already done.

How wRC+ Is Calculated (Without Getting Lost in It)

You don’t need to memorize the formula to use the stat, but understanding the logic is worth a minute. A Quick Reference Guide is available here

It starts with wOBA (Weighted On-Base Average), which assigns a run value to every offensive event based on how many runs that event actually generates. A home run is worth more than a single. A walk is worth more than an out. Unlike batting average, which treats every hit equally, wOBA weights each outcome by what it actually produces in terms of runs.

From wOBA, the formula builds Weighted Runs Created (wRC), then adds two adjustments that traditional stats completely ignore:

**Park factor adjustment.** Hitting a ball 400 feet at Coors Field is not the same as hitting it 400 feet at Petco Park. The thin air in Denver inflates offense. The marine layer in San Diego suppresses it. wRC+ accounts for where the hitter plays his home games.

**League and era adjustment.** A .400 wOBA in the steroid era meant something different than a .400 wOBA in a lower-run environment like 2014 or 2025. wRC+ recalibrates so that 100 always equals the average hitter in that specific season.

The result gets multiplied by 100, which is why the scale centers on 100 instead of 1.00.

Three Players That Make wRC+ Click

Aaron Judge: What Historically Great Looks Like

Aaron Judge’s 2025 season produced a 204 wRC+. That means Judge was more than twice as productive as the average MLB hitter at creating runs. His .331/.457/.688 slash line, 53 home runs, and 18.3% walk rate all pointed in the same direction. A hitter operating in a tier almost no one has ever reached.

For context, Barry Bonds holds the all-time single-season record with a 233 wRC+ in 2004. The list of players who have ever posted a 200+ wRC+ season is historically short. Judge has done it multiple times. Over the last four seasons combined, he’s posted a 204 wRC+. That sustained level of production is essentially unprecedented in the modern game.

wRC+ is what makes it possible to say that clearly. Without it, you’re comparing slash lines across different parks and different offensive eras and getting nowhere. With it, the case makes itself.

Wyatt Langford: Reading a Development Curve

Wyatt Langford’s 2025 season looked modest on the surface. A .241/.344/.431 slash line over 134 games, a 118 wRC+ for the full year. Solid, not spectacular.

The wRC+ tells a more interesting story when you split it.

From the start of July through the end of the season, Langford posted a 135 wRC+. That would have ranked sixth among all American League hitters over a full season. He cut his infield fly ball rate from over 10% to under 3%. He halved his soft contact rate. Quality of contact, plate discipline, ballpark effects at a pitcher-friendly Globe Life Field. All of it pointed toward a hitter finding his swing.

A scout watching the full season slash line sees a good, developing player. The wRC+ trajectory shows something sharper. A 23-year-old with a legitimate case to be one of the better offensive players in the American League if the second half represents his true level.

Steamer projects him for a 124 wRC+ in 2026. ZiPS is more bullish. The number matters because it’s asking the right question.

Jose Ramirez: The Consistent Baseline

Jose Ramirez doesn’t make the headlines Judge does. He also doesn’t need to.

Over the past several seasons, Ramirez has been one of the most consistent offensive producers in the American League, routinely posting wRC+ marks in the 137-166 range. His game doesn’t follow a traditional power-hitter profile. He hits for average, draws walks, and generates extra-base hits at a high rate without the dramatic home run totals that dominate highlight reels.

Traditional stats undersell him. wRC+ captures him accurately. It sees through the surface-level noise and measures what actually matters: how often does this at-bat result in runs, relative to what everyone else is producing?

The answer, consistently, is: a lot more than average.

Why wRC+ Over OPS or OPS+?

OPS (On-Base Plus Slugging) is the most widely used advanced stat in mainstream baseball coverage. It’s a significant improvement over batting average. It’s also imprecise in ways that matter.

OPS adds two numbers that aren’t measured in the same units. On-base percentage and slugging percentage don’t scale the same way, so a point of OBP and a point of SLG don’t contribute equally to run scoring. OPS treats them as if they do.

wOBA, which underlies wRC+, weights each offensive outcome by its actual run value. A walk versus a single versus a home run each gets its true weight, not an approximated one.

OPS+ makes a ballpark adjustment but uses a different methodology that’s less precise than what wRC+ applies. If you’re already using OPS+, wRC+ gives you the same interpretive framework with better math underneath it.

The practical difference isn’t enormous on a game-by-game basis. Over a career, or when comparing players across very different parks, it compounds.

Common Mistakes When Using wRC+

Trusting small samples. A 160 wRC+ over 80 plate appearances in April is interesting. It’s not evidence of anything reliable. wRC+ stabilizes around 300-400 plate appearances for most hitting outcomes. Before that threshold, treat the number as directionally useful, not definitive.

Ignoring position. A 110 wRC+ means something different depending on where the hitter plays. Middle infielders and catchers with 110+ are offensive contributors. Corner outfielders and first basemen with 110 are closer to replacement level at the offensive side of their ledger.

Using it alone. wRC+ measures offensive value only. A 90 wRC+ hitter who plays Gold Glove defense at shortstop might be worth more than a 115 wRC+ hitter who is a defensive liability in left field. Layer in defensive metrics like DRS and OAA, plus baserunning value, to get the full picture.

Forgetting it’s context-neutral. A bases-loaded double with two outs in the ninth counts the same as a bases-empty double in a blowout. wRC+ doesn’t reward clutch situations or penalize production in low-leverage moments. That’s a feature, not a bug. It measures the hitter’s skill, not his luck about when he happened to get hits.

How I Use wRC+ in My Analysis

Every hitting evaluation I publish this season will reference wRC+ as the anchor for offensive value. When I’m building a case that a hitter is outperforming his contract or quietly sliding toward a down year, wRC+ adjusted for park factors is where the argument starts.

The reason is simple. I want to know what a hitter actually contributed, not what his context made him look like. A guy hitting .280 in a bandbox with runners on base constantly is not the same as a guy hitting .265 at Globe Life Field with a below-average lineup around him. wRC+ sorts that out.
