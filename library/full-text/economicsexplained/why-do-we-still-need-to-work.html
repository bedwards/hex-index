When Cynes in the dreary midst of the Great Depression wrote an exciting essay predicting a future where people worked three hours a day and the biggest problem was how to occupy the leisure, people's imaginations ran wild dreaming of a utopia that their great grandchildren would enjoy. But it didn't come true, at least not all of it. He did get some things right though. For example, he claimed that the standard of life in progressive countries 100 years hence would be between 4 and 8 times as high as it is today.

And in 2011, real GDP per person in the UK was a little over five times higher than 1931 levels, and most other advanced economies have grown by even more. As he predicted, technological innovations and capital accumulation drove this growth. And to be fair to him, average work hours have indeed fallen in most advanced economies since 1929. So, it is true that technological driven gains in productivity have afforded us one, increased living standards, and two, less work actually required to achieve it.

But the reduction in work hours has been nowhere near as dramatic as Kane's predicted. Plus, if we look closer, we can actually see a concerning pattern. Most of the reduction in working hours actually occurred ages ago. In the middle of the 20th century, from 1980 to now, annual work hours have barely budged in most of these countries, notably in the US and the UK.

And this data only shows average hours per worker. It doesn't show total labor force participation. In 1948, in the USA, just 32% of working age women worked. Now, it's around 58%.

So the average worker might be working slightly less hours but a high percentage of people are working compared to the mid- 20th century. And this begs the question, so theoretically we can afford to work less. So why don't we? Well, one theory is that when we are faced with a real wage increase, we recalculate.

Here two counteracting forces are at play. Firstly, the wage increase makes it easier to reach the income of a targeted standard of living. This income effect may spur individuals to choose less work hours and more leisure. But the second effect works the other way.

When your wage increases, the opportunity cost of leisure, that is the income sacrificed by choosing not to do work, is now higher. This substitution effect means leisure costs more, which incentivizes individuals to actually increase their working hours, perhaps even offloading other domestic tasks like cooking and cleaning so they can focus on performing more paid hours. This also creates formal jobs for other people in the service industry. What seems to have happened over the last century is that this substitution effect has been bigger and the income effect smaller than what can implicitly assumed.

Individuals have chosen not to reduce their working hours because the income sacrifice when doing so is much larger than it used to be. In a way, this makes sense. New technologies haven't just improved productivity. They've also created new goods and services, increasing consumer demands, and in turn our incentives to earn income through work.

Eating at restaurants, having a car, holidaying abroad, phones, even basic central heating and washing machines. None of these things were available or common in the 1930s. The point is, although our real wages have increased, so too have our minimum standards for what we consider a decent and dignified quality of life. The quality of life that for most individuals earning minimum wage or even median salary necessitates full-time employment.

In this sense, we can't afford to work less, at least on an individual level. There are, of course, other factors, though. As Canes himself recognized, humans, like all animals, are evolutionarily hardwired to work. In simpler days, if you didn't hunt, forage, or build shelter, you'd literally die.

Our natural insistence to preoccupy ourselves with work is baked deeply in our DNA and remain solidly in place. Long hours are also, for better or worse, seen as virtuous, whilst part-time work is often stigmatized, viewed as a lack of commitment, and humans are competitive, status driven. How successful someone is often equates to their material riches. Ultimately, what we've seen is that as an economy, we've chosen to work more and consume more rather than work less and consume less.

Technological innovation has created new consumer demands and Kane's underestimated this. But because our productivity is now so high, there's not a lot of honest, tangible work left to actually do. What we're left with are economies where the non-essential sectors make up the bulk of GDP. And that's fine.

But within a lot of these sectors, the work being done has questionable value. Like writing reports that no one will read, delivering powerpoints that do not affect decision-making, or even entire roles purely existing because of bureaucracy that could be considered unnecessary. So although popular, Graber's theory of jobs is vigorously contested and critiqued. An EU based study rejects this theory altogether. A basic counterargument is that value is subjective.

If you're being paid to work in a particular role, then there's demand for that role and therefore it has value and meaning even if it makes you feel like an NPC or a cog in the machine. Critics provide alternative explanations for self-reported meaninglessness. But how can pointless tasks and time wasting exist in the private sector where market competition and profit motives are supposed to weed out inefficiencies? One answer is that these inefficiencies are hidden. In conventional economic thought, the employees income and probability that they will be retained is a function of their marginal product of labor.

That is the additional revenue the company earns from hiring the employee. The higher an individual's marginal product is, the higher their salary and lower their chances of being fired are. But in reality, it's often extremely difficult to accurately attribute company revenues to individual employees. If a mechanic fixes three cars per day at an average price of $100, then it's clear to work out their marginal product of labor.

But with a remote worker in a more collaborative supporting role, it's less clear. Even if we knew they responded to six emails, updated three spreadsheets, drafted five presentation slides, and attended five meetings in a day, the revenue production from those activities is so indirect that it's very difficult to calculate the true value. That's why many companies use time sheets where employees record what they spend their time on each day, mapping that to revenue generating client agreements. But this creates perverse incentives.

Employees can simply exaggerate the time it took them to complete client work in order to increase the utilization rate. Typically a key performance indicator. Obviously exaggerate too much and questions will be asked, but let's be honest, it can be pretty easy to get away with. This is nicely captured by Parkinson's law.

Work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion. So accurately calculating the marginal product of labor isn't usually feasible. Employers approximate and employees know this. So if they're not busy, they need to at least ensure that they look busy.

The end result is that in many office environments, presence equals performance. And the theatrical display of looking busy is valued more than your actual output. Employers are being deceived and aren't getting the most out of their workforce, whilst employees are incentivized to engage in productivity theater and spend huge portions of their working life essentially doing nothing. Now, could the solution be a 4-day work week? Between 2022 and 2024, different versions of 4-day work week pilots took place all across the world. They mostly followed the 180 100 rule.

That is, employees maintained 100% of their salary, worked 80% of what they previously did, and maintained 100% of their output. The largest pilot was in the UK, which included 61 companies and 2,900 workers. Overall, it worked. Revenue stayed the same, even increased in some cases.

Productivity increased significantly. Costs were lowered since staff turnover reduced and sick leave plummeted. The well-being of employees improved, and team morale increased. 92% of participating companies continued the 4-day work week after the trial ended, and similarly positive results were found in other countries.

The businesses also benefited from attracting more top talent from their respective labor markets, improving their human capital and productivity. These pilots show that Parkinson's law exists, that work does expand to fill the time available to do it. If you reduce the available time, much of the bloated, unnecessary or unfocused activity simply vanishes by necessity. Employees can no longer afford to pretend to be busy or engage in pointless tasks because if you don't produce the same output in 4 days, then the penalty will be reverting back to the 5-day pattern again.

Now, a 4-day work week probably doesn't suit all sectors, particularly where output has stricter relationship with time, like manufacturing, or where services need to be provided 5 days or more, like healthcare, education, retail, or hospitality. And where it may be possible, a one-sizefits-all Fridays off approach won't necessarily be the solution. There are also major challenges and drawbacks that haven't been fully considered. Sectors that move to a 4-day work week would receive increased labor supply from people attracted to this better work life balance.

This risks pushing down wages in 4-day work week jobs. It would also increase the inequality of working hours across the economy, creating a two-tier system between blue collar and key workers who remain working 5 days and cushy white collar office workers who work less days without a pay cut. It's easy to see how resentment could grow amongst those who remain working 5 days. And perhaps fairly, they may demand better compensation for their relatively longer hours.

Furthermore, if the newfound attractiveness of 4-day work week sectors reduces labor supply to 5-day work week sectors, then natural market pressure to increase 5-day work week wages would add to the political movement for fairer compensation. Then there's the practical side of things. In many cases, employees may have too much to squeeze into just 4 days. If their previous output can't be maintained, then businesses may have to hire additional employees or pay overtime to maintain capacity.

This would mean higher costs, which businesses would offset by reducing wages or increasing prices. In other words, inflation. This is still a real risk despite the early success stories because participation in the 4-day work week pilots was voluntary. The companies who registered to take part were the types of companies where it was most likely to succeed.

So, the positive results are not generalizable to all businesses in the economy. In social research, this is called self- selection bias. Nevertheless, even when taking this major limitation into account, the results do provide compelling evidence that the 4-day working week can work for some businesses. This warrants further exploration.

Furthermore, it would be silly to say that we shouldn't have it at all just because it doesn't work across the board. And with other new dynamics in the labor market like artificial intelligence improving at accelerating rates, the automation of work is set to continue, maybe even speed up. Meaning in theory, we could use the productivity improvements to create more free time for ourselves as Kane's originally envisioned. Given the stagnating of working hours in the last few decades, drastic change is unlikely to happen.

That said, the longerterm trend seems to favor the 4-day work week. We slowly transitioned from 14-hour 6-day weeks during the industrial revolution to the now standard 8 hour 5day weeks despite fierce resistance at the time. The shift became the norm and we can all agree that we're better off for it. But since the mid- 20th century, there's been no groundbreaking reforms despite the productivity gains.

That's why the biggest reason we might still be working so much might simply be inertia. Nudging and supporting more organizations to at least consider a 4-day working week is the only way systematic change to working hours could possibly occur. Time is a resource like any other, a finite one, and arguably the most valuable one that we have. The more of it we have to spend on what we want, then the higher our life satisfaction and fulfillment becomes.
