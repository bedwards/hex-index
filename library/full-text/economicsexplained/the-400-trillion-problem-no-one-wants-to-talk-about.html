Most pension systems around the world run on what's called a pay as you go model. The money you pay in today doesn't go into a vault with your name on it. It goes straight to today's retirees. Then when it's your turn, the next generation of workers fund your retirement.

This system worked well enough in the decades after World War II because economies were growing, birth rates were high, and there were plenty of workers to support every retiree. In 1950, the United States had more than five times as many workers for every retiree as they have now. And that meant a massive base of contributors supporting a relatively small number of people collecting benefits. And remember that's also including the influx of women entering the workforce. So the change is even more drastic than the numbers alone would suggest. By 2035, it's expected to fall even further to 2.3. We're seeing this trend everywhere across Europe, Japan, and South Korea.
