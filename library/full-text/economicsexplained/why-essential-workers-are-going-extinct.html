On paper, becoming a teacher or a doctor still looks like one of the safest bets you can make. By contrast, the median full-time UK worker earns roughly £39,000 a year before tax.

In other advanced economies, the pattern is similar. Across the OECD, teachers earn statutory salaries that cluster around 56,000 [music] to $64,000 depending on the country and the education level they teach. That puts teacher pay around or slightly above the OECD's average annual wage, which sits at roughly $61,000. Doctors go even further.

Schools and hospitals are built on the assumption that teachers and doctors will always be there. Yet, across rich countries, that assumption is on a collision course with reality. Despite all the apparent advantages, schools and hospitals are finding it harder to stay fully staffed.

For most of the past century, the way rich countries supplied essential workers, followed a fairly predictable pattern. Teachers and doctors were trained domestically, usually through heavily subsidized public systems because governments understood these roles as core national infrastructure rather than ordinary market jobs. Each year, a new cohort of graduates would enter classrooms and hospitals while older workers gradually retired. As long as the number coming in roughly matched the number leaving, the system stayed in balance.

Schools remained staffed, hospitals kept functioning, and shortages were rare and usually temporary. That model worked because the underlying demographics supported it. Back then, populations were younger, birth rates were high enough to guarantee a steady flow of future workers, and demand grew slowly enough for systems to keep up. Training capacity didn't need to expand aggressively because replacement was gradual and largely self-sustaining.

But now, the system is breaking down. Across almost every high-income country, populations are aging rapidly, which puts pressure on healthcare from two directions at once. Many doctors are approaching retirement just as the population itself is getting older. In countries like Japan, Italy, and Germany, more than 1 in five people is already over the age of 65.

And that share continues to rise. And older societies need more health care, not less, because people live longer and spend a greater share of their lives managing chronic conditions. At the same time, birth rates across developed countries have fallen well below the replacement level of 2.1 children per woman. In countries such as South Korea, Italy, Spain, and Japan, fertility rates now sit closer to 1.2 or lower.

In practical terms, this means fewer young people entering the workforce just as demand for teachers and doctors is accelerating. So, the obvious question becomes, why don't we train more of them? The problem is that training new teachers takes years and training doctors often takes a decade or more from university entry to full qualification.

That means the shortages visible today are not the result of recent policy failures but of decisions made 10 sometimes 20 years ago like how many training sites to fund or how much strain these systems could be expected to absorb. When retirement waves arrive there is no way to accelerate the pipeline quickly enough to compensate and the pandemic just made this timing problem worse. But the deeper problem is that these shortages aren't just about timing. They're about incentives. While teachers and doctors earn above average wages, in many countries they earn less than other professions requiring similar levels of education, responsibility, and time investment. Also, public sector pay structures tend to place a ceiling on earnings over the course of a career with defined salary bands that contrast with private sector professions where pay can continue to scale with experience, performance, or market demand.

At the same time, getting into these professions has become a longer and more expensive process [music] with higher student debt, rising housing costs in the cities where the work actually is, and a payoff that comes only after many years [music] of training. Day-to-day work has also become more demanding. Long hours, administrative overload, and high responsibility have become normal, particularly in healthcare, where staff shortages feed directly into heavier workloads for those who remain. For many high-erforming students, private sector alternatives often offer faster financial rewards, more flexibility, and fewer constraints, making teaching and medicine comparatively less attractive despite their long-term stability.

So, the result is a slow compounding erosion. Fewer people begin training, more leave earlier than expected, and the workforce ages faster than it can be replaced. So, how have governments managed this problem until now? Payraises and retention policies have helped, but when faced with shortages they can't fix quickly, most governments have reached for the same lever.

If you can't train enough teachers and doctors at home, you bring them in from elsewhere. Migration is a faster fix than expanding universities. It's also cheaper than raising pay across the public sector and far easier politically than long-term reform. So, governments redesigned their visa systems to compete for essential workers.

Doctors and nurses were fast-tracked with processing times shortened compared with standard skilled visas. In the UK, that led to tens of thousands of arrivals in a single year. In 2022 alone, over 73,000 health and care workers were sponsored on skilled visas, and the number continued to rise into early 2023. So, in the short term, visa changes did what they were meant to do.

Over the past decade, this approach allowed schools and hospitals in developed countries to keep running without sudden spikes in class cancellations and hospital waiting lists. Even as some countries expanded training opportunities or experimented with retention bonuses, migration remained the fastest way to relieve pressure in the short term. The problem is that migration doesn't create new workers. It just moves them around.

So when high-income countries recruit teachers and doctors trained abroad, they're often pulling talent out of places that already have shortages of their own. In theory, that should at least protect developed countries from shortages. But in practice, it rarely does for long. Migrant professionals are less tied to a single system and more willing to move again once better opportunities appear, which means migration can keep filling gaps, but it struggles to build a stable workforce.

Many arrive, gain experience, and do essential work.
