Bologna was the first stop on our way to Ann’s writers’ workshop outside Sienna last November. Ann had yet to see the birthplace of mortadella, and it had been 15 years since I was there researching salumi with Brian Polcyn. Hearing of our plan, Ruth Reichl told us about the late Jonathan Gold, peerless food writer; Gold’s favorite restaurant in all the world was All’ Osteria Bottega, she said. So, well, we had to go. Was Jonathan ever wrong? Not that I’m aware of.

It’s a humble, family restaurant, wood chairs with straw seats, wood tables, maybe 40 seats. The three lovely pastas, roasted potatoes, cardoons(!), and pork tartare were all outstanding (Ann wouldn’t get near the tartare). But it was the Secondi Piatta that stood out: Cotoletta di Vitello in osso alla “Petroniana”: veal cutlet on the bone in the style of Petroiana, sometimes simply called cotoletta di Bolognese. “I know what I’m getting,” Ann exclaimed the moment she saw it on the menu.

It’s Italian simple—a schnitzel with ham and cheese. But none of us knew this particular Italian preparation. It is so beloved, there’s a FB page called friends of Petronaina, Amici della Petroniana, devoted to the dish.

We’d flown to Florence from JFK the night before, had driven an hour and a half to Bologna, skipped any kind of nap, restored ourselves with wine and a plate of guanciale and salumi rosa, then wandered the markets till it was time to freshen up for dinner. We arrived at the little restaurant in that lovely, hazy, excited state of being in a new city after much travel and no sleep. Ann experienced a kind of exotic rapture as she devoured the breaded, fried, bone-in chop.

“I have to learn to make this,” she said.

And this is how great dishes nudge themselves into your life’s repertoire of repeatable recipes. Part of the repertoire because it is so deeply satisfying—a breaded fried cutlet, topped with prosciutto and cloaked in melty Parmigiano Reggiano. Repeatable because, like so much of the Italian repertoire, it’s dead simple. Yes, you’ve got to flour, egg wash, and bread the cutlets, but that’s really the only extra effort required and boy is it worth it. We’ll be making this a lot.

She’s made it twice now, and while she’s back in Providence to finish her novel in solitude and teach a workshop, I had time to create a proper recipe I’m excited to share. There are plenty of recipes on the internet—but I wanted one that hewed to our particular tastes. One reputable cook does it with chicken. Oy. If you only eat chicken breast, fine, but I wouldn’t call it alla Bolognese. You could bread and fry a cauliflower “steak,” top it with prosciutto and reggiano—I’m sure that would be delicious but definitely not in the style of Bologna!

Bologna is the capital city of the Emilia-Romanga region, famous both for its prosciutto di Parma and its Parmagiano-Reggiano, so it’s a natural pairing. Bone-in chops cook better because of the bone, and the bone makes for a more impressive presentation. But off the bone is acceptable. In the US, where veal is not easy to find, pork is typically used. Which is how we found it last week—to our great surprise—at Il Gigante in Ridgewood, Queens. We’ve never seen it on a menu here and asked where the chef, Davide Bugamelli, was from. “Bologna,” our server told us.

While all kinds of variations are possible, one aspect of the dish seems pretty consistent across the board. All cotolettas in this style finish the cutlet in a small amount of broth. In a covered pan, its steam helps melt the cheese and the broth tenderizes the cutlet without drying it out.

In the northern region of Emilia-Romagna, the cooking fat would traditionally have been butter or lard. I think butter is the best choice for flavor, but you can use olive oil or vegetable oil. I like a mixture of butter and olive oil for the way it browns without overcooking the butter.

There are two strategies, described in the two photos above and below: simply allowing the cheese to melt over the prosciutto (top) or—and if you’re a restaurant this makes the pickup a lot more efficient—making a Parmigiano-Reggiano sauce, basically a mornay using reggiano, which you can spoon over the cutlet (below).

One last note: if it’s November and you’re in Italy, shave some truffles over it. That’s classic.

In the 1970s, restaurants in Cleveland sometimes served “pork chops Italianne”—or at least that’s what my dad called it. He LOVED “pork chops Italianne.” He even made it once at home—pork chops coated with Progresso “Italian-style” breadcrumbs, stuffed with ham and cheese and baked. I’m sure it was deli ham, not prosciutto, and the cheese was processed Swiss (like a Midwestern ham and cheese sandwich). But I wonder if its provenance can be found in Bologna’s Cotoletta Petroniana. Wish my dear dad were around so I could make the original for him.

Cotoletta di Petroniana: A Recipe

The following recipe is for two, but it’s easily doubled or quadrupled as you wish. If you’re making this for four or more, you can simplify the pan situation by frying the pork chops two or three at a time; top them with prosciutto and reggiano; next, put a sheet pan with a 1/4 inch of broth in the oven then turn the oven to 300˚F.; when the oven is heated, put the chops on the sheet pan and turn the oven to broil; serve when the cheese is melted.

I like to serve the cutlets with a salad and mashed potatoes.

Ingredients

  • 2 eight-ounce bone-in pork chops, with the meat pounded to half it’s original thickness (ask your butcher to do this for you if don’t have a meat mallet)

  • Salt to taste

  • Pepper to taste

  • Flour for dredging

  • 2 eggs beaten until uniformly mixed

  • Breadcrumbs (I use panko for the crunch)

  • 2 ounces butter (4 tablespoons), plus, optionally, another ounce to add to the broth

  • 1 ounce olive oil (2 tablespoons)

  • 1/2 cup beef broth (or chicken or vegetable)

  • 3 or 4 large, paper-thin slices of prosciutto (about 3 ounces)

  • Parmigiano-Reggiano as needed (about 2 ounces)

  • 2 healthy squeezes of lemon (optional)

Season the cutlets with salt and pepper. Dredge them in flour, shaking off excess. Dip the cutlets in the egg, then in the bread crumbs so that they’re well breaded.

In a large sauté pan with a lid, heat the butter and olive oil over medium high heat. When the fat is hot, fry the chops until they’re golden brown, a couple minutes for each side (they will cook again in broth). Remove them to a plate.

Pour off any fat in the pan and return it to the burner. Add the broth to the pan. As it heats, lay a sheet of prosciutto on each, and top the prosciutto with finely grated Parmigiano-Reggiano.

When the broth has come to a simmer, add the extra ounce of butter if using and swirl it in, then add the cutlets to the pan and cover. Cook until the cheese is melted.

I like to give the chops a squeeze of lemon and some freshly cracked black pepper to serve. Feel free to sprinkle any remaining cheese on top as well.

A fan letter, old school …

I get a gratifying amount of gracious emails about my work. But last week I got a fan letter. Like I haven’t gotten in 20 years.

I received an envelope from my agent, which contained another envelope. The second envelope had been addressed to her agency, c/o my name. Actual fan mail. Fan snailmail. This rarely happens anymore. The envelope and the letter had been typed. I just looked at it as if it were an actual relic, and not a missive typed a week earlier. Ann did the same.

It’s always sweet to get fan mail. We write in solitude and send books out into space and we don’t know who they’re going affect, or even if they’ll affect anyone at all.

But holding a hard copy of the letter, the type so deep into the paper I could feel the impression of the letters, I thought, “Someone took the time to roll a sheet of paper into a typewriter, compose a letter, correct it in pen, find the address of my agent, type the envelope, stamp and mail the thing.” I don’t know. It gave me the sense, for a moment, that maybe the world isn’t falling apart as fast as it sometimes seems.

Thank you, Josh Hinkle. Please know that your words are appreciated. You’ll be receiving a handwritten note shortly. Connection is all.

What we’re drinking …

The Pink Lady! The weather is so fine, the leaves have come out full force, the tulips in Abingdon Square are electric red and yellow, and I felt like a pretty spring cocktail. A Pink Lady was just the thing. How often do you have one of these? Make one for your partner and enjoy the spring air.

This seems to be one of those cocktails, like The Bee’s Knees, that was born of the terrible gin made during Prohibition and ingredients that could mask its flavor. Here applejack, lemon juice, grenadine and an egg white turn plain old gin into a heady, pastel-colored, protein shake.

The Pink Lady for two:

  • 3 ounces London Dry gin

  • 1 ounce applejack

  • 1-1/2 ounces lemon juice, freshly squeezed

  • 1/4 ounce grenadine

  • 1 egg white

  • Possible garnish: brandied cherry, a scattering of pomegranate seeds, or even a raspberry

Combine the cocktail ingredients in a shaker and give it a good, solid shake to break up the egg white. Add ice and shake ten or twenty times. Strain into a Nick and Nora glass. Garnish as desired.

Notes: I made an ersatz grenadine using this recipe from Epicurious (white sugar is fine to use if you don’t have demerara). Here’s a recipe that uses whole pomegranates. Also, I didn’t have any applejack on hand, so I used a Cognac. Delicious. Maybe we should call this one a Fallen Pink Lady?

What we’re watching …

We were eager to watch the filmed version of Merrily We Roll Along with Jonathan Groff and Daniel Radcliffe, which we’d seen at NYTW and which went on to win a Tony for its Broadway revival last year. If you love Sondheim and musical theater, you’ll appreciate the uncommonly intimate vantage of a screen. Unlike at the theater, the characters’ nuanced expressions are magnified, as is the meaning of the play. So what you remember as a complex, jaunty-tuned, ever-clever musical about the disintegration of the friendship between three artists (a composer, a lyricist, a novelist), is really a truly dark tale of trading artistic integrity for fame and money and personal pleasure.

The storyline runs in reverse time. We begin at the dark end when fame has consumed the main character, Frank the composer. Alcohol has consumed his dear novelist pal, Mary, and the lyricist, Charley, has remained true to his artistic ideals but has little to show for it. By the time we get to the end of the show, when all the characters meet and are filled with optimism, artistic dreams and hope for the future, it’s painfully poignant because you’ve already watched what will happen to them.

We were willing to take a shot on Gore Verbinski’s Good Luck, Have Fun, Don’t Die even though it sounded ridiculous: a man dressed in wires, tubes, and other accessories favored by the homeless appears in a diner explaining that he is from the future and he must take back a group of people or the world will end—any volunteers? Oh, and this is the 117th time he’s done this. The man is played by Sam Rockwell, reason alone to watch the movie. But Ann checked out of this comic-horror-sci-fi bizarro flick with its apocalyptic vision of a world addicted to its phones. I only stuck with it a day later because we forked over $19.99 to stream it and I wanted to see what happened. Vastly not worth the money or time.

American Classic, written by Bob Martin, who wrote Slings and Arrows, a dark comedy about a fictional Shakespearian theater troupe, and The Drowsy Chaperone, a Tony-winning musical that lovingly mocks 1920s jazz-age theater tropes, does similar comic work with Our Town as the play that anchors the 8-part series starring Kevin Kline and Laura Linney. It’s outrageous and fun, a story with all kinds of twists, and it works beautifully on multiple levels. But simply watching Kline, as a washed up Broadway star returning to his small Midwestern hometown, perform Shakespeare in a bank is worth the price of admission. (Which is free with a 7-day trial subscription to MGM+, which is how we watched it.)

Fantasy Life is a small indy rom-com that takes place in Manhattan and Martha’s Vineyard about a depressed young man prone to panic attacks who becomes the babysitter, or manny, of the children of a musician and an actress in her fifties who hasn’t acted in a decade. After a very slow and quiet beginning, the movie picks up steam when the babysitter, early thirties, falls in love with the mom and the mom doesn’t discourage his affection. We loved this movie.
