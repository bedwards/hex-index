Ann asked to make tomato soup and grilled cheese sandwiches for my first home-cooked meal after knee replacement surgery nearly two weeks ago. Ultimate comfort.

I hadn’t had this quintessentially American pairing in years, and it was a pleasure.

When I was a boy in Cleveland, Mom would sometimes have this lunch waiting for me when I’d come in from shoveling the driveway on a snowy Saturday: heaven. Hot rich Campbell’s Tomato Soup—she used a canful of milk, pronounced “melk” if you’re from Detroit, instead of a can full of water—Wonder Bread buttered on the outside, browned in a pan till it’s crunchy, with gooey Kraft singles in between. Fruity acidic soup with the rich cheese and crunchy fried bread. In the immortal words of Neil Diamond, So Good!

It was early April when I hobbled up the stairs to our flat, cold outside, and Ann’s preparation was a great winter’s look toward the bounty of tomatoes in the summer. She uses a mix of canned tomatoes and whole tomatoes that she roasts till charred—riffing off a recipe by Ina—all of it cooked with fresh herbs and blended smooth. Everyone knows winter tomatoes from the grocery store are facsimiles of what tomatoes should be. But canned, well, that’s what you use in the winter. Tomatoes that were picked at the peak of ripeness and canned that same day.

recently wrote a terrific Substack about canned tomatoes in winter (In Defense of Canned Tomatoes). It’s an excellent account of why canned are better than store-bought tomatoes, why they perform better, how they’re canned, why San Marzano tomatoes really are the best.

Rule: Always buy whole peeled tomatoes.

Fun Fact: Classic Neapolitan pizza calls for canned San Marzano’s, not fresh.

Pro Tip: Sometimes tomato sauce can be too acidic; many recipes call for balancing this with a bit of sugar. Brennan says, no—instead add a little baking soda, an alkaline salt that raises the pH without changing the texture or masking the tomato flavor. A 1/4 teaspoon is plenty for a medium sized pot.

And Brennan finishes off the tomato newsletter with what may be the simplest tomato sauce there is, just three ingredients: olive oil, garlic, and canned tomatoes (more on this in the recipes below).

Caveat Emptor … the insidious marketers are tireless …

I don’t know how long it took me to read the label, but it was years before I actually looked at the words above and saw that transposed over the perfect San Marzano-shaped plum tomatoes are not the words San Marzano, but rather “San Merican Tomatoes.” I’m sure the company will hold a collective hand to their breast and say, “We of course intended this as an homage to San Marzano tomatoes.”

These are California grown and packed tomatoes. And thanks for clarifying “no salt”—as if there is usually salt in canned tomatoes. There shouldn’t be. I’m surprised they didn’t slap “Gluten free!” and “Fat free!” on there as well.

All this being said, The Times’ Wirecutter recently chose its four favorite canned tomatoes, one of which is the SMT brand, and only one of which is San Marzano DOP. My advice: taste and decide for yourself! Just be aware of what you’re buying.

The history of tomato soup with grilled cheese …

When Ann said she was making tomato soup, I thought, what a perfect time for this dish, with the weather going from weirdly hot to freezing. A perfect time to appreciate canned tomatoes and the great Tomato Soup and Grilled Cheese combo while we wait for glorious summer tomatoes. (It got weirdly hot this week in NYC; remember tomato soup is excellent chilled.)

The tomato-soup-grilled-cheese pairing is truly an American classic, born of this country’s great contribution to the world of gastronomy: shelf-stable processed foods and mechanical innovation.

Here, three American innovations came together in 1928 to cement the dish into our culinary culture.

  1. Campbell’s Tomato Soup, 1895. Tomatoes, as we know, can really well (though the first processed soups were jarred). And that’s how the company began. Canned tomatoes were less expensive than fresh produce, with no compromise in nutrition. After being in the canned vegetable business for 26 years, the company began offering its first heat-and-serve soup.

  2. James Kraft introduced processed “cheese” in 1916. Taking innovations from Swiss chemists (1911), Kraft mixed bits of cheddar cheese with emulsifying salts, which bound the fat and the water so that the cheese didn't separate when melted. The emulsified cheese could be poured into loaf molds to set up to a texture that could be sliced. It got a great business boost from WWI; this cheese was easy to transport to American troops—we entered the war in April 1917—and didn’t spoil quickly .

We always have this on hand but don’t use it for grilled cheese. We keep it to use to enrich instant ramen—delicious and melts beautifully into the soup.

Note the above photo: nowhere on the package is it ever called cheese. Cheese is not even mentioned except as one of the ingredients. It is simply “Kraft Singles,” and below this, “white American.” It does say “no artificial flavors” (as though that would matter to someone using the product) and “a great source of calcium.”

Kraft had been slicing cheese for years before 1956, when Arnold Nawrocki, working at a Pennsylvania cheese company, invented a machine that individually wrapped the sticky cheese slices in polymer-coated cellophane. The company couldn’t really convey the import of their innovation. But in 1965, Kraft invented its own and brought “Kraft Singles” to market.

And 3, Sliced bread was introduced to America by a Missouri baking company in July, 1928, thanks to a machine invented by Otto Frederick Rohwedder. After a year or so, the whole country embraced the product as one of the best things ever.

The Great Depression brought these three innovations in food technology together in the grilled cheese with tomato soup school lunch or an inexpensive, satisfying, and deeply comforting dinner during hard times.

In the end, what we have come to deplore—processed food—gives us one of the world’s greatest pairings. Of course this would in turn lead to our using a good farmhouse cheddar or a melty gruyere instead of processed cheese, sliced sourdough bread, and our own tomato soup. So all’s well the ends well!

The two excellent tomato soup recipes …

I’ll start with Chef Brennan’s 3-Ingredient tomato soup because it is so simple and quick—15 minutes start to finish. I loved it. It was too garlicky for Ann, who said it should be called Tomato-Garlic Soup. One small can of tomatoes and half as much olive oil, seasoned with garlic, s & p—that’s it. Brennan offers the option of thinning with water or veg stock, but I like the purity of just water.

Adapted from the newsletter Terrance Brennan Home Kitchen.

  • 1 cup extra-virgin olive oil

  • 4 medium garlic cloves, thinly sliced

  • 1 (16-ounce) can whole peeled tomatoes, preferably D.O.P. San Marzano

  • Salt, to taste

  • Freshly ground black pepper, to taste

  • Water or Vegetable stock as needed

Add half the olive oil to a saucepan over medium heat, then add the garlic and cook until the garlic is just golden brown around the edges (careful: it can go from brown to burnt in a blink).

Add the tomatoes to the pan with a good pinch of salt and simmer 10 minutes. Add some fresh, finely ground pepper.

Using a hand blender or a countertop blender, puree till smooth. As you do so, add the remaining olive oil in a thin stream so that it is emulsified into the tomatoes. Chef Brennan likes to serve this at the consistency of heavy cream: if it’s too thin, simmer briefly till reduced; if too thick, add water.

Serve with grilled cheese sandwiches.

Serves 4

For a more herbal variation, Brennan gives two suggestions: add chopped basil before blending, or make a rosemary sachet and simmer with the tomatoes, removing it from the tomatoes before blending.

The Promiscuous Reader, , adapts her favorite Ina Garten tomato soup:

This recipe combines both roasted fresh tomatoes and canned tomatoes for a more complex flavor. It’s also got loads of fragrant fresh basil and thyme, as well as sautéed onion, with vegetable or chicken broth. It’s deeply comforting, especially as you brace yourself for the nerve blockers shot into your knee to wear off.

  • 2 pounds ripe plum tomatoes, cut in half lengthwise

  • 1/4 cup plus 2 tablespoons good olive oil

  • 1 tablespoon kosher salt

  • 1 1/2 teaspoons freshly ground black pepper

  • 1 cup chopped yellow onions (1 onion)

  • 2 garlic cloves, minced

  • 1 (28-ounce) canned San Marzano whole tomatoes, with their juice

  • 2 cups fresh basil leaves, packed

  • 1 teaspoon fresh thyme leaves

  • 1 quart chicken stock or water

  • 1/2 cup half-and-half

Toss the halved tomatoes with 1/4 cup olive oil, add salt and pepper, and roast in a 425˚F oven till they’re well-charred, 45 minutes or so, rotating halfway through. Remove from the oven.

In a pot over medium heat, add the olive oil and sauté the onion till well-softened, 5 to 10 minutes. Add the minced garlic and sauté just until softened. Add the remaining ingredients and simmer for 40 minutes. Blend or pass the mixture through a food mill. Serve with grilled cheese.

Serves 6 to 8

Again, both these soups can be served cold if that’s what the weather calls for.

A couple thoughts on the grilled cheese …

I love a good cheddar on sourdough. But gruyere or Emmental make excellent grilled cheese. I made this combo last week, but I couldn’t find raclette, which I really wanted to try on a grilled cheese; so I did a mix gruyere and gouda, which was excellent.

Did you know that it’s perfectly fine—indeed, much more convenient if your butter is hard—to spread the outside of the bread with mayonnaise. Mayo gives you the same crunchy brown surface as butter with little flavor compromise.

To review the recipes …

  1. Terrance Brennan’s 3-Ingredient soup: a can of tomatoes, garlic, olive oil, simmered 10 minutes and pureed.

  2. Ann’s variation on Ina’s canned/roasted tomato soup, which includes sautéed onion and garlic, basil and thyme—lovely and also very easy, a little longer to cook as it simmers 40 minutes before being blended.

  3. Thomas Keller’s four-star “Soup and Sandwich” cheese course—featuring the great tomato consommé. It’s a dish anyone can make at home.

  4. And one last variation. We had a quart of Ann’s soup leftover, which we froze. When the weather gets cool again, we will reheat it and add 1 cup of cooked rice for a tomato-and-rice soup leftovers meal.

One final note on … “So Good” …

Not long ago we saw the excellent movie, Song Sung Blue, about a Neil Diamond cover band in Milwaukee and starring Hugh Jackman and Kate Hudson. Most everyone is familiar with the Neil Diamond song “Sweet Caroline,” which has become so universally popular it’s practically a required singalong. And when the chorus comes to the line “…Good times never felt so good …,” everyone naturally shouts, “So good! So good! So Good!”

It’s so catchy in fact, that we can’t help ourselves—whenever Ann takes a bite of something, or I do, and she or I utter the words, “So good,” the other repeats it three times: So good! So good! So good!

When the movie came out Ann read a story, she can’t remember where, about Diamond’s inviting Jackson and Hudson to his home for dinner with his family. Jackson and Hudson recounted the fact that in the Diamond household, whenever anyone exclaims “so good,” others at the table actually stand and shout, “So good! So good! So good!”

I love that!
