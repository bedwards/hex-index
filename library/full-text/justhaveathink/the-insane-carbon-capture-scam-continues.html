So let's ask some searching questions about the detail. Starting with the size of the challenge.

According to the United Nations most recent GAP report, human activity in 2023 resulted in the release of just over 57 billion metric tons of CO2 equivalent gases into the atmosphere. That's an increase of 1.3% on the previous year. And for those not acquainted with the jargon of these sorts of reports, the term CO2 equivalent is just a convenient way of lumping all greenhouse gases into one single metric using what are known as global warming potential or GWP values of each greenhouse gas. Those GWP values are set out in the greenhouse gas protocol issued by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change or IPCC.

Carbon dioxide is ascribed a value of one and every other gas has an equivalent value based on how much more effective it is at warming the atmosphere. Then some clever buffins with posh calculators multiply all those values by the actual quantity of each gas released and that gets us to the overall CO2 equivalent number quoted by the UN. Some of that total comes from things like methane and nitrous oxide emissions from agriculture and land use change and also methane from our waste management systems. To assess carbon capture and storage, it's more useful to look at global energy related CO2 emissions which according to the International Energy Agency reached 37.4 billion metric tonses in 2023.

So 37.4,000 million metric tons. The middle-of-the-road emissions projection that can be found in the latest IPCC report shows that limiting global warming to only 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels would require us humans to capture and permanently store 8.7 billion tons of CO2 every single year. The Global CCS Institute tells us that as of November 2023, there were a total of 41 CCS projects operating globally with a combined carbon dioxide capture rate of approximately 49 million tons per year, which is just a little over 1/ half of 1% of that IPCC requirement. The CCS Institute did point out though that there were another 361 million tons worth of projects in the pipeline.

So, how's that going? Well, according to the International Energy Agency, as of Q1 2025, just over 50 million tons of CO2 capture and storage was in place, reflecting what they call a modest year-over-year increase from 2023. So, at an uplift rate of a million tons a year, we just need to be a little patient for about 8 and a half thousand years or so while the industry ramps up to full carbon capture capacity. Instead, let's focus on the economics of carbon capture and storage. At first glance, CCS looks deceptively straightforward, as I outlined right at the start of the video. Essentially, you bolt some capture equipment onto the side of a smoke stack, pipe the carbon dioxide into a compressor, and bury it in the ground. But the real world costs are anything but simple.

The IEA estimates that even capturing highly concentrated CO2 streams like those from ethanol or ammonia production costs about 15 to25 US per ton. But capturing CO2 from much more dilute flu gases like those coming out of a power station is far more costly. Typically between 40 and $120 US per ton. And those figures don't include the energy penalty.

In other words, the extra fuel needed just to run the capture system. Retrofitting a coal fired power plant with CCS can increase fuel use by between 14 and 40%. While gas plants take an 11 to 22% hit. So even before you start thinking about pipelines and monitoring or liability for leaks, the economics look grim.

That's why historically CCS has piggybacked on something else. Enhanced oil recovery or EO. By pumping captured CO2 into depleted oil wells, companies can squeeze out additional barrels of crude that would otherwise stay underground. That extra oil generates revenue, effectively subsidizing the high cost of capture.

And in the USA, it also generates a tax incentive under the so-called 45Q rules designed to reward the sequestration of carbon dioxide. According to the Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis, more than 70% of all CCS projects worldwide rely on EO and between 80 and 90% of all CO2 ever captured has ended up being used to extract oil from depleted seams. So what we get is a sort of perverse climate paradox. Technically CO2 is being captured and injected underground, which means US operators can pick up their $60 to $80 per ton.

Thank you very much. But that CO2 is being used to produce more fossil fuel, which of course gets burned and releases yet more CO2. The net benefit to the climate can be very small or in some cases even negative depending on how much extra oil is produced and burned. I've covered this grotesque insanity several times before on the channel, but each time we come back to it, it still does my heading.

No wonder critics say CCS looks less like a serious climate solution and more like a subsidy machine. The current UK government has been warned it could be on the hook for as much as 6 billion quid in guarantees if its T-side CCS project fails to deliver. Even the industry's own track record is shaky. Chevron's Gorgon LNG project in Australia, touted as the largest dedicated CCS facility in the world, has repeatedly missed its targets and faced regulatory action for underperforming.

And most recently, Norway's state energy provider Ecuinor has been forced to retract claims that its slighter facility had been capturing about a million tons of CO2 annually when official figures showed the real number to be closer to 100,000 tons. So when you put all of that together, the slow progress of CCS becomes much less of a surprise. The economics don't work without subsidies or oil revenue. The climate benefits are undermined by EO.

The scale is tiny compared to global emissions. And the track record is littered with underperformance. But it's this very latest research published in the highly respected journal Nature in early September that is perhaps the most damning of all. Rather than the 5 to six degrees Celsius of avoided warming that the fossil fuel industry claims to be achievable with the various CCS technologies, the researchers on this paper found that the realistically available range is between 0.35 and 1.2° C.

And that's assuming all CO2 captured will be permanently sequestered and not used for enhanced oil recovery, which as we've seen is in itself a highly unlikely scenario. The research finds that there just aren't as many genuinely usable storage pockets in Earth's geological makeup as had been previously touted. Historical scientific literature estimated between 10 and 40,000 billion tons worth of storage capacity, mostly in sedimentary rock formations. The fossil fuel industry typically claims there to be around 14,000 gigatons of which the paper explains 13,400 gigatons worth is currently undiscovered and only 253 million tons worth is economically viable.

This latest research establishes a so-called prudent planetary limit of only around 1,460 billion tons worth. Again, assuming all the CO2 is genuinely permanently captured without displacing and liberating other fossil fuels. The strong conclusion of the paper is that only stringent near-term gross emissions reductions can lower the risk of breaching this limit before the year 2200, which of course is a date that is far too far into the future for us humans to give a damn about, isn't it? And almost certainly too far away to represent a problematic wrinkle for our dear politicians.

CCS has always been politically attractive, of course, because on paper it looks like the ideal compromise. Fossil fuel companies get to keep drilling, mining, and selling their products. Governments get to tell their voters they're tackling climate change, and everyone hopes the costs will come down with scale. But both the IEA and the IPCC stress that CCS is not needed everywhere.

In fact, in power generation and most of the energy system, renewables, efficiency, and electrification are already cheaper and more effective for cement or ammonia plants and maybe for steel production, all of which have hard to abate emissions. CCS will quite likely play an important role further down the line. But they are not the major contributors to overall global emissions.
