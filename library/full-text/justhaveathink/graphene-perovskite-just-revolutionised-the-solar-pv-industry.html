So, it's all about the wavelengths of sunlight that can be captured and used within a solar cell. Some semiconductor materials can absorb a very wide range of wavelengths, which means a large amount of all available sunlight is being utilized, which is great. But those materials typically only deliver a tiny electrical voltage, which effectively makes the solar panel useless.

There are alternative semiconductor materials that have properties that result in much higher, more usable voltage, but those materials typically only capture a very small range of wavelengths, which means most of the sun's energy gets wasted. All of which is extremely frustrating for solar PV developers. Back in the 60s, two physicists called William Shockley and Hans Yoikim Kaiser worked out that the semiconductor material with the optimum balance of wavelength capture and voltage was silicon and that the maximum theoretical efficiency of a single junction photovoltaic cell was just over 30%. But the efficiency of a silicon solar cell drops off quite a lot if there are defects or blemishes in the material.

So the cells have to be subjected to extremely high temperatures to get those defects out. And for reasons of physics that are well outside the scope of this video, the photons of light that hit the electrons in the silicon need a bit of extra help from things called phonons to provide enough energy to liberate those electrons and help them jump up into a state where they can be harvested off into an electrical circuit. Getting randomly hit by a photon and a phonon is much less likely than just getting hit by one or the other on its own. So to increase the chances of that happening, the silicon layer has to be made quite thick.

Now for reasons of physics which again we won't delve into here perovskites don't need that extra phonon to get their electrons moving which means they can be manufactured as very thin films. Perovskites can also be chemically manipulated or tuned to capture different wavelengths of light to silicon which means they can be adapted for a wide range of different light sources and they can be applied as a thin layer on top of silicon wafers creating so-called tandem or multi-junction cells. The resulting cell can then absorb a far wider spectrum of light while maintaining a good working voltage and in theory overcoming that shockly Kaiser limit of roughly 30% efficiency. That revelation started a whole field of tandem research that's still going on today.

You see, not all solar cells are made for panels that sit outside on our roofs. Some are calibrated to work indoors in low light conditions. So what does that mean?

Well, am stands for air mass, and the 1.5 number represents the strength of sunlight after passing through 1.5 times the thickness of the atmosphere. That corresponds roughly to the sun at about 48° above the horizon, which is typical of mid- latatitude regions. And the G stands for global, which takes into account both direct sunlight and diffuse scattered light from the sky. So, this is clearly a standard designed to achieve uniform testing for solar panels that will spend their operational lifetime outside.

So when you see the phrase cell efficiency under standard test conditions or STC, that generally means the cell was measured at 25° C under AM 1.5G illumination at 1,000 watts per square meter. Now that is not the relevant test for very small solar cells designed to replace the batteries that are currently used in billions of tiny devices that run our modern world, discreetly providing constant energy in the background without most of us even realizing they're there. We're talking about things like TV remotes, calculators, toys, lights, e-readers, and myriad products that provide control systems for so-called internet of things or IoT devices. So the IEC is the International Electrottechnical Commission. Their test doesn't look at the sun's spectrum, but instead focuses on how you actually measure and report the electrical output of peroskite solar cells under ambient light conditions. And this is how we start to see those apparently impossibly high efficiencies. The shockly Kaiser limit that I mentioned earlier is derived assuming standard outdoor fullsp spectrum sunlight conditions which closely correlate to the AM1.5G test we just looked at.

Efficiency is limited by nature in this instance. In other words, lots of the incoming photons have either more energy than a semiconductor can deal with, in which case it's wasted as heat, or less energy than is required to bump electrons out of the semiconductor, in which case the photons are not absorbed at all. But indoor LED lights very efficiently emit photons that are mostly in a part of the visible range that just happens to align very closely with the so-called band gap of Halo cell's tuned ambient peroskite module. So what's that then?

Well, essentially the band gap represents the amount of energy needed to nudge an electron from where it normally sits in something called the veilance band up into a more excited state in what's known as the conduction band. If you want to capture power from an indoor LED light, then you tune the peroskite to a band gap of 1.5 to 1.6 electron volts. That means almost every photon that arrives can be harvested and there's very little wasted excess energy. And because the input power of an LED light is so well matched to the tuned perkite solar cell, it can achieve an apparently very high power conversion efficiency under those indoor test conditions.
