7 Barriers to Scaling Wartime Production

MGen Ed “Hertz” Vaughan

As an experienced private sector manufacturing engineer, I’ve seen production from every angle—small shops building first articles, companies pushing thousands of units per month, M&As that change supply chains, and now from the inside of the Department of War, where we’re scaling critical munitions production at speed.

Ideas don’t win contracts. Production does. Prototypes don’t win wars. Production does.

Here are 7 barriers to scaling industrial production:

  1. Great Idea ≠ Manufacturable Product. A clever design in a lab doesn’t mean it can be built reliably, repeatedly, and at cost. That gap kills companies.

  2. Prototype ≠ Production System. What works once—or even ten times—often breaks at scale. Tooling, tolerances, workflows, and supply chains all change.

  3. Design Isn’t Built for Scale. Engineers optimize for performance. Manufacturers optimize for throughput. Those are not the same thing—and redesign is almost always required.

  4. Automation Is a Different Problem Entirely. Even mature products still rely on “artisan” steps. Converting those into automated processes requires new engineering, not just better equipment. Often new people are required too.

  5. Supply Chain Fragility. Scaling exposes weak links—single-source suppliers, long lead materials, quality variability. At volume, small risks become system failures.

  6. Time-to-Scale Is Consistently Underestimated. Everyone’s timeline looks great in a pitch deck. Reality includes rework, yield loss, workforce training, and process stabilization. If you’ve never personally scaled production, then hire advice on how to do it.

  7. Founder Hubris and Organizational Blind Spots. Confidence is necessary. Arrogance is fatal. I’ve seen teams ignore data that contradicts their assumptions—and they stall out. The ones who scale are the ones who learn fast and adapt. Sometimes the smartest thing a founder can do is get out of the way and hire experienced people to lead the next step.

Can you build it 10,000 times? Can you do it next quarter? Can you do it tonight? That’s the bar.

Requirements Without Factories: Why the Pentagon Must Reconnect Design to Production

John Ferrari and Dillon Prochnicki

The Pentagon treats industrial production capacity as an afterthought rather than a design constraint. Requirements writers inside the services seek the most capable system imaginable. Their incentives reward performance, not manufacturability.

  • Predictably, the system becomes complex, expensive, and slow to produce.

  • What is insufficiently asked during these early stages is whether the industrial base can actually manufacture the system in meaningful quantities.

  • The wars in Ukraine and the Middle East, along with rising tensions with China, have demonstrated a basic truth: wars consume weapons at astonishing rates.

  • Qualitative superiority matters, but we forget that quantity has a quality all of its own at our peril.

  • When adversaries, such as Iran today, look at American stockpile depletion rates and production timelines, the message they get is that the U.S. can be outlasted.

  • That is a terrible strategic signal, and it is a direct consequence of a procurement system that treats industrial capacity as someone else’s problem.

  • Despite the best efforts of Pentagon leadership, the result is a new requirements architecture that is faster and better prioritized but structurally incapable of answering a fundamental question: “Can the industrial base actually build the solution at the scale and speed the force design demands?”

  • The gap persists because of three reinforcing failures:

    • First, the incentives are broken. Requirements writers are measured on specifying the most capable system, not the most buildable one.

    • Second, the acquisition community doesn’t push back. PEOs and PMs are measured on executing to the requirement, not on telling the requirements community that the design can’t be manufactured at scale.

    • Third, the industrial base has no voice in the room. While A&S has industrial base responsibilities and conceptually is part of the solution, how those considerations are incorporated is unclear.

  • The good news: The new requirements architecture creates natural insertion points for producibility analysis, which won’t require additional bureaucratic layers.

    • First, the MEIA’s charter, ostensibly still being developed, should explicitly include a producibility assessment for each Key Operational Problem (KOP) solution it evaluates.

    • Second, the service-level requirements process reviews directed in the memorandum should incorporate producibility as a mandatory consideration.

    • Third, the RRAB should consider production feasibility when it makes programming and funding recommendations.

  • Speed without producibility is just a faster way to design systems the industrial base cannot build.

  • Producibility should enter at requirements definition, gets assessed during solution analysis, and informs resource allocation.

Our Take: We could not agree more. AoAs today do not take these considerations into account despite the continued demonstrated post-design issues. R&E could take an important by bringing in these key elements to drive more informed decisions.

Taiwan Receives First U.S. MQ-9 SkyGuardian Drones

Taiwan received the first batch of an advanced drone order from the U.S. last week that is set to bolster Taipei’s over-the-horizon targeting capabilities.

  • The MQ-9B SkyGuardians are designed to loiter for up to 40 hours and are equipped with advanced ISR sensor payloads – bringing a much needed boost to Taipei’s monitoring capabilities amid challenges from Beijing.

  • The MQ-9B’s radar can also detect and track maritime targets at ranges between 150-300 kilometers, or up to 186 miles.

  • Two additional drones will arrive in 2027.

  • Taiwan’s drone push includes various classes of drones that will be deployed from the smallest to largest formations.

  • From small squad-deployed attack and first-person view drones to one-way-attack, medium-altitude UAVs and unmanned surface vessels, autonomous systems will play a major role in protecting Taiwan in the coming years.

  • The Taiwanese government is also eyeing the procurement of up to 200,000 UAVs and more than 1,000 unmanned surface vessels in its upcoming defense bills.

China Seeking AI to Counter U.S. Military Strengths

The Chinese military is looking to acquire artificial intelligence systems that can counteract perceived U.S. warfighting advantages.

  • The nation’s interest in these AI decision aids is the result of cultural and political limitations within the People’s Liberation Army.

  • China is one of many modern militaries seeking AI decision support tools.

  • China has also made specific requests to industry for space targeting capabilities, both for targets on land and in space, adding more supporting evidence to warnings about China’s offensive space ambitions.

  • They have also sent numerous requests for technologies to detect U.S. naval assets on and under the sea.

AUKUS Submarines: US and UK Partners Show the Realities Facing Australia’s Small Fleet

In 2056, 30 years from now, when Australia has eight nuclear powered submarines, we will have two deployable submarines consistently available. That’s assuming all goes well with AUKUS’ “optimal pathway” and we do in fact have eight SSNs in service.

  • Expecting a better outcome than this is likely to lead to disappointment.

  • The problem with nuclear submarines is shared by all submarines: they are hugely complex electro-mechanical devices and they operate in extraordinarily demanding environments – both physical and threat.

  • As the inaugural head of Australia’s Submarine Agency, Vice Admiral Jonathan Mead has said, nuclear submarines are “more complicated than the Space Shuttle or the International Space Station”.

  • In addition to the everyday maintenance performed dockside, submarines require periodic overhauls that take them out of the water for one, two or more years.

  • What that means is that world’s best practice is essentially a 3-to-1 ratio: for every three submarines a navy has, one is available for operations.

  • Moreover, there is a vicious cycle of submarine availability and submariner availability—if submarines aren’t available, submariners can’t go to sea to train and qualify. But if you don’t have sufficient number of qualified submariners across the necessary critical skills, then you can’t take submarines to sea.

  • So availability and deployability is a complex, interrelated challenge of both people and materiel that we can only skim over lightly here.
