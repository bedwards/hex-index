It only takes about 11 hours to assemble a modern iPhone, which means the total labor cost to assemble an iPhone 16 Pro in China is just 40 bucks, barely 4% of its selling price. In other words, even if Apple did pay American wages, even if it passed this entire cost on to consumers, and even if it had no way to automate the process and save some money, the price of an iPhone would still only increase by $146, less than what many spend upgrading their storage. And for Apple, achieving independence from Beijing would be worth a fortune. In November 2022, the company lost a billion dollars a week after China's extreme CO zero policies led to protests at its most important factory.

In the span of one week in 2023, the Wall Street Journal reported that China banned the use of iPhones for official business and US Congressman Mike Gallagher attacked the company for yielding to China's demands, which he called quote unthinkable corporate cowardice. To Washington, Apple is too accommodating of China. To Beijing, it's not accommodating enough. Whatever it does to appease one country provokes the other.

The only real solution is to leave. All of which is to say $146 might be a small price to pay for Apple's escape. If only it were that simple. 146 is the wrong number to focus on because the cost of labor is not the source of Apple's dependence on China.

Chinese labor, after all, has grown considerably more expensive over the past two decades. For those $363, Apple can buy 2 hours of Indian labor. There, English is an official language and its population is now even larger than China's. Yet despite these advantages, only about 7% of the world's iPhones are made in India.

Any country can assemble the iPhone. But Apple doesn't need to make an iPhone. It needs to make 590 every minute. It needs to make 35,000 per hour, 849,000 per day, 5.9 million per week.

That's the challenge facing Apple and the number to focus on. Out of 200 countries on Earth, just one is able to manufacture the iPhone at its current price, scale, and to its exacting standards. Others supplement production, but none could replace China, which is still home to about 90% of the company's manufacturing. Delivering any product, never mind the most successful consumer device in history, every September like clockwork is no easy feat.

And there are two additional complications. First, demand fluctuates wildly throughout the year. Nearly twice as many iPhones are sold between September and Christmas as the slow spring months of April, May, and June. Second, Apple hates holding inventory.

CEO Tim Cook once called it quote fundamentally evil. You're in the dairy business, he said. If it gets past its freshness date, you have a problem. In 2012, the market research firm Gartner estimated that Apple churns through its stock 74 times a year, meaning the average iPhone is in the company's possession for just 5 days from the time it takes off in a 747 in central China to the time it sold to a customer in Pittsburgh.

5 days. And that number has likely gone down over the past decade of Cook's leadership. It's one thing to produce 200 million new iPhones a year. It's another to make each one within 5 days of when it's needed.

To accommodate such highly variable demand without holding inventory, Apple's manufacturing partners like Foxcon must double their supply of labor, hiring as many as a million additional workers in the leadup to September before quickly dismissing them around the new year. Most governments, usually eager to boost employment, would consider this more a liability than an asset. According to Patrick McGee in his new book, Apple and China, Foxcon has at times employed 1.7 million people. Walmart, the world's largest private employer, has 2.1 million.

So imagine what would happen if Walmart laid off half its entire workforce every December, throwing them onto the streets without severance. Unemployment offices would be overwhelmed, public budgets drained, and families plunged into a sudden traumatic state of poverty each and every year. But not in China. For nearly as long as the PRC has existed, Chinese citizens have been legally tied to the same region as their parents, trapped essentially in place.

Beginning in the 1980s, Beijing loosened these requirements, allowing people to move freely around the country, but with a catch. You can now work anywhere, but you can still only receive government services where your family is officially registered. Say, for example, you were born in a rural part of Gansu province whose per capita GDP is just $7,000, about the same as Libya. The government won't stop you from working in a factory outside Beijing, where the per capita GDP is over four times higher, about the same as Estonia.

But it won't allow you to use those Estonia level hospitals, daycarees, or schools. The massive wage differentials between rural and urban areas pull migrants toward factories while the legal restrictions prevent them from gaining a foothold in cities, trapping them in this vulnerable, transient state. By law, they can give but never take. This is not just exploitative in the usual low wage, low worker protection sense.

Cheap labor and lax regulations after all can be found just as easily in the Philippines, Bangladesh or India. No, far more important to Apple, it's exploitative in the exact way its high demand volatility, low inventory business demands. It's enormously flexible. This permanent underclass of 300 million or so migrant workers is always at the ready, yet never able to escape their condition, making them easy to hire and easy to fire.

Factories raise wages during busy months, after which most workers return to their hometowns, fall back on lower paid gigs like farming, and wait until the next iPhone is introduced. For Foxcon, the tedious tasks assigned to workers, the brutal military-like routines, the 12-hour shifts, and the drab concrete environment are features, not bugs. It doesn't want to keep workers satisfied because it doesn't want them to stay. These jobs are disposable by design.

The average employee leaves after just 68 days, according to an internal memo discovered by Patrick McGee. Sooner or later, they need to see their children, who they're almost always forced to leave with grandparents back in their hometowns. Not to mention, they'll eventually need medical care, an ounce of vitamin D, or a breath of fresh air. Another source of cheap, ondemand labor are students.

When Foxcon needs some extra hands, local governments bust students in from vocational high schools for two, three, six-month quote internships, a requirement to graduate. So, why does Beijing bend over backwards to help a private American corporation? The answer to that question has a lot to do with the number 50. That's the number of daily business class seats between San Francisco and Shanghai Apple once reserved from United Airlines.

Those 50 daily seats, each valued around $4,000, illustrates the company's level of involvement and investment in its supply chain. While most companies outsource manufacturing so they can forget about it, Apple refuses to leave the fate of its most important product in someone else's hands. To maintain its strict quality control, improve efficiency, and meet its tight September deadlines, it sends its engineers on something like $20,000 trips a year to Shanghai alone. As of 2019, it was United's largest customer, accounting for $150 million of its annual revenue.

It even convinced the airline to open new routes to Hongjo and Changdu. Now, normally Apple is among the most secretive companies on Earth. It certainly doesn't tolerate corporate espionage or theft of its IP. But when it comes to its manufacturing processes, it has a strong incentive to share its knowledge to stop Foxcon from becoming too powerful.

The more industrial expertise leaks out of its factories, the more competition Fox Con is faced with and therefore the less leverage it holds over Apple. Between 2007 and 12, Apple's profit margin grew from around 20% to nearly 35, largely thanks to Foxcon. Yet, despite enabling that success, Foxcon's profit margin fell. Its costs go down, its production up, and before you know it, that factory has spawned an entire industrial ecosystem. A cluster of expertise, machinery, and suppliers all in one place. These industrial clusters are so much faster, cheaper, and more efficient that they often swallow their entire market.
