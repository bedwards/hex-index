President Barack Obama was fond of the phrase the arc of history. His supporters, he said on the evening of his first election in 2008, had voted to “put their hands on the arc of history and bend it once more toward the hope of a better day.”

I have long doubted that history has an arc any more than it follows predictable cycles. Obama was, in fact, adapting something the Reverend Martin Luther King Jr. said in a sermon at Washington’s National Cathedral in 1968. “The arc of the moral universe is long,” King declared, “but it bends toward justice.”

Perhaps this is true of the moral universe; God alone knows. But I don’t think it’s true of human history. The path of the past is not an arc. It is nonlinear—at times jagged like an electrocardiogram, at times flat as a pancake. It doesn’t bend toward anything. There has been one immense discontinuity in economic history: the Industrial Revolution. Another may be getting started with the advances in artificial intelligence, though it’s still too early to be sure about the Singularity. The trends and cycles discerned by modern sages are mostly illusions, like the general progress toward peace and goodwill my friend Steve Pinker attributes to the Enlightenment, an intellectual movement of the 17th and 18th centuries that signally failed to prevent the bloodiest wars in human history from occurring in the 20th. It is 2026 and we are back at it, slaughtering our fellow men and women in Eastern Europe, the Middle East, and Africa.

What history has in abundance are not arcs but arches. The triumphal arch—rounded on the inside with two great legs and a square entablature on top—was one of the many enduring legacies of ancient Rome. In the days of the republic, such a monument to a great victory was called a fornix, but Augustus, the first of the emperors, preferred the term arcus and associated such structures exclusively with the imperial family. Soon they were everywhere in Rome. There were 36 at one time, of which three survive.

The path of the past is not an arc. It is nonlinear—at times jagged like an electrocardiogram, at times flat as a pancake.

Since then, each empire worthy of the name has had its arch: the Brandenburg Gate in Berlin (constructed 1770–1771), the Arc de Triomphe in Paris (built 1806–1830; opened in 1836), the Narva Arch in Saint Petersburg (constructed 1812–1814), the Wellington Arch in London (built 1826–1830), and the India Gate in New Delhi (constructed 1921–1931). A few wannabe empires have them, too. Bucharest has one; so does Pyongyang.

The United States is an exception. Though there are three arches in New York—including the one in Washington Square, dedicated to the first president in 1895—there are none in the city that bears his name, Washington, D.C. But that is about to change.

Plans have been published for a giant, gold-bedecked victory arch that has already been nicknamed the Arc de Trump. “Despite overwhelmingly negative feedback from the public and preservation groups,” the BBC reported in its customary prim-headmistress tone, “a panel made up of Trump allies voted to move the project forward with some tweaks on Thursday. The 250-foot monument, if given final approval, would be taller than the U.S. Capitol building and the Lincoln Memorial, and feature a golden Lady Liberty–like statue with a torch and crown.”

It will be “the GREATEST and MOST BEAUTIFUL Triumphal Arch, anywhere in the World,” Trump wrote on Truth Social, where imperial edicts are published these days.

What triumphs will this arch commemorate? Well, the president answered that question in a veritable barrage of “Truths” posted in the space of under an hour on Friday morning:

This deal is in no way subject to Lebanon, either, but the USA will, separately, work with Lebanon, and deal with the Hezboolah [sic] situation in an appropriate manner. Israel will not be bombing Lebanon any longer. They are PROHIBITED from doing so by the U.S.A. Enough is enough!!!” —April 17, 9:57 a.m.

“Now that the Hormuz Strait situation is over, I received a call from NATO asking if we would need some help. I TOLD THEM TO STAY AWAY, UNLESS THEY JUST WANT TO LOAD UP THEIR SHIPS WITH OIL. They were useless when needed, a Paper Tiger!” —April 17, 10:13 a.m.

Like the price of oil in different markets and at different times, there is sometimes quite a wide spread between Truth, in the president’s sense of the word, and reality. Since April 8, when Trump called off the obliteration of Iran’s civilization, there has been a ceasefire. I wasn’t sure about this at first, but the fire certainly ceased. Iran has reportedly fired almost no missiles since April 8, and just a few drones.

In Lebanon, meanwhile, President Joseph Aoun and Israeli prime minister Benjamin Netanyahu have agreed to a 10-day ceasefire beginning April 16. The significance of this negotiation is that it has been detached from the talks between the United States and Iran that happened in Islamabad just over a week ago—a significant concession by the Iranian regime, which hitherto treated Israel’s campaign against the Iranian proxy Hezbollah as inseparable from Iran’s war with Israel and the U.S.

From all of this it might reasonably be inferred that the “Seven Weeks’ War” has concluded. If so, what have we learned?

We should start with the fact that the Strait of Hormuz remains, in practice, closed. In the period after Friday’s presidential Truth storm, vessels began to move toward the strait. But then they turned back, apparently in response to Iranian threats and some warning shots. As I write, traffic through the strait remains at a fraction of its volume before the war began on February 28. The president has reverted to threatening “to knock out every single Power Plant, and every single Bridge, in Iran. NO MORE MR. NICE GUY!” On Sunday afternoon, Trump announced that U.S. forces had fired on and seized an Iranian-flagged cargo ship attempting to breach the American naval blockade. The ceasefire is set to expire on Wednesday.

Here is the puzzle. Every expert on commodity markets knows that the closure of the Strait of Hormuz is a very big and very bad deal. To quote energy expert Rory Johnston, “Over the first month of the crisis alone, the global system lost more than 200 million barrels of oil. April’s losses will be equivalent to the entirety of the IEA’s [International Energy Agency] record coordinated SPR [Strategic Petroleum Reserve] release.”

By any measure, this is the biggest disruption to the global oil market we have ever seen, bigger than the shocks of the 1970s. And something equally big is happening to the global supply of urea, a crucial ingredient of modern fertilizers. Prices have soared. And don’t forget the hit to sulfur, crucial to many industrial processes, and helium, essential for semiconductor manufacture. The shock is already palpable in many developing economies.

Yet the world’s financial markets, and especially the U.S. stock markets, appear not to have registered this. At its low point, on March 26, the Dow Jones index was down 6.2 percent compared with February 27, the day before Operation Epic Fury began. The S&P 500 was down 5.8 percent and the Nasdaq 5.6 percent. At Friday’s close, however, all three markets were up 1 percent (Dow), 3.6 percent (S&P), and 7.9 percent (Nasdaq). According to one analyst, the last 10 days rank in the 99.7th percentile of all 10-day returns in stock market history. Sure, oil prices are higher, but Brent crude on Friday was just 25 percent above its prewar price.

How are we to make sense of this? Since the war began, I have written essays warning of 1) the danger that the conflict goes global, 2) the risk of recession if the conflict is protracted, and 3) the nightmare that this is the American version of the 1956 Suez Crisis. Either Wall Street has canceled all its subscriptions to The Free Press, or I am missing something.

I can offer three possible explanations:

  1. Mr. Market is seeing through all the noise and realizing that the war will be over by the end of May, and normal service will be restored.

  2. Mr. Market loves AI more than he hates the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

  3. Mr. Market is as clueless as he was in July 1914, September 1929, December 1972, August 2000, October 2007, and February 2020—all months when stocks reached highs shortly before reality struck devastating blows.

I struggle a bit with the first theory. On Friday, stocks and bonds rallied while oil sold off because the president asserted:

THE STRAIT OF HORMUZ IS COMPLETELY OPEN AND READY FOR BUSINESS AND FULL PASSAGE, BUT THE NAVAL BLOCKADE WILL REMAIN IN FULL FORCE AND EFFECT AS IT PERTAINS TO IRAN, ONLY, UNTIL SUCH TIME AS OUR TRANSACTION WITH IRAN IS 100% COMPLETE. THIS PROCESS SHOULD GO VERY QUICKLY IN THAT MOST OF THE POINTS ARE ALREADY NEGOTIATED.

Only after the market closed did it become apparent that little, if any, of this was true. On Sunday morning we got Trump’s latest threat of obliteration. Sure enough, on Monday Iran was going to “have a great and prosperous future” if its “new leaders (Regime Change!) are smart.”

Theory two is better. Compared with where it was in 1973, on the eve of the first big oil shock, the U.S. economy is diversified, resilient, and tearing ahead with a technological innovation potentially as significant as railroads, electrification, and nuclear fission. U.S. companies lead China’s in the race for both commercially viable AI (Anthropic’s annual recurring revenue is growing exponentially) and artificial general intelligence. The U.S. also dominates computing capacity in terms of its ownership of the world’s most advanced semiconductors. On top of that, the U.S. has abundant and cheap natural gas as well as oil, so it ought not to be short of power (if it can sort out its shambolic permitting system and not-very-national grid).

In short, the U.S. is the AI superpower. Part of what blew Iran’s military away was AI-enabled targeting combined with weapons of exquisite precision. Meanwhile, the Europeans are doubly delusional: They don’t realize how heavily they rely on the U.S. both militarily and technologically, and they don’t realize the transatlantic alliance is at death’s door. Remember hyperpuissance, a term used by the French minister of foreign affairs Hubert Védrine in 1999 to refer to a nation with unrivaled dominance? It’s so much more fitting today.

Yet there are reasons also to be skeptical about theory two. Let me walk you through the events of the war so far.

The United States and Israel tried regime change; it didn’t work. Or rather, they got regime change—Iran became an Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps–led military dictatorship. That was not an improvement.

The U.S. won an overwhelming military victory with air and naval power and scarcely a boot on the ground. But it destroyed less of Iran’s missile- and drone-launching capabilities than at first appeared.

Then there was a hostage crisis. Iran took both the Gulfies and the Strait of Hormuz hostage. The result was a massive economic shock for the world that required a rapid resolution.

The choice was between 1) military escalation (boots on the ground or strikes on Iranian infrastructure), and 2) a diplomatic deal. Trump chose 2.

In Islamabad, the U.S proposed big economic concessions in return for some kind of change in the status of Iran’s enriched uranium stockpile, as well as the reopening of the strait. Contrary to the president’s social media feed, the Iranians did not accept.

In any case, the devil of any deal will be in the details, not the Truth headline. (When the small print finally comes out, every former Obama and Biden official will be ready to tell The New York Times that it’s worse than the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action.)

Meanwhile, the Iranians have survived regime change and discovered that closing the strait is just as powerful a lever in economic warfare as they had always hoped. It’s not, despite the Russian quip, an “economic nuke,” because unlike a nuclear weapon you can use it.

Where we go from here is fairly predictable. I would be surprised if Trump now deploys ground forces. There will be more negotiation, so Islamabad, here we come. There may have to be more bombing, if the Iranians dust down the North Vietnamese playbook of stringing the U.S. negotiators along. And the final compromise will take longer to be agreed upon than Mr. Market currently believes. The consensus in prediction markets is this will be over by the end of May, but remember: It took Henry Kissinger more than four months to get the 1973–1974 oil embargo lifted.

How will this war leave the Middle East? True, the U.S. remains militarily dominant and China seems unwilling to step in as the new regional hegemon. But the Carter Doctrine seems no longer quite compelling. In 1980, Zbig Brzezinski drafted the key line: “An attempt by any outside force to gain control of the Persian Gulf region will be regarded as an assault on the vital interests of the United States of America, and such an assault will be repelled by any means necessary, including military force.” As I write, Iran still seems to control the Strait of Hormuz.

However, even if the strait is declared “open” at some future date, no one will trust that it really is open. The Saudis and the Emiratis have the option to build more pipeline infrastructure to avoid a future crisis. But there will be a new and enduring risk premium on Iraqi and Kuwaiti oil as well as Qatari liquefied natural gas. All the Gulf States are asking themselves how much protection their relationships with the United States gave them. Answer: Not much. All the Western investors who thought data centers in the desert were a smart idea are also heading back to the United States.

Then there’s Israel. Netanyahu gambled on being able to collapse the Islamic Republic with U.S. support. Collapse may yet happen, but it can’t be the base case. I make it a total of four ceasefires that Trump has imposed on Bibi since last June. Elections have to happen in Israel by October. It’s anyone’s guess who wins, but few polls imply a Likud majority.

The transatlantic alliance may be the most significant casualty of this war. Most Europeans and Brits don’t yet realize just how much their leaders have blown it in Washington. Europeans will probably come to realize the full cost of disappointing Trump only when Russia tests them. Regardless of whether Mark Rutte manages to salvage the NATO Summit in Ankara in July, Vladimir Putin knows that Trump’s commitment to the alliance is seriously weakened.

On the other hand, Ukraine now has a market in Europe and the Middle East for its surplus drones and drone-defense technology. Moreover, it has built its own cruise missiles that it can use without Western constraints. It turns out that the European country with the most advanced military capabilities is Ukraine—which is neither a member of NATO nor the European Union. The old question was: Can Ukraine be admitted into the EU? Maybe it’s the EU that needs to be admitted into Ukraine.

Overall, the axis of authoritarians comes out stronger. China and Russia have very openly helped Iran with weapons and targeting intelligence without paying any price for doing so. As in 2025, when China won the trade war by restricting U.S. access to rare-earth elements, so in 2026: If the axis can inflict economic pain on the United States, it can win. And the economic pain is just beginning. Americans will find the costs of Hormuz closure becoming more visible as the shortages and price hikes slowly but inexorably work their way via Asia through the global supply chains we rely on for so many goods. Already, 70 percent of American farmers say they can’t afford the fertilizer they need.

Moreover, U.S. precision-missile stocks have been drastically depleted. To rebuild its arsenal, the Pentagon still relies on critical minerals processed in China. Does America look stronger than it did two months ago? Not from Beijing. And, compared with Iran, China has many more options to impose economic pressure in a calibrated manner, without firing a shot. Beijing already knew this but just had its thesis confirmed. As Eyck Freymann argued in these pages last week, nothing happening in the Gulf right now is making Taiwan more secure.

However this war turns out, there will be a meaningful gap between reality and Truth Social.

Finally, Ferguson’s Law—that any great power that spends more on debt interest than on defense risks ceases to be a great power—remains a binding constraint on U.S. public finance as the federal debt grows and interest rates remain elevated. This will make the president’s proposed big hike in defense spending difficult, and Republican legislators know it. The United States is certainly a stronger economy than Britain in 1956. But it is in a worse fiscal position. Perhaps AI will save the day with a productivity miracle, but don’t hold your breath. Right now the public backlash against data centers is moving faster than the productivity statistics.

That brings me to theory three: Mr. Market can be very wrong. Twenty years ago, I published a scholarly article about the European bond market in the summer of 1914. I asked why political events appeared to affect what was then the world’s biggest financial market, the London bond market, much less between 1881 and 1914 than they had between 1843 and 1880. In particular, I asked why the outbreak of World War I—an event traditionally seen as having been heralded by a series of international crises—was not apparently anticipated by investors.

Was it because of the spread of the gold standard, increased international financial integration, or changes in the fiscal policies of the great powers? I suggested that the increasing national separation of bond markets might offer a better explanation. But even this structural change could not explain why the London market was so slow to appreciate the risk of war in 1914. To investors such as the Rothschilds—supposedly the best-informed people in the world at that time, as their Wall Street counterparts are today—World War I truly came as a bolt from the blue.

There is a lesson here, and it is not “the wisdom of crowds.” However this war turns out, there will be (as was true of the trade war last year) a meaningful gap between reality and Truth Social. After all, Trump objectively lost the trade war with China. But how many Americans really know that, or care?

Perhaps the only way to tell if a triumph has really occurred is the old Roman way: See if the arch gets built. History is full of arches, as I began by saying. But the number of arches that were designed but never erected is not trivially small. As we wait to find out if this really was the “Seven Weeks’ War,” I note that the Arc de Trump exists on Truth Social, but not in reality.
