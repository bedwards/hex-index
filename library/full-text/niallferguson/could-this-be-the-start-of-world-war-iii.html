Four years ago, when Russia launched its full-scale invasion of Ukraine, people wondered if it was the beginning of World War III. Today, seven days into the latest war with Iran, the same concern has reemerged. It is not a foolish question.

Unlike World War I, which began in 1914 when all the European empires more or less simultaneously activated their war plans, World War II was in truth a series of regional conflicts that did not converge into a unitary global conflict until the end of 1941. Japan’s attack on China escalated slowly from 1931. The German challenge to the post-1918 European order began in 1936 and did not trigger war for three years, at which point the Soviet Union was on Germany’s side. The war in the Mediterranean did not begin until June 1940, with Italy’s declaration of war against the United Kingdom and France.

We have seen war break out in Eastern Europe in 2022 and then in the Middle East in 2023. The most recent U.S.-Israeli onslaught on Iran may look to future historians like a staging post to a global conflagration.

Blitzkrieg only works if you don’t run out of Blitz.

But I stress “may.” Because they may also simply write about Gulf War III. And the better ones may see that this war can be understood only in the context of Cold War II.

My base case is that the U.S.-Israeli war against the Islamic Republic of Iran cannot and will not be prolonged at the current frenetic intensity beyond a few weeks. In that case, the effects on the oil market will not be as large or as sustained as they were in Gulf War I (1990–91, when President George H.W. Bush drove Saddam Hussein out of Kuwait) and Gulf War II (when his son overthrew Saddam but then got bogged down in an Iraqi insurgency), and will be substantially less than the oil shocks of 1973–74 and 1979.

However, the timeline remains unclear. There is not yet a clear pathway to smooth “regime alteration”—a change at the top that defangs Iran as a threat to the United States and its allies. As long as what remains of the theocratic regime continues to fire missiles and drones at its neighbors, their citizens will live in fear.

Meanwhile, the rest of the world—disproportionately Europe and East Asia—will bear the economic costs of this fresh bout of geopolitical and economic uncertainty.

“We are well on our way to achieving the objectives of #EpicFury,” Secretary of State Marco Rubio wrote on X on Wednesday. “Destroy their missile launchers. Destroy their missile factories. Destroy their Navy. So they can never hide behind these things to develop a nuclear weapon.” That is good, even if it omits President Donald Trump’s original stated intention to help the Iranian people against their tyrannical rulers. But 10 questions remain to be answered to determine whether it’s Gulf War III, just the latest installment of Cold War II, or something as bad as World War III:

  1. How long will this war last?

  2. Do we have enough firepower relative to Iran?

  3. Can the Iranians keep the Strait of Hormuz closed?

  4. Can the Iranians hit Saudi and other Gulf oil infrastructure?

  5. At what point does Iranian regime alteration happen?

  6. How widespread will the war get?

  7. Can the U.S. get the oil flowing even if the war drags on?

  8. How big a shock would it take to damage the U.S. economy?

  9. How long can the Republican Party sustain this domestically in a midterm year?

  10. What does the war against Iran mean for other actual or potential conflicts?

1. How long will this war last?

The best way to look at this question is through the lens of energy. If this conflict drags on, and with it the disruption of the global oil market, the worst-case scenario is that we risk reenacting the 1970s.

In 1973, after the United States supported Israel in the Yom Kippur War, the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries (OAPEC) cut production targets and announced an embargo on the U.S. From October to November, OAPEC lowered global oil production by 7.5 percent, and by March 1974, oil prices (adjusted for consumer price inflation) had risen nearly 300 percent from prewar levels. Even after OAPEC lifted controls in March, the oil price fell by just 21 percent through the end of 1974. The shock sent consumer inflation surging to above 9 percent, tanked the stock market, and certainly contributed to the unraveling of Richard Nixon’s presidency.

A second price shock came with the Iranian Revolution, which disrupted 7 percent of global supply between October 1978 and January 1979, and sent oil prices up more than 150 percent. Oil did not return to its prerevolution price level until 1988. This time inflation peaked at just nearly 15 percent in early 1980. Jimmy Carter was the next president to pay the price of an oil shock.

Gulf War I began when Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait in August 1990. The oil price surged 151 percent. However, once the U.S. launched Operation Desert Storm in January 1991, that trend reversed. The oil price dropped around 35 percent in the day following the start of U.S. intervention. The impact on consumer price inflation was substantially less—the peak was 6.2 percent in December 1990—though it did not help Bush Senior in his doomed quest for a second term.

In the case of Gulf War II, the oil price rose 48 percent in the months leading up to the U.S. invasion of Iraq, dropped when it was clear that Saddam had been overthrown, but then remained elevated as the War on Terror dragged on messily for years.

The impact of Operation Epic Fury has already surpassed the peak of Operation Midnight Hammer—the U.S. air strikes on Iran last June. As I write, Brent crude is trading at $85.37 a barrel, 18 percent above where it stood on the eve of the war. Should the Strait of Hormuz remain closed for multiple weeks, the disruption to oil flows might begin to resemble the two major oil shocks of the 1970s and prices might go higher and stay there. On the other hand, it could be like June last year, when oil jumped roughly 18 percent in the first 48 hours as Israel struck Iranian nuclear and military sites, peaking at $80, but then sliding back to $67 as a ceasefire was agreed.

So how long will this last? According to Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth, “You can say four weeks, but it could be six, it could be eight, it could be three.” That’s fair. But wars that last, say, six days have happened in the Middle East. On the other hand, George W. Bush’s war lasted eight years. One report on Thursday said the Pentagon thinks this could go on until September. That would be bad.

2. Do we have enough firepower relative to Iran?

Blitzkrieg only works if you don’t run out of Blitz. Much depends on how long the U.S. and Israel can sustain their intensive bombing campaign and how quickly they can suppress Iran’s ability to fire missiles and drones. Armchair strategists began to fret about this early on, contrasting the limited numbers of interceptors for Patriot or THAAD systems in the Gulf region with the 2,000 ballistic missiles believed to be in Iranian hands, not to mention the even larger number of drones.

I was a little uneasy, I confess, when the president insisted on Tuesday that “the United States Munitions Stockpiles have, at the medium and upper medium grade, never been higher or better.” That did not read exactly like, well, Truth. However, Iran’s firepower appears to be fading rapidly. It is below what we saw in last year’s 12-day war. The number of ballistic missiles being fired at the United Arab Emirates (UAE) has declined steeply since the first day of the war. That is a sign that the U.S. and Israeli attacks on Iranian launchers are succeeding. The U.S. struck more than 1,700 targets in the first three days of the war. Israel flew 1,600 sorties, dropping 2,500 bombs in three days. These are remarkable figures. The goal is simultaneously to destroy the regime’s command and control system and to take out its military infrastructure. It seems to be working.

The problem is not the missiles, but the Shahed drones. Everyone ignored the Ukrainians at Davos when they screened a simulation of Russian Shaheds hitting Paris, Brussels, and Davos itself. No one in the Gulf was able to imagine them hitting Dubai or Riyadh—a strange failure of imagination considering the Shahed was invented in Iran.

3. Can the Iranians keep the Strait of Hormuz closed?

Right now the answer is yes—but not because the Iranians have massive firepower. A handful of strikes on tankers in the first phase of the war sufficed to scare both shippers and insurers, so that traffic is down by 90 percent or more. It is not only exports of Gulf oil and natural gas that are affected but also food imports to the Gulf states and fertilizer exports.

4. Can the Iranians hit or otherwise disrupt Saudi and other Gulf oil infrastructure?

Minor damage was caused on March 2 to the Saudi oil refinery at Ras Tanura and Qatar’s Ras Laffan Industrial City. There have also been threats to the city of Dhahran, where Saudi Aramco’s headquarters are located. But the most immediate consequence of the closing of the Strait of Hormuz is that much of Iraq’s crude production has shut down because of a lack of transport vessels and storage capacity. Multiple fields—totaling about 1.5 million barrels per day—have gone offline. That figure could rise to 3 million. And Iran could also wreak havoc by hitting Saudi Arabia’s desalination plants.

5. At what point does Iranian regime alteration happen?

If no one waves the white flag, do we have an intransigent Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) regime fighting to the death, or a compliant one, ready to negotiate? What is the risk of a civil war? Or a complete disintegration of Iran? Or intervention by another power?

It was not enough to kill Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. Israel is targeting scores of hard-liners within the IRGC, the clergy, the regime’s intelligence apparatus, and the bayt-e rahbari (Supreme Leader’s Office). On March 3, a building housing the office of the Assembly of Experts—the clerical body tasked with selecting Iran’s new supreme leader—was destroyed. Nevertheless, Khamenei’s son Mojtaba Khamenei is apparently on the brink of being named as his successor. The regime may be in its death throes, but it is not yet dead.

6. If Iran keeps fighting, how widespread can the conflict get?

By my count, this war is already affecting more than a dozen countries in the region. In addition to Iran and Israel, there have been missile and/or drone strikes on the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, and Oman. In Lebanon, IDF ground forces are going after Hezbollah. In Cyprus, Iran launched a drone attack on a British military base. In Iraq, pro-Iranian militias are causing trouble. Azerbaijan has now been hit. It is only a matter of time before the Houthis get Yemen involved. This is by far the most extensive Gulf War of them all.

Can it get even worse? Iran might be holding back missiles for one big, concerted strike on Gulf oil production. But that would be very risky because it might provoke its Arab neighbors into actively joining the air offensive, which would bring hundreds more aircraft into Iranian airspace.

7. Can the U.S. keep the oil flowing if the war drags on?

This is, in many ways, the crucial question. Insurers and protection and indemnity clubs did not waste time canceling war risk policies for vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz soon after the war began. Some are now demanding new premia far above the standard rate of 0.25 percent of a ship’s replacement value.

On Tuesday President Trump proposed that the U.S. Development Finance Corporation (DFC) “provide, at a very reasonable price, political risk insurance and guarantees for the Financial Security of ALL Maritime Trade, especially Energy, traveling through the Gulf.” He added that “the United States Navy will begin escorting tankers through the Strait of Hormuz, as soon as possible.”

This has not done the trick. Partly the problem is that the DFC’s $205 billion liabilities ceiling does not suffice to cover the sheer number of tankers going through the Strait—more than 350 a week in normal times. This could be an expensive scheme. The last time the U.S. issued major war risk coverage for merchant shipping, via $250 million of appropriations in World War II, the Treasury received only $91 million from the unwinding of the fund at the end of the war—a loss of $159 million, or $2.8 billion in today’s money. In any case, the U.S. Navy is too busy fighting a war to provide escort services to mostly foreign shipping companies. The response of most shippers and oil traders was deep skepticism about Trump’s proposal.

8. How big a shock would it take to affect the U.S. economy?

The U.S. economy slowed at the end of last year, growing at an annual rate of 1.4 percent in the fourth quarter, mainly but not only because of the government shutdown. Headline consumer price inflation was 2.4 percent in January, down from 2.7 percent in December. New jobs are still being created, but preponderantly in healthcare.

Since the war began, U.S. equities have been hit less hard than those of big oil-importing countries. Bonds have sold off modestly, while the dollar rallied relative to energy importers’ currencies (unlike in April’s tariff tantrum). The higher oil prices certainly won’t help with inflation and won’t make it easier for the Fed to cut rates this year. But we shall need to see much worse news and a much bigger oil price spike for real pain to be felt on both Wall Street and Main Street.

9. How long can the GOP sustain this domestically in a midterm year?

Unlike their British cousins, who now believe (if the MP Emily Thornberry is representative) that “wars are only ever resolved through negotiation,” Americans are still quite keen on military victory. It seems as if Republican voters will back Trump as long as the war doesn’t drag on too long.

The president’s only real domestic-political headache is the ever-widening split within the MAGA movement. Former Fox News anchors Tucker Carlson and Megyn Kelly are among those arguing that the United States has been duped into waging war on behalf of Israel. Implausibly, their case was also made by The New York Times. This surely misreads the evidence. It’s true that Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has long favored the assassination of Khamenei and the destruction of the Tehran regime. But it is not in Trump’s nature to do favors for other world leaders, especially when it involves serious economic and political risk.

This is by far the most extensive Gulf War of them all.

The reality is that Trump himself has been consistently hostile to Iran for his own good reasons. He never believed in the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action. He never trusted the Iranians when they were “talking and talking and talking.” And if MAGA has serious doubts about this venture, it’s odd that it was Vice President J.D. Vance who advised Trump to “go big and go fast.” True, the Israelis had hacked the traffic cameras in Tehran, enabling them to track Khamenei’s security detail, and did the cutting-edge social network analysis that laid bare the structure of power around the supreme leader. But it was a U.S. source (so the Financial Times reported) who confirmed where Khamenei would be on that fateful Saturday morning last week.

The reality is that the U.S. and Israel have mounted their joint operation because each has an interest in terminating the Islamic Republic. The Israeli interest is regional. The American interest is global.

10. What does the war against Iran mean for other actual or potential conflicts?

The United States is waging Cold War II with China. As I have argued before, a global struggle with a Communist superpower gives rise to a classic three-body problem: Inevitably, the United States is drawn toward three regions of the world, each of which exerts its own gravitational force. There is Europe or the North Atlantic. There is the Middle East or the Persian Gulf. And there is the Far East or the Indo-Pacific. (It’s really the three-bodies-of-water problem.) In practice, because resources are finite and Ferguson’s Law places an effective ceiling on the defense budget, the U.S. cannot be actively engaged in all three theaters at once. Indeed, these days it can handle only one war at a time.

So the war against Iran is not, from an American perspective, a regional undertaking for the benefit of Israel. It clearly does not help other American allies and friends. For the Europeans, India, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan—all major hydrocarbon importers—every day of this war imposes a heavy cost in the form of more expensive oil and gas. The war therefore makes sense only as part of the U.S. global cold war with China.

Does it help bring peace closer in Ukraine, where Russia can be considered as a Chinese proxy (inasmuch as Beijing has kept President Vladimir Putin’s war machine going with vast deliveries of dual-use technology)? Sadly, no. I asked a Ukrainian friend in the defense technology business what he thought.

“People don’t understand that Dubai is about to look like Kyiv like in 10 days,” he texted back, “and Iranians make 3000+ shaheds [drones] per month. U.S. made 650 PAC3s [Patriot missile interceptors] last year and in the end this is going to majorly fuck Ukraine because nobody will give Ukr any air defenses because Gulf will just buy it all.”

Nor does it help for Trump to be reminded that one reason the U.S. is short of interceptors is that his predecessor gave a significant number to Ukraine’s president Volodymyr Zelensky.

Moreover, higher oil prices and a decline in the global supply of sanctioned crude will benefit Russia’s budget, which had been suffering from the low price of Urals oil. Already the U.S. has begun quietly easing sanctions on Russian oil to try to offset the effects of the Hormuz closure. Trilateral U.S.-Russia-Ukraine talks were supposed to be happening in Abu Dhabi this week. Needless to say, they were postponed.

We are left with China. “What is your message to Iran’s allies, namely Russia and China?” Pete Hegseth was asked yesterday. “They are not really a factor here,” he replied.

Sure.

Almost all of Iran’s exported oil—520 million U.S.-sanctioned barrels—went to China last year, about 15 percent of Chinese seaborne crude. Iran was second only to Saudi Arabia as China’s main supplier. Another 1.4 billion barrels came from the five other countries that ship oil through the Strait of Hormuz. Put differently, 38 percent of oil passing through the Strait of Hormuz went to China in Q125. At the same time, Iran’s aggressive missile-building program was a Chinese-enabled project, entirely reliant on Chinese components, machine tools, and technical guidance, as Zineb Riboua has pointed out.

The war therefore makes sense only as part of the U.S. global cold war with China.

Consider this war from Beijing’s perspective. First the United States ousts Nicolás Maduro and ends Venezuela’s role as a Chinese client and source of sanctioned oil. Then the United States offs Ali Khamenei and ends Iran’s role as a Chinese client and source of sanctioned oil. Quite a flex, isn’t it? No mean power play ahead of the summit between Presidents Trump and Xi Jinping in Beijing at the end of this month.

Unless the war drags on.

China is far less reliant on Gulf oil than America’s Asian allies because it has been busily building up its strategic petroleum reserves. As of March 2026, China holds around 1.3 billion barrels in storage—enough to cover over 100 days of imports if the Strait of Hormuz stays shut. Japan relies on the Strait of Hormuz for approximately 31 percent of its total energy needs, South Korea 30 percent, and Taiwan 38 percent. For China the figure is 10 percent.

A prolonged war in the Gulf would also be a serious problem for American deterrence in the Indo-Pacific as it would run down stocks of all kinds of American weaponry that is expensive and slow to replace: not only PAC-3 magazines but also SM-6 missiles, for example. The race is on to replenish U.S. capabilities in Asia with the next generation of lower-cost, faster-made weapons: what the military men call “cheap kill.” Hypersonic missiles like Castelion’s; airframes like Divergent Technologies’; futuristic supply planes like JetZero’s; autonomous drone swarms like Auterion’s. But it is a race. And the faster the United States runs to apply the lessons of wars in Ukraine and now Iran—above all, the need for low-cost, large-scale weapons systems—the greater the risk that China decides to make its move against Taiwan now. Before we are ready.

Unlike Russia’s invasion of Ukraine four years ago, the U.S.-Israeli air war on Iran really ought to be short. However, uncertainty lingers over the Iranian regime’s resilience—the depth and breadth of its fanaticism—and its capacity to inflict enough damage to keep the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed or otherwise to reduce Gulf oil exports. Under these circumstances, as in 2022, the world’s importers of fossil fuels must scramble for scarcer and dearer resources. The longer the war lasts, the heavier the costs for Asian and European oil and gas importers, and the more money for Russia.

Even if there is a swift regime alteration in Iran, the world will not swiftly revert to the status quo ante. Defense expenditures will continue to rise. Investments in the new generation of unmanned weapons systems and drone defenses will grow. There will be more, not less, nuclear proliferation. And the two superpowers will inexorably draw closer to some kind of moment of confrontation.

This year, the war in Iran probably reduces the risk of a new conflict in East Asia. But what happens in 2027 and 2028 will depend on who wins Gulf War III and how quickly they win it. Gulf War I (1990–91) was short. Gulf War II (2003–2011) was not. This isn’t World War III. But if it drags on, Gulf War II is potentially an event as significant as the 1973–74 oil shock. As well as being economically disastrous, that was one of the more dangerous moments in Cold War I. Today is best understood as an equally dangerous moment in Cold War II.
