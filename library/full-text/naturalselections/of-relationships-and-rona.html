Early 2020, life as we knew it took an unexpected turn. Talk of viruses. Compulsory masks. Lockdown. Confusion. Fear. A long list of new rules. Can I walk the dog? Can my kids still play sport? Is the gym open? Friday night drinks? What about my niece’s wedding? Welcome to Victoria, Australia, the lockdown capital of the freakin’ world.

Used to backyard barbies, most Aussies were prepared to ‘do their bit’ to beat ‘Rona. But days in iso—isolation—turned into weeks. The gym remained closed, weddings were postponed indefinitely, and we started losing touch with our friends and family. Students were skipping online lessons to lose themselves in gaming, doom scrolling, or lying semi-comatose on their unmade beds amid a nest of food scraps and apathy.

With hubby still off to his job in ‘essential services’ each day, I felt the isolation like everyone else. There I was, clinging to the edge of the curtains in the front room, staring mindlessly out at the deserted street.

My elderly neighbors, Jim and Gail, were quite active in our area before ‘Rona came to town. Worried about Gail, who suffered from depression, I scanned the street before sneaking across to their door. Gail’s pale smile contrasted with the dark smudges under her eyes.

After we’d shared tea, chocolate bikkies1 and a few belly laughs, I felt better. We all felt better – for a while. Gail was doing it much tougher than me and it occurred to me that for many old folks still living at home, my short term iso was their normality. A scary thought, but what could I do to help?

I decided to contact Ageless, a community group that matches volunteers with elders as buddies. On the form, I ticked “female”, “lives locally”, “creative”, “educated”, and “a reader”, as my preferences. Two days later I received a response. They matched me with an old bloke, an ex-motor mechanic, living an hour and a half away. Really? I had had a difficult relationship with my own father, similar vintage, and was unsure what I could offer this old guy.

“I don’t know,” I told Fiona from Ageless.

“Look, Doug’s a lovely old man, easy to talk to, and he’s happy just to have a phone chat once every week or two.”

I sighed. What if we didn’t click? If I was going to do this, it would have to be a genuine connection or I would lose interest.

“He’d really appreciate it. His wife died a couple of years ago and he’s pretty lonely.”

“Hasn’t he got family?” I asked.

“Yeah, but … you know. They’re busy doing their thing.”

“Hmm, s’pose.” I still wasn’t buying it. “But wouldn’t he be better off with a man to talk to?”

“No, that’s the thing,” said Fiona. “He said everyone around him is a man, and with his wife gone, he just misses talking to a woman for a change.”

My heart went out to him in that moment. “Alright, alright, I’ll do it.”

“You will? Great. Thank you so much, Donna.” Fiona’s voice was coated in a thick layer of professional sweetness. “I’ll send you the details.”

I hung up the phone, called my dog, Molly, and headed to the park for my allowed exercise.

Crossing the carpark at the entrance to the oval2, I heard laughter. Adult laughter. What a strange sound. I looked about and noticed feet sticking out the open tailgate of a nearby four-wheel drive.

“Hey, Donna!”

There was one of my neighbors, a young mum, sitting in the back of the vehicle with another woman. I grinned at them. “Mel, hi, what are you two up to? An illegal gathering?”

“Oh, god yeah. I was going off my nut!’ Mel exclaimed. “Do you know Nisha? She owns that cute pair of cavoodles3 that are always tied up out the front of the coffee shop.”

“Hey, not always! Not now anyway. Can’t even get a cup of coffee these days.” Nisha’s face was mournful for a moment before fixing me with a broad grin. “Hi, Donna, nice to meet you. Mel speaks highly of you.”

I laughed. “Hi, Nisha. Yeah, well, Mel doesn’t get out much.”

“That’s true!” Mel lamented, and we all laughed.

I continued on into the park, past the closed signs plastered over the play equipment. It had felt so good to talk to people. Maybe connecting with Doug would be good for me too, as long as we could find something to talk about. I unclipped Molly, threw the ball, and the worry dropped away as I watched her, like a furry bullet, race after it.

The next day, I got the go-ahead from Fiona and called Doug.

“Hello?” His voice was gentle, modulated.

“Hi Doug, this is Donna. I’m … er … the volunteer who was going to call? To have a chat …?”

Doug turned out to be a very interesting and lovely old guy. Apart from being a motor mechanic, he had also been a prison officer, a keen glider pilot, a short-wave radio enthusiast and a long-term community volunteer working with disadvantaged youth.

I was curious about Doug’s loneliness. Didn’t he have family nearby?

“Yes, but you know, when you don’t get out and do much, there’s not much to talk about. They already know everything.”

I thought back to my mother, a widow living alone. Her place was a couple of hours away and my visits were not regular. When I did visit, I would pull the local paper over to me and scan it as we chatted over tea and biscuits.

One day, as I was half listening to her, she said, with emotion in her voice, “Talk to me. You’re the same as your brothers. You all come in and sit down and read the paper. Just talk to me.”

I stared at her pain-filled expression in silence. She had mentioned how lonely she was in the past, but I had never understood what that meant. In her face, in her voice, in the splaying of her hands, I had my first glimpse of just how crushing loneliness could be.

“Okay, sorry.” I closed the paper and pushed it from me.

Mum passed before ‘coronavirus’ became a household word, but as we all sank deeper into iso that scene would return to haunt me, bringing a more profound understanding of what she felt.

From then on, I rang Doug each week, often over a cup of tea and a biscuit, just as if we were sitting together. One day, I discovered he had a computer and was attending online meetings with his fellow radio enthusiasts.

“Wait, you have a computer?”

“Yes, I do.”

“Well, why don’t we have an online meeting?”

It was funny seeing the person behind the gentle, melodic voice, and I’m sure he felt the same curiosity. We had a few online chats before returning to the simplicity and reliability of a phone call. Then we started sending each other jokes, photos and even links to video clips through email. Sometimes Doug prefaced his with “Hoping this doesn’t offend.”

One of the first things Doug told me was how he had lost his wife to cancer a couple of years before. He missed her terribly.

“I’ll probably talk about her one day, but I’m not ready yet,” he said.

“That’s fine, Doug. No hurry. We’ve got lots to talk about,” I reassured him.

Doug slowly opened up over time until eventually “my wife” became “Val”, and I learned they’d been inseparable since they were teenagers.

Once, between lockdowns, my husband and I visited Doug at his home on our way back from a camping trip. Doug was thrilled to have us there, and we had a lovely time at his dining table swapping stories and just enjoying each other’s company.

We had been friends for more than a year when I received a call from Fiona at Ageless informing me that as part of their compliance for funding, all volunteers had to be fully vaccinated against Covid.

“I’m sorry. I know you were reluctant to get it. I need to ask you, have you been vaccinated?” Her tone showed discomfort.

“No, and I won’t be.” I felt myself prickle.

“Well, unfortunately, that means we are going to have to take you off the books.”

“What? That’s ridiculous. We only have phone contact.” I stopped myself from saying more.

“Yes, I know, but our policy states …”

She droned on, but I stopped listening.

“You are welcome to continue to call Doug if that’s what you both want, but it won’t be through us. I’ll be contacting him to explain the situation and to ask him if he wants us to provide a new volunteer.”

“Sure.” I had disengaged.

“Okay, well, thanks, and sorry once again. If you ever change your mind and get vaccinated, give me a call. We’d love to have you working with us again.”

“Thanks, and if your organization ever sees sense and drops this nonsensical requirement, you give me a call,” I responded a little smugly. She gave a nervous laugh.

“Oh, that won’t be happening. It’s been written into our funding agreement for the next five years.”

I rang Doug the moment I got off the phone. I wanted to reassure him that I was not about to disappear. We had discussed Covid, vaccines, and the whole bit. He was vaccinated. He knew I wasn’t. No big deal.

“So, it doesn’t really change anything with us. We can still call and chat. It just means it’s not through them anymore,” I explained.

“Of course we can. That’s ridiculous.”

“Fiona will call to see if you want a new volunteer.”

“I feel like just telling them to bugger off,” he muttered.

“Well, think about it. You know? You might like to chat with another person, that’s up to you.”

“Okay,” he responded. “I’m not keen at the moment, but I’ll think about it.”

And with that, Doug and I stepped away from being an official arrangement and became just two friends who called each other and kept in touch.

Doug said yes to the agency, and they found a local woman who popped around to see him. He told me about it when we were next chatting. “Yeah, they sent someone around. I suppose she’s alright, nice enough, but she’s not you.”

Doug probably had no idea how much that little acknowledgement meant to someone who had spent a lot of her life trying to be seen as normal, especially so in those troubled and divisive times.

The new volunteer didn’t last long, but Doug’s and my relationship continued to strengthen. I wanted to visit him again, but wasn’t sure what his family would think. There was a lot of irrational fear in the community about the unvaccinated. Human interaction had become fraught with danger.

I didn’t tell Doug when I was sacked from my teaching job for refusing to get the jab, but it was a very hard time as my husband lost his state government job around the same time. We had signed a contract to build a new home earlier in the year and were worried we might lose everything.

Fortunately (or one could also say, ‘ludicrously’), the jab mandates were only at a state level. Federal government employees were not being forced to take it. Within a few months we had both gained new jobs. In my new role, I found several others like me, refugees from the Covid mandate. It felt so good to have others to talk to about the madness.

I talked up my new role to Doug. He told me about his latest booster. It worried me, but I said nothing. What a fucked-up world we were living in.

I saw Doug early in 2022, when he came to my city to participate in a documentary on our old prison. He had worked there for some years during the 1960’s. He showed me through the old bluestone structure, now sitting empty, and related some of the horror stories from his time there as a guard.

At the end of June ’22, on a Thursday afternoon, I was working in the office waiting for Doug to call as he had promised, but the call never came. I tried to contact him repeatedly the following day, but there was no answer. I was worried, it was so unlike him, but I didn’t have any other way of reaching him.

Two days later, after much searching, I found the social media group for the local short wave radio club he belonged to, and there was his death notice.

It hit me hard and I felt very alone in my grief. Did the family even know I existed? Did they care? The stigma of being unvaccinated was something ever present in my dealings with life.

Through the post on socials I found his son. I sent him a message introducing myself, and gave him my number.

The following day, Doug’s daughter called me. To my surprise and delight, they knew all about me and had been trying to find me. Would I please come to the funeral? And another call later, would I please say a few words at the funeral because, in his last years, I was probably closer to Doug than anyone else.

Doug was a healthy 85 year old who went to bed as usual one night, but didn’t get up the next morning. His family found him later In the day, with his little dog curled up faithfully by his side. He had no history of a heart condition. He might have lived until he was a hundred.

Still, he went to a better place, reunited with Val. His family hadn’t visited him much during his last year or so because they wanted to keep him safe from the virus. How ironic. How sad. His daughter would sometimes stand in his yard and talk to him, but Doug desperately wanted her to come inside, put her feet under the table and have a cuppa with him. How long had he lived without the warmth of a loved one’s embrace?

I attended Doug’s funeral and met his family and friends. They thanked me for all I had done for Doug. When I spoke, I shared some of Doug’s loneliness and his life stories. Just as importantly, I told them what their father, grandfather, mate, had done for me. Doug was a true gentleman. He was thoughtful, dignified, curious, caring, and had a great sense of humor.

Life has moved on and I’ve reluctantly returned to a job in education. I still find myself talking to Doug at times. My relationship with Doug restored some of my faith in humanity, at a time when common decency was severely lacking. Like many others around the globe, Doug should still be alive. He will, however, live forever in my heart.
