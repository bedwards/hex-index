Here, I explain why the government does not have authority just because it’s democratic.*

1. Naive Majoritarianism

I’ve written elsewhere about the problem of political authority: Roughly speaking, what gives 535 clowns in Washington the right to tell everybody else what to do?

If you raise this issue in class, someone is sure to say, very early on, that those people have the right to rule because we chose them to rule over us, through the ballot box. (This applies to only about half the world’s nations. On this view, the other governments are all illegitimate.)

To illustrate, consider a hypothetical:

Democratic Dinner: I’m out to dinner with four friends. At the end of the meal, we discuss how to split the bill. John T. Kennedy suggests that I should pay for everyone’s meal. I decline. John, however, decides to take a vote, whereupon it emerges that the other four people all want me to pay. “Well, that settles it,” he declares. “Pay up.”

Am I now obligated to pay for everyone’s meal? If I refuse, are they entitled to force me to pay?

If you answered “no,” you may have some common sense.

What this example suggests, apart from that I need new friends, is that the will of a majority does not override or nullify the rights of a minority or an individual. I have rights over my money; those rights are not suspended just because a larger number of people want to take it from me.

What needs to be accounted for, in the case of government, is why the government may do things that would violate rights if anyone else were to do them. That is why political authority poses a philosophical problem. Appeal to the will of the majority cannot answer that. The majority are not entitled to violate the rights of a minority; nor, therefore, can they delegate the authority to do so to a government.

2. Deliberative Democracy

But some philosophers still believe that democracy in some way confers political legitimacy. One school of thought is the “deliberative democracy” people, such as Jürgen Habermas and Joshua Cohen. I find them hard to understand, so this exposition will be vague. I think their view is that

(a) In a democracy, people ideally ought to deliberate in a certain way about public questions, with everyone having an equal voice, everyone seeking to make decisions based on the reasons offered, etc.

(b) If this happened, the outcome would be legitimate, in the sense that the state would be entitled to coercively impose any policies thus arrived at.

(c) This has some sort of relevance to why some actual governments have authority. (?)

I have two problems with this argument:

The infeasibility of deliberative democracy

First: No society does anything like what these thinkers imagine, nor would it be feasible to do so in any large society. It is not possible, e.g., for 100 million people to all be heard. Political decisions in democratic societies are the product of such things as the whims of uninformed and irrational voters, favor-trading between politicians, lobbying by special interest groups, and deception and manipulation by politicians. They are not the product of ideal, rational deliberation among all interested parties.

This being the case, it is unclear how the hypothetical scenario of a “deliberative democracy” has any bearing on the legitimacy of any actual governments, now or in the future—except perhaps to underline how far they are from being legitimate.

The irrelevance of deliberation

The second problem is that even if the ideal of deliberative democracy were realized, it would not confer any authority. You cannot suspend other people’s rights merely by deliberating in a special way before performing an otherwise rights-violating action.

In the Democratic Dinner example, suppose that, before demanding that I pay for the dinner, the people at the table all engage in an amazing deliberation, in which everyone has an equal voice, everyone presents their reasons for and against forcing me to pay for the meal, etc. Only after doing that do they all vote to make me pay. Am I now obligated to pay? Are they now entitled to force me to pay?

3. Equality and Authority

The other major strand of argument for the authority of democratic government appeals to the value of equality. Roughly, the idea is that

  1. We are obligated to treat each other as equals.

  2. To do this, one must obey democratically-made laws (with qualifications**).

  3. Therefore, we are obligated to obey democratically-made laws.

(**Advocates of this argument, such as Thomas Christiano, make exceptions for blatantly inegalitarian laws.)

Why believe (2)? There are two main lines of thought there:

(i) If you refuse to go along with democratic decisions, then you are placing your judgment above that of the majority of your fellow-citizens.

(ii) To treat everyone as equals, you have to support equal advancement of everyone’s interests. Democracy is needed to promote the equal advancement of everyone’s interests. (Cue arguments that all other forms of social organization do much worse.) So you have to support democratic government. To do that, you have to obey democratically-made laws.

I remain unpersuaded …

Why equality?

What does it mean to treat each other as equals, and why should we do it?

Argument (i) above suggests that we have to treat everyone as having equally sound judgment about policy issues. But everyone obviously does not have equally sound judgment. Some are much smarter than others; some are much more rational; some are much more benevolent. It is thus unclear why we would be obligated to act as though that were not the case.

How to treat others as equal

If there is a sense in which we (citizens) are all equal, it would be that we have equal rights. In that case, I suggest that treating everyone as equals in the relevant sense would amount to respecting the same rights for everyone. This does not require obeying commands issued by a majority, and certainly not commands that themselves violate rights.

Rights before equality

Again, the problem of authority is specifically a problem of justifying government actions that would violate rights if done by anyone else. Why can the government do such things? The equality argument does not answer this, because the importance of respecting others’ equality does not outweigh or nullify individual rights.

Suppose that I explicitly refuse to recognize others’ equality, though I do not violate their rights. E.g., suppose I explicitly tell my friends that I consider them inferior to me (in whatever sense is relevant to the equality argument). I’m not coercing them, taking their property, or anything like that, but I am regarding them as unequal. Can they now use force against me, to punish me or stop me from continuing my equality-denying behavior?

Clearly not. Nor, similarly, can they justify taking my money on the grounds that I have to give them the money in order to “treat them as equals.” Nor, finally, can a majority of fellow citizens justify coercing me to obey their will on the grounds that I have to obey them in order to treat them as equals.

It is, by the way, kind of bizarre to suggest that if I don’t want to follow other people’s orders (e.g., I don’t want to give them my money, or I want to use recreational drugs that they disapprove of), then I am treating them as unequal. It rather seems to me that they would be treating me as unequal by issuing orders of a kind that everyone agrees would infringe on my rights if issued by a non-democratic organization.

But even if you buy that I would be guilty of treating others as unequal, that just doesn’t stop it from being the case that I am acting within my rights.

The imperfect duty of support

Argument (ii) supposed that we’re obligated to support the equal advancement of everyone’s interests. Maybe that’s true, but if it is, this would be an “imperfect duty.” I.e., you’re not obligated to support that in every possible way on every possible occasion.

So suppose that I have $1000 that I can either use to pay my taxes or donate to a charity that will fight inequality (in whatever sense you think is relevant). Assume—as is entirely realistic—that the charitable donation would do more to promote equal advancement of interests than the tax payment.

Advocates of government authority would want to say that I have to pay the tax, and I do not have to donate to the charity. But if the duty to obey the law (including paying taxes) is founded on the duty to support equal advancement of interests, then there would clearly be stronger reason to give the money to the charity. So, if I’m choosing between the two, I should donate to the charity. It can’t be that I’m obligated to pay the tax but not to give to the charity.

4. Democratic Defects

The inference that because we live in a democracy, the people have approved all the laws is a fiction. These are reasons why, even if you thought that the four dinner guests should be able to force the fifth to pay for everyone’s meal, you still shouldn’t conclude that it is legitimate to enforce all the laws in a democratic society:

  • We were never given a choice about whether to have a government in the first place, or what form of government to have, with what institutional structure, what offices, what procedural rules, etc.

  • Voters often say that they do not want either of the candidates they are offered.

  • Candidates can be elected on campaign promises that they are free to break after taking office.

  • Candidates give very little information about what they plan to do in the first place, and elections are determined more by such things as looks, height, and group affiliations.

  • Elections are strongly influenced by money, and only people with connections to wealthy donors have a realistic chance of attaining high-profile public offices. This is one reason why most politicians are lawyers.

  • In the U.S., the party in power in a given state commonly uses that power to draw districts in weird shapes so as to ensure that they remain in power (the practice of “gerrymandering”).

  • Not all representation is proportional to population. Wyoming and California each have two Senators, meaning that one Wyoming resident gets about the same Senate representation as 67 Californians.

  • Voters are commonly extremely uninformed or ill-informed, largely because it is not in their interests to learn about political issues. They thus have little idea of what they are voting for.

  • Most laws are not even made by elected officials. They are made by unelected bureaucrats. (See the >180,000 pages of the Code of Federal Regulations.)

  • Even when elected officials pass a law, they generally do not read it before voting.

  • Many laws were made a long time ago and might no longer pass today. To some extent, we’re being ruled by past generations who are now dead.

Thus, even in a nominally democratic state, the laws are not necessarily authorized by the people.

5. Conclusion

Do Donald Trump and Chuck Schumer have the right to tell you what to do, because they were elected?

No, they do not.
