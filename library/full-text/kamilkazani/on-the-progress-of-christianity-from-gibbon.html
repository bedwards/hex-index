1. By the end of Republic, faith in the old Roman gods was already in a visible decline. Although it still remained a part of the state ritual, and participation in the ritual was compulsory, fewer and fewer people were finding any deep meaning in these rituals. Like, as a citizen, as an official, as a member of a corporation, or just an inhabitant of a town, you were still obliged to take part in all these (religious) festivities, ceremonies, sacrifices to gods. But all of that was merely one’s public, official duty. The actual, private belief in anything of that was in a steep decline long before the crucifixion of Christ, and long, long, before a first Christian set his foot on the land of Italy.

  2. As it usually happens, decline of the old faith started the polite, fashionable society, in the upper echelons of social hierarchy. Philosophical scepticism gained its first foothold there, and, from this upper echelons trickled down the hierarchy, to the lower and lower floors. It is very important to notice, that it was not a result of any particular foreign influence, or whatever. The deconstruction and denigration of the old religion was started by the Roman elites themselves. From there, it trickled down.

  3. Now it was of course not supposed to trickle down. The Roman elites still believed that religion was good and necessary, for the plebs. Them not believing in old gods anymore, and not retaining much faith in the rituals (which were still compulsory!) was supposed to be a topic of intellectual conversation in the fashionable society, and nothing more. This knowledge was supposed to be limited to the upper elites, and sort of hidden from the simpletons who could not live without their silly old superstitions. In the long run, however, this knowledge turned out to be impossible to hide.

  4. Popular masses have a natural instinct to mimic their social betters, and emulate their opinions. They soon started to notice that smart, sophisticated people, their masters do not believe in Jupiter anymore. It may be difficult to establish the exact ways this knowledge was revealed to the simpletons. It may very well be that it was leaked out by their slaves, clientele, all the sorts of dependant people. You can normally assume that masters cannot hide very much from their servants and slaves, and that slaves know their masters way, way better than their masters know them. Anyway, once the masses realised that - according to the fashionable society - Jupiter does not exist, and rituals do not work, that was a mortal blow upon the old Roman religion. Its decline created a lacuna that must have been filled with something.

  5. For the most part, it was filled by the oriental cults, and religions. Interestingly enough, the native cults and religions of the Western part of the Roman Empire do not seem to affect much cultural influence upon the Latin core of Rome. To the contrary, they themselves become thoroughly latinised. That is, perhaps, because all these Gaulic and Celtiberic tribes were deemed so infinitely below in terms of culture and sophistication, that - from the Roman perspective - they just did not have anything interesting to say. Nobody in Rome was emulating druids, nobody was adopting and western gods.

  6. The Eastern gods, however, aggressively expanded into Rome itself. Notice, that their expansion was (often) considered troublesome and problematic, even back then. We should not exaggerate the actual extent of Roman tolerance, and political correctness. They may not have had a religious fanaticism, at least at that point, but they most certainly had ethnic prejudices and biases. The Egyptian religion, in particular, was seen as especially alien and repelling. I recommend you to check out Juvenal’s Satire 15, ‘An Egyptian Atrocity’. The negative representation of Egyptians by a Roman satirist is oddly resembling negative stereotypes of the South Asians, in modern social media.

(Stereotypes of other orientals, hellenized or not, were no less negative. Syrians were problematic, with their Orontes “polluting” Roman Tiber. The Jews most certainly were. In fact, when you are trying to understand popular imagination of a culture, I very much recommend you to start with comedy and satire. Like, were I trying to penetrate into the ancient Athenian imagination, I would of course start with Aristophanes and leave all other “serious” authors for later. Read comedy, my friends. That advice well applies both to the ancient and to the modern cultures)

  1. Despite all the negative stereotyping, and regardless of the periodic repressions and persecutions, the oriental gods have successfully expanded into Italy and, with time, their worship was completely normalised. I remember a very vivid impression of walking through the ruins of ancient Ostia - the seaport of Rome. Serapeum, Serapeum, Mithraeum, Serapeum again. All around, I see the temples of eastern - and disproportionately Egyptian gods. Obviously, by the Egyptian gods I mean not so much the original Egyptian religion as it existed in the times of pharaohs, but rather a certain Hellenic-Egyptian synthesis that originated from the conquests of Alexander the Great. Anyway, by the 2nd century - that is where these ruins date from - this syncretic religion basically won, being both popular and well accepted in the metropole itself.

(Check out The Golden Ass by Apuleius. That is the only Roman novel that survived, and - most fortunately - it is a comedy. This book gives a good insight into how popular and how influential were Egyptian gods in the Rome of the Antonines. But I don’t want to give you any more spoilers)

  1. Through the troubles of the late 2nd and 3rd centuries, Rome was seeing many and many revolutions, supreme power circulating from one ambitious provincial to another. And it were “the princes of Asiatic extraction”, specifically from Syria, that did the most for the progress of yet another oriental sect - Christianity.

  2. It all started with Alexander Severus. And the thing with Alexander Severus, is that he was not actually a Severus. Despite bearing the name of Septimius Severus, the founder of the dynasty, he did not bear any biological connection to him. He was a Syrian, from Homs (then called, Emesa).

  3. Long before assuming the throne, Alexander Severus married a Syrian woman. She was coming from the local dynasty of priest-kings who ruled over the city of Emesa, and worshipped a Syrian-Hellenistic deity Heliogabalus. Like, the Egyptian deity Serapis that conquered a huuuge following in Rome, was a Hellenistic version of Osyris, Heliogabalus was a local version of Baal.

  4. So, this Emesene family, who worked as the priests of Baal, acquired lots of power and influence, first gaining senatorial dignity (within the Rome itself), and them marrying their girl to a future emperor of Rome.

  5. After the true, biological line of Severan dynasty was interrupted, these Emesene guys (who were not connected to Septimius biologically) claimed the throne, and continued to rule under the Severan name. The first emperor of what was in fact the new Emesene dynasty was Sextus Varius Avitus Bassianus nicknamed Heliogabalus after the Baal-ic deity he worshipped.

  6. The second emperor was Alexander Severus, his brother. Now this prince put a status of Christ in his home shrine, together with other deities, effectively adding Jesus to an already existing syncretic pantheon.

  7. Now few revolutions later, Rome was ruled by another Syrian, Philip the Arab. Coming from the city of Busra, he rose to the chief of the Praetorian Guard, overthrew the sitting emperor and became an emperor himself. Upon seizing power he showed so much protection to Christianity, that some believed he was actually a Christian himself. He may or may not have been, anyway, he seems to be the very first Roman emperor suspected of being a secret Christian worshipper.

  8. We have an interesting indirect evidence of the protection & promotion he showed to the Christian sect. When he was overthrown and killed himself, by yet another usurper, the usurper ordered to massacre Philipp’s entire family and court. In Christian tradition, these massacres were usually labeled as yet another religious persecution of Christians (which was, technically speaking false). It is just that the court of Philipp had so many Christians, that the usurper exterminating that court, was later labeled as a religious purge. Which it wasn’t.

  9. Speaking of purges. We tend to somewhat exaggerate their overall extent and impact, portraying the history of Christianity under the Roman power as a never-ending mayhem of slaughter and violent persecution. This picture, of course, includes some elements of truth. But it is too simplistic and taken altogether, false. Were Christianity to be systematically exterminated, it probably would have been exterminated altogether, like for example in Japan1.

  10. In practice, Roman treatment of Christians was inconsistent and idiosyncratic. Yes, being a Christian, and particularly refusing to participate in compulsory religious ceremonies remained a crime. But the specific implementation of this law varied greatly from region to region and from era to era. Waves of violent persecution were alternated with the long periods of grudging and dismissive toleration. Perhaps, even more importantly the actual regions of persecution were almost always limited to a specific city, province, or town. Nero’s repressions, for example, were mostly limited to the city of Rome, and did not really target Christians in other cities. What you had was some strikes falling on some Christian communities in some regions of the empire from time to time.

(Some emperors tried to uproot Christianity, others tried to regulate it. The famous restorer of the empire Ambrosius Aurelian, reviving it from the 3rd century crisis, inclined to the latter approach. Aiming to restore the supremacy of Rome over all the separatist provinces, he sided with bishops of Rome over their rivals from the East. I wonder to which degree the later Popes of Rome derived their absolute power over the Christian community, from a Pagan emperor who was simply trying to centralise the empire, and concentrate all the decision making powers, on every level, in the capital)

  1. What is more, Roman persecutions lacked the theist zeal that would later make the Christian church to so mercilessly stomp on all the various heresies. Roman magistrates disdained the Christians, despised them, were surprised and astonished by their superstitions. Yet, they did not really hate them, nor did they see any particular reason to fear. By and large, they treated them relatively mild, if not by the standards of religious tolerance of today, then, at least, by the standards of the later Christian rulers and prelates of the Church. Christians, once they came to power, treated each other infinitely more cruelly (and thoroughly, and systematically), than they had been ever treated by the Pagans.

(Here, I must say, I see certain parallel between the Christian and the Communist sects. Communists, when they came to power, were slaughtering each other way more diligently and zealously than they had been ever persecuted by the Tsarist authorities)

  1. The true, mass, and the empire wide persecution, came only at the very end of the Pagan Rome, in the age of Diocletian. By that time, the Christian Church got powerful enough, and popular enough, and influential enough to truly scare much of the existing elites and the established priesthood of other religion, which simply had not been the case in the age of the Antonines. By that time, many and many saw a real threat in the propagation of that odd sect, and feared its influence. We can see it, because around this period the persecutors - for the first time - started copying Christian practices, emulating rather than disdaining the persecuted sect. Fearing the influence of a Christian church, they tried to build a parallel Pagan church, largely mirroring the Christian one, in its discipline, hierarchy, territorial organisation.

  2. These efforts, however, resulted in almost nothing. Almost immediately after publishing his empire wide edict in Nicomedia, suppressing Christianity (it is noteworthy enough, that we don’t have the text of this edict, and know it based only upon more or less vague descriptions. it is important to understand that we don’t really know the history of the period very well, it is all rife with holes, and assumptions, and extrapolations. we know that Diocletian suppressed Christianity, but we are not very sure what he said which exact measures he prescribed), Diocletian retired from power, leaving the empire to four new emperors (check out the Tetrarchy). Unsurprisingly, the system of Tetrarchy broke down very soon, and all these new Augusts and Caesars started fighting each other, as soon as the master was gone.

  3. Now a one single emperor of the united empire might want at least to try to fight Christianity, at this point. But four, and then very soon five Caesars locked in a merciless Darwinian competition against each other just could not afford that. Some of them never really wanted to fight Christianity in the first place. Others tried to, but soon found themselves compelled to win the favour of a too popular and too influential sect. By the moment Constantine started his march south, from York to Nicomedia, all the real and potential claimants for the Roman throne competed in who will be a better defender and protector of the Christian religion.

  4. Consider the battle of Malvian bridge traditionally celebrated as a Christian triumph over the Paganism. Nothing, however, can be further from truth. By the moment of the battle, both Constantine, the emperor of Gaul, and Maxentius, the emperor of Italy were patrons and protectors of the Christian creed. So what happened is that one protector of Christianity killed and defeated the other. And moreover, when he moved further east, to fight the emperor of the East Licinius, Licinius, too, was the protector of Christians. By the late stages of the civil war on the ruins of Diocletian’s tetrarchy, the question was not whether a Christian prince will defeat a Pagan one, but rather which of three very and very pro-Christians princes will triumph in the end.
