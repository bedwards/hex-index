Today, I am going to introduce some basic ideas for the general theory of revolution

Notice, that I am not discussing any political change in particular, but laying the basic concepts that will serve as a groundwork for the future empirical discussions. For now, a bit of theory.

What do we need to know about the revolution?

First. That revolution never happens at once - by a single blow - but over the time, iteratively. A not bad first approximation for how does the revolution works in practice would be picturing it as a sequence of coups1.

Sequence. That means more than one.

The power doesn’t just fall from the hands of the old regime to the “revolutionaries”, but changes hands several times, before you reach any kind of stability at all. Some of these phases are passing so fast, that they are getting completely memoryholed later.

(For example, the Russian revolution of 1917-1918 included, at the very least, five coup-events, each of them leading to a radical transformation of the entire political landscape. So, although the popular imagination tends to fuse them all into a one singular event, that had not been the case, historically and chronologically speaking)

One implication of that principle being. People who executed the original coup on the iteration 1, rarely ever play any part in governance on the iteration 5 (or six, or seven). More often than not, they had been sidelined, forgotten and memoryholed long, long ago, and, therefore, will not be playing much role in the subsequent official narrative.

Second. Once we have established that revolution proceeds as a sequence of coups (albeit keeping in mind this is but the first approximation, and not an exhaustive description), we need to find out where are these coups happening, geographically speaking. Like, okay. There’s the first coup, second coup, third coup… Nth coup.

Where are they taking place? In which location?

It would be a great mistake to presume that all of these coup-events are spread more or less evenly, over the entire country’s territory. Not at all. By and large, all of these coups are localised in the political capital and its immediate vicinity, with the rest of the country following their lead.

(The French revolution does not happen in France. It happens in Paris, and in the Île-de-France. The Russian revolution does not happen in Russia. It happens in St Petersburg, and its immediate surroundings. Whatever is happening in the capital causes a huge, disproportionate, defining effect on the course of events)

Reflecting the fact that revolutions - in the proper sense of the word - do happen only in the heavily centralised countries, with a heavily pronounced capital city which is recognised as the one and only centre of political life, that everyone else obeys and submits and is looking up to, in everything.

Long story short, revolution can only happen in a country where all the political life had been thoroughly extirpated, except for the national capital. Which makes anyone who controls the capital nearly omnipotent, as the rest of the country will just obey them with no murmur2.

Third. High level of political centralisation in the country plus political instability in the capital city is the necessary, yet insufficient condition for the revolutionary change. For in practice we can observe examples of the opposite: long sequences of coups in the capital of a highly centralised state, that do not result in any kind of deep political transformation whatsoever.

Example: 18th c. Russia. The Russian eighteenths century is often referred to as “an era of palace coups”, for they are indeed happening very, very often. Once in a while, you are having a a coup; a Tsar or a dynasty, or a royal favourite is getting overthrown, always by the force of arms, and the composition of government and the court changes. That, however, does not produce a “revolution” in a modern sense, that is a deep, and fundamental change in well, everything.

Why? Well, again, part of the reason is that all of these coups are happening within a very, very narrow circle of the high society. The court aristocracy of St Petersburg. The high officials of St Petersburg. The imperial guards officers of St Petersburg. That is it, basically.

(these are all largely the same people, or at least, the same families)

All of these endless Russian coups never ever involve anyone outside of this narrow circle of St Petersburg high society. Only the noble, and the high ranked of the capital do participate - and no one else. The general populace of St Petersburg - that is its lower classes - does not. Other cities of the country, including their privileged classes do not.

(Like, if you are not a high nobleman physically located in the capital you may as well not exist, for you will be playing absolutely no role in all of these court politics, nor in any of these coup-events)

Interestingly enough, the army - that is the huuuuuuuuuuuuge number of troops garrisoned all around the Russian empire, does not participate either. Like, the imperial guards can kill a Tsar and put another Tsar on his place. But some troops from let’s say Riga marching on St Petersburg to put someone else on the throne, is completely unthinkable, unheard of. That just never happens in reality.

All of these endless palace coups, and counter-coups, and coup-events are executed by only a handful of the ultra-privileged in the imperial capital, and no one else. Notice, how the great majority of the country, is not even trying to participate in all these internal havocs of St Petersburg, which is deposing and imposing emperors upon the country.

Why?

Because they are not invited to

And that is a very important thing to understand

For an outsider, can rarely ever participate in the high politics of high circles - unless by their direct invitation

That is a crucially important point, that I want to stress again and again. Fools tend to greatly exaggerate the factor of “taking” or “capturing” power, which is supposedly happening “by force”. All of these bully, toughguy, Conan the Barbarian-style rhetorics have very little do to with the actual revolutionary dynamics3. Contrary to the popular delusion, taking the power “by force” or - an even greater absurdity - against the wishes of the power in being, is not a very common nor a very realistic scenario of action against an already established, internally stable regime.

How do you take the power then, if not by force?

You are getting invited into power, and entering through the widely open doors, with the massive help and assistance from inside.

Otherwise you can’t.

Okay you may ask. But why the heck would anyone invite you into power? Like, people in the government, people in the narrow elites, must love doing politics in their narrow circle without involving anywhere else. Why the heck would be they inviting outsiders?

Ah! Now that is a good question

So, basically the reason - and the primary factor of involving outsiders - and including them into politics (otherwise they can never participate) is the political entrepreneurship by the members of the elite high circle.

What is political entreneurship?

Let’s recall Joseph Schumpeter’s classic definition of a “normal”, business entrepreneurship. From Schumpeter’s perspective, the job on an innovator (= that is entrepreneur) consists in introducing new factors of production into the production process, or making a new combination out of the existing factors.

(Like, for example, you wanna create a new data centre. You can either build it all from scratch - long, and costly, or you can buy an existing factory, with the power generation capacity and stuff, and it will be super cheap, and will give you an upper hand over the competitors. So, what you did is that you introduced a new factor, and that gave you a competitive advantage)

Now apply this logic to politics. Political entrepreneurship is when you are introducing new forces, new resources, and new people into the political process

(and, therefore, inviting them all into politics)

Now why the hell would you be doing that? I mean as a member of the elite high circle

And the answer is:

Because you are stuck in a conflict with the other members of the same circle, and you want to get an upper hand in the conflict. Now to defeat them, you include more people, more resources, and more forces into the game.

(Now let’s continue our business vs political entrepreneurship comparison. Imagine you are an American businessman producing some sort of consumer electronics. To get an upper hand over your competitors, you outsource it all into Asia. And that indeed gives you an upper hand, and will allow you to beat your competitors, and defeat them, and triumph - for a while. Until your contractors inform you they don’t need you anymore, and can continue the business themselves, without your participance. They learnt everything they needed, they got everything they needed - that is connections, and contacts, and the direct contact with customers - and they can make it all and sell it all without your involvement at all. You got completely sidelined, and thrown out of business, and that would have never ever happened unless you invited your future undertakers yourself, driven by the fierce competition with your existing rivals. You can apply the same logic to the political processes as well. New players cannot normally enter the game unless by an invitation of someone already in the game)

And you are probably not the only actor doing that! There are almost certainly many players doing just that, trying to outcompete - and that means outbid - each other. And, as you are all trying to win, by throwing some more and more cards on the table - which means involving more and more outsiders into the game - the process is gradually getting out of control

Just a few iterations later, you guys - and that means all of you, all the members of the original narrow circle without a single exception - will be sidelined, and all your petty vendettas, and holy wars, and jealousies, and hatreds, will be memoryholed. Not necessarily because people aim to erase them, but because nobody will give a damn anyway. You guys lost, you got kicked out, some other people are running the ball, so what you wanted to achieve in the beginning does not really matter and no one cares anymore.

For the agenda has changed completely. And it is now set by the outsiders.

That is called - the revolution

And the radicalising dynamics that we associate with the revolution - that is political agendas, and political goals getting increasingly more extreme, over the time - is largely driven by the increasing flow of the outsiders who do not belong to the original high circle, who, increasingly, have no connection with that circle at all (like, on an iteration 5 or 6, most political players do not know the players from iterations 1 or 2, and - quite sincerely - have very little idea of why it had all started and what had been the original plan) and - most importantly - have no stake in the existing order of things.

The progressive radicalisation of revolutionary process - from moderate revolutionaries of an iteration 1 to the extremists of an iteration 5, largely reflects the different social composition of the whole set of political players on the iterations 1 and 5, respectively. Those who started it all on the iteration 1 tend to belong to the upper elites, who have hella lot to lose, and having a lot to lose causes a moderating effect on their views and agenda. Those who play politics on the iteration 5, have nothing to lose, and all to gain, and their particular interest makes them considerably more radical compared to the original initiators of the revolutionary events.

Summary

Let’s make a brief conclusion, so it will be easier to follow through

For a successful revolution, and that is a rapid political change, shaking the entire social hierarchy down to the very bottom, you need not one condition - but three

First, you need a high degree of political centralisation. That is, all the political life, and all the agency in the provinces, must have been thoroughly extirpated by the previous regime. This extirpation of political life is necessary, because it is making the new regime, established in the course of a revolution (and on its very mature phase) effectively omnipotent.

Second. You need a chronic political instability in the capital city, and in its surroundings. You need a frequent change of regimes, frequent coups. Power must be changing hands, and must be changing hands very fast. For that, you need a high degree of discord within the ruling elite, the high degree of conflict and disunity.

All of that is important but still not enough

Third, and most important. For a revolution, and that is a transformative political and socio economic change, happening over a brief period of time, you need more than just centralisation plus instability. You need a fervent competition in political entrepreneurship, by the existing elite. For it is the existing elite, who is introducing new actors, and new interest groups, and the increasingly broader social circles into the political process. Elites are inviting them all to participate, and welcoming them, and opening the doors wide, and that is the only way they can enter.

Why on earth would they be doing that?

To gain an upper hand in an internal, intra-elite conflict

It is aiming to defeat their rivals (from the existing elite), that the elites are involving more, and more actors into the politics, and, competing with each other, and trying to outbid each other, and then it all turns into a flood, for the new entrees are doing the same, so that in a few iterations the entire composition of the political theatre changes, and it now solely consists of the people with no stake in the existing order of things, of the people who could not even dream of ever attaining power on the iteration 1, who lack any conservative interest, who have nothing to lose, and everything to gain, from the most radical socio-political transformation imaginable.

And it is on this stage, and with the new composition of political class, and with all of the original players sidelined, that things start to get really, really nasty.
