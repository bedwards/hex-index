When I arrived in Washington DC in 2021, it felt like a one party state. I mean, the Democratic party state, of course. Republicans looked totally sullen and depressed, and lowkey. At this point in history, they felt more like a ceremonial opposition within a mono-party regime rather than anything else.

Institutions of federal government felt blue to the core. Overall, it looked like the Dems seized the central power, and the GOP was reduced to a bunch of disgrantled oppositionaries who can do nothing but grumble, totally powerless.

(What made a particular impression on me, was how the bureaucrats talked to businessmen. They talked to them down. Wow, I thought. It feels like Moscow in the year 2000)

Same year, I had a conversation with a friend, and confessed to him, that were I doing American politics, I would be betting on the red. Would bet on the another great red resurgence, and a massive red wave that is gonna wash away whatever the balance of power currently exists in Washington.

I didn’t and could expect the timing, of course, not could I expect the specific forms, it would take. I made this bet based on the general logic of events, which, personally, I imagine in the shape of a pendulum.

Left - Right - Left - Right - Left - Right

The pendulum swinging back and forth, never really staying in the middle, but going all the way to the opposite direction, only to return back in a few moments.

This is how it works.

So, if the pendulum went far blue - as it happened in 2021 - it meant that the next swing was going to bring it far red. And that is exactly what happened.

Following this logic, the far right swing we are observing today must be followed a far left swing, and relatively soon1.

***

That is not all, however

We love to imagine it all in terms of a symmetric binary opposition, and that is largely true, in relation to any specific moment of time. The right swing produces a symmetric left swing, and vice versa. This indeed a not bad approximation at any given moment in history.

In the long run, however, this approximation does not work very well

For a simple binary model, where the monolithic “left” oppose the monolithic “right” stops working the moment we introduce a generational factor.

Let me give you an example

I really like the following remark, regarding the Russian politics. It is a joke, of course, but jokes tend to grasp certain features of reality more precisely than the serious commentary, exactly because the jokes are censored less.

How is the Russian public opinion polarised:

Boomers: Stalinists vs anti-Stalinists

Millenials: Putinists vs anti-Putinists

Zoomers: SJWs vs Bigots

Again, this is is a joke. But, as the saying goes, every joke has a grain of joke

In this particular case, it grasps a very important truth. Which is:

The principles of political polarisation vary from one generation another

So, yes, in every generation you have X vs anti-X sort of things

But X itself, what people are polarised about, changes

For example, the figure of Stalin was really, really important in the context of Russian politics, just three or four decades ago. At this point, it belongs to history, even the Millenial generation treating him on pair with Sargon of Akkad, and not something that is somehow relevant to the problems of the modern day.

The only people who care are really, really old, and they are going to die soon and be replaced by the next generations who were formed by the different formative experiences, hold different sets of beliefs and will be polarised around something else, entirely. And if we don’t see it, it is because we are over-focused on the soon to be irrelevant beliefs of octogenarians in power, rather than on what their (inevitable!) successors think of and believe in.

So what you have is not a one-layer symmetrically organised holy war (rightists vs leftists) but rather a multi-layer pie in which every layer - and that is every generation - is polarised in a different way, from all the other layers.

***

Now another thing to consider is that every generation is different not only in respect of what it is polarised (that is - disunited) about, but also in a sense what it is not polarised about. Like, it is not only what people fight over that matters, but also what they agree upon. Not only the nature of political polarisation varies from one generation to another, but also the nature of political consensus.

And, as the popular opinions are changing, the old shibboleths, the old rallying cries, and by the way, the old taboos, are all stopping to work, as the old generation is gradually passing away, and their successors just do not care at all about the old beliefs and the old opinions. And nothing, perhaps, reflects this change in generational opinions as much, as a striking re-habilitation of socialism, in the minds of younger Americans.

Which is an interesting thing to think about. For socialism used to a total anathema, just a few years ago, and any suspicion of socialism would disqualify you from any serious participation in politics. Like, I do not want to go into a long (and meaningless) discussion of what does/does not constitute “real” socialism, but it is difficult to argue that in the very recent past, an accusation of “socialism” used to be a stone, dragging your down. It used to be scary to be called a socialist. Until now.

Now, however, one can openly campaign as a socialist, and win by a landslide

So what does it mean?

Well, it means that the popular opinions have changed

I think that the elder generations, and, in particular, the older conservatives tend to be in a state of denial. They either minimise, or straight away refuse to see the change of opinions, or they admit it, but in a form of cheap caricature. “LGBTQ activists with hairy armpits and no jobs”, more or less. That is of course a cope, and a massive cope, because it frames the scary new ideas I don’t like, as something fringe, marginal, confined within a few percent of bohemians and eccentrics that exist in every society.

Well, that isn’t the case. For the change of opinions is not limited to a few weirdos and unemployed - as the elder conservatives would like to think - it is perfectly visible within the “normie” majority of the young. I would even say that it is particularly visible among the more functional, better socialised young normies with an IQ above room temperature.

Recently, I talked to a friend who works in crypto. At the time, he was doing an MBA at one of the top American unis. He shared how they were having a discussion on socialism vs capitalism, and he was the only one who stood for capitalism. He, personally, was shocked by that. Was amazed to find himself in a minority.

Again, I was not present at the discussion. It is quite possible, that not everyone had any strong opinion on this matter at all. It is very much possible that some people were undecided. But even the most charitable interpretation implies that he was the only one who declared for capitalism, firmly.

That is, on the business executive program

Once again, it is not few campus weirdos who never had a job. It is the people who finished their degrees, got a job, acquired some work experience, enrolled into a high ranked program - most probably aspiring for an upward executive career. Now this kind of demography - and that is well above average quality of human capital demography - does not seem to be scared by the word socialism, but rather attracted by it.

You, of course, may reject my point, dismissing it as anecdotal evidence

Okay. Let’s have a look at the actual piece of data then

That is the distribution of votes on NYC mayoral primaries, by median income

Both the right-wing, and centrist liberal folk loves to post this map as a kind of “own”. Do you see who is voting for the socialists? It is the riiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiich

Well, that is a self own, really

It just gives us one additional piece of evidence to our argument that the quality of human capital does correlate with the propensity for socialist ideas. Reflecting a massive change of opinion, of monumental scale, that is happening - or has already happened - among the younger Americans.

You can rarely find a young person of high human capital who would be shocked and horrified by the accusation of “socialism”.

Like you can, but it is very, very hard

Almost everyone is left-wing on economy, and - as almost everyone is left-wing economy - it means that the popular consensus has changed

You just did not realise it yet

***

Still, claiming that the whole political balance will be shifting left, is not giving us the full picture. For indeed, what unites the great majority of the young is that they are more or less left-coded in terms of economy. That means that their socio-economic demands, in the great majority of cases, would be coded as something leftist, and rarely ever rightist. There is indeed a massive, tectonic shift of opinions towards the left in the socio-economic issues.

BUT

That does not hold true, when it comes to culture

I am positively certain that the left-coded economy is a popular, winning, uniting message for the new era. But I am much less convinced that the left-coded culture is. And, for a good reason.

For many years, American politics were structured in the following way. You divide society into goodies and baddies, based on their identity. All the credit belongs to goodies, all the blame to baddies.

Men vs women

White vs POC

Cis vs LGBT

And so on, and so forth

For a while, you could build a working narrative, and a functional political machine based on this model. Yet, as it often happens, the seemingly perfect machine started running out of the fuel it turned out to have been working on, all along

I mean the natural goodwill of the “baddie” side to play by these rules

Appeal to the altruism and benevolence

Not the Dem propaganda, but again, appeal for benevolence

What do you see here?

What you see is fundamentally an appeal to the benevolent, charitable, magnanimous sexism. Do not think of yourself, forget of your own interest. Think of women. That is an appeal to benevolence, which can only ever work when the other side (in this case, males) has lots of it.

At some point, however, it became apparent that the benevolent sexism, specifically, is running thin, and what you see is more of the non-benevolent one, especially among the young.

The young right wings, increasingly, appear to be unwilling to play by the old rules. Not that they are rejecting these rhetorics. Not at all. They are acting in the same logic, and symmetrically. They are appropriating the enemy language, the enemy line reasoning, and then reversing it. That is why the great deal (or, perhaps, most) of groyper discourse looks like the exact mirror reversal of left-coded rhetorics.

Men bad → Women bad

White bad → POC bad

Cis bad → LGBT bad

So, basically, if the feminist argument had been that women are structurally oppressed (= men have it better) and, therefore, deserve special care and protection, then the modern right-wing argument is increasingly becoming that it is men who comprise an oppressed class (= women have it better) and must shake off their chains.

That is how crusade against the patriarchy turns into a revolt against the longhouse.

***

The left-coded economy is immensely popular. In fact, the great majority of the young hold some form of left-coded beliefs in that regard. That may serve as a great unifying factor, and a platform for the re-birth of the Democratic Party, after the crushing, humiliating defeats of the last years. Step by a step, iteration by iteration, the popular consensus is almost guaranteed to be shifting in this direction, especially as the economic circumstances will be exacerbating (I think they will).

BUT

The old left-coded politics, as they had been practiced for many years cannot serve as the basis for unity, nor there can be any kind of social consensus around them. The appearance of this consensus - that might have existed through the 2010s - was a temporary delusion, that was shattered away the moment it felt like the Blues lost institutional power. Identity politics are divisive, and have always been, and that they did not generate a mass resistance on the other side was but a temporary situation.

Now that gives a clue to understanding the shameful, disastrous defeats of the Democratic Party. At a certain point, the Democrats started fully prioritising the cultural issues, forgetting that the left-coded cultural platform is not that popular at all. In fact, a huge number of voters will be voting against the Blue, solely because of the opposition to their cultural agenda and no other reason. Meanwhile, the left-coded economic platform is immensely popular, and is getting as close as it could be to the national consensus, uniting both the Blue voters and the MAGA core constituency. And yet, the Democrats had been doing very little focus on it.

Which means that the Democrats should be doing the opposite of what they had been doing lately. They should deepen and radicalise their economic agenda, once again becoming the party of change rather than of status quo. They should monitor, and listen to the popular socio economic demands, of which the universal healthcare would be a good start. At the same time, they should abandon the divisive, toxic cultural baggage of the 2010s, stop insulting and antagonising the entire demographies, based on their identity, and focus on building the better future for everyone, irrespective of their race, ethnicity, gender and sexual orientation.

Although the US may indeed be going through its transition from the republic to empire, I believe that it is currently experiencing the very, very early stages of the transition.
