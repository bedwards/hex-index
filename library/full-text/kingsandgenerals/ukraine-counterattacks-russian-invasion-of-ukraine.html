Early in the war, we noted the Russian army's communication difficulties in Ukraine. At some point, the Russians started using Elon Musk's Starlink satellite terminals to gain access to the internet to establish communication within and across units.

Most recently, Ukrainian officials claimed that Russia's drone operators use Starlinks to extend the range of their strike drones. And Ukraine's newly appointed Minister of Defense, Mikuel Federov, praised by commentators for his efficiency and competency for his previous endeavors, appealed to Elon Musk to solve this issue. To his credit, Musk, despite not being exactly known for his sympathies for Ukraine, decided to disable all unauthorized Starlink terminals in Ukraine, almost 550,000 terminals. As a result, while the Ukrainian military, which also relies on Starink for its communications, was able to register its terminals to continue using them, Russian units suddenly lost their communications and saw a decrease in the effectiveness of their drones.

This has been causing challenges for the Russian army in Ukraine and the Ukrainian army has been trying to capitalize on the situation in February. In our previous videos, we have discussed the new infiltration tactics used by the Russian army. In a nutshell, at this point of the war, Ukraine does not have continuous uninterrupted defensive lines with infantry manning trenches throughout the front line. Proliferation of drones on both sides has made this impossible since drones can easily detect the gathering of infantry and take them out.

On top of that, Ukraine still has manpower shortages, which prevents them from deploying more troops to its forward defensive lines. As a result, Ukraine now defends by deploying troops to a well-fortified network of drone resistant strongholds in tactically significant areas and choke points and uses its drones 24/7 to detect enemy movement. Detecting approaching vehicles is much easier than detecting small groups of infantry, sometimes as few as two people. Hence, in recent months, Russia has been increasingly using the tactic of infiltrating the Ukrainian rear in small infantry groups.

On the one hand, many Russians are killed in these attempts. But on the other hand, sometimes poor visibility caused by foggy or rainy weather makes it difficult for drone operators to detect the enemy movement, which allows the infiltrators to advance undetected. Then they hide, waiting for more infiltrators to join them, along with trying to detect Ukrainian artillery and drone operator positions. In both cases, good communication is paramount for the success of the mission.

The disabling of Starlinks have made secure communication between infiltrators and the main Russian force very difficult. On top of that, often these small groups raise the Russian flag in the areas they have infiltrated and filmed this process to create an illusion that Russia has captured another village or town. This has made the life of oathent mappers more difficult. Such areas are often depicted as gray or Russian controlled.
