During the spring of 1481, Memed II, the most ambitious and active sultan yet seen by the Ottoman state, had unexpectedly died at the age of 49. During his 30-year reign, the Sultan had waged relentless wars across two continents, winning many great victories and gaining new lands for his empire. For decades, rulers in the east and west feared his armies as the echoes of Ottoman drums became synonymous with inevitable defeat. However, with Memed's death, a new era would begin for the Ottoman Empire, marked by war between the former Sultan's two sons, the Elder Shisad and the younger Shazeda Jem.

According to Ottoman tradition, all royal princes had an equal claim to the throne, not just the eldest member of the imperial dynasty. It was thought that only through divine will and the prince's competence in political and military affairs could he secure the throne. In past Ottoman succession periods, the eldest claimment often secured the throne due to their inherent experience and networks within the royal court compared to their younger siblings. But this was not always the case.

During the interregnum after the battle of Anara, Memed I the first had gained the army's loyalty and famously defeated several of his elder brothers to secure the throne. Often after a prince had secured the throne, it was expected that he would eliminate all threats to his power to ensure the stability of the state against any future civil wars, including executing his siblings. Although it was not a widely popular practice among the public, state sanctioned fretside ensured the stability of the Ottoman realm and the continuity of the imperial family for almost an entire century before me II's reign. As a result, the Sultan had officially codified the practice into law, thereby indirectly planting the seeds of future conflict between his sons.

On a cold winter day in 1447, Beaazit was born in Demotica near the capital city of Adena. He was the eldest son of Shazed Memed, the first in line to the Ottoman throne and Gulbahhatun, an Albanian concubine. After his father's conquest of Constantinople in 1453, the 7-year-old Beazit was sent to Amasia in eastern Anatolia to serve as the provincial governor of the region. During these years, Bezich received a first rate education from the most prominent Islamic scholars of the day.

From an early age, his education reflected the Ottoman model for princely training, combining Islamic scholarship, military instruction, governance, and exposure to court politics. He studied theology, law, literature, and languages, developing a reputation as a thoughtful and pious prince rather than a purely marshall one. Beazit's long tenure in Amasia proved decisive in shaping his political identity. Unlike some princes whose provincial appointments were brief, Beazid governed Amasia for over two decades.

During this time, he gained extensive experience in administration, taxation, military logistics, and provincial justice. His inner court attracted scholars, poets, and religious figures, reinforcing his image as a cultivated and devout ruler. Bea's years in Amasia also allowed him to establish strong ties with key Ottoman institutions, particularly the military elite. He cultivated the loyalty of the geniseries and provincial cavalry commanders, a relationship that would later prove crucial during the 1481 succession crisis.

His governance style emphasized stability and consensus over aggressive expansion, reflecting a temperament marketkedly different from his fathers. While Memed II pursued relentless conquest, Beaazid preferred cautious administration and internal consolidation. Relations between Beaazid and his father were complex, as Memed often reportedly questioned Beazid's suitability as a successor, viewing him as overly religious and insufficiently aggressive. These tensions did not result in open conflict, but they created uncertainty regarding succession and encouraged factionalism within the royal court in Constantinople.

Despite this, Beazit remained loyal to his father, refraining from any premature attempt to claim power and even fighting alongside him at his great victory at the battle of Oshluk Belly in 1473. However, the Ottoman prince's main challenger to the throne would not be his father, but rather his younger half brother, Jem. Jem Sultan was born in 1459 in Adena, the youngest son of the Sultan and his concubine, Chichek Hatun. From an early age, the Ottoman prince was recognized for his intellectual curiosity, artistic talent, and charismatic and energetic personality.

Following court custom, Jem was appointed provincial governor to gain practical experience in governance and was assigned to Castonu in northern Anatolia in 1469. However, during the crisis years of 1472 and 1473, he served as his father's regent in the imperial capital while he campaigned against Uzen Hassan. In the aftermath of the battle of Oshluk belly, Memed's favorite and popular son, Shazad Mustafa, had suddenly died, thus paving the political path for the young gem. In 1474, he was assigned to govern Ka, a recently conquered province in central Anatolia that remained a hub of unrest.

The local region had long been ruled by the rival Caramanid dynasty and retained strong local identities resistant to Ottoman central authority. Jem's governorship placed him at the center of a difficult task, enforcing imperial rule while managing local Turk notables who viewed Ottoman rule with suspicion and even hostility. This experience shaped Jem's political outlook and provided him with a distinct support base which was sidelined under his father's administration in favor of men with demer backgrounds. Jem's relationship with his father was reportedly steadfast.

Memed II valued Jem's intelligence and cultural refinement, but did not clearly signal him as a preferred successor. Some contemporary accounts suggest that court factions sympathetic to the more liberal gem hoped he might inherit the imperial throne, especially given Beazid's perceived religious conservatism and reluctance toward continuous warfare. However, Memed's failure to designate an heir left both princes in a state of uncertainty, allowing rivalry to develop beneath the surface. With the sudden death of Memed II during the spring of 1481, the race to the throne between his two sons began.

The Sultan's death was initially concealed by members of the imperial court, a common practice intended to manage the transition of power. This secrecy, however, quickly became a source of conflict. Grand vizier Caramel Memed Pashia attempted to control the succession process and is widely believed to have favored gem. As an Ottoman statesman with a Turkish background, the Grand Vizier saw the younger prince's support base among Anatolian elites as the best way to curb the ever growing influence of the Balkan Dev Shemer class in government.

His efforts to support Jem included delaying the announcement of the Sultan's death and seeking support for the young prince in the imperial capital. Nonetheless, these maneuvers provoked fierce resistance from the geniseries fiercely loyal to Bea, whom they regarded as a safer and more predictable ruler who would protect their political interests. As a result, the enraged generies revolted in Constantinople and assassinated Nemed Pashia, therefore effectively dictating the outcome of the succession before either prince could reach the city. The Devshma Ishach Pasha was appointed as Grand Vizier while Beazid's teenage son Chiseda Kokud who was present at the capital was briefly placed on the throne as regent.

Despite the chaos in the imperial capital, messengers from both factions began racing across Anatolia to reach their respective princes first. But Ba Bay bay of Anatolia and supporter of Bayazit Sinan Pashia had set up checkpoints on all roads leading to Cona, thus intercepting messengers to Jem while allowing the news to reach Amasia first. 2 weeks later, Beaazid along with 4,000 horsemen arrived in Constantinople where he was welcomed by cheering janiseries who even during the celebratory occasion reminded the prince to increase their salaries. With the backing of the army Ulma and the bureaucracy, Beazid was proclaimed sultan in the newly built topappa palace.

The race to the throne was over before one side knew that the race had even started. One week after Beers had secured the throne, news finally arrived in Cona to the shock and horror of Jem. Immediately, the young prince rejected his older brother's authority and declared himself Sultan. He ordered coins struck in his name and had the Friday sermon read on his behalf.

Both of which were essential symbols of sovereign authority in the Islamic world. Jem portrayed himself as a people's sultan who would end the oppression and high taxation of the central state over the provinces. Attracting the support of the Turkish notables of Anatolia, local craft guilds and minority religious sects such as the Alvies, Jem attempted to legitimize his cause further by proposing a negotiated settlement to Beazid. He suggested dividing the empire with Beaazit ruling the Balkan provinces and with him governing Anatolia.

While such arrangements had historical precedent in other Turk and Islamic states, Beaazit categorically rejected the proposal. The new Sultan responded to Jem with the Arabic proverb, "There is no kinship among rulers." To accept division would have undermined the Ottoman conception of a unified empire and risked a return to the chaos of earlier civil wars. With diplomacy failing, Jem began to prepare for armed conflict. He gathered an army composed largely of Anatolian troops, including irregular cavalry and soldiers drawn from the former Caraman territories.

These forces were motivated by loyalty and local grievances, but they lacked the discipline, training, and cohesion of Beazid's standing army. Gem advanced westward with a force of 4,000 toward Bersa, a city of immense symbolic importance. As the first significant Ottoman capital, Bersa represented legitimacy and continuity, and its capture would have strengthened Jem's claim while potentially attracting wavering supporters of his brother. In a brief battle near Inigul, Jem routed Aas Pashia's local forces and captured Bersa shortly thereafter.

Beazit II responded swiftly and decisively to Jem's advance. Securing Constantinople and backed by the geniseries, Beaazit mobilized a large and welle equipped force. His army included elite infantry, provincial cavalry, and auxiliary units drawn from across the empire. Beaazit's strategy was shaped by the lessons of Ottoman history, particularly the interregnum following the defeat of Beaazit the first in 1402 when prolonged civil war nearly destroyed the state.

Determined to avoid a similar catastrophe, Beaazit aimed to defeat Jem quickly and decisively before the rebellion could spread or invite foreign involvement. Luckily for the Sultan, he had the support of Gedic Ahmed Pashia, the general who had invaded Italy and captured Atranto the previous year. The army he commanded in Albania was redirected away from returning to Italy in order to cross swords with fellow Muslims. After a month of gathering up their armies, the two sons of Memed met near Yan Shahir, but by the end of the day, the disciplined ranks of the janiseries proved too great a challenge to Jem.

The battle ended in a bloody chaotic victory for Beaazid. After only 20 days, Jem's rule in Bersa had met its end. With his family and some 40 loyal followers, he fled, escaping the pursuing army and beginning his new life in exile. After a three-day stay in Cona, Jem fled to take sanctuary with his father's enemy, the Mamluks.

Sultan Kiteb's lavish reception boosted his confidence, and he believed that with Mamluk's support, he could assemble another army to overthrow his brother. This led Jem to refuse Beazit's request to offer a generous pension so Beazit could live out his life in Jerusalem. After a six-month stay in Egypt, during which he became the first member of the House of Osman to perform the Hajj to Mecca, Jem set out again for Anatolia with 60,000 duckets and thousands of soldiers from Syria. By the spring of 1482, he had gathered his forces in Aleppo and shortly after continued to Cona, joined by both the also exiled Kasmbe of the Caramanids and the rebellious governor of Anara, Memed.

With their support, Jem now had a more potent force than before. But during this lull in hostilities, Beazid had not been idle either, as he gathered a large host to crush his brother, who was now besieing his old seat of Cona. The Sultan rushed into Anatolia, making his way to the former Caramanid capital. In an ambush, the governor of Anara was captured and beheaded, resulting in his followers changing sides to his cause.

Hearing the news of his allies death, Jem broke the siege on Ka. But during the panicked retreat, the morale of his massive army broke and dissolved away without even seeing a single battle. For the second time, Jem was defeated and powerless, fleeing his brother's wrath. To make matters worse, his last remaining ally, Casimbe, surrendered his men and renounced his claim to the Caramanid throne in return for gaining the governorship of Messi.

losing all of his support and now cut off from Syria by Beazid's agents. All seemed over for Jem. For the Sultan, Jem's survival beyond Ottoman borders represented a constant threat, one that could be exploited by any European power seeking leverage against the empire. Consequently, Beazit adopted a policy of containment rather than confrontation, preferring to neutralize Gem through diplomacy and financial payments rather than military action. By late 1482, an agreement was reached between Beaazit II and Ubison in exchange for a substantial annual payment of 40,000 duckets.

The Knights agreed to keep Jem in custody and prevent him from returning to Ottoman territory or rallying foreign support. This arrangement effectively transformed Jem into a political hostage whose continued detention served Ottoman interests as much as it served those of his captives. For the knights, Jen became a source of income and diplomatic relevance. For Beaazit, he became a manageable threat rather than an unpredictable one.

Upon coming to power, Beaazit felt his realm needed a period of resppite to address the problems left by his father. The Sultan first ruled out any serious consideration of a military campaign against the west in the near future as he wanted to end the total domination of the Devshma elite class that brought him to power in the first place. After defeating Jem in 1482, Beazit used false rumors of Gedic Ahmed Pash's complicity in his brother's rebellion first to dismiss the general from his command before executing him on charges of treason. As Western Europe celebrated the death of the Grand Turk, a series of counterattacks by Christian powers took place on the European holdings of the Ottoman realm as it was ravaged by civil war. By the fall of 1481, the foothold fortress of Atranto was recaptured by Duke Alonso of Calabria and his coalition army comprising troops from Italy and Hungary.
