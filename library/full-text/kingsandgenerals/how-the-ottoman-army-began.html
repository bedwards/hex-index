Like many Turk peoples throughout history, the early Ottomans initially developed as a primarily warrior nomadic society. In its earliest form, the Turk tribes of Central Asia used conventional nomadic tactics of warfare, emphasizing mobility and light cavalry.

For many centuries, the Turkish peoples employed such tactics against settled peoples from China to Eastern Europe with great success. From the mighty Gtkurks to the lethal Bulgars and the rampaging page and Kumman Kipteks, Western and Eastern monarchs alike trembled in fear behind the safety of stone walls as such nomadic groups raided their lands. However, over time, these nomadic groups adopted the military systems of the settled peoples they conquered and integrated with, thereby abandoning their ancestral traditions. An early example of such a shift can be seen with the Seljic Turks of the 11th century, who had encountered both the Islamic and Byzantine worlds during their conquests of the Middle East.

As a result, the Seljuks adopted a military made up of slave-based armies, heavy infantry, and frontier forces composed of nomadic border guards. The early Ottomans of the 14th century would follow in the steps of their Seljic ancestors. According to their own chronicles from later centuries, the founders of what would become the Ottoman Empire arrived in Bethnia's marulans with the second significant wave of Turkmen migration into Anatolia in the 13th century following the Mongol invasion of the Middle East. Led by a certain etal Gazi, the prototman state of the Caya tribe laid the early groundwork for an army that would remain active for over six centuries.

The tribe was a nomadic pastorialist Turkman group, but its tribal structure and connections were probably very loose due to the general settlement policy of their Seljic overlords. To protect law and order and establish centralized control, the Seljuks divided large tribes or clans of former tribal federations into smaller groups. They settled them across their lands, ensuring that no single tribe would grow powerful enough to challenge them. The Caya tribe was settled in the region surrounding the towns of Symanage on the edges of Bethnia near the border with Bzantium.

Probably during this brief period, they remained nominally under the control of the Chobanit Bays who were the official governor's general known as Bele of Northwest Anatolia for the Seljic state. The Seljic Sultans of Cona ruled their realm through appointed local governor generals and dozens of semi-autonomous chieftains whose tribal leaders were known as Uch Bay or marcher lords. Subsequently, Erul and his Kaya tribesmen became minor warriors under the greater Seljic umbrella. However, several developments and drastic changes helped this obscure tribal group become a viable political entity in the centuries that followed.

The Mongol onslaught and victory over the Middle Eastern Islamic states, including the Seljuks, uprooted the central Asian Turk tribes and forced them to migrate westward in several waves, while also destroying the effective government control that had been imposed on them. Between the establishment of direct Mongol rule over Anatolia in 1243 and the rebellion of the Mongol commander Smis in 1298, the border regions increasingly came under the control of independent Turkman leaders. These leaders frequently attacked and raided Byzantine territory, establishing their own autonomous bails and occasionally clashing with one another. In 1300, Mongol troops finally defeated Selemis, but in the process, they also lost the limited control they had over the border regions of Anatolia.

Additionally, the Byzantine military system weakened and collapsed after the recapture of Constantinople in 1261 as attention and financial resources shifted to the city and the empire's European possessions, leaving Anatolia neglected. It was this violent and unstable world that Utaril's successors would take advantage of to create the Ottoman military. With the overthrow and killing of the Chubanit Bay in 1292, the Caya tribe lost its lead lord. Seven years later, its leader Osman Gazi declared independence from the Seljuks of Ka.

Establishing itself as the Ottoman Baic, the new state now found itself in control of its own destiny. In the century after they arrived in Anatolia, the Ottomans grew their army from 400 men at the battle of Yessman in 1230 to around 5,000 at their first field battle victory over the Byzantines at the Battle of Baffus in 1302. A common misconception that military enthusiasts often hold about early Ottoman history is that early Ottoman soldiers were simple tribal Turkman cavalry lacking tactical or technical skills. Contrary to that assumption, Ottoman-mounted archers were seasoned veterans who participated in numerous campaigns under the Seljuks and possibly other Islamic powers.

Moreover, many served as mercenaries and had previously served in the Byzantine army, as seen in Alexios Philanthropos's failed Anatolian rebellion in 1295. Acting as allies or mercenaries alongside soldiers from other Turkman states, helped them enhance their military expertise. In their triumph over the Byzantines in 1302, the Ottomans fielded a light cavalry army with limited infantry reserves. The cavalry consisted of n pneumatic horse archers from tribal backgrounds and volunteers.

They fought using traditional step tactics of their ancestors, gathering intelligence on the enemy, selecting the battlefield, engaging in vanguard actions, and creating surprise at every level. They harassed enemies with successive cavalry charges and showers of arrows, employed ambushes and faint retreats to disrupt enemy formations, and ultimately surrounded and destroyed the disorganized fleeing enemy. After winning his first victory over the Byzantines, all of Osman's neighbors were in a state of turmoil and chaos. Between 1303 and 1306, the Catalan mercenary company hired by the Byzantines inflicted heavy losses on the Turk Baix of western Anatolia before turning on its pay masters and inflicting further losses on them.

In the meantime, the unscathed Ottomans were gaining victory after victory in Bethnia, which saw the charismatic Osman gain many subordinates and Turkman warriors to his cause from Anatolia. After each victory, he provided his followers with more and more spoils of war, and thus his forces continued to grow larger. War was not the only contributing factor to the growth of the Ottoman army. Another crucial pillar strengthening the state was the presence of strong artisans guilds and urban brotherhoods with links to the Islamic Sufi order like the Ahis Guild.

These guilds served as socioeconomic, religious and political organizations and functioned as paramilitary groups. The AIS produced weapons and military gear and provided light infantry. However, their primary role was consolidating the Ottomans territorial gains by establishing branches in occupied towns. After conquest and settlement, they quickly organized socio-economic life, established law and order, and formed defense units against potential enemies.

To Osman's good fortune, the leader of the local ais religious order, Shikabali, was also his dear friend and trusted mentor. In an attempt to secure an official alliance with the influential religious order, the Ottoman ruler had married Edibali's daughter, Rabia Balahhatun. According to later Ottoman chronicles, one night while Osman was staying at Edabali's home, he had a dream in which a large tree sprouted from the earth, covering the entire sky with its leaves and branches. The following morning, he discussed his vision with his father-in-law, and the Islamic preacher told him that it symbolized God's ordaining him and his successes to rule a worldly empire stretching around the earth.

The tale of Osman's dream would become a significant foundational story for the House of Osman in later centuries, serving to justify Ottoman successes as divinely ordained during the 15th and 16th centuries. It is clear that ongoing conflicts between the Byzantines and the Ottomans had a religious aspect. Both sides utilized religion to justify and legitimize their goals and warfare, but they were also pragmatic enough to rely on each other's renegades and mercenaries and to develop systems of coexistence and cooperation. But overall, official Ottoman attitudes to Christians and Jews were primarily sympathetic.

It is important to remember that Osmanghazi's military actions in the region were not simply a conflict between Muslims and Christians as many Byzantine peasants enlisted into Ottoman service. As the central authority of the Byzantine court weakened in its Anatolian provinces, more and more local Byzantine governors and Christians began making individual deals with the rising Turk powers in the region. This was done to maintain their socio-economic and military status in their local settlements. as Turk power was on the rise.

One such local governor was Michael Cosies of Kmenia becoming one of Osman's dearest companions. Michael Cosies served the Ottomans for many years as a regional diplomat and informant. Sometime between 1304 and 1313, he converted to Islam adopting the name Kus Mahal. The descendants of Kus Mahal, later known as Mahalier, would go on to serve the Ottoman state as marcher lords all the way up to the 18th century.

Although Islam justified military expansion and raids, Ottoman warriors, also known as Gazis or holy warriors, were not strictly religious fighters, but rather raiders called Akundes. Their raids against the Byzantines were carried out without a detailed master plan or strategy, but followed a general pattern. The Turkman marcher lords tended to assign particular regions to their subordinate tribes or groups for ongoing raids after establishing a strong base of operations. This decentralized system of command and control was highly effective against Bzantium's weak defenses in Anatolia.

These constant unpredictable attacks by various chieftains played a crucial role in the collapse of the Byzantine presence in Anatolia by the 1300s. Unfortunately, this decentralized approach to conquest also caused many issues for the Turk ba in the region. Sometimes subordinate leaders became uncontrollable after gaining strength and carving out their own territories. A good example of this problem was the weakening of the Giannit Baik.

By the end of the 13th century, the Gianids had become the strongest Baic in western Anatolia. Its commanders successfully took control of all the Agian coastlands and inland areas, each operating independently from one another. Then instead of remaining loyal to their Gianid lords, they declared independence and founded the Baix of the Caracids, Sarahids, Idendids, and Menti. While the leaders of large tribal groups went to great lengths to stay independent of their Seljic overlords, they were equally unsuccessful in controlling the ambitions of their subordinate commanders and tribal leaders.

The Ottomans managed this decentralization successfully and kept commanders under tight control by beginning the transformation of tribal nomadic warriors into a standing army earlier than the other baiks. Bethinia's geography and environment influenced the Ottoman military. It supported farming and semi-nomadic lifestyles but wasn't suitable for full nomadism as pastoral lands couldn't sustain large armies or large numbers of horses. Many followers were settled or semi-nomadic and religious scholars and former Seljic officials brought sedentary traditions.

Their coexistence with Byzantine villages affected newcomers leading to more Turkman warriors settling which in turn transformed lifestyles and altered military and political structures. This is evident in the Ottoman army's transition from large horseback warb bands to specialized infantry and cavalry units, each with distinct roles in battle. The early Ottoman military displayed typical weaknesses of a nomadic army. It was not a permanent standing force, but a collection of autonomous military groups that relied on tribal warriors and volunteers.

Although capable of defeating local Byzantine governors and even the emperor's army as at the battle of Pilicanon in 1329, it had difficulty capturing fortified positions and castles by direct assault. This was primarily due to the lack of effective infantry and siege equipment. The rapid capture of castles in Bethnia and later in Thrace depended on bribes, surprise attacks, and natural disasters, most famously seen in the fall of Gallipoli in 1354. When these methods failed, the Ottomans used blockades and starving castles with an influx of refugees, gradually draining resources until surrender.

This slow and costly process was especially challenging for large wellstocked towns. Bersa, Nikaya, and Nikomedia held out for 9, three, and four long years, respectively, before falling, resulting in widespread destruction and long recovery periods for these cities. The early Ottoman army's infantry was primarily composed of volunteers from the countryside, lacking a formal command structure and occasionally included some AI warriors. However, by the time of Osman's son and successor, Orhanghazi, the state had grown to the point of needing to establish a standing army.

According to Ottoman Chronicles, Grand Vizier Alen Pashia proposed establishing infantry units in 1325, which would also serve as the Sultan's personal force, known collectively as the Hassa. Soon after, a branching infantry corps was created as a means to centralize the state's army away from independent-minded Gazi commanders called the Yaya Corps. Many of its recruits came from Muslim and Christian villages eager for regular incomes from the state and tax exemptions. However, most continued to work on their farms during peace time.

Members of the core brought their own personal weapons and equipment during times of mobilization, such as swords, axes, and bows. While serving as royal guards to the Sultan and his family, they wore high white bonnets called Akbuk, which were more distinctive than the red bonnets worn by the AI warriors. Although it was the first attempt in Ottoman history to create a centralized army, the Yaya failed to become specialized units with tactical and technical capabilities on the battlefield due to its semi-professional status and thus lost its prestige over time. Although even as the Yayas lost their significance in the military hierarchy, their numbers continued to grow.

Wealthier individuals who could afford horses formed a new cavalry corps called the Muslims. By the 1360s, the Yaya and Muslim cors were reorganized into fixed-size auxiliary forces, each approximately 20,000 strong. They no longer received salaries, but were granted tax exemptions instead. Their status became hereditary with physically capable elder sons succeeding their fathers over the course of the decades.

Their primary role was now to support the main Ottoman army during military campaigns by performing tasks such as transporting supplies, building roads and bridges, and safeguarding supply convoys and baggage trains. While the Yaya did not fully meet the Ottoman military's expectations as an infantry force, it served as a practical support and auxiliary force. This also highlights a key trait of the early Ottoman army that gave it an advantage over other Turk powers of Anatolia and the Christian powers of the Balkans, adaptability and open-mindedness. Although the Ya and Muslim cause failed to provide the new Natal Ottoman state with a professional standing unit, its leaders now turned to reorganize its already active Turkman cavalry.

However, there were three main problems with attempts to centralize the army under the Sultan's authority. The first was that such Turkman units lacked a cohesive command and control system as they were loyal only to their immediate leaders and focused more on material gains through raiding than on the central state's objectives. The second was that leaders of these units tended to create independent personal domains in newly conquered territories, thereby eroding the central state's power over time. The third was that over time n pneumatic tokan units would often lose marshall favvore and military qualities as they assimilated themselves into sedentary life alongside their families in newly conquered lands.

To overcome these challenges, Ottoman leaders gradually introduced reforms to control and transform the Turkman cavalry within their domains. The principal outcome of these measures was a specially tailored Seljic system. This was a system of land tenure and taxation that allowed military officers to collect taxers from assigned land in exchange for their military service. This system had been fundamental to the Seljic administration of old, enabling it to manage vast territories by granting local leaders control over land and its revenues, ensuring their loyalty while providing soldiers and funding for the state.

Nearly all the Turk ba in Anatolia sought to develop their own systems during the 14th century. However, by the 1350s, all momentum had shifted in favor of the House of Osman. Following the first Ottoman conquests in Europe, the new Timar system emerged. These new lands, which remained the Sultan's legal property, were divided according to their tax value called Timar and were given to the Turkman warriors and their leaders who were rebranded as Timali sapahi.

The state granted such land to individual soldiers who demonstrated high merit and made significant contributions during military campaigns. But more importantly, these soldiers controlled only the land's tax revenue, not the land itself, similar to the Byzantine Preree system of old. Furthermore, it was a temporary land allocation, not a permanent one, allowing the state to revoke it if the Sapahi was unable to provide military service. The Zapahi was required to participate in military campaigns with his horse, weapons, and other equipment.

And based on the value of the land, he might also have to supply some armored retainers called jibelli. Depending on his success in battle, he could increase his timar by gaining additional land. But if he failed to perform his duties properly or fled from battle, he would lose all his rights and face punishment. The conquest of new lands in Europe led to the distribution of more and more timars by the Sultan and thus a large number of Turkmans from Anatolia flocked into the ranks of the Ottoman army.

This snowballing effect would have dire results for the Turk ba of Anatolia which now saw their power base shrink over the decades as they had no room to expand their domains. Although its final form would not be finalized until the 15th century, the Tamali Sapahi would prove to be the core of the Ottoman provincial army for many centuries to come. With the creation of the Yaya and Muslim cause, as well as the new provincial Tamali Sapahi Corps, the Ottoman state had taken its first steps in establishing a military that would one day strike fear into the hearts of its enemies. However, the question of a professional standing army controlled directly by the Sultan was yet to be addressed.
