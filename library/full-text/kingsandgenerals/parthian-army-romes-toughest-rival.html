The Paththean Empire made its mark in the history of Eurasia as the most formidable enemy of the Roman Empire. And yet, while historians possess a treasure trove of information about the Roman legions, we know precious little about the structure of the Paththean armies, which routinely defeated them. For decades, many assumptions have been made about the Paththeians and their military. As the Paththeians themselves were of nomadic origins, many scholars once believed that their army must have resembled their roots, consisting of a disorganized ad hoc cavalry force that was drawn up at the Shahan Shar's whim.

Yet, more recently, archaeological discoveries and modern readings of texts have yielded evidence that the Paththean military was a far more sophisticated force than previously assumed. That's Much like with Roman and Greek military history, the study of the Paththean military began with German scholars in the late 19th and early 20th century, whose interpretations would lay the groundwork for decades to come. The framework for the study of the Paththean military was based on several major assumptions. The first was that because the Paththeanss were originally a nomadic people who conquered the sedentary cities of Persia, their army must have been dominated by nomadic forms of warfare as seen on the Eurasian step. The second was that the Paththeians were more heavily reliant on cavalry than its predecessor or antecessor, particularly horse archers and catifacts.

The final assumption was that the Paththeians had no permanent standing military with nobles and their retinues being called up to serve on an ad hoc basis during times of war. While the origins of Paththeons as a step society were important to understanding Paththeia's army and society, much of that understanding was based on outdated conceptions of what step societies looked like and how the Paththeians actually integrated into the settled and urban heartland of Persia after migrating there in the 1st to 2nd centuries B.C.E. Step societies were not wanderers roaming across the plains, constantly moving from place to place. Instead, their pneumatic habits were regulated on seasonal patterns, which saw them alternate between seasonal pastures, usually a summer and winter pasture, living in each place for about half of the year.

Step peoples were familiar with many technologies beyond animal husbandry and archery, including ironwork, limited agriculture, and various other crafts and disciplines. Consequently, the nomadic Paththeians and their settled cousins in the cities of Persia were not a world apart, but in fact shared many cultural practices. This made it easier for the Royal Architect dynasty of Pathia to adopt local administrative and cultural practices upon conquering the Iranian plateau, utilizing the same systems of military recruitment and supply that have been used by previous Persia-based empires such as the Akimeds and the Salucid. The Paththeian military structure was divided into multiple groups of military authorities, all of which were subservient to the Shahans Sha, but each with some degree of independence in military matters.

While it was long believed that the Paththeians did not possess a permanent standing army, this is now known to be incorrect. This misconception traces its roots to the 3rd century historian Herodenos who made this inference because the Paththean army did not operate like the professional Roman army leading him to consider them different which in turn biased modern scholarship. It is now known that the Paththeon Shahensha had a royal bodyguard known in Greek sources as Dorifuri many of whom bore the title of Seamatelax an honorific similar to the Spatharios a class of elite bodyguards who served the Roman Emperor in later centuries. Many of these men were part of an administrative body composed of Philly and Senesis meaning friends and relatives which was heavily based on the prior salucid administrative model.

Like other step societies the royal guard corps known as the Gundi Shahen Sha was decimally organized. Officially it numbered 10,000 but in practice it appears to have usually operated at a strength closer to 4 to 6,000 men. The king also utilized large numbers of mercenaries at his disposal, including fellow nomadic nations such as the Smatians and Sackers, as well as the Greeks among others. Alongside the king's royal guard corps and mercenary body, the Paththean military also consisted of several large and powerful noble families who employed both their own personal retainers and were obliged to call up levies at the king's request.

The members of these great landowning families known as Eltheri in Greek sources and Azodan in the Paththean language typically comprised only a small part of the Paththean military each having their own retainers of lesser and singenesis. The first century historian Josephus provides a relatively simple yet useful image of these forces describing the Jewish noble Zamaris of Babylonia who had a force of 500 horse archers of which 100 consisted of his filianis. Likewise, of the 50,000 men who faced down the Roman legions of Mark Anthony in 36 B.CE, only 400 belonged to this group of noblemen and their immediate family members, suggesting they supplied a small portion of the total force, at most a few thousand men. In a decimally organized army, these nobles were likely divided between those who commanded 100, 1,000, and 10,000 troops.

Although in practice, the size of the force they brought to bear varied considerably based on factors such as family, clan, and estate size. On their own, these two groups would form a considerably sized force in the range of 10 to 20,000 men, but could not field the majority of the soldiers in armies reported by historians. Large families such as the Suririn and Karin clan were able to field armies of 10,000 or more on their own with the Surin clan being chiefly responsible for the famous defeat of the Roman general Cassus at Ker in 53 B.CE. Overall, classical authors claimed that Paththean armies could reach a size of up to 120,000 men.

That number is dubious. However, claims of 50 to 60,000 are not out of the question. In addition to the primary royal gund operated by the shahens sha, state-f funed garrisons were spread across the empire's borders, such as the lines of counter fortifications attested in modern-day Syria and Iraq, similar to those of the Romans. Other fortresses were positioned throughout Paththeia, primarily to protect trade routes and control and monitor the movement of tribal groups in the mountains alongside significant fortification chains that controlled the step peoples to the north and monitored the Indocians to the east.

These garrisons were under the control of local governors referred to as strategy in the Greek sources but properly titled Marzban in the Iranian languages. Paththe's client states also maintained significant armies for their kings to draw on. The systems by which these forces were recruited, supplied and paid are not well attested. However, limited evidence suggests that previous salucid and aimemen institutions were still in place to supply these men who could then be called up by the governors, noble families or the shahensa himself.

It is known that thousands of Greek soldiers were assimilated into the army of the paththean king Fratis II after his defeat of the helenic saluced ruler Antiocos IIth in 129 B.CE and Greeks are mentioned among the armed forces in the regions of Babylonia and Mesopotamia. It is likely that the old Saluced system of Claruki and Katakai therefore survived in the former Salucid corps and formed the basis of Paththean recruitment in this region. The Claros or Katacos was an institution where a soldier was granted land within a kind of commons whose produce and revenues supported their ability to serve full-time as a soldier. Larger grants to support cavalrymen or heavy infantry often came with provisions to assist in the maintenance of these soldiers, typically smaller farmers who owned land within these commons.

These men were then called up for the campaign and usually dismissed back to their homes when the campaigning season ended. Unlike the salucid or tomic armies, these men would not serve as pike armed hopletes but in the Italian fashion. Armed with a large shield, body armor, heavy javelin and spear and with a sword in reserve. The other system of recruiting semi-professional landed soldiers was to use an evolution of the Akimemen Hataru system whereby young men typically around the age of 20 were recruited and made to serve for a certain number of years as full-time professional soldiers before being granted land in exchange for their service with the expectation that they would respond if called up for a campaign.

Both of these institutions thus formed a sizable active reserve much like a modern national guard. The final major source of manpower for the Paththean military consisted of tribal groups and semi-aututonomous cities and regions within the Paththean Empire. The largest of these were the client states who answered to the Shahen Sha and could provide sizable armies ranging from as small as 6,000 to at least 15,000 not counting Armenia. The large Paththean vassal kingdom of Media Atropatini could allegedly raise as many as 50 to 60,000 during a revolt.

However, its manpower reserve in the service of the Paththeians was typically much smaller, although still considerable. Many of these client states and tribes brought their own specialized skill sets to the battlefield and could be considered analogist to Roman auxiliaries depending on the particulars of the arrangement. Peoples such as the Kadusi provided Javeliners, the Koti slingers, Cosan archers, and the Mardians, Uxi and Deameites infantry alongside internal forces. As previously mentioned, foreign mercenaries were always an option for the Paththeians who often recruited significant numbers of stepped nomads to their corpse.

For instance, the Seka were recruited by the Surin family and fought at carry against Cresus. The value and effectiveness of the Paththean infantry is often understated. While the Paththeians ostensibly relied on their cavalry as their decisive striking arm, any core of infantry capable of holding its own against the Romans in a field battle must have been well-trained, organized, and led. While the average Paththean infantryman may not have been quite as consistently wellarmed and armored as the average Roman legionary, their use of a large shield, heavy javelin and spear, and sword put them generally up to the task.

And if armies from other cultures and time periods are any indication, the wealthier veterans in the first few ranks must have been wellarmored. While few details are given about the tactics of Paththean infantry, their deployment can be inferred from earlier and later periods which point towards the use of mixed formations with heavy infantry in the first few rows, skirmishes and archers in the middle and infantry in the rear rows protecting against an attack from behind. Indeed, the Romans modified their usual deployment to fight similarly in this region as evidenced in inscriptions, texts, and the manual of the tactician Aryan. Paththean armies were also quite adept at siegecraft and military engineering as evidenced by the fortifications present at cities like Articata to Granuketa and others in Armenia and Media Atropatini.

Indeed, Roman bans on weapons and iron exports to Paththeia have been directly aimed at preventing skilled Paththeon and Cisanian engineers from building siege equipment and artillery. Conversely, these men also helped build the fortresses they garrisoned, much like Roman military engineers, as evidenced in inscriptions from Syria and Iraq. Paththean cavalry operated as many step cavalry armies do, relying on encirclements, faint retreats, or other flanking attacks. While the Paththeon deployment of catifacts is emphasized in Roman sources, the proportion of their army actually consisting of fully armored riders or fully armored riders and horses appears to have been quite low.

Serena's army allegedly only possessed 1,000 catifacts out of a total force of 10,000. While not insignificant, this broadly tracks with the norms of many pre-industrial societies using metal armor. These men would have been heavily armed and armored, riding on fourhorned or highbacked step saddles, enabling them to use two-handed or couch lances, wearing helmets, male, scale, or lamela body armor, laminated plate or scale armor on the arms and legs, and armed with long swords, large daggers and maces. The rest of the cavalry would be somewhat more modestly armored with medium cavalry used for skirmishing and harassing and horse archers supporting the catifact formation.

Paththean cavalry tactics are rather wellknown thanks to the account of the battle of Kerry in which the army of the Roman triumpvia crisis was famously annihilated by a Paththean host. Roman sources describe the use of wheeling and envelopment formations that match the Cyian and Allen drills of the much later Byzantine strategic telling of assaults that suddenly spled out parallel to the battle line to reveal the oncoming mass of catifacts behind the horse archers or complete encirclements where the cavalry fired into the surrounded Romans riding in concentric circles around them. These sophisticated and coordinated assault formations were enabled by an effective battlefield supply chain where archers were resupplied by camels carrying quivers of arrows as well as fresh changes of horse as mentioned at Carry and by the later 3rd century Roman author Casius Dio. The Paththeians were also led by competent and intelligent commanders who knew better than to throw cavalry at solid blocks of infantry, particularly the Hellenistic fallances of the Salucid or the Roman shield formations.

Instead, the purpose was to weaken and expose points on the enemy line by softening them up with archers and launching a mass assault as an initial test to determine if the enemy army was low in morale, poorly trained, or otherwise vulnerable to breaking and running. If this tactic failed, the Paththeons then resorted to hitand-run tactics to draw the enemy out of formation. If this succeeded, then the wheel could break away once again to assault with catifacts. If this failed, the Paththeians then resorted to attempting a faint retreat to lure the enemy either out of formation or into the open where they could then perform a complete envelopment.

The question is now thus, if the Paththean military was far more dynamic and professional than historians originally assumed, then why did it ultimately fail? Unlike the Hellenistic armies of the Salucid they faced before, the Paththeons faced a more adaptable and capable military in the Romans with an economic engine that outmatched their own. Roman iron mining, grain production, shipping lanes, and logistical depots enabled a force that could supply and maintain a permanent standing professional army, which could mobilize rapidly against incursions and stay in the field longer. While the Paththeanss had an advanced system of granaries and supply depots of their own, the need to rely on territorial armies that were mustered and collected into the campaigning army slowed their ability to respond to Roman incursions.

The reliance on both cavalry and catifacts in particular meant their army was susceptible to the gradual decay of the freeholder class as estates were acquired by wealthy families sapping both manpower and the income needed to arm and train such heavy cavalry. Finally, the Paththean client kingdoms often had their own political interests, and Roman interference made retaining control of certain minor powers to counterbalance the influence of others impermanent, creating internal conflict that then diverted military resources from where they were otherwise needed. Ultimately, such a situation would lead to the Paththeon's demise, as their focus was divided during one such civil war, resulting in their overthrow at the hands of the House of Sassan, who founded the Cisanian Empire.
