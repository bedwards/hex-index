Each pursuing steam propulsion to outpace and outgun wooden adversaries. In 1814, Robert Fulton designed the first paddlehe warship Demolos conceived to defend New York Harbor against the British. By the late 1820s, commercial paddle steamers along the Atlantic coast had become common, and navies began experimenting with similar power plants on warships. Yet, early single cylinder reciprocating steam engines were heavy and fuel inefficient.

By the early 1840s, paddle frigots with a displacement of 3,000 tons emerged, but they were impractical due to their bulky side paddles, which hindered the mounting of large numbers of broadside guns. They were vulnerable to damage and splitting the ship into two fighting halves. The four and a half after gun decks complicated fire control. The ships themselves were still wooden and the combination of heavy propulsion plants with the need for protection, stability, and seaorthiness strained wooden hulls.

The propulsion still demanded significant weight and space, leaving little room for heavier armament, ammunition, and stores. The decisive technological leap forward toward practical, fast, and wellprotected steam warships came with the screw propeller. The British achieved a dramatic breakthrough in 1839 with their first screw propeller steam ship Archimedes, which demonstrated the screw's superiority over the traditional paddle wheel. The Rattler, launched by the Royal Navy in 1843, proved the point in a dramatic tugofwar against the paddleheer in 1845.

The screw propulsion was more efficient and could be placed underwater out of sight of enemy fire, freeing space for armor and guns. The propulsion plant's weight could be better contained, allowing more robust protection without sacrificing speed or seaorthiness. Most important to navies was that the elimination of the cumbersome paddle wheels meant that broadside firing ships could now be machinepowered. The adoption of screwdriven steam ships quickly moved beyond experimental vessels.

By the mid 1840s, Britain was constructing screw liners, ship-of-the-line hybrids that still carried sails, but now depended on steam for propulsion. As propulsion transformed, so too did firepower. In 1821, the French artillery officer Href Pixon published his vision of shellfire that could penetrate wooden hulls and upend the era's tactical assumptions. The idea of heavy shellfired Pixon guns mounted on ships with armor soon took hold.

Pixon also expressed a broader strategic concept of novel force maritib consisting of frigot size and high-speed smaller vessels crewed by 30 to 50 men. France began testing Pixon guns in 1824 and adopted them fleetwide by the 1830s.

While Britain did not delay long in following suit, the upshot was that firepower was evolving toward greater penetrating power, longer ranges, and shells that could disable a wooden hull more quickly, which in turn forced navies to pursue greater protection, mobility, and firepower in tandem. The Pixon vision did not abolish sail or make wooden ships instantly obsolete, but it clarified a new path. Systems that combined heavier firepower with better protection and mobility would redefine the balance of naval power. Screw liners with pixon guns dominated the scene for a decade.

Then in 1845, Dupom submitted a proposal to build an ironclad steam-powered warship. The result was launched in 1850, a 28 gun warship with a displacement of 2,366 tons named Napoleon. She embodied a new approach that combined a modern steam plant with armor considerations, something that wooden ships would soon struggle to match. The British hastily responded with their Agimemnon, and for the next decade, the wooden crew liner remained the centerpiece of a naval arms race between France and Britain known as the Great Race.

Britain had the advantage in numbers, but France had superior design and individual ship performance. During the Crimean War, a small Turkish fleet was annihilated by the Russians at Cinnipe on the Black Sea, mainly by shellfiring guns that easily destroyed the Turkish wooden vessels. This led Napoleon III to request that 10 armored steam-propelled floating batteries be built for his navy so that they could safely bombard Russia's fortifications in the Baltic and Crimean Peninsula. However, France's lack of industrial infrastructure led it to ask Britain to build five of these.

In October 1855, three French floating batteries joined 13 ships of the line to bombard a Russian fort on the Kinburn Peninsula. The vessel's 4-in iron cladding withtood numerous shell hits as its guns silenced the Russian batteries. In 1857, the Commission Superior Central announced that as a result of the greater penetrative power of rifled guns, all work on wooden warships was to be halted and hence all men of war would be ironclads.

She carried 4.7 in armor plates backed by 17 in of timber capable of resisting British 68 pounders at a distance of 20 m.

She pulled a maximum speed of 13.1 knots and sported sails alongside her steam powered crew. Laga effectively rendered Britain's fleet of over 200 wooden ships of the line obsolete, igniting widespread alarm over the risk of invasion and the loss of naval supremacy. The fleet was expensive, but Duid Delom assured the minister Damarine that the warship's impact on the balance of power at sea would more than justify their price tag. Britain's reaction to Lagla was immediate and jolting. If Britain was to maintain its naval standing, something had to be done and done quickly. The Admiral T invited proposals from commercial firms for an ironclad that could best the French threat.

Iron had already begun to prove its worth as a shipb building material. It offered greater strength and more internal volume than wood, a combination that promised sturdier hulls and better protection without sacrificing space for machinery or munitions. The era's first ironbuilt vessel had already demonstrated the potential in civilian service. The Aaron man launched in 1821 for the London Paris route.

The Royal Navy's own foray into iron came a decade later with HMS Albert launched in 1840. With iron now established as a viable medium for warship construction, the Admiral turned to two engineers to push the concept forward. Isaac Watts, the Navy's chief constructor, and Thomas Lloyd, the chief engineer. They would produce two ironclad ships, the HMS Warrior on the Tempames and Black Prince on the Clyde that would blend the old and the new.

Their guns would be arrayed in long broadsides like a traditional man of war and the ships could be powered by either steam or sail. Although they were classified as frigots, these vessels were intended as capital ships and were dubbed ironclads. The design aimed to outclass Lagla in speed, protection, and armorament. Yet, it bore limits.

The armor belts could not blanket the entire hull, and vital areas such as the steering gear would remain vulnerable. Warrior, the larger of the two, progressed rapidly from lay down to service. Construction began in May 1859, and she entered service in October 1861. She marked a milestone as the world's first seagoing ironclad warship built entirely of rot iron for its frame, plating, and structural elements.

The hull employed a box girder design to provide longitudinal strength while keeping weight down relative to a wooden hull. Its cellular structure reinforced both battlefield resilience and the stress of ocean travel with iron plates riveted to the frames in watertight joints. A clip bow improved hydrodnamic efficiency for speeds under sailor steam. And at the four stood a figurehead carved from wood depicting a classical warrior with sword and shield.

Warrior measured about 380 ft in length and displaced roughly 9,284 tons. The midship's armor belt consisted of 4.5 in of rot iron backed by 18 in of teak with interlocking iron plates bolted to the hull in sizable blocks. The armor covered the central portion of the ship about 213 ft in length rising roughly 16 ft above the water line. The ends remained unarmored but were divided into watertight compartments to limit flooding in the event of damage.

The armorament evolved during construction and over the early years of service. Initially, Warrior carried a broadside of 40 smooth boore 68 pounder guns, 19 on each side of the main deck and one on the four castle and a chase gun in the bow and stern. As construction progressed, the arrangement shifted to 26 muzzle loading 68 pounders on the main deck, 10 Armstrong rifled breach loaders, and four 40 pounder Armstrong guns on the upper deck with smaller guns for boats. Between 1864 and 1867, the armament was revised again to 24 rifled muzzle loaders of 7 in and four 8-in rifles with all guns capable of firing both solid shots and shells.

Warrior was shiprigged with a sail area of around 48,400 ft, capable of about 13 knots under sail alone. When steam power was added, a two-cylinder trunk engine built by John Penn and Sons drove a single propeller via 10 rectangular boilers, delivering roughly 5,772 indicated horsepower and achieving about 14.08 knots on sea trials. The combined use of sail and steam could push her to around 17.5 knots. Her call capacity, about 853 long tons, gave a range of roughly 2,100 nautical miles at 11 knots.

Among the more distinctive features were a hoisted propeller weighing about 26 tons that could be raised to reduce drag under sail and funnels that could telescope to minimize wind resistance. Warrior's crew comprised roughly 50 officers and 656 ratings. She stood as a direct assertion of Britain's industrial prowess and naval ambition, more heavily armed than Lagua and built entirely of iron, marking the emergence of the first true seagoing ironclad warship. Yet warrior would not fire a shot in anger for some years.

Commissioned into the Royal Navy on August 1st, 1861 under Captain Arthur Cochran, she spent her early years as the fleet's flagship showpiece, leading the Channel Squadron to patrol home waters from Northern Europe to the Eastern Atlantic. Warrior escorted royalty on foreign visits and flew Britain's flag in courtesy calls at home and abroad, the living embodiment of a nation's pride in its industrial and maritime supremacy. By 1862, navies across Europe had adopted ironclads. However, the first battles using them would take place across the Atlantic.

At the time of the warriors launch, the American Civil War was underway, and politically the Confederates were more favored in Europe. The Union Navy was acutely aware that if the Royal Navy were drawn into intervention with ironclads like Warrior, it would be at a grave disadvantage. This together with the Confederates conversion of a steam icebreaker, the Enoch train, into the ironclad CSS Manasses, spurred the Union to develop a fleet of armored turret ships. The first battle between ironclads broke out on March 9th, 1862, when the CSS Virginia, converted from the hull of the USS Marramck into an ironclad Kasmmet gunship, faced the USS Monitor at Hampton Roads.

The two ironclads fought for about 3 hours, each trying to ram the other while their shells bounced off the armor. The clash drew worldwide attention, demonstrating that armored ships could dominate traditional wooden warships and signaling the obsolescence of the wooden wall. The indecisive jewel prompted a broader strategic reaction. Britain and France halted construction of wooden hull warships and shifted emphasis to revolving turret designs, recognizing that the age of wooden walls was ending and that iron and steam would shape naval power for generations to come.

The launch of HMS Warrior did more than introduce a new class of ship. It signaled a fundamental shift in naval power. By marrying a row iron hull, steam propulsion, and evolving armament, warrior embodied a move away from wooden walls toward ironclad fleets. It also spurred a short-lived Anglo French arms race, but British maritime supremacy would remain unchallenged for the following three decades.

The French knew they could not compete and as a result adopted the Jundikol doctrine, postulating fleets of small, fast, and heavily armed craft, becoming known as torpedo boats, operating in coastal waters as a counter to the enemy's battle fleet. By 1866, the Royal Navy had ordered its first oceangoing turret ship, HMS Captain, though she still relied on auxiliary sails. Naval gunnery would progressively embrace larger caliber weapons with greater range, replacing broadsides of cannon with rotating turrets or barbettes. Muzzle loading gave way to bridge loading, and by the 1870s, wooden ships of the line had vanished.

By 1883, Warrior was superseded by newer, better armed, and better protected ships. On May 14th, she entered Portsmouth for the last time under her own power. In her 22 years of service, six years were spent in full commission and eight as a firstline unit. The warrior archetype seeded a design norm that all major navies would adopt, the pre- dreadnaugh, a standard compromise that endured until 1906 when HMS Dreadnaugh redefined power, protection, and propulsion and ignited the Anglo-German naval arms race.
