Hurricane Katrina was an entirely expected storm. It was certainly a bad category 5 hurricane, but it was nowhere near the worst. Plenty more powerful storms have hit the mainland US since 2005, yet none came even close to the degree of human tragedy that Katrina unleashed. The reason this one became perhaps the most remembered modern US hurricane, even two decades on, has little to do with the weather itself.

Rather, it's because the truest disaster was the response. In the days immediately before and after the storm, a series of operational failures unfolded, and the consequence was dozens, hundreds, perhaps even more than a thousand preventable deaths. The earliest stages went as they should have. The US's weather agencies tracked the storm from the earliest phases of its formation.

And as it rapidly intensified and its severity became clear about 48 hours out from landfall, the Federal Emergency Management Agency or FEMA initiated level one disaster operations at its headquarters, as did its two regional offices whose territory lay in the storm's path. From here on, all three offices would be staffed and working 24/7 while all 15 of the emergency support functions at their disposal would now be activated. Importantly, as the agencies align with these emergency support functions allude to, while FEMA is often assumed to be the federal arms solely responsible for disaster aid and relief, it's better understood as the agency tasked with coordinating response and aid for help with major infrastructure problems. For instance, it'll coordinate with the Army Corps of Engineers for firefighting, the Department of Agriculture, which houses the US Forest Service.

Rather than run the show itself, it's the role of FEMA to listen to what local and state level emergency offices need, then coordinate with other federal offices and the private sector alike to procure the physical goods or expertise required in the disaster stricken area. But while not its primary intention, FEMA does still have some resources of its own, and these had been mobilized by noon on Saturday. Still 2 days out from landfall, the region 6 office mobilized one of the AY's five mobile emergency response support units or MS to Camp Bogard, Louisiana. An armada of 275 vehicles carrying massive power generators, heating and cooling systems, various telecommunication systems, multiple thousandgal tankers of fuel and water, and water purification systems.

MS units represent the brunt of FEMA's on the ground capabilities. With each MS unit is also a mobile emergency operation vehicle, a truck and trailer configuration capable of serving as a mobile headquarters and communications aid. And in this unit goes by the name of Red October. In addition to the MS unit, FEMA staged nearly 550,000 L of water, 700,000 lb of ice, and 330,000 meals ready to eat at the camp along with other supplies positioned elsewhere around the region.

The day before landfall, the map of supplies and personnel deployed at the federal level looked like this, with an emergency response team member being appointed federal coordinating officer here in Baton Rouge, where federal response would coordinate with the state of Louisiana's office of emergency preparedness. The storm seemed massive. Experts believe that if it lived up to its billing that the levies surrounding New Orleans were likely to fail, but the response appeared coordinated and ready. But as Katrina barreled towards the coast, signs of the response's potential limitations began to flicker.

On account of high winds, not all water and food sent to supply the Superdome, the major designated shelter, made it to the location, further limiting already constrained supplies. And some disagreement between New Orleans Mayor Ray Negan and FEMA head Michael Brown had already bubbled up on how to use the Superdome to begin with, as the latter argued that it shouldn't be a shelter of last resort since engineering reports showed the structure may not even hold in the event of a category 3 hurricane. A third party seemed to draw a similar conclusion as the American Red Cross deemed the stadium too dangerous and refused to stage their own staff there during the storm, announcing they'd provide resources to the shelter only after it had passed. Part of the hesitation stemmed from the concern that opening up the facility could encourage people to stay in the city rather than evacuate.

And in the days and hours when these hurried conversations were going on, the primary focus was to get as many people out of the city as possible. New Orleans had a specific evacuation plan that called for the southernmost parishes to leave first. These, Laroo, Plaean, St. Bernard, and St.

Charles were notified to start evacuations on Saturday at 9:00 a.m. by their parish leaders. That afternoon, sheriff's deputies were managing the intersections and traffic lights were turned off to expedite traffic out of town. By Sunday, deputies were going doortodoor to tell anyone left to leave.

With a nearly 98% evacuation rate and ultimately only three deaths in the Plaemen's community, this strategy worked. But not all areas were this successful. The next parishes, Lower Orleans, Upper Orleans, and Jefferson parishes were all supposed to evacuate by implementing a contraflow traffic strategy, which meant turning all lanes of New Orleans highways into one-way roads directed out of the city, thus increasing capacity. Problem was, these evacuations were only quote recommended.

In fact, different parishes used different wording, but none of them conveyed the urgency, and no one made the explicit call that residents actually needed to get out. By the time that Mayor Negan gave the direction at 11:00 a.m. on Sunday, less than a day out from landfall, that residents of New Orleans really did need to get out and they needed to get out now, there were an estimated 70,000 people still in the city. Besides intense gridlock due to sheer numbers, the problem now was that many of the tens of thousands of the remaining residents had no way of actually leaving.

They lacked transportation or were either disabled or elderly and couldn't travel alone. Officials offered some solutions. They asked these remaining residents to meet at coordinated pickup zones where buses would pick them up. But these buses only transported these vulnerable populations as far as the Superdome, where an estimated 12,000 people were sheltered by Sunday evening.

In the early hours of Monday, August 29th, Hurricane Katrina did what forecasters expected it to. It smashed into the Gulf Coast with 25 ft waves and 130 mph winds. And while New Orleans dodged the brunt of the storm's direct impacts, the surge of water that followed became the critical problem. Unsubstantiated reports of levy issues began rolling in in the morning.

Then at 6 p.m., as the skies began to clear, a Coast Guard flyover with a FEMA official aboard verified as much. The fragile levies tasked with keeping water out along the low-lying north side of the city had failed, as engineers had feared they would. As the storm gave way, flood water poured into the city, and the race was now on to get supplies and responders in and to get the stranded out. And immediately, a massive problem.

Flood water had reached this building and the rising water destroyed its emergency generator. This building was home to Mayor Negan's office and served as New Orleans City Hall, housing the city's emergency operations center or EOC. Today, in a post Katrina reality, New Orleans has the ability to take its emergency operations center on the move in the event that the EOC office is compromised. But that wasn't the case in 2005.

So, the office and mayor had to relocate here to the Hyatt Regency where the office would be stationed for the following days. Still, communications were dead. One phone line from the mayor's room would occasionally connect an outbound call, but could not receive them. Same for email.

Not until 3 days after landfall would the command center be able to make calls or receive emails. This created a massive command and control problem, turning a large but expected natural disaster into an utter debacle. Formal national scale disaster relief efforts in the US are structured as a federalist system that begins with and relies on local governments to pull resources from state and federal levels. The larger governments don't proactively push resources in.

They technically wait for these smaller local governments to ask for them. FEMA, the National Guard under the purview of the Department of Defense, the state of Louisiana even were thus all waiting to hear exactly what supplies Mayor Negan and the city's command center needed and where. But with cell towers down, landlines flooded, and the internet cut off, the city couldn't get information out. Of course, it didn't help that New Orleans parish officials had not adopted satellite phone backups in the years prior.

Nor did it help that federal planners hadn't positioned enough communication backups near enough to the city. Recognizing the problem at hand, that pole logistics would not be possible without being able to connect the joint field office in Baton Rouge to city officials, FEMA attempted to push communications capabilities into the city. The day following landfall, MS vehicles, including Red October, were mobilized from Barksdale Air Force Base and headed to the city corps to establish comms. There they would meet a FEMA coordinator being flown in by helicopter to form a unified command, oversee commodities ordered, and build out an operational base for search and rescue.

While the coordinator made it to the Super Dome, there was little he could do but wait for the communications vehicles, which by nightfall had only made it as far as Lafayette, as navigating debris made progress slow. The next day, it became clear they wouldn't make it at all as flood waters were too high for the vehicles to make it to the city core, turning Red October back to Baton Rouge. They had gotten tantalizingly close as one MS vehicle made it as far as an overpass within view of the Superdome. But no farther 2 days after landfall, as the city went from under siege by a storm and back to miserably hot summer, FEMA had yet to establish stable communications between the local emergency officials and Baton Rouge, where state and federal resources waited for direction.

While the lack of prepositioned communication resources within the city would be criticized in the months and years to follow, for now this meant major chaos in getting needed resources in for the thousands upon thousands of pounds of commodities palletized and positioned around New Orleans. Food, water, ice, tarps, generators. Disaster planners had designed a system where requests would be made by city and parish operation centers. They'd carefully be input by the state, and they'd be executed and tracked by FEMA through its database.

But now commodities were being requested via word of mouth and over the radio by overwhelmed parish officials. They were being hastily input at the state operation center without any vetting and half-hazard filling. And they were reaching or not reaching FEMA unforatted, unverified, and often just incorrect. A logistics chain carefully designed to procure and track resources, but built around the assumed reliability of phone lines and internet access had now turned into a stresscharged game of telephone.

As goods did begin to make their way into the city, they were the wrong goods or the wrong amount of the right goods. Truckloads of ice when food was needed, a truckload of ice when 10 truckloads were needed. In some parishes, the request they swore they made just never showed up at all. And without much of a real coherent paper trail, no one knew exactly why they didn't.

Without communications, logistics failed at all levels. Localities didn't get what they needed or were too overwhelmed to even make the request. While FEMA sent truck drivers hauling ice on 4,000mi or 6,500 km wild goose hunts as they struggled to figure out where to store then distribute goods on the fly. All the while, as federal, state, and local emergency operators worked to scramble goods into the city, FEMA slowly began to mobilize to get people out.

In the years prior, New Orleans officials had done significant amounts of planning on evacuating people from the city before the big one hit. But less thought had actually been given to getting people out after a major weather event. And this had disastrous consequences. One of these unforeseen challenges occurred in what was supposed to be the main evacuation space for refugees, the Superdome.

Up to 12,000 people made it inside pre-landfall, but on Monday morning, parts of the arena's roof tore open, compromising both power and water infrastructure in the facility. The predictions of structural deficiency proved correct. By Tuesday, the day after landfall, the shelter was providing little shelter. Its supplies were dwindling.

Its lower levels were flooding. Its safety and level of sanitation were dropping. It would need to be evacuated as soon as possible. And yet, people were still arriving in hordes from now sunken parts of the city.

By Wednesday, the population of the Superdome swelled to 23,000. As time drew on, the safe haven of last resort was proving anything but safe. The electricity system failed, and the backup generator could only power the internal lighting. The field level flooded, the plumbing failed, and the refrigeration systems went out, and food started to quickly spoil in the heat and humidity.

Media reports were alarming and tension was increasing. But because it took until early Wednesday morning for FEMA to officially assign the US Department of Transportation the task of mobilizing buses nationwide to assist in evacuating the Super Dome, getting people out was going to take a while. Because much of the region had been devastated, the plan was to evacuate those in the Superdome to the Houston Astrodome, some 350 mi or 560 km away. But the city school buses that had been staged to evacuate people before the storm were now underwater.

So officials scrambled to find buses across the state along with drivers willing to take on the task, which seemed increasingly dangerous as overblown or unsubstantiated reports of violence began to permeate from the city. By that evening, nearly 2 days after landfall, bus evacuations finally began, but it was hardly a major mobilization as a meager 120 evacuees headed towards Houston. Scrambling to make up for lost time, emergency contracts with bus companies as far as New York were set up while the governor passed an executive order commandeering school buses. But evacuations stopped and started in fits and starts as rumors of sniper fire and other violence grew louder thanks to faulty reporting by national media.

With such degraded communications infrastructure, leaders assume these rumors to be correct. While in retrospect, many have blamed the assumptions of violence on racially motivated stereotypes of the city's population. And all this chaos and suffering was playing out at the designated shelter points. Things were far worse at the convention center where thousands headed for shelter but found no supplies, no security, and no emergency responders along with these slightly elevated points.

An I 10 intersection that was called Cloverleaf, the Industrial Canal levies, and some Mississippi River levies. All exposed to the elements with only the Cloverleaf harboring the slightest supplies for medical rescues. Retreating from rising waters with no better options, the number of evacuees in these areas slowly grew throughout the days following landfall, while Coast Guard crews only added to the count as they launched rescue missions from Zephr Field, plucking people from roofs and depositing them at the same impromptu safety zones by boat. By Thursday, the US Coast Guard had 43 aircraft and over 2,000 personnel at the Aviation Training Center in Mobile, Alabama, a staging point left relatively unscathed compared to Air Station New Orleans.

Among these were 80 Coast Guard swimmers deployed to not only look for trapped people, but to start removing debris, downed power lines, and decimated infrastructure to clear the way for more emergency services. At peak, the Coast Guard teams were rescuing 750 people an hour by boat, and 100 people an hour by air. But for those rescued to high points like the I 10 clover leaf, which reached 4,000 at one point, they had to wait, surviving for days on meager rations dropped by helicopter and food that was left from a convenience store below. It reached nearly 100° at points and it rained at other points.

And as the days drew on, bodies floated past the cloverleaf while people in desperate need of medical attention died on it. By Friday, evacuations to the Superdome and Convention Center were finally properly underway with 475 buses deployed to transport people to the Astrodome, which quickly housed up to 25,000. As more than 200,000 sought refuge in Texas, overflow locations in San Antonio and Dallas popped up, too. Things moved slower at the convention center, where nearly 20,000 had now taken refuge.

By Thursday, the center received some supplies. By Friday, rescuers finally made it there. By Saturday, the last of the refugees had been cleared from the Superdome. And that night, the convention center would also be fully evacuated.

As for the overpass, that wouldn't be fully evacuated until Monday, a full week after the storm had hit. From here, search and rescue took center stage while the city, state, and nation began to wrap its head around how it would bounce back from the storm. Ultimately, it would take more than a year for the Superdome to reopen as a football stadium. And by the time it did, it was different.

With half a billion dollars in repairs, it was roomier, sleeker, more modern, easier to navigate. With time and at great cost, it was forever changed. So too was the city itself. In the 20 years since, New Orleans has built back at the cost of well over $100 billion.

Its population is starting to near what it was in 2005 before the storm. Its levies are now stronger. Its emergency planners are now more prepared. It's now more ready.

But it will also never be the same. Not solely on account of a storm, but on account of a storm exacerbated first by failed physical infrastructure and flooding, then by flooded and failing disaster response infrastructure. New Orleans is forever changed, principally because its population is forever changed. A slow, poorly executed response made worse by racially charged rumors of violence and more acutely felt by the city's poor and blacker populations pushed many of these very people out.

Whether it was the trauma associated with sheltering in a dangerous, disgusting, and dimly lit Superdome, or the PTSD stemming from close calls as the water poured in, or the prolonged suffering while waiting for rescue, Katrina and its response sent tens of thousands to spread across the American South and never come back. On account of failed logistics mixing with local long-standing social issues, what began as a large national disaster became a century defining human disaster that tore a city down to its very foundation and both highlighted and exacerbated issues of racial and economic disparities. On account of failed logistics, one of America's oldest and proudest cities changed forever.
