Each line being run by a technology more likely to be seen at a ski area. The gondola now a decade old with its first lines entering operation in 2014. Melopherico, as the network is called, has been framed as fantastical by travel vloggers and opulent and gimmicky by political rivals.

But so far, the unconventional system has worked out as it celebrated servicing some 520 million passengers in its first 10 years, expanded from three initial lines to 10, and grew from daily ridership of 90,000 to 200,000. The core of the logic behind La Paza's gondola system is that it solves the city's fundamental geography problem. The city is spread across steep, rugged terrain within a canyon where elevation ranges from 9,000 ft or 2,700 m to 12,000 ft or 3600 m. Generally, the wealthier and employment-rich areas sit lower and nearer to the river, while more working-class neighborhoods trend higher into the hills, punctuated by the sprawling city of Elto, sitting at top the plane above the canyon entirely.

Connected by a single highway and endlessly winding back streets, Elto is traditionally where La Paz draws much of its workforce from. Mere miles away, but 1,000 plus feet or 300 plus meters higher in elevation. While climbing such a steep grade is a unique problem for a traditional transit system, it's one well within the comfort zone of the technology that specializes in similar terrain, albeit for far different purposes. Gondilas, of course, are primarily designed for ski resorts, which earns them an opulent connotation.

But the engineering behind them is ingeniously simple and remarkably cheap. It's simply a steel wire rope suspended by pylons and anchored by two terminal stations. Inside the terminals, electric motors turn bull wheels which spin the rope at a pace that is consistent and reliable. This means installation is simple, requiring only concrete foundations for towers and terminals.

In fact, given the harsh winter weather at ski resorts, the majority of the work of gondola installation is typically scheduled over the summer, meaning it's common for one to go up after some prep work in just a few months. These same principles make gondilas low impact in dense cities, requiring just a few small plots for concrete pylon bases, and quick to install, often going from approval to operation in just a few short years. Most urban systems use detachable grip designs where cars slow down for passenger loading and disembarking. The actual hall rope typically moves at 9 to 15 m or 15 to 24 km/h, far faster than the speed at which passengers can safely load.

So when cabins enter the terminal, a flattened contoured steel frame compresses the coils and releases the grip, detaching the cabin and carrying it through the station on a separate slower track. Once it completes a 180° turn with the wheel, another seal beam compresses the springs and it reattaches to the wire before it begins its return trip. All of these parts, the cabins, the springs, the rope, the pylons, even the terminals are highly standardized. That's to say, the pylon that might end up in Le Paz is the exact same design of the pylon that might end up in Aspen, just with a customized height and angle, making them fast and low cost to produce on mass.

This cost and construction efficiency contributed to the lightning fast 6-year timeline to complete La Paz's 10 lines. Of course, gondlas are not perfect as public transit. After all, they weren't designed for it. Just like at 9:00 a.m.

on a powder day at a ski area, Melopherico experiences big lines during peak commuting hours due to the network's lack of surge capacity. A train or a bus line can simply schedule more vehicles during peak hours, but across Le Paz's 31 stops, a 10person gondola cabin is bound to head out every 12 seconds, but never any quicker. Capacity is fixed. And then beyond that, gondlas still just can't compete with the baseline capacity of trains.

Just the A-line of Medigene Columbia's Metro, for example, can move the entirety of La Paza's Gondola systems hourly capacity across the city with room to spare. There are also some issues with reliability, as at ski resorts, gondilas often operate from 9:00 a.m. to 400 p.m. during the ski season, then cease operations altogether for months long off seasons, leaving plenty of time for maintenance.

When operating as a public transit system, there's less room to fit this in without disruption. When comparing a gondola with a train, it's worse in just about every way but cost. But cost matters a whole lot in transit development, especially in a place like La Paz. Establishing some sort of public transit system has been a point of discussion in Bolivia's capital since the 1970s.

In the 80s, as both La Paz and Elto continue to grow, local leadership contracted help from MIT to analyze some options. The potential solutions, as they saw it, were trolley buses, light rails, or cable cars. All had their merits: speed, capacity, cost. All had their downsides, speed, capacity, cost.

But nothing came of it. In the decades that followed, traffic only got worse. Elto only grew bigger, and still nothing. Any system would be too costly.

It would take too long to build, and it would threaten too many jobs for the army of informal, influential minibus drivers shuttling between the cities. Then an economic boom coincided with the presidency of the brash socialist Evo Morales. Of indigenous descent himself, Morales recognized public transportation in the nation's capital as a popular opportunity for government investment. A means to recognize Bolivia's nationalized and burgeoning gas export economy through a public project that would help all, but particularly the workingclass and indigenous majority commuters.

It would be innovative, putting Bolivia on the map. It would be highly visible, helping with political popularity, and most importantly, it would be the most expedient option. Phase one of the project would consist of the red, yellow, and green lines, collectively servicing 11 stations running 6.5 m or 10.5 km and connecting Elto at two separate locations. This would cost a projected $235 million, a sum that the government could nearly cover with Treasury reserves alone.

While a massive expenditure for a country of Bolivia's economic scale and situation at $23 million per kilometer for a turnkey system that would open just 2 years after formal government approval, Meloperico was comparatively cheap and lightning fast to deploy. Even bus rapid transit systems, usually considered the cheapest fastest option, struggle to meet these costs and speeds. Bogatas system, for example, has exceeded that of the gondola's first phase at $24 million per kmter for one section and $33 million per km for another. And then there's the prohibitably expensive metro lines of Medigene and Bogota that have come in at 98 and $81 million per km respectively, each with decadel long construction timelines.

By floating over the city rather than reshaping it, Le Paz avoided radical road reconstruction and minimized property purchases, saving the government millions of dollars and years in construction. But cost per kilometer really doesn't matter in the long run if people don't use it. A transit system is only as good as its wrership. Core to the project's long-term viability was the belief that it could justify a one-way fee of about 43, slightly more than that of the minibuses.

It needed a large number of fairly lowincome individuals to decide to pay more for reliably faster commutes. But a decade in, it's done just that. Across the network, the average rider is commuting 22% faster and no longer has to worry about protests or flooding closing down roadways. They can leave home at the last minute, confident that today's commute will take exactly as long as yesterday's.

Melopherico in turn requires no operational subsidies from the government. But while breaking even gives confidence that the system can continue operating regardless of politics, it's hardly the success metric of a transit system. For that, consider a user living somewhere around here and commuting to Le Paz every morning. This excursion was once a 40-minute drive at best that could require haggling over price with a minibus operator and running the risk of being late on account of worse traffic than usual or weather or minibus breakdown.

Now that commute is just 10 minutes with no haggling, no worry of delay, no road stress, no car noise even. The impact of such a change is hard to overstate as it's proved nothing short of lifealtering for many. Now students can commit more time to study, commuters more time and energy to work and careers and all residents more time to interact with the two cities rather than trying to navigate between one and the other. Gauntless have provided a bridge between the economically and ethnically divergent cities.

In turn, now Leaz residents have a reason to visit the vibrant markets of Elto. No matter how one looks at it in the form of empirical study or an anecdotal interview with the Daily Commuter, the systems having a profoundly beneficial impact across the entire metro area it connects. Le Paz is far from the only Latin American city with a cable car system. And while it is the most expensive, it wasn't even the first. Medigene Colombia started the phenomenon in 2004.

The city is built into a wide valley with its wealthier core sitting primarily at the flat center and lower income areas generally creeping up onto the hills above. Unlike Le Paz, it does have a metro and tram system, but this primarily sits on the valley floor, leaving the lower income areas isolated from the rest of the city. So like leaz gondilas were implemented as a means of connecting these lower inome areas to the rest of the public transit network saving their residents from sitting through the horrendous traffic the areas can experience. Also like leaz studies indicate that in the newly connected areas employment is up crime is down and quality of life is just simply better.

With such a seemingly strong case behind them the urban cable car has been spreading like wildfire across Latin America. Caracus has first opened in 2010, Rio's in 2011, Calli's in 2014, and Mexico cities in 2016 as the first of a network that's now up to five lines with plans for plenty more. It's no wonder why. A confluence of factors make Latin American cities uniquely suited for gondola public transit.

They are more often located in mountainous areas, partially due to the prevailing landscape of the region, partially due to the historical choices of settlement locations. They are also often lower income, meaning city and national governments have less they can spend on grand capital spending projects like metros and trains. Many of these Latin American cities experienced their most rapid growth during the era of the automobile. So they sprawled far more than their European counterparts and yet due to the often informal growth of their lower income areas frequently lacked strong central planning to build high-capacity trunk roads like their North American counterparts.

So these factors uniquely combined to boost the use case for the gondola. The transit method is relatively inexpensive to build, yet comparatively fast due to the glacial speeds on the roads, especially in the hilly areas where travel speeds slowed down even more. This equation makes gondilas make sense. But what about the rest of the world?

Lacking this unique combination of factors, can gondas still serve a purpose as public transit outside of Latin America? There are certainly fewer systems outside the region. Far fewer cities in the rest of the world are built within hilly environments. And in order for a gondola to even begin to make sense as a transit option, there needs to be some geographic impediment that makes buses slower or trains impractical.

But elevation change isn't the only potential geographic impediment. For example, the first urban cable car serving as public transit in France opened in 2016 in the rather small city of Breast in the far west of Britany. It's a historical port town split in two by the deep coastal Penfeld River. For centuries, the banks of this river have been used as part of the city's naval base.

But in recent decades, the facility has shrunk as the Navy's industrial functions modernized and moved elsewhere. This left these workshops abandoned. So, the city purchased them with the intention of renovating into a new cultural and commercial center. The only problem was that the city center of Breast is directly on the opposite side of the river, and there were no bridges forming a direct route between the two.

At about a 20-minute walk, it was unlikely that the new Atalier Dapisan would become part of people's downtown shopping, dining, and sightseeing routines. The city considered a bridge, but costs would have been high between 30 and $60 million according to estimates due to clearance requirements. The river below was still quite actively used for naval functions, meaning any bridge needed to be high enough to allow oceangoing vessels to pass beneath, which made construction more complicated and timeconuming. So rather they turned to the cable car.

Its construction cost was just $20 million. Nowadays, the Telefreak de Breast runs every five minutes during peak periods, carrying a maximum of 1,200 passengers per hour over the Penfeld. The service has not been without stumbles. It's experienced a number of extended shutdowns due to defects and mechanical faults, but the project was approved under the expectation of 600,000 passengers a year, and counts have mostly sat between 700 and 800,000 a year.

All indicators suggest this was a success both for individual users and for the city of breast. But that doesn't necessarily mean cable cars as transit over rivers is always a good use case. The north side is a 3minute walk to a dockland railroad station whereas the south is 5 minutes to a tube station. In these cities and beyond, what was once considered fanciful is increasingly considered the most pragmatic option.
