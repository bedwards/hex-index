When conjuring up an image of Japan's ultra successful passenger rail system, one likely thinks of this, the Shinkansen. It's a fair association. This was, after all, the world's very first highspeed rail line, collapsing what was a 6-hour journey between Japan's two largest cities into just three. Only getting faster since its opening in 1964 and adding more and more service to match demand.

This route ran an average of 372 trains a day in 2023, shuttling 158 million passengers on the year. Across six decades, the line's total passenger count sits north of 6.5 billion, all without a single derailment, without any serious accident, without even a single fatality. All of this success opened the door for the Shinkinson network to expand nationwide. As it has grown, this network's become a point of national pride and international envy.

And yet, it only begins to explain why Japanese passenger rail works so singularly well. Annually, Japanese rails rank next to the likes of India and China, two far larger countries in total wrership. And most of the rail companies themselves managed to turn healthy profits while keeping customers content and services frequent. Much of this mileage traveled and profit garnered is done on the backs of trains moving at far more conventional speeds.

The fact is, the success of Japanese rail is punctuated by highspeed options, but the bedrock to its success is still run-of-the-mill conventional passenger trains. Passenger works here because the trains are trusted.

They're safe and always on time. They're so frequent that the traveler doesn't feel like they're sacrificing flexibility. and they offer so many unique routings that no matter if it's a daily commute, a weekend trip to the city, or a ski vacation to the north, taking the train just makes the most sense. Being fast helps, but the success has just as much to do with a culture of rail as it does technological achievement.

Setting the stage for this culture to take root is the fact that rail on the island does have some geographical advantages. Because of a rugged mountainous topography, Japanese population centers are focused and dense. Because of its island geography, these urban areas are distant from one another by European standards, but not American or Australian ones, allowing for earlier and easier rail connections between them. With rail taking hold in the final years of the 19th century, expanding the network was associated with industrializing and connecting the nation.

So, as Japan prioritized growth in the beginning of the 20th century, it did so on the back of the rails. Yet, while freight and industry helped lay the tracks, they really didn't need them postwar. As a country with limited raw natural exports and ample port access to begin with, then increasingly favorable conditions for trucking, passenger rail since the midentury has not really had to compete with freight for line access. But geography and timing aren't solely responsible for success.

Japan, for one, is a car country. In car exports, Japan's the third largest in the world. In car ownership, it's at 670 per 10,000 people, placing it comfortably in the world's top 30 near countries like Canada, France, and Norway. And cars, it seemed, might spell the end of passenger rail in Japan in the 1980s.

Established in 1949, Japanese National Railways, a staterun enterprise, owned 80% of Japanese rail after World War II and was eager to expand in the post-war period. It did so to the world's marvel with the launch of the Shinkansen in 1964. And yet, while a massively successful addition to the network, 1964 marked another first. The first year the nationalized rail posted a loss.

From there, as car ownership rose, trucking companies grew, and airlines expanded, things only got worse. Millions of yen in losses quickly turned to billions, then eventually trillions. With failed reforms ranging from network expansion to rate hikes across the 60s and 70s, rail share of total passenger traffic dropped from north of 50% to the mid20s while freight cratered from a 50% share to 5%. Undeniably overstaffed, inflexible in its strategy and bloated in bureaucracy, JNR spiral continued into the 1980s.

Burdened by a slowing national economy and a nationalized rail system that's debt had now reached 20 trillion yen, a number comparable to the debt of entire nations at the time, Japanese politicians were now urged to take on bolder reform. Quietly, the idea of privatizing the rails began to bounce around subcommittees. Then by the mid80s, it seemed the only logical option to halt the downward spiral. The process of privatizing JNR was more than anything a process of division.

One organization would become nine and the idea was that while in some they'd fulfill the same function, the division between them would lead to strength not found in their nationalized form. The core of the network was divided between JR West, JR Central, and JR East. More or less, JR West would operate rail west of Nagoya. JR Central would operate between Nagoya and Tokyo, while JR East would operate within and east of Tokyo.

The logic of where to place the borders was dictated largely by traffic patterns. Planners believe that by defining the borders such that as much travel as possible stayed within a company's borders, each company could specialize its operations to best serve the unique needs and wants of that region. For example, among other things, JR Central could learn how to best operate the superhigh traffic Tokyo Toasaka long-distance route, whereas JR East could focus on how to operate Tokyo commuter service most effectively. This map fulfilled that goal well.

Upward of 95% of all passenger trips stayed within each rail company's territory. Beyond that, another goal of the map was for each of the companies to inherit a balance of profitable and unprofitable lines. For example, JR West would inherit the Coobe line, relied upon by daily commuters to travel from this highly populated area to Osaka proper with tremendous passenger counts. This was also tremendously profitable.

But along with that, it would inherit the Gibbe line running through a sparsely populated mountainous region of the Hiroshima Prefecture. With operating speeds often far slower than that of the bus, its passenger counts were tiny and therefore it was deeply unprofitable. Japanese railways can technically close down lines, but gaining approval is a difficult and time-consuming process that often isn't worthwhile. So in practice, the companies would largely continue to operate low traffic lines using the profits from hight traffic ones.

But with these geographic divisions, the benefits and burdens were shared roughly equally between the three companies. But then there was the crown jewel both physically and financially of the Japan rail network, the famed bullet train, the Shinkansen. While each of the three companies would inherit some of the network, their portions certainly were not created equal. JR Central would get the least at 344 mi, but this stretch from Tokyo to Osaka was the original Shinkansen, traveling between the largest and second largest metro areas in the country.

Demand on the stretch was tremendous, and so was profit. JR West inherited more track, 388 mi, but demand and profit in this area was lower, whereas JR East got 519 mi of track, but through the less populated north of the country, where the Shinkinson struggled to achieve strong financial returns. Therefore, at first, the Shinkinson lines were the only not owned outright by the JR companies, and instead they were placed within a newly formed entity called the Shinkinson Holding Company, which would then lease the track and associated infrastructure to the operators. The fees for this would be calculated not based on the capital cost of the infrastructure itself, but rather proportionally to the profits of the rail segments as a way of correcting for the geographic imbalance in profit margins.

But this is not the entirety of Japan. This is the entirety of Honshu, the main island of Japan. There are three other major islands that make up the country, Kyushu, Shikoku, and Hokkaido. The profitable Shinkansen network did not yet reach the islands except for a small portion of the Sonos Shinkansen reaching Fukuoka and their lower population densities meant they lacked as many profitable conventional lines as well.

So whereas JR West Central and East were formed with the intention of quickly becoming profitable, these three were treated differently. Most notably, they'd receive a subsidy from the government to offset their inevitable losses. Also sliced off from JNR was their freight business. Freight rail was never massive in Japan and it only decreased as the truck industry grew and took market share away.

JNR's freight business therefore operated at a loss. It also tended to operate over long distance spanning beyond the borders of the individual JR companies. So they rather formed one nationwide freight company. That way no one JR company would be burdened with its losses while JR freight could reduce costs by not having to maintain track and rather just pay for track usage.

Finally, the last of the nine new companies was called the JNR Settlement Corporation. It acted as an umbrella company owning all the other companies while it itself was owned by the Japanese government. This mattered in part because of debt. There was a huge amount of it and just simply distributing it amongst the JR companies themselves would leave them in the same struggling position as JNR.

Therefore, only a portion of the trillions of yen in debt was transferred to the JRs, distributed amongst JR West, Central, and East, whereas the island JRs received none. Another substantial portion went to the Shinken Holding Company, roughly equivalent to the value of its assets. Then the vast majority of liabilities went to the umbrella company, JNR Settlement Corporation. The hope was that through time, the value of the JRS would increase and therefore the eventual sale of the companies could cover the debt.

JNR Settlement Corporation was owned and controlled by the government. So ultimately, this meant that Japan's railways weren't fully privatized, at least at first. Rather, they were divided into government-owned companies that were intended to act as private companies would and strive for profits. The state of the Japanese railways was such that the newly formed JRs would hardly be attractive investment opportunities, meaning the government would struggle to take them fully private at what they considered a reasonable valuation.

Rather, the thought was that they needed to give time for the reforms to take hold and improve the company's prospects. But more practically, the manner of full privatization chosen was to list the JR companies on the Tokyo Stock Exchange. And there are certain requirements that need to be met for listing, such as pre-tax profits in the period immediately before listing needing to be more than 40% of paid in capital. Crucially though, the Tokyo Stock Exchange would only list companies at least 5 years old, and the JR companies were brand new.

Of course, in order for this grand plan to work, for the railways to become self-sufficient, for the government to be free of its debt, for Japan's rails to improve their service, the JRs had to genuinely effectively reform, and they certainly did. Bureaucratic bloat was quickly fixed. 90,000 redundant employees were removed, largely through transfer offers to other jobs in the public and private sectors. Prior to privatization, there was one employee for every 311,000 mi or 510,000 km traveled by passengers.

After it was up to 897,000 mi or 1,443,000 km. And despite the cuts in headcount, service actually improved. The frequency of service on average increased. Serious accidents hald and improvements were made to stations and rolling stock.

Financially, things improved too. The revenue to cost ratio went from 0.771 to 1.167, meaning on average, the train companies were earning an operating profit thanks to a combination of cost reductions and revenue increases. And this wasn't thanks to heavy government subsidies. Subsidies were far lower overall than prior to privatization, nor was it thanks to fair increases.

The rate of increase actually slowed significantly relative to prior to privatization. Perhaps the primary confounding variable was the fact that Japan's economy was in a boom throughout much of the period immediately postprivatization. So there was plenty of demand for rail travel. But all in all, the process worked.

So much so that as soon as JR West, Central, and East hit the required 5-year mark, they began the process of listing on the Tokyo Stock Exchange. Through the sale of shares, the JNR Settlement Corporation generated revenue that was used to pay down its debt. While the JRs were also profitable enough to start buying the Shinkinson network from the Shinkinson holding company, further generating revenue for the settlement corporation. But beyond the reforms on the rails and in company structure, it was another crucial change that allowed the JR companies to flourish.

Prior to privatization, Japan's national railway really stuck to the rail business itself. But other private rail operators in Japan were finding profits in a different business, real estate. Long before privatization, smaller private rail operators were pioneering a technique of using rail to grow the value of land and property. Basically, the better an area's rail access, the higher its property value.

In Tokyo, privately held Tokyo corporations started developing commuter lines in the 1920s. This is Geoga, nicknamed Little Europe or Liberty Hill. This approach to leverage real estate around stations to invest in retail and hospitality was likely born from some necessity and not just ingenuity thanks to the country's railroad nationalization act which nationalized 17 of the country's 37 private railways from 1906 to 1907 when it was enacted. Part of its limitations restricted private railways from directly competing with government lines.

So the private operators pivoted and looked to real estate and diversified assets in order to create revenue when building new rails. But it turns out that that technique worked tremendously well. So much so that the JRS adopted it following privatization in 1987. This happened all over the country.

Japan's railways have a recipe for success. The structural reforms created companies capable of turning a profit. The real estate businesses made that profit diversified. And the network these companies inherited was such that they could run fast, frequent, reliable service at an affordable cost.

But it didn't turn out to be a one-sizefits-all solution. Jushu listed on the Tokyo Stock Exchange in 2016, becoming the first of the island JRs to do so. But JR Shikoku and Hokkaido still lag far behind. J Hokkaido struggles most.
