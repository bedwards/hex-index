R.J. Petit, RHP, Colorado Rockies

The Rockies start their new era of pitching with a pick that I’m kind of split on in regards to his fit at Coors Field. Petit is a huge dude (6’8”) who is efficient in his spin, creating a two-plane fastball shape that averaged close to 16” vertical break and 11” arm-side movement at 95 mph. This “efficient spin” causes large total movement fastballs (think above average vertical and arm-side) to take a haircut at parks of high altitude. It’s not going to be as effective, and we’ll probably see it drop from the 43% he threw it to lefties last year. You’d think this haircutting of total movement would help sinkers drop more. While it does, the loss of arm-side movement makes them less reliable from a ground-ball standpoint. I guess the positive is that Petit gets so much arm-side on his sinker, it should still be around an average shape.

Usage is the thing to watch with Petit, and it gets to a larger organizational point with the Rockies: they’ve been throwing too many fastballs. In 3 of the last 4 seasons, they’ve been top 8 in combined four-seam and sinker usage in the league. Those are the two pitches that are put in play the most, in the park that hurts you the most for balls in play. It’s not a great combination. Shapes that are less affected by altitude are those that don’t move much from a “short-form” movement perspective. Another way to visualize this is by looking at Baseball Savant plots and targeting pitches close to the center of the plot. Coors-proof pitches are those that spin as close to a bullet as possible. For pitchers with efficient fastballs, this usually means their short, tight sliders don’t take too much of a hit from a movement perspective, so while Petit’s fastballs will become less reliable, his slider should survive and be by far his most used pitch.

This slider is the prize of his mix. It’s bordering on “death ball” territory, which is classified as sliders that drop more than average, generally from slots that are more vertical than average (like Petit’s). He got a bunch of whiff and chase, it was around the zone a good amount, and I’d expect to see this pitch thrown 50%+ against righties (it’s currently 43%) and 35% or more against lefties (currently 23%). His slider performed better than his changeup to lefties, which is why it makes sense to turn his slider into his primary and back off the four-seam against the handedness I’m more concerned about. Petit will be a test case for the Rockies (hopefully) new philosophies. If they copy-paste his approach from the Tigers, he’ll struggle at Coors. He’s a reliever who can probably work in some leverage spots given how barren the Rockies’ bullpen is. A 4.50 ERA across 60 innings would probably be a win.

Jedixson Paez, RHP, Chicago White Sox

Paez had a stellar 2024, posting a 3.17 ERA and FIP with a pristine 3% walk rate. A calf injury sidelined him for over three months of the 2025 season, forcing him to post an innings total under 20. Workload is the primary concern here, but given the White Sox's selection, I’d assume they’re thinking optimistically about the upper bound of his innings in 2026. The White Sox have to cover innings, and they won’t spend a lot of money to achieve that goal, so they have to accept risk like this or there’s no chance of overperforming expectations.

The glaring strength of Paez’s profile is his ability to throw strikes. His 71% pre-two-strike zone rate in 2024 was 11 points above the MiLB average (plus to plus-plus territory). Almost the entirety of his repertoire held strike rates north of average, even when pushing into secondaries. His command remained intact over his small sample of 2025 innings as well. If there was ever a High-A pitcher to make a three-level jump to the show and not be overmatched from an in-zone competitiveness standpoint, it’s Paez. One small note I’ll mention is that he has a pretty pronounced head whack, aka, he’s looking toward first base after release. This is often associated with poor command, at least by old school folks. It’s fun to see a pitcher with some of the best zone feel in the minors provide an exception to the “rule.”

Between 2024 and 2025, Paez’s slot rose slightly. His extension dropped 2-3”, his release point went up 2-3”, and I’d project his arm angle climbed from 35° to 40°. This added a subtle amount of vertical break to both his four-seam and sinker, but also created depth on his larger breaking balls (his sweeper went from -1” vertical break to -5.7”). He went from throwing his changeup, sinker, and sweeper greater than 24% against right-handed hitters to flattening out his pitch distribution. He now throws four pitches greater than 11% to righties, with a slightly more concentrated distribution against left-handed hitters (>23% sinker, cutter, changeup). It’s an incredibly deep mix, necessitated by his below-average velocity.

The White Sox have had success with seam effects under the tutelage of Brian Bannister. The requirement for that is some cut to a pitcher’s fastball, which Paez does. The floor here feels pretty strong. If they can coax a ~4.20 ERA out of him in 120+ innings, it’s a smash pick. Paez is the arm that will have the most impact among those selected in the Rule 5 Draft.

Griff McGarry, RHP, Washington Nationals

McGarry is a prototype spin monster who hasn’t shown enough strikes to project anything more than a name we ogle because of the big shapes he can create. On top of that, he also has intriguing release traits, with a well below average release point and above-average extension. And as if there weren’t enough pros here before we tackle the big con, McGarry’s four-seam shape deviates considerably from his low arm angle. This results in a relatively flat approach angle (-4.3° VAA) and shape from a style of arm that I feel like we normally see more cut-style fastballs from.

McGarry throws a true plus to plus-plus sweeper, with enough depth on it (shown by the negative vertical break) to flash it against left-handed hitting. I’m also a fan of his shorter slider shape, which he throws more to right-handed hitters when he’s behind in the count. None of these pitches, however, are competitive frequently enough that we can reliably get McGarry into counts where hitters will actually chase him out of the zone. I would guess this is why McGarry is throwing so many fastballs in the minors. He threw 54% to righties and 65% to lefties in 2025. It’s just not working. McGarry had a 19% walk rate in 2023, 15% in 2024, and 14% this year. It’s not impossible to make it with so few strikes, especially when we can probably project a 30% strikeout rate, but it’s difficult to actually perform well. 5 of the 10 arms with the worst walk rates in MLB as relievers last season had ERAs above 4.50 (minimum 40 IP).

McGarry is the kind of arm I love. There’s spin, multiple quality breakers, room for a sinker to righties (maybe that will help his strike rate?), and a fastball shape that is distinct. But given how modeling has converged around big spin and shapes, there are a lot of arms like this in the minors. Even if McGarry is slightly more appealing, I struggle with how to project command improvements in pitchers who have never shown any. He’s a pure reliever for me right now, unlikely to push into leverage with a walk rate this high. The unlikely ceiling outcome is a righty version of Brendon Little’s 2025—high walk, high strikeouts, and success, but needs luck to push toward 1.0 fWAR reliever territory. Let’s see if the Nationals’ new pitching hires can work some magic.

Jose Rodriguez, RHP, Los Angeles Dodgers

Rodriguez’s nickname in the Dodgers orgnaization is “Tibu,” short for tiburon, which is Spanish for “shark.” The nickname originates from his father, who had the same nickname during a career that spanned from pro player to GM in Mexico. Rodriguez is a gangly righty with low spin capacity and more of a “pronator” profile from a shape standpoint. This means he’ll have trouble creating more lateral breaking-ball shapes, but the baseline velocity he possesses raises the floor for everything he’s doing. The positive byproduct of this pronator-style of pitching is that they often end up with strong changeup shapes, which Rodriguez has. His changeup averages -1” induced vertical break, which is ~4” more drop than the average changeup from his arm angle at the same velocity.

I used “gangly” to describe Tibu because he’s 6’6”, and his delivery is very active. He sets up crossfire (the numbers on his jersey are facing the batter before he starts his motion), his front leg comes up to touch his armpit when not throwing from the stretch, and he has a Johnny Cueto-esque turn to his delivery before he uncoils into release. Although these elements are descriptive without objective variables to point to and go, “oh, that’s why he’s hard to pick up as a hitter,” the one thing we can point to is his release height. Pitchers in the 25-30° degree arm angle area average a 5.5’ release. Rodriguez is up at 5.8’. While the league likes to target low release, flat-approach arms, I’ve wondered for a while whether more distinct heights for pitchers can create release points that don’t line up with points in space that hitters are used to seeing those arm angles from. While this doesn’t appear to be helping his fastballs, which both had xSLGs above .470 in the minors this season, it could be helping his secondaries, which both ran whiff rates above 48% against both left- and right-handed hitters.

The large knock is that Rodriguez’s walks regressed last season. He went from an 8% walk rate in years past to 14% across his 54 innings of work in 2025. Even with the 8% walk rate, he was never really a “command” guy, posting zone rates below that of the major league average but being able to hold chase rates above 32%. His chase dropped to 27% this season, and it seems to be the driving factor in his ballooning walk rate. Perhaps it’s not so much of a command problem as it is a hitters-got-better-and-stopped-chasing problem. It’s also fair to attribute some of the command regression to a knee injury he dealt with for parts of 2025. And if two reasons aren’t enough for you, a third is that his shapes regressed when he transitioned to the MLB ball at Triple-A. All of these likely had some impact on his unimpressive 2025.
