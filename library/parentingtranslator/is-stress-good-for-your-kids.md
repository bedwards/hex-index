One of the parenting mantras that I say to parents (and to myself) over and over again is this: **“A manageable amount of stress in the presence of a loving caregiver is GOOD for children.”** What this means is that even some of the “difficult” moments your child faces, or the mistakes you make as a parent, can actually be beneficial for their growth and resilience.

Although every parent knows they can’t shield their children from all hardships, none of us want to see our kids struggle—it goes against every instinct we have. Imagine the feeling when you find out your child is being bullied at school or that they cried when you dropped them off at preschool. Your immediate urge is to fix the situation and make it better. Sometimes, if you can’t, you might even blame yourself. It can be even more painful to realize that a parenting mistake might have caused your child some difficulty—maybe you forgot to sign them up for the soccer team, or allowed them to watch a movie that gave them nightmares for weeks.

It is beyond painful to see our children go through these experiences. Yet, we also know that if we remove every hardship or stressful event from their childhoods, they will grow up not knowing how to face the inevitable challenges that life throws their way. Although it might be painful to witness, it is vastly better for them to learn how to cope with these situations while they are still under our roof than when they are on their own as an adult.

### The Research

Research backs up this rational line of thinking, consistently showing that stress can be good for children. In the research this is called **stress inoculation**. Think about it like a vaccine— allowing children to experience small amounts of stress in a controlled way increases their chances of fighting off stress in the future.

[Research](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20939649/) finds that people who have faced some life adversity are much better off than people who have faced no adversity— they are happier and more satisfied with life and show fewer signs of mental health symptoms or post-traumatic stress. They also some less bothered by recent events. In other words, the old platitude of “what doesn’t kill you indeed does make you stronger” seems to be true.

Experiencing stress in childhood in particular seems to have a large impact. [Research](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1999-03308-005) finds that people who have experienced stress in childhood cope better with spousal loss, illness, and major accidents in adulthood. Similarly, people who experience work-related stress as teenagers showed [fewer mental health effects of work stress as adults](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/development-and-psychopathology/article/abs/early-work-as-a-source-of-developmental-discontinuity-during-the-transition-to-adulthood/5D7047754B5F3FF673109D4E296CF01C). [Research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3933029/pdf/nihms548541.pdf) also finds that children with moderate amounts of life stress are the least reactive to stress when compared to children with very little stress or very high stress. Adolescents who experience moderate amounts of stress also show [better mental and physical well-being](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39310005/).

### So How Do You Tell the Difference between Manageable and Toxic Stress?

It is clear from that research that that some manageable amount of stress is “good” for children. Yet, research also shows that stress can reach a toxic level in which case it is damaging to children, impacting both their physical and psychological development. Research has even found that this type of stress [can change the way the brain functions.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4561403/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) So how can you differentiate manageable stress that is “good” for children from the toxic stress that is damaging? How do you decide when it is too much for your child?

There are a few rules of thumb. Manageable stress relatively low in intensity, temporary or time-limited, and occurs in the presence of loving and supportive adults. Stress also feels manageable to children when they feel like they have some control over the situation (for example, when it is a problem that they can try to solve). Examples of manageable stress include short separations from parents, a conflict with a friend, not winning a game, not being invited to a party, and having to speak to an unfamiliar adult. On the other hand, toxic stress is chronic and intense and usually out of the individual’s control. Examples of toxic stress include any kind of abuse or neglect, poverty, bullying, ongoing conflict between parents, or extreme pressure to perform without sufficient support.

### How To Keep Stress From Becoming Toxic?

Here are some research-backed strategies parents can use to prevent stress from becoming toxic

1.  **Perceived sense of control**: [Research](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1527280/) finds that children who have more control over their lives feel less distress in stressful situations. Give your child more of a sense of control over their environment by giving more choices, teaching problem-solving skills, replacing their helpless thoughts with more empowering thoughts (turning “I can’t” into “I can try my best” for example), and teaching them to control their emotions even if they can’t control the situation.
    
2.  **Social support:** [Research](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0153715&type=printable) finds that stress may not lead to negative life outcomes when children have positive family and friend support. You can prevent stress from becoming toxic by supporting and nurturing your child during stressful events— provide them with validation and empathy, coach them in using coping skills, and help them to problem-solve.
    
3.  **Reframe stress**: How you think about stress really does matter too. [Research](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23437923/) finds that when people believe that stress enhances performance, productivity, and well-being, they show better health and work performance. Explain to your kids the concept of “good stress” by comparing it to a vaccine which ultimately makes their immune system stronger or explain that their brain is a muscle which becomes stronger when challenged.
    
4.  **Sleep and exercise:** [Getting enough sleep and regular physical activity](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/smi.2896) seems to protect children from the harmful effects of stress.". You can help your child to stay healthy by having a regular bedtime schedule and routine, limiting screens, and engaging in regular activity as a family.
    

### **What This Means in Practice**

So some stress is “good” for children, but how do you change your behavior as a parent to allow them to experience stress?

-   **Encourage children to try something that makes them a little nervous**, such as climbing on a new piece of equipment at the playground, talking to an unfamiliar person, or trying a new sport. Praise them for their effort whether or not they are successful in the activity.
    
-   **Provide children with a lot of validation, empathy, and reassurance when facing stress**. Facing stress unsupported is what is harmful to children. Watch your child for signs that the stress is going from challenging to overwhelming, and intervene when necessary.
    
-   **Notice when your own anxiety may be influencing your responses or decisions**. Ask yourself whether safety is really a concern or whether you are being overprotective.
    
-   **Teach your child coping skills, such as deep breathing, self-talk (“I can do this even though it is hard”), and asking for help, when you are both calm**. Model using effective coping skills for your own stressors (talk out loud so your child can hear your own emotional regulation and problem-solving strategies)
    
-   **Give your child a chance to solve their problems on their own before you step in to help**. For example, your child will learn a lot more and gain a lot more confidence if they figure out how to solve a conflict with their sibling on their own than if you step in and solve it for them.
    
-   **Don’t stress when you make parenting mistakes.** You are giving your child a chance to handle some adversity and showing them that everyone makes mistakes sometimes. You are also helping them to develop realistic expectations for all relationships— other people will disappoint them from time to time and they need to learn to cope with that.
    

### Overall Translation

No parents enjoy seeing their child go through a stressful situation but research shows us over and over again that small amounts of manageable stress is really good for children. Manageable stress is different from toxic stress in that it is more mild, time-limited, and feels somewhat under control. Stress is also more likely to be beneficial when it occurs in the presence of loving caregivers. Allowing children to face stress under the care of your loving guidance will prepare them for when they inevitably face stress as an adult without you there to guide them. Rather than shielding children from all discomfort, guiding them through it teaches them that stress is a normal part of life—and that they are capable of overcoming it.
