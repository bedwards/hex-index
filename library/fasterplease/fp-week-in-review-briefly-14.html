✨🧠 Teaching students to think, not just scroll, in the Age of AI

Classroom Context. Artificial intelligence could become the most powerful learning tool ever created — a personalized tutor that adapts to each student, like the “Illustrated Primer” in The Diamond Age. But as AEI’s Frederick Hess notes, schools already mishandled the last digital shift. The smartphone era should have been a warning; instead, it became a blueprint for distraction.

The Smartphone Syllabus. Schools handed kids glowing rectangles long before they understood how children actually behave with tech. Now, nearly all middle and high schoolers check their phones in class, academic performance has slipped, and attention spans have frayed. Screens became the lesson plan; learning paid the price.

The Teaching Trap. If schools simply bolt AI onto today’s classroom routines — worksheets, shortcuts, prepackaged lessons — it risks accelerating shallow learning rather than deep reasoning. The danger isn’t AI itself; it’s misusing AI to make bad instruction more efficient.

Rethinking the Lesson Plan. Some educators are shifting back to low-tech, high-cognition methods: pen-and-paper quizzes, in-class essays, and Socratic dialogue that forces students to articulate what they actually think. It echoes the first-year law school model: interrogate ideas, don’t outsource them.

Up Wing Up Shot. The goal isn’t to ban AI but to teach students how to think with it — by strengthening their analytical muscles, not just their prompting skills.
