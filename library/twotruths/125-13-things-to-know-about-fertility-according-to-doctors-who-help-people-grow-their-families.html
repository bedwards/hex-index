Think back to what you learned about getting pregnant. For me, it was more like what I learned about not getting pregnant: high school health classes, vague conversations, the birth control pill, and, always, a far-off idea of maybe someday having babies of my own.

Today, fertility conversations feel different. There are preconception classes, workplace benefits, and increasingly, more state-by-state coverage for assisted reproductive technology (ART)—fertility treatments such as in vitro fertilization (IVF)1 and intrauterine insemination (IUI)2, an avenue about 250,000 people in the U.S. pursue every year. Last month, Costco made news by offering members virtual appointments and referrals for discounted IVF and IUI cycles. Meanwhile, in an effort to tackle a declining birth rate, France plans to send a letter to all 29-year-olds reminding them of the pressing nature of fertility—a move critics say misses the mark.

At the same time, there are conversations about rising maternal age and falling birth rates3 all over the globe.

In many ways, the fertility conversation is a welcome one. “Greater awareness about fertility, reproductive health, and preconception planning can be incredibly empowering and help people make more intentional decisions about family building,” says Lora Shahine, MD, a physician at Pacific Northwest Fertility, an Ivy Center in Seattle, WA.

Upfront, accurate, evidence-based education empowers women without fear, guilt, or pressure, says Danielle Dang, MD, senior medical director at the virtual women’s health clinic Maven Clinic.

Yet, fertility conversations have a way of going sideways4. Fertility doesn’t exist in a silo; it is personal, political, societal, and deeply emotional. Family looks different for everyone, which is why fertility education must be thoughtful and option-based.

“It’s already so hard to be a woman, and guilt is a real thing,” says Dang. “We don’t need guilt. We need to be moving these conversations upstream. Regret from a lack of information is really real.”

We recently spoke with Dang (who conceived her son via an egg donor) and Shahine to better understand what that looks like. Below are 13 takeaways from our conversations—and what helpful, proactive fertility education includes.

1. Most people will not face infertility.

There are many paths to parenthood. The vast majority of healthy couples in their 20s and 30s will also conceive within a year, says Dang. About 13 percent of people experience infertility—defined as not getting pregnant after a year of regular sexual intercourse without using birth control if you’re under 35, and 6 months of trying if you’re over 35.

If you have actively been trying to get pregnant after these timelines and are struggling, touch base with your OB/GYN. The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) also recommends an evaluation before trying if you’re over 40.5

2. Preconception care matters. It’s not as prescriptive as you may think.

Did you see the recent articles about trimester zero? Well, first: “Optimizing fertility” is important. What truly supports fertility is consistent health habits and individualized care, which is why it’s key that you work with a medical provider you know and trust. (This could be an OB/GYN to start; you might also work with a reproductive endocrinologist, depending on where you are in your journey.)

There is solid evidence6 that certain preconception steps matter, says Shahine. These include taking prenatal vitamins, managing chronic conditions, maintaining a healthy weight, limiting tobacco and excess alcohol, and addressing known reproductive issues.

The worry is that preconception is becoming commodified into a wellness category. Shahine urges caution around any advice that is “overly rigid, expensive, or anxiety-provoking” (protocols that are “marketed with stronger claims than the evidence currently supports” and can lead women to feel pressured instead of empowered).

3. There are medical conditions that impact fertility.

Sometimes, conditions such as polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS) or endometriosis negatively impact your ability to get pregnant. If you have these conditions—or think you might—it’s important to talk with your healthcare provider, as there may be other preconception changes you can make, Dang says, including medications or hormone level checks.

4. Timing matters.

While we learn a lot about how not to get pregnant, it’s important to know that “you can’t get pregnant all the time,” says Dang. The conception window is really just about six days per cycle—the five days before ovulation and ovulation day itself. Peak chances of conceiving fall in the two days just before ovulation, and the window closes after the egg is released. It’s possible to get pregnant slightly outside of this window, but it’s not as likely.

5. Infertility is not a ‘women’s issue.’

Language and focus on fertility are almost always centered around women—and women often carry the brunt of fertility treatments—but about half of fertility issues are due to male factors, such as sperm quality, DNA fragmentation, and other issues.

6. Fertility doesn’t fall off a cliff; it does decline steadily with age.

Age remains the single biggest factor in fertility, affecting egg quality and your chances of pregnancy, says Shahine. But ACOG itself notes that “35 is an arbitrary threshold, and many age-related risks don’t become clinically significant until 40 or later.”

Think of your 20s and early 30s as your “prime reproductive years.” Around 1 in 4 healthy women in this window will get pregnant in any given menstrual cycle; by age 40, that figure drops to around 1 in 10, with a gradual decline in between.

Male fertility also declines with age—sperm motility, shape, and DNA quality all gradually worsen—just more slowly and less predictably than egg quality does.

7. Many women have healthy pregnancies and babies after 35; that said, ‘advanced maternal age’ is still a thing.

If you get pregnant after the age of 35, docs call that “advanced maternal age” (the hopefully retired term was “geriatric pregnancy”). Today, about 1 in 5 pregnancies in this country is in a mom over 35; about 11% of first-time pregnancies occur in women over 35. (For the first time in U.S. history, more babies are also being born to women over 40 than to teenagers.)

Also key to remember: Pregnancies over 35 do come with an increased risk for complications such as gestational diabetes (which occurs two to three times more often), preeclampsia (the risk rises significantly with age), and cesarean delivery. Compared to women in their late 20s, overall pregnancy risk is about 1.4 times higher at ages 35 to 39, 1.8 times higher at 40 to 44, and more than three times higher after 45.

Part of this has to do with the fact that egg quality declines with age, but also that as we get older, we’re more likely in general to have chronic conditions such as high blood pressure or diabetes.

The truth about ‘clinically studied’ supplements

There’s a lot we don’t know about women’s health—women were largely excluded from clinical trials before 1993, and the female reproductive years remain among the most underfunded areas in medicine. 8. Many pregnancies from fertility technologies such as IVF are straightforward and healthy. They can also come with increased risks.

Those risks include things like having a premature baby, a baby with a low birth weight, delivering by way of cesarean, having placenta previa, having preeclampsia, and others.

These risks tend to be higher if you’re carrying more than one baby versus one, and ACOG notes that it “remains unclear” to what extent these associations might be related to the underlying causes of infertility.

“IVF pregnancies (especially for women over 40) are often managed more closely by OBs (for example, more monitoring), and sometimes with a maternal-fetal medicine specialist7 involved,” says Dang.

9. Fertility benefits are a step in the right direction—and they’re not the same as support systems for families.

Workplace coverage for fertility services can reduce financial barriers, expand access to care (particularly at younger ages, so that people can learn about options like egg freezing earlier), and acknowledge that building a family is an important part of employees’ lives. “For many patients, these benefits are truly life-changing,” says Shahine.

These benefits are not everything, though. “What patients consistently tell me they need is comprehensive support: paid parental leave, flexible work schedules, affordable childcare, and workplace cultures that truly accommodate parenting,” she says.

If you’re a regular Two Truths reader, you know that any conversation about pregnancy or parenthood must also include a conversation around the supports families need to thrive. (Not to mention: The mere decision to have a child isn’t always a decision in a post-Dobbs country.) We’re fighting for a lot of these supports over at Chamber of Mothers.

10. Egg freezing is an incredible option—and timing matters.

If you want biological children, there’s no “right” time to freeze your eggs, yet “there is a clear window where egg freezing works best—and it’s earlier than many people expect,” says Dang. Outcomes are best before age 35, and many clinicians start to counsel more carefully after age 38, when success rates decline more noticeably.” As egg quality decreases with age, it takes more eggs and more cycles to achieve the same chance of success, she says.

One larger study found that patients who froze at 35 or younger had about a 52% chance of a live birth, compared to 19% for those who froze at 40 or older.

Before moving forward with egg freezing, you’d typically undergo general fertility testing—an Anti-Müllerian Hormone (AMH) blood test to measure remaining eggs—and an ultrasound.

11. Fertility treatment outcomes have improved dramatically in recent decades, but they’re not a promise of a baby.

Thanks to advances in freezing technology, frozen eggs now perform comparably to fresh eggs—a big shift from even a decade ago.

Fertility care, overall, is also becoming more precise, personalized, patient-centered, and accessible. This is due to advances in lab technology, AI-assisted embryo assessment, genetic testing, freezing techniques, and healthcare coverage, helping improve outcomes while reducing some of the guesswork and physical burden of treatment.

Yet, Dang, who had a seven-year infertility journey, says: “I want women to know that this is an option; it’s insurance, but it doesn’t guarantee anything.”

12. Good fertility education informs and supports without fear-mongering.

There is no one right way to have a family. “There are options like egg freezing, embryo freezing, donor eggs, donor embryos, adoption, or choosing not to have children at all,” says Dang.

“The goal is always thoughtful, personalized planning,” Shahine says, “not creating pressure that everyone must act by a certain age.”

She adds that proactive fertility care involves understanding your reproductive timeline, knowing your personal risk factors, knowing your options, and having honest conversations earlier rather than later so you can make choices that align with your life goals.

13. Support matters.

A fertility journey can take a significant emotional toll; finding community and support can make a real difference.

Postpartum Support International’s free fertility challenges support groups and RESOLVE’s peer-led support network are free and offer spaces to connect with others who understand what you’re going through.

A therapist, ideally one trained in perinatal mental health, can also support your journey. ❍

1

IVF is a widely known form of ART and one of the most successful; it has been in use since 1978. While it began with success rates in the single digits, now, about half of cases for women who are under the age of 35 result in a pregnancy.

During IVF, a woman takes hormonal medications (by way of injections) to mature eggs in the body. Eggs are then retrieved from the ovaries through a surgical procedure, an egg is fertilized with sperm in a laboratory setting, then an embryo is transferred to the uterus. A week or two after a transfer, if the transfer is successful, a pregnancy can be detected.

2

During intrauterine insemination (IUI), sperm is collected, washed, and concentrated in a laboratory to isolate the healthiest sperm. Around the time of ovulation—sometimes supported by fertility medications—a clinician uses a thin catheter to place the prepared sperm directly into the uterus, bringing it closer to the egg to increase the chances of fertilization. Pregnancy can typically be detected about two weeks after the procedure.
