Why don’t Amtrak trains have changing tables on them? That was the question we found ourselves asking as we put together this issue and came across the Baby Changing On Board Act. The bill, sponsored by Congresswoman Lauren Underwood, just passed through the House and would make travel more family-friendly. It points to just one of the countless barriers families face when traveling.

Travel is not easy, especially during a season when schedules notoriously don’t line up and paychecks may more readily go to camp or childcare.

And travel is important. It’s good for your health. It’s one of the main things we remember from childhood. (Fun read: Cassie’s ‘The Power of Traveling with an Infant—Even if They’ll Never Remember It.’)

Two things can be true:
I accidentally fell into the habit of traveling with almost nothing to occupy my kids. It wasn’t an intentional decision—and sometimes they’ll pack coloring supplies or something of the sort on their own—but often, they lose interest quickly. If we’re flying, sure, they’ll watch the TV in front of them on a plane, or they’ll come up with something else to do. I’m certainly not against packing toys for kids…and sometimes, I do! But I’m also a big believer in letting kids be bored; it’s something we’ve lost track of as adults. We can pretty much always be entertained. I write a lot about mental health and know the benefits of daydreaming and of simply…being. Travel is a wonderful time for kids to embrace boredom and develop their sense of curiosity about the world around them. —C

» PS: A quick note about kids’ headphones

Something I’ve learned through my health reporting: Volume-limiting features are a must for small ears; kids are more susceptible to noise-induced hearing loss. Good headphones made for kids limit the volume to 85 decibels. Continued exposure to noise above this level can be damaging. (Normal conversation rings in at about 60 dB; an average airplane cabin hits about 80 to 85 dB.) Plenty of products today sell headphones that limit the volume to those levels. I’ve used these from Puro and these from Happy Plugs. —C

The more you know

  • About flying while pregnant… When I was about 30 weeks pregnant with my first daughter, my best friend and I took a trip to Sedona. We hiked the red rocks just before sunset, explored the Grand Canyon, drank tequila (her) and mocktails (me), and saw psychics. It’s a trip I’ll always remember fondly; my last before I became a mother. I wrote about it—as well as what to know about traveling while expecting—for Condé Nast Traveler. FWIW, The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) states that “in the absence of complications, occasional air travel is safe for pregnant women.” But the piece includes some other basics, like when you might need a doctor’s note to fly (it depends on the airline), potential risks (be sure to stay hydrated and move around), and tips for a comfier trip. —C

  • About bringing formula and breast milk through airport security... Although not every TSA agent is *quite up to speed*, you can do it—and, yes, in quantities greater than 3.4 ounces. Formula, breast milk, and puree pouches are considered “medically necessary liquids,” per TSA regulations. This policy holds for ice packs and other accessories required to keep things cool, too.

  • About breastfeeding in public and private spaces… It’s legal in all 50 states, D.C., Puerto Rico, and the Virgin Islands. Period.

  • About lactation rooms and pods… The Friendly Airports for Mothers Act required that all medium and large airports have a dedicated lactation area in every terminal by the end of 2021; the Friendly Airports for Mothers Improvement Act stated small airports had to do the same by the end of 2023. Look for Mamava pods—freestanding lactation stations in airports (and major transportation hubs like bus and train stations, and even Walmarts); you can find all of their spots here. Or, if you want portable privacy, pack a comfy cover. We like these by Bodily and Amma.

Baby on board: A new bill that benefits families on the go

The House just passed the Baby Changing on Board Act. This bill would require new Amtrak trains to be equipped with baby changing stations in all restrooms.

“Parents and caregivers shouldn’t have to worry about whether there’s a safe, clean place to care for their kids while traveling,” said Rep. Lauren Underwood. “I am so pleased that this legislation has passed the House and is now one step closer to becoming law. This common-sense, bipartisan legislation is a practical and necessary step to make sure families have access to the amenities they need on Amtrak.”

Resources and Support For Maternal Mental Health

  • Emergency assistance is available 24/7 at 911

  • The National Suicide Prevention Lifeline is available 24/7 at 988

  • The National Maternal Mental Health Hotline (1-833-TLC-MAMA or 1-833-852-6262) provides access to a trained counselor 24/7 and is available in Spanish and English

  • Postpartum Support International provides educational resources on PMADs, free support groups, webinars, advanced trainings for providers, and more

  • Postpartum Support International’s provider directory includes a list of thousands of trained professionals organized by state

  • The Motherhood Center offers counseling, support groups, and webinars

  • The Postpartum Stress Center offers educational resources, counseling, a referral list of trained providers, and advanced training for providers

SUPPORT YOUR MENTAL HEALTH WITH POSTPARTUM SUPPORT INTERNATIONAL (PSI). PSI is a global champion for perinatal mental health that connects individuals and families to the resources and support needed to give them the strongest and healthiest start possible. Visit postpartum.net for information on perinatal mental health disorders, access to 30+ free, online support groups, an online provider directory, the PSI HelpLine, local support coordinators, a perinatal mental health discussion tool, specialized support resources, and more. Call the PSI HelpLine toll-free at 1-800-944-4773 for basic information, support, and resources. Support via text message is also available at 800-944-4773 (English) and 971-203-2773 (Español).
