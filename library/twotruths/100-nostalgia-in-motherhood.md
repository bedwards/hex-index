### *Nostalgia*.

To us—and to you, too, we’re guessing—it’s a *feeling*.

Maybe it hits you out of nowhere; the moment you lie down to stare up at the clouds with your toddler and are instantly transported to a time when *you* were a small child in the great outdoors; a specific moment when boredom—and all its opportunities for wonder—still existed. You can still taste it on your tongue: Fun Dip; the way tiny freedoms once hit so differently.

Or maybe it’s something you chase: the fuzzy feeling of late nights sans bedtime routines; quiet morning coffees; or the baby years, once they’re in the rearview mirror.

Life is messy. Emotions are too. Sentiments—of what was and what is, of longing for yesterday and appreciating today, of grief and of happiness—live together. Sometimes always.

We can’t experience nostalgia when we’re in it; the present moment will always be clouded by chaos, logistics, and the distractions of daily life. But someday, it’s likely you’ll long for—*become nostalgic for*—the exact days you’re in right now.

Every year, we publish an issue on nostalgia—what it is, and how we keep it, make it, and return to it, and maybe, most importantly, how we let these planes of existence, of time, coexist in harmony.

We hope you enjoy this year’s rendition—and the upcoming Labor Day long weekend—no matter what bubbles to the surface for you.

# **Nostalgia, defined**

Nos·tal·gia

*Noun.* A sentimental longing or wistful affection for the past, typically for a period or place with happy personal associations. 

*—Oxford English Dictionary*

# **On the timing of nostalgia**

There’s a reason nostalgia hits when it does. In *[The Emotional Calendar](https://www.amazon.com/Emotional-Calendar-Understanding-Influences-Milestones/dp/0805091300)*, psychiatrist John Sharp, M.D., explains how certain seasons, months, and anniversaries can trigger emotional responses. Our experiences are etched into our brains.

The end of summer creeps near, and suddenly a childlike itch of excitement may rise. September can feel bittersweet, filled with the pull of home. For me, Labor Day weekend will always feel like Cape Cod: driving over one of two bridges that connect the seaside summer spot to mainland Massachusetts, then spending long weekends by the ocean as we did nearly every year growing up. For you, this weekend may stir up something entirely different—specific memories, unexpected feelings, maybe even sadness.

And so, as another Labor Day rolls around, know that these feelings aren’t random—they’re part of the map our lives have left on us. —*Cassie*

# **On seasons of sleep**

When I first started writing about maternal health, a mentor of mine — and a brilliant perinatal mental health expert — Birdie Gunyon Meyer, R.N., M.A., PMH-C, told me that we have seasons of giving and receiving in life, and that when you’re a new mother, you’re in the season of receiving. My youngest is now two. She sleeps through the night most nights. I’m out of the season of crushing sleep deprivation that I once feared would never end. I don’t miss it. I remember the hard of those nights. I also sometimes feel nostalgia creep in — for the quiet, for the still, for the littleness. I recall a mother in her 60s or 70s once telling me how she vividly remembered feeding her baby in the middle of long winter nights, as snow fell outside her window. To her, it was ordinary and exhausting and also sacred.

# **On creating ‘core memories’**

### **Can you manufacture nostalgia?**

Is it possible to sear a moment into your mind so that it stays with you—not just the look of it but the feel of it, the smell of it, the sounds of it, too? 

You’ve likely heard the term “core memory.” 

On Instagram, countless Reels offer instructions—and audio—for making one: *“Put this song over a video of something you never want to forget to make it a core memory.”* It might seem sort of silly, sort of ‘extra,’ sort of…something. And there’s debate over whether taking a photo [improves](https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/2021/08/05/1022041431/to-remember-the-moment-try-taking-fewer-photos) or [impairs](https://www.iflscience.com/taking-photographs-does-something-extremely-weird-to-your-memory-and-no-one-knows-why-48037) the memory it aims to capture—but I think there really is something real to this concept.

Here’s what I know to be true: 

On a sweaty summer day in the thick of pandemic lockdown, I took my daughter to our rooftop deck and filled an inflatable pool. I remember the distinct smell of the hose water and the way it took me right back to a moment in my childhood spent running through the sprinkler in the backyard. I wanted to drink it in, to be back there again—screeching with my brother, slipping on our wet grass, with my mom tooling around the garden on the fringes of the frame.

My daughter’s laughter pulled me to the present; we splashed in her pink, polka-dot pool (with a swimsuit to match). I remember the bright blue sky above us, the coolness of her wet skin as she climbed into my lap, how that listless afternoon—just another slow, quiet day in a series of slow, quiet days—seemed to stretch out forever. 

Today, I remember all of it—not just the memory, but the memory within the memory—because I snapped a few photos and printed them out. Because I’ve looked at them often enough now that that day, and all its details, are etched into my mind. Because I made a conscious effort to capture an average afternoon on my deck—not because I thought it was a really important or profound moment, but because I know that nostalgia isn’t reserved for the extraordinary; it’s often the moments in the margins that hit us hardest when we feel them in the future.

I know, without I doubt, that I would have lost that entire experience without those photos. (I can’t remember anything about the day before, or the day after, even though they were likely very similar—and I know it’s because I didn’t photograph them.) So yes, I almost always keep a camera (most often my phone) within reach, which isn’t ideal; but it allows me to transport myself to the past, and that power to time-travel is so precious and priceless to me. When I feel the days passing too quickly for my liking—when I notice little legs growing longer than I could have imagined—I take so much comfort in the knowledge that I’ve saved all these beautiful little glimmers along the way.

**» TRY IT:** Any moment can be a core memory if you want to make it one. Look around you, soak it in, and snap the picture. Choose to feel it all: not just what’s around you, but what’s happening in your head, too. Jot a note in your journal, or take some video and set it to some stirring music. Then look back in wonder, whether it’s in three years or 30, to see what kind of memory you manufactured; what kind of magic you remember. *—Kelsey*

# **On making new traditions**

We all have family traditions—some you love, some you could do without. And there comes a time when our roles—as children, daughters, and siblings—start to change. There comes a time when the role of “parent” assumes a more prominent position in our lives. There comes a time when *we* have to (get to) decide which traditions from our past we want to keep, which we want to alter, which we want to toss, and how to make new traditions and memories of our own.

For example, we recently started creating a family bucket list for each season: Ahead of winter, spring, summer, and fall, we jot down three things each of us wants to do that season. Things can be small (*watch the leaves change*) or bigger (*go birdwatching*). The list is never Pinterest-worthy, but that’s not the point; it’s something we look forward to.

**» TRY IT:** Start small and if you’re still feeling stuck, remember: Stripped down, nostalgia is simple. It’s the smell of watermelon. The taste of saltwater on your lips. Cracking rocks open with cousins to find mica and thinking it’s magic. Running around an empty beach. It’s a *feeling* — and that makes it the simplest and also the most complex thing in the world. *—Cassie* ❍
