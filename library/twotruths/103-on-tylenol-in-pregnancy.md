# **On Tylenol in Pregnancy**

In *Vogue*, Fiorella Valdesolo said it best this week: [The latest in maternal health fear mongering? Tylenol](https://www.vogue.com/article/tylenol-pregnancy?_sp=5778e25d-d919-4fdc-8256-41d7a806dce3.1758587033412).

As you likely saw, RFK Jr. joined President Trump at a widely publicized event to claim that taking Tylenol (acetaminophen) during pregnancy may be linked to autism—a claim that was met with swift and intense pushback from physicians, major health organizations, and decades of data.

In *Vogue*, our friend Rachel Blake, M.D., an OB/GYN, reminded that acetaminophen remains one of the safest and most studied pain-relief and fever-reducing options during pregnancy.

Meanwhile, Erin Erenberg, CEO of [Chamber of Mothers](http://chamberofmothers.com), told the mag that the administration’s messaging risks further eroding trust in medical institutions and leaving pregnant women especially vulnerable to misinformation.

# **…And Vaccines**

All of this news comes just a week after the current administration proposed changes to the childhood [vaccine recommendations](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/09/24/how-to-get-covid-mmrv-hepatitisb-vaccines.html).

Here’s what happened: The Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP)—a 17-member panel that RFK Jr. recently overhauled with his own picks (many of whom have expressed skepticism about [the safety and efficacy of vaccines](https://www.nbcnews.com/health/health-news/rfk-jr-advisory-panel-concerns-anti-vaccine-bias-rcna213496) to begin with)—voted against MMRV (the combination shot that protects against measles, mumps, rubella and chickenpox), for children under the age of 4. They cited a small risk for febrile seizures in that age group—something physicians are already aware of.

Many public health professionals have expressed concern about the motives behind the discussion and vote. Jake Scott, M.D., a clinical associate professor of infectious diseases and geographic medicine at the Stanford University School of Medicine, [said](https://www.nbcnews.com/health/kids-health/cdc-panel-measles-chickenpox-vaccine-recommendation-rcna231986): “This feels like using a known, disclosed, managed risk to undermine confidence in the entire schedule.”

It’s important to know that the recommendation isn’t final. The acting director of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) typically has the final say on whether to adopt changes, and it’s unclear when this decision will be made.

# **A New Reality of Public Health**

That brings us to something else entirely: a new phase of public health in this country. Due to a deep distrust of the federal government (ICYMI, [ACOG is no longer accepting federal funding](https://www.medpagetoday.com/obgyn/generalobgyn/116798)), Democratic-led states on both coasts have launched regional health coalitions.

-   On the West Coast, California, Oregon, and Washington, later joined by Hawaii, formed the **West Coast Health Alliance** to issue shared, science-based vaccine guidance, ensure transparency, uphold public trust, and protect access to credible immunization recommendations.
    

-   In the Northeast, states including New York, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Connecticut, Massachusetts, Maine, Rhode Island, and New York City have created the **Northeast Public Health Collaborative**, with the goals of making independent vaccine recommendations, improving coordination on public health emergency preparedness, data sharing, and ensuring their guidance isn’t constrained by what they view as weakened or politicized federal vaccine policies.
    

Essentially, these states will work in blocs to share resources and data, making their own public health recommendations.

# **Paid Leave Gets Another Chance**

The **FAMILY Act** is back in Congress, led by Sen. Kirsten Gillibrand and Rep. Rosa DeLauro. The 2025 version would provide up to 12 weeks of paid family and medical leave, with more flexible use, stronger protections for low-wage and part-time workers, and funding through a dedicated trust fund.

# **A New Program That’s Actually Helping Moms**

In Flint, Michigan, a program called [Rx Kids](https://rxkids.org/) has been quietly changing lives. It promised $1,500 to all expectant mothers during pregnancy, plus $500 per month for the first year of their infant’s life. Launched in 2024, this was the United States’ [first community-wide, unconditional cash transfer program for new families](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2025/sep/15/rx-kids-flint-michigan-pregnancy).

Now, researchers at Michigan State University have published two studies confirming what will surprise no one: The program was linked with lower levels of postpartum depression, a stabilization of families’ housing, and improvements in infant outcomes—saving millions in NICU spending along the way.
