When Catherine Birndorf, MD, co-founder and medical director of The Motherhood Center in New York City, talks about feeling good in motherhood, she uses the term maternal euthymia. It’s a medical term that essentially means a stable mood—not depressed, not manic. It’s a “North Star” of sorts for reproductive psychiatrists like Dr. Birndorf who specialize in women’s mental health throughout the reproductive years. Maternal euthymia is a state where both a mother and an unborn baby avoid the harmful effects of untreated psychiatric disorders like anxiety and depression.

Never heard that term? I hadn’t either, and I’ve been reporting on maternal health for nearly a decade. There’s still so much we don’t know.

Lately, the conversation around perinatal mental health hasn’t been focused on maternal euthymia; it has been focused on fear-mongering around medication. Last July, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA), under Health and Human Services (HHS) Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., convened a panel of largely antidepressant-skeptical voices to discuss the safety of Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) in pregnancy.

The event drew fierce criticism from the medical community (we covered it here). Nonetheless, as big-stage events have the capacity to do, it made people wonder: Should I be taking an SSRI while pregnant?

More than 1 in 7 American women takes prescription medication for depression, and SSRIs—which include medications like Zoloft, Lexapro, and Prozac—are the most prescribed class of antidepressants. Roughly 6 to 8 percent of pregnant women take one.

Most of us, though, are having the wrong conversation about them. Which brings me back to maternal euthymia. Dr. Birndorf and the two other reproductive psychiatrists I spoke to for this story—a piece about what to know about antidepressants and pregnancy—told me that even that framing of “Should I take an SSRI in pregnancy?” isn’t quite right. Treating perinatal mental health conditions, after all, is about reaching that state of well-being and, largely, about weighing risk—in this case, a well-studied, effective medication against the risk of untreated depression or anxiety.

So, for World Maternal Mental Health Day—a day meant to raise awareness about perinatal mental health disorders—Two Truths spoke to three leading physicians to answer common questions about SSRIs and pregnancy and explain what you actually need to know.

Our experts:

  • Catherine Birndorf, MD, co-founder and medical director of The Motherhood Center in New York City

  • Sarah Oreck, MD, a reproductive psychiatrist and co-founder and CEO of the virtual mental health platform Mavida Health

  • Anita H. Clayton, MD, chair of psychiatry and neurobehavioral sciences at the University of Virginia

First, let’s talk about the impact of untreated perinatal mental illness.

When we think about SSRIs in pregnancy, we tend to think a lot about the SSRIs. We worry that medication will have some kind of an impact on our growing baby—and that’s perfectly sound reasoning and worth wondering about. But here’s what we don’t always think about as much: What’s the impact of untreated depression on a fetus?

When we bring that question into the equation, it becomes clear that being exposed to depression during pregnancy is more problematic for the fetus than being exposed to an SSRI that’s working, says Anita H. Clayton, MD, chair of psychiatry and neurobehavioral sciences at the University of Virginia.

Let’s dig into some of the research. First, studies show that untreated depression during pregnancy is associated with increased risk of preterm birth and low birth weight. Emerging evidence also links elevated symptoms of prenatal depression or anxiety to behavioral and emotional problems in children.

One 2006 study published in JAMA found that among pregnant women with a history of depression, 68% of those who stopped antidepressants saw their depression come back after having it under control. (Twenty-six percent of people who stayed on their meds also saw symptoms return, exemplifying the fact that pregnancy is far from “protective” for mental health.) More recent reviews of research have found that, in those who discontinue meds, the risk of having depression return in pregnancy is most pronounced in women with severe or recurrent depression.

Untreated mental health conditions in pregnancy also have a way of “snowballing and getting worse in the postpartum,” says Sarah Oreck, MD, a reproductive psychiatrist and co-founder and CEO of the virtual mental health platform Mavida Health.

Untreated postpartum depression can, of course, negatively impact your health and also your family’s—impairing attachment and even increasing the risk that a partner gets postpartum depression. “At its core, this is a family systems issue—one that ripples outward in ways that are often underestimated, from challenges with returning to work to an estimated economic cost of $14 billion,” says Dr. Oreck.

So, what does the research say about the safety of SSRIs in pregnancy?

We’re all told a lot about what we cannot do or take in pregnancy (“policing pregnant bodies,” as Dr. Oreck says). See: HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. suggesting that Tylenol use in pregnancy is linked to autism. (It’s not.) Yet, “almost all of our medications are considered relatively safe1 in pregnancy,” says Dr. Birndorf. In fact, there are very few medications docs really suggest avoiding in pregnancy—one being a medication called Depakote2, which is used to treat bipolar disorder.

“Almost all of our medications are considered relatively safe in pregnancy.” —Catherine Birndorf, MD, co-founder and medical director of The Motherhood Center in New York City

“There is also really good research to support the relative safety of all the SSRIs in pregnancy,” adds Dr. Birndorf. SSRIs are the most studied class of medication used during pregnancy, according to Postpartum Support International.

Much of the data available is on Prozac, the oldest SSRI, which has been around since 1987, and much of it comes from the Scandinavian countries, which keep extensive databases on their populations. This has created a pool of millions of patients who have been on SSRIs throughout pregnancy.3

The major question people have with SSRIs is around birth malformations or defects, says Dr. Birndorf. For background, the rate of major malformations in the general population is 3 to 5 percent, regardless of medication. “SSRIs do not increase risk above that baseline,” says Dr. Birndorf.

Another thing you may have heard of is pulmonary hypertension of the newborn, a condition in which the blood vessels in a newborn’s lungs remain constricted after birth. SSRI use and untreated depression each appear to be associated with a small absolute increased risk of pulmonary hypertension of the newborn4. “The baseline risk is 1 to 2 in 1,000 versus 3 in 1,000 for depression or SSRI use,” says Dr. Birndorf.

Neonatal adaptation syndrome is another term you may have heard. About 20 to 30% of babies exposed to SSRIs in pregnancy are born with some temperature or blood pressure dysregulation; they may be slower to cry, and on occasion, there can be breathing issues, says Dr. Birndorf—symptoms that can be distressing for new parents. However, these issues are almost always “temporary and self-resolving,” she says, noting that neonatal adaptation syndrome very rarely requires a NICU stay, and most babies go home with their parents.

OB/GYNs and pediatricians are aware of the condition, and breastfeeding can help, Dr. Oreck adds.

Also worth remembering: When most of us hear that a medication carries a risk in pregnancy, we hear: This will harm my baby. But that’s not what risk means in medicine. Risk means probability, not certainty. Dr. Oreck often uses the example of driving to help her patients better understand risk: The same way getting in a car carries a risk of an accident without meaning you will get in one, taking an SSRI during pregnancy carries certain statistical risks without meaning those outcomes will happen to you or your baby, and for the vast majority of women, they won’t.

So, why do some OB/GYNs still advise women to stop SSRIs in pregnancy—and why do we still question SSRIs in pregnancy?

In part, it could be because OB/GYNs are not always trained in maternal mental health. While most agree that this education is important, and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) recommends that all women be screened for mental health conditions in pregnancy, maternal mental health is not a part of the national curriculum for OB/GYN residents. How much exposure a doctor gets, then, may depend largely on their training program, interest, experience, or history.

OBGYNs also have some of the highest malpractice premiums, so there is also an aversion to perceived risk-taking, adds Dr. Oreck. Some OB/GYNs may also be averse to prescribing mental health medications in pregnancy because some data were historically analyzed incorrectly, says Dr. Clayton.

In 2005, for example, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issued a warning that the SSRI Paxil may increase the risk of heart malformations. This spread fear about SSRI use more broadly in pregnancy. The problem, as future research revealed, was that early studies were flawed. They compared women on SSRIs to healthy women without depression, rather than to depressed women who weren’t taking medications. A large New England Journal of Medicine study of nearly 950,000 pregnancies that corrected for this found no significant increase in heart malformations in women on SSRIs. The link, then, could have been from depression itself or a cascade of other issues such as smoking, obesity, or stress—not the medication. The FDA warning remains on Paxil’s label to this day, which continues to drive unnecessary fear among women and providers. (See footnotes for more information about why Paxil may have fallen out of favor5.)

Ultimately, hesitation around SSRIs in pregnancy could circle back to psychiatry in general, which Dr. Birndorf says has “always been the black sheep of medicine.” She says that too often we tend to view psychiatric medication as “optional” and medication for “physical” health conditions as necessary. Yet, while the decision to take a medication is always a personal one, if you are symptomatic during pregnancy, it’s critical to treat your depression or anxiety.

Also, there’s just not a lot of research on women in general, and pregnant women specifically. (It wasn’t until 1993 that women were required to be included in federally funded clinical trials.)

And with all of this in mind, how do you navigate the healthcare system?

Unfortunately, we’re still at a time where we have to advocate for ourselves as women in medical appointments (stay tuned for our full issue on that next month!). Everyone—doctors included—can also have blind spots. It’s why Dr. Oreck recommends patients bring articles, information, or videos into their appointments and ask their providers what they think about a specific topic.

When it comes to SSRIs and pregnancy, your doctor also “may or may not” be able to make the decision with you, says Dr. Birndorf. She says the ideal scenario is being able to consult with a reproductive psychiatrist on any type of psychiatric medication (both Mavida Health and The Motherhood Center can help with this, and Postpartum Support International has a database of specialized providers).

If a provider tells you that you have to go off an SSRI? Dr. Birndorf suggests asking if they would mind checking in with a colleague in psychiatry, bringing something you have read (like this article!), and requesting that you work collaboratively. “You want a partnership with your provider. You want to be able to talk about things. You want to be able to say, ‘I’m worried about going off my meds,’” says Dr. Birndorf. “Everyone deserves a good conversation to make a good decision.” ❍

A note on the research: The studies cited throughout this piece are largely observational—meaning researchers tracked outcomes in women who chose to take or not take SSRIs, rather than randomly assigning them to treatment or placebo. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs), in which study participants are randomly assigned to a treatment or placebo, are considered the gold standard in medicine because randomization eliminates the biases that can skew observational data, like the fact that sicker patients are more likely to be medicated, which can make a medication appear riskier than it is.

RCTs don’t exist for SSRIs in pregnancy—and exist for very few medications used in pregnancy—because randomly assigning a pregnant woman to receive a medication or a placebo raises ethical concerns: You cannot ethically withhold a potentially necessary treatment, nor expose a fetus to an experimental intervention without consent.

4

This was one of the claims made at the July 2025 FDA panel. Panelists cited it as a reason to restrict SSRIs in pregnancy. ACOG and the Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine both pushed back, noting the absolute risk is small and that the evidence doesn't support restricting treatment on this basis.

This has important history: The FDA’s 2006 warning regarding SSRIs and Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension of the Newborn (PPHN) was softened in 2011/2012. Later, large cohort studies and meta-analyses found that while a small increased risk might exist, many studies could not consistently reproduce the high risks reported in initial, small, or case-control studies.
