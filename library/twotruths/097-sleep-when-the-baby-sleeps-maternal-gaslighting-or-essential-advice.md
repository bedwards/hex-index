### **In the early postpartum days—when sleep becomes currency, chaos, crisis, and too often seems just out of reach—one phrase gets thrown around again and again:** ***“Sleep when the baby sleeps.”***

You’ve likely heard it. It’s a familiar refrain for those in the throes of bone-deep exhaustion. For years, we’ve heard this advice ourselves, watched it get tossed around social media, seen it bashed in headlines or glorified as life-saving , and wondered what moms really thought about it. So recently, we decided to poll *Two Truths* readers to learn more about how the phrase (we’ll call it ‘SWTBS’ for short) really lands. The 4,800 responses show that, as it turns out, moms have strong—and often conflicting—feelings about it:

-   19% of you said you love sleeping when your kids sleep
    
-   41% of you wish you could do it more
    
-   Another 41% struggle with the real-life application of ‘SWTBS’
    

The advice sometimes helps us (a whisper of permission to rest in a world that rarely grants mothers any). It also bothers us, reminding us of the gaping holes in support systems, and—more generally—ignoring how many families function. One *Two Truths* reader noted, “perfect advice for the first-born”; another simply asked, “How?”

Some also said the advice was misunderstood; for others, it marked the beginning of something darker: “This is when the indoctrination starts,” one *Two Truths* reader told us. “‘Sleep when the baby sleeps’—because there is no other form of support. This is how we show mothers they are all on their own, disguised by offering the shallowest of advice. It’s culturally accepted gaslighting—‘supporting’ mothers while putting everything on their shoulders.”

So what’s the truth?

This week, we’re sharing your responses and our reflections on the phrase ‘SWTBS’, whether it helped or hurt, and what it reveals about how we treat mothers in the postpartum period. We also share ways to actually get rest in those early weeks—even when support is thin and the advice feels thinner.

## **‘SWTBS’: Love it**

Many *Two Truths* readers found the phrase “Sleep when the baby sleeps” to be a balm of sorts. Even though you may not be able to *sleep* when the baby sleeps, one reader told us, “It’s meant to encourage moms to rest.” Another added that it’s meant to “convey that your own rest matters more than the house being clean,” and more of you told us that you *needed* your sleep postpartum—and that being able to even score a little bit of it helped you in strides.

And it does, indeed, help. It likely comes as no surprise that some [87%](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7082815/) of new mothers experience clinically significant sleep disturbances, with [many](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5408463/) averaging fewer than six hours of sleep a night and rarely getting more than a few hours max at any given stretch.

Sleep in the postpartum period is a cornerstone of healing. It plays a crucial role in supporting immune function, hormone regulation, and postpartum physical recovery. Sleep deprivation impairs decision-making, increases irritability, heightens anxiety, and lowers overall life satisfaction.

For new parents, this can make otherwise manageable stressors feel insurmountable. [One study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6760397/) found that women with poor sleep quality in the first month postpartum were significantly more likely to report depressive symptoms at three months postpartum, even when researchers controlled for a predisposition to depression or anxiety.

“People are triggered \[by the advice\] because they miss the wisdom \[in it\],” one reader told us. “You must rest.”

## **‘SWTBS’: Leave it**

Many readers we polled on ‘SWTBS’ felt the complete opposite way, as though SWTBS is when a conditioning of sorts begins: It’s how we quietly signal to mothers that they’re on their own, pretending to care, while placing the entire burden to rest squarely on a mother’s shoulders.

Moms who opposed the advice also shared that ‘SWTBS’ felt “impossible” with a baby who “only wants you,” like it disregarded “all of the other things that do not pause when the baby sleeps,” and like the words can make you feel like a “failure” if you can’t indeed ‘SWTBS’ (maybe you have other children, maybe you have no help, maybe you’re lacking a village, maybe you… just can’t sleep).

Others felt that being able to ‘SWTBS’ “comes with privilege” and that it feels like “advice to give mothers without any action or help, without our village.”

Another mom told us that ‘SWTBS’ “lowkey reinforces that your life should be baby-centric.” She went on to ask: “How do you make room for your own pleasure?” It’s a question that stopped us in our tracks and made us think back to [our crucial conversation with Tanya Cotler, Ph.D, all about maternal desire](https://twotruths.substack.com/p/092-maternal-desire-the-missing-link-in-mental-health-for-moms). In that interview, she eloquently told us that symptoms like anxiety and depression often arise from “the tension between what you want and the squashing of that.” To feel well, a mother must be allowed to want. To want deeply. Boldly. And without apology.

If you are a consistent reader of this newsletter, you know that mothers in this country are woefully under-supported. The U.S. largely lacks affordable, accessible childcare; does not have a federally protected paid leave policy; and is in the midst of a maternal health crisis. This is a country where highly treatable maternal mental health conditions are the leading cause of postpartum death.

Mothers who are bothered by ‘SWTBS’ have every right to be. Today, mothers often find themselves mothering in isolation: You may not have a community you can trust. You may not have family nearby. You may not be able to afford some of the services that promise you sleep (a night nurse, a postpartum doula). Someone telling you to SWTBS can feel like *just another thing you can’t do right*. And it can, in ways, reinforce the dangerous idea that “*if only I could do something different/better*, then I could *feel* different/better; I could *be* different/better.”

And that’s a lie. One with sky-high costs.

## **‘SWTBS’: Two things can be true**

This brings us to our last batch of responses: readers with mixed feelings about ‘SWTBS’.

Some of you felt that the advice shouldn’t be about *sleep* in particular, but rather about time for yourself. One reader told us that she wished the advice read more like, “Do whatever you need to take care of yourself while the baby sleeps.”

Others told us that they loved the advice but are still “trying to figure it out.”

One mom responded by saying, “*IF ONLY*!” She added: “The truth: That’s the only place I want to be right now.”

More moms told us that instead of ‘SWTBS’, they wished that we—as a society and culture—“normalized making \[motherhood\] a team effort.”

Because that’s the biggest lie in all of this: that you were meant to mother alone. You indeed were not. In fact, it wasn’t until recently that mothers began mothering in intensive, siloed ways, largely by themselves, and the pandemic didn’t help things.

Throughout history, parenting has been a communal act. Now, support and help too often go hand-in-hand with privilege when help in motherhood was never supposed to be a privilege. It was always a given. *It should be a given*.

A lack of help and support in motherhood is all too common. And if you lack help and support, that’s not your fault; it’s not a problem for you to fix on your own, *and*, often, the people most negatively impacted by issues are the ones who wind up solving them.

All of this can be, and often is, true.

**In the end, there are many annoying and polarizing opinions about sleep and ‘SWTBS’—as is the case in many aspects of motherhood, from feeding (formula or breastmilk?) to work (working mom or stay-at-home mom?)—that can wind up dividing moms into camps when the truth lives somewhere in the middle. A platitude of sorts, ‘SWTBS’ has both an underbelly of harmful messaging and a well-intended message of well-being. The truth is both: We desperately need rest in the postpartum period, and we need to comprehensively and systemically fix the broken support structures for parents in this country to ensure that rest. We need to make it clear that rest and support aren’t optional, but rather requirements for a fully functional mother, family, and society. All of this can be true. ♡**

# **How to get ‘anchor sleep,’ the smart strategy one M.D. recommends for essential postpartum rest**

#### Here’s one of many truths of postpartum: A few hours of uninterrupted sleep can feel impossible, are extraordinarily helpful, are not enough, *and are* a goal worth striving for.

I’ve written a lot about the link between postpartum mental health and sleep (including [this](https://www.saatva.com/blog/maternal-mental-health-sleep/) piece for Saatva), but one of the most helpful takeaways came from our friend and psychiatrist Pooja Lakshmin, M.D., author of *Real Self-Care*. (Don’t miss our conversation with her: *[Boundaries and bubble baths: Real talk on Real Self-Care](https://twotruths.substack.com/p/007-boundaries-and-bubble-baths-real)*.) Years ago, she introduced me to the concept of aiming for anchor sleep—a longer stretch of rest, ideally four hours (but less is okay), in every 24-hour period.

In early motherhood, sleep is often broken into short, unpredictable chunks—not exactly ideal for your body or brain. Anchor sleep is a way to carve out one consistent block of rest that can help stabilize your mood, support your physical recovery, and even reduce the risk of postpartum depression. Overnight hours are ideal, but anchor sleep doesn’t have to be at night—what matters is that you get it. If you’re not sleeping while the baby sleeps, consider asking a partner, friend, or trusted helper to step in so you can sleep. And if sleep isn’t coming? Aim for rest. It has benefits.
