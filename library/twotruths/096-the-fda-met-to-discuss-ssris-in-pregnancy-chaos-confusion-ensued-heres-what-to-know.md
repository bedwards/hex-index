This week, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) hosted [a meeting](https://www.fda.gov/patients/fda-expert-panels/fda-expert-panel-selective-serotonin-reuptake-inhibitors-ssris-and-pregnancy-07212025) with 10 physicians, psychologists, and other panelists to discuss the use and safety of antidepressant medications called selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)—a class of drugs that includes medications like Zoloft, Prozac, and Lexapro—during pregnancy. Three panelists were based outside of the U.S.; one runs a clinic focused on helping people taper off psychiatric medications.

The panel has since faced widespread criticism for being unbalanced and for promoting misinformation—including suggestions that SSRI use in pregnancy is linked to autism and birth defects, suggesting that many women feel “coerced” to go on SSRIs for depression, and questioning whether antidepressants work at all (as well as whether or not depression needs to be treated by medicine at all).

-   The professional organization, **The Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine**, [said](https://www.smfm.org/news/smfm-statement-on-ssris-and-pregnancy) its members were “alarmed by the unsubstantiated and inaccurate claims made by FDA panelists concerning maternal depression and the use of SSRI antidepressants during pregnancy.”
    
-   **The International Society of Reproductive Psychiatry** expressed “grave concern” over the content and tone of the meeting and said the discussion was “marked by misinformation, anti-scientific rhetoric, and stigmatizing language.” The group said the meeting minimized or dismissed the fact that “most SSRIs carry a low risk of major birth defects and can be a critical part of a treatment plan for depression and anxiety during pregnancy.”
    
-   Steven J. Fleischman, M.D., president of the **American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists** (ACOG), said in a [statement](https://www.acog.org/news/news-releases/2025/07/statement-on-benefit-of-access-to-ssris-during-pregnancy) that the panel was “alarmingly unbalanced.”
    

Perinatal mental health professionals—those who specialize in mental health during the reproductive years—also took to social media and [various](https://www.motherjones.com/politics/2025/07/the-fda-held-a-misinformation-fest-about-antidepressants-in-pregnancy/) [news outlets](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/07/21/health/antidepressants-ssri-pregnancy-fda.html) to share their concerns and clear up confusion.

Only one panelist, Kay Roussos-Ross, M.D., an associate professor in the departments of obstetrics and gynecology and psychiatry at the University of Florida, emphasized the critical importance of antidepressants in pregnancy:

“Not every single woman will need an antidepressant, but for those that do, this is life-changing, and this is lifesaving,” she said.

Ultimately, the panel did not recommend any new warnings or label changes to the drugs, though the idea was discussed. (In a story about the meeting, *The New York Times* ran with the headline, “[F.D.A. Panel Debates ‘Black Box’ Warning for Antidepressants in Pregnancy](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/07/21/health/antidepressants-ssri-pregnancy-fda.html?unlocked_article_code=1.Y08.7N7V.KATPFlNWFJR3&smid=url-share).”) But the meeting did reignite fears and confusion around antidepressant use in pregnancy, and maternal mental health in general, without spending much time at all on a far more pressing issue, which is:

**Mental health conditions are the leading cause of pregnancy-related deaths in the U.S.** They are also highly treatable through a combination of therapy, medication, and support.

Dr. Fleischman of ACOG said the panel “did not adequately acknowledge the harms of untreated perinatal mood disorders in pregnancy.” And **untreated mental health conditions in pregnancy can be life-threatening**, increasing the risk of preeclampsia, substance use, and even suicide.

There are many different ways to treat maternal mental health conditions. And when it comes to SSRIs, we have good, longstanding evidence that they are generally safe in pregnancy; **we also need more of it**. Historically, pregnant women have been excluded from clinical trials, leaving [vast gaps in women’s health research](https://twotruths.substack.com/p/044-trending-womens-health-research), often prompting more questions than answers.

Dr. Fleischman of ACOG said as much, noting, “ACOG agrees with the panel’s assertion that randomized trials to assess the safety of SSRIs have not been conducted and should be prioritized.”

He also said that **SSRIs were slated to be part of new research working groups formed by the FDA and the National Institutes of Health (NIH), but that those efforts have now stalled due to the current administration’s recent funding cuts to the NIH.**

…and many others who advocate every day for evidence-based, mother-centered care. As Lucy Hutner, M.D., told me recently for a piece I wrote for *[Goop](https://goop.com/wellness/parenthood/maternal-mental-health-study/)*, the field of maternal health was built from the ground up by a handful of experts committed to listening to what women were really struggling with.

This panel notably lacked those experts—the ones on the front lines leading this field.

“As a reproductive psychiatry community, we’ve worked tirelessly to reduce stigma and ensure patients receive informed, compassionate care,” [Nichelle Haynes, M.D.](https://www.rpcaustin.com/team/nichelle-haynes), a board member of the International Society of Reproductive Psychiatry, told *Two Truths*. “Watching that progress undermined on such a public stage is not only disheartening—it’s dangerous.”

If you’re currently taking an SSRI and pregnant, or thinking about becoming pregnant, talk openly with your doctor. Share your experience with the medications. The choice to take an SSRI during pregnancy is a deeply personal one. SSRIs are not universally indicated for all pregnant women, *and* “they remain one of the most evidence-supported pharmacologic tools available for moderate to severe perinatal depression and anxiety,” says Dr. Haynes.

If you are struggling, remember that maternal mental health conditions are treatable. Support and help are available.
