If you’ve ever stood in the middle of your kitchen wondering if your spatula is slowly poisoning your family—or opened Instagram only to be told your couch, your water, and your kid’s birthday party favor are all toxic—this issue is for you.

At some point in the not too distant past, making healthy and safe choices for our families started feeling like a trap: conflicting studies, fear-mongering headlines, social misinformation overload, and whiplash-inducing environmental policy changes. If you’re anything like me, you’re feeling overwhelmed and unsure as to what actually matters.

What do we need to pay attention to—and what could we let go of?

I’ve been following Lindsay Dahl, author of the new book Cleaning House: The Fight to Rid Our Homes of Toxic Chemicals, since her days at Counter (formerly Beautycounter). There, she regularly spoke about systems and policy and legislation and nuance—things that are too often missing in these conversations. She’s one of the first people who really made me realize that so much of what we’re expected to fix inside our homes was never supposed to be our sole responsibility in the first place.

Dahl has helped pass more than 30 consumer safety laws and built a career at the intersection of environmental health, corporate accountability, and public policy. She’s now the Chief Impact Officer at traceable supplement company Ritual (a partner of Two Truths), writes a Substack on science, politics, and the culture of wellness, and is a board member of Chamber of Mothers (of which Cassie and I are both co-founders).

Her new book brings all of that experience together. It’s part scientific primer, part memoir, part behind-the-scenes look at how change actually happens—and explores the truth about toxins, health, and consumer culture without spiraling into fear-mongering or moral judgment.

In this interview, we get into…

  • Four changes you can make today to tackle the majority of everyday toxin exposure

  • Why the most impactful thing you can do has nothing to do with what you buy

  • The concept of the “risk pie” (which should help lower your stress level)

  • Dahl’s refreshing take on “good enough” parenting

  • Why this burden shouldn’t be on our shoulders—and what to do about it

This Q&A has been edited for clarity and length.

Kelsey: Lindsay! I am so excited to talk to you today. Let’s get right into it. Your book is titled Cleaning House: The Fight to Rid Our Homes of Toxic Chemicals. There’s a lot going on there! The concepts you talk about in the book—reducing toxin exposure and cleaner living—can feel overwhelming to tackle. They’ve also been deeply politicized recently. But this book, because of who you are and your very unique experience, is like the antidote to both of those issues. So when it comes to toxic chemicals, can you set the scene for us: Where do we begin, and why do we begin?

Lindsay: I always joke that I won’t tell you to clean your house. The title is a metaphor for cleaning house of a lot of different things: cleaning house of dogmatic thinking—because people can get really obsessive about this issue—and cleaning house when it comes to our government leadership. It intentionally has many meanings.

At the end of the day, there are two headlines people should know. Number one: This shouldn’t be on our to-do list. We shouldn’t have to read a book or reference a list or download an app to find products that don’t have toxic chemicals. It shouldn’t be on our shoulders and there are a lot of simple things we can do to help move the market and move our government to act.

And the second is that this is grounded in really strong, old, peer-reviewed science. If you think about Rachel Carson back in the 1960s with pesticides [a marine biologist, author, and conservationist whose work is credited with launching the modern environmental movement], or Lois Gibbs [who led moms fighting against chemical waste leaking from the Love Canal landfill built under an elementary school] or Erin Brockovich, the real person who the movie was based on (who also endorsed this book!)...this is an issue that’s been around for a long time. And social media has made it feel toxic, but it’s actually a very real issue. And with a few simple steps, you can reduce the amount of exposure in your home—especially as you are entering the parenthood journey, which is when a lot of people are really thinking about this. I try to make it not overwhelming, because it shouldn’t be.

“We shouldn’t have to read a book or reference a list or download an app to find products that don’t have toxic chemicals.” —Lindsay Dahl

Kelsey: Yes, exactly. Because it does feel overwhelming. If I’m picking up this book and thinking, “I want to make my home healthier,” but every time I turn on the news or open Instagram, there’s a new study—the black spatula warning, the takeout containers lined with PFAS, and on and on—it’s hard to follow. It can make you want to throw your hands up in the air and give up. So where do we start? What are the most impactful choices we can make?

Lindsay: I’ll give you a top priority in four different categories. If you do these four things, you’ll be in a pretty good spot.

First, the kitchen. Cookware is the number one place to look. Not all chemicals are toxic, but we want to focus on the most toxic ones—especially PFAS, or “forever chemicals,” which stay in our bodies and the environment. So prioritize cookware and opt for things like plain stainless steel or cast iron. For water quality, Dahl recommends DIY assessment through a service like TapScore (which can test for PFAS and many other contaminants, then makes recommendations for remediation options if necessary).

Next is furniture—couches and mattresses. Look for flame retardant-free and PFAS-free. You can flip over a couch and check the tag now, which wasn’t the case a few years ago. If you see something like “stain-resistant”—you know, with a picture of a wine glass—that’s a red flag that PFAS may have been used.

Then, for beauty and personal care, look for MADE SAFE or EWG Verified—those are certifications that let you know those products are good to go. Or, you can shop at a retailer that does the homework for you, like Credo.

Kelsey: I have to tell you—when we moved a couple years ago and I had to buy new furniture, I went down this exact rabbit hole. I was trying to avoid PFAS, and it was so hard to find anything. But then a few years later, I started seeing more options hit the market. Has consumer demand driven that change?

Lindsay: Absolutely. Every time you ask a question, search for something like “PFAS-free,” or check a company’s FAQ, they’re taking in that data. I’m the one receiving those questions at Ritual, so I know how much of a conversation it starts internally.

Consumer demand—and where we allocate our dollars—has taken market share away from companies that aren’t prioritizing safety. Consumer power has completely transformed the market. When I started 20 years ago, you couldn’t find children’s toys; maybe, if you were lucky, there was one small, wood-focused natural toy store and they were very rare; now you can go to Walmart and find wood toys that are safe.

And the price point has really come down. The only way to get a flame-retardant-free couch 15 years ago was to have one custom-made, and it was a minimum of $10,000. Now you can go to Costco and you can purchase a couch there because of their retailer policy, which is where we buy our couches, because we have young kids and they’re bouncing all over them.

Kelsey: That brings up something I think about a lot—the tension between what we’re doing in our homes and what’s happening at a policy level. Shopping is getting easier, but there’s still a massive burden on individuals. We can make better choices, but the most impactful decisions are not being made in our homes but at a policy level by the people we vote into office.

Lindsay: The number one most impactful thing you can do is call your members of Congress and ask them to avoid rollbacks of existing laws on toxic chemicals, and pass new proactive laws to remove toxic chemicals from the consumer marketplace. It takes less than two minutes and costs no money.

You don’t need to know anything technical and you don’t have to call about a specific bill. You can call and say, “I don’t want toxic chemicals in my products, my food, my air, or my water, and I’m watching how you vote.” You can be really generic. You’ll likely get a generic response back, but it still matters. You’re communicating with the staff who advise the senator or representative. You can call, leave a voicemail, or email. It all counts. And you can do it repeatedly.

There are many families who don’t have the time, resources, or education to navigate all of this. Being able to shop your way through it is a point of privilege, and it should come second to pushing elected officials.

If you look at history: When we banned lead in gasoline, you saw an absolute plummet in lead levels in children. When we banned phthalates in toys, the levels dropped. That’s where you can really make the largest shift. Remember: These chemicals do not stay contained in our homes; it becomes toxic chemical pollution in the air, water, and the food we eat.

Kelsey: Something I found very helpful in the book—and a huge relief, honestly—was when you talk about doing “good enough” and the idea of the risk pie.

Lindsay: The risk pie comes from a cancer researcher named Richard Clapp. He described risk as a pie with different slices—our genetics, our environment, our products, the food we eat, our occupations. If you can remove one slice, or one risk factor—like upgrading the filter that you’re using for your tap water—great. There are things we can do that are actionable, and there are things we can’t control. That should take the pressure off.

Don’t take away joy from your kids over this. If they bring home a toy you don’t love from a birthday party, let them play with it. I say that as someone who helped ban phthalates from toys—I still let my kids play with it.

People assume they’re going to come into my home and find no plastic—no, I’m just like everyone else, and I know that I have more impact calling my state and federal elected officials, and by asking companies really hard questions, than I have in trying to curate some perfect home environment.

“Don’t take away joy from your kids over this.” —Lindsay Dahl

Kelsey: That is such a relief to hear and makes me think of a few months ago when I was trying to find a good baby doll for my kid, and ended up in a deep Google spiral about plastics. Where should we start in those moments?

Lindsay: I’m going to give you maybe a satisfying or unsatisfying answer: Don’t worry about it. Unless your child is teething and constantly putting something in their mouth, a plastic toy is not where your biggest risk is. Focus on the big exposures: the mattress they sleep on, the water they drink, your cookware, and products that stay on their bodies all day. That covers about 80% of the risk. If you want one tool, look for MADE SAFE certification. But otherwise, don’t stress over every micro decision.

Kelsey: Oh that is an extremely satisfying answer—and exactly what I, and probably many others, needed to hear. Dial it down, focus on the big things, and let go. Have fun. Buy the toy. Call your representatives.

Lindsay: Exactly.

Kelsey: What else do you want readers to know about Cleaning House?

Lindsay: I’m more hopeful now than I’ve ever been. I’ve gone toe-to-toe with the most powerful industry, the chemical industry, plus the oil industry, for decades, and we have won time and time again solely because people have taken the time to raise their voices. And that translates to any issue that you care about right now. I think what people are really craving is an understanding of how our government works and how our voices can fit into it. So at the end of the day, we need to protect our democracy, because it’s how we make change happen.

Kelsey: That reminds me of a line I highlighted in your book: “Pissed-off moms have led some of the biggest health and environmental wins.” Which feels like the perfect point to wrap up our chat. ❍
