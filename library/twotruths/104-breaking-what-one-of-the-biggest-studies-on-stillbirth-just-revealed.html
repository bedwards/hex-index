What I know from interviewing women who have lost babies is that the experience can bring you to your knees. It can isolate you, take your words, make you feel scared, and rob you of hope. Many mothers have told me that what they felt most after loss was alone.

And yet, one of the most meaningful ways to support someone through loss is to talk about it. I’ll never forget the mother who told me that after losing her son in infancy, people didn’t say his name; it was all she wanted to hear.

Too many aspects of maternal health are like this: unspoken, stigmatized, and therefore…understudied.

Which is why today’s breaking news matters: Published in the leading medical journal JAMA, researchers at the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health and Mass General Brigham just released one of the largest studies of stillbirth to date (looking at about 2.8 million births). The study comes out during a moment when women’s health research is under siege in this country.

Stillbirth is considered the death of a fetus after 20 weeks of gestation; stillbirth impacts some 21,000 families each year in this country, and nearly half of those that happen after 37 weeks are thought to be preventable.

“There is very little research on the stillbirth burden and risk factors in the U.S.—shockingly little, relative to how tragic the outcome is,” Haley K. Sullivan, B.S., one of the study’s co-authors, told Two Truths.

Recently, we spoke with Sullivan and her study co-author, Mark Clapp, M.D., M.P.H., a maternal-fetal medicine specialist at Mass General Brigham, about:

  • What you need to know about stillbirth

  • What this research means for women’s health in general

  • What stillbirth prevention strategies look like

  • What needs to change

First, what the study found

The new research finds that 1 in 147 U.S. births end in stillbirth, a higher rate than the current estimate of 1 in 175 from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). (For comparison, gestational diabetes impacts anywhere from 1 in 20 to approximately 1 in 11 pregnancies; maternal death happens once for every 5,350 births.)

The vast majority of pregnant women will deliver a healthy, live-born infant and not experience a stillbirth, says Mark Clapp, M.D., M.P.H., one of the study’s co-authors. Sullivan adds that even most pregnancies that have risk factors for stillbirth result in a live birth.

Yet, Dr. Clapp says stillbirth “may occur more often than many people think.” Sullivan reminds that there are “considerably more stillbirths in the U.S than child deaths due to prematurity, SIDS, accidents, and other, more commonly studied and addressed risks, combined.”

As is true across maternal health, disparities exist. In low-income areas, this study found that the stillbirth rate was 1 in 112. In areas with higher proportions of Black families, the rate was 1 in 95. Sullivan suspects the stillbirth rate is even higher in births on Medicaid, too (this study looked at births on commercial insurance plans, not on Medicaid).

The study found that some 70% of stillbirths occur in pregnancies with at least one known risk factor—things like hypertension, diabetes, or declining placental function. (If you have risk factors, it’s critical to review them with your healthcare provider.)

Nearly 28% of stillbirths, though, happened with no identifiable risk factor—and that was particularly true for pregnancies that went beyond 40 weeks. After 40 weeks, 40% of stillbirths had no known cause—a profound pain for families and a major barrier to prevention.

Some of these findings reinforce long-recognized patterns: For instance, previous research has found that Black women are more than twice as likely to experience stillbirth as white women, and data have historically ballparked that anywhere from 25 percent to 50 percent of stillbirths have no clearly identifiable cause.

This new research provides a large amount of data and updated figures, outlining just how many deaths are likely preventable and noting that a substantial portion of stillbirths occur in “low-risk” pregnancies. It also shows persistent and inexcusable disparities in stillbirth, particularly around race.

The study underscores the urgent need for new prevention strategies. It underscores the urgent need to fund women’s health research.

What does stillbirth prevention look like?

Dr. Clapp points out that professional medical societies, such as the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) and the Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine, provide healthcare professionals with guidance on when to use tests—such as biophysical profiles (ultrasounds that look for things like movement and muscle tone) and fetal nonstress tests (to measure fetal heart rate)—to screen for declining placental function.

However, the tests are imperfect and there may be barriers—cost, transportation, time—to accessing them.

ACOG also recommends that medical testing occur after stillbirths, including genetic and placental testing. “While not every family may want this testing to occur, many families and stillbirth advocates have stated that they wanted more information about the causes of their stillbirth and were not able to receive additional testing due to the costs,” says Sullivan. “Federal or state governments could cover the cost of pathology testing after stillbirth.”

And while new research is certainly needed, Sullivan notes that the United States could also learn from other countries’ research on stillbirth prevention. For example, stillbirth rates in the United Kingdom and Australia have decreased after the implementation of “stillbirth prevention bundles,” she says. These include a number of interventions aimed at reducing stillbirth rates, including recommendations to quit smoking, screening for growth restriction, campaigns for awareness around decreased fetal movement, advocacy for side sleeping after 28 weeks of pregnancy, and shared decision-making about the timing of delivery for high-risk pregnancies.

What can you do?

There has been a huge amount of advocacy and research dollars spent on reducing rates of other conditions that impact infants and families; stillbirth prevention deserves similar attention and resources.

There are ways to advocate for stillbirth prevention policies on a larger scale and get the support you or a loved one needs if you are personally impacted by stillbirth.

♥️ Support the SHINE for Autumn Act
The Stillbirth Health Improvement and Education (SHINE) for Autumn Act—named for Autumn Joy, a baby stillborn in 2011—is federal legislation focused on stillbirth prevention.

If passed, it would focus on preventing stillbirths through enhanced data collection, research, education, and awareness, and would mark the beginning of a longer-term solution to reducing stillbirth rates.

The bill, which has bipartisan support, has been reintroduced in Congress and is awaiting movement. You can contact your representatives and urge them to support.

♥️ Support yourself

There are many resources—from support groups to hotlines and educational sites—for those who have experienced (or love someone who has experienced) stillbirth. Here are just a few:

  • Our friends and partners at Postpartum Support International have a hotline, 1-800-944-4773, and free virtual support groups for stillbirth/infant loss; their support page also lists partner support organizations.

  • Groups like Star Legacy Foundation and Rachel’s Gift offer free support for pregnancy and infant loss, including stillbirth.
