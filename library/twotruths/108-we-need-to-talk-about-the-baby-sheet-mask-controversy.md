When I shared a comment on [a news post](https://www.instagram.com/p/DRSKJK9D61j/) covering the baby sheet mask drama yesterday, I wasn’t planning to write about this topic further—so many others have already spoken up about why this particular beauty product is problematic—but then someone replied to my comment and said, **“It’s really not that deep.”**

It made me so...sad. Disheartened. “It’s not that deep” is a sentiment I have seen a lot of moms sharing in the discourse around this “sheet masks for toddlers” brand launch, but ***it is “that deep.”*** Let me explain.

(But first, if you missed it: The outpouring of criticism began with the launch of Rini back on November 6; commentary picked up again when the brand’s celebrity co-founder dismissed concerns in an [interview with](https://www.today.com/parents/toddlers/shay-mitchell-responds-backlash-childrens-skincare-line-rcna244795) *[Today](https://www.today.com/parents/toddlers/shay-mitchell-responds-backlash-childrens-skincare-line-rcna244795)* on Wednesday.)

It’s hugely important that as parents raising young children, we firmly understand the impact that capitalism and patriarchal beauty standards have influenced how we feel about **ourselves**, how we view **our own place** within the beauty industrial complex, and **how we define “self-care.”** Because we can either perpetuate the problem, or we can be a part of changing it.

Now I’m going to be real with you before we dive in: I am what one might call a “skincare girlie.” I love a sheet mask every now and then. I want my skin to look good. I have actively participated in upholding traditional beauty standards during my years as a beauty writer and editor. I frequently offer skincare recommendations that I stand behind, for both adults and children, in this newsletter.

I have learned, and I can now acknowledge and admit—only relatively recently, to be honest—that the reason I want my skin to look good (or to “be healthy,” as the beauty industry so often frames it) isn’t because it’s empowering or because I truly enjoy rubbing my face with lotions and oils twice per day. **The hard truth—the one I think more people need to understand—is that “good” skin is equated with attractiveness, youth, physical health, wealth, and even inherent, moral “goodness.”** That is why we chase it. That is why we spend time and money attaining it. That is why we see it as a form of “self-care” and that’s why it is marketed to us as such.

In a patriarchal society, all of this equates to more power. Period. Our appearance matters to how others perceive us—and, generally, the more attractive we are, the better we are treated. (This is not my personal opinion, but it’s supported by a large body of research revealing that thin, conventionally attractive, white women [earn higher salaries](https://people.com/attractive-people-earn-up-to-usd5k-more-a-year-8781514) and find more success in their careers…etc., etc., etc.) **To simplify things: If we lived in a society where pimples were put on a pedestal, sheet masks wouldn’t exist.**

So now that that’s out of the way, let’s talk about the absurdity of marketing sheet masks to children as young as 3 (via their moms) under the guise of “bonding” and “self-care.” **Sheet masks for children are a single-use product that require tons of extraneous packaging that directly contributes to the destruction of the earth that those 3-year-olds will eventually inherit.** There is nothing “healthy” about overconsumption. It is harmful on a global scale.

Now, is it “self-care” or “bonding” to participate in what can be positioned as a “skin health” “ritual” with your child? *No—I’m sorry, but no.* **There are so many deeply impactful ways to care for ourselves, our bodies, our minds, our souls. There are so many ways to bond with our children and involve them in the things that matter to us. Sheet masks are not one of them.**

Sheet masks are a for-profit product in a patriarchal, capitalistic beauty industry that tells us we’re flawed, and then sells us the “fix” for that flaw in the form of an environmentally-damaging, single-use, completely unnecessary product. What’s more: Some brands are now marketing unnecessary products to younger and younger customers…which will inevitably cause harm to their skin…which will keep them stuck in a vicious cycle of buying more products to fix the problems created by…products. (I am so tired just thinking about this.)

The beauty industry has long sold the ideal of “baby skin” to adults (think: *“get a youthful!”* and *“ageless!”* all that), and now it is selling “baby skin” to literal babies. **A big problem here is that gratuitous skincare for children isn’t only unnecessary; it’s actively harmful.** The skin is an essential part of our immune system and generally, the skin’s microbiome thrives best when it’s left alone. Unless a parent is using a specialty product to treat a true medical condition (like, say, eczema), using unnecessary skincare products on kids can cause actual harm: dryness, dermatitis, inflammatory issues, etc. (There are plenty of baby skincare brands that make great ranges of gentle, everyday, and necessary products for kids—like body wash, shampoo, lotion, balms, SPF, etc. That’s a different category than what we’re discussing here.)

The celeb co-founder behind the sheet masks said she created them as a way for kids to participate in their mom’s skincare routine; it’s a “shared moment,” she stated. As a mother of two young children who constantly interrogate my every action, I think \*all the time\* about how I should explain my own skincare and beauty routines. *Why do I put all that “stuff” on my face? Why am I putting on makeup today?* I don’t have all the right answers, and I struggle often, but **I will never tell my daughter that adhering to beauty norms rooted in patriarchal beauty standards is a form of self-care. It’s not.** Self-care, for me, is getting rest…and snuggling my kids…and reading a book…and putting away my phone…and spending time in nature. It’s setting a boundary when I need to. [It’s embracing my authentic desires.](https://twotruths.substack.com/p/092-maternal-desire-the-missing-link-in-mental-health-for-moms) **Performing beauty is not self-care.**

And listen…acknowledging all of this, I’m still going to apply a few beloved products to my face before bed tonight that will (hopefully) help reduce fine lines and redness. *It’s a moment to myself! It’s a ritual!* But should I call this self-care? No. Again—the pursuit of aesthetic beauty is a byproduct of patriarchy. **If mirrors didn’t exist and society valued wrinkles, these products wouldn’t exist and I would find another way to fill those five minutes at the end of my day.**

Here’s the crux of it all: **Sitting with this topic can be deeply uncomfortable because we are all victims of a capitalist society that has profited off our insecurities while selling us on the fallacy that seeking aesthetic beauty is a form of self-care.** It can feel awful to acknowledge.

As mothers/parents, we first need to acknowledge this truth for *ourselves*—we need to understand the real root of our motivation—and then we can better help our kids navigate the world and the pressures it is already imposing on them. ❍
