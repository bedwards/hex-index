## **The State of Women’s Aging Interventions**

## **1\. HRT (Hormonal Replacement Therapy)**

*What is it?* Systemic HRT, as opposed to local HRT (which we will cover below), involves supplementing the body with externally sourced (*exogenous*) hormones to restore levels that naturally decline with age. It serves two critical functions: (1) ADDRESS major symptoms of perimenopause and menopause such as vasomotor symptoms, and (2) PREVENT the cascading deterioration of cardiovascular, skeletal, and cognitive systems that is triggered along with the loss of estrogen.

> **The dual protective and restorative actions of systemic HRT qualify it as a - if not THE - ultimate longevity intervention for women, and the most powerful evidence-based solution today to treat female aging.**

*There are a few core components of HRT:*

-   **Hormones -** HRT may involve supplementing all three, or just one or two, of the following hormones
    
    -   **Estrogen** (typically estradiol)—the cornerstone of treatment - available in various forms such as oral pills, gels, creams, patches.
        
    -   **Progesterone**, usually taken alongside estrogen for women with an intact uterus to protect the uterine lining from abnormal thickening or cancer. Beyond this protective role, progesterone also supports sleep quality, mood stability, hair thickness, and bone health - available as oral pills, gels and suppositories.
        
    -   **Testosterone** is gaining traction in women’s health, especially among progressive clinicians. While promising, it remains off-label in the U.S. for women, so I'll set it aside in this conversation. [For those who are curious, here is a meta analysis covering its safety and efficacy](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31353194/)
        

**Types** - there are 2 distinct types of hormones

-   🐎 **Synthetic** (non-bioidentical) hormones like Premarin which formulations are derived from equine sources. They have molecular structures different from human hormones and were used in most controversial research studies.
    
-   🌱 **Bioidentical** hormones which exactly matching the molecular structure of hormones produced by the human body (generally derived from plants).
    
    -   *Note: bioidentical hormones themselves come in two forms—those that are FDA-approved and those that are compounded ie custom-mixed by pharmacies. While compounded hormones use the same molecules, they are not subject to FDA oversight, which means their quality and consistency can vary.*
        

**Dosage** - dosage is just as important as hormone type. The appropriate **type, dose and timing of HRT** depend on a range of factors, including [symptom type and severity, health conditions, presence or absence of a uterus, contraceptive needs, and patient preferences](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37002679/).

> *In short: HRT incredibly complex. There is no one-size-fits-all. Dosage and type depends on multiple factors, and finding the right balance often requires ongoing monitoring and thoughtful personalization over time.*

*How efficient (and how safe) is HRT?*

The story of HRT is filled with controversy but the latest research is unequivocal: **for most women, the benefits far outweigh the risks** (with certain medical exceptions). When initiated early—ideally within 10 years of menopause—HRT not only provides relief from disruptive symptoms, it also acts as a **powerful preventive therapy**:

### **2\. Lifestyle: The Foundation Often Ignored**

These powerful, evidence-based approaches benefit all women experiencing hormonal decline, **whether using HRT or not.** Though well-documented in research, they often get dismissed as generic public service announcements *("eat healthy, exercise more")* when they should be considered FOUNDATIONAL given their proven efficacy:

-   🌱 **Anti-inflammatory diet** becomes crucial as both aging itself and estrogen decline amplify systemic inflammation. Estrogen naturally suppresses inflammation; without it, inflammatory processes accelerate. Research shows implementing an anti-inflammatory nutritional approach can reduce inflammatory markers by 30-40% in postmenopausal women, partially compensating for the lost anti-inflammatory effects of estrogen.
    
-   💪 **Strength training** becomes non-negotiable as menopause triggers accelerated sarcopenia—in the first 1-3 years post-menopause alone, women lose 10-15% of muscle mass (followed by approximately 8% per decade), alongside a 2-3% annual decline in bone mineral density. Strength training is the only way to effectively counteract this deterioration.
    
-   💊 **Targeted supplements** become important as various bodily systems previously supported by estrogen need additional help, such as magnesium for sleep, omega-3 fatty acids to reduce inflammation; or adaptogens to help with stress.
    

### **3\. Genitourinary Treatments: Often Overlooked, Deeply Needed**

Women's genitourinary tissues are profoundly affected by estrogen decline. For many, systemic HRT alone doesn't resolve these changes:

-   🎯 **Local HRT** delivers precise, low doses of estrogen directly to vaginal tissues to address specific menopause symptoms (vaginal dryness, discomfort, urinary urgency, and recurrent infections)
    
-   💉 **Effective, non-hormonal treatments** also exist - While formal research on them remains limited, patient-reported improvements are compelling enough to warrant their consideration as legitimate solutions:
    
    -   **Pelvic floor therapies** (like [Emcella](https://btlaesthetics.fr/for-providers/emsella-providers)) use targeted electromagnetic stimulation to strengthen the muscles that control bladder function, helping reduce urinary incontinence.
        
    -   **Vulvovaginal restoration** - using lasers (photomodulation, CO2), hyaluronic acid injections or PRP (platelet-rich plasma) injections, aims to correct the tissue changes from estrogen decline that impact comfort during daily activities and intimacy. Evidence for their effectiveness still needs to build up.
        

## **The Reality: Treatment Gap**

Despite the existence of these evidence-based interventions, the statistics are shocking:

-   **75-80% of women who seek medical help for menopausal symptoms are dismissed or inadequately treated** (*Source - Sarah Gottfried)*
    
-   **Only about [7% of women in the US](https://www.menopause.org/publications/clinical-care-recommendations/hormone-therapy) and lower numbers in Europe ([6% in France](https://www.inserm.fr/dossier/menopause/)) are currently on HRT, despite the overwhelming evidence of its benefits**
    

Why does this gap persist? The barriers are deep and multifaceted—embedded in our medical systems, research priorities, and cultural attitudes. In our next newsletter, we'll examine these barriers and explore how they're finally beginning to break down.

## The Barriers: Why Effective Solutions Aren't Reaching Women

### **1\. The Misinformation Legacy**

In the 1970s, ~25% of menopausal women were on HRT, widely recognized as both safe and effective. Then came what Dr. Peter Attia has called **"*****[the biggest screw-up of the medical field in the last 25 years](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=80QfqqMFcoc)*****"**: the Women's Health Initiative (WHI) study from [2002](https://www.webofscience.com/wos/woscc/full-record/WOS:000176807000024). This deeply flawed study claimed HRT caused breast cancer, prompting the medical establishment to HALT hormone therapy entirely for two decades with profound implications on women’s health.

Though the WHI has since been methodologically discredited at length and leading organizations like The North American Menopause Society fully [endorse HRT treatments](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13697137.2024.2394950#d1e349), the damage lingers. The initial alarm made headlines; the correction barely did. So today, we’re still stuck in the wrong conversation: not *how to personalize and optimize HRT*, but *whether it’s safe at all*—despite overwhelming evidence that it is not just safe, but the most powerful intervention for women aging.

For women trying to educate themselves, the landscape is a mess—riddled with outdated narratives and half-truths. There are other common misconceptions about HRT that need to be debunked for its broader adoption:

-   **The birth control shadow**: Many women entering perimenopause grew up rejecting hormonal contraception. Their caution is understandable, but here's what's missing: HRT is fundamentally different. Birth control uses synthetic hormones at much higher doses to override natural function; modern HRT uses bioidentical hormones designed to restore what the body is losing. Women often conflate the two.
    
-   **Cancer concerns**: Knowing that roughly 70-80% of breast cancers are estrogen receptor-positive (ER-positive), many women are wary of supplementing estrogen. However, estrogen doesn't cause cancer outright—it interacts with complex systems including inflammation, immune response, and genetics. The nuanced reality is lost in oversimplified narratives.
    

### **2\. Systemic Failings of the Medical System**

#### Lack of medical training

Pretty shocking: even OB/GYNs—supposed experts in women's reproductive health—often receive little to no training on perimenopause and menopause. This is true pretty much anywhere in the world. The reality is that OBGYNs are trained as experts of reproductive organs, not of hormonal health across the lifespan.

> [6.8%](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30711122/) *[of new medical](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30711122/) practitionners in the U.S. say they feel prepared to treat menopause in a primary care or ob- stetrics and gynecology setting. And only [20% of OB/GYN residency programs offer formal menopause training at all.](https://www.contemporaryobgyn.net/view/survey-shows-menopause-curriculums-lacking-in-residency-programs?utm_source=chatgpt.com)*

This massive educational gap was worsened when HRT was effectively taken off the table: Menopause was recast as a “natural life stage,” not a treatable condition. And while lifestyle interventions can be incredibly powerful, most medical programs still offer little or no training in nutrition, sleep, or exercise science. **The result? A massive care vacuum for women in midlife.**

We urgently need more robust research on solutions for women’s aging—especially HRT—to address the critical questions that forward-thinking physicians are actively debating:

-   How early should HRT be prescribed for preventative purposes?
    
-   Should testosterone supplementation become standard, given its central role in women’s biology?
    
-   How efficient is HRT in managing the less “obvious” yet incredibly prevalent symptoms such as mood and anxiety disorders?
    
-   Which formulations and dosages are most effective for different women?
    

#### Misalignment of incentives

My personal take? The biggest barrier to women receiving proper care today is how fundamentally misaligned our medical system is with women's actual needs in midlife.

Women require personalized care (accounting for unique symptoms, medical history, and preferences), integrative approaches (combining lifestyle interventions with pharmacology), and longitudinal support through a complex transition that spans years. Yet our healthcare system increasingly optimizes for quick, one-off transactions. The reality is that women's health solutions—particularly HRT—are complex and multifaceted. Companies pioneering this space like Midi Health have developed ***40+ different protocols*** to address this complexity. This depth of care requires time, specialized knowledge, dedicated clinical pathways, and comprehensive follow-up—elements our current medical system simply isn't designed to deliver.

In a healthcare environment that prioritizes volume and simplicity, taking the time to properly treat a menopausal woman is, bluntly, "not worth it" for most providers. The economic incentives don't align with providing the care women actually need.

The treatment gap is huge, but it's finally beginning to be addressed. **In the next newsletter, I'll explore who's working to close this gap and how they're reimagining women's healthcare for the future.**
