1. The Longevity Market Is “Overrated”

Max Marchione, founder of the digital preventative health clinic Superpower (which wants to be the “Tesla” of healthcare and garnered an impressive waitlist) recently shared his thoughts about the state of the Longevity industry.

His insights resonated with me on several levels.

First, the excitement around longevity has cooled over the past few months. We've moved past the initial "longevity" trend and beyond the wave of curiosity that influencers like Andrew Huberman and various podcasters helped spread - just look at how the topic is trending on Google Trends:

The main challenge of the "consumer" longevity industry lies in human psychology. Beyond the initial peak of curiosity, patients have come to realize that true longevity and healthspan optimization require long-term lifestyle changes, which are tough to maintain. Achieving these goals demands willpower, time, and money, and as we've seen, it's difficult for most people to stick with essential routines like diet, sleep, and exercise. As a result, many longevity-focused companies and clinics are facing retention challenges, and the total addressable market (TAM) for pure longevity companies is likely much smaller than initially anticipated.

While lifestyle changes are crucial, they're not the only path forward. The field of longevity is multifaceted, with potential breakthroughs coming from various directions. For instance, while controversial, medications like Ozempic point to a future where pharmacological interventions might play a role in longevity and healthspan extension. The current lull in excitement shouldn't be misinterpreted as a dead end; rather, it's a natural part of the innovation cycle. We may be experiencing a temporary "winter" in longevity research, akin to the AI winters of the past, but this doesn't negate the field's long-term potential.

2. Longevity Now: Diagnostics, Biotech, and Performance

a) Diagnostics Stratification for Early Disease Detection

I recently had an enlightening conversation with Lutz Finger, venture partner at Cherry Ventures, Faculty member at Cornell University, and former Google Health product lead, about the critical role of advanced diagnostics in unlocking healthspan potential.

Why do Diagnostics matter in the first place? We’ll state the obvious: early detection is crucial. The earlier we detect diseases, the sooner we can intervene and improve lifespan and healthspan (early detection can lower the risk of heart attack or stroke by up to 50-70%, reduce breast cancer mortality by 20-40% and so on).

The Problem: We’re bad at diagnostics - even routine ones

The Problem: We're bad at diagnostics - even routine ones. For instance, in the U.S., 23% of adults with diabetes and 80% of pre-diabetic adults are undiagnosed. Even where screenings exist, many cancers are still diagnosed late. A stark example of this is colon cancer: despite its slow growth rate and the availability of effective screening methods, it remains a leading cause of cancer deaths. This highlights a crucial gap between our diagnostic capabilities and their practical implementation.

The other major problem looming in the background? We’re getting older and sicker, and our healthcare systems aren’t equipped to handle the growing burden. As more people live longer with chronic conditions, the strain on diagnostic systems will only intensify, overwhelming an already stretched healthcare system.

The solution: Smarter diagnostics stratification

Of course part of the solution lies in deploying more and more accurate diagnostics tools by advancing diagnostic technology. We're seeing significant strides in this area - look at Apple’s recent announcement of the Watch now FDA approved diagnostics tool for detecting sleep apnea, represent major steps forward.

But what we'll also need is smarter and more reliable risk stratification for diagnostics. The solution isn't simply conducting more tests; it's about making them more targeted. We need diagnostics that stratify patients based on risk factors such as lifestyle, medical history, family history, and key biomarkers. This approach would help identify the right patients for screening at the right time, catching diseases earlier when they have a smaller impact on healthspan. By focusing on high-risk individuals, we can avoid unnecessary screenings, reduce the strain on the healthcare system, and maximize the positive impact on longevity and overall health.

Lutz pointed out an intriguing possibility for the future of diagnostics: leveraging advanced AI models, similar to the large language models we see in other fields. These models could potentially analyze vast amounts of health data - imagine processing the electronic health records of an entire city - to provide context for individual diagnoses. This approach could revolutionize how we identify and predict health risks.

Until we fully adopt these smarter diagnostic systems, broad, out-of-pocket tools like Prenuvo or Neko Health will continue to fill the gap.

b) Long on Longevity Biotech

No amount of diet, sauna, exercise, or supplements will extend lifespan that much. What will is biotech - Max Marchione

This sentiment is widely shared among longevity researchers and investors. This is why many of them show little interest in consumer-focused longevity applications like saunas or supplements. In the long run, the real breakthroughs in healthspan and lifespan will come from longevity biotech, which is poised to unlock profound advances over the next 20 to 30 years.

Sebastian Brunemeier, General Partner at Healthspan Capital and one of the most prominent angels in the space, made a poignant presentation about the promise of longevity biotech at the Longevity Founders Forum and graciously allowed me to share the contents of his presentation below:

Longevity Biotech will replace the Pharma symptom palliation model

A century ago, the idea of developing ‘magic bullet’ drugs to cure infectious diseases was seen as impossible—yet the development of antibiotics and chemotherapies fundamentally changed medicine. Today, the same shift is happening in longevity biotech: instead of simply managing symptoms, new drugs are targeting the root causes of aging at the molecular and cellular levels.

"Root cause medicine will replace the Pharma symptom palliation model" - Sebastian Brunemeier, Longevity Founders Forum

The number of Longevity biotech companies is increasing exponentially

3. Longevity “Wellness”

Finally, I agree with Max Marchione’s excitement about companies focusing on Performance, but I’d go a step further: it’s not just that a new breed of “performance” companies is emerging—longevity science is blending into the wellness space. The wellness industry is being transformed with cutting-edge science, from data-driven approaches to pharmacology, all with a focus on performance and longevity.

As I discussed in my previous post, this new form of “medicalized wellness” brings together tools and interventions that not only help people feel better now but also improve long-term health. While we can call this “performance,” it’s ultimately about optimizing healthspan and blending longevity with everyday wellness.

The longevity industry holds immense promise, and I'm excited to see it entering a more "focused" phase (though some might argue it was always there and just got sidetracked by biohacking and wellness trends), where diagnostics and biotech hold immense potential.

As a parting thought, I'll leave you with a few slides from Sebastian Brunemeier on why longevity— even if it no longer makes media front pages and biohackers lose steam —remains one of the biggest challenges to solve and opportunities of our time.
