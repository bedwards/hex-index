After months of closely following developments in American healthcare, I want to explore a story that's being overshadowed by controversy: Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s recent appointment as head of the Health and Human Services (HHS) under Trump’s administration, and his vision for transforming the medical system.

While headlines have zeroed in on his polarizing views—most notably his controversial stance around vaccines or fluoride—another, largely overlooked aspect of his vision has sparked significant excitement among voters and part of the medical community: a bold effort to make root-cause medicine the cornerstone of the American healthcare system, confronting our chronic disease epidemic and challenging the fundamental failures of our current system.

RFK Jr.’s approach, grounded in principles of root-cause medicine, aims to transform how we think about and treat disease: focusing on underlying causes rather than just managing symptoms. If implemented at the highest level of U.S. healthcare, these ideas could trigger a paradigm shift that ripples globally—moving root-cause medicine from the margins to the mainstream of medical practice.

In this article, I'll unpack RFK Jr.'s core ideas about chronic disease and his early policy proposals, drawing from his recent roundtable with prominent doctors and nutrition experts, and his advisors' appearance on Joe Rogan's podcast.

America's Chronic Disease Health Crisis: Kennedy's Wake-Up Call


In recent roundtables and interviews, RFK Jr. has pointed to a "societal-scale health collapse", and a range of statistics that paint a stark picture of America's chronic disease epidemic - especially when viewed holistically rather than in isolation:

  • 74% of Americans are overweight or obese.

  • 50% of adults have type 2 diabetes or pre-diabetes—up from 1% in the 1960s.

  • Young-onset dementia has tripled since 2012.

  • One in two Americans now face a cancer diagnosis in their lifetime.

  • Young adult cancers up 79% in the last decade.

  • Autism rates have soared from 1 in 150 in 2000 to 1 in 36 children today.

  • Infertility rises 1% annually; 25% of men under 40 face erectile dysfunction.

  • Autoimmune diseases increasing by up to 13% yearly.

2024 will be the year when all these diseases reach record highs. Yet, few are asking the most important question: why are all of these diseases rising simultaneously? Our medical system, focused on managing individual diseases, is missing the bigger picture.

Modern diet and lifestyle is breaking our core biology - Calley Means, RFK’s advisor

The problem? The same one functional and integrative medicine has been pointing to for years: we have a metabolic health crisis (read more about it in my dedicated post). Modern diets, environmental toxins, and sedentary lifestyles are creating oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and chronic inflammation—all of which assault our metabolic health and create a cascade of chronic diseases. And while these issues feel insurmountable, they're also the key to turning this crisis around.

The Roots of America's Health Crisis: Beyond the Numbers

According to RFK, the rise of chronic diseases in the U.S. didn't happen overnight. It's the result of systemic changes that began decades ago, driven by corporate interest, regulatory failures, and cultural shifts.

Big Food and Ultra-Processed Foods

Uncomfortable truth: the rise of chronic disease tracks closely with the rise of ultra-processed foods in the 1980s. During that decade, following the Surgeon General's report on the dangers of smoking, tobacco companies began pivoting to the food industry. By the 1990s, tobacco corporations acquired major food companies, and applied the same tactics that made cigarettes so addictive:

  • Engineer foods to make them biologically addictive.

  • Capture institutions of trust to position ultra-processed foods as healthy.

  • Pay scientists to obscure the harmful effects of these products.

By the 1990s, 50% of American food production was controlled by former tobacco companies. Ultra-processed foods, which didn't exist before the 1980s, now dominate the market.

Today, 67% of children's calories come from ultra-processed foods—products created in labs rather than grown in fields. Unlike whole foods, these products bypass the body's natural satiety signals and hijack human biology, leading to overeating and metabolic dysfunction.

These products, as Kennedy's advisors argue, are "weapons against human biology", with devastating consequences for public health.

Big Pharma and the Chemical Assault

For RFK Jr. and his advisors, chemicals saturate our food, water, and environment, and also have devastating consequences for public health. These substances—many deliberately added to our food system—are not just contaminants but active agents driving metabolic dysfunction and chronic disease. Let's look at the stats:

  • Pesticides: 6 billion pounds of pesticides are sprayed on global crops each year, many of which are linked to cancer, neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer's, and obesity. Glyphosate, widely used in agriculture, has been classified as a probable carcinogen by the World Health Organization and is strongly linked to endocrine disruption and mitochondrial damage.

  • Plastics: studies estimate the average person ingests a credit card's worth of plastic every week. What's less known is that plastics mimic estrogen, disrupt hormone balance, and are linked to infertility, obesity, and metabolic disorders.

  • Synthetic molecules in our food: There are many synthetic molecules in our food—especially ultra-processed food—that are also hijacking our metabolism in ways that we don't understand.

    "The only difference between a synthetic molecule and a drug is intended use, but they are both synthetic molecules made in factories and labs (..) and if the intended use is for food, you can synthesize almost anything you want and put it in food” - Calley Means, RFK’s advisor

Medical Institutions Breakdown

Finally, RFK Jr. has been very vocal about how corporate interests have compromised public health institutions. Let’s look at stats again:

  • 75% of the FDA's funding comes from pharmaceutical companies.

  • The American Diabetes Association is funded by soda companies like Coca-Cola and processed food manufacturers, undermining its guidelines.

  • 95% of USDA dietary guidelines committee members have ties to Big Food or Big Pharma.

The other issue? Broken medical education: Despite the clear links between chronic disease and diet, 80% of U.S. medical schools do not require a single course in nutrition. Physicians are trained to treat symptoms with drugs rather than address metabolic dysfunction through lifestyle and dietary changes. This gap reinforces the pharmaceutical industry's dominance and perpetuates the cycle of chronic disease.

RFK’s Policy Proposals

The specific details of RFK’s policy have not been laid out yet, but we know from his campaign that the following ideas are at the center of it:

  • Incentivize a Healthier System: Promote policies that encourage healthy food choices and exercise over pharmaceutical interventions.

  • Integrate Nutrition into Medical Education: Address the lack of nutrition training in medical schools—currently, 80% of U.S. programs do not require even a single course on nutrition.

  • Reform Dietary Guidelines: Push for science-based dietary guidelines free from corporate influence to prioritize public health over industry interests.

  • Focus on Preventive Health: Shift the healthcare model toward wellness and prevention rather than reactive disease management.

  • Public Education: Launch grassroots awareness campaigns on the importance of nutrition, lifestyle, and the fundamentals of health, including sleep, exercise, and circadian rhythms.

  • Challenge Big Food and Big Pharma: Encourage physicians and policymakers to advocate against the undue influence of corporations in shaping healthcare practices and public trust.

Can it work? At the core, of these policies are two initiatives that are both ambitious and challenging:

  • RFK is taking direct aim at the entrenched influence of Big Food and Big Pharma—two of the most powerful industries in the U.S. The scale of his proposals reflects his determination, but implementation will be challenging given the deep financial ties and systemic entrenchment of these industries.

  • Many of Kennedy's proposals also rely on public policy interventions, but their effectiveness in addressing systemic health issues is still to be determined. This was discussed in a recent episode of Peter Attia’s podcast: public health policies targeting issues like obesity have often struggled to produce measurable outcomes.

"There is no compelling evidence that any of this has had a meaningful impact. You can cherry-pick here and there...but did it lower obesity rates? Those have never been shown." - David Allison, PHD in the Peter Attia podcast

While policies addressing food environments and community health remain important tools, these critiques highlight the challenge Kennedy faces in delivering meaningful and scalable change through policy alone.

His willingness to put these hard truths at the center of public discourse is bold, even if his proposed solutions remain to be fully articulated and tested.
