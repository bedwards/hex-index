How do you screen for metabolic disease?

There can be significant developments between being in a state of good health and developing metabolic disease. Oftentimes, the symptoms of metabolic sickness go unnoticed or don't manifest until the condition becomes severe: its earliest stages, the disease is often silent (more than 84% of US adults being unaware that they have pre-diabetes!). Knowing the prevalence of the disease even in seemingly healthy individuals, it is advisable to get screened for it.

Standard screening

In Part 1 of this series, we outlined the standard guidelines for screening Metabolic Syndrome by the National Cholesterol Education Program (NCEP) Adult Treatment Panel III (ATP III), which involve a combination of blood tests and functional tests. However, these screening guidelines are dated from 2005, and may not be the most effective way to evaluate metabolic health: they fail to account for the necessary nuances of the disease and may not be able to detect it before it progresses to irreversible stages, such as diabetes and NAFLD.

“I won’t suggest that this is the best way to evaluate metabolic health. I think there are many more nuances that we’re going to go into, but at a minimum, I think everybody should know where they stand on those things.” — Dr. Peter Attia, MD and leading Longevity doctor.

Advanced screening

More advanced screenings are available to properly assess metabolic health: Dr. Peter Attia, one of the leading longevity MDs, approaches it by looking at fasting glucose or fasting insulin levels, kidney and liver functions, lipid levels (including clues of insulin resistance such as the triglycerides to HDL ratio), and triglyceride levels. These advanced tests include:

  • An advanced blood test - which involves looking at a broader set of biomarkers, using stricter ranges to determine what is considered "healthy" versus "not healthy". As a reference, here are the “ideal” ranges Peter Attia uses in his practice.

  • The use of a Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM) - which involves wearing a small biosensor on your arm to measure glucose levels in real-time throughout the day and night (the most popular is the Freestyle Libre). CGMs not only help measure fasting insulin and glucose levels, but also provide a more detailed view of the body's glucose response, including glucose peaks, recovery time and the impact of exercise and sleep on your glucose levels. As a reference, here are the “ideal” glucose ranges Levels, one of the leading CGM consumer apps recommends to their users.

  • A liver ultrasound to evaluate the presence of fat, fibrosis, or scarring in the liver, indicating the occurrence of fatty liver disease or NAFLD which are severe metabolic diseases.

  • Finally, for the most advanced form of screening, a V02 max test can be conducted to assess fat and carbohydrate oxidation under load, as well as a Dexa scan to evaluate general health by looking at subcutaneous body fat, muscle mass, and VAT (Visceral Adipose Tissue).

Preserving and reversing metabolic disease

The 4 pillars of metabolic health

Nutrition, exercise, sleep, and stress have a significant impact on our overall health, longevity, and specifically our metabolic health. While the mechanisms and advice we’ll outline may seem basic, that is precisely the point: our modern lifestyle and diet has killed the “healthy fundamentals” of these pillars in dramatic ways, driving the metabolic health crisis.

The first obvious two? Nutrition and exercise

🍎 We know that metabolic syndrome is mainly derived from poor nutrition (read Part 1 of this series), specifically from consuming bad and excessive calories - which end up accumulating in the bloodstream, as subcutaneous fat, or as fat around the organs.

=> How can you optimize nutrition for metabolic health? While many may wish for a miracle pill or fancy diet, unfortunately, the answer is much simpler and less exciting, according to Christopher Gardner, one of the world's leading experts in nutrition.

“Eating more whole foods and vegetables, less refined grain and added sugar would resolve 75% of nutritional issues” - Christopher Gardner, PHD Professor (Research), Medicine - Stanford Prevention Research Center

And as far as diet goes, Stanford researchers from the Prevention Research Center studied a variety of diets (Keto, etc), and concluded that the Mediterranean diet was the most effective for controlling blood glucose.

🏋️‍♀️ Exercise increases glucose uptake into muscle (both during and approximately 2 hours following a 30-minute session). When you exercise, your glucose is used by the muscle tissue for energy and to replete stores: it clears the bloodstream faster, and lowers blood glucose levels: the more you exercise, the better your metabolism, the healthier you are.

=> How do you optimize exercise for metabolic health? You should exercise regularly, obviously. The AHA recommends at least 150min/week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity or 75min/week of vigorous aerobic activity. And the best hack yet? A 2-minute walk after a meal really mitigates extreme blood glucose responses.

The less obvious ones? Sleep and stress

😴 During sleep your metabolic health gets an overhaul: your body releases glucose from the liver (which acts as a glucose warehouse during fasted states) to fuel repairs and other physiological processes. Therefore, sleep deprivation and variability in sleep schedule have been linked with major increased insulin resistance.

⇒ How can you optimize sleep for metabolic health? It is crucial to get 7 to 9 hours of sleep every night at a consistent time.

😟 Both acute (peaks of stress) and chronic stress are problematic. Specifically, chronic stress prolongs your body's high-alert state beyond its intended duration. This eventually leads to a breakdown in your insulin and glucose responses, increasing the likelihood of fatigue, unhealthy eating habits, weight gain, insulin resistance, and cardiovascular disease.

⇒ How do you improve stress? Find a stress reduction method that works for you. This can include meditation, breathwork, or exercise. Additionally, you can explore Andrew Huberman's podcast for effective stress reduction protocols.
