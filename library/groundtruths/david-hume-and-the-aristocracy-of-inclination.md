All throughout David Hume's Essays: Moral, Political, Literary, we find a curious smattering of seemingly contradictory claims about the possibility of, advantage of, and existence of an "elite" segment within society. In particular, Hume is ambivalent about the existence of a cognitive elite, or a group of people with excessive ability to think, philosophize, and engage in the "nobler arts" of life. Though I believe that what we’ve read of Hume's work is marked by a deep belief in the fundamental equality of mankind, I also contend that this work openly admits the existence of a deeply inequitable distribution of a certain quality which leads men to vastly different outcomes in their work and their lives. The group that benefits from this unequal distribution is what I'll call the Aristocracy of Inclination. It effectively constitutes an elite, and it somewhat unsurprisingly counts Hume as a member.

Hume is clear that he believes most of humanity to be endowed with strikingly similar degrees of mental and artistic competence, on both the level of the individual and that of the population. As for the individual, Hume tells us that, "though the persons, who cultivate the sciences with such astonishing success... be always few, in all nations and all ages; it is

impossible but a share of the same spirit and genius must be antecedently diffused throughout the people among whom they arise."1 For that of the population, he says that "the natural genius of mankind be the same in all ages, and in almost all countries, (as seems to be the truth).”2 He makes allowances for differences of “fortune, and endowments of the body,”3 but overall his picture of human cognitive ability is that of an equitably distributed quantity. To call Hume anti-egalitarian would simply be wrong, and yet that conspicuous word "almost" in the latter quote gives the slightest hint that to call him an egalitarian would not be telling the whole truth either.

And yet, it is even more abundantly clear that Hume's landscape of humanity is not a flat one; he believes in the existence of intrinsic hierarchy, reaching even as far down as the level of biology. All men, he seems to believe, are endowed with similar levels of ability, and yet their accomplishments rarely weigh out equally in the final judgment. But it’s not just their accomplishments that differ among men, it’s also their stations in life and society.

Early on in his essay On Commerce, Hume tells us that "the bulk of every state may be divided into husbandmen and manufacturers”7, which Hume defines as producers of raw materials and unfinished or commodified goods. Later in the essay, he indicates the existence of a third set of people, previously unmentioned, who, by sheer fact of not being part of the bulk of every state by definition constitute a minority. This group he variously calls "artizans", producers of luxury goods and the "improvers of liberal arts.” This third group arises in advanced states, as a polity amasses "time and experience" adequate to create the surplus of materials and commodities necessary to unlock the next evolutionary form of their economy. Looking back to the concept of stadial history so popular with Hume and his friend Adam Smith, it's clear that Hume is implying a sort of leap forward for those societies that have the ability to give rise to this third class of producers, and the fact that husbandry and manufacturing only come once men "quit their savage state" is a subtle yet unmistakeable value judgment. It is better to be a manufacturer than a savage, Hume is seemingly saying, yet best of all is to be an improver of the liberal arts or a manufacturer of luxury goods (a job that

So then, if all men are nearly equal in ability, but there exists a wide range of jobs to be done, by what methods ought we distribute the tasks of organizing and maintaining an economy? Furthermore, at the risk of inserting a more modern perspective, how does a society decide who has the privilege of being an "improver of the liberal arts" instead of a toiler in the fields? Hume's rather unsatisfactory answer seems to be: according to temperament. "Almost every one has a predominant inclination,”8 he tells us, and some simply have an inclination towards the life of the mind. In Hume's words, they experience a greater "passion" and derive a deeper "enjoyment" from the objects strewn about this landscape. And yet, Hume's theories on the delicacy of taste, descriptions of which match very closely to his descriptions of “enjoyment," outright state that this is a faculty which can be actively cultivated. So Hume believed that temperament could, within some reasonable bounds, be altered and molded with intention. But how?

We are "pretty much masters what books we shall read,”9 says Hume. As in Saint Augustine's famous conversion narrative with its admonition to "take up the book and read,” Hume seems to be saying that it is within every man's power to develop a delicacy of taste and therefore expand his ability to enjoy the "nobler arts" of life. Though Hume's upbringing was somewhat modest, he was clearly not thinking of a blacksmith in the Hebrides when he imagined the man who might spend his time studying "poetry, eloquence, music, or painting", though I don't doubt Hume would wish this man a highly delicate mental palate if he could wave a wand and grant it. Yet those with delicacy of taste enjoy "an encrease of humanity", strengthened judgment, greater enjoyment, "juster notions of life"10, and to top it all off, an improved social life with regards to both friendship and love. Given a deep understanding of Hume's argument, it's hard to imagine, on balance, who wouldn't trade their sickle and scythe for a pen and paper in Glasgow.

Yet after a few glasses of claret, I suspect Hume would admit that it's a good thing that not everyone gets the chance to make this trade. Though he clearly holds dear his noble, liberal arts, he is also unambiguous about the value of commerce. “The greatness of a state, and the happiness of its subjects... are commonly allowed to be inseparable with regard to commerce,”11 he says. Commerce (and by extension its constituent parts, including manual, indelicate labor) for Hume is an unalloyed good. It protects the interests of states and it

strengthens men. The only issue? That the study of beauty and a cultivation of delicacy of taste "draw off the mind from the hurry of business and interest,”12 i.e., the practice of commerce. But does this not mean that the nobler arts in fact draw us away from that which lead to the greatness of states and the happiness of subjects? Are liberal arts, the study of beauty and the cultivation of taste, at odds with commerce, that binding, absolutely good force?

While I can understand the existence of edge cases wherein an 18th Century laborer engages in poetry, painting and rhetoric after returning home from work as a way to "draw off the mind" from a long day of threshing wheat, I don't think this is Hume's idea. No, I think Hume has in mind something more like a learned gentleman retiring to his study after a long day of supervising said threshing of wheat. Hume tells us that not all men will have the temperament for the pursuit of the nobler arts, and that point is taken fairly enough, except that Hume himself tells us that temperament, which is rooted in enjoyment, can be cultivated. So who gets to cultivate it? Hume doesn’t have a satisfactory answer to this question. Where he does address it, he does so only in passing. In The Sceptic, as previously noted, he gives brief attention to the fact that men differ in bodily endowment and fortune. In Of the Rise and Progress of the Arts and Sciences, he mentions the latter quality again. ”The arts of luxury," he says, "and much more the liberal arts, which depend on a refined taste or sentiment... are always relished by a few only, whose leisure, fortune, and genius fit them for such amusements.”13 If fortune affords only a few the opportunity to study the liberal arts, which

lead to a greater enjoyment of them, and if a greater enjoyment can lead to a change in temperament, which “governs him... through the whole course of his life,”14 then it is only the temporally fortunate who form the slim elite capable of grappling with beauty and exercising agency over that which governs their internal lives — and that is for the best.

There is an implicit normative statement in Hume's work: all men are created equal in most important respects, but we must not encourage everyone to pursue the nobler arts. Someone needs to work the fields and keep the wheels of commerce turning, and cultivation of taste pulls men away from labor and industry. Labor and industry, as the foundations of a safe and prosperous state, are unassailable institutions which must continue on, unencumbered by the distractions of the liberal arts. At least for most men. Some are destined for beauty, and some for the mill. As such, Hume is an elitist not of description, telling us that the world is comprised of some men who are born better than others by nature, and many who are worse, but of prescription. He seems to be saying that we may all be born with similar faculties, but that it’s best to leave the cultivation of the nobler arts to those fortunate few who have the natural inclination towards them, meaning the time and learned ability to engage with them. That is, it’s best for everybody if we leave the business of the liberal arts to the Aristocracy of Inclination, or those who can build and construct their own inclinations. In other words, to that small elite which looks suspiciously similar to those men who make up the conventional aristocracy, and somewhat unsurprisingly, to Hume himself.
