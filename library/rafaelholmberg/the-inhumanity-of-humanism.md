If we follow the standard line of sociological interpretation beginning with Émile Durkheim in the 20th century, then politics and humanistic compassion or emotion cannot be separated. Politics, as the collective formation of a social contract to mitigate the uncontrollable forces of nature, required an element of ‘social cognition’, of empathetic relationality. At the same time, however, and especially since the dawn of neoliberal State-planning in the 30s, political structures and financial powers seem to largely proceed mechanistically, *divorced* from any humanistic concerns. Politics, if we are to judge by the global exploits which have characterised Western foreign policy in the last 100 years, operates by a cold and calculating rationality aimed at territorial domination and financial accumulation. A consideration of global human welfare is unsurprisingly irreconcilable with the aggressive tactics of concentrated ideological and economic appropriation.

In older times, we avoided the news in order to shut out the ‘real world’. Today, however, this ‘shutting off’ is performed by the very act of *watching* the news we once avoided. By a sophisticated, mediated inversion, we watch the news only to avoid confronting the reality of what is taking place. The abstract and helpless sympathy which is invoked in the most horrifying images of death, starvation, genocide, and disaster serve to immobilise us: they invoke precisely the same form of humanism which, rather than act as the cause for political intervention by the layman, *prevents* any sustained critique of the political economy responsible for these horrors. Humanism appears to justify the very crises which generate a humanistic compassion. Even worse, it is clear that, for almost any political crisis, the aspect of humanism involved can easily play both sides.

The violent expansion of the post-war US empire (through financing coups and far-right militias) justified itself though the Truman doctrine by appealing to compassionate anti-fascist sentiments in the Western body politic. The destruction of Vietnam, Cambodia, and Laos, for example, ‘worked’ by claiming to defend the humane ideals of liberal democracy. Even the ‘friendly faces’ of Bush and Obama, claiming to compassionately defend individual rights, justified countless brutal attacks on civilian populations under the banner of the war on terror. It seems, then, that the coldest forms of political exploitation have functioned by appealing to a close sense of personal compassion for individuals. Even (failed) resistance movements and anti-establishment activists have deployed the same reliance on humanism, on a universal compassion towards disenfranchised subjects. Humanism, it seems, veils an inversely inhumane, cruelly exploitative (in the case of the status quo) or impotent (in the case of failed resistance movements) politics. The importance of this becomes clear when we look at our current political situation, and in particular at the role played by humanistic appeals.

In August, the UN took the step to declare a state of famine in Gaza, in the wake of Israel’s threats to illegal takeover of Gaza and forcibly displace its Palestinian citizens. News coverage from around the globe has soberly depicted the miserable conditions of Gaza (and, what is just as noteworthy, the West Bank) with images of starving and brutalised Palestinians. The decisive call from the international community is undoubtedly a humanistic one: to empathise with a population in which families and communities have been utterly decimated. We are nonetheless no closer to a serious solution. What is more paradoxical, the same humanistic appeals are made by supporters of Israeli foreign policy: to recognise the everyday threat the Middle Eastern Jews face from Hamas, and the destructive treatment that Jews have been subjected to by Europeans. Humanism appears to play both sides, and yet for both sides it seems almost entirely ineffective.

With Ukraine, the same appears to be the case. Trump has made an obvious shift regarding Russia, from an indeterminate hostility towards Zelensky to economic sanctions and a growing irritation with Putin’s uncooperative ‘diplomacy’. Beyond Trump, however, most of the European leaders have decisively opposed the Russian invasion. Their interests for doing so may privately be financial and geopolitical, but their ‘manifest discourse’ (as Freud would call it) is clearly humanitarian: what is at stake is the freedom and rights not only of individuals but of humanity as such. And yet if we pay even minimal attention to Russian statements, the same humanistic principles are espoused: invading Ukraine was an ethical duty. Ukraine harbours neo-Nazi sentiments, they represent a decadence that directly threatens the welfare of mankind, and they transgressed security agreements intended to secure a global peace. As with Israel and Palestine, appeals to humanistic treatments are once again rife, and yet this humanism contradicts itself, playing both the Ukrainian and the Russian side.

At this point it is crucial to note the critiques of humanism provided by the Frankfurt School during the 1940s and 50s and the French political theorist Louis Althusser in the 1960s. For figures such as Adorno and Horkheimer (two of the most important Frankfurt School thinkers), humanism and individualised compassion are fundamentally a form of disavowal of the real problem. What they recognised was that compassion for *the other* was a mediated way of dismissing a larger political body – the other conceals, rather than reveals, a broader political exploitation. In other words, by emotionally fixating on the plight of a single individual, we do not build a bridge to articulating a more global suffering, but *inhibit* our recognition of these global structures of exploitation by narrowly personalising them. For Althusser, in turn, the real task was to embody a certain theoretical antihumanism, to mirror the impersonal mechanisms of ideological appropriation with an impersonal and thereby effective approach to articulating the increasingly precarious status of political economy. Humanism, for Althusser, was something akin to an ‘empty category’, a way of having empathy immobilise itself. Radical political change requires an abstraction from any single individual, to recognise suffering as constitutively inscribed in systems of economic colonisation.

Without a theoretical appreciation of politics, humanism runs into a deadlock: it is malleable enough to never provide any real opposition to the most brutal political regimes. Even the Nazis, and we can see this just by a quick glance at Hitler’s *Mein Kampf*, weaponised a form of universal compassion: a true humanistic embrace of humanity’s welfare was, for Hitler, reason enough for the problematic Jews to be exterminated. Today, we recognise that humanitarian compassion has decisively taken this further (paradoxical) step. Humanism plays both sides by outsourcing political responsibility from States and onto individual subjects. In *Mother Courage and Her Children*, Brecht recognised a similar problem when political leaders speak of ‘virtue’. Appealing to the virtues of civilians, to their humanistic and courageous responsibilities, is already a sign of a State that has failed its civilian population, calling on its humane compassion as a smokescreen for its own impotence.

Humanism has, in short, become an inhumane method of treating political inequality and brutalities, a form of localised compassion which displaces responsibility onto an ineffective body politic. In this sense, the compassion generated by endless news coverage of humanitarian crises exists not so much in order for the body politic to rise up against the injustices of a late capitalist world. This coverage of crisis acts, instead, as a method of immobilisation, of relinquishment to the injustice of the world. At best, it encourages us to donate a few small sums to an obscurely defined charity to clear our conscience just enough to go on with daily life.
