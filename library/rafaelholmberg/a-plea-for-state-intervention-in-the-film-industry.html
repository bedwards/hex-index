Somewhat unusually for a philosopher, I recently wrote a piece for VICE arguing for the central administration of Hollywood budgets by the US Government, in an attempt to improve its aesthetic outputs by the means of financial regulation. The piece was, as you can imagine, shortened and largely adapted to VICE’s editorial style. I’m republishing the longer, original draft here, hoping that you may get something different from this extended piece.

Not even the most devoted free-market, economic libertarians, including Trump himself, have been able to avoid the need for government regulation in financial markets. The free market has showed itself to be too volatile, quickly aggregating massive but violently precarious concentrations of wealth and power. Left to themselves, markets appear easily self-destructive and a threat even to their capitalist investors. In order to avoid a general economic collapse, a truly faithful, free-market capitalist will therefore inevitably embrace an element of anti-capitalist State regulation. But if we are all happy to accept regulation across a range of economic industries, why have we yet to accept the idea of government regulation in one of our most treasured industries: the film industry?

Let’s take a quick look at some of the biggest films of the last few years: Jurassic World: Rebirth (which required a plot absurd enough to justify a seventh Jurassic Park franchise film: using dinosaur blood to cure heart disease), the latest Superman reboot (this time patronisingly appealing to millennials and Gen Z by exploring the ‘emotional experience’ of being a superhuman alien), a second instalment of the Lilo & Stitch live-action remake (which, to appropriate Jorge Luis Borges on the Falklands War, was as interesting as a comb to a bald man), the 5th John Wick film (this time with the entirely pointless addition of even more assassins with tragic backstories), a new onslaught of Marvel Cinematic Universe films (somehow stubbornly persisting with a new set of heroes after what we hoped would be the conclusion of this franchise with Avengers: Endgame), the 6th Final Destination film (a series which, more than any other franchise, has survived by quite literally repeating the exact same plot in each film: a group of people ‘avoid death’, but nevertheless end up dying anyway), Sonic the Hedgehog 3 (a truly democratic film franchise, where in 2019 the public successfully bullied Paramount Pictures to redesign Sonic following the trailer of the first instalment), or Moana 2 (which, despite its claims to inclusion and ‘representation’ of marginalised cultures, is in fact a denigrating mockery of Polynesian culture).

There is a common theme to all of these: their unoriginality or profound lack of creative risk-taking as well as their staggering box office figures. Each film is either an unwanted live-action remake or the new installation in a franchise that has already been senselessly beaten like a dead horse. Not only this, but they all largely feature a small group of absurdly high-paid Hollywood stars, all of whom generally play the same character (or rather play themselves) in every role. According to Forbes, the five highest paid actors of 2024 earned a total of $447 million – around the same as Tonga’s entire GDP for the same year. In the US and Canada, Paramount Pictures had a total revenue of $879.5 million in 2024, whilst Universal Studios had a total revenue of $1.88 billion. In total, the ‘Big 5’ film studios (Universal, Paramount, Warner Brothers, Walt Disney Studios, and Sony Pictures) earned a total revenue of $106.73 billion dollars (roughly equivalent to the GDP of Bulgaria in 2024). Walt Disney Studios alone earned $91.36 billion.

As many directors have explained, reboots, remakes, and reinstalments all depend on box office earnings – a billion-dollar-earning film will secure an almost instantaneous sequel. And yet these financial excesses permeating modern Hollywood are almost entirely negatively correlated with the quality of its blockbusters. The money circulating through the film industry seems, in other words, to directly undermine anything approaching cinematic value. It is of course worth mentioning that there are occasionally interesting modern blockbusters. Perhaps somewhat controversially, I was impressed by the subversive ideological premises of David Gregory’s Zombies of the Third Reich (2025), which seemed to unintentionally play with the idea that if we admit that Nazis represented the purest evil imaginable, we have to strangely admit that Nazi zombies are somehow more digestible, more familiar to liberal democracy, than everyday Nazism. Others may disagree with this, but will undoubtedly have found the occasional recent film which stood out from the general trend of automated Hollywood remakes.

But what is most noticeable is that these standout films are becoming more and more of an exception. In fact, a large portion of moviegoers know that good films are today a rare thing – and yet they nevertheless continue paying to watch the most recent blockbusters. In a very interesting twist, they sustain an industry in which they themselves have already lost hope. For example, we privately (or openly) caveat every viewing of a new superhero film with the acknowledgement that it is a lazy, unimaginative project, but this disavowal permits us to eventually enjoy it indirectly. But whether our enjoyment is ironic or not, whether it is self-critical or ‘authentic’, studio companies nevertheless receive our money. Here again we are exposed to an equivalent logic between film and political economy. Like the film industry, today’s dominant economic structures rely on the same self-critical distance of consumers, where we permit ourselves to enjoy something only by initially denouncing its exploitative economic underbelly. Many of us are aware of the violent potential of global capital and of the 20th century methods of economic domination. We generally admit that aggressive neoliberal expansion has aggravated the climate crisis and geopolitical antagonisms, and yet things do not change.

Even Mark Zuckerberg and Bill Gates express some type of anti-capitalist sentiment: on the topic of billionaires, Zuckerberg (with a net worth of $249 billion) told us that “no one deserves that much money”, and Gates has continuously expressed sympathies with anti-privatised, socialist policies. But the paradox of this anti-capitalist sentiment at the heart of capitalism is that it does not inhibit our consumerism – instead, it justifies it. It produces a uniquely contradictory reasoning: ‘so long as I explicitly state that having politically influential billionaires is a problem, I can continue contributing to a system which produces these very billionaires.’ In other words, we are continuously disappointed by the outcomes of the late capitalist dream, but this does not keep us from fuelling it. In the same way, most people recognise a profound crisis in the film industry, but we continue to privately finance it.

To put it simply, it appears that the film industry is a market which has gone seriously awry through the forces of financial interests. If unchecked financial interests inevitably threaten economic enterprises, it is not difficult to also argue that exactly the same financial interests are corrupting the film industry. As we have endlessly witnessed, individual consumer intentions are nowhere near powerful enough to counter broader financial structures – what is required is not a ‘shift in attitude’, but rather an administrative intervention. It is because of this that I propose the need for a State regulation in the film industry. The idea of centrally regulating cinematic productions is admittedly not new: the Soviet Union had a very specific solution to this problem of economic priorities infringing on cinematic creation. Soviet control over Russian cinema – in particular with the beginning of the Stalinist era – insisted that aesthetics should be State-mediated, specifically in the service of Soviet propaganda. In the first decade of Stalin’s rule, film makers were forced to adapt their artistic techniques to political interests. The experimental stylisations of Eisenstein’s The Old and the New (1929), for example, were considered by the administrative nomenklatura to deviate sharply and dangerously from the Stalinist principles of Soviet Realism. Following this recognition that experimental cinema could threaten the legacy of the Bolshevik revolution, screenplays had to be submitted to the Soviet Central Committee in order to ensure that neither the plot nor even the styles of mise-en-scène contradicted the principal concerns of the Party.

Soviet state-regulation of cinema therefore implied tying film production – and aesthetics themselves – to the determining role of ideology. My suggestion, however, has little to do with a central ideological message. It is less radical but would in turn be far more effective. The intervention I suggest isn't simply the old forms of direct state involvement in the contents of films. Instead, the interventions I would like to see are specifically financial, limiting Hollywood budgets and thereby influencing the context in which cinema is produced. The repetitive and often unoriginal outputs of contemporary cinema are the effects of Hollywood’s unregulated profit-driven market, which fixates on the highest-paid actors and maximising studio revenues, with cinematography appearing as little more than an afterthought. We got the 36th MCU film (there are indeed 36 films so far in the Marvel Cinematic Universe) not because the director or Marvel Studios felt a drive for cinematic-artistic expression, but because the film industry has become like any other economic industry: profit potentials were seen. This is today an openly stated aim of producers and directors. When asked about the possibility of a new Final Destination instalment, director Steven Quale gave a bluntly honest answer: “We'll see how this one performs internationally, and if it makes as much money as the fourth one, I'm sure Warner Brothers will want to make another one.” Directors no longer conceal the wish to make money behind a veil of ‘artistic passion.’ Instead, they gladly proclaim that the reasons for making the next film are entirely financial – if it didn’t succeed, they would likely be just as happy working in investment banking or with tech startups.

The problem, in short, is the enormous amounts of money circulating around Hollywood, which have an overwhelmingly stronger influence over cinema compared with artistic integrity. If most of us are unhappy with modern cinema, then the first step would be to regulate the flow of money tied to it. My suggestion for government intervention in the film industry would therefore be implemented through specific financial restrictions aimed at discouraging actors, directors, and studios from making profits the principal motive for film production. Budgets, and therefore actor and director earnings, would be centrally limited by state administration to an amount of money which ensures that writing, casting, locations, production, editing, set management, paying extras etc. would proceed unhindered without going above a certain necessary amount. Actors, directors, writers, producers and so on could of course earn a respectable living from their trade, but manufacturing wealth would no longer be the raison d’être of cinema. The logic of this implementation is somewhat straightforward, but the history not only of film but many artistic movements suggests that great inventions emerge when profit is in fact absent, or rather when finance is reduced to an irritating background noise. The exact figures of these government regulated cuts to Hollywood budgets would be a matter of a longer legislative deliberation, and don’t matter so much for the moment.

Unlike the Soviet method of state regulation, there is no teleological reasoning to this proposed intervention: it is not, in other words, proposed in the service of some ideological end or even in the name of promoting a specific form of cinema. Some may have a preference for New German Cinema and others for the films of Billy Wilder – manufacturing personal taste or returning to a period of film for which I am particularly nostalgic is not the intention behind my plea for regulation. The intervention would rather be preventative: the forms of cinema which it would produce cannot be predicted, but a reasonable bet could be made that their main goal would at least not be solely financial. The popular film market is dominated by live-action remakes, dried-up franchises, and a hegemonic group of extremely well-paid actors. If the precarious state of the film industry were transposed into a market economy, it would have even the most modest neo-Keynesian trembling. If we’ve learned one thing in the realm of art from the centuries-long Western project of modernisation (including all of its successes and atrocities), it is that a neat cut between aesthetics and economy is almost impossible to draw. Art reacts to material relations, and sometimes even conditions new political movements. Today, in order to uphold aesthetic standards for film we should recognise the colossal role played by financial interests. Where financial interests of dominant political systems have gone awry, government regulation is the least that can be done. What the film industry is suffering from is in part precisely such unmitigated financial control. What, then, can be lost with a plea for government regulation in the circulation of capital from Hollywood?
