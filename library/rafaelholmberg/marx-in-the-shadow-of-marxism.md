The only way Marx is rendered digestible in academic discourse is by de-militarising him, by making him one amongst several philosophers of his time, or by retrospectively Hegelianising him. To declare a fidelity to Marx is all too often functionally entangled with an obfuscation or conceptual reframing of Marx’s basic project. From this obscure status of the Marxist legacy, Christoph Schuringa argues that we today have to [actualise Marx](https://www.cambridge.org/us/universitypress/subjects/philosophy/nineteenth-century-philosophy/karl-marx-and-actualization-philosophy?format=HB&isbn=9781009304801), or rather see Marx himself as the actualisation of philosophy. Marx’s relation to philosophy was neither to merely extend its theoretical enterprise, nor to reject philosophy in favour of a more ‘practical’ outlook. Marx rather implemented a third alternative: to re-conceptualise philosophy as inherently practical, to reject the forced disparity between theoretical philosophy and practical anti-philosophy. Marx saw in philosophy, as a political force, what philosophy had failed to see in itself.

Yet there is an uncomfortable implication to this project of actualising philosophy by considering Marx beyond the confines of his complacent Hegelianising. If Marx is kept alive partially by being watered down, by being repeated through Hegel or framed as yet one more philosopher, then to be an *actual* Marxist is to risk the true death of Marx. The project of actualisation is also a form of marginalisation, to reject the taming of Marx in those philosophical practices which keeps him (dysfunctionally) active. Marx himself can in this sense be equated to Freud’s paradox of the symptom. The symptom actively impedes our subjective functioning, and yet it acts as a type of structural mediator. If you remove the symptom, you often invite a more acute disorientation where *nothing* is the problem, and a solution thereby becomes impossible. In other words, imperfect functioning becomes the *only* form of functioning, the alternative to which is not good functioning, but no functioning at all. To extract a purer Marx simultaneously implies marginalising him to the point of abjection, or (from the academic perspective) losing a Marxist project altogether.

With this state of affairs, nobody, it seems, is really a Marxist. Being a Marxist has so far in fact implied the obfuscation of Marx’s doctrine. And yet at the same time, a large non-academic consensus depicts the status of Marx in a very different light. In a local newspaper operating in my home town - the Isle of Wight - a [recent article](https://www.islandecho.co.uk/karl-marx-whose-theories-enslaved-millions-holidayed-in-ryde-and-ventnor/) detailed this island’s frequent visits from Marx in the early 1880s. During his time here, Marx would have been working on volume three of *Capital*, my own theory here being that he did so as a countermeasure to the negative rumours spreading about him socialising amongst the middle classes of southern England. Unsurprisingly for the Isle of Wight’s conservative constituency, the article depicts Marx as a terrifying villain responsible for the death of tens of millions of people.

This type of McCarthyist propaganda which identifies Marx, Stalin, Kim Jong Un, and genocidal totalitarianism in a single declaration will of course argue that the complex failures of the Soviet Union reflect the fact that its ‘Marxist core’ is itself corrupt. When the same intentional administrative malpractices, however, led to the death of hundreds of millions of Indians under British occupation, this is declared as a mere contingency, that the essence of European democratic enlightenment is without faults. A propaganda structure of double standards may keep this villainising of Marx alive, and yet it has the very strange additional effect of assimilating any liberal failures to Marxism. The first comment on the article in fact suggests that, despite the extensive failures of Marxism, Keir Starmer insists on following a Marxist ideology. This bewildering assimilation of the UK Government’s reactionary politics with anything akin to socialism or communism cannot, however, be dismissed as harmless background noise. It should instead be framed according to the backdrop of the accusation of ‘cultural Marxism’ that neoconservative reactionaries diagnose the status quo with. The insistence that the establishment is an authoritarian Marxist regime has accompanied the rise in power of multiple far-right parties both in Europe and the Americas, who consider themselves to be the antidote to a ‘radical leftism’ that aspires to the communist ideals of universal healthcare and minority rights.

Between the actualisation of a forgotten Marx and the universal Marxism that the right laments, it seems we are at a very strange impasse: nobody and everybody is a Marxist. Marx is both universal and virtual, an unexpressed possibility *as well as* the status quo. From both the popular and the academic perspective then, an important question arises: what, in both a conceptual and a historical sense, is Marx or Marxism? Marx may well be the practical inversion of Hegel. He offers concrete deployment of the dialectic in Capital to deduce the social categories implemented by the modes of production and circulation of capital. In other words, a component of the break between Hegel and Marx is that Marx is the particular to Hegel’s universal. Hegel constructs ontological or logical forms of the becoming of the concept whilst Marx grounds his engagement with ideology in the structure of surplus value production and the translation of this into rates of profit.

But the strange inverse of this argument is also true: Marx generalises and abstracts the very *concrete* principles that are indistinguishable from Hegel. The critique of political economy offers itself as a universal method, applicable to the basic coordinates of a capitalist regime and as such in fact de-nationalised. Capitalist exploitation is borderless, and so is its critique. Marx laid the foundation for our understanding of decentralised, global post-Fordist capitalism, and the communist society is (much like the universal applicability of Laplanche’s generalised seduction) an anti-capitalist account not bound to any specific culture. Communism, as [Baudrillard insisted](https://www.sup.org/books/sociology/illusion-end), speaks from *beyond* a limit that it defines only as it moves towards it - it speaks from the paradoxical perspective of the other side of the end of capitalist practices that require their originary support in the concept of a State. In other words, it speaks a universal language that is only partially consistent to the present. Hegel, on the other hand, is incapable of thinking the development of an Ethical State without framing it within a determinate, particular case or example of a living body politic. The conjunction of subjective freedom with its reciprocal construction of a three-body (legislative, executive, and judicial) State, is only frameable when applied to a specific people, to a concrete population. Freedom, for Hegel, is bound up within the confines of a State which acts as the inscription of the individual as a moment of universality, a universality which is ethically reflected in the activity and projects of the subjects of *this* State. In other words, it seems at the same time that it is Marx who operates at the level of abstract universals whilst Hegel deals in concrete particulars.

The Hegelian monarch, and Marx’s problem with this monarch, reveals precisely this inversion, where Marx becomes the universalist and Hegel the particularist.. The monarch is for Hegel a performative symbolic inscription. It is the moment in which the Ethical, three-body State retroactively *affirms* its existence as being concretely in accordance with the essence what it was prior to the monarch. It is a necessary moment of subjective affirmation of the State as constituted identically to its essence. This retroactive affirmation, Hegel argues, is only possible where the substantial existence of the State doubles itself in the form of a single subjectivity that is certain of itself. This moment of substance as subject, as a retroactive actualisation of itself, is located in the achievement of subjective self-certainty in an objective constitution. The monarch is therefore a gestural doubling, in which the ethical substance of the State fulfils itself by appearing as a subject.

For Marx, this singular instance of determinate subjective embodiment is misguided. The State does not end with a subject, but begins with it, or rather with a plurality of subjects. These subjects are the fodder or masses which lie at the ground of the mechanism of the State - they constitute the economic base of the social as such. These are the subjects of primitive economic accumulation and the eventual stratification of the social by the division of labour. There is not, for Marx, anything assimilable to *a* subject which, in its determinate expression of a universal self-certainty, constitutes the State’s affirmation of itself. That the Ethical State requires a monarch is is for Marx a dogmatic restriction of ethics to an all-too determinate instance. In other words, Hegel is too dogmatically materialistic. He restricts the Ethical State to the condition of a particular, determinate instance of subjectivity at the cost of a more generalised politic of universal liberation.

Perhaps the real conclusion is that Marx entertains a form of asymptotic approach to Hegel: he is both the concrete dialectical alternative to Hegel’s generalisations, and the universal counterpart to Hegel’s narrow particular conditions for the State. We repeatedly see, therefore, that Marx is both assimilable and irreducible to Hegel’s original intervention. Two Marxists perhaps mostly clearly exemplify this interminable tension in the Marx-Hegel conjunction: Lenin vs Althusser. For Althusser, Marx is defined by an ‘epistemological break’, a new science of philosophising from material, economic premises.

Althusser describes several features of Marx that Hegel’s idealism failed to articulate: the Ideological State Apparatus furnishing the everyday coordinates to reproducing the fundamental conditions of capitalist-economic modes of production; an analysis reformatted to be deployed on the ideological level of our subjective-discursive modes of interpellation into a decentralised, diffused State apparatus; a non-idealised, un-centralised understanding of consciousness as posited within a pre-existing set of material conditions. Yet Althusser’s grand treatise on the independence of Marx and his undeniable break from previous methods of philosophical questioning was *[Reading Capital](https://www.versobooks.com/en-gb/products/139-reading-capital?srsltid=AfmBOorr4YzB3xhRtv-vaL34YWpNieFWma-mSee5JtI4j6rhRtD0pccZ)*. Althusser’s purism towards Marx is clear: the very ‘object of enquiry’ posited in *Capital* is itself furnished by the new method of questioning which Marx installs. With Marx, new disjunctive temporalities of independent historical and economical ensembles/regimes are opened up, and a scientific analysis grounded in the recognition of a constitutively ‘blundered view’ of ideological enquiry is furnished.

Lenin, on the other hand, had famously stated [in his posthumous Conspectus from 1929](https://www.marxists.org/archive/lenin/works/1914/cons-logic/index.htm) that we need Hegel’s *Science of Logic* in order to understand Marx’s *Capital.* Hegel and Marx can in this sense be seen as constituting the perfect pair of political analysis, with Marx being the logical extension of Hegel. [For Althusser](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/althusser/1969/lenin-before-hegel.htm), if anything ‘we need Marx in order to understand Hegel’. Eventually, an impossible compatibility emerges not only between Marx and Hegel, but between Marx and himself. The perplexing division between Hegel and Marx should be located as a division *internal* to Marx. As I have argued elsewhere, the question of Marx’s debt to Hegel is the wrong question. It is this *indeterminacy* in the Marx-event which renders his status self-contradictory, and yet it is this internal inconsistency which lends Marx a distinctive conceptual and practical weight. Nobody has yet to truly embody Marxism, and yet we are all already Marxists.

Writing in defence of Marx today, then, implies embodying a certain indeterminacy. It is of course nonsense to suggest that the Labour Party or the Democrats - who stand for the same global capitalist appropriation that Marx rejected - are latent Marxists. And yet this hysterical insistence seen from Javier Milei to Elon Musk that ‘we are being governed by Marxists’ expresses a certain inverted truth: the attribution of Marxist doctrines to non-Marxist politics reflects the division not only between Marx and Hegel, but between being a Marxist and Marx himself. The contradictions inherent to Milei and Musk - they are free market capitalists only when the free market acts in their favour, after which they are happy with old fashioned State regulation - could easily act as the double to the perpetually self-distancing position of Marx. Recollect Marx’s own disavowal of himself regarding his comments on Jules Guesde and Paul Lafargue: “if they are Marxists, then I myself am not a Marxist.” Marx is divided not only historically in relation to Hegel, but conceptually in relation to himself, it is this paradox which is most clearly stated in the fact that everybody and nobody is a Marxist.
