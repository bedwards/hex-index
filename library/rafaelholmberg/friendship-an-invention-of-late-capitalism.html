One of the greatest achievements of new media structures (whether it is procedural film franchises, TikTok, live streaming, algorithm-designed music, YouTube, or ‘independent’ news outlets) is the method in which they retroactively reformat whatever preceded them. Or as Guy Debord put it, new media ideology installs a new temporality, reframes itself not as a violently ‘new’ or irreducible formation, but rather imperceptibly imposes itself as the natural outcome of historical development. The transgression of new media is perplexing enough to merge with, and reconstitute, what previously appeared neutral, or outside the zone of ideology. If a friend behaved as a commercial search engine, storing and selling off everything they knew about us, we would rightly be very concerned. And yet the real meaning of this comparison between friendship and the internet is more perplexing than it initially appears. A strange double-obfuscation characterises this image. On the one hand, it uses a disorienting form of comparison between incomparable categories which has only become possible with the increased ‘mediatisation’ of the social and private sphere.

This forced homogenisation – the conceptual equation of otherwise incompatible or qualitatively irreconcilable objects – is central to the structures of postmodern exchange which Fredric Jameson diagnosed. Whether it is through the play of film (and its migration into online video consumption) on the position of the observer himself; the transgression in postmodern architecture of the boundaries between ‘inside’ and ‘outside’, where both categories become untenable, thereby defeating the original ‘sheltering’ purpose of housing itself; or the making-public of private life through the economically manufactured image of the inner self in late capitalist appropriation – the new media empire is not self-contained. It operates instead by a perpetual displacement and inversion of limits, not by the conjunction of opposites, but by eradicating the basic notion of opposites in a homogenous entanglement of distinct forms.

We saw exactly this in one of Michel Haneke’s great postmodern treatises, The Seventh Continent (1989), a more faithful representation of the self-destructive logic of late corporatised family life than more popular films such as Falling Down (1993) or Office Space (1999). The film displays a series of anonymised fragments or snapshots (in which faces are rarely revealed) of a suburban family and the labour of its daily routines. The dull sight of their morning breakfast table, with procedural conversation about plans for the week, becomes a regular sight; the familiar lies of the family’s young daughter about absurd trivialities such as being blind when at school; the father’s disinterested performance of a corporate office job; and the mother’s household and social management whilst nevertheless hoping to maintain a sense of identity – these snapshots are repeated to produce the unquestionably uncanny aspects inherent to repetition. Repetition is never just repetition, as Freud showed, but rather a reproduction which frames the alienating core of the repeated thing. Repetition does not reproduce the same, but rather it reproduces unforeseen exceptions, an intimate foreignness lodged in whatever scene is repeated. These fragments of a mediocre lifestyle continue until we reach what appears to be their inevitable conclusion, a conclusion so inevitable that is undoubtedly occurs with the great shock of any event which we know all too well is coming: the family violently destroys their home, smashing up every possible piece of furniture and decorative object, before the father poisons his daughter, his wife, and himself.

Not only do we become aware, in the most harrowing sense, of the anti-capitalist, formless destructiveness paradoxically generated by and internal to what they reject, the pleasantries of the modernised world of global industry. In addition, a sense of guilt is produced in the viewer. The viewer’s participation in the same structures of which the culmination are represented becomes unsettlingly clear. The snapshots in fact appear as edited and collated as an ode to (or indictment of) the viewer, making them not only a passive receiver of media, but an active producer of its horrors. We are reminded of Joan Copjec’s statements on what psychoanalytic theory provided the modern movie-goer with:

“with the introduction of psychoanalysis into film theory the axis of investigation was shifted away from a focus on the narrative to the axis of the spectator-screen relation; the question became: How is the spectator "sutured" into the film?”

In the new media structure, the subject is a feature inherent to the object of consumption, or rather what lies outside of media (take, for example, friendship in the case of the Mullvad VPN advert), becomes at its core mediatised, internally stamping it as always having been a feature of media exchange. This obfuscation of limits in the dawn of modern media was already hinted at by Walter Benjamin with the social function of the camera. The camera, Benjamin wrote in his famous essay (The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction), did not simply act as one of many methods of representing and distributing reality to a mass audience. Instead, it came to actively construct the very reality which it distributed. The camera – in the form of film and news coverage – became inscribed in the world that it reproduced. In other words, much like Hegel argued that the radical emergence of subject from a natural substance retroactively reconstructed this same substance, the camera obliterated the possibility of anything like a ‘neutral’ reality. No such thing as a pure reality could any longer be separated from its biased formulation. Reality as such became structured as its own displaced representation, it was itself imprinted with the permanent possibilities of cinematographic qualities. Ultimately, with the camera reality was augmented and recognised only for its latent value as an object of film.

Jean Baudrillard took this inner mediatised quality of a post-camera world to one of its postmodern extremes. When he was asked to cover the Gulf War for French media, he agreed on the condition that he would write on it solely based on what was reported by CNN. This was, he argued, the most faithful way of commenting on the war, since what was ‘actually taking place in the Middle East’ was becoming a naively hypothetical question, more fictional that the way it was represented in Western news outlets. The most accurate account that he could provide was one based on the simulacral representations of the war through CNN, leading him to confidently argue that the Gulf War was the first war that never took place, or rather which took place only though its sensationalised media representation. Behind this representation, nothing akin to what we know as the ‘Gulf War’ ever took place.

Marshal McLuhan, often called the father of media theory, although this is clearly misguided when we consider the role played by the Frankfurt School and its description of the ‘culture industry’, sheds an interesting light here. Media comes to its own not by itself as the extension of natural reality, but when it is merged with other forms of media. It is not media ‘as such’ which matters, but rather when two or more forms of media begin to mingle, to interpenetrate and transgress each other. In this mode of inter-media communication, the simulacral effects of media are not diminished, but rather augmented. Much like Jean Laplanche argued that, in order to have a trauma, we need two traumas – the traumatic event needs to redouble itself as its own retroactive justification – in order to have a medium, we need a multiplicity of media.

Let’s return, however, to the advert and its play on the paradoxes of new media. It is certainly true that if a friend behaved like a search engine – collecting years of personal information and selling it off to advertisers – we would be deeply concerned. But this irony conceals a more profound obfuscation, or an asymmetrical framing between ‘friendship’ and media or search engines.

If we follow the logic of the advert, we see that it offloads the problem or enigma onto search engines or the internet as a whole - that it presumes, in other words, that the obscurity lies only on the side of search engines. The faulty assumption here is that friendship is a self-evident experience. If psychoanalysis teaches us one thing about intersubjective relations, it is that the most familiar person (the Nebenmensch, neighbour, or intimately present other) embodies an intimacy which is at the same time plagued by a profound opacity. Friendship is a distinct type of paradox: in the proximity of the other, we recognise not only a sense of belonging, but the intrusion of an unassimilable alterity, a desire that is distinctly alien from our own, and a decentring intention emanating from in which we feel just as recognised as we feel objectified and decentred. We ask not only what we want from them, but what they want from us. In other words, the desire of the other alienates us, and friendship is situated precisely on this intersubjective deadlock where familiarity is coloured by a perpetual disharmony – someone may be our friend, but the question of why they are our friend, what they want from us, is an unanswerable impasse.

Derrida recognised a similar paradox when he spoke of the ontology of the animal. The question he asked was not only what type of strange being the animal is for us, but what type of disconcerting anomaly we must represent for the animal. Specifically, Derrida wonders, what does my cat see when (before a shower, for example) I undress in front of it. The gaze of the animal, he argues, comprises a double-enigma. Not only does it highlight our indefinite questioning of the impossibility of knowing what the animal thinks, but it also reflects in a new light an obscurity or self-deception at the heart of subjectivity itself. What we see in the uncanny gaze of the animal is not only our alienation from it, but our alienation from ourselves – the kernel of incommensurable non-identity lodged within subjectivity itself. The animal simply ‘outsources’ an enigma which is always-already directed towards ourselves. Similarly, a friend is more than a companion. A friend is an interminable question-mark which operates not only between people, but within personhood itself, between the subject and itself. We may observe search engines with an unsettling level of perplexity, but we should not let this trick us into thinking that friendship itself is not marked by its own form of alienating obscurity.

New media deploys a distorted use of what otherwise appears as most familiar to us (such as friendship) - a distortion which we nevertheless feel at home with, since by the terms of the advert, we would indeed be concerned if a friend collected and sold our private information, but we feel comforted in that the advert fulfils our fantasy of what a friend should be. In this sense, media is not only a self-contained enigma, but an enigma which reconstructs the very things which it refers to.

Jacques Lacan framed a very similar anomaly in his discussion of bathroom doors. By itself, when it was first invented, a door after all appeared to be nothing else than a door. A convenient method of sheltering or enclosure which nevertheless enables the movement from one space into another. When a door is supplemented with such a social and symbolic dimension, with the signifiers ‘Gentlemen’ and ‘Ladies’ a sight that we are familiar with in restaurants, schools, or cafes, it is no longer ‘just’ a door.

The door becomes implemented within a structure of social customs, performative rituals, excess-meaning, and even prohibitions. Either one of the two door becomes the sign of a certain exclusive experience, an arbitrary permission for one sex and a prohibition for the other - it becomes inscribed within the contingencies of the Symbolic. In order to understand new media, therefore, we should recognise its double-action: not only does it constitute itself by the translation of media by media, by the entanglement of multiple forms of media which obscure any one medium, however familiar we claim to be with it (including friendship). Additionally, media retroactively reconstructs the things to which it refers. The emphasis becomes placed on the medium, such that its ‘pure content’, the neutral referent behind the medium, becomes irretrievable and even impossible to think of.
