In his very recent book A Social History of Analytic Philosophy, the philosopher Christoph Schuringa interrogates the ideological conditions which led to the hegemony of analytic philosophy and the marginalisation of continental philosophy in the English-speaking world. The self-proclaimed ‘depoliticised’ status of analytic philosophy - with its endless scepticism, logical propositions, careful approach to the mind, language, and metaphysics - is in fact far from being as free of politics as it likes to claim. A history of post-war ideological purges (most notably during the McCarthy era) ensured that Marxism (one of the pillars of continental philosophy) would be dispelled from philosophy departments, thereby allowing the a-historical, non-radical, and politically ‘neutral’ structure of analytic philosophy to take centre stage. Schuringa insists, therefore, that we should ‘politicise’ analytic philosophy: we should recognise that it is built on a deeply reactionary ideological foundation, acting as the non-political philosophical support of a broader political landscape.

But what about the opposite case? What about where politics claims to be free of the speculative mediations of philosophy? In such cases, the same level of critical suspicion should be directed at popular political movements which claim to speak a completely pragmatic language, or which claim that we do not need philosophy or elaborate theoretical enquiries into contemporary ideology. This position is where Charlie Kirk, and the America First reactionaries, should be placed. The figureheads of the new right - whether it is Charlie Kirk, Michael Knowles, Candace Owens, Matt Walsh, or Tucker Carlson - brand themselves as avatars of common sense. However this appeal to common sense, to ‘be rational’, is little more than a smokescreen concealing an acute absence of seriousness and a deployment of the same ‘postmodern’ tactics as they claim to diagnose in the left.

Once again, we are facing a strange deadlock. ‘Serious’ philosophers or political theorists dismiss the America First pundits as unserious charlatans, refusing to engage with them. But thereby these same reactionaries proceed unchallenged. Returning to the strange case of analytic philosophy, we should not be afraid to admit that philosophy is always politically engaged. Conceptual frameworks and ontological systems not only reflect the political context in which they were written, but they also have inevitably practical consequences. In this sense, even practical, everyday life requires (and reflects) philosophy.

Consider, for example, the conjunction between theoretical physics (and its speculative origins) and the basic coordinates of communication today. Isaac Newton’s writings would during their time have been considered works of philosophy, especially considering their reliance on Descartes. If we then read Einstein’s theory of relativity, it is clear that his breakthrough (and reworking of Newton) started as a series of speculative reflections on the motion of clocks, observers, trains and so on. Even the shift to quantum physics came with questions on the nature of being, reality, and knowledge. Yet this largely theoretical move from Newton to quantum physics was immanently stamped with a practical implication: the development of modern technology, travel, and advanced forms of communication such as smartphones and television.

Political movements, too, cannot be disentangled from philosophy. Rousseau and the French Revolution, Marx and socialism, Montesquieu and the liberal-democratic division between legislative, executive, and judicial government bodies, or Proudhon and anarchism, are just a few examples of a philosophical ground for practical political action. The point with Charlie Kirk, then, who desperately avoids philosophical reflection, is that he has never been truly challenged. Today, this challenge to his clichéd, often nonsensical claims is a task which even the most academically insulated philosopher should seriously take up. The young Democrats such as Dean Withers or ‘Parker’ go a marginal way in pointing out inconsistencies in the America First logic, but their position is nowhere near radical enough. Ultimately, they represent the reactive tendency to occasionally label Charlie Kirk as a transphobe or a misogynist, which only inversely strengthens his populist fan base (a fan base which will always dismiss questions of social justice as secondary to the universal feeling of disenfranchisement facing the American working class).

Rather than taking aim at occasional problematic utterances, the task, I argue, is instead to ‘philosophise’ MAGA’s practical political stance - to frame it within a broader philosophical narrative in order to see it as a totally misguided, relativistic reaction to political instability. MAGA responds to a very real concern, and to concrete material struggles of a large portion of US workers who have been alienated by the liberal left. But whilst the Democrats have failed, MAGA is clearly the wrong solution. As I will argue, MAGA is little more than the postmodern solution to the new antagonisms of postmodernism itself. Charlie Kirk is not a reasonable defender of US prosperity who sometimes says racist or misogynistic things - he rather reflects a complete absence of critical enquiry, as so many populist figures do. The first question to ask is therefore a simple one, but one which has not been taken seriously enough: why has the new reactionary movement gained so much popularity? Because the Democratic-liberal left has failed to provide any serious alternatives to the economic antagonisms of late, globalised capitalism. Charlie Kirk is therefore able to speak of freedom - and to claim to defend freedom against the Democrats - because both sides refuse to ask the more difficult question of the flaw in the basic structures of today’s political economy.

If we look at some examples of Charlie Kirk’s arguments, we can begin forming a clearer picture of this problem: Kirk defends Israel’s destruction of Gaza because Hamas “should have known what would happen” in the aftermath of October 7th. They should have known that Isreal would refuse to restrain themselves, and would likely retaliate by killing tens of thousands and displacing and starving millions of Palestinians. The onus of judgement and rational decision-making is thereby entirely placed on Hamas: Israel will always respond violently, so Hamas should be the more ‘mature’ agents in the conflict. But the obverse implication of this argument is not considered by Kirk: Israel ‘should have known’ what would result from decades of occupation and exploitation of Palestine - eventually a militant, anti-Israeli, fundamentalist movement would emerge in the space left behind by Israeli policy. Perfectly mirrored in this opinion is the ‘leftist’ opinion (despised by Charlie Kirk), which makes the US or the West responsible for almost all of today’s geopolitical problems.

Take another example: Charlie Kirk buys into the ‘cultural Marxist’ conspiracy that identity politics, wokeness, Bolshevism, critical race theory, Fidel Castro, and the Cultural Revolution can all be neatly placed alongside each other under the category of Marx. Any honest engagement with Marx’s analysis of the dynamic mechanisms of capital or of dialectical materialism’s inversion of Hegel would soon realise that this simplification is disingenuous. But for the new right it works nevertheless. Conservatives all criticise postmodernism for relativising and isolating itself within theoretical language. There are times, for example with the Yale University ‘poststructural deconstruction’ tradition - which refuses to seriously engage with Derrida’s phenomenological-ontological studies, instead choosing to poeticise writing or simply read each other’s writings on Derrida rather than Derrida himself - where this generalisation of postmodernism rings somewhat true. Many Lacanians and Derrideans write about each other’s writings on Lacan and Derrida, but never about Lacan and Derrida themselves. They declare a theoretical fidelity to them, and in so doing obscure and distort them. But the strange fact is that the methods of the Yale postmodernists can be seen directly reflected in conservative libertarianism. When Charlie Kirk or Jordan Peterson speak of critical race theory or postmodernism, it is clear that they speak of other conservatives’ strawmen of postmodernism. Kirk performs the same self-affirming bias as the Ivy League poststructuralist: reading only what what others say about something, rather than reading the thing itself.

Charlie Kirk is therefore little more than a representative of postmodernism’s confrontation with itself: he opposes the decadence of modernity, but reflects its methods, reproducing the substanceless opportunism (such as seeing in Trump a revival of traditional Christianity) that he claims to loathe in the left. Similarly, Kirk does not support gay marriage because heterosexuality is implied to be the traditional or natural norm. But if psychoanalysis teaches us anything, it is that there is no ‘normal’ sexual position - sexuality is painfully assumed in childhood by a complex series of identifications and by the external intrusion of the social sphere within the internal (personal) mechanisms of desire. The heterosexual and the homosexual positions are, in a Lacanian sense, deviations, since all we have is the failure to establish stable sexual relations. Homosexuality should be ‘problematised’ for the same reasons that heterosexuality should be problematised. Although when it has been pointed out to Charlie Kirk - most notably during a recent debate at the Cambridge Union - that sexuality is dynamically unstable and contingent to historical forms of social identification, and that homosexuality has often been an accepted feature of civil life, he simply disavows the fact, claiming that he “still thinks it is wrong”. Ultimately, Kirk appeals to emotions just as much as the ‘irrational left’.

In all of these examples, the flaws in Charlie Kirk’s thinking are clear enough, but the problem repeated by Democrats is treating them as individual political points, rather than recognising them as expressions of a philosophical (or lack thereof) context to political expression. As a whole, they reflect the central absence of any speculative, philosophical enquiry at the heart of the new forms of right-wing populism. And yet faced with this inconsistent anti-philosophical pragmatism, barely any philosophers choose to speak up. The Democrats are incapable of providing any serious opposition, inadvertently preferring to feed into the very problem they attempt to remedy, whilst the new right approaches economic or geopolitical problems by some obscene postmodern relativisation, somehow framing the failures of political economy as due to the death of the family or the rise of identity politics. This text, therefore, is little more than a plea to take the new right-wing revival seriously by reconsidering its internal lack of seriousness. To take it seriously by philosophising politics, just as we are recognising the need to politicise philosophy. If there is a political or ideological backdrop to philosophy, then the inverse should also be accepted: there is a latent philosophical rationale to even the most a-theoretical politics. Charlie Kirk may not be a considered a worthy opponent for the academic philosopher, but it is for this reason that it is more urgent than ever to engage with him. If even Kant’s purely formal, contentless ethics could be concretely politicised by Lacan, then there is no reason for philosophers to refuse to engage with today’s reactionary populism.
