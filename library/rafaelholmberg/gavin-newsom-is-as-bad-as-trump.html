The Trump Administration is notoriously good at appealing to popular demands, except for when these popular demands act against the Administration’s interests. Where Trump meets with public opposition, he resorts to the familiar face of bureaucratic censorship which he so vehemently condemned. It is clear that Trump’s avowal of freedom is a contingent avowal, that he defends freedom, the free markets, and economic meritocracy, only insofar as the markets work in his favour. In this sense, Trump is not (economically) vastly different from his globalist, Democratic opposition: when obscure and precarious financial practices are threatened by the volatility of the free market, bank bailouts, the Federal Reserve, or State intervention are inevitably relied on for support. It is in other words clear that Trump is a threat to the same grassroots Republicanism on behalf of which he was elected.

But with the protests taking place in California, a new opposition is emerging: the core of the problem is not only with Trump but also with the side that opposes Trump. Mass protests in Los Angeles continue in the wake of the largest mass deportation in US history, which has targeted even those migrants with legitimate documentation, and Trump having authorised the mobilisation of 2000 National Guard troops in the city. Trump’s federal intervention is claimed to have “unlawfully” bypassed the Californian State’s usual regulation of National Guards, prompting Gavin Newsom to file a lawsuit against the Trump administration that is still undecided. Newsom’s push against the brutal recklessness of Trump’s mass deportation tactics is now placing him as the centrepiece of a Democratic opposition to Trump’s increasingly authoritarian tone. In doing so, the California Governor would appear to embody the liberal values of inclusion, social justice, global welfare et cetera.

The problem, however, is with the performative nature of Newsom’s reaction: Newsom simply rebrands the same ignorance of the cause of the migration problem that Trump displays. Of course, the Republican tactic of mass deportation and anti-migration policies inevitably only serves to reinforce the global economic tensions that produce a migration crisis in the first place. And yet on the other hand, an empty Democratic or liberal openness to migration leads to the exact same antagonisms that furnish protectionist, proto-authoritarian reactions. The same unfounded framing of the migration crisis provided by Trump is mirrored in Newsom’s gestural opposition, which does little more than insinuate that Trump is a fascist. The real problem, in other words, is that neither Trump nor Newsom seem able to ask the more difficult question of why there is a migration crisis in the first place.

The migration crisis, as is increasingly coming to be recognised by established political commentators, is inscribed within a larger, global precarity in economic and political structures. The legacy of the post-war Truman doctrine – to establish an international economic and military hegemony in the name of liberal democracy – led to a series of brutal foreign policy directives that directly destabilised and uprooted multiple secular democratic governments across Latin America, East Asia, and the Middle East. It is hardly surprising that the financial exploitation which has characterised 80 years of Western foreign policy (a fact even admitted by ex-CIA director James Woolsey) has actively hindered the democratic growth of much of the third world. At the same time, economic alliances with the profiteers of Western exploits – for example Saudi Arabia or Qatar – have failed to encourage them to accept responsibility for the outcomes of the destruction of neighbouring countries. Between Democrats and Republicans, there have been little historical differences in the overwhelming role played by economic interests and strategic interference in producing a global economic inequality.

It is here that we have the problem with the ICE spectacle: Newsom and Trump form a false antithesis. They both stand for the basic economic carelessness which produces the same crisis that they take opposing stances on. It is not enough to adopt the Republican position and simply reject migration, nor does the Democratic alternative work, of implying that nothing is really wrong. In fact Newsom’s (or the Democratic) openness simply serves to fuel the same right-wing dialogue that leads to brutal, protectionist crackdowns on migration. We can take a page from the German philosopher Theodor Adorno’s Minima Moralia here, and claim that even liberalism is plagued with an authoritarian ‘potential’. By not recognising the historical and global-economic forces that have contributed to the migration crisis, liberal and authoritarian positions will continue to co-exist, and we will not find the right coordinates to even begin addressing this crisis.

The Middle American and working-class worries manufactured and yet reflected in the Trump Administration’s election (to put America first) are the same problems that are ignored by Democratic liberals. If we want a true solution to the migration crisis, we should therefore begin by recognising that Trump’s protectionism and Newsom’s liberalism ultimately lead to the same dead-end. A serious approach will need to embody and express the geopolitical precarity responsible for the migration problem, something which neither party is currently providing. As the financial historian Niall Fergusson puts it, all natural disasters are at the same time economic disasters. The migration-producing scale of a humanitarian catastrophe is not possible without a broader, underlying economic instability.

Without a recognition that the migration crisis reflects a global economic injustice that the West is to a large part responsible for, the false solutions given by Democrats and Republicans will only perpetuate the problem at hand. What is needed is a collective engagement with the humanitarian antagonisms formed by our current political economy. Without this, Newsom is just as much of a threat to social justice and economic stability as Trump himself – Newsom’s liberal democratic openness only serves to provoke the exact populist reaction (concerns of a mass migration wave and the effacement of ‘cultural identities’) that Trump is currently satisfying. The alternative we are seeing today to Trump’s protectionism is a short-term solution, and it will continue to be until it can address the more difficult question of why migration crises exist in the first place.
