With a desperate optimism, today’s liberal voices seem to enthusiastically welcome the signs of internal disorder facing right-wing parties across the world. They welcome these far-right cracks as signs that the right-wing empire is collapsing. If we should learn one thing from recent history, however, it is that internal conflict and inconsistency do not prevent far-right violence, but rather justify it. With the help of psychoanalysis we can even better understand why this liberal optimism is unwarranted.

One of the more baffling psychoanalytic insights from Freud to Klein and Lacan is that the more an unpleasant idea or feared object seems irrational or self-contradictory, the more pathologically effective it becomes as a traumatic agent. Rather than weakening the persecutory function of an unconscious wish or a traumatic phantasmatic object, inconsistency only makes the wish or the object less containable and more subjectively overwhelming.

When an object, however unpleasant, is coherent, we are able to locate and isolate it, to treat it as a stable identity which can be consciously confronted and remedied. But it is in fact the very inability to coherently articulate the identity of an object which, rather than defeating the object by virtue of its inconsistency, makes it impervious to conscious articulation and therefore renders its destructive potential all the more malleable. On the political front, this psychoanalytic fact is making itself unpleasantly clear with today’s populist politics, and it is a lesson which should not be forgotten when we look at the media’s confused response to all of the anomalies emanating from the right-wing camp across the world.

Over the last few weeks, liberal newspapers and commentators in the UK have been getting excited over the apparent (and often very real) cracks in Reform UK, which continues to threaten to mark the end of the UK’s bipartisan politics between Labour and Tories. Through leaked calls and messages, including evidence of backbiting, top-down silencing, and even the break between Farage and the far-right agitator Tommy Robinson, it seems that the UK’s right wing is facing serious struggles and an ironic lack of internal agreement.

Similarly in the US, internal disorder on the political right appears to be making itself present to the media. The Epstein files have led a lot of conservatives to distance themselves from Trump, considering his very likely involvement with Jeffrey Epstein (with even Elon Musk accusing Trump himself of being in the Epstein files). Extreme Christian nationalists have in turn ‘realised’ (as if it was not evident from the beginning) that Trump is not some faithful traditionalist, but a cheap opportunist who said whatever was necessary to win the Midwestern vote. Even Trump’s support of Israel has led Christian right-wing protectionists to abandon him (often with some antisemitic justification).

Nick Fuentes, for example, accused JD Vance of being a race traitor (for having married an Indian American woman) and therefore being unfit to represent the true interests of the right wing (a movement which in Fuentes’ increasingly popular characterisation is sick of Blacks, immigrants, and Jews). It is clear that if the new right is willing to occasionally defend representative democracy, it will defend the most extremist and unrepresentative version of representative democracy possible. What the American right is experiencing has therefore understandably been described as a civil war – although this domestic disorder of the right is certainly not limited to the US. There are other reasons, however, that the term ‘civil war’ is misleading, beginning with the fact that (unlike the basic idea of a civil war) this one is strengthening the right.

What is happening to right-wing populism is in no way a sign that it is in existential trouble. I want to stress that the liberal media is dangerously wrong to be optimistic about these inconsistencies in the modern right, and to characterise them as little more than a civil war. In fact, if we should have learned anything over the last decades it is that right-wing populism does not become weaker when it faces internal disagreement, but rather can become all the more violent. A brief look at the earliest iteration of MAGA already suggests this. At first, Trump was marginalised even in the Republican Party, where the traditionalist mainstream recognised him as an unprincipled opportunist, and he had to constantly struggle against the Senate and Congress. The Never Trump movement of the GOP most concisely summarised this period of Republican heterogeneity. In 2016-2017 the Republicans and the MAGA movement were characterised by deep disagreements and irreconcilable anomalies. This internal disagreement did not, however, signal the downfall of MAGA. Rather, disagreement laid the precise foundations for a more aggressive takeover by the MAGA empire of the US political system.

Other examples of recent right-wing populist movements make this same logic very clear. Jair Bolsonaro – recently sentenced by the Supreme Court to 27 years in prison – was at his most effective (and violently effective) not when his policies received a consensus of general support from his party, but when the far-right Brazilian Liberal Party was plagued with deep internal conflict and opposition to Bolsonaro. When Covid was ravaging Brazil’s population and the State leadership was divided regarding their approach to managing the pandemic, Bolsonaro refused any serious preventative measures (including social distancing and masks), ultimately contributing to over 700,000 deaths.

Having lost the 2022 election to Lula, and with much of the Liberal Party urging for a democratic exchange of power, Bolsonaro was complicit in a coup d-état and planned to use his militant far-right supporters to regain power. Official testimonies even show that Bolsonaro was planning a military ‘self-coup’ as a strategy for securing power. Overall, his Supreme Court conviction included the following charges: armed criminal conspiracy, attempted abolition of the democratic rule of law, attempted coup d’état, violent destruction of public property, and damage to protected national heritage sites. What needs to be stressed is that the violence of Bolsonaro’s populism was made possible not despite, but because of, the cracks in Brazil’s right-wing leadership. Bolsonaro is a testament to what is missed by much liberal optimism when it enthusiastically welcomes the cracks in right-wing populism: it is these very cracks which can easily justify even more aggressive tactics by populist movements.

The modern left’s at times naïve view of the right wing or State authority as effective only when it is in internal agreement is a strange trace of 18th century enlightenment thought and its uniquely isolated revolutionary potential. The spontaneous eruption of Jacobin violence in the French Revolution, for example, was based largely on a sentiment that the old aristocratic regime was failing, that they were too internally disordered to maintain any serious opposition to the will of the masses. The Jacobins may well have been right about this, but the crises faced by modernity are radically different in kind than those experienced by the pre-capitalist French classes. As such, disorder amongst elites or amongst conservative movements is today in no way a guarantee that they can easily be overcome. In fact, this right-wing disorder seems to paradoxically be in service of the right-wing cause.

At its most extreme manifestation, we should recognise that right-wing disorder produces right-wing violence rather than dispels it, as can be seen by looking at the Nazi exterminations. A fact which certain more interesting Holocaust films get right is that the Nazi leadership did not have some all-encompassing general agreement over the extermination of Jews. The Hegelian argument might be true that the mystical ‘secrets of the ancients’ were secrets even to the ancients themselves, but it appears even more true that the secrets of the Nazis were secrets also to many of the Nazis themselves. For even middle and higher officials, where details of the extermination were often kept secret, what was really going on in the concentration camps was a shock.

In psychoanalysis, this paradox is perfectly accounted for by the fact that when the Other is absent, their rule becomes all the more effective. For Freud, paternal authority, the authority constitutive of the law, is never affirmed in the immediate person of the father, but rather when the father has ‘died’ – when there in fact seems to be no real father. Totem and Taboo is Freud’s greatest treatise on this enigma of the law as operative only through an internal rejection of itself, by a fundamental irrationality. Modern authority even in its often-ridiculous right-wing manifestations operates by exactly this logic.

Where the law forms a consistent whole, when it entirely accounts for all of its effects, it becomes all too easy to isolate and critique. In the same way, when the right wing is in a state of homogenous agreement, its flaws become easy to point out. But when the right contradicts itself, when it is heterogeneously marked by internal disagreement and conflict, it becomes far more slippery and opportunist. And it is this inconsistency, as history is continuing to teach us, which permits it to be even more aggressive and more impossible to counter.

Neither MAGA nor Reform UK are simply revivals of Nazism – arguing so is simply a disingenuous disavowal of the real problem of right-wing populism and its equally destructive liberal-democratic precursor – but the argument here is that the 1930s and 40s left would have been entirely wrong to optimistically welcome the signs of heterogeneity and non-agreement in the Nazi party. It is in fact this very internal disorder which permitted some of the greatest Nazi atrocities to proceed unhindered.

Today, then, the enigmas of right-wing populism – those moments where its discourse seems to actively contradict itself – should not be uncritically welcomed as signs that things are going awry in the populist camp. Things may be unstable, but this instability easily lays the foundations for something far worse. The destructive potential of internal conflict and inconsistency should be directly recognised. Even if certain strains of US or UK nationalism and populism do fade away – for example if Reform UK lose and if Brazil are allowed a few years of democratic elections – the seeds are already firmly planted for something far worse to take their place.
