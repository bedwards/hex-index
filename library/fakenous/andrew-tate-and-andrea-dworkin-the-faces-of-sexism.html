Here, I consider why there are woman-haters and man-haters, how the two are similar, and where both go wrong.

1. Gender Resentment

Many people harbor resentment or disdain towards the opposite sex. There are many women who basically think that men are bad—exploitative oppressors, always on the verge of committing rape and mayhem, chauvinistic, self-centered, etc. On the other hand, there are many men who basically think that women are bad—untrustworthy, gold-diggers, incompetent, mentally unstable, etc. Both groups commonly find the opposite sex to be cold-hearted and exploitative.

Aside: “Men are exploitative” is an example of what they call a “generic” in philosophy of language, a generalization in which the quantifier is unspecified. (Are we talking about all men, some men, most men …?) Other examples include “dogs are smarter than cats” and “mosquitos carry the West Nile virus.” Often, it’s just a statement about averages or majorities, but not always. Only a small percentage of mosquitos actually carry West Nile, yet we can still say “mosquitos carry the virus.”

In our example: perhaps “men are exploitative” means that the frequency of exploitative behavior by men is more than one would expect, or significantly more than that found in women, or something like that.

The callousness that the two sexes complain about tends to take different forms. Women tend to complain that men lie to get sex, pretend to be interested in a serious relationship, then leave as soon as they get sex. On the other hand, men complain that women cheat on their partners.

These things happen partly because there are differences in what each sex finds upsetting; thus, each sex fails to grasp how incredibly upsetting these behaviors are to the other sex. They also happen partly due to individuals not caring about others’ perspectives or what effect they have on other people.

So there’s a sexist guy; so what?

Obviously, it’s not just one guy. There are many men who harbor hostility or prejudice toward women. This is why Tate has millions of followers—10 million on Twitter, 12 million on Instagram. So Andrew Tate’s following is roughly a thousand times mine. Of course, he also has many people who hate him or have no interest in him; I don’t know how to quantify that.

There must be millions of people who substantially agree with him, though they may be afraid to say so in public under their own names.

The flip side of misogyny is misandry, hatred of or prejudice toward men. Some quotes:

“[M]ale pleasure is inextricably tied to victimizing, hurting, exploiting…” (Andrea Dworkin)

“The proportion of men must be reduced to and maintained at approximately 10% of the human race.” (Sally Miller Gearhart)

“I feel that ‘man-hating’ is an honorable and viable political act, that the oppressed have a right to class-hatred against the class that is oppressing them.” (Robin Morgan)

“Any man will follow any feminine looking thing down any dark alley; I’ve always wanted to see a man beaten to a shit bloody pulp with a high-heeled shoe stuffed up his mouth, sort of the pig with the apple….” (Narrator in Andrea Dworkin’s novel, Mercy, p. 327)

Comparisons

I’m not going to try to determine which of these things, misogyny or misandry, we have more of, nor which one is worse, as that is a waste of time. I’ll rest with the observation that these phenomena are very similar, and we have a lot of both.

They differ in their spheres of influence. Tate-style misogyny is mainly found on the internet and social media, in low-status forums. Dworkin-style misandry is found more in high-status, elite forums, such as university courses and books and magazines from big publishers. The misandrist authors quoted above are still read in gender theory and feminism courses.

Three of Andrea Dworkin’s books have recently been re-issued as Penguin Classics, 20 years after her death. I don’t know how many copies they have sold, but it must be a lot. There must be millions of people who substantially agree with her.

Depending on your political orientation, you may be more worried about one side of the coin. If you live in the academic world, or elite cultural circles more generally, you probably hear a lot more winging about misogyny, while you see more instances of misandry, which almost no one ever calls out.

If you’re part of that world, you might not even notice the casual sexism all over the place because you’re trained to only see sexism against females. Statements that would shock you with their sexism would pass unnoticed if the sexes were swapped. For example, in job searches in the academic world, it is perfectly commonplace to say that we should try to hire a woman for such-and-such position, or to cite femaleness as an advantage of a particular candidate. It would be shocking, and illegal, for someone to say the same thing with “man” substituted for “woman.” (Of course, it is illegal and immoral both ways.)

Incidentally, people in the academic world can go directly from a meeting in which the importance of discriminating in favor of women was openly discussed, to loudly complaining about how the profession is so horribly biased against women, with no sign of awareness of the incongruity.

How can you tell whether you’re the oppressed group or the group in power? Here is a hint: if high-status people in your group are frequently, openly going on about their membership in group X, the importance of supporting group X because of the travails X-members have to endure, and/or their personal support for X-members, then X is not an oppressed group. X is a favored group. If they were oppressed, people would downplay their membership in X. The people who didn’t do that would lose status and would not be in coveted positions.

In the 1950’s, for example, black people applying for jobs did not emphasize their race. If applying by mail, they would not include a statement talking about being black. That’s because they would be discriminated against. Today, for many elite jobs, especially in academia, people absolutely advertise any minority status.

2. Feminism = Reverse Sexism

We don’t have to argue that people like Andrew Tate are sexists; that fact is generally acknowledged, including by the people themselves.

What is less well acknowledged is the second sexism, as David Benatar calls it, sexism against men or in favor of women. The name for people who support this kind of sexism is “feminists.” Just as wokism is reverse bigotry (bigotry directed in the reverse direction from the traditional forms of bigotry), feminism is reverse sexism. Most of these sexists, however, refuse to acknowledge what they are and actually deny that feminism is a form of sexism.

When I was in graduate school, I took a course in feminist ethics. The professor defined “feminism” at the outset as something like the view that men and women have equal rights, or should be treated equally, or something like that—something that everyone in elite circles takes for granted. I was surprised to learn thereby that I and everyone I knew (including conservatives) had been “feminists” all along.

I soon recognized this as part of a motte-and-bailey strategy. Feminism was initially defined as an anodyne, almost entirely uncontroversial view so that we’d all have to identify as “feminists.” But then when the feminist philosophers start talking about “feminist ethics,” “feminist epistemology,” or the specifics of other “feminist” views, it becomes clear that something much more controversial is going on. If feminism is just the view that men and women are equals, then how is there a “feminist epistemology”—how do you get a theory of knowledge out of that anodyne starting point?

What actually happens in feminist literature is that one attacks men, complains about how bad things are for women, extols things that one views as feminine (women’s ways of knowing, women’s approach to ethics, etc.), advocates for the (perceived) interests of women as against those of men, and advances other left-wing political views. None of that falls out of a belief in equality.

(By the way, some of this involves taking on board stereotypes that earlier feminists would find sexist. E.g., feminist ethics is an “ethics of care” because women’s approach to ethics is allegedly based on caring rather than on reasoning using abstract principles.)

If there were a literature devoted to talking about how women are constantly abusing men, how men’s ways of doing things are better than women’s, and advocating for the interests of men, we would have no trouble classifying that as a sexist literature. You know, like the content produced by Andrew Tate and his followers.

Now, you might wonder how fair the misandry quotes I produced above are. You might think: “Oh, that’s just a few extremists. Most feminists are not like that!”

Sure, I deliberately took quotes from a few extremists. Just as I did with the misogynist quotes—I deliberately picked offensive comments from an extreme male chauvinist. If you nevertheless found the misogynistic comments concerning, you should also find the misandrist comments concerning. Both are concerning because these people have a wide reach. Millions of people listen to Tate, and millions of people also listen to man-hating authors such as Dworkin. Dworkin really is completely in good standing in elite cultural circles (as Tate is not).

If I said we needn’t worry about anti-female sexism because few men are as extreme as Andrew Tate, I think people would reject this argument. For one thing, milder forms of sexism are still objectionable—e.g., if someone just thinks we should give preference to men over women in hiring decisions, almost everyone in elite circles would consider that completely unacceptable. But the analogous view, with “men” and “women” reversed, is held by almost everyone in the academic world, and pretty much every self-described feminist holds more extreme views than that. Sexism is not an occasional excess of the more extreme feminists. Sexism is the core of feminism.

3. Understanding Modern Sexism

Human beings are very prone to prejudice in favor of their own group and against other groups. Few people, however, hate another race or another nation with the venom of today’s man-haters and woman-haters. Today’s sexists, I believe, are also more bitter than the sexists of previous generations.

Why is there this phenomenon, and where have these people gone wrong?

Basically, people are selfish. Not 100% selfish (most people are not sociopaths), but really very selfish. E.g., most people would not donate $1000 to save someone else’s life. In their personal interactions, people think almost entirely about their own needs and desires, and hardly at all about the other person’s needs and desires.

This is true in all sorts of relationships. E.g., your boss at work probably hardly thinks at all about your interests. It’s probably also true of you. You probably hardly think at all about your boss’ interests, or your coworkers’, or your neighbors’. Usually, though, things work out okay because we are influenced by social norms, which prohibit us from directly harming other people.

But there is one area of life where things regularly go badly wrong—that is with sexual and romantic relationships. Why?

  • a) People have particularly strong emotions and desires in this area. This is for evolutionary reasons: for evolution, reproduction is the #1 priority, so it gives us strong feelings and desires related to that.

  • b) People are regularly interacting with classes of people (the opposite sex) who have large systematic psychological differences from themselves. Feelings and desires connected to reproduction is the one area of psychology where sex differences are largest. (See this post about sex differences.)

  • c) We don’t have well-developed or enforced norms concerning this kind of relationship. We have prohibitions on some obviously harmful behavior (rape, sexual assault), but we have no prohibitions on, say, lying to get sex, or hitting on one of your friends’ partners, or leaving your partner for one of his friends, or

    saying cruel things to members of the opposite sex. Not only don’t we have legal sanctions for these things; we don’t even have significant social sanctions, nor do we try to teach young people not to do these things.

Combine those factors with the general human callousness and selfishness, and you have a recipe for a lot of pain in human life. Many people have repeated negative experiences with the opposite sex because these factors just continue to apply, leading to bitterness toward the opposite sex as a whole.

Now, these are the mistakes that misandrists and misogynists are making:

  1. They think the problem is tied to a particular sex. But the central problem is human callousness. People are very selfish; neither sex is obviously much worse than the other. We all evolved by natural selection. It’s just that you don’t feel your own sex’s callousness because (assuming you’re heterosexual) you never enter romantic relationships with them, so you never get the chance to be harmed by them in this particular area. But if you tried it, you’d find your own sex to be assholes too.

  2. They think the opposite sex behaves particularly badly in sexual or romantic relationships. Actually, people are constantly being callous toward each other in all contexts. It’s just that it doesn’t hurt as much in most other contexts. Most people are almost entirely amoral.

  3. They only look at other people. Part of our self-centeredness is that we are constantly thinking about how others have harmed us, but seldom about how we have harmed others. When you realize that people of both sexes are assholes, you should also start to suspect that you, too, are an asshole.

Conclusion: Don’t hate the opposite sex. Hate people in general, including probably yourself.

No, wait, don’t really hate them, but recognize their flaws. Don’t expect people to treat you decently when it isn’t in their interests and there aren’t strong social norms.

Try to be kind (without exposing yourself to abuse), particularly to the opposite sex, and particularly to people who have emotional attachments to you. Because those are the cases where it matters most.

You have to do this even if you don’t want to, even if you you have stopped caring about the other person or never cared about them. You don’t just have obligations to people when you feel attached to them. You have obligations to everyone whom you can hurt. We don’t teach this to people, but we should teach it as part of basic moral education.
