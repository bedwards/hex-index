Objections to Direct Realism

By Michael Huemer

Here, I rebut objections to direct realism.*

[ *Based on: Skepticism & the Veil of Perception (Rowman & Littlefield, 2001), ch. 6. ]

There are many objections to direct realism that the proponents of the objections seem to think are really great but that are in fact not great at all. Many of the objectors seem to have little idea of what direct realism is and don’t bother to try to define it. If you don’t know what it is, see my earlier post, “A Version of Direct Realism”. I won’t here repeat that account but will just launch into the objections.

1. The Argument from Perspective

David Hume’s “refutation” of direct realism:

But this universal and primary opinion of all men is soon destroyed by the slightest philosophy, which teaches us, that nothing can ever be present to the mind but an image or perception …. The table which we see seems to diminish as we remove farther from it. But the real table, which exists independent of us, suffers no alteration. It was, therefore, nothing but its image which was present to the mind. These are the obvious dictates of reason, and no man who reflects ever doubted that the existences which we consider when we say this house and that tree, are nothing but perceptions in the mind and fleeting copies or representations of other existences which remain uniform and independent. [Enquiry, XII.1, punctuation modernized]

It looks like the syllogism is:

  1. The table we see seems to diminish when we move away from it.

  2. The real table does not diminish.

  3. Therefore, the table we see is not the real table.

This is logically invalid. There is no contradiction in an object seeming to do x without doing x, so there is no reason why [the table we see] may not be identical to [the real table].

Thomas Reid suggested that one is directly, visually aware of the angular magnitude of the table, rather than its linear magnitude. This is the size of the angle that the table subtends at the eye. The angular magnitude changes as you move away from the table, so there is not even any illusion here. The angular magnitude of the table is a relational property, but it is a perfectly objective, physical feature, not something in the mind, so there is no support for indirect realism here.

2. The Argument from Illusion

Take a genuine illusion: a straight stick, half-submerged in water, appears bent. Here’s an argument:

  1. In this case, you are directly aware of something that is bent.

  2. No relevant physical object is bent in this case.

  3. So you must be directly aware of a non-physical object.

Reply: You are not aware of anything that is bent. You merely see something (the stick) that appears bent but is in fact straight. There is nothing inconsistent in that, and there is no need to introduce a non-physical object of awareness.

Perhaps the argument could be strengthened by adding the premise:

1a. If you are directly aware of x, then x must be exactly as it appears in all respects.

This would rule out being directly aware of a physical object that appears bent but isn’t. However, the indirect realist could not accept 1a, since 1a supports the following argument for direct realism:

1a. If you are directly aware of x, then x must be exactly as it appears in all respects.

2a. In perception, the things you are directly aware of appear to be physical objects.

3a. Therefore, in perception, the things you are directly aware of are physical objects.

3. The Argument from Hallucination, part 1

Suppose you have a perfectly realistic hallucination of a sloth. The indirect realist would argue that in this case, you are directly aware of a mental image of a sloth (obviously not a physical sloth, since there is no physical sloth in the offing). If so, it’s plausible that such an image also occurs during normal sloth perception, in which case it is also plausible that you would be directly aware of that image. It is then plausible that that image would be the only object of direct awareness in normal perception.

Reply: A hallucination is not a state of awareness of anything, as I define awareness (see “A Version of Direct Realism”). A hallucination is merely a state that seems like awareness of something but is not, just as a false belief may seem to the believer like knowledge but is not in fact knowledge of anything.

4. The Argument from Hallucination, part 2

  1. When hallucinating a sloth, one has the same justification for believing that a sloth is present as one has when normally perceiving a sloth.

  2. When hallucinating a sloth, one’s justification for believing a sloth is present does not consist of one’s being directly aware of a sloth.

  3. Therefore, during normal perception, one’s justification for believing a sloth is present also does not consist of one’s being directly aware of a sloth.

(Based on Richard Fumerton, Metaphysical and Epistemological Problems of Perception.)

Reply: I agree with (3). One’s justification for believing a sloth is present, in both cases, consists of its seeming to one that a sloth is present. See my earlier posts: Compassionate Phenomenal Conservatism, Phenomenal Conservatism and the Internalist Intuition.

5. The Double Vision Argument

You can experience double vision by pushing on one eyeball in a certain way, or simply attending to an object that is out of focus in your visual field. An argument for indirect realism:

  1. In the case of double vision, you see two of something.

  2. There are not two physical objects.

  3. So you must be seeing two mental things.

Reply: (1) is false. In a case of double vision, one sees a single object, which falsely appears in two places (or: one sees the same object twice).

6. The Time Gap Argument

There is always a time delay between when an event happens in the physical world and when you experience it. In some cases, you might even see an object that no longer exists — e.g., a star could have burned out a thousand years ago but (if it is more than a thousand light-years away) still be visible in the sky. In such a case, you might think (?) you must be seeing a mere mental image. If so, it’s plausible that that is always what you’re seeing.

Reply: You can see a physical object as it was in the past (usually a very short time in the past, but sometimes long ago, as in the case of distant stars). There is no need to say that you see something non-physical.

7. The Causal Argument

There are causal intermediaries between the physical objects we perceive and our experiences of them. E.g., light must travel through the air, cells in the eye must be stimulated, and the brain must process the information from those cells. Therefore, our perception of the physical object is not “direct”.

Reply: This is just a confusion about the issue. See again my post, “A Version of Direct Realism”. The issue between direct & indirect realism concerns whether one’s awareness of physical objects is based upon one’s awareness of mental objects. It is not about whether there are any causal processes between the object and your experience (which is not controversial).

8. The Argument from Secondary Qualities

Some philosophers believe that secondary qualities, such as colors, smells, and sounds, are illusory, and that only the properties used in theoretical physics are part of physical reality (e.g., mass, charge, spatial and temporal properties).

Focusing on colors, the indirect realist may argue:

  1. We only ever see things with colors.

    1. Comment: We see objects through the contrast between the object’s color and that of the background.

  2. There are no colors in the objective, physical world.

  3. So what we see is not part of the objective, physical world. (From 1, 2.)

    1. Comment: So it must be that we are seeing mental images, or something like that.

There are several arguments for (2). Some have to do with the variations in color perceptions among people or among different species. Some point to hypothetical variations in color experiences, such as the inverted color spectrum. Some skeptics point out that fundamental scientific explanations of everything we observe can be given using only primary qualities (including theoretical physical properties such as electric charge). And some argue that there is no single, physical property in common among all the objects that appear to us to have a single color.

I won’t go into those, as it would take too long. Suffice it to say that some smart people hold that view.

Reply:

a) I think a better view is that colors of physical objects are (identical with) sets of spectral reflectance distributions. These are complex dispositional properties of objects to reflect different wavelengths of light to different degrees. For defense of this view, see David Hilbert. This enables us to reject premise (2) above.

b) I also think premise (1) could be false, particularly if (2) is true. Suppose physical objects don’t really have colors. They still have spectral reflectance distributions, though, which enable us to discriminate objects from their surroundings. If, for philosophical reasons, these spectral reflectance distributions don’t count as colors, then there is something illusory about our perceptions of objects. However, it would nevertheless not be the case that the objects don’t exist at all or are not perceived at all.

Compare: Suppose you see a white bear that happens to be illuminated by orange light, so it looks like an orange bear. In this case, you do not see the actual color of the bear. But it is false to say that you don’t see the bear. This shows that you can see an object even when its apparent color is illusory. (You couldn’t be seeing the bear if nothing about it was as it seemed, though. E.g., if it looked to you as if there was an orange motorcycle, then that would not count as your seeing the white bear. But if you are reliably tracking the bear’s spatial properties, then you count as seeing the bear.)

Conclusion

Hume was wrong. None of the objections to direct realism are compelling, and many reflective people hold that perception gives us direct awareness of physical objects.
