Here, I argue that the past is infinite.*

1. Objections to the Infinite Past

1.1. Completed, Actual Infinities

Some people argue that the past cannot be infinite because

(i) There cannot be an actual infinity. If the past were to be infinite, it would be an actual infinity since every event becomes actual as of the time it happens (and stays actual). On this view, it’s okay to have an infinite future, because the future is never fully actualized, but all past events are fully actualized.

Or:

(ii) An infinite series cannot be completed. (Imagine the absurd idea of “counting to infinity,” or counting through all the natural numbers.) If the past were to be infinite, then the series of past events would constitute, as of now, a completed infinite series.

Why think there cannot be an actual infinity? Aristotle and Aquinas’ argument was basically this:

  1. Everything actual must be fully determinate.

  2. Infinity is not a determinate quantity (and is not compatible with having a determinate quantity).

  3. Therefore, there cannot be an actual infinite.

People also like to cite paradoxes of the infinite, which allegedly can and should be avoided by denying the possibility of actual infinities. William Lane Craig likes to cite Hilbert’s Hotel:

Assume there is a hotel with infinitely many rooms, all of which are filled. A new guest shows up. Can he be accommodated (without kicking anyone out)? Yes. You tell each guest to move to room number (n+1), where n is their current room #. This leaves Room #1 free for the new guest.

Then an infinite number of new guests shows up. They can also be accommodated: move each guest to room #2n, where n is their current room number. This leaves rooms #1, 3, 5, and so on free for the new guests.

It’s counter-intuitive that you should be able to accommodate infinitely many additional guests in a hotel that is already full. To avoid this, we’re supposed to say “actual infinities are impossible”.

Replies:

(a) This view is refuted by Zeno’s Paradox. Two variants of Zeno’s paradox:

Zeno’s endless series: For an object to move from point A to point B, it must first go half the distance, then half the remaining distance, and so on.

Zeno’s beginningless series: For an object to move from point A to point B, it must first go half the distance. But before it does that, it must go 1/4 the distance, and before that 1/8 the distance, etc.

These are infinite series, yet they are completed every time an object moves.

They are also actual infinities. Every step is actually completed by the time the object reaches its destination; they are not merely capable of being completed. So completed, actual infinities are possible after all.

(b) What’s wrong with Aristotle & Aquinas’ argument?

To say that the past is infinite, we need not say that there is any particular attribute of any particular thing whose measure is infinity. Rather, we need only say such things as “for every time, there is a time before it” and “for any duration d, there is a past duration longer than d.” We don’t have to say there is a duration called “infinity”.

(c) What about Hilbert’s Hotel?

I think the (allegedly) counterintuitive result is correct. It just is the case that you could put all the new guests into rooms, and still have (different) rooms for the old guests.

For solutions to other paradoxes of infinity, see my book, Approaching Infinity. They don’t require denying the possibility of “actual infinities”; one need only deny the possibility of infinite natural, intensive magnitudes, and there is a natural theoretical rationale for this.

1.2. The Big Bang Theory

The traditional understanding of the Big Bang Theory (as held, e.g., by Stephen Hawking) was that time itself began at the time of the Big Bang ~14 billion years ago, so it makes no sense to ask what happened before the Big Bang. Maybe philosophers should shut up and listen to the physicists?

Replies

(a) There are also cyclical models in cosmology that have an infinite past. E.g., Roger Penrose’s Conformal Cyclic Cosmology.

(b) I think the Hawking version of Big Bang cosmology, with a beginning of time, is a bad theory because it proposes an extremely improbable initial state happening for no reason. I.e., the universe just starts out in an extremely low-entropy state, with a huge amount of energy concentrated in a tiny region, moving outward at the first moment of time. There could be no causal explanation for this initial state, since that is allegedly the first thing that ever happened. Penrose calculates that the probability of having such a low-entropy state, if you picked a possible state of the universe at random, is 1 in 10^10^124. It is not satisfying to posit such an improbable thing happening for no reason.

I have a better theory: maybe the universe appeared in the year 1950, already in the state it was in fact in at that time, for no reason. This is actually more likely than the standard Big Bang theory, because the entropy of the universe in 1950 was much higher than what it was at the time of the Big Bang, which means that the 1950 state is more intrinsically probable than the Big Bang state.

I assume you find the 1950 Theory implausible. If so, you should also find the Hawking Big Bang theory implausible. (This is not to say a Big Bang didn’t happen; it is to say that it didn’t just start out like that for no reason. Some sort of explanation is required.)

2. For an Infinite Past

2.1. Intuitions About Limits

Imagine someone proposes that there is an edge of space. You go up to the edge of space. If you try to stick your hand out past the edge, maybe your hand would just stop, no matter how hard you push. However, it still seems that you could ask about what is on the other side of this invisible barrier that you’ve hit. Intuitively, it seems that there must be an answer to that, even if the answer is that there is nothing (no objects) there. But then there is still a there there, even if nothing can go there. It seems impossible that the location itself isn’t there.

Similarly, imagine someone proposes that time just started. It seems that you could still ask about what happened before this, and there must be an answer to this, even if the answer is that nothing (no events) was happening. It seems impossible for the time itself to not be there.

Btw, the idea of a beginning of time is not formally contradictory; nor is that of an edge of space. There is a coherent mathematical structure for time with a beginning—e.g., the mathematical structure of the non-negative real numbers. Likewise, there is a coherent mathematical structure for a space with an edge—e.g., the mathematical structure of a sphere. It is just that, intuitively, it seems that these things could not be the structure of space and time.

2.2. What Could Explain the Beginning of Time?

Apropos of the point about the Big Bang and the 1950 Theory: it seems that if we posit a beginning of time, we should want an explanation of why time began. (Why? Not everything requires an explanation. But something like the beginning of time is weird enough that it seems like it needs an explanation. Especially if it started with the universe in a really improbable state.) But there could not be an explanation of this.

God

Sometimes, people say that God, existing “outside time,” created time. Does this explain it?

It does not. Even God could not do this. Creation is an action, and actions occur in time. If there was no time, or if God was not in time, then God could not perform any actions.

Creation is also a causal notion (to create is to cause something to exist). But causation also requires time; if time didn’t already exist, then nothing could cause anything.

Creation is also a kind of change, specifically, a change from a state where a thing does not exist, to a state where the thing exists. To create time would therefore require there to first be a time when time didn’t exist, followed by a time when time exists. But there could not be a time when time didn’t exist.

Possible reply: Maybe God’s action of creating time occurs simultaneously with the first instant. So time exists as of the time He performs this action, so that avoids the objection that actions require time to exist.

Reply: The problem is one of metaphysical priority. The ability to act depends upon one’s existing in time. So it cannot also be that the existence of time depends upon some particular action. (Dependence relations cannot be circular.)

Secular causes

If you buy the above argument about God, you should also agree that nothing else could create time if time didn’t already exist. No event or state of affairs (whether an action by a conscious being or not) could cause time to begin.

It therefore seems that there could not be any explanation for the beginning of time. It seems that any explanation must cite some state of affairs of some kind—some thing or things existing and being some way. It also seems that there would have to be a time at which that state of affairs obtained, in order for it to explain anything. So any explanation would presuppose the existence of time.

Aside: Are there any facts that are outside time? Well, maybe purely abstract facts, such as the fact that 7 is a prime number, are outside time (there is no time at which that “happens”). However, such purely abstract facts could not explain something contingent and concrete like the beginning of time.

2.3. Past/future symmetry

Almost everyone agrees that there can’t be an end of time; time must continue forever into the future. (Btw, even if all physical processes freeze, that won’t be an end of time; that will just be a time when things are static.)

It’s not inconsistent to hold an asymmetric view, where you posit a beginning but not an end of time. But it’s odd. If you agree that it is impossible that time has an end, wouldn’t it be similarly impossible for time to have a beginning?

The “no actual infinity” people say that the future is an infinite potential, whereas the past is actual, and that’s why their view rules out an infinite past but not an infinite future. However, this wouldn’t explain why an end of time is impossible; it only explains why an end of time isn’t required by the “no actual infinity” view.

If you think an end of time is impossible (which I assume most people think, including the people who posit a beginning of time), it’s probably on intuitive grounds. If so, I suggest that there is a parallel intuition about a beginning of time. When you think about the alleged last moment of time, it just seems that there must be an answer to what happens after this moment. Just as, when you think about the alleged first moment of time, it just seems that there must be an answer to what happened before it.
