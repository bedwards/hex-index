I was listening to a podcast recently, where the guest related that his father had once told him, “I don’t care if you have wealth or professional success. I care if you have honor.” What is honor, such that it could make sense for a parent to cite it as the sole or primary thing he wants for his child?

## 1\. Examples

The best way to start analyzing anything is to look for examples. A variety of things elicit intuitions about honor or dishonor.

### a. Footrace

First, an example of honorable behavior. This happened in 2012. The first place runner in a footrace was confused about where the finish line was and stopped short of it. The second place runner could have taken advantage of the mistake to pass him and claim the first place. Instead, he pointed out the correct finish line to the first runner, then ran behind him until he crossed. When asked why he did this, the 2nd place runner explained, “He was the rightful winner. He created a gap that I could not have closed if he had not made a mistake.” (I learned of this example from a talk by [Dan Demetriou](https://morris.umn.edu/directory/dan-demetriou), who has written a lot about honor.)

### b. Piet Retief Massacre

This happened in 1838 in South Africa. Dutch settlers (led by Piet Retief) wanted to make peace with the Zulu living in the area. The Zulu required that the Dutch retrieve some cattle that had been stolen by another tribe. The Dutch settlers retrieved about 700 cattle from the other tribe and returned them to the Zulu. They then signed a treaty with the Zulu, granting the Dutch a certain amount of land to live on.

A few days later, the Zulu king invited the Dutch to a feast in their honor, but the Dutch had to leave their weapons outside. They did this, then ate and drank with the Zulu. Then the Zulu king started chanting, “Kill the wizards.” The Zulu fell upon the Dutch and massacred them.

This must be among the most dishonorable things that has ever happened.

### c. Judas

Another famous case is Judas’ act of betraying Jesus in exchange for 30 pieces of silver.

Which is more dishonorable, example (b) or (c)? (c) might be deemed more dishonorable because Judas was a friend and follower of Jesus and because he did it solely for material gain, rather than (as the Zulu king may have thought) to protect his people. On the other hand, the Zulu had just accepted the 700 cattle from the Dutch as the price of peace, invited them to a feast, and attacked them while they were unarmed.

### d. Malcolm Reynolds

Some dialogue from *Firefly*, “Serenity”:

Simon: “How do I know you won’t kill me in my sleep?”

Mal: “You don’t know me, son, so let me explain this to you once: If I ever kill you, you’ll be awake. You’ll be facing me. And you’ll be armed.”

### e. Honesty

We have “honor codes” in universities that have to do with not cheating or lying. We also have the expression “to give one’s word of honor,” which associates honor with honesty and promise-keeping.

### f. A woman’s honor

There is also the notion of “defending a woman’s honor”, where “honor” appears to refer to chastity. This usage is related to so-called “honor killings”, in which people murder a woman for lack of chastity, or even for being a victim of rape. It appears that the killers think they have to kill the woman because she has lost her “honor”.

### g. Honor cultures

There is some famous work on “honor cultures” in anthropology. This mainly refers to cultures in which people have swift and violent responses to any perceived insult or injury.

### h. Honors and awards

Sometimes, we refer to prizes or ceremonies designed to recognize achievements as “honors”. E.g., the Nobel prize is the greatest honor in science.

## 2\. Threads

It is not easy to find common threads in all of the above examples. What does sportsmanship have to do with killing rape victims? Perhaps there are just multiple different senses of “honor”?

Nevertheless, let’s look for some threads (see [Demetriou’s encyclopedia entry about honor](https://philarchive.org/rec/DEMQDO) for more on these threads).

### a. Fair competition

One recurring thread is fairness of competition or combat (see cases 1a, 1b, and 1d above). The second-place runner in 1a wants to win the race, so why doesn’t he seize the opportunity provided by the other guy’s confusion? Because it would be a cheap, merely technical victory. He wants to win by being the best runner, not by the other guy not knowing where the finish line is. If he won in the latter way, it wouldn’t mean anything. He would always know that he did not earn it.

This kind of norm may serve to limit the destruction caused by competition and conflict and to make competition more socially beneficial. In peaceful competitions, it is best for society if the “best person” wins, i.e., the person who is actually best at the activity the competition is about. Perhaps this helps to explain why many societies value honor?

In the case of violent conflict, it is better if we have some limits, as in the norm of not killing people in their sleep. If people could do that, this would make killing people too easy. It’s better for everyone if we can all go to sleep without worrying about being murdered. (Of course, it would be even better if we never had to worry about being murdered at all.)

In war as well, there should be some limits (e.g., no killing women and children), and there has to be a way to end the conflict. In case 1b, the Piet Retief massacre, the behavior of the Zulu meant that there could be no peace negotiations, and no limits; it could only be an all-out war to the death.

Academic “honor codes” are connected to fair competition as well. You can view students as competing with each other for academic recognition. Even if the class is not graded on a curve, it remains the case that the value of a grade depends on how other people are doing (if everyone gets an A, then your A doesn’t mean anything), so you are effectively in competition with all other students. Fair competition requires that students not cheat; hence, cheating is dishonorable.

One might think of life more generally as filled with competition. In your career, you’re to some extent competing with other people in your line of work. In your love life, you are perhaps competing with other members of your sex. Etc. Almost every goal-seeking activity has some competitive element. So perhaps fair competition norms can be extended broadly. You can think of things like dishonesty, promise-breaking, and betrayal as “cheating” in the general competition of life. If you attain success through such means, it is a meaningless victory; it doesn’t show that you have actual merit. And again, it is better for everyone if we have limits on the competition, if we can make and accept promises, etc.

(I still can’t connect this to chastity; that just seems like a completely different use of “honor”.)

### b. Prestige

Another thread is that honor has to do with social recognition and respect; hence we “bestow honors” on people. Perhaps “honor” means that which is worthy of social recognition and respect. The diversity of the examples in sec. 1 above would then simply reflect the variety of things that are deemed worthy of respect in different societies.

> *Note:* Societies can be misguided, so what is honored in a given society need not be genuinely honorable. For example, some societies honor skillful raiding of neighboring tribes and stealing of their stuff, which I don’t consider at all honorable. Likewise, many societies censure female promiscuity, which I don’t consider at all dishonorable.

But this seems too broad. We bestow “honors” on people for, e.g., being really good at science, and that seems appropriate. But being good at science does not make one honorable or “a man of honor”. So again, it seems that we just have different uses of the word “honor”.

Perhaps “honor” in the aretaic sense (the sense in which it is a moral virtue, as in “a person of honor”) refers to a subset of the *traits of character* that are worthy of respect. Which character traits are included? Integrity, courage, loyalty, and honesty are all part of honor. But kindness and temperance, while admirable, are not part of honor. I am not sure why.

### c. Emotional reactions

Perhaps “honorable” or “dishonorable” acts are unified by the emotional reactions they elicit or ought to elicit. Though kindness and temperance are admirable traits, they don’t seem to call forth quite the same emotional reaction as the virtues of, say, courage and loyalty. Likewise, failures of courage and loyalty seem to call forth a different sort of emotional reaction from the normal reaction to failures of kindness or temperance.

It’s not easy to say exactly what these reactions are. It is natural to cite shame or contempt as the emotional reaction to dishonor (depending on whether the dishonor is one’s own or someone else’s). However, shame and contempt also seem like apt responses to some failures of temperance, yet these are not thereby dishonorable.

Perhaps honor, like most other things, is undefinable, yet we have an intuitive sense of it.

## 3\. The Neglect of Honor

Part of why the topic of honor interests me is that I see honor being neglected in our society. I don’t think we teach children about honor anymore. (I assume that we used to do so, but I don’t know the history.) Moral philosophers theorize endlessly about justice, wellbeing, and autonomy, but hardly ever about honor. Some of the examples of honor from the past are almost incomprehensible to modern readers—e.g., a military commander waiting for the enemy army to finish crossing a river before attacking, so as to ensure a fair fight. I am not necessarily saying that one *should* do that. But something is lost in a society that no longer understands why someone would do that.

Conservatives have traditionally cared more about honor than progressives (who would put more emphasis on justice and equality). But I’m not sure that even the right wing of our current society cares much about honor any more. Now it seems that both leftists and rightists just want to “win” at all costs. It is a value system for shallow people.

In American universities, and I assume the same is true of the corporate world, it often seems that people’s main desire is to cover their own asses—not necessarily to *faithfully follow* the rules imposed by the authority figures, but to act in such a way as to *not get caught* for breaking them. Example: we illegally discriminate in hiring all the time, but we avoid *putting in writing* our discriminatory plans and we try to hide who is responsible.

I don’t know the ultimate consequences for society. I don’t know whether society can continue to function with people motivated by such things as *the desire to avoid getting in trouble*, with no internal sense of honor. But even if society continues to function like that, I think this is a recipe for a lot of shabby, ignominious lives led by people who are not worth knowing.
