Sometimes, we switch jobs, change partners, or move to a different city in search of something new, but once the initial excitement subsides, we realize that very little has changed on a deeper level: We are the same people. Our inner life has not been fundamentally altered. We are still prone – as much as we ever were – to negative thoughts, anxiety, or saying things we don’t mean in the midst of an angry outburst. And we know then that it was not so much a city or a job or a person we were running away from, but ourselves.

Can we escape from ourselves?

We can try to forget ourselves by becoming fully absorbed in an activity – an engrossing novel, a game, or a creative project. Some opt, alternatively, to take various drugs that can alter one’s states of consciousness.

Such techniques can provide a respite, but the effects are temporary. At some point, the absorption phase is over, and we are left to our own devices, alone with our thoughts, and we run into our old selves once again. It is as though there are parts of us that act as intruders occupying our own minds. We want to discard those parts, and we may succeed in temporarily silencing them, but while we can win an individual battle against them, we seem unable to win the war. The intruders invariably come back. One might say, pace Sartre, that hell is not other people: Other people we can choose to avoid. Hell is the place we can never get away from no matter where we go: our own minds. Or at any rate, our minds’ oppressive side.

The nameless protagonist of Dostoyevsky’s existentialist novel Notes from Underground suggests a different strategy: embracing yourself and taking solace in the realization that you cannot change. At one point the narrator goes so far as to say that after struggling for years to overcome his own darker side, he came to rejoice (yes!) in his own depravity because he saw it as inescapable:

I got to the point of feeling a sort of secret abnormal, despicable enjoyment in returning home … acutely conscious that that day, I had committed a loathsome action again, that what was done could never be undone, and secretly inwardly gnawing, gnawing at myself for it, tearing and consuming myself till at last, the bitterness turned into a sort of shameful accursed sweetness, and at last – into positive real enjoyment. Yes, into enjoyment, enjoyment! I insist upon that... The enjoyment was just from the too intense consciousness of one’s own degradation; it was from feeling oneself that one had reached the last barrier, that it was horrible, but that it could not be otherwise; that there was no escape for you; that you never could become a different man; that even if time and faith were still left you to change into something different you would most likely not wish to change; or if you did wish to, even then you would do nothing.[1]

This type of solace – or if you wish, resignation – is generally open only to those who believe they cannot change. The key here is to go from “It is difficult” to “It is impossible.” It is likely that even a resigned person does not see personality and character transformation as unachievable. For this reason, the joy of passivity can hardly ever be complete. There would be that nagging suspicion that we gave up too early; that we didn't really try.

There is nonetheless something to be said for the strategy of embracing one’s undesirable parts. The argument in favor of it is not exactly the one the nameless narrator offers (though it may be the one Dostoyevsky implies). Earlier, the narrator asks himself whether his own inner mental life resembles that of other people, and he speculates that it does not. It seems to him he is unique in being mentally tormented. But actually, he is an average person with an average set of demons. Our demons may seem terrible to us, because none of us knows what thoughts and tendencies other people are struggling to overcome. We should, perhaps, know better. Some of the darkness inside us has to do with our being human. Even when we can improve our characters and personalities, we cannot become members of a different species and discard the human burden.

That we are not the people we wish to be gives us no occasion to rejoice – the nameless narrator’s heroic pronouncement to the contrary notwithstanding – but to the extent the undesired parts of ourselves are exceedingly common and appear to come packaged with being human, we can, perhaps, forgive ourselves. Happy angelic personality might just not be an option for creatures like us.

Still, we have to ask: What can be done besides self-forgiveness? Might we be able to become different people (while admittedly, remaining people)?

Change sometimes happens, of course. It may come unannounced. One day, you realize you no longer enjoy the music that moved you previously; or have grown tired with the job that was once your dream job, or you drifted away from your former best friend. We may also get lucky and develop — without working on it — in precisely the ways we want. Much as you may discover you've grown to dislike some type of music, you may find you are no longer frightened of what used to disturb your sleep or that you are much calmer, or less anxious than you once were.

But we cannot just sit waiting for favorable change to happen, nor is resignation a great path to joy, so at other times, we work on ourselves deliberately, for instance, by attempting to become less prone to negative thoughts, or thoughts about things we’d rather forget. Changing oneself intentionally in these ways can be quite difficult, indeed. It is precisely this seeming inability to mold our personalities at will that we perceive, ultimately, as a failure to escape from ourselves.

Possible strategies for effecting lasting change have nonetheless been suggested. One that is worth mentioning is that of shifting attention from ourselves to other people; thinking more about others and less about ourselves.

This issue is a very important one, and it deserves a separate discussion. (We tend to think happiness for us resides in the satisfaction of our own desires, but in fact, we derive great psychological benefits from making someone else happy. Researchers are working on the neural correlates of this phenomenon.[2]) There are several questions one can ask in this regard, for instance, are you a truly caring and generous person if you do things for others because you expect it will make you happy? And why don’t we already know we could be happier by thinking more about other people and less about ourselves? But I set these issues aside.

There is something else I wish to comment on before closing this discussion. There are times when we succeed in doing something we did not think ourselves capable of doing, such as traversing very difficult terrain, operating an airplane, or leaving an organization we and everyone else thought we would never leave. This latter kind of change is often experienced as liberation. What is interesting here is that the taste of liberation is present in such cases even when we never actually thought we were unhappy with the old self and always worked on the assumption that the inner obstacles – commitment to an organization, say, or fear of height – are part of who we are.

And perhaps, they were. They were part of the self we used to be. It is just that, even when we accept who we are, we like to be able to break free.

We can, thus, come close to escaping from ourselves, not, indeed, by going anywhere but by doing things we did not think we could do. When this type of case is portrayed in fiction – for instance, when committed housewife Nora from Ibsen’s play Doll’s House eventually decides to leave her husband – we may experience empathetic liberation on behalf of the character. There is something empowering about simply observing others do what they never thought they could. To see a person overcome inner obstacles and break free from him- or herself in this way holds the promise that perhaps, we too can escape from ourselves and become new people; and that maybe, just maybe, in however small a way, we might be able to break free from the human condition.
