[ *Based on: Skepticism and the Veil of Perception (Rowman & Littlefield, 2001), chs. 1-3. ]

1. What Is Skepticism?

There are many “skeptical” positions in philosophy—moral skepticism, inductive skepticism, external-world skepticism, etc. What they have in common is that they are positions that challenge a significant class of common sense beliefs.

By “common sense beliefs,” I mean beliefs that have about the highest degree of initial plausibility of any beliefs (they seem really obvious on their face). They should be cross-cultural and cross-temporal (e.g., people in all societies in all times will believe that a hand exists when they see one in front of their faces). Denying one of them is a sign of insanity (unless you’re doing philosophy). Note that this is a stipulative, technical use of “common sense” (it does not mean “belief that a lot of people have”!), so don’t complain that this isn’t what “common sense” means.

To “challenge” a belief is to advance a view that could not rationally be embraced in conjunction with said belief. E.g., I could not rationally believe, “I have two hands, but I have no justification for thinking that.” Hence, [I have no justification for believing I have hands] poses a challenge to [I have hands].

A skeptic will challenge some large class of common sense beliefs, not just one or two beliefs. Here, I’m interested in these two forms of skepticism:

External World Skepticism: The view that we lack knowledge of the external world (the world independent of our minds).

Global Skepticism: The view that we lack knowledge of anything whatsoever.

Also, there are different reasons why we might lack knowledge, including:

Certainty Skepticism: We lack knowledge because our beliefs are not absolutely certain.

Justification Skepticism: We lack knowledge because our beliefs are not even justified.

I find certainty skepticism uninteresting. Here, I’m interested in justification skepticism (both global and external-world).

2. Four Skeptical Arguments

a. The Regress Argument

  1. To be justified in believing P, you must have a reason to believe P.

    1. Comment: Imagine I say that I think the world is coming to an end next year, you ask me why I think that, and I reply, “Oh, no reason.” You would conclude that I’m not justified in this belief.

  2. And that reason must itself be justified.

    1. Comment: As above, but suppose I first say, “I think the world is coming to an end next year because the Plutonians are going to launch a lethal nuclear strike then.” You ask me how I know about the Plutonians’ plans, and I say, “Oh, I have no reason to think that; I just guessed.” Again, you’d conclude that my original belief is unjustified. [These examples are from Richard Fumerton.]

    2. This leads to a series of reasons that must stand behind any justified belief. But…

  3. You can’t have an infinite series of reasons behind any actual belief.

  4. Circular reasoning can’t generate justification.

  5. Therefore, no belief can be justified.

    1. Comment: We’ve ruled out that you can have a justified belief with no series of reasons, or with a series of reasons that has an ultimate starting point (steps 1-2). We’ve also ruled out an infinite series of reasons (step 3) or a circular series (step 4). Those are the only possibilities.

Assuming that knowledge requires justification (as we usually assume), this means that we know nothing at all (not even this!).

b. The Reliability Argument

To be justified in believing P, you must first have justification for thinking that the method by which you formed the belief that P is reliable. Example: Say I form the belief that the world is coming to an end next year by consulting a Tarot card reader. I would need some justification for thinking that the Tarot card reader is reliable.

It also seems that you can’t use a method to show that that very method is reliable. E.g., the Tarot card reader can’t just read the Tarot cards to verify that her card reading is reliable.

This leads to a parallel regress problem. You have to use a new belief-forming method to verify the reliability of the first method. But then you have to verify the reliability of the second method. And so on. Again, if we assume that infinite regress is impossible and circularity doesn’t generate justification, then we have to conclude that it’s impossible to justify anything.

c. How can you get outside your head?

David Hume argued something like this:

  1. No one is ever directly aware of anything external; we’re only directly aware of things in our own minds.

  2. If (1), then we can know about the external world only if we can know that certain mental phenomena are signs of certain external, physical phenomena.

  3. But the only way to know that would be by observing correlations between mental and physical phenomena, which we can’t do because of (1).

  4. So we cannot know about the external world.

Comment: (3) is a weak link. The challenge for non-skeptics is to come up with a better account of how we might know that a given mental phenomenon is a sign of a certain physical phenomenon.

d. The brain in a vat & skeptical scenarios

Brain in a Vat (BIV) example: Imagine that people develop technology for keeping a brain alive in a vat of nutrients and electrically stimulating the brain so as to produce in the brain a perfect simulation of interacting normally with the real world. A super-computer runs the simulation and makes sure to send the brain the right stimuli. Brain scanners also read what the brain is trying to do so that the computer can modify the simulation accordingly.

Everything would seem perfectly normal to the brain. Just the way they seem to you now, in fact. So … how do you know that you aren’t a BIV right now?

There are various other skeptical scenarios like this. E.g., maybe this is all a giant dream. Maybe God is deceiving you by planting false images of a physical world in your mind. Maybe you’re actually a sentient program in a simulated world. Etc. These have in common that they explain how everything could seem to you the way it currently does and yet your beliefs about the external world could be radically mistaken.

In this case, skeptics say:

  1. To know anything about the external world, you must first have a reason to reject the hypothesis that you’re a BIV.

  2. You have no reason to reject the BIV hypothesis.

    1. If you were a BIV, all your sensory experiences would be as they are.

    2. Therefore, your sensory experiences are not evidence against the BIV hypothesis. (From (a), assuming that if H predicts E, then E isn’t evidence against H.)

    3. Sensory experiences are the only evidence you have relevant to the BIV hypothesis.

    4. So you have no evidence against the BIV hypothesis. (From (b), (c).)

    5. So you have no reason to reject the BIV hypothesis. (From (d), assuming that there are no non-evidential reasons.)

  3. So you don’t know anything about the external world. (From 1, 2.)

(The arguments in (2c) and (2d) could equally be stated in terms of justification for belief rather than “knowledge”.)

3. Why Discuss Skepticism?

There are and have been very few skeptics in the history of philosophy. Most philosophers try to avoid skepticism, and some ridicule it. Yet epistemologists have still discussed it at great length. Why?

The answer is that there are arguments like those summarized above, which are intellectually interesting. They start from general, plausible principles about knowledge and justification, yet they lead to radically revisionary conclusions. So it seems that there is some kind of incoherence in our beliefs and intuitions about knowledge and justification.

If we could figure out what goes wrong in these arguments, that ought to be illuminating. E.g., is it that you can know stuff about the external world without knowing whether you’re a brain in a vat? Or can you know you’re not a BIV without having any reason to think you’re not? Or do you have non-evidential reasons to reject the BIV hypothesis? What might these reasons be? Any of these things would be very interesting.

4. Easy Answers

Here are two easy answers to skepticism.

a. Is it self-refuting?

Global skepticism is self-refuting, so that goes for the arguments in 2a and 2b above. One can’t rationally hold, “P, but I have no justification to think so.” Also, if the arguments in 2a and 2b succeed, then they are counter-examples to their own conclusions. Hence, they can’t succeed.

External world skepticism, however, is not self-refuting. EW skepticism holds that no one knows any contingent propositions about the external world. This is not itself supposed to be a contingent proposition about the external world. It is allegedly a necessary truth (no one could possibly have external-world knowledge). So it doesn’t apply to itself.

b. The G.E. Moore shift

Suppose there is an argument, “A. B. Therefore, ~K”, where A and B are skeptical premises, and K is the proposition that we have some kind of knowledge that the skeptic is questioning. This argument is valid iff: A, B, and K are jointly incompatible. That is equivalent to “A, K, and B are incompatible” and “B, K, and A are incompatible.” So all of the following are equally valid:

(i)

A.

B.

:. ~K.

(ii)

A.

K.

:. ~B.

(iii)

B.

K.

:. ~A.

There’s no reason to assume that only argument (i) can be endorsed. G.E. Moore says (roughly) that, of these three arguments, the best is the one whose premises are most initially plausible, prior to argument.

But surely that is not argument (i). Prior to argument, it seems extremely obvious that I know, for example, that I have two hands. This is more plausible than just about any abstract, philosophical premise about the nature of knowledge that someone might put forward. (Ex.: It’s more obvious that I know how many hands I have than it is that all knowledge requires reasons.)

So arguments (ii) and (iii) are generally going to be more rationally persuasive than argument (i). The skeptic is just irrationally picking the least plausible of the three.

This point pretty much applies to all arguments that skeptics advance. Recall from sec. 1 that the skeptic is challenging “common sense” beliefs, which are the most initially plausible of all beliefs. Any less-than-common-sense premises are thus going to be inadequate to the task.

I think this is correct. However, it does not answer the questions raised in sec. 3 above: it does not tell us what is wrong with the skeptical arguments, which is really why we care about these arguments.
