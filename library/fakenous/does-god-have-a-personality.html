The gods of polytheistic religions have definite personalities—Zeus is quite different from Athena, Hades, etc. But does the traditional God of monotheism have a personality?

A great deal of literature talks about various divine attributes, such as omnipotence, omniscience, omnipresence, self-existence, and moral perfection. But these are generally not personality traits (though moral perfection comes closest). What is God like as a person? What would it be like to hang out with Him? What kind of music does He enjoy? What kind of art? What would He like to do on the weekends if He weren’t busy running the universe and such? Who is His best friend?

Why do these come off as strange questions? And why don’t theists who like talking about God talk about these questions?

1. Unknowability

Maybe theists don’t talk about these things because we have no way of knowing the answers. No one has gotten a chance to ask God what his favorite flavor of ice cream is yet, so theists wisely abstain from speculating about it until we get more empirical evidence. Maybe we’ll find out in the afterlife. (I bet it’s cinnamon.)

But this doesn’t seem to be the explanation. Theists have speculated about God all over the place. The traditional divine attributes are pretty much speculations without empirical evidence. (They have a priori arguments, but come on, it’s speculation; they’re not like the a priori proofs in mathematics.) They also speculate about things like why God might have allowed cancer to exist, or natural disasters, or what happens to you after you die, or the structure of heaven and hell, or how many and which people are going to heaven. So it doesn’t seem as though theists are generally averse to talking about things when there is a shortage of empirical evidence.

2. Invidiousness

Perhaps theists don’t like talking about God’s personality because this would alienate some people. If it turns out that God likes rap music, a whole bunch of theists are going to feel alienated. If he doesn’t like it, a different group of people will be alienated. God needs to be everyone’s god.

On the other hand, traditional religions rarely have a problem with invidiousness. They might say, e.g., that a particular race is God’s chosen people; that a particular religion should conquer the world; that people from other religions deserve, and will receive, infinite torture.

Maybe the people who create religions don’t mind being invidious between societies, but they don’t want to be invidious within their own social group. Maybe religions that did discuss God’s personality in detail simply would not have succeeded.

This is a plausible factor. But I don’t think it’s the whole story, because the questions I raised about God are not really taboo or upsetting. They are merely odd.

3. Some Character Traits

Actually, theists have ascribed some definite character traits to God. God has been described as loving, forgiving, jealous, vengeful, just, and so on.

Yet somehow, I don’t feel that I have a sense of what God is like as a person. One problem is that God has been ascribed contradictory traits—he is forgiving yet vengeful; he is loving yet condemns most people to infinite torture.

His character traits are also shown in various stories in the Bible, e.g., turning people into pillars of salt, letting Satan torture one of his most faithful followers to win a bet, getting jealous of non-existent other gods.

I get the feeling, however, that few religious thinkers take the stories about God in traditional religious texts literally. If a person actually did all the things attributed to God, that would be a horrible person. Most theists do not appear to think of God as having the horrible attributes he displays in religious texts—they do not think God is petty and spiteful, for example.

4. The Personality Behind Creation

Maybe we can discern something about God’s personality from the character of the universe that he created. This would be similar to how you could learn things about an artist’s personality from his works.

I don’t think I can, though. I don’t know what kind of person would create a universe such as this. I don’t see any values consistently promoted through the design of the universe. For example, it’s neither consistently good nor consistently evil; it mostly seems indifferent to good and evil. It contains some very interesting things, such as life on Earth, but it is mostly hydrogen atoms and vast empty spaces. I don’t believe I’ve ever known a person who, if they could create a universe, would create something like this.

5. Can God Have a Personality?

Here is my thought: Maybe God has no personality; maybe God, as traditionally understood, could not have a personality.

Digression: Think about the game of chess. Different players have different styles of play. Some are more aggressive, some more defensive, some more creative, some more conventional. It’s like a chess personality, which is presumably based on the personal feelings and preferences (other than the general preference for winning) of the individual player.

However, as computer chess programs become more advanced, they converge toward the same style—or perhaps no style. With sufficiently advanced analysis, it is going to be revealed which move, out of a set of alternatives that may display different styles, is in fact objectively best. Advanced computer chess engines will converge toward just playing the optimal move. If you’re simply playing the objectively best move every time, that isn’t really a reflection of any personality. Compare: If you provably have exactly one move that enables you to avoid checkmate, then playing that move does not exhibit any “style” or “personality.” By the way, if human observers perceive optimal play as showing some personality, that’s really a reflection of human observers’ having some bias toward certain kinds of (suboptimal) moves.

Iskra Fileva makes the related observation that one only appeals to character traits to explain an action when there is more than one rational action available; if a person performs the only rational action in a given situation, that doesn’t evince any particular character trait (see “Two Senses of Why” in Questions of Character).

To return to God: God is supposed to be a perfect being. Just as the perfect chess engine simply plays the objectively best move, the perfect being would simply perform the objectively best action at any given time. Just as there is no room for personality to determine the chess engine’s decisions, there would be no room for personality to influence God’s actions.

Personality results from imperfection. If you are imperfectly loving, then you love some beings more than others, or you love some beings more (or less) than they are objectively worthy of love. The perfect being would love everyone in the manner and degree (whatever that is) that is most appropriate. If there are differences in how much it is appropriate to love different people (e.g., if it is appropriate to love good people more than bad people), then the ideal being would have appropriately different degrees of love. If there are no such differences, then the ideal being would love everyone equally.

If your aesthetic perception is limited, then you will appreciate some kinds of art more easily than others, leading to your having favorite art works that reflect your personality. A perfect aesthetic observer, however, would be equally sensitive to the aesthetic merits of all art works and would enjoy each in exact proportion to their objective aesthetic quality. Or, if there is no such thing as objective aesthetic merit, then the ideal aesthetic observer would be equally open to all aesthetic experiences. He would be capable of enjoying every art work that anyone can enjoy, in the best way it can be enjoyed. He could enjoy rap music in the way it is best enjoyed, while at the same time being equally open to enjoying classical music in the way it is best enjoyed.

The same goes for other preferences. If you prefer what is obviously, objectively best, that doesn’t show any particular personality—at least, not an interesting one. E.g., if I prefer a bowl of chili over a punch in the nose, that doesn’t show any interesting personality traits on my part. Human beings can have personalities, though, without being blameworthy for them, because it is often unclear to human minds what option is best. In that case, a rational person can choose one of the options that seems about as good as any of the others.

But for God, nothing is ever unclear. God always knows, instantly and with certainty, which option is best. Because God is perfect, he must choose what is obviously (to him) objectively best. If God had any degree of preference for anything other than goodness, then that preference could sometimes, when the difference in goodness between two options was sufficiently small, dictate a preference for a less objectively good option over a more objectively good option—and then God would be imperfect.

6. Implications

I mentioned in an earlier post that we tend to like virtuous people better than vicious people. But we don’t just like virtue. Part of what we like about people is their personalities. A person who just acts like a goodness-maximizing machine all the time wouldn’t be very likeable. It would thus be hard, perhaps impossible, to be friends with that person.

Likewise, though we are supposed to love God, it would be hard to like God in the way that you like your best friend. We never hear about God’s personal friends; he only has worshippers and enemies. This is not just because of his power and authority (the right person can very well, e.g., make friends with the emperor of the universe). It’s because of God’s lack of personality.

This is one way in which ordinary human beings are better off than God. If there is a God, perhaps that is why he made imperfect beings such as us.
