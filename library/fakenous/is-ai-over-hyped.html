I suspect that people have been over-hyping the importance of AI and how it’s going to change our lives. I doubt that it is as revolutionary as people are saying.

Why do I say that?

1. It Couldn’t Very Well Be Under-Hyped

People have been making really extreme statements about AI. Some people are saying that it is literally going to kill everyone, perhaps within a few years. (See Yudkowsky & Soares.)

Others think it’s going to usher in a virtual paradise on Earth, curing aging and making us all virtually immortal geniuses. (See Kurzweil.)

The CEO of Google thinks that AI will be a more profound discovery than fire or electricity. (See Pichai.)

The CEO of Open AI says that AI will prove to be the most important technology ever created. (See Altman.)

Not everyone is that dramatic. But enough people are being super-dramatic about AI that it is hardly possible that AI is under-hyped at this point. If we’re making an error in one direction or the other, it’s almost certainly in the over-hyping direction.

It’s like if your friend tells you that person P is the greatest genius who has ever lived. When you meet P, are you going to have a higher or a lower opinion than your friend? Almost certainly lower. It couldn’t very well be higher, and although it could be the same, that is a priori unlikely. Most people are not the greatest genius who has ever lived.

In general, most things that are said by someone to be the most X thing in the world (for some attribute X) are not in fact the most X thing in the world.

2. The History of Hype

Which brings me to the history of hype. A lot of things have been over-hyped. And here, I include both overly optimistic and overly pessimistic predictions.

The Dramatism Bias

Human beings, especially journalists and other authors, have a bias towards the dramatic. That includes predictions of extreme transformations of all kinds. Whether positive or negative, these predictions entertain us and attract attention. We might even decide to believe (or tell ourselves that we believe) these predictions for entertainment purposes.

Bias toward the dramatic is the best explanation for why there have been so many failed disaster predictions. In 1969, Stanford ecologist Paul Ehrlich predicted that “unless we are extremely lucky, everybody will disappear in a cloud of blue steam in 20 years.” In 1970, Harvard biologist George Wald predicted that civilization would end within 30 years unless immediate action was taken on our environmental problems. In the 1970’s, scientists were predicting a new ice age in the early 21st century. Etc. For more, see Progressive Myths, ch. 20.

Past AI Hype

More to the present point, people have been making overly optimistic predictions specifically about AI for many years. From Wikipedia:

  • 1958, H. A. Simon and Allen Newell: “within ten years a digital computer will be the world’s chess champion”; “within ten years a digital computer will discover and prove an important new mathematical theorem.”

  • 1965, H. A. Simon: “machines will be capable, within twenty years, of doing any work a man can do.”

  • 1970, Marvin Minsky: “In from three to eight years we will have a machine with the general intelligence of an average human being.” [Edit: This quote may be bogus: https://www.openphilanthropy.org/research/what-should-we-learn-from-past-ai-forecasts/ —mh]

Note: The computers of 1978 did not in fact have the intelligence of a human being.

This makes it more plausible that what we are being treated to presently is another round of overly dramatic predictions.

Recent transformations

If you’re as old as me, you may think: “But I have personally witnessed the rise of more than one life-altering technology. Personal computers, the internet, cell phones, social media. AI seems plausible as the next item in this list.”

Fair enough. When I was born, none of those technologies existed. No one owned a personal computer, let alone a cell phone; today, I use the computer every day, and I carry a phone everywhere. I would remind you, however, that people did not correctly anticipate how (or how much) these technologies would affect us. In 1964, science fiction author Arthur C. Clarke sort of predicted the rise of the internet:

That’s remarkable, given that no one at the time even had a personal computer.

But he did not correctly anticipate the effects. He thought it would result in a dissolution of cities, but in fact, cities have only grown. As far as I recall, no one anticipated the most common everyday uses of computer technology—social media. When social media was first starting, no one knew how much it would affect our lives.

That is to say that great transformations do happen, but they are generally not the ones we expect.

Why? Basically, because the world is extremely complex and chaotic, and the possible futures are far more numerous than the things that a normal person can come up with when they sit and think about it. It’s like we’re thinking of 10 possibilities and guessing which one of them will happen, while there are actually 1 million possibilities that we’re not thinking of.

So I’m not predicting that there won’t be any major transformations of our society in the next 50 years. I’m predicting that whatever they are, they won’t be what we’re expecting. Maybe AI will have big effects, but different ones from the ones people are anticipating. Or maybe some other technologies will appear that will prove much more important.

Anecdotal experience

I like current AI tools. I have completely replaced traditional internet searches with AI questions and answers. It’s convenient and time-saving. But it has hardly transformed my life.

You might say: “Give it time!” But the AI boosters have been predicting amazing transformations to happen really quickly. Based on what people were saying when Chat GPT appeared in 2022, shouldn’t I expect my life to be transformed by now?

Given the current sort of AI models that we have, I don’t see further improvements of these models completely altering human life. Say these companies keep expanding their models to have more parameters and more processing power. Say they train the models to have fewer hallucinations. Then AI will be even more convenient, more reliable, and save us even more time. Many jobs (like driving) will probably be automated, which will be good for the economy, similarly to past rounds of automation. But I don’t see it ushering in either utopia or Armageddon.

The best hope is that AI will help us make major scientific breakthroughs. That seems to be the main thing that high intelligence would be useful for. However, I’m skeptical about this …

3. The Limits of AI Understanding

I sense that it won’t be so easy to unlock nature’s secrets. Building a computer network with huge processing power and asking it to solve the hardest problems that scientists have been struggling with just doesn’t seem to me like the kind of thing that would work in this world.

I suspect that the reason it seems more plausible to other people is that most people tend to see artificial intelligence as a genuine kind of intelligence. Highly intelligent people tend to produce intellectual breakthroughs, so super-intelligent computers should produce even more breakthroughs. Most people see the computer as understanding (or sometimes misunderstanding) the things that it talks about. They think that when the AI says, “Honey badgers belong to the weasel family,” the AI means that honey badgers belong to the weasel family—and that in some sense, the AI sort of knows what a badger is, what a biological family is, etc.

I don’t think any of those things. I don’t think the AI in any sense knows what a badger is. One reason is that the AI has never met a badger, never seen a badger, never interacted (in relevant ways) with any animal. The LLMs we’re using have only been trained on sequences of text. And—even if you ignore questions about whether a computer could have mental states at all—I don’t think that just knowing about strings of text and how words are correlated with each other in a large body of human-produced text is enough to understand the actual things the words are supposed to be about. (I first heard this argument from Jessica Heine.)

I’ve previously expressed my skepticism that computers can have any mental states at all. But even if they have mental states, and even if they can think about things, they are not thinking about the things that they appear to be talking about. They could at most be thinking about words. And I am very skeptical that one can make major scientific breakthroughs—however intelligent one is—by just thinking really well about words. The AI would only be mining word-sequences that humans have produced and trying to extrapolate to other word-sequences that humans have not yet come up with but that are somehow implied (in a purely syntactic-pattern-matching sense) by the existing word-sequences.

I wouldn’t completely rule out that this could help. Maybe patterns in words reflect patterns in thought, and patterns in thought reflect patterns in the world. And so maybe there is some progress to be derived from a very sophisticated and subtle examination of word patterns. I certainly hope that this helps us make scientific breakthroughs (especially since I’m still waiting for the cure to aging). But it just doesn’t seem super-promising on its face. Maybe it will help with the easiest problems, but I doubt it will solve the most difficult scientific problems. The hardest scientific problems probably require a superior understanding to that previously articulated by human beings, and therefore they are unlikely to be solvable by examining patterns in the word sequences humans have produced.
