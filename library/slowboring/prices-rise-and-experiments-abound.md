Housing systems across the United States are under pressure from multiple directions. Changes to the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) have left local providers scrambling to maintain programs, while cities are getting creative with existing laws and turning to zoning reforms, accessory dwelling units, and transit-oriented projects to boost supply. At the same time, rising home prices and worsening affordability are reshaping how Americans approach housing and homeownership.

\---

#### States re-evaluate policies amid HUD changes

**Disruptions to supportive housing across the U.S.  
**HUD’s shift away from permanent supportive housing is causing upheaval across the country, with local agencies warning they may lose long-standing programs even as needs intensify.

In New England, providers cited by the [Boston Globe](http://Boston) say the new funding rules for the [Continuum of Care program](https://www.hud.gov/hud-partners/community-coc) could destabilize hundreds of residents who rely on long-term rental support. The rules cut support for permanent supportive housing in favor of transitional, work-or-treatment-required shelter models, prompting a [multistate lawsuit](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2025/11/25/metro/lawsuit-trump-hud-homeless-funding-ri/?p1=BGSearch_Overlay_Results&p1=Article_Inline_Text_Link) to block the cuts.

In Los Angeles, LAist reports that local officials estimate HUD’s new funding requirements could remove long-term rental support for [roughly 5,000 people](https://laist.com/news/housing-homelessness/lahsa-funding-hud-trump-nofo-cuts-housing-first-shelter-permanent) currently housed in permanent supportive units. On top of that, plans to gut [more than 80 percent](https://laist.com/brief/news/housing-homelessness/trump-administration-plans-mass-firing-at-office-that-funds-homelessness-programs) of the HUD staff who administer homelessness grants have left providers scrambling to redesign programs, renegotiate contracts, and brace for widespread displacement if the cuts take effect.

And in Michigan, [the Detroit Free Press reports](https://www.freep.com/story/news/local/michigan/2025/11/29/hud-grant-funding-homeless/87449354007/) that agencies across the state may have to shut down supportive housing, rapid-rehousing, and shelter programs that thousands rely on to stay housed. With winter approaching and demand for services high, Michigan nonprofits say the abrupt shift away from permanent housing models leaves them scrambling to find replacement funding or prepare for rising homelessness as contracts expire.

**Supply, vacancy, and funding pressures for Portland’s low-income housing  
**Portland’s subsidized housing system is showing strain from multiple angles. A recent study conducted by the real-estate analytics firm CoStar and reported by [The Oregonian](https://www.oregonlive.com/business/2025/12/nearly-1900-affordable-portland-apartments-sit-empty-while-thousands-need-homes.html) found that nearly 1,900 subsidized apartments — about 7.4 percent of the city’s roughly 25,409 “affordable” units — are currently vacant. Housing providers cited by The Oregonian warn that even the subsidized rents remain too high for many low‑income households, potentially explaining why units sit empty even as demand remains high.

At the same time, Oregon Public Broadcasting [reports](https://www.opb.org/article/2025/12/01/portland-oregon-home-forward-housing-rent/) that the city’s public‑housing authority, Home Forward, has announced a $35 million budget shortfall. Under financial pressure, Home Forward has announced it will cut staff and roll back certain services starting next month. The cuts stem from HUD’s $14 million reduction in federal funding for the Housing Choice Voucher program combined with [rising operational and maintenance costs](https://www.wweek.com/news/city/2025/12/01/home-forward-says-it-faces-35-million-budget-gap) across Home Forward’s subsidized housing portfolio, Willamette Week reports. As a result, the housing authority has paused issuance of new vouchers, extending its years-long waitlist for thousands of low-income residents.

The high number of available units combined with restricted access caused by funding and eligibility problems highlights a growing disconnect in Portland’s housing system. Even though there is a large “affordable” housing stock, structural barriers and financial strains are preventing that housing from reaching the people who need it.

#### **Declining affordability reshapes homeownership expectations and financial decision-making**

**Housing affordability down nearly half from 2020  
**A new national report by Oxford Economics finds that housing affordability has become “[considerably worse](https://cdn.roxhillmedia.com/production/email/attachment/1770001_1780000/2fb748688e7bd2e539d48aaef57158505fbdeb72.pdf)” than it was five years ago. To qualify for a typical single-family home in 2025, the study concludes that a household now needs an income of about $110,100 — nearly double what was required in 2020. As a result, only around 38 percent of U.S. households can currently afford to buy a home, down sharply from 57 percent in 2020. [Economists cited by Newsweek](https://www.newsweek.com/housing-affordability-considerably-worse-than-five-years-ago-11139969) say the shift underscores persistent supply shortages, high mortgage rates, and lagging job growth.

**Young Americans increasingly giving up on homeownership, moving toward crypto  
**Fortune reports that many younger Americans are [“giving up”](https://fortune.com/2025/11/30/housing-market-affordability-crisis-hopelessness-gen-z-millennials-crypto-doom-spending-quiet-quitting/) on the dream of homeownership. The median home-price-to-income ratio has ballooned: in 2025, Fortune reports, the cost of a median home is nearly 5.8 times median household annual income, compared to 3.6 times in 1984. With first-time homebuyers at record lows and many renters unable to meaningfully save, many are turning to riskier investments, [such as cryptocurrencies](https://www.thestreet.com/crypto/markets/economists-warn-unaffordable-housing-is-forcing-young-americans-to-crypto), or adjusting their lifestyles to accommodate long-term renting.

#### **Local experimentation, development, and reform**

**Massachusetts rent-stabilization petition reaches early milestone**  
Supporters of a proposed statewide [rent-stabilization ballot measure](https://www.wbur.org/news/2025/11/20/rent-control-ballot-question-massachusetts-2026-michelle-wu) last month announced they turned in more than the signatures required to advance the question toward the 2026 ballot, signaling an organized drive to revisit the state’s long ban on rent control and move toward broad rent-stabilization rules. The campaign framed the effort as a response to widespread affordability pressures, while opponents warn of market distortions and a polarizing statewide campaign next year.

**At the same time, the state’s accessory unit law shows mixed early results  
**A 2024 law legalized accessory dwelling units (A.D.U.s) on most single-family lots outside Boston, but the policy has yet to [bring about widespread construction](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2025/12/01/business/tiny-houses-massachusetts), the Boston Globe reports. Between January and July 2025, the state received 884 A.D.U. applications across 170 communities, with roughly 550 approvals, but actual building remains slow. Builders point to permitting delays, local zoning restrictions, and high costs (often $190,000 to $250,000 for modular units) as persistent obstacles. While A.D.U.s could help ease housing shortages without altering neighborhood character, the early experience suggests that legalization alone isn’t enough and additional policy support and financing may be needed to translate interest into meaningful growth.

**Baltimore housing reform stalled over skepticism about zoning reform**Baltimore’s city government is considering a major zoning overhaul under the [Housing Options and Opportunity Act](https://mayor.baltimorecity.gov/news/press-releases/2025-05-12-mayor-brandon-m-scott-introduces-legislation-expand-housing-access) — a package of reforms designed to expand access to multifamily housing. If passed, the legislation, which would be the final piece of the reform package, would allow many single-family homes to be converted into duplexes, triplexes, or four-unit buildings. It would also introduce a new “low-density, multifamily housing” category, relax bulk and yard restrictions, and eliminate mandatory off-street parking for new construction. The plan also includes changes to building codes and moves the city’s Zoning Administrator into the planning department to streamline decision-making. At the same time, city officials rolled out an interactive map to show neighbors how the proposed changes might affect individual blocks and properties.

Proponents say the moves will make housing more abundant, affordable, and undo longstanding exclusionary zoning. But the legislation [did not receive a vote](https://www.thebanner.com/politics-power/local-government/baltimore-council-housing-zoning-5GBQCTZPTVBJPF75YIH5BAIQVA/) in committee on Monday after residents voiced concerns that it could undermine neighborhood character and enable disruptive development, particularly in historically Black middle-class neighborhoods, the Baltimore Banner reports. Originally slated for full council consideration Thursday, the legislation is indefinitely stalled.

**Alders approve downtown New Haven rezoning plan to allow denser housing  
**In downtown New Haven, Conn., the city’s Board of Alders on Monday voted unanimously to approve a new Downtown For All zoning overlay district designed to enable denser residential development and more affordable housing options.

Eli Sabin, alder for the seventh ward, told me that by focusing on reform for downtown development, the board avoided significant pushback from residents opposed to housing density, while centering new housing around existing transportation, schools, jobs, and businesses. The biggest points of concern, he said, were about historic preservation during construction.

Key elements of the overlay include reducing the minimum average unit size for multifamily buildings (from 1,000 square feet to 400 square feet), allowing taller buildings under increased floor area allowances, and permitting small-unit types — including single-room occupancy units. As part of the overlay, for housing on city‑owned lots, at least [30 percent of units must be subsidized](https://www.nhregister.com/news/article/downtown-for-all-affordable-housing-new-haven-ct-21219363.php), in keeping with the city’s broader plan to address housing affordability and accommodate cost-burdened households.

Sabin said that New Haven is not at the same stage of the housing crisis as other cities that have passed significant zoning reforms like [Cambridge](https://www.abundanthousingma.org/cambridge-ends-exclusionary-zoning/), Mass., or [Berkeley](https://berkeleyca.gov/community-recreation/news/new-zoning-changes-allow-faster-cheaper-process-build-homes), Calif. But as the population grows and in order to avoid future, deeper housing troubles, Sabin said, “the ability to build at that scale will be important to make sure that we’re building enough homes to accommodate the demand.”

**Indianapolis council backs three PILOTs for affordable housing  
**The Indianapolis City County Council unanimously approved three [payment‑in‑lieu‑of‑taxes (PILOT) proposals](https://www.wrtv.com/news/local-news/in-your-community/downtown-indy/city-county-council-approves-three-pilot-proposals-for-affordable-housing-projects-across-indy-metro), covering a 78‑unit senior community, a redevelopment at Lafayette Square Mall, and Capitol Station Apartments off Madison Avenue, giving them tax‑incentive support to address needed renovations or redevelopment.

According to supporters, WRTV Indianapolis reports, the move preserves or brings new affordable housing stock to Indianapolis, including senior housing, and extends useful life for existing affordable properties rather than letting them deteriorate.

**The Salt Lake suburbs leaning on transit‑oriented development  
**Several suburbs around Salt Lake City are increasingly using [Housing and Transit Reinvestment zones (H.T.R.Z.s)](https://www.kuer.org/business-economy/2025-12-02/the-salt-lake-burbs-are-leaning-on-transit-oriented-development-to-squeeze-in-housing?_amp=true) to encourage housing and commercial development near existing transit hubs like commuter‑rail or light‑rail stations. Under the H.T.R.Z. framework, local governments can capture a portion of the incremental property‑tax revenue generated by new or redeveloped property within the zone and use it to offset development costs such as infrastructure improvements, multifamily construction, or affordable housing requirements. For example, South Jordan is planning roughly 4,500 housing units around a light‑rail station in the Daybreak area, while Sandy recently approved a development of 1,480 housing units, a hotel, and office space within a third of a mile of a commuter‑rail station. KUER reports that supporters view H.T.R.Z.s as a tool to help close the region’s long-term housing deficit while encouraging growth near transit and reducing sprawl. Officials caution, however, that H.T.R.Z.s are only one tool and additional strategies will be needed to meet projected housing demand.

**San Francisco’s “family zoning” plan approved after years of debate  
**In a 7 to 4 vote, San Francisco’s Board of Supervisors on Tuesday [approved](https://www.sfchronicle.com/bayarea/article/sf-mayor-lurie-s-family-zoning-plan-passes-21218981.php) Mayor Daniel Lurie’s “family zoning” plan — a major upzoning proposal that redefines zoning to allow taller, denser housing in many previously low-density neighborhoods across the city’s north and west. The rezoning covers roughly 60 percent of the city (excluding some eastern and southern neighborhoods), raising height and density limits so multifamily housing and mid-rise apartment buildings become permissible in new areas. The approval comes after three years of debate and marks Lurie’s “biggest legislative victory” according to the San Francisco Chronicle.  
  
Even so, a [city economic analysis](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1lpexmRoDuPoR2qG0Zq0LYUUtJWaY7kpx/view) projects the changes could yield about 14,600 new units over 20 years, which, though an increase from possibilities under current zoning rules, is well below the approximately 82,000 new homes the city is supposed to build under state mandates by 2031.  
  
Supporters argue the plan is a needed step toward alleviating the housing crisis and meeting legal obligations, while critics point to lacking affordable‑housing protections, potential displacement, and doubts about whether the upzoning will translate into actual production given high costs and regulatory barriers, the Chronicle reports.

**California’s “builder’s remedy” pressures cities to allow more housing  
**A little-known state law, called [the builder’s remedy](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/20/business/economy/california-housing-nimby.html), is giving California developers a tool to bypass local zoning when cities fail to meet state housing requirements, the New York Times reports. Developers have used the law to propose high-density projects in places like Beverly Hills, where zoning rules and local opposition have historically blocked tall buildings. Advocates cited by the Times describe it as a “NIMBY sword of Damocles”: The mere threat encourages municipalities to approve housing projects they might otherwise resist. While the law has existed since 1990, it only began to be widely exploited in 2022, creating a “credible threat” that is prompting cities to approve more housing voluntarily. Early evidence suggests the tactic is reshaping negotiations between developers and municipalities, though the ultimate impact on California’s housing shortage will take years to measure.

**Rural Colorado sees sharp home‑price surge, eroding affordability  
**Colorado is [leading the nation in rural home prices](https://www.denverpost.com/2025/12/01/rural-home-price-affordability-colorado/), the Denver Post reports. A recent Redfin analysis shows that rural households in Colorado now need to earn around $171,084 per year to afford the median-priced rural home, which is more than double what rural households nationally need. Since the pandemic, rural home prices in Colorado have surged about 72 percent, while local household incomes rose only 35 percent. That gap, combined with higher borrowing costs, property taxes, and insurance premiums, has severely deteriorated affordability. Much of the buying pressure in rural Colorado comes from second‑home buyers and remote workers with higher incomes, especially in scenic or resort‑adjacent areas, according to the Post, leaving many long-time residents priced out of the market.

**Boulder’s record‑setting push for affordable housing  
**Boulder, one of Colorado’s most expensive cities, [announced its largest one‑year investment](https://bouldercolorado.gov/news/city-boulder-announces-affordable-housing-fund-awards) in affordable housing, dedicating about $21.38 million to support development of new affordable homes. The funds will be directed to two organizations that will add 184 permanently affordable homes to Boulder’s housing stock — a major boost for households earning 30 to 60 percent of area median income and low-income seniors.

A news release by the city says the funds help advance Boulder’s goal that 15 percent of all homes in the city be permanently affordable by 2035.

**Experimenting in Minnesota  
**New Ulm, the 14,000-person Minnesota city, has approved “[experimental builds](https://www.startribune.com/faced-with-a-housing-crisis-this-southern-minnesota-city-is-saying-yes-to-experimental-builds/601477433),” including tiny homes, shed houses, and more flexible zoning to allow non-traditional housing types. The Minnesota Star Tribune reports that the move reflects growing urgency even in smaller cities and rural areas to increase housing supply in response to demographic shifts and rising demand.
