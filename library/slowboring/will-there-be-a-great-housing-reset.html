Next year promises a subtle shift in the housing market. Forecasts suggest wage growth may finally outpace home-price gains, offering tentative breathing room for prospective buyers. Yet across the country, affordability pressures persist, local governments experiment with zoning and funding tools, and vulnerable households face heightened risks amid evictions, climate threats, and inadequate supply. From national trends to city-level initiatives, here’s a snapshot of what’s shaping U.S. housing as 2026 approaches.

ROAD to Housing Act hits a dead end

The House of Representatives stripped the bipartisan ROAD to Housing Act from the National Defense Authorization Act, dashing what many had called the most significant federal housing-reform package in a decade. The act — which passed the Senate Banking Committee unanimously — would have cut zoning and regulatory red tape, boosted financing for manufactured and modular homes, expanded rental assistance, and raised the cap on banks’ public-welfare investments. With its removal, a broad slate of supply-side reforms and affordability tools are now delayed. Supporters are vowing to press forward in 2026.

At the same time, the House wants to show it is not indifferent to supply-side housing concerns. The Financial Services Committee last week held a hearing titled “Building Capacity: Reducing Government Roadblocks to Housing Supply,” in which committee members reviewed roughly 40 bipartisan bills and proposals that aim to encourage housing production.

Topics ranged from easing rules for manufactured and modular homes to streamlining rural housing programs and construction financing reviews. The session signals a renewed congressional focus on supply-side reforms, with more hearings and targeted bills expected as lawmakers seek actionable fixes.

Elsewhere in the federal government, the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) has pulled back sweeping proposed changes to its $3 billion Continuum of Care grant program, which I wrote about last week, after lawsuits from states, cities, and nonprofit groups were filed. The original revisions would have sharply restricted how much funding could support permanent housing, shifted the program away from its long‑standing housing‑first model, and barred grants to organizations focused on transgender communities. Critics warned that these moves could jeopardize the homes of tens of thousands of people, and cities began to evaluate how they could adjust their own budgets and programs to support vulnerable residents. Facing legal pressure, HUD now says it will reassess and issue a revised policy before the January application deadline. The abrupt reversal drew sharp criticism from the court overseeing the case, with the presiding judge decrying what she called “intentional chaos,” Reuters reports.

California housing battles go local

The city of San Francisco announced that its new “Housing Accelerator Fund” — designed to subsidize construction of below-market homes and incentivize development — has reached $100 million in seed funding. The San Francisco Chronicle reports that city officials hope the fund will jumpstart more subsidized housing projects in neighborhoods where cost and financing have stalled production. The announcement comes as the city contends with aggressive state-level zoning and a heated debate over the right balance of density, affordability, and neighborhood character.

In parallel, candidates in the race to replace House Speaker Nancy Pelosi in her San Francisco congressional district are putting housing policy at the center of their campaigns. State Senator Scott Wiener, a longtime YIMBY champion who is advocating for streamlined development, is up against Connie Chan, a San Francisco supervisor. Chan is a more traditional progressive who characterizes herself as a champion of deeply subsidized housing. The San Francisco Chronicle reports that the contest reflects a sharp divide over whether to prioritize market-rate development, social housing, or a mix of both.

Meanwhile, on Tuesday the Los Angeles City Council unanimously voted to make permanent in the city’s municipal code the Affordable Housing Streamlining Ordinance, which was initially launched under Karen Bass as Executive Directive One. Under the new law, “100 percent affordable housing” projects can fast‑track approval, cutting review times dramatically from typical six‑ to nine‑month delays to as little as 60 days. The streamlined pathway is lauded by advocates as essential to address the region’s affordability crisis. The Los Angeles Times reports that over the past three years nearly 490 development proposals promising more than 40,000 affordable units were filed, but only 44 of those proposals had moved into construction before this change. That said, exemptions for single‑family zones and rent‑controlled buildings may limit the law’s impact across neighborhoods.

Modest reform in Connecticut

Governor Ned Lamont on Monday signed controversial legislation requiring every municipality in Connecticut to adopt local housing plans as part of a broader effort to tackle the state’s housing shortage. The law makes it easier to repurpose commercial buildings into homes and loosens certain parking requirements for new residential developments. By enabling more streamlined permitting and encouraging multi‑unit housing, supporters say the move will help create more affordable and diverse housing options for working families, renters, and people experiencing homelessness. But some critics cited by Inside Investigator caution that the law’s success hinges on how regional and local governments implement the plans.

Critics on the other side complain that loosened zoning controls will pressure smaller towns and challenge local control. The level of controversy around these relatively modest changes underscores that the urbanized Northeast continues to be a laggard region in terms of housing reform.

Market trends show a slow recovery and modest gains

Income growth to outpace home prices next year
Redfin’s 2026 forecast predicts a modest housing-market shift: Wage and income growth are expected to outpace home-price growth next year, giving some potential buyers a bit of breathing room. The report suggests a “slow recovery” where prices inch up slowly, mortgage rates ease slightly, and affordability improves gradually, but only enough to help some buyers. For many — especially younger and first-time buyers — the relief may still feel limited.

Home-price growth slowing in 2026
A Reuters poll forecasts that U.S. home prices will rise just 1.4 percent in 2026 — the slowest pace in 14 years. Rising mortgage rates and limited entry-level inventory are expected to constrain growth, though affordability pressures for first-time buyers will persist. The forecast, which is drawn from assessments by “property experts” and economists, points to a price plateau, rather than broad relief for buyers.

Caution from homebuilders
Toll Brothers, a major national homebuilder, signaled a cautious outlook for 2026. The company is bracing for a slow housing market amid high costs and uncertain buyer demand. The Wall Street Journal reports broader hesitancy from builders, who suggest that even if supply-side policies and forecasts improve, real-world production may lag as high mortgage rates and other economic concerns keep buyers from making moves in the market.

Affordability pressures, housing stress, and experimental solutions

Over 75 percent of U.S. homes unaffordable for median buyers
A recent analysis from Bankrate found that more than three-quarters of homes for sale nationwide are effectively out of reach for median-income households. The assessment is a measure of the gap between median household income, which Bankrate estimates is about $80,000, and the income needed to afford the median-priced home, which the company puts around $113,000. CBS reports that first-time buyers now make up only about 24 percent of home sales, down from nearly 50 percent in 2010. The outlets point to a steep rise in mortgage rates, which CBS reports should drop slightly in 2026, that has kept homeowners in place and led to a lack of houses on the market.

Income, affordability, and climate exposure collide
Fortune reports that America’s housing‑affordability crisis is increasingly intertwined with climate risk. As high costs push lower‑income households out of established, safer communities, many are forced to settle in areas more vulnerable to wildfires, floods, and hurricanes. Income is effectively determining who faces the greatest disaster exposure. Compounding the problem, insurers are raising rates or withdrawing from high‑risk regions, making it harder for residents to protect their homes.

Evictions surge in Nashville
The Nashville Banner reports a sharp increase in eviction filings across the city, a symptom of worsening conditions and a zoomed-in look at a dynamic playing out across the country. Rising rents, stagnating wages, and an inadequate supply of affordable units are pushing more tenants toward displacement, especially lower-income and rent-burdened households.

Backyard storage units as housing in L.A.
The New York Times published a story that follows 33-year-old Junior Estrada, one of roughly a dozen tenants living in unpermitted backyard storage units in L.A.’s San Fernando Valley. The structures have no running water, no power, and rents of $600 to $800 a month. The landlord, already charged with multiple misdemeanors for illegal dwellings, had powered the units using Tesla batteries before utilities were cut off.

The story is an example of how California’s severe affordability crisis forces even full-time workers into unsafe, informal housing arrangements. As the property faces closure and tenants receive 30-day notices, the article highlights the thin line separating the housed from the unhoused, and how unregulated “last resort” units have proliferated amid the region’s housing shortage.

Housing shortfall ahead for Colorado Springs
A regional housing-needs assessment for Colorado Springs found a shortage of about 27,700 units and projects a need for roughly 60,000 additional homes by 2035 — which would be more than double the city’s current pace of housing production. The report warns that current inventory and rental-price trends fail to match the demand from seniors, young professionals, single-person households, and low- to moderate-income workers in Colorado’s second most populous city, which was once known for having more millionaires per capita than any other place in the country. City officials say they will use the data to craft a comprehensive Housing Action Plan in 2026.

Developers convert aging New York City parking garages into housing
As buildable sites in Manhattan grow scarce, developers are transforming older parking garages (even some flagged for structural dangers after a 2023 collapse) into new housing, the New York Times reports. In the West Village, a former garage is becoming a seven-unit ultraluxury project, with some penthouses priced at $57.5 million and $85 million as buyers compete for discreet, loft-like spaces. Similar conversions at lower prices are underway across the city.
