In January of 2022, then-President Joe Biden, wracked by public anger over inflation and desperate to show progress on the cost of living, announced a [crackdown on consolidation in the meatpacking industry](https://x.com/josh_wingrove/status/1477946588609851393), which he hoped would bring down consumer prices or at least create the impression of decisive action.

Four years later, President Donald Trump, losing support on his signature issue of immigration and having failed to conquer Greenland, [announced the exact same thing](https://x.com/RealPNavarro/status/2015815421090222115), also hoping to either make progress on consumer prices or at least create the impression of masterful action.

This is part of a broad suite of initiatives the administration has rolled out in 2026, all of which seem designed to harness economic populist energy. Trump [signed an executive order purporting](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/01/stopping-wall-street-from-competing-with-main-street-homebuyers/) to make it harder for large investors to buy single-family homes, and he said banks had a January 20 deadline to [reduce credit-card interest rates to 10 percent](https://abcnews.go.com/Business/wireStory/trumps-deadline-cap-credit-card-rates-looms-banks-129306487). When that didn’t happen, he said Congress should pass a law [capping credit-card interest rates for one year](https://www.americascreditunions.org/news-media/news/trump-calls-congress-enact-10-apr-cap-one-year). His administration also said it would let people tap into their 401(k) savings to make a down payment on a new house, but then the president [said he actually doesn’t support this](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/boards-policy-regulation/trump-not-huge-fan-advisers-plan-open-401k-plans-buying-homes-2026-01-23/).

I think this kind of flailing slopulism probably works a bit better for a Republican than a Democrat, because it plays against type.

But Trump is going to find that he has the same basic problem as Biden: If you want to address public concern about inflation, you need to both alter people’s lived experience of inflation and also alter the news coverage of inflation.

If you claim that antitrust enforcement against meatpackers will make beef cheaper, that probably sounds like a great idea to most people. But is beef actually going to be cheaper? I don’t think it will. And whatever Trump is doing with single-family homes definitely won’t make them cheaper. Credit-card interest rates have not actually been capped, and if they were capped this would have downstream consequences that people would find very annoying.

Last but not least, all of these affordability gimmicks suffer from the egg problem.

#### Inflation is a macroeconomic problem

Remember egg prices? When they soared due to avian flu? And not only were eggs expensive, supermarkets wanted to avoid setting market-clearing prices (which would have been even higher), so they allowed egg shortages to pile up. This was annoying and a huge story.

But then in the spring of 2025, egg prices started to fall. And once they fell, it stopped being a huge story and people moved on to grumbling about other prices.

The lesson that Trump doesn’t want to learn here is that inflation is a macroeconomic phenomenon. He campaigned on an explicit and wildly unrealistic promise to deliver negative inflation. He has not fulfilled that promise, and, in fact, inflation has been consistently above the Fed’s target rate. And while interest rates have fallen, they are still higher than they were pre-pandemic. It’s hard to see them falling further while inflation is still up above 2 percent.

*Inflation is lower than in 2022, but borrowing costs are still high.*

When elected officials ask me about this, I tell them exactly what an economics textbook would tell them: The [solution is deficit reduction](https://www.slowboring.com/p/the-case-for-progressive-austerity). I said this when [Joe Biden was president](https://www.slowboring.com/p/its-the-right-time-for-austerity), and it’s true today.

It still drives me a little bit insane that D.C. was deficit mad in 2010 and 2011 when nothing about the macroeconomic situation suggested that the budget deficit was a problem. In a different macroeconomic environment, though, deficit reduction is exactly what you need to bring interest rates and inflation down simultaneously. That’s why Bill Clinton made a [deficit reduction package](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omnibus_Budget_Reconciliation_Act_of_1993#:~:text=The%20Omnibus%20Budget%20Reconciliation%20Act,Revenue%20Reconciliation%20Act%20of%201993.) the center of his 1993 economic agenda. That’s how the idea of deficit reduction ended up with a political halo that it lost when I was in my late 20s.

It’s totally true that deficit reduction politics is kind of bummer politics.

Deficit reduction means that gimmicky, popular Trump tax proposals like “no tax on tips” are probably a bad idea. Deficit reduction means that Democrats should be restrained on new spending because [raising taxes is hard](https://www.slowboring.com/p/raising-taxes-is-hard), and they should probably be dedicating their “tax the rich” energy to deficit reduction.

Deficit reduction also means that something like Trump’s enormous increase in the immigration enforcement budget isn’t just a culture war gambit; it imposes real costs on the economy.

So I do understand why nobody is particularly eager to move into fiscal austerity mode. But if you want to address the public’s concerns, you need ideas that work.

#### This slop won’t do it

I not only sympathize with but even endorse the sense that there’s something off about an economy in which the kind of people who historically would have been buying starter homes are instead renting. The fact that this is happening to such a large extent that investment firms want to buy single-family homes and rent them out is a potent symbol of a broken housing market.

But banning the investor behavior genuinely does not accomplish anything.

If you’re renting because you can’t get a mortgage, then banning investors from buying single-family homes is not going to get you a mortgage. The right way to understand this proposal is that it’s [protectionism for small-time landlords](https://www.slowboring.com/p/protectionism-for-small-landlords). Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent [got this right](https://www.buzzfeed.com/briangalindo/scott-bessent-says-retirees-buying-12-homes-backlash) while making a gaffe — the person who is helped here is someone like Bessent’s very rich friends who apparently are buying “five, 10, 12 homes” to finance their retirements.

If you want people who are currently renting to be able to buy homes, you need some combination of reduced mortgage standards and more construction of starter homes. Trump is constantly winking at NIMBYs and doing things, like [imposing tariffs on Korean lumber](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cwyw3ynwe37o), that are counterproductive. But in principle, totally banal free-market conservative ideas could be a big help here, and he’s floundering for no reason.

This meatpacking idea I think we can be pretty confident won’t work.

In fact, [Tyson just settled](https://www.feedstuffs.com/livestock-and-poultry-market-news/tyson-settles-with-retailers-in-beef-antitrust-lawsuit) with retailers in a private antitrust lawsuit against the big beef firms a few weeks ago. On the one hand, you can say this shows that the people who’ve been complaining about concentration in meatpacking were onto something. Tyson and another company that settled earlier, though, [deny any wrongdoing](https://www.jdjournal.com/2026/01/02/tyson-foods-in-82-5-million-beef-price-fixing-settlement/) and say that a little settlement is just what you get when you come under this kind of political pressure. The settlements amount to about $140 million, which sounds like a very large number but Americans eat [83 pounds](https://utahstatemagazine.usu.edu/environment/real-beef-done-well-an-eco-friendlier-meal-using-smart-foodscapes/) of beef per person per year. Which is just to say that the alleged wrongdoing that the meatpackers are officially not admitting to is actually tiny relative to the beef market.

Meanwhile, though meatpacker profits did soar back in 2022 when inflation was surging nationally, this chart from Fred Ashton at the American Action Forum shows that [profits have fully normalized](https://www.americanactionforum.org/insight/unpacking-trumps-meat-packing-allegations/). It’s not clear what we’re investigating. The chart divides up the retail price of beef between the money that goes to the beef producers (blue line), the retailers (green line), and the packers (red line). As you can see, the packer share is low and at its standard historical level.

*[Fred Ashton](https://www.americanactionforum.org/insight/unpacking-trumps-meat-packing-allegations/) calculations based on [U.S.D.A. Meat Price Spread data](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/meat-price-spreads)*

Elected officials and news consumers should know that meatpacker concentration is a longstanding grievance of America’s *ranchers,* who feel that they should be able to get higher prices for their cattle.

Recent antitrust doctrine has downplayed this kind of complaint in favor of prioritizing consumer interests. If you want to argue that regulators should care less about the retail price of beef and more about the welfare of ranch owners, that’s a reasonable debate to have. But I suspect there’s just a tradeoff here where packers’ pricing power is mostly beneficial for consumers. It’s a two-sided market (buying from ranchers and selling to retailers) in which the downstream side is inherently more competitive because beef competes with other proteins. So the packers squeeze the ranchers and some of the benefit flows through to us. Note that the other big thing America’s ranchers want is [tariffs on foreign beef](https://www.theatlantic.com/newsletters/2025/12/cattle-ranchers-trump-beef-prices/685130/), which is unambiguously bad for consumers.

The credit-card price cap is a bit of a different story. This polls incredibly well and I would encourage Democrats to pass a law delivering what Trump is promising here.

Once you actually implement the interest rate cap, suddenly a bunch of people are going to be unable to get a credit card and they’ll be mad. It’s a popular policy that’s ideal to implement when the opposition is in the White House and will be left holding the bag when it backfires.

Ben Dinovelli makes a [game effort in the Washington Monthly](https://washingtonmonthly.com/2026/01/26/the-case-for-capping-credit-card-interest-rates/) to argue that there’s a competition problem here, but I feel like his heart isn’t really in it. He says “the 10 largest card issuers represent [83 percent](https://www.federalreserve.gov/publications/files/ccprofit2025.pdf) of the market.” That just doesn’t sound like very much concentration to me. The top 10 automakers have [94 percent of the American car market](https://www.storagecafe.com/blog/top-10-largest-car-manufacturers-in-the-us/).

Beyond that, even if this somehow did work and let people buy more stuff on cheap credit, that would only tend to push prices further up. At the end of the day, inflation is still a macroeconomic issue, and it needs a macroeconomic solution.

#### It’s time to get boring

If you don’t like fiscal austerity, the alternative is to focus on raising the productivity growth rate so that the split between inflation and real growth for any given increment of nominal spending improves.

This is, I guess, what both the Biden and Trump administrations have been casting around for as an alternative to unfun budgetary measures.

But realistic moves to increase productivity are also mostly boring, earnest, somewhat politically difficult reforms, not this kind of populist slop. Subsidies for ethanol, for example, are an absurd boondoggle that [turn land into energy](https://x.com/dwallacewells/status/2016137555968688276/photo/1) in a wildly inefficient way. Getting rid of them would have a modest positive impact on food prices.

You can just go on like this through the pointy-headed wonk classics. Tariffs are bad. The Jones Act is bad. Dentist “[scope of practice](https://www.slowboring.com/p/dentists-are-bad)” rules are bad. America’s pilot training times are [absurdly long for no good reason](https://www.slowboring.com/p/the-fed-cant-save-the-economy). The United States is afflicted by [protectionism for elevators](https://www.sightline.org/2026/01/11/video-fixing-north-americas-big-elevator-problem/), even though nobody would step into an elevator in Tokyo or Milan and fear for their life.

Just as in the [specific case of the stationary bandits of New York City](https://www.slowboring.com/p/the-stationary-bandits-of-new-york), these are mostly not particularly ideological problems or well-aligned with the main axis of partisan conflict.

Trump conceptualizes economic growth almost exclusively through the stock market, so he’s focused on promoting the interests of the big technology companies that are already successful. What he doesn’t have is an appreciation of markets or economic logic more broadly that would let him try to unlock these dozens of tiny drags on the growth environment.

In his defense, his predecessor didn’t either.

Dealing with annoying, long-running interest-group pathologies in a way that requires assembling bipartisan coalitions and working with earnest wonks on serious policy analysis is not as fun as trying to crush your enemies or deliver on your team’s long-held priorities.

As with the deficit reduction question, though, at some point people need to ask themselves what they actually believe. It definitely *seems* like voters want lower interest rates and lower prices. Even though you almost certainly cannot deliver actual deflation, it is certainly possible to make policy choices that tend toward less inflation and lower interest rates. If Trump thinks that’s important, he should try to make those choices.

If he doesn’t think affordability is important, then best of luck to him. But this kind of slop economics as a halfway house isn’t going to actually help anyone, and that isn’t going to help him.
