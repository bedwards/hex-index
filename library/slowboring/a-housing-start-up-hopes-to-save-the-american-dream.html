After years of reading each other’s work and debating America’s housing crisis online, four men who met through housing Twitter decided it was time to stop writing and start building. Today marks the official launch of the American Housing Corporation, a start-up based in Austin that aims to bring affordable family housing back to American cities and the American dream back to American families.

The founders — Bobby Fijan, a real estate developer; William Davis, a software and mechanical engineer known online as @YIMBYLAND; Riley Meik, a former aerospace engineer; and Harris Rothaermel, a software engineer — began working together at the beginning of 2024, united by frustration that existing solutions to housing scarcity weren’t solving the problem at scale. Each founder came in with a different angle on the same crisis, but what connected them was the conclusion that none of these perspectives mattered in isolation.

“To tackle the problem,” Fijan said in an interview with Slow Boring, “you need to be the manufacturing company, and you also need to be the developer.”

That insight became the foundation of the American Housing Corporation. Unlike many venture-backed housing start-ups that attempt to sell new construction technology to traditional developers, the American Housing Corporation decided to vertically integrate from the start. The company buys land, manufactures building components, acts as the general contractor, and ultimately sells or rents the homes itself.

The decision was shaped by watching earlier modular and prefab companies struggle. Their technology worked, but selling it to developers, who each had their own floor plans, incentives, and timelines, often didn’t.

The company’s approach draws inspiration from mid-century developer Joseph Eichler, whose distinctive homes transformed parts of the Bay Area. Fijan and Meik bonded over their shared admiration for Eichler’s philosophy: sell the lifestyle of a well-designed home, not the innovation behind it.

“Ultimately, what we’re really trying to do is just build something good for the customer,” Fijan said. “The product is not, like, the new Sears manufactured housing company, or to manufacture this, or module that, or analyze that. It’s just about building nice houses.”

The American Housing Corporation is targeting the “missing middle” — often multi-unit housing that falls between single-family homes and large apartment buildings in size. The company’s focus is particularly on townhouses, which Fijan argues are where the housing crisis hits hardest. Traditional builders can efficiently construct large apartment buildings in cities or single-family developments in the suburbs, but smaller infill projects of three to six townhouses in the urban centers where people want to live are paradoxically more difficult and expensive to build, even though they’re desperately needed.

“It’s frankly harder to build three townhouses than to build a 250-unit apartment building,” Fijan said.

The company uses structural insulated panels — thick, energy-efficient wall sections with mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems already installed. The panels are fire-resistant and perform like masonry or steel for buildings up to three stories. The key innovation is that the American Housing Corporation built its own press to produce panels in dimensions that fit inside standard shipping containers, allowing the company to manufacture in one location and ship economically across the country. This solves a major limitation of modular construction, which typically requires that factories be located near building sites.

The company recently completed its first three-story unit (a demonstration model, not a home for sale) in Austin in less than 12 weeks. Because of regulatory requirements, there will be a delay of several months before they can begin building commercial projects. But Fijan expects the company to be active in two markets, likely Texas and New Mexico, by year’s end, with additional states in pre-development.

Rather than marketing their homes as products of a tech start-up backed by venture capital, the American Housing Corporation plans to list them on conventional platforms like Zillow. The goal is for buyers to choose the homes because they fit their needs and budget, not because of how they were built.

With early funding from venture capital and institutional investors, including what Fijan describes as the “venture capital arm of a very large public entity investor,” their long-term ambitions are enormous: to eventually build a million homes per year, roughly 10 times more than D.R. Horton, which is currently the biggest homebuilder in America.

“The problem is worthy of that kind of goal,” Fijan said.

For now, the company is focused on a narrower mission: helping young families stay in cities longer, through urban or infill starter homes designed for the phase of life when Fijan said the American dream feels most fragile.

Supply-side wins

Cotati, Calif., becomes Sonoma County’s first city to adopt transit-oriented zoning. The Cotati City Council voted unanimously to adopt new transit-oriented zoning for land near its SMART train station, becoming the first city in Sonoma County to do so and adding to the growing number of California cities and towns implementing similar reforms. The move makes Cotati eligible for state infrastructure grants and clears the way for a mixed-use housing project on more than three acres adjacent to the station. Housing advocates cited by the Press Democrat point to the plan as a potential countywide model for walkable, lower-cost development.

D.C. Policy Center pushes regulatory reform. A new report from the D.C. Policy Center argues that moves such as eliminating minimum parking requirements, expanding by-right multifamily zoning, and streamlining permitting could significantly increase housing supply in transit-accessible neighborhoods.

Denver sets ambitious 2026 targets. Denver Mayor Mike Johnston unveiled a 2026 agenda committing the city to deliver 2,500 affordable housing units and permit an additional 5,000 homes, in a move that would tie housing production directly to broader economic development goals.

D.C.’s largest office-to-residential conversion breaks ground. Councilmember Brianne Nadeau highlighted the groundbreaking of the Geneva, a major office-to-residential conversion in Ward 1 set to create 532 new homes, including 60 permanently affordable units. The development, which Nadeau called the largest of its kind in the city’s history, repurposes underused office space to expand the housing supply, leveraging significant green-energy financing and marking a key step in D.C.’s broader strategy to increase affordable and mixed-use housing in downtown neighborhoods.

Local reform debates

San Jose rethinks inclusionary housing after programs fail to deliver. The city is considering revisions to its inclusionary housing requirements. The new proposal would shift affordability targets toward middle-income households after programs designed to encourage density failed to create meaningful housing development. City leaders are also considering enhancements to existing multifamily incentive programs, including tax reductions and fee waivers, to make building homes more financially feasible for developers.

“I would rather get the housing built — create homes and opportunities for people — grow the local economy and have an ongoing and growing tax base than fight over one-time fees, taxes, and other requirements that we’re not even getting because projects are too expensive to build and can’t secure financing,” San Jose Mayor Matt Mahan told the Mercury News. “We’re optimizing for the wrong thing. We need to optimize for the outcome at scale, which is building the housing we need where it makes sense.”

L.A. reconsiders its mansion tax. The Los Angeles City Council is considering changes to Measure ULA to address concerns that the tax on high-end property sales may be slowing down housing construction, even as the measure has generated more than $1 billion for affordable housing and homelessness programs. Councilmembers are poised to draft a proposal for the June ballot that could modify the tax to better support new housing development and affordability efforts in the region.

Philadelphia councilmember’s development ban accidentally triggers permit rush. Since Philadelphia Councilmember Jeffery Young introduced legislation in December to ban residential development around the former Hahnemann University Hospital site, developers have rushed to secure permits for 824 apartments in the area. The Philadelphia Inquirer reports that projects include conversions for hundreds of units in vacant hospital towers. Young argues the bill preserves future job-generating uses for the building, while critics say it amounts to illegal spot zoning that blocks transit-adjacent housing on a site that has sat vacant for six years.

San Diego Unified advances educator housing plan. The school district selected development proposals for three of five planned workforce housing sites, with about 1,500 affordable units for district employees. If built, the portfolio would roughly double the amount of educator workforce housing currently planned statewide.

Solutions and experiments

Tempe combines affordable housing with a fire station. The Phoenix suburb plans to co-locate 50 subsidized units with a new fire station on a vacant Big Lots site, improving both housing and emergency response times in an underserved area. It’s the metro area’s first such project and the first investment under Tempe’s voter-approved affordable housing bond.

Philadelphia has a $2 billion housing plan. The city’s “Housing Opportunities Made Easy” (HOME) plan aims to build and rehabilitate roughly 30,000 homes over four years, pairing affordability with neighborhood revitalization. Supporters argue that tackling dilapidated housing and expanding affordable options can reduce certain types of crime by stabilizing communities, using housing policy as public safety policy.

Colorado Springs is focused on urban renewal for attainable housing. The city is advancing two projects designed to increase housing affordable to households earning around the area median income, using tax increment financing to support infrastructure. The focus on “attainable” housing — not just deeply subsidized units — recognizes that middle-income families are also getting squeezed out.

California’s homelessness program shows early results. A collaborative program in Oceanside and Carlsbad reduced unsheltered homelessness by 17 percent after clearing a large creek encampment and rapidly connecting residents to housing with subsidies and wraparound services, Voice of San Diego reports. With grant funding set to expire, other California jurisdictions are paying attention to whether the model is scalable.

San Francisco supportive housing faces a budget squeeze. Supervisor Bilal Mahmood is pushing to increase funding for supportive housing services in the Tenderloin, after providers warned that Mayor Daniel Lurie’s proposed budget does not keep pace with rising labor costs.

Policies and politics

HUD orders citizenship verification for 200,000 tenants. The Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) has directed public housing authorities to verify the immigration status of nearly 200,000 tenants within 30 days, following a joint audit with the Department of Homeland Security. HUD says the review identified roughly 25,000 deceased tenants and nearly 6,000 individuals it claims are ineligible non-citizens. Agencies that fail to comply could face sanctions, and HUD has signaled it will attempt to recover payments it deems improper.

Wyoming lawmaker pitches $1-per-acre land sales. Representative Jacob Wasserburger introduced legislation to sell state land at $1 per acre in 10-acre parcels for single-family homes, branding it as an affordable housing strategy. Early analysis cited by WyoFile suggests far fewer acres would qualify than advertised, and critics argue the plan lacks infrastructure funding and would primarily benefit households already able to build.

Americans say affordability is getting worse. A recent national New York Times/Siena poll shows a majority of voters believe achieving a middle-class lifestyle, including homeownership, is harder than for the previous generation, with many expressing worry about affording rent and other costs. More than half of respondents said housing and education have become largely unaffordable, underscoring broad economic anxiety that is shaping public opinion and political priorities around housing policy.

Market context

Single-family home prices rose 0.6 percent in November, translating to a 1.9 percent annual increase, according to the Federal Housing Finance Agency. Housing costs nationwide remain a central affordability crisis, with rents and home prices far outpacing wage growth.

Democrats are pushing to expand housing supply and ease zoning restrictions to unlock dense development, while some Republican proposals focus on mortgage reforms, down-payment support, and tax incentives to reduce barriers to homebuying, the Los Angeles Times surmises. These reflect divergent party approaches to the same problem, but the scarcity certainly isn’t partisan.
