For today’s post, as a bit of a break from the news cycle, I wanted to think through an alternate history scenario involving a world leader who tried something dramatic that worked in the short term but unambiguously failed to achieve its goals in a broader sense.

An idea that seemed to fit the bill was Dwight Eisenhower’s handling of the Suez Crisis of 1956. This is a bit of an obscure episode. To summarize briefly (we’ll discuss more later), a new Arab nationalist government came to power in Egypt led by Gamal Abdel Nasser. Nasser, as an Arab nationalist, was supporting the revolutionary movement in Algeria and also backing forces in places like Iraq that were hostile to existing regimes aligned with British oil companies. This brought him into conflict with the governments of Britain and France, and in turn he was cozying up to the Soviet Union. He tried to get Western financial support for the Aswan Dam project, but was denied. So he decided to nationalize the Suez Canal, which at the time was operated by a private company owned by British and French investors. The United Kingdom and France responded by teaming up with Israel to invade Egypt.

Eisenhower was afraid that this would radicalize Arab public opinion against the West and push them all into the arms of the Soviet Union, so he leaned hard on the invaders to abandon the project and let Nasser seize the canal. He threatened to tank the British currency, [signaled](https://www.nam.ac.uk/explore/suez-crisis?utm_source=chatgpt.com#:~:text=The%20British%20asked%20for%20a%20loan%20from%20the%20International%20Monetary%20Fund%2C%20but%20the%20Americans%20refused%20it%20unless%20a%20ceasefire%20was%20agreed.%20They%20also%20threated%20to%20sell%20the%20US%20government%27s%20sterling%20bond%20holdings%2C%20which%20could%20devalue%20the%20pound%20and%20undermine%20Britain%E2%80%99s%20foreign%20exchange%20reserves.) the U.S. would not cushion the Europeans from the economic fallout, and otherwise made it clear that the U.S. was all in on forcing its closest allies to reverse course.

This worked in the sense that it got them to withdraw. But the strategic goal of getting Nasser to align with the U.S. didn’t work at all. His prestige in the region rose, he took Soviet financing to build the dam and became a major purchaser of Soviet arms, and he would later unify Egypt with Syria. The U.S. then ended up backing Israel versus Egypt in the Six Days War and the Yom Kippur War, with Egypt squarely aligned with the Soviet Union.

Given that siding with Egypt didn’t achieve what Eisenhower wanted, it seems natural to ask what would have happened if he’d just been more chill and said this was between Egypt, the U.K., and France?

#### Historians like Ike

As I read more about this, I was interested to learn that the conventional wisdom among historians seems to be that Eisenhower did the right thing here.

That’s partly because most scholars are broadly sympathetic to the post-colonial movements of the 1950s and 1960s and think it’s unfortunate that the U.S. frequently found itself on the wrong side of them.

In the case of Cuba, the widely accepted view is that American hostility to Cuban nationalism ended up pushing the revolutionary movement into the arms of the Soviet Union in a way that could have been avoided.

Vietnam is perhaps the clearest case, though.

The U.S. spent many years, at great cost to itself and even greater cost to Vietnam, trying to stop the anti-French Communists from taking over the country because we feared a leftist Vietnam would be a pawn of China and the U.S.S.R. We failed, but in the long run it turned out that Vietnamese leftists are also Vietnamese nationalists who don’t want to be under China’s thumb and are happy to collaborate strategically with the U.S. It seems like we could have gotten to this same outcome much more quickly and with much less bloodshed by being more open to Ho Chi Minh’s point of view.

Ike’s approach to the Suez Crisis, in this view, is a positive example of how the U.S. should have handled the post-colonial era more broadly.

When Britain and France formally walked away from their colonies in the Middle East, they set up these tame successor regimes that maintained sweetheart deals for Western oil companies. Arab nationalists hated this idea, and often ended up aligning with the Soviet Union to seek change. America, rather than being a dupe for British and French imperialism, should have acted more consistently like Ike. On this view,[1](#footnote-1) the problem is that Eisenhower didn’t follow up on the opening with Nasser. He continued to back France in Algeria, sent Marines to Lebanon in 1958 to prop up a pro-Western regime, and was hostile to the leaders of the [14 July Revolution in Iraq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/14_July_Revolution#Immediate_effects).

I don’t think this critique makes a ton of sense. There’s no world in which the U.S. is going to be indifferent to the overthrow of friendly regimes. Nasser’s Arab nationalist view held that the entire status-quo political order throughout the region, not just in Algeria, was a direct instantiation of Western colonialism and needed to be overthrown.

That was simply not the view of the U.S., and it’s not really a view that makes sense: Countries like Qatar and the United Arab Emirates that have maintained regime continuity are not Western colonies. And Nasser extended this view to the entire existence of Israel, precipitating multiple wars. Even at a time of much lesser American political support for the Jewish state, that wasn’t plausibly going to be America’s view. So the conflict we got was more or less inevitable, and the attempted reconciliation was a somewhat pointless detour.

#### The neutral road not taken

If the U.S. had simply stayed on the sidelines, it seems unlikely that the invaders could have achieved their full objectives.

France in particular had far-reaching aspirations to topple Nasser and install a new regime that would halt support for rebels in Algeria, which supposedly would make the French war there winnable.

The problem is that subduing Egypt militarily was not really an easier objective than subduing Algeria. Yes, France had allies in Egypt. But Britain was willing to settle for less (forcing Nasser to give back the canal), and Israel was along for the ride to degrade the Egyptian military but obviously had no desire for a long-term engagement in domestic Egyptian issues.

In the short term, the likely outcomes were either that Nasser would strike a deal on the canal to save his own skin or else he would be overthrown by someone from the inside. Britain wouldn’t have been able to re-establish quasi-colonial control of Egypt and France wasn’t going to be able to fundamentally shift the situation in Algeria. But the situation could have been resolved in a way that kept Britain actively engaged in the Middle East, retained Anglo-French control of the canal, and dealt a humiliating blow to Egypt’s pretensions to regional leadership — especially because, absent an unlikely diplomatic deal, Israel could have held onto the Sinai Peninsula as a bargaining chip for the future.

In this world, anti-Western nationalism continues to roil Iraq and other countries and progressive scholars blame Eisenhower’s handling of the Suez Crisis for that.

Realistically, though, the upshot of Egypt’s humiliation would be to take some of the wind out of the sails of pan-Arabism.

In the real world, the Suez Crisis was followed by a [short-lived political union between Egypt and Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Republic) and by an Egyptian-led military coalition fighting Israel in the Six Days War. The biggest impact of an earlier clipping of Nasser’s wings would be that this almost certainly doesn’t happen.

It’s not as if everything becomes rainbows and unicorns between Israel and its neighbors. But by all accounts, the Jordanian government was not enthusiastic about going to war in 1967, and simply felt it was politically untenable to stay out of Egypt’s fight, given both anti-Israel public sentiment and Nasser’s prestige as a leader. With Egypt more chastened, Israel would continue to be plagued by [Palestinian guerrillas operating out of the Jordanian-controlled West Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_fedayeen#1956_to_1967), but there would be no actual war with Jordan and, as a result, the West Bank never becomes controlled by Israel. In the real world, the Jordanian government [got tired of independent armed P.L.O. activity by 1970-1971 and shut it down](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_September).

#### A different Palestine

That [Black September](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_September) conflict between the Jordanian government and Palestinian militants happened *after* Jordan lost control of the West Bank in the Six Days War.

In our timeline, that war doesn’t happen and Jordan doesn’t go to war with Israel and therefore doesn’t lose the West Bank. This means the Palestinian element inside Jordanian society is much larger, and is perhaps able to prevail and in effect turn Jordan (plus the West Bank) into a Republic of Palestine.

In the real world, American diplomacy eventually got Israel to return the Sinai to Egypt in exchange for recognition as part of a larger swap that brought Egypt out of Soviet alignment and into the American sphere of influence. In the counterfactual world, things take the same trajectory. All the Soviet arms purchases in the world aren’t going to dislodge the British from the Suez Canal or Israel from the Sinai. But the U.S. is able to facilitate a deal whereby Egypt gets the canal (in exchange for monetary compensation) and the Sinai (in exchange for peace with Israel) and receives American aid and military equipment.

The result is that by the end of the 1970s the Arab-Israel conflict is pretty much a state-to-state conflict centering Israel versus Jordan. The official diplomatic stance in this conflict (pre-1967 in the real world and ongoing in the alternate timeline) is that Israel wants access to the holy sites in East Jerusalem and in the West Bank more broadly. Jordan’s position is that Palestinian refugees who fled during the original [1948 war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1948_Arab%E2%80%93Israeli_War) should be able to return to their homes.

This is the kind of dispute that is very much amenable to compromise, and it ends with some kind of cash compensation to resolve the refugee situation and a deal around the ability of Israeli tourists to visit East Jerusalem.

The Jordan-Israel relationship is tense and the border comes to be characterized by a giant separation wall to prevent guerrilla infiltration, but both sides derive economic benefit from peace and foreigners’ ability to visit confidently, so things basically work out. The secular aims of Zionism are secure, and the more expansive religiously motivated nationalism remains perennially marginal.

#### The contingent Israel-Palestine conflict

My basic take here is that a lot of people read aspects of the post-1967 Israel-Palestine conflict — in which Israel is ruling over a large swath of territory inhabited by non-citizen Palestinians — back to an earlier period of time when Israel’s disputes with its Arab neighbors had a very different character.

On the one hand, that ahistorical view bolsters the basic notion that Eisenhower standing up to Israel was admirable. Many people (myself included) believe that it would have been constructive for Bill Clinton to draw a harder line on settlements, or for George W. Bush to see rebooting peace talks after the Second Intifada as critical to America’s regional strategy, or for Barack Obama to have successfully leaned on the Israeli electorate to return a Kadima government in 2009. So the idea of a time when the domestic politics were different and an American president would simply lean hard on its client state has nostalgic appeal in some quarters.

But the main elements of the occupation simply did not exist in 1956.

More broadly, it’s the occupation that was inaugurated in 1967 that makes the Israel-Palestine conflict so intractable. The bitter feelings and ill-will about displacement engendered by the 1948 war are understandable.

But a *lot* of people around the world were violently displaced in the aftermath of World War II. And this led to a *lot* of bitter feelings. The government of West Germany, for example, [did not accept the new borders of Poland until 1970](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oder–Neisse_line#West_Germany). Italy did not officially accept its [loss of territory south of Trieste until a 1975 treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Osimo). Which is just to say that 50 to 60 years ago it was not particularly unusual for there to be unresolved diplomatic issues lingering from the events of the 1940s. It was the legacy of the 1967 war that transformed this from a normal nasty post-WWII situation into a highly unusual one.

But that didn’t have to happen.

The Jordanian authorities were aware all along that the war was a dubious proposition. The Suez Crisis, however, created an atmosphere of hubris inside the Egyptian government. They believed that they had squared off against not only Israel but also Britain and France and won.

What really happened is that the Eisenhower administration waged economic warfare against Britain on Egypt’s behalf, leading the anti-Egyptian coalition to collapse. There was, however, no real need for the U.S. to do this. The effort to tempt Nasser out of alignment with the Soviet Union completely failed and never made sense in light of his ideology. Gassing him up like that and encouraging the British to completely withdraw from involvement in the Middle East only made things worse.
