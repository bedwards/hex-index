The 2026 Milan-Cortina Winter Olympics will feature athletes who are millionaires, Instagram influencers, and employees of multinational corporations. None of this would have been possible 40 years ago because, in addition to the comparably recent advent of social media, until the 1980s the Olympic Games were reserved for amateur athletes.

In 1984, if you accepted free ski equipment from a manufacturer, you could lose your Olympic eligibility. By 2026, the athletes who do not have equipment sponsors are the oddities. Those who are predicted to be top competitors at the Olympic level have almost certainly already been identified and sponsored by some company like Nike or Red Bull or Toyota. There are plenty of exceptions, though, including Stephen Nedoroscik, a.k.a. the “pommel horse guy,” who emerged in 2024 as a dark horse and won a bronze medal with very little prior public notice.

The professionalization of the Olympics happened quickly and with remarkably little public debate. But the shift from amateurism went beyond just allowing athletes to make money. It fundamentally changed what the Olympics are, who competes, and what we’re watching.

#### **Amateurism’s last stand**

The 1988 Calgary Winter Olympics marked the end of the [era of amateurism](https://www.nytimes.com/1981/11/08/sports/the-games-olympic-athletes-must-play-for-money.html).

Tennis had already gone professional at the Summer Games in Seoul that same year, with German Steffi Graf achieving the first-ever “[Golden Slam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Slam_\(tennis\))” when she won a gold medal in addition to all four major tournaments. Winter sports had held out longer, though. The reasons were partly ideological and partly practical, in that many winter sports had smaller audiences and more limited commercial appeal, so there wasn’t as much money to professionalize anyway.

But the old amateur rules were byzantine and increasingly absurd. Figure skaters could perform in ice shows but [could not be paid](https://www.nytimes.com/1980/05/12/archives/for-skater-and-others-medals-are-costly.html), instead receiving reimbursements for “expenses.” Some athletes developed [workarounds](https://www.nytimes.com/1981/11/08/sports/the-games-olympic-athletes-must-play-for-money.html) where equipment manufacturers would pay money into trust funds managed by national federations. The rules created a system where countries with [state sports programs](https://www.nytimes.com/1989/12/11/sports/sports-in-east-germany-a-system-under-siege-e-german-perquisites-draw-fire.html) — particularly the Soviet Union and East Germany — had a massive advantage because their athletes received support that was not technically payment.

When the International Olympic Committee finally [abandoned amateurism](https://www.nytimes.com/1986/03/30/sports/ioc-expected-to-ease-amateur-policy.html) in the early 1990s, the stated rationale was leveling the playing field. If Eastern bloc athletes were effectively professionals funded by their governments, why handicap other athletes who needed private sponsorship to train full-time?

#### **The period of transformation**

The transformation of the games into today’s highly professional, celebrity-driven era happened in stages.

**1992 Albertville, France**: During [the first “open” Winter Olympics](http://washingtonpost.com/archive/sports/1992/01/05/no-longer-anything-amateur-about-us-olympic-effort/95e0e8c2-afe6-4a9b-bb0a-548d0685ba33/), which also marked the first games since the [collapse of the Soviet Union](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1992-02-09-sp-3577-story.html), figure skaters could finally acknowledge their ice show income, and alpine skiers no longer needed elaborate trust fund schemes.

“Almost anything goes,” Christine Brennan wrote in [the Washington Post](http://washingtonpost.com/archive/sports/1992/01/05/no-longer-anything-amateur-about-us-olympic-effort/95e0e8c2-afe6-4a9b-bb0a-548d0685ba33/) in 1992, “as long as it is funneled to the athlete through a trust fund, which is administered by the national governing body of each sport.”

But the National Hockey League (N.H.L.) [kept its players from competing](https://www.cbc.ca/archives/canada-olympics-nhl-1995-1.6320506).

**1998 Nagano, Japan**: The N.H.L. [shut down its season for two weeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_hockey_at_the_1998_Winter_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_tournament) to send players to Japan. This was the big symbolic break, as the Winter Olympics’ marquee team sport now featured the world’s best professionals. N.H.L. Commissioner Gary Bettman sold the decision as [growing the game](https://www.leadersmag.com/issues/2013.4_oct/PDFs/LEADERS-Gary-Bettman-National-Hockey-League.pdf) globally, and international star players on Canadian and American teams [signaled their approval](https://www.nytimes.com/1998/01/18/sports/hockey-with-all-star-game-olympics-nhl-tries-extend-its-global-reach.html).

The games [didn’t go so well](https://thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/olympic-hockey-meltdown) for the Canadians or the Americans … but that’s a story for another time.

**2006 Turin, Italy, and 2010 Vancouver, Canada**: These games marked the beginning of the social media era. Athletes were starting to build personal brands. Shaun White became a [household name](https://www.nytimes.com/2006/03/19/sports/othersports/white-leads-the-way-in-making-helmets-cool.html) not just for winning gold in 2006 but for his Red Bull sponsorship and video game deals. Ahead of the 2010 Olympics, the energy drink company built White a [private halfpipe](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xk4p9u02BS0) in Colorado and began making videos with the snowboarder.

**2014 Sochi, Russia**: In 2014, Forbes wrote that the winter games were “dubbed the ‘[digital games](https://www.forbes.com/sites/aliciajessop/2014/02/24/the-growth-of-social-media-usage-poses-new-concerns-for-olympic-games-organizing-committees/).’” Olympic athletes had become simultaneously content creators, brand ambassadors, and professional athletes. The distinction between “amateur” and “professional” had become meaningless.

That year, the International Olympic Committee (I.O.C.) launched the [Olympic Athletes’ Hub](https://www.olympics.com/en/news/sochi-2014-social-media-metrics-hot-numbers-cool-conversations-all-yours), a social media platform that combined feeds from Olympians across Instagram, Facebook, and Twitter (now X). The I.O.C. found that Olympians and their teams made 90,000 social media updates in total, with top athletes gaining hundreds of thousands of followers and likes during the games.

#### **The professionalization dividend**

The case for professionalization is straightforward: it made the Olympics more fun to watch and fairer to athletes.

Consider hockey. The 1980 “[Miracle on Ice](https://www.nytimes.com/1980/02/23/archives/us-defeats-soviet-squad-in-olympic-hockey-by-43-us-six-turns-back.html)” is one of the sport’s great underdog stories because the U.S. team was composed of college players and one returning Olympian, yet they faced Soviet professionals (technically “amateurs” supported by the Red Army). The Soviet team was a four-time defending gold medalist, but the United States \*SPOILER ALERT\* won. It was romantic, and I’m a sucker for a great sports story, but it was lesser hockey compared to the N.H.L.-era Olympic tournaments, which feature higher quality play.

While the 1980 game is an enduring moment in history, the 2010 Vancouver final between the United States and Canada is considered among many hockey fans to be the [best hockey game](https://www.espn.com/olympics/winter/2014/icehockey/story/_/id/10381624/2014-sochi-olympics-oral-history-2010-gold-medal-game) ever played.

Beyond the marquee sports, professionalization helped athletes in less popular sports sustain their careers. Before the 1990s, almost all athletes had to [work regular jobs](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1985-06-27-fi-10910-story.html) while training. (This is still [true for many athletes](https://www.washingtonpost.com/posteverything/wp/2016/08/10/i-won-gold-in-1984-like-many-olympians-today-i-worked-day-jobs-to-get-there/) today.) The sports were accessible mainly to people wealthy enough to not need employment or to citizens of countries with state sports programs. Professionalization, via sponsorships and prize money from World Cup circuits, made it possible for talented athletes from more countries to compete.

The medal count data supports this conclusion (though there may be additional reasons for increased participation). In [1988](https://www.olympics.com/en/olympic-games/calgary-1988/medals), 17 countries won at least one medal in Calgary, with 11 countries winning gold medals. In 2022 in Beijing, that number was 23 out of the 29 countries that earned medals.

#### **The case against professionalization**

Even if you support professionalization, some concerns are worth taking seriously.

The most obvious loss is the underdog narrative. The Miracle on Ice doesn’t really happen in an era of professional hockey. [Eddie “the Eagle” Edwards](https://www.bbc.com/sport/articles/c8rm8zp2n0po), the British ski jumper who finished last at Calgary but became a folk hero, would not qualify today. In fact, his involvement played a large role in the end of amateurism. Following his performance in 1988, the I.O.C. instituted what became known as the [Eddie the Eagle Rule](https://www.nbcsportsphiladelphia.com/beijing-2022-winter-olympics/who-is-eddie-the-eagle-and-how-he-still-impacts-the-olympics/198595/), requiring Olympic hopefuls to compete in international events and be placed in the top 30 per cent or the top 50 competitors, whichever is fewer.

The Olympics are now more competitive — an actual display of the many of the best athletes in the world — but they are also more predictable.

There’s also a legitimate question about whether professionalization actually leveled the playing field or just changed who had the advantage. Under amateurism, countries with state programs dominated. Under professionalism, wealthy countries with well-developed pro-sports leagues and sponsorship markets dominate. In the winter, Norway still wins the most medals, and the United States, Russia, and China remain top competitors.

The more subtle loss is cultural. Professionalization has created a class of full-time Olympic athletes who, in some cases, have more in common with each other than they do with their fellow citizens.

This is incredibly visible in figure skating, where top competitors [train at the same facilities](https://www.wavy.com/news/figure-skaters-often-train-with-their-biggest-rivals-but-why/) (most famously in [Canada](https://time.com/6146993/ice-dancing-olympics-2022-teams/), regardless of nationality), have coaches who work with multiple national teams, and compete against each other constantly on the [Grand Prix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISU_Grand_Prix_of_Figure_Skating) circuit. When they meet at the Olympics, they’re colleagues having a particularly important day at work instead of representatives of their countries meeting for the first time.

Perhaps the most interesting case study in Olympic professionalization is hockey, because it is the one sport for which professionalization has been somewhat reversed.

The N.H.L. participated in the Winter Games from 1998 through 2014. Then it [skipped 2018](https://time.com/4947041/nhl-players-2018-winter-olympics/) due to disputes with the I.O.C. over who would cover costs, including for travel, insurance, and accommodations, which the I.O.C. had previously handled. The league planned to return for 2022, but [Covid threw a wrench in that](https://www.houstonchronicle.com/texas-sports-nation/general/article/AP-source-NHL-to-withdraw-from-Olympics-after-16720720.php).

The problem is structural, due entirely to the players’ full time jobs as professional athletes. Olympic hockey happens in February, right in the middle of the N.H.L. season. Taking a [two-week break](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EBK2l8R1j28) disrupts the league schedule, risks player injuries, and doesn’t generate direct revenue for team owners. The N.H.L. has leverage because it is the only premier professional hockey league, and is ostensibly home to the best players in the world.

This creates a bizarre situation in which the Winter Olympics’ showcase team sport might or might not feature the best players, depending on negotiations between the I.O.C. and the league. Hockey has achieved professionalization’s logical endpoint: The Olympics need professional athletes, but professional leagues don’t necessarily need the Olympics.

Another difficult aspect of Olympic professionalization is that most sports — especially winter sports — don’t pay very well. Yes, Mikaela Shiffrin and Shaun White have made millions, but most Olympic athletes earn modest incomes from a patchwork of national federation support, small sponsorships, and prize money. Professionalization made the Olympics accessible to more athletes, but it didn’t make most winter sports lucrative.

For athletes who compete in less buzzy sports, sponsorships can be hard to come by.

American women’s rugby player Ilona Maher started posting videos documenting her time in Tokyo and quickly found it to be a way to get sponsorships and make more money as a female athlete in an oft-overlooked sport. Maher was a professional athlete, but she was not making enough money to be one full time, so she turned to content creation to supplement her income.

“I don’t think it was really the way that I wanted, to be a content creator, but I just saw the value in it and I put my time and effort into it because I want to be able to play the sport, but I also want to have a comfortable life and be able to live well,” she told [Olympics.com](https://www.olympics.com/en/news/ilona-maher-usa-rugby-sevens-star). “I knew that it was a really important avenue to take.”

#### **Professionalization improved the Winter Olympics more than it harmed them**

The competition is better. Athletes are compensated for their work, even if modestly. The old amateur system was hypocritical and advantaged countries with state sports programs. The loss of underdog stories is real but probably not worth maintaining an unfair system to preserve.

But it’s important to be clear-eyed about the fact that the Olympics have fundamentally changed and are now part of the global sports-entertainment industry, and that the athletes are workers in that industry. The romantic notion of the Olympics as separate from commerce was always somewhat false, but it’s now completely abandoned. That’s fine, maybe even good, but it’s worth acknowledging what was lost.
