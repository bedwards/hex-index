The Senate is currently voting on a deal struck Thursday that separates Department of Homeland Security (D.H.S.) funding from five other spending bills and gives lawmakers two weeks to negotiate a new deal on immigration enforcement.

If this vote passes both the Senate and the House (likely on Monday), D.H.S. will be funded with a two-week continuing resolution as both parties attempt to hash out a longer-term deal for the department. Democrats are demanding concessions on federal immigration enforcement, including an end to roving patrols, tighter warrant rules, use-of-force accountability, and requirements that agents wear identification and body cameras. Full-year funding for the other agencies will pass unobstructed. If the vote fails, the federal government will be partially shut down until an agreement can be reached.

Republicans control both houses of Congress, but some Democratic senators are required to overcome a filibuster in the upper chamber. With Senate Democrats hanging together, President Donald Trump and Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer are negotiating restrictions on immigration enforcement as the price for Democratic cooperation on a funding bill that would continue the normal operations of departments such as defense, labor, and health and human services through September.

But the deeper story is an institutional design failure that has allowed immigration enforcement to operate without the basic rules and oversight that apply to virtually every other form of law enforcement in America.

Immigration enforcement, in principle, applies to non-citizens, and for that reason agencies like Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) and Customs and Border Protection (C.B.P.) operate under norms and rules that diverge from mainstream American law enforcement. Sometimes this happens deliberately (as with administrative warrants) and sometimes it happens through neglect (as with body cameras). The result is a system that operates with less oversight and transparency, a reality that has been increasingly on display as the Trump administration aggressively deploys immigration-enforcement personnel on the streets of American cities.

The political challenge is that immigration is such a polarized issue that basic governance questions get subsumed into larger debates about whether there should be more or less enforcement. But, crucially, enforcement is a separate policy from governance, and the former isn’t what this article is about. You can believe in robust immigration enforcement *and* believe that agents should wear cameras and need judicial warrants to enter homes. You can believe in generous asylum policies *and* believe that when deportations happen, they should follow clear procedures with proper documentation.

#### **The Minneapolis shootings changed the conversation**

[Less than a week ago](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2026/01/24/us/minneapolis-shooting-alex-pretti-timeline.html), C.B.P. agents shot and killed Alex Pretti, an intensive care nurse and Minneapolis resident, following what the [administration initially described as an assault on federal officers](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/25/politics/trump-officials-shifting-rhetoric-alex-pretti). White House Senior Advisor Stephen Miller [called](https://x.com/StephenM/status/2015127971485413805?s=20) Pretti a “domestic terrorist” and would-be “assassin.” But [internal C.B.P. assessments](https://www.mprnews.org/story/2026/01/28/internal-review-contradicts-white-house-narrative-of-prettis-death) make no mention of Pretti attacking officers or threatening them with weapons. The disconnect between the initial public narrative and the actual evidence is stark, and it is proof of why accountability mechanisms matter.

Throughout January, as the Trump administration’s immigration enforcement ramped up dramatically, there have been multiple shootings — including the killing of Renee Good — videos of unidentified masked agents confronting and arresting residents, and reports of agents attempting to [enter Ecuador’s consulate](https://www.reuters.com/world/americas/ecuador-says-ice-agent-tried-enter-consulate-minneapolis-2026-01-28/) in Minneapolis. Republican Senator Bill Cassidy called for a “[full joint federal and state investigation](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/republican-calls-are-growing-for-a-deeper-investigation-into-fatal-minneapolis-shooting-of-alex-pretti),” warning that “[the credibility of ICE and D.H.S. are at stake](https://thehill.com/homenews/senate/5705206-cassidy-ice-dhs-concerns-minneapolis-shooting/).”

When a G.O.P. senator from Louisiana who is currently facing a [primary by a Trump-endorsed candidate](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/2026-election/rep-julia-letlow-launches-louisiana-senate-primary-bill-cassidy-rcna254950) is raising concerns about federal law-enforcement overreach, you know the politics have shifted.

#### **The body camera gap**

For the record, ICE already has a body camera policy. President Joe Biden issued an [executive order in 2022](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2022/05/31/2022-11810/advancing-effective-accountable-policing-and-criminal-justice-practices-to-enhance-public-trust-and) requiring federal law enforcement to use body cameras, and ICE issued updated [guidance in 2024](https://www.ice.gov/news/releases/ice-announces-updated-policy-body-worn-cameras) directing agents to wear cameras “in all aspects of ICE enforcement activities conducted by ICE personnel in furtherance of the ICE mission, excluding certain investigative activities.” Trump [rescinded Biden’s order](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/DCPD-202500115/pdf/DCPD-202500115.pdf) shortly after he began his second term, but in February 2025 ICE once again published an [updated policy](https://www.ice.gov/doclib/foia/policy/19010.3.pdf) that cameras should be activated “as soon as practicable at the beginning of an enforcement activity” including during arrests and searches.

But ICE claims not to have the resources to give cameras to all its officers. The [February policy explicitly states](https://www.ice.gov/doclib/foia/policy/19010.3.pdf) that “enterprise-wide implementation shall be dependent on availability of appropriated funding resources.” According to the Washington Post, ICE had distributed about [4,400 cameras for about 22,000 officers](https://www.washingtonpost.com/immigration/2026/01/24/ice-bodycams-cameras-shootings-immigration/) as of June 2025.

Also according to [the Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/immigration/2026/01/24/ice-bodycams-cameras-shootings-immigration/), D.H.S. actually proposed reducing spending on body cameras in its budget. So the policy is simultaneously official and unfunded, making it an institutional reform that exists only on paper.

#### **Why don’t immigration agents follow the same rules as cops?**

Eight states now [mandate body cameras](https://www.ncsl.org/civil-and-criminal-justice/body-worn-camera-laws-database#:~:text=Eight%20states%20now%20mandate%20the,for%20up%20to%20one%20year.) for all law enforcement statewide, and many police departments have had them for years. The [basic architecture](https://nij.ojp.gov/topics/articles/body-worn-cameras-what-evidence-tells-us#:~:text=Video%20captured%20by%20body%2Dworn,consistent%20with%20the%20assigned%20initiatives.) is well understood: Officers activate cameras during enforcement actions, footage gets stored securely, and there are protocols for when footage can be reviewed or released.

Immigration enforcement, meanwhile, operates under a completely different set of rules. ICE and C.B.P. agents can conduct arrests using [administrative warrants](https://www.ice.gov/immigration-enforcement-frequently-asked-questions#:~:text=ICE%20uses%20two%20types%20of,do%20ICE%20officials%20wear%20masks?), which are documents signed by immigration officers, not judges. Unlike [judicial warrants](https://www.motionlaw.com/the-difference-between-judicial-and-administrative-warrants/#:~:text=A%20judicial%20warrant%20is%20an%20official%20court,search%20of%20private%20property%2C%20seizure%2C%20or%20arrest), these don’t authorize entry into private spaces without consent. But in practice, the distinction can be blurry, especially when agents are masked and unidentified.

That lack of clarity creates obvious problems. When police make an arrest, there are usually body cameras, dash cameras, multiple officers with badge numbers, and clear procedures for use of force. When ICE makes an arrest, there may well be none of that. Agents can wear masks, refuse to show identification beyond a generic badge, and operate without cameras. If something goes wrong, there may be no independent record of what happened.

The argument for this setup is [operational security](https://www.ice.gov/about-ice/opr#:~:text=What%20is%20OPR?,homeland%20security%20and%20public%20safety.); immigration enforcement is different from regular policing because it deals with national security concerns and sensitive investigations. But that doesn’t explain why agents need to cover their faces when arresting someone for a civil violation. It doesn’t explain why cameras that are mandatory for F.B.I. agents executing search warrants aren’t mandatory for ICE agents doing the same thing.

#### **The warrant problem**

The Democrats’ demands also reportedly include requirements for [judicial warrants](https://www.npr.org/2026/01/29/nx-s1-5693050/trump-minneapolis-government-shutdown-funding-immigration-republicans#:~:text=What%20Democrats%20want:%20judicial%20warrants,method%20of%20entering%20private%20residences.) in certain circumstances. This gets at another fundamental issue of the role of administrative versus judicial warrants.

Local police need a judicial warrant signed by a judge and based on probable cause to enter someone’s home and make an arrest. ICE can use an administrative warrant, which is signed by an immigration officer and doesn’t require judicial review. The legal distinction is that immigration violations are civil matters, not criminal ones, so the [Fourth Amendment protections work differently](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB10362).

The question is what level of judicial oversight should be required when federal agents conduct home raids. Whether a violation is classified as civil or criminal doesn’t change the practical reality that armed federal agents are breaking into homes and taking people into custody. The person being arrested doesn’t experience it as meaningfully different from a criminal arrest. And the potential for abuse — using excessive force, violating people’s rights — is certainly just as real.

#### **What might good policy look like?**

If ICE is going to exist, it needs guardrails. If you were designing immigration enforcement from scratch with consideration of both effectiveness and accountability, you would probably want agents to be subject to the same constraints that govern other law-enforcement entities:

**Universal body cameras**: Every ICE and C.B.P. officer conducting enforcement actions should have a camera. I have not done the math, but if local police departments in small towns can afford them, I imagine D.H.S. can afford them. And following the model used by many police departments, cameras should [activate automatically](https://www.dhs.gov/science-and-technology/saver/st-automatic-activation-body-cameras) in certain contexts, for example when an officer draws a weapon or when a vehicle pursuit begins. Relying on officers to remember to turn them on creates gaps in coverage.

**Clear identification requirements**: Agents should wear visible badges with numbers and should be required to identify themselves. Operational security concerns can be addressed through redaction of footage and careful release protocols. Agents should not need to operate anonymously if they are acting within the bounds of human rights and civil liberties.

**Judicial warrants for home entries**: If you’re going to break down someone’s door, a judge should have reviewed the evidence first. This is a basic check on power that exists in virtually every other context. The civil-criminal distinction shouldn’t exempt ICE from this requirement.

**Independent review of use-of-force incidents**: When ICE or C.B.P. agents shoot someone, there should be an independent investigation, not just an internal review. The footage should be released promptly unless there are specific, articulable reasons not to.

None of these constraints would prevent immigration enforcement from doing its job. They would just ensure that the job is done with appropriate oversight and accountability.

#### **The shutdown politics are a symptom**

Accountability measures shouldn’t require crisis negotiations that threaten to bring the federal government to a halt. Rather, they should be the default setting for how federal law enforcement operates.

The fact that the Democrats have to threaten a government shutdown to get these reforms reveals that without external pressure, the system defaults to minimal accountability. ICE had body camera policies on the books but no funding to implement them. The Trump administration has the authority to require cameras but chose to reduce funding for them instead. Absent a forcing mechanism like shutdown negotiations, these reforms don’t happen.

If, for now, the Senate avoids a partial shutdown, who knows if the negotiations will produce some kind of deal in two weeks. Maybe Democrats get some version of their demands. Maybe they don’t, and this whole episode becomes another data point in the ongoing dysfunction.

But either way, the underlying problem will remain. Immigration enforcement lacks the basic institutional safeguards that we expect from law enforcement in America. That’s a design choice, not an inevitability, that is increasingly hard for [Americans to defend](https://www.theargumentmag.com/p/immigration-is-turning-into-a-disaster) as enforcement scales up and abuse grows.

Whether this moment of crisis leads to actual reform depends on whether enough people decide that accountability mechanisms aren’t negotiable add-ons but basic features of how law enforcement should work. The fact that this is a shutdown-triggering debate, and that body cameras and identification requirements are this hotly negotiated, indicates how far America still has to go.
