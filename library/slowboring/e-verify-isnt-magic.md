**[Nicholas:](https://open.substack.com/pub/matthewyglesias/p/sunday-mailbag-thread-484?utm_campaign=comment-list-share-cta&utm_medium=web&comments=true&commentId=205330582) I was traveling on Sunday and surprised to see Amtrak had pre-emptively canceled most trains between Boston and New York. Is this just an extension of pandemic-era malaise leading Amtrak to say “let’s just not do it?” Is there a technical reason they can’t run the trains in the snow? This probably sounds like a dumb question, but I’m curious what the actual limitations are and chatgpt is not giving satisfactory answers. The MBTA was still operating on a normal schedule between Boston and Providence and Southern RI. As far as I can tell, so was Metro North between New Haven and New York.**

Very large volumes of snow can prevent trains from operating, but, generally speaking, heavy rail is one of the modes of transportation that is most resilient to snow, as seen in the continued operation of Boston’s M.B.T.A. commuter rail. Here in D.C., the metro system has been running even as almost everything else in the area is paralyzed.

My understanding is that Amtrak behaves more conservatively than a commuter railroad with this kind of thing because the potential failure mode of an intercity train getting stuck in a remote location is a lot worse. If you start running the trains and the snow is worse than expected and then you end up with a train full of people stuck outside of Elkton, Md., you have a big problem on your hands. Shorter-range services have an easier time getting everyone to a station in case of emergency. Is that a good reason in overall cost-benefit terms? I don’t really know. But unlike some stuff Amtrak does, it doesn’t strike me as unreasonable on its face.

The biggest weakness of E-Verify, as I understand it, is that the system basically confirms that the identifying documents a worker presents represent the identity of an authorized worker. It does not confirm that the person presenting the documents is in fact the person who the documents say they are. I wrote about this when covering the bogus Trump/Musk theory that [Social Security is paying out benefits to dead people](https://www.slowboring.com/p/trump-is-lying-about-dead-people). The actual situation is not that people fraudulently use Social Security numbers to collect benefits (you’d get caught if you tried this), it’s that they fraudulently use Social Security numbers that aren’t theirs to work and pay taxes.

The other thing is that there are a dozen ways to work under the table.

A classic one is cleaning houses or doing some type of gardening work for cash wages. A more modern example is that a lawful resident signs up to do DoorDash deliveries or drive an Uber or another gig-economy task, but then the work is actually done by someone else, and that person splits the earnings with the account holder of record.

What’s more, especially if you think about this in a small business context, the fact is that no matter how “mandatory” you make E-Verify, it’s hard to enforce this. Let’s say I’m running a restaurant in Florida. My brother-in-law says his cousin who’s here from Venezuela without papers needs a job. I hire him to wash dishes and sweep up and pay him a modest wage in cash. Who’s going to stop me? I can’t claim him as an employee on my tax documents, but then again I can probably get a great deal on his pay.

The issue with all of these things is that E-Verify or no E-Verify, we’re talking about mutually beneficial transactions between private parties. It’s challenging to enforce laws against something like that in all kinds of contexts.

Which is not to say that legality makes no difference.

There is a big difference between the actual present-day reality of buying and selling cocaine and a hypothetical world in which there is an overt, fully legal cocaine wholesaling and retailing industry.

The spread of legalized gambling has made a big difference relative to the status quo when I was younger. But even when gambling was illegal, what that meant in practice was you didn’t have television ads for sports betting or casinos opening up in every random suburb. A person who wanted to bet on football games could generally figure out a way to do that. The government, in the gambling case, I think made a prudent decision to not try all that hard to actually prevent people from gambling. The vibe was more that if you wanted to gamble, you were expected to do it in a low-key, underground way.

Drugs, of course, were a different situation.

We had a high-profile, highly kinetic “war on drugs” that featured lots of guys with guns kicking down doors and trying to find concealed drugs and otherwise making a real effort to create a situation in which nobody was buying and selling drugs.

Pretty famously, this didn’t work that well. Which isn’t to say that it had no impact at all. It’s just a reminder that making a rule that says “nobody can sell cocaine anymore” doesn’t mean that nobody sells cocaine anymore.

By the same token, a big push for an employment-side crackdown on illegal immigration would require not just E-Verify but a good amount of fairly intrusive enforcement. You’d need to do workplace raids, you’d need stiffer penalties on employers, you’d probably need to enforce the rules against household workers, you’d need to really get into the guts of how the gig-economy apps work, you’d need some stings, you’d need to cultivate snitches.

None of which is to say that it’s undoable, just that it’s a much more complicated problem than “push the E-Verify button.”

**[Max Power:](https://open.substack.com/pub/matthewyglesias/p/sunday-mailbag-thread-484?utm_campaign=comment-list-share-cta&utm_medium=web&comments=true&commentId=205066908) I thought this quote from the [NYT profile of Morris Katz](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/01/25/style/morris-katz-political-strategest-mamdani.html) was telling: “If you’ve wanted to be president since you were 5 years old, a large percentage of the electorate thinks you’re a loser. There’s a ‘no more losers’ thing. It’s harsh, but it’s true.” I read this as an argument that all you need is “no loser” candidates and you can do whatever you want on policy. Adam Jentleson pushed back against this. I agree with the Yglasian take that you do in fact need the advocate popular policies and not advocate unpopular policies to win in swing areas, but I’m curious what you think of his take that everyone who’s wanted to be president since they were 5 is somehow an unelectable loser. I feel like some of the best politicians are often people who have been extremely ambitious from a young age, e.g., Bill Clinton, or a Josh Shapiro/Marin O’Malley type. But plenty of less conventional types can also succeed -- as long as they advocate policies popular in their jurisdictions. Do you think there’s an advantage to the cool/authentic vibe, or are ambitious careerist types equally capable assuming they have good political skills?**

I think there’s a kind of bizarre dynamic in all these Katz profiles where a political consultant who lives in New York is talking to a journalist who lives in New York. And all of their friends and colleagues live in New York. Their star candidate also lives in New York and just had an impressive election win in New York. And then there are tons and tons of Democratic Party donors in New York, and the top Senate Democrat is in New York and so is the top House Democrat. There are also plenty of Democratic Party professionals who like New York so much that they live there rather than in Washington, D.C.

And this sheer mass of New Yorkers causes them to fall into a kind of N.Y.C.-centric black hole where they aren’t really acknowledging that even though New York is a very large city, it is also *one of the least-representative places in the entire United States of America*.

This just deeply distorts everything they think about and say.

Every professional Democratic Party operative I know — no matter how moderate — who lives in New York City thinks Zohran Mamdani is undeniably cool. But lots of people I know who do not live in New York City think that it is obviously insane to be standing around in New York City complaining that one of the major policy problems there is that taxes are too low. Because taxes in New York City are in fact really high! Among non-New Yorkers, this is kind of an infamous thing. Most people in most places don’t know (or care) what a “halal cart” is and also don’t want to ride the bus.

Josh Shapiro, meanwhile, is very popular in a critical swing state. Andy Beshear is very popular in Kentucky. Josh Stein is very popular in North Carolina.

It’s true that none of these guys are as cool as Mamdani. But that’s because, literally, a bunch of influential media people in New York just *decide* what is cool. What they like about Mamdani is that he appeals to people like them. Which is fine, everyone likes politicians who appeal to them personally.

But you get New York media cool kids deciding that “me and my friends like this guy” is an objective quality that he has and they are simply observing. It’s not, though. Most Americans would rather live in the suburbs of Charlotte than in Brooklyn. And if people with that set of tastes got to decide what is and isn’t cool, they would probably decide that Josh Stein is very cool.

I obviously didn’t attend any other undergraduate institutions so I can’t really say what the education would have been like elsewhere.

I personally feel like I learned a lot in my philosophy classes and that it was a well-run department that does a good job with education. It’s also my observation that the overall setup of Harvard College is certainly such that if you want to, you can earn a degree in four years and not work very hard or learn very much. If it were up to me, I wouldn’t run the institution that way.

That said, I kind of feel like the core value proposition that Harvard puts forward is that if you go there, you’ll meet a bunch of people who will go on to become important.

And, indeed, I met Ruben Gallego in college and former Wisconsin Democratic Party Chair Ben Wikler, and also former C.F.P.B. head Rohit Chopra and *also* former F.T.C. Commissioner Alvaro Bedoya. So as a basic networking exercise, it completely delivers. Your mileage may vary on the merits of that, but it seems to me that it mostly [does what it says on the tin](https://www.slowboring.com/p/making-policy-for-a-low-trust-world).
