The amount of money you’re able to earn at age 40 is in part a function of the on-the-job learning you’ve done earlier in your career. But the opportunities you got to do that learning earlier in life are in part a function of underlying competency, which continues to be relevant to your age-40 earnings.

These factors are particularly hard to untangle because, even though there are definitely hierarchies in the American education system, they tend to be a bit vague and informal — there’s no official rank-ordering of colleges or other schools.

The way the Air Force slots enlisted airmen into various career tracks, based in part on test scores, is an interesting exception to that vagueness and informality.

What makes the slotting particularly interesting is that it’s not entirely based on test scores, because the Air Force doesn’t exist to make sure everyone is treated fairly — it exists to perform a specific mission. And in order to perform that mission, it needs a specific quantity of people in specific jobs. If there’s an unusually large number of vacancies in a specific occupation at the time you happen to sign up, that increases your odds of being assigned to it, even if in a different year your score might have been just a bit too high or just a bit too low for that assignment. The average quality of the new enlistees also varies somewhat from year to year. So a score that might have been above average in 2019 when the national labor market was strong might have been below average in the weak labor market of 2010.

This allowed Julie Berry Cullen, Gordon B. Dahl, and Richard De Thorpe to conduct an [interesting study](https://econweb.ucsd.edu/~jbcullen/research/w34215.pdf) where they look at what happens to people who get assigned to jobs for which they have unusually high or unusually low test scores.

[What they find](https://www.nber.org/digest/202511/job-mismatch-and-early-career-outcomes?page=1&perPage=50) is that overqualified applicants are unusually likely to quit (and underqualified applicants unusually *unlikely* to quit), but they’re also more likely to be promoted (again the reverse is true for the underqualified).

Beyond attrition, the people who are overqualified for their jobs display a range of problems. They manifest more behavioral issues, they get worse performance evaluations, and they do worse on general knowledge tests about the military. They seem, in other words, to be kind of pissed off and demotivated by being slotted into a career track that is beneath them.

But, they still do better on job-specific assessments and are more likely to be promoted.

I’ve found that this is an unpopular take because it’s snobby and elitist, but there’s a lot of evidence that intelligence is an asset in a wide range of scenarios, and that’s what you’re seeing here. The Air Force only has so many smart recruits to go around so they need to prioritize. But when they end up with a surplus of smart recruits, those recruits are better than average at their lower-status jobs.

The thing about this paper that is of broader interest to me, though, is that these different occupations have very different profiles in terms of earnings potential when recruits transfer to civilian life.

The researchers estimate that every 10-percentage-point increase in ability surplus leads to a 10 percent *decline* in predicted civilian earnings, while a 10-percentage-point ability deficit leads to a 13 percent increase in predicted civilian earnings. The long-term impacts may be smaller because differences in underlying ability typically reassert themselves. But they are still real.

The years of on-the-job training in specific technical fields don’t just vanish, you’re sort of screwed by overmatching — which the authors think helps explain why overmatchers don’t put in much effort.

Everything about this is perfectly reasonable from the standpoint of the Air Force trying to manage its personnel needs. But it illustrates an underrated point: Even totally reasonable meritocratic systems can be unfair in important ways.

#### Life isn’t fair

This is all based on the Armed Forces Qualifying Test, which by all accounts is a pretty well-designed and reliable test.

Still, the nature of tests is that a one-point difference in scores doesn’t represent a large gap in ability. A person could easily go up or down by a very small amount based on a bad night’s sleep or a poorly timed illness or some other happenstance of life. This is why I think it should be broadly reassuring that [regression discontinuity studies of exam high schools in New York and Boston](https://www.nber.org/papers/w17264) show that it doesn’t actually make a huge difference whether you get in or not. These schools are “good” overwhelmingly because of selection effects; the treatment isn’t literally zero but it’s quite small. But some of this is that the high school curriculum is just not that esoteric or specialized no matter where you go.

In contrast, you probably can’t pick up [aircraft-hydraulic maintenance skills](https://www.airforce.com/careers/maintenance-and-repair/aircraft-hydraulic-systems) with a little effort in your spare time. I don’t know exactly how lucrative these skills are, but I’d imagine working on aircraft hydraulics in the military is a big leg up in getting aircraft hydraulic work in the private sector. Airplanes are expensive, and I would like my hydraulic maintenance to be done by someone with a demonstrated track record of not fucking it up.

The fact that whether you get training in a good specialty field is a function not only of tiny, somewhat arbitrary variance in test scores, but also of factors that are entirely outside your control is a bitter pill.

It’s not as if life is completely out of your hands. If you manage to stick with it and not quit, you will in fact be promoted above your less competent Air Force colleagues. But you’re still stuck on the bad track.

As a person who likes things to be logical, I sometimes wish we could avoid these hard test thresholds altogether and do probabilistic admissions. In other words, while some scores would give you a 100 percent chance of getting the job (or the slot in school or whatever) a slightly lower score would give you a slightly lower chance, but not a zero percent chance.

This would, I think, more accurately reflect what the tests are really telling us. But someone who missed out on a good Air Force job assignment despite having a better score than some people who did get the gig would then have his own legitimate complaints. With high schools we can at least try to tamp down the scarcity mindset, have better overall educational opportunities, and focus on the non-positional aspects of learning. But even though the military performs an important job training function, it’s not a job training program. They are trying to fight and win wars. They can’t just maximize everyone’s job opportunities — they need to assign people to do the things that need to be done. And this does generate some arbitrary and unfair outcomes.

#### The ineluctable power of luck

One sort of perverse thing that I see all the time on the internet is people wanting to insist that rich, successful businessmen whose political views and/or business practices they dislike are all also morons. I’m sure there is at least one person out there who has made a ton of money managing a successful business but is also a moron. But with any realistic assessment, this is pretty unlikely. I think Elon Musk is a bad and aggravating person, but you’re not achieving on his level without also being very smart and hard-working.

Conversely, though, a lot of rich guys are too impressed with their own intelligence and hard work, and the intelligence and hard work of the other rich guys they know, and end up not thinking very critically about the role of happenstance in life.

I think about my son, who is 10 and on a swim team. He’s not the single best 10-year-old swimmer in the D.C. area, but he is *much* better at swimming than the average 10-year-old. He’s put a lot of hard work into that, and he also happens to have a body that is well-shaped for swimming. But it’s also the case that when he was really little and his parents just wanted him to get some swim lessons so he’d know how to swim (lessons it was not difficult for his family to afford), the most convenient place to do that happened to be our local Y.M.C.A. And he just happened to get assigned to an instructor there who was a wonderful teacher, and also quite tough and ambitious, and someone he happened to really like and whose opinion mattered to him. It never would’ve occurred to me or my wife to have him do competitive swim, but the teacher felt that he had potential and set him on his current trajectory.

By the same token, I try to be humble about the happenstance elements of my career.

Nick Confessore and Richard Just were kind and helpful to a college student they only knew from a handful of blog posts. More broadly, though, I got into the blog game at exactly the right time. A few years earlier and my ability to write really fast would not have been particularly useful. A few years later and it became much harder to break into “blogging” when the opportunities were more for specialists. I work hard and I’m genuinely good at what I do — I write quickly about a wide range of topics and I remember a whole lot of factual information — but there’s a lot of happenstance that went into that [very particular set of skills](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jZOywn1qArI) being remunerative.

Like a lot of successful people, I made a smart bet on myself at a particular point in time. Circa 2004 or 2005, there was still a strong sentiment that the web was junior league, that a young writer ought to hone his skills through long-form print features. I had the wisdom to see that this was not the future and that I should focus on where I already had an edge. But there’s a lot of survivorship bias to this kind of thing — the world is full of talented, hard-working people who made bold bets that didn’t pay off.

It’s fun to think you’re just more tough-minded than all the risk-averse herd-followers out there, but you were also *luckier* than the people whose bets didn’t pay off.

#### A better world for everyone

Notably, these aren’t really examples of *bad* unfairness.

It’s not the kind of unfairness of Jim Crow or a hereditary aristocracy or people getting away with cheating. The issue is that, because of path dependencies, small quirks of fate can have large impacts on outcomes. The Air Force is trying to meet their needs for personnel. But those needs vary over time, as does the human capital available to them. They have what seems like a pretty good system. But the system, as it operates, can generate long-term outcomes for people that are unrelated to their talents or efforts.

There’s an extensive literature on the long-term consequences of graduating school at a time when the labor market happens to be weak (see [Lisa Kahn’s paper](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0927537109001018) or [Hannes Schwandt and Till von Wachter’s paper](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/701046)), and they turn out to be pretty bad.

A study looking at [detailed Norwegian data](https://www.iza.org/publications/dp/6820/good-skills-in-bad-times-cyclical-skill-mismatch-and-the-long-term-effects-of-graduating-in-a-recession) links the recession theme to what we see in the Air Force data: Your earnings trajectory is a function of your underlying ability but also of your specific occupational training. If you graduate into a strong labor market, employers are willing to “reach down” into the ability stack to find people to do valuable work. If you graduate into a weak labor market, the odds are decent that you’ll end up under-matching relative to your ability. You’ll have chances to catch up over time, but the lack of early career experience in a lucrative field tends to add up.

You can also think of this in terms of technological change.

Over the years, we make progress and that’s good on average. But specific technological disruptions create losers as well as winners. And in particular, the scale of the wins and losses can be quite sensitive to where you happen to be in life when the change hits. I came along at just the right time to ride the wave of the text-based internet. Other people have been hurt by technological change that makes skills they’ve honed over the course of a career obsolete. If that happens to you when you’re 63, it’s no big deal — you just coast for a couple of years and retire. If it happens when you’re 25, that’s annoying, but you’re young enough to adapt. But if it happens when you’re 50, you’ve built up an entire career’s worth of job-specific skills that are being devalued at a time when you’re still well short of retirement.

This kind of thing is why, despite what I think is a healthy level of respect for markets and capitalism, I don’t fundamentally want a pure market distribution of income. Basically functional, well-designed systems can be very capricious under the hood. We need to have progressive taxation, good public services, and a strong safety net to sustain a win-win society. And everyone ought to understand that, even though hard work and talent and intelligence are important, we’re also all kind of bouncing through life with a certain degree of randomness.
