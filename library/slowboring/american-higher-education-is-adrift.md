Last week, The Atlantic published an article about the growing share of students at elite college campuses who [receive some form of disability accommodation](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/2026/01/elite-university-student-accommodation/684946/).

A lot of these college campuses are quite old. For example, at Harvard, a student in a wheelchair or with some mobility impairment would find it challenging to complete a 100-percent-standard course load with zero special accommodation because of the built environment. My sophomore year, I was randomly assigned to live on the fourth floor of a dorm with no elevator, which was mildly annoying to me and my roommates but would’ve been totally unworkable for some of our fellow students.

The reason schools should make accommodations for these students is that being able to walk up four flights of stairs has nothing to do with the mission of the university. It’s just happenstance that the campus is full of old buildings. Some of the old buildings have been retrofitted to be accessible, which is good. And to the extent that retrofits are impossible (as is the case with the Kirkland House annex where I lived), provision is made so that the school doesn’t assign someone to live there who wouldn’t be able to do so.

These sorts of disability accommodations can have nontrivial financial costs, but Harvard is a wealthy university in a wealthy country, and the costs are a price worth paying to live in a decent and inclusive society.[1](#footnote-1)

But this is not the kind of accommodation The Atlantic is talking about.

There is no proliferation of students in wheelchairs. The issue is the proliferation of students making disability claims largely based on mental health, largely in order to get extra time on tests. And taking tests is very much part of the core mission of undergraduate education.

Time pressure is either *not* an important part of the assessment, in which case nobody should be subjected to it, or else it *is* an important part of the assessment, in which case everybody should be subjected to it.

Maya Sen from the Kennedy School, who I generally think has good takes, reacted to this story by saying that abuse of accommodations is “[far from a pressing national policy problem](https://x.com/maya_sen/status/1996216758101831746).” And I can see where she’s coming from there. But I think it’s just one manifestation of something that really is a pressing national policy problem, which is that the stakeholders in the American higher-education system can’t really articulate what it is they’re trying to do. So as various controversies pop up — about disability accommodations or viewpoint discrimination or admissions — there’s not a coherent response because there are no guiding principles to refer back to.

And that’s pretty important. These institutions play major roles in American society. They are nodes in our entire scientific-research enterprise and a major export industry. Employers rely on them to perform both screening and social capital functions. They are informal-but-significant authorities and sources of expertise and producers of knowledge.

They’re also adrift and unmoored to the point where something as banal as my observation that climbing stairs is not an important part of the educational mission but taking tests is seems alien to the whole ethos.

#### The disaggregated university

One issue here is that, while American society as a whole is pretty interested in the undergraduate-education function of universities, it seems to me that professors mostly aren’t.

They’re not hired to be teachers, and they’re not really evaluated on their teaching ability in a rigorous way. In K-12 education, we have fitfully engaged with the idea of trying to measure student growth rates (rather than just absolute levels of knowledge) and doing demographic adjustments and assessing teacher effectiveness with complicated value-added models.

All of this is controversial, but it’s also an accepted part of the education landscape. There is pretty broad consensus that, at least in principle, a school is supposed to help students learn things over time, and that if they are learning at a rate that is faster than you’d expect based on national averages or demographic characteristics, that’s good, and if they’re learning at a rate that is slower than expected, that’s bad.

None of the professors who I’m friends with hate teaching or look down on it or secretly despise their students. But the question of what does a great undergraduate education look like just doesn’t loom that large for them. It’s a job that they do, and they are happy to do it. But what they’re interested in, intellectually, is their own research.

Meanwhile, off over in one corner is an admissions office, which is making decisions about who comes to the school. And in another, there’s a disability office making calls about accommodations. Maybe someone else is making D.E.I. policies (or hastily renaming them under conservative pressure), and someone else is running extracurriculars. Sports are a huge deal at a lot of schools, but obviously the athletic director is not answering to the faculty.

Ideally, the sports program makes money so everyone’s happy, but even so it’s a small minority of sports that are driving revenue. I’m sure there’s a reason the University of Maryland has a [golf team](https://umterps.com/sports/mens-golf), but it’s not entirely obvious to me what that reason is or who exactly would be in charge of deciding whether it’s a good one or how it aligns with the university’s overall mission.

#### Mission helps in a crisis

Twenty years ago, I’d have said this sort of aimlessness and mission creep wasn’t actually a big problem. It was true even then that a lot about elite American higher education didn’t make a ton of sense, but the system seemed to work well enough. Enrollment was on the rise, America dominated the global rankings of research universities, and the public’s concerns with higher education were overwhelmingly about cost and student loan debt.

And those were very real problems, but they were primarily problems with the *lower* end of the higher-education spectrum, where too many students were enrolling without graduating or entering degree programs that had very little payoff.

In that discourse climate, I felt annoyed that conversations about “college” were constantly dragged back to events on selective college campuses that enroll a minority of American students and weren’t actually the epicenter of the most pressing issues.

Today, though, I think we’re dealing with a crisis of confidence and legitimacy in the higher end parts of the system. On the one hand, the Trump administration is waging a [misguided](https://www.slowboring.com/p/trumps-war-on-harvard) war on [academic freedom and basic research](https://www.slowboring.com/p/trumps-targets-need-our-help) at top schools, creating an enormous problem for the long-term future of the country. On the other hand, we have situations like the one at the University of California San Diego, where [a large number of the students admitted can’t do](https://www.theargumentmag.com/p/when-grades-stop-meaning-anything) math.

UC San Diego is not the tippy top of the higher-education pyramid in the United States, but California runs [three systems of schools](https://www.ppic.org/publication/californias-higher-education-system/). There’s California Community Colleges at the bottom, the California State University system in the middle, and the University of California system at the top.

The San Diego campus is supposed to be part of that top-ranked system, and that’s supposed to mean something. So I was quite surprised to see [some academics say they don’t see a problem here](https://x.com/joftius/status/1991590671279616127), except maybe that [UC San Diego deserves more money](https://x.com/joftius/status/1991979573547073947) and we should [ignore all complaints](https://x.com/joftius/status/1992182055682142419?s=20) about universities being too left-wing.

I’m not going to say that all or even most professors agree with Loftus’s take on this. But I do think this series of statements is reflective of a nontrivial school of thought in academia, one that is both eager to secure more public money for institutions that are already heavily subsidized while also being totally dismissive of public concerns about political skew and ideological bias.

Of course, political polarization across occupational categories strikes in other domains as well. Police departments are overwhelmingly conservative in ways that made their reactions to the policing controversies of the late 2010s tone-deaf and counterproductive, especially in more liberal cities. But cops were able to fall back on a pretty clear consensus that the *purpose* of a police department is to generate a safe and orderly city, which then let them win a bunch of specific fights over the past five years about how to do that.

Academia, by contrast, is all over the map.

I think that what we should be trying to do at the top schools is select a group of strong students and then subject them to a rigorous course of study. That would call for a pretty [heavy reliance on standardized testing in admissions](https://www.slowboring.com/p/sat-optional-affirmative-action), for not generally accommodating things like A.D.H.D. and anxiety, and for [making students do more and harder homework](https://www.slowboring.com/p/college-students-should-study-more) and giving them tougher grades. People would, of course, disagree at the margins about the specifics. But the main reason higher ed isn’t currently run that way is not because of small disagreements around the margins — it’s big picture disagreement about goals and institutional mission. And it only becomes more important to resolve this disagreement as the whole system comes under increasing political pressure.

#### The consumer university

One issue highlighted by the fact that the Trump administration is holding research funding hostage to unrelated political agendas, of course, is the overall relationship between that research and the rest of what universities do.

A traditional view suggests that one useful aspect of undergraduate education is to directly expose bright teenagers to top researchers who do cutting-edge academic research. It’s not entirely clear to me that this is true, but it’s a plausible hypothesis and one that we’ve run with for several centuries now.

There’s also a newer idea that the government should be a major source of funding for scientific research and, in part due to mid-20th-century happenstance, most of that research funding is funneled through existing universities rather than autonomous research institutes. As research funding has come to be a tool for coercing private universities into following a political agenda, it’s probably worth asking some questions about the overall logic of that setup.

It’s also worth asking why different higher-education institutions aren’t more different from one another. There are a couple of schools — [St. Johns](https://www.sjc.edu) and [Deep Springs](https://www.deepsprings.edu) come to mind — that pursue clearly non-standard approaches to undergraduate education, but they’re quite small in number.

The decentralized nature of the American higher-education system hasn’t led to actual diversity of approaches — it just has everyone competing to converge on the same set of prestige markers.

For example, lots of schools used to have “teachers college” in the name. Looking at Stephen F. Austin State Teachers College or Framingham State Teachers College or Indiana State Teachers College or Maryland State Teachers College at Towson, it’s pretty clear that their institutional mission was training people to be teachers.

That seems like a valid and important institutional role. America has a lot of teachers, and there’s a clear public interest in training them well. But this was a low-prestige function that institutions wanted to shrug off. So now, instead, we have Stephen F. Austin State University, Framingham State University, Indiana University of Pennsylvania, and Towson University. But public school teachers normally get paid extra if they have a master’s degree, so universities are happy to offer master’s degrees in education even though [these programs on average deliver no value in terms of teacher effectiveness](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/who-profits-from-the-masters-degree-pay-bump-for-teachers/).

As another example, I’ve seen a lot of conservatives crowing about the success of [Southern universities in attracting more students away from the Northeast](https://www.foxnews.com/us/southern-universities-reportedly-see-massive-influx-northeast-students-seeking-sunshine-greek-life) because the “frats and football” package has obviously more right-wing vibes than the Ivy League.

That’s fine as far as it goes. But the underlying dynamic here of schools competing for students on the basis of catering to teenagers’ whims actually just replicates a lot of stuff that conservatives claim not to like about higher education. Are these schools going to crack down on grade inflation? Make kids study worthwhile stuff instead of puppetry? [The University of Alabama lazy river and water slide](https://urec.sl.ua.edu/facilities/pools/) seem fun, but are they a good use of our student loan dollars?

I’m sorry to end this on a cranky-old-man note, but I do think it’s an important point. It happens to be the case that in the recent past there was a nexus between left-wing politics and making undergraduate education a drag, largely because of Covid protocols and a particular kind of struggle session dynamic around privilege. At the end of the day, though, having pure student choice as the only structuring principle of undergraduate education is not going to address any of the problems in that sector, most of which are downstream of a lack of clear mission that could inform individual decisions.
