The first time I ever heard a land acknowledgment, I was on a panel at a nonprofit conference in Colorado. A Native woman stood up in the audience and started shouting and demanding the floor. Most people were confused, but a few were cheering her on. When the moderator let her speak, she asked to do a “land acknowledgment.” I didn’t know what that was, but she was granted permission and said something about the land to scattered applause before we moved on.

As far as activist tactics go, it was pretty good.

It was much easier for a progressive moderator at a conference with mostly progressive attendees to just say yes than to try to have the woman forcibly removed. It’s a small-bore example of the strategic logic of nonviolent protest, and it succeeded in getting large swathes of progressive America to preemptively start doing land acknowledgments.

The 2024 Democratic platform, for example, commences with a fairly innocuous land acknowledgment, stating that they were gathering on “lands that have been stewarded through many centuries by the ancestors and descendants of Tribal Nations who have been here since time immemorial.” But the Native Governance Center’s guide to Indigenous land acknowledgments tells us, “Don’t sugarcoat the past. Use terms like genocide, ethnic cleansing, stolen land, and forced removal to reflect actions taken by colonizers.”

I think it’s worthwhile to consider the distinction here.

The Democratic platform land acknowledgment is a symptom of a party that is more focused on internal coalition management than on winning elections. What it says in your platform almost certainly doesn’t matter, but a disciplined political party focused on winning would not be making this sort of concession to activist demands. That being said, if hailing the stewardship of those who came before us became a widely adopted ritual in American life, that seems unobjectionable on the merits to me. It’s a bit cringe, but so is singing the national anthem at the start of a youth sports event. There’s never going to be a cringeless set of national rituals, and rituals are important.

But the stolen land claim is an ideological provocation that I think needs to be rejected. National Students for Justice in Palestine describes its mission as “supporting over 400 Palestine solidarity organizations across occupied Turtle Island (so-called North America).” I think both friends and foes of the anti-Zionist movement understand that they are sincere in their desire to delegitimize the Israeli state, and it’s worth taking seriously the fact that there is a parallel (albeit more far-fetched) movement to delegitimize the United States of America and that this needs to be contested by American liberals in a real way rather than appeased. Not least because it’s hard to explain what’s wrong with the increasingly deranged tenor of the Trump administration’s anti-immigration rhetoric unless you’re able to articulate the values of traditional American civic nationalism.

Abraham Lincoln, of course, understood Americanism far better than whatever groyper creeps are running the Labor Department. He wrote to Joshua Speed about the anti-immigration agitators of his time:

As a nation, we began by declaring that “all men are created equal.” We now practically read it “all men are created equal, except negroes.” When the Know-Nothings get control, it will read “all men are created equal, except negroes, and foreigners, and catholics.” When it comes to this I should prefer emigrating to some country where they make no pretence of loving liberty — to Russia, for instance, where despotism can be taken pure, and without the base alloy of hypocrisy.

Lincoln isn’t the last word on the details of immigration policy, which is complicated and admits of a lot of nuance. But he should be the first word. America is a country whose institutions are committed to the noble principles of human freedom and equality, and we need to be able to say in both directions that those institutions are legitimate.

The problem with indigeneity

I think it’s a good idea to remind people from time to time that no piece of existing property on the earth represents some kind of unbroken morally legitimate chain of title going back to original appropriation out of a state of nature. There are interesting thought-experiments about this in Locke and in Nozick, but that’s not what the actual history of this country is like.

It’s not what the actual history of any country is like.

Notably, it’s not what the history of the pre-European peoples of North America is like. When Europeans first explored the Potomac River Basin, it was inhabited by the Piscataway people. Those people appear to have arrived in the 14th century, possibly from further inland. We don’t know that much about the culture or language of the people who were there before the Piscataway, but there have been people here for thousands and thousands of years. It also appears that the migration of the ancestors of present-day Native Americans into the western hemisphere was preceded by a different group whose genetic legacy persists today in an unexpected relatedness between some Amazonian peoples and the residents of the Andaman Islands off the coast of India.

It’s natural that people living under European colonial rule wanted to organize politically to achieve self-government. “This is our land, you stole it” is a natural argument for a nationalist movement to make. But people say all kinds of things. Vietnamese legend describes the Vietnamese people as coming into existence where they live today. But really, most people who live in Vietnam today are descendants of people who seem to have gone south from somewhere in China, established a polity in what is today the northern part of the country, and then largely (though not entirely) displaced the Chams, who previously ruled the central part of contemporary Vietnam.

I think it’s natural that the protagonists of various anti-colonial movements are taking inspiration from each other and finding common cause.

But there is no real historical or moral logic to the claim that each country is the rightful possession of whichever ethnic group happened to have dominated it right before it was dominated by Europeans.

That’s a rhetorical conceit that did a certain kind of work in the third quarter of the 20th century, not a real historical analysis. In the countries that were decolonized, they now have their own issues of democratic governance and ethnic minority populations that are handled in more or less satisfactory ways, the same as anyplace else. Except we have this ideological hangover suggesting that the American polity is uniquely or distinctively illegitimate.

American society is not an ethnic project

Meanwhile, on the other end of the horseshoe, you have Stephen Miller arguing that we should apply a collective guilt logic to Afghan refugees for the crime of one man.

This is a style of argument that got Miller and my shared Ashkenazi ancestors expelled from various countries at various times, but has mostly not been applied to us in American history. I think we should be able, as adults, to agree that while waves of immigration obviously do change the United States, it’s simply not the case that we are copying and pasting foreign societies onto America.

Part of the perversity of this whole thing is that American restrictionists are importing un-American conceptions of nationalism.

Along with that, they have imported a whole European discourse about “mass migration,” which supposedly differentiates the current situation from the past in a way that makes no sense in the context of American history, where large-scale immigration has been the norm. They also have this intense focus on small numbers of Muslim refugees, when obviously the actual “mass” of recent migration to the United States has been from Latin America (the small number of Middle Eastern immigrants to the United States are a highly selected group) and, to a lesser extent, from Asia.

But if you plop somebody down in majority Hispanic San Antonio, they’re not going to be confused about whether they’re in Mexico. It’s an American city! The median household income of American Hispanics is much higher than the median household income in any Latin American country. Latin families assimilate to speaking English over two to three generations, just like those who arrived in all the earlier waves of immigration to the United States. When we’re in San Antonio, my wife makes us stop at Taco Cabana, which is clearly an establishment that reflects the influence of Mexican immigration in the sense that they sell tacos but is also extremely American (the founder was a German-ancestry guy from Fredericksburg).

One reason societies don’t just replicate on new soil is that the circumstances are totally different.

People with limited skills and professional qualifications who move to the United States from poor countries are able to dramatically increase their earnings while washing dishes, repairing roofs, delivering DoorDash, driving Ubers, mowing lawns, or taking care of little kids. That’s why people come here, and the affluence of America is itself a big jolt to culture and society.

We also have different institutions in the United States. El Paso is not run by drug cartels in the way that Juárez is. And then the diversity of American society is itself a major causal influence. When I first lived here, my neighborhood in Washington, D.C., was an Ethiopian ethnic enclave. But living here wasn’t like living in Ethiopia — it’s politically, socially, and economically integrated with the surrounding area. A lot of those families have moved to the suburbs as prices have risen, but we still have lots of kids from families that speak Ethiopian languages at home at my son’s school. And those kids are growing up in the United States of America and socializing with the kids of white gentrifiers and immigrants from Central America and D.C.’s historic African-American population. It’s a uniquely American situation, because it plays out in the United States of America.

By the same token, we have several Native Americans serving in Congress and I am fondly hoping that Mary Peltola will be back among them soon. Neither the process of assimilation and integration nor the ideals of equality across ethnic divides are perfectly smooth or flawless. But America does pretty well on both of these scores. The United States is not a repressive white ethnostate (as the left would have it) or a fragile white ethnostate (as the right would have it). It’s a constitutional democracy composed of individual human beings and governed by liberal ideals.

Individuals matter most

Probably the most striking violation of Miller’s claim that immigrants simply “recreate the conditions, and terrors, of their broken homelands” is the incredible success of Indian-Americans.

India is a very poor country with a lower G.D.P. per capita (in purchasing power parity terms) than Jamaica, Guatemala, or South Africa. But Indian-Americans are a super-elite group, with a median household income way above the American average, and are quite well-represented among C.E.O.s and at elite colleges.

The secret here is that there are a ton of people in India, and those who’ve immigrated to the United States are a non-random subset of that vast population. The people who’ve come here are above average in drive, conscientiousness, and ambition, and in the United States, they have a level of opportunity that far exceeds what’s possible in India. The presence of new people does shift American culture and society. But the new people are, in fact, individual human beings, not representatives of statistical averages.

If you want to argue that our visa rules should be altered to emphasize selectivity more, that’s a reasonable conversation. But it’s not a conversation you can reasonably have if you’re starting from the Millerite ethnocentric premise.

People don’t normally want to uproot themselves from their home countries for no reason, so migration outflows are likely to come from places that are having problems. We also know, though, that dysfunctional social institutions are contingent. North Korea and South Korea have wildly different outcomes. China in 2025 and China in 1975 have very different outcomes. The Cuban economy is a mess because of communism, not because it’s populated by Cubans — look at Miami. And it’s perverse for conservatives, of all people, to have adopted a totally paranoid interpretation of people’s desire to leave Venezuela.

If you want to be selective, be selective. But it seems to me that a lot of conservatives have adopted a weirdly negative, anti-market view of this.

Obviously if you’re only allowed to have five immigrants, then you’d really like them to be five super-geniuses with incredible technical skills. The whole economy, though, is built around normal non-genius people doing things for each other in exchange for money.

If you believe in markets at all, then the people bagging groceries and mopping floors and picking vegetables and driving forklifts and cutting hair are all contributing to society. If you’re here working for a living and not committing crimes, that’s good! I think we should probably do a better job of structuring our immigration policies around temporary visas for less skilled workers and easier access to citizenship for the most talented people. But the premise of any kind of reorganization should be that immigration is a positive opportunity for the country to capture, not a dire threat to the nation.

Political scientist Alexander Kustov published a very good book earlier this year titled “In Our Interest: How Democracies Can Make Immigration Popular” about the need to reframe both the immigration debate and actual immigration policies around the pursuit of national interest.

This should be doable, especially in the United States of America, which has such a strong narrative about itself as “a nation of immigrants” and where major leaders from Washington to Lincoln and beyond deliberately fostered immigration as a strategy for national strength.

But to return to the land acknowledgments, we can only foster a politics of national interest if we believe in promoting the interests of the nation! I sometimes see takes that more or less amount to saying Democrats should get more into patriotism because that will help them win elections. So I want to be clear — that’s not really what I’m saying here. There is an actual divergence of views that left-of-center people need to think about. Is immigration good because immigration is good for the United States of America, or is immigration good because the United States is a fundamentally illegitimate enterprise that deserves to be destroyed by an influx of foreigners?

The latter is clearly what Stephen Miller thinks most Democrats believe, but I don’t believe for a minute that he’s right.

I think there is a tendency to go along with demands for land acknowledgments because progressive leaders are trying to do coalition management and it seems so low stakes. I’ll admit to being biased here as a generalist writer, but I do think the ideological superstructure of what you’re saying matters, even when the short-term practicalities are low. If we want to teach people about the cruelty and barbarism of human history, that’s great.

But if we want to defend the ideas of liberalism, pluralism, democracy, and all the rest we can’t accept the premise that there’s something uniquely criminal about the origins of the American state.
