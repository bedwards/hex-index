In yesterday’s post, “[American higher education is adrift](https://www.slowboring.com/p/american-higher-education-is-adrift),” Matt proposed a reconsideration of the mission and goals of higher ed in this country, alongside a recalibration of the sector’s spending. He put it this way: “The University of Alabama lazy river and water slide seem fun, but are they a good use of our student loan dollars?”

To answer that question, it helps to understand how large public research universities are actually funded. These institutions operate on complicated funding models where multiple revenue streams support multiple missions, such as teaching and research but also self-funding auxiliary units like athletics, housing, and dining. These divisions exist not because of internal preferences but because of legal, regulatory, and political requirements. Federal research agencies forbid the use of grant dollars for unrelated purposes; state legislatures demand transparency about how taxpayer appropriations are spent; athletic departments negotiate their own media contracts; and bond rating agencies evaluate the financial strength of each component separately. The result is that, from an accounting perspective, universities look less like singular organizations and more like federated structures with legally distinct revenue streams that cannot be casually intermingled.

#### **The academic and research core**

At the center of the university are its instructional and research functions: faculty, libraries, academic advising, research infrastructure, and student support. This core mission is typically funded primarily through four buckets.

-   **Tuition and student fees:** These are often the single largest source of operating funds for academic units. Tuition typically comprises a [majority of revenue](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/explainer/state-funding-and-college-costs-reviewing-the-evidence/), often exceeding state contributions.
    
-   **State appropriations:** Public universities still rely on state funding, although its relative share has [generally declined](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0272775725001281) over recent decades.
    
-   **Sponsored research:** Federal and private research grants [boost universities’ research missions](https://www.ucdavis.edu/blog/direct-vs-indirect-costs-federal-research-funding-explained). While grants don’t cover all institutional research costs, they support faculty salaries, labs, facilities, and overhead recovery.
    
-   **Philanthropy and endowment distributions:** Private support fuels scholarships, endowed chairs, research initiatives, and capital projects. Many universities now [emphasize diversified fundraising](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0738059323000652) beyond tuition and state funds to stabilize support.
    

These funds come with constraints. Tuition and state dollars flow into a general fund that supports teaching and academic administration. Federal grants are restricted and cannot be repurposed. Donor gifts usually carry explicit or implicit expectations. Together these form the stable backbone of academic operations.

Federal assistance also plays a foundational role in sustaining the academic core. For most large public universities, federal support goes toward student aid, such as Pell Grants and federal loans, enabling students to enroll without universities bearing the full cost of need-based support. It also includes research funding through grants that underwrite scientific discovery as well as indirect cost recovery that helps maintain laboratories and compliance infrastructure. These federal research dollars make it possible to recruit top faculty and operate world-class labs. In many cases, total federal flows exceed state appropriations.

#### **Auxiliary units**

Outside the academic core are auxiliary units, which function like [internal business lines](https://exploelevate.org/blog/the-business-of-auxiliary-services#:~:text=Revenue:%20All%20income%20generated%20by,portion%20of%20school%2Dwide%20expenses.). They include athletics, housing, dining, student health services, parking, bookstores, and — in some cases — health care facilities like hospitals and clinics. They raise their own revenue, pay their own expenses, and often borrow for their own capital projects.

They are kept separate for several reasons:

-   States prohibit using tuition or appropriations to subsidize discretionary auxiliaries like athletics.
    
-   Federal grant rules prevent research funds from being diverted to unrelated activities.
    
-   Auxiliary units often carry different risk and debt profiles.
    
-   Transparency helps avoid cross-subsidization that could draw public criticism.
    

#### **How this looks in practice**

Let’s take the University of Michigan as an example. It sits in U.S. News & World Report’s list of [top 20 national universities](https://www.usnews.com/best-colleges/university-of-michigan-ann-arbor-9092) and attracts high-performing applicants from well beyond the state. As an “[R1](https://www.aeaweb.org/resources/institution_classifications/r1)” research institution, it is among the country’s top universities in annual research expenditures, totaling [several billion dollars](https://research.umich.edu/news-and-issues/research-annual-reports/fy25-research-annual-report/). In addition, the university has one of the largest athletic programs in the country; its football stadium, “[the Big House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michigan_Stadium),” is the largest athletic stadium in the U.S. and the third largest in the world. In 2024, the school signed Bryce Underwood, a then 17-year-old quarterback, to a [deal worth over $10 million](https://www.sportingnews.com/us/ncaa-football/news/bryce-underwood-nil-money-explained-michigan-champions-circle/243a41ba76189ecd313ad693).

The University of Michigan’s operating budget for fiscal year 2026 is [reportedly](https://regents.umich.edu/files/meetings/10-25/2025-10-IX-1.pdf#:~:text=The%20university%20is%20well%20prepared%20for%20the,budgeted%20total%20operating%20revenue%20of%20$15.6%20billion.) $15.6 billion, but only a fraction of that total flows through the general fund that supports its academic operations. The fund supports instruction, research infrastructure, student services, and core operations. Its major revenue sources, which [totaled about $2.8 billion](https://provost.umich.edu/mythbreaking-the-budget/) in fiscal year 2024, are tuition and fees, state appropriations, and indirect cost recovery from sponsored research. State funding contributes only about 13 percent of this academic budget, with tuition making up a far larger share.

Tuition and tax dollars do not pay for the university’s athletics, according to the university’s provost office. Intercollegiate Athletics at Michigan is an [auxiliary unit that must self-sustain](https://provost.umich.edu/mythbreaking-the-budget/) through its own revenue from sources including ticket sales, media rights, and donations. Other auxiliary units include campus housing, dining, and health services. The athletic department projected a balanced budget of about [$266.3 million for fiscal year 2026](https://mgoblue.com/news/2025/6/12/general-michigan-athletics-projects-balanced-budget-for-fiscal-year-2026), including $15 million in direct support from the university. However, this funding is structured to keep the athletics unit operational and compliant with recent legal and regulatory changes, especially [recent laws allowing revenue to be shared](https://www.michigandaily.com/sports/how-new-regulation-affects-michigan-nil-in-the-revenue-sharing-era/) with athletes.

Public universities across the country operate on similar models.

The University of Maryland’s [nearly](https://finance.umd.edu/budget/facts-and-figures/operating-budget#:~:text=The%20University%20of%20Maryland's%20\(UMD\)%20current%20funds,2005%20*%20FY%202004%20*%20FY%202003) **[](https://finance.umd.edu/budget/facts-and-figures/operating-budget#:~:text=The%20University%20of%20Maryland's%20\(UMD\)%20current%20funds,2005%20*%20FY%202004%20*%20FY%202003)**[$3 billion operating budget](https://finance.umd.edu/budget/facts-and-figures/operating-budget#:~:text=The%20University%20of%20Maryland's%20\(UMD\)%20current%20funds,2005%20*%20FY%202004%20*%20FY%202003) for fiscal year 2026 reveals a similar structure. Tuition and fees, state support, restricted grants, auxiliary revenues, and health system operations remain distinct. Its auxiliary units account for a meaningful but separate part of the budget, with Maryland Athletics announcing plans to [increase fundraising](https://umterps.com/news/2025/11/3/terrapin-club-maryland-athletics-to-raise-350m-in-support-of-university-of-maryland-comprehensive-campaign.aspx) to propel the department.

On the Pacific Coast, at the [University of Washington](https://www.washington.edu/opb/uw-budget-overview/), state appropriations make up only about 5 percent of the total budget, while tuition accounts for around 8 percent. Sponsored research constitutes roughly 18 percent, and auxiliary enterprises about 12 percent. The single largest component, nearly half of the university’s overall budget, comes from its health system operations. This enormous clinical enterprise is financially and administratively distinct from the academic budget.

#### **So what about that lazy river?**

The University of Alabama’s lazy river is a part of the university’s [Student Recreation Center](https://urec.sl.ua.edu/facilities/pools/#:~:text=Swim%20laps%20in%20the%204,%2C%20water%20slide%2C%20and%20more.), which is in theory funded as an auxiliary budget. But [student fees](https://news.ua.edu/2025/10/5-things-to-know-about-urec/) are baked directly into tuition, and membership in the rec center is likely funneled into the auxiliary recreation budget, making it entirely possible that student loan dollars are, at least, in part paying for the lazy river. This is the case at Louisiana State University, where the school’s lazy river has [drawn criticism](https://www.wsj.com/articles/lsus-lazy-river-and-the-student-fee-sham-1513381917?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=AWEtsqdvXNdnYDXvn1ZJmXAOxR8Vka4GaqRxkIZ2AWW9UXnnpj_XAMoN5gJ_QtH4UnQ%3D&gaa_ts=693c5ce3&gaa_sig=vn5UHva9fh5Wh2uZkbSQGgmzW9tRdEhDuVyCtU9XVLCp2PovLCbct60WzAqh_2eHxepU-ZUd4Tl7Zk8Je-RuVg%3D%3D) due to a sharp student-fee increase to finance the project. The University of Alabama has not responded to requests for comment.

Even though these funding streams are legally and financially separate, changes to one part of the university can still have indirect effects on others. Cutting a football program would not unlock a windfall for research; instead, it would remove the athletic department’s largest revenue source and force major reductions within athletics itself. The university’s academic budget would remain more or less unchanged, though indirect effects on alumni engagement, donor patterns, or institutional visibility could emerge.
