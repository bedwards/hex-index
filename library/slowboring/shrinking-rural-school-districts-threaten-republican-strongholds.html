Education Week reports that 70 percent of districts across Wisconsin have lost students over the past decade, and the trend is similar across much of rural America. In California, some small districts that once enrolled more than 1,000 students in the early 2000s are now down to roughly 400 students in 2024. These long-term demographic shifts are tied to aging populations, declining birth rates, and limited local job growth in America’s most sparsely populated areas. And it’s becoming clear that they also threaten the institutional backbone of many Republican-leaning communities.

Faced with fewer students and less revenue, districts have begun making structural changes that would have been politically unthinkable a generation ago.

The most widely covered example is the rise of the four-day school week. In 1999, only about 100 school districts used the shortened schedule. Today more than 2,100 schools in 26 states have adopted it. District leaders, like those in rural Texas, commonly point to teacher shortages and declining enrollment as the main reasons for adopting the structure.

Nebraska Public Media in August published a thorough report on the effects of the model: There is evidence that rural districts that implement it see higher levels of teacher recruitment and retention and strong improvements in student behavior and attendance, but the financial advantages for districts are not yet widely significant. What’s more, the model leads to knowledge gaps and threatens to put students who are on free-lunch programs or live in abusive households at higher risk. And for rural families, who are often poorer and work blue-collar jobs, the burden of finding additional child care can be financially ruinous.

But the political dynamic is complicated and leaves communities with few options — voters fiercely oppose tax increases, legislators resist new education spending, and rural boards are left with almost no levers to pull except the schedule.

The four-day model is overwhelmingly rural; almost no major urban districts have adopted it, but it is not a particularly ideological trend. Colorado, a reliably blue state (which historically was purple until its urban centers exploded), now has roughly two-thirds of its districts on altered schedules. The issue cuts across partisan lines in state houses, but the consequences do not. Blue or purple states may host these districts, but the rural voters living in them tend to be Republican, meaning the political cost of demographic decline is borne disproportionately by red constituencies.

At the same time, a slower but significant wave of consolidation is working its way through state houses. Education Week reports that states including Wisconsin, Vermont, Ohio, Mississippi, and Pennsylvania are actively considering or implementing district mergers, school closures, or both. These measures tend to generate local controversy, but fixed costs remain high and continue to grow, even as student populations shrink.

States have experimented over the last couple of decades with a range of structural fixes to keep rural schools afloat as enrollment falls. Maine launched one of the most sweeping efforts in 2007, consolidating nearly 300 districts into Regional School Units to share staff, central offices, and specialized programs. This model offered efficiencies but also sparked local resistance and eventual withdrawals when savings fell short of expectations. Vermont’s Act 46 pushed a similar wave of mergers to streamline governance and equalize opportunities across small towns. Wisconsin and Iowa have encouraged “whole grade sharing” agreements that let neighboring districts combine students in certain grades without fully merging. Mississippi has pursued state-mandated consolidations to stabilize chronically small or under-resourced districts. Across the country, districts rely more and more on shared services (including transportation fleets, superintendents, and special-education teams) to manage fixed costs. But, as school districts in rural communities across the country continue to shrink, it is clear that consolidation and cooperation can do little to offset the fiscal and demographic pressures reshaping rural education.

The root problem is a widening gap between enrollment and school structure. Between 2019 and 2024, 68 percent of districts with at least 500 students lost enrollment, yet fewer than 1 percent closed schools. Education Week reports that Covid “hold harmless” funding temporarily shielded districts from the financial consequences of shrinking rosters, but those protections are expiring. State leaders could choose to stabilize rural schools by backfilling lost revenue through targeted aid formulas, dedicated rural funding streams, and initiatives like the Secure Rural Schools payments, but these are typically short-term, underfunded, or politically contested rather than robustly expanded. So far, neither major party has shown much appetite for doing so. Instead, local school boards, which are often the most visible Republican-led institutions in their communities, are left to make the cuts and bear the blame.

These trends point toward a larger structural challenge for conservative America. Rural schools are more than educational institutions — they anchor housing markets, local tax bases, community identity, and the viability of small-town life. As enrollment declines continue and fiscal pressures mount, more districts will experiment with shortened weeks, shared services, and consolidation. The longer states avoid confronting this, the more rural communities — and, with them, Republican strongholds — risk losing the foundational infrastructure that once sustained them.
