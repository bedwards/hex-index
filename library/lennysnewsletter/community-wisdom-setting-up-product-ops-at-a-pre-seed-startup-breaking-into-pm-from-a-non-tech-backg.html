1. Setting up product ops at a pre-seed startup

Q: Hi everyone! I just joined a pre-seed startup as a Fractional Head of Product. One of the initial things they’re asking me to tackle is in the area of product operations (e.g. what tools to use, how to share information across the org, etc.). This is my first experience at a startup (most of my experience is growth-stage or enterprise and I wasn’t the one making these types of decisions. Additionally, I have been in job-search mode for the past 15 months, and a lot has changed in the product-tool area in that time. Here are my questions: What is the best way to approach this? Right now there are multiple systems, as the engineering team (also fractional) has their own ways of working (different tools, etc.). It’s driving the CEO crazy, but she is a non-tech person who is starting a tech company. Are there any “best practices” you advise for a company at this stage? I’m worried about things like protecting IP on free tools, etc. while balancing with overall costs for tools, etc. ↗️

—Rayna M

Joshua Herzig-Marx: I love this stage of company! I often see founders make three mistakes—especially if it’s their first time starting a tech company:

  • They pretend that process (especially written process) can solve alignment problems. The only thing that can solve alignment problems is more conversations—especially between the CEO and the rest of the company.

  • They keep the engineering team at arm’s length and make the relationship too transactional. The engineers they need at this stage are eager to engage with the market and understand user needs, market dynamics, and organizational goals and constraints.

  • They hope someone else will handle the unpleasant parts. The CEO’s job is to find PMF without running out of money. That means lots of conversations with potential customers, lots of founder-led selling, and lots of trying to keep everyone focused on the right ICP and shipping faster ... which at this speed means editing, not broadening.

Personally, I like to keep things simple and flexible. I’d probably be using:

  • User story mapping as our primary product management and design artifact

  • Aggressive milestone-driven roadmaps set by the engineers.

I’d probably leave it at that to start!

Rayna M: Great ideas. Thank you. I am a huge fan of user story mapping in particular. One of the reasons they hired me was because the founders realized that there’s a communication gap and know it will only grow when the product formally launches. That being said, one of the big issues is that engineering is using Slack and the rest of the org uses Teams (they are a Microsoft house). Async conversations, where documents are saved, etc. need to be streamlined. I want to help them solve this first. Then I can move on to the more product-focused stuff like user story mapping and milestone-driven roadmaps (although this will need to come quickly as well).

Joshua Herzig-Marx: That’s an easy problem to solve: everyone uses Slack. Teams was built to solve problems of larger tech-enabled companies. Slack was built to solve tech startup problems. They need to switch.

Drew Burchfield: Yeah, having lived through a moment post-acquisition where there was a Teams/Slack split-teams situation, definitely agree with Joshua Herzig-Marx. Less daily overhead by doing logical streamlining is the way it initially sounds like. Find a few other glaring things like that, assuming the CEO agrees, and you’ve got your short-term impact.

Rayna M: Yes, I’m leaning heavily towards streamlining. I’m only on day 2, so I need to observe and weigh the benefits/weaknesses of each tool and how the different teams work. Cost is a factor as well.

Joshua Herzig-Marx: Coming in as fractional is tough. You’re being hired for your judgment, but what you lack on day 1 is context. My personal opinion is to take on as much of the “physical” labor as possible as soon as you join (running meetings, writing docs, etc.) and try to push off things that require judgment at least until you’re past the first week or two.

Rayna M: Thank you. That is great advice. Their goal is to bring me on full-time, but they either need to get funding or the product needs to go gangbusters out the door.

David Jorjani: I’ve only been part of early-stage and building from the ground up (except for post-acquisition). Doing this now with two startups (8th and 9th) as a fractional. I’d second what Josh said. Stay close to the work, see where things happen and build from there. I’m assuming Slack, GitHub, Google Workspace. I’ve kept work in GitHub for most things, plus some integrations into our paid Slacks. We also (still) use Figma for designs, but I’d suggest keeping it simple and adding systems and processes more slowly than you may imagine is needed until it hurts to stay the course. The cost of distraction, onboarding, adopting, maintaining new systems is very high, especially when everyone is busy with other work. Feel free to respond or DM with specifics. Happy to help.

Rayna M: This is a non-tech org that is moving into the tech space. Other than Engineering, most people want to stay out of the weeds. Any tech detail will be for me, and I will need to translate and communicate to the CEO, etc. The CEO hates Google and won’t even go into Notion (which is very well-organized). They are primarily in the Microsoft ecosystem (which I, honestly, prefer as well. I only like Google Suite/Workspace for collaboration). There are more complexities and politics that I can’t share here, but I will figure it out. Thanks, everyone, for all the advice.

David Jorjani: So your role is not just the expert. You’ll likely be the arbitrator of balance. You figure out the right balance of tech and non-tech and bridge the gap between engineers and the business. It’s more challenging and nuanced. Two of the last businesses I was leading product for were like this. You’ll build credibility and trust with the engineers and the CEO and show them you understand their perspective and can make decisions considering them. Have fun!

Rayna M: Yep. It certainly seems like it’s going that direction. It will be interesting.

2. Breaking into PM with a non-tech background

Q: Hi community! First non-intro group message for me, and I hope this isn’t spam. Wondering if anyone in the community has experience transitioning into a product management role mid-career from totally different areas (non-engineering, non-tech). I have a few years of product-adjacent experience at a tech startup (customer success), but the vast majority of my ±15 years have been in other, less related areas. Curious if anyone might have some experience and pointers they might be able to share about what it took to break into the space laterally. ↗️

—Karim

Steven Jones: Are you looking for your first product management job? I’d leverage your domain experience (do you have 15 years of experience in a certain industry?) and then try to find a product job with a startup that may be more willing to take someone on with less product-specific experience but places a high priority on domain experience.

Hanna Zoon: How I went into PM was that I was hired as a user researcher/UX designer, and after a few months they were like “you are always so opinionated about what we should build and also you hang out with our users and stakeholders all the time anyway, please do this PO thing on top of your role” and I was like “okay.”

Jamie Wilkey: I feel you on this, @Karim. I was a pharmacist for 10 years and then ran my own company for 5. I just landed my first formal PM job this past summer, and the only way I got in was to leverage all the PM work I did as a founder and to take a super pay cut to get in as a Product Manager I role. It is giving me great experience, but gosh it is a hit to my pride to feel like I’m starting over again in a career. I’ll do this job for a full year (or 18 months) to get that experience under my belt, and then I want to make the move into health tech at a startup.

So I guess that’s my advice: break in wherever you can find an opening and then start working your way up. It is hard to feel like a beginner again mid-career, but I’ve learned so much about product that I feel like it is worth it. I just have to put blinders on and not compare myself to my peers who’ve been in product much longer than me and have the big, fancy titles.

Karim: Thanks, @Steven Jones, @Hanna Zoon and @Jamie Wilkey. These are all really useful insights. @Jamie Wilkey—your experience seems similar to mine, having built and run my own startup and preparing to kind of take a hit in terms of career stature—but that’s ok. I love feeling like a beginner and learning quickly—plus I’m not starting completely from scratch. Just hoping that when I do break in, I can grow and advance quickly.

Thanks again, you guys! Appreciate your insights!

Z Rossi: Late to the party, wanted to double down on the above from @Jamie Wilkey.

Although I did it within one company: went from retail department supervisor to administrative assistant (to finish my degree), to intern (demotion), to Business Analyst, Sr. Data Analyst, Manager 3rd-party Product and Order Management Systems (OMS), to Platform Product Manager (demotion), to AI/ML Sr. Product Manager.

My lessons learned:

  • Volunteer for the jobs people don’t want to do

    • I took over two Product Manager roles in practice if not in title, doing this while I was an analyst and sr. analyst, which gave me knowledge and experience to speak to in an interview and use in practice

  • Seek out opportunities to learn

    • People like explaining what they do, and it helped me pick up all kinds of technical skills along the way

  • Don’t be afraid to take a risk

    • A “demotion” that allows you to learn new things is actually a degree program you are getting paid to do, that comes with real-world experience and networking

I love to chat with people on a non-traditional path to product. Feel free to DM me if you ever want to chat, do a mock interview, etc.

3. Being an AI champion without burning out

Q: As someone who uses AI tools like Claude Code and Gemini every day, I got the opportunity to be the AI champion within my company and for my clients. However, I found myself doing a bunch of repetitive lunch & learning sessions and then many coworkers came to me with many basic questions. On one hand, I would love to help others in my company to better use these helpful tools. On the other hand, I feel like it’s not my main job and time-consuming. Curious, any other AI champions out there? How did you approach this? ↗️

—Angelina You

Steven Jones: Are you formally the AI champion or just the de facto go-to person? It sounds to me like this is an opportunity that you can leverage to make yourself truly indispensable. I would try to galvanize your role. Work with marketing to be featured in a webinar. See if there may be an opportunity to give a presentation to the executive. Chair a committee on AI best practices.

Highlight the value you are delivering and see if you can get approval to hire an intern to help you deliver on this value.

Unless you truly are not interested in trying to make this a bigger part of your role. In that case, it is important to be able to defend your time, and it’s fair to place appropriate limits. I would suggest working with your manager ahead of time so that everyone is on the same page and you have air cover when you do push back.

James Conway: Build an FAQ with Loom videos? Use your support ticket system to have an Ask Angelina flow (naturally, use AI for the responses).

Final thought... Are they asking the questions not because they want to know how but to get your permission?

Skip Shean: How about using AI to make a “getting started with AI in business” course that you run them through before they can come to the next-level stuff you want to be training on?

Miroslav Pavelek: I would recommend to create some channel/chat (whatever you use) and let others also answers the questions. Essentially building an internal AI community.
