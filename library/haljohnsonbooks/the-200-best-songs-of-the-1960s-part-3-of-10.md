## 159 [Duke of Earl](https://youtu.be/Qa88gqvXJMc) by Gene Chandler (1961)

In the early ’60s music really seemed to be experimenting with how stupid it could sound. I know that comes off as insulting, but I mean this in a good way, in the way The Ramones would try something similar a decade or so later. We think of rock ’n’ roll of the 1950s as a *stripped down* version of previous pop music, but you could also call it (and people did!) a *dumbed down* version of previous pop music. You dumb it down enough and only the good parts show.

We’ll have dumber songs later on in this list, and we’ve had [arguably dumber](https://haljohnsonbooks.substack.com/i/176958740/alley-oop-by-the-hollywood-argyles) songs previously, but right now we have “Duke of Earl,” a nonsense lyric over the durpiest of doo-wop backgrounds. *Duke-duke-duke of Earl* sounds like someone making fun of doo-wop singers, but it’s also, and this is important, irresistibly catchy. Makes you want to go *duke-duke-duke* yourself, doesn’t it? It’s like the Twist—almost not a dance, but don’t you want to try it out?

The lyrics don’t make more sense from that point on. It’s also a love song because songs have to be love songs. “We’ll walk through my dukedom. / It’s a paradise we will share.”

One thing I love about pop music from the 1920s is that the singers so often sound like they’re joking, and, similarly here, Gene Chandler sounds like he’s in on the joke. The way he dresses on the album cover seems to indicate that this is half a novelty song.

## 158 [I Sat Back and Let It Happen](https://youtu.be/4cDWIqYkWRM) by Leroy Van Dyke (1962)

Leroy Van Dyke is better known for his single “[Walk on By](https://youtu.be/8QlqQA8CyjE),” which was a monster hit in 1961 but is still not [the “Walk on By” you’re probably thinking of](https://youtu.be/vsGsCvJWEo8). Even better, though, is this uptempo story of a man who loses his girl through Hamletty inaction: “I sat back and let it happen.”

There’s a tradition in country music especially of allowing a chorus’s words to return at the end of a song with the meaning reversed—The Oak Ridge Boys practically [specialized](https://youtu.be/J-HHIgvdsjs) [in](https://youtu.be/y-hgBTzka90) [it](https://youtu.be/DkNHqGPnK2k) (check those links!)—and here Van Dyke fantasizes about a future in which his girl has returned to him and they can “sit back and let it *happen*” (his stress). Not passively allow love to fade, but make love happen i.e. bloom.

Just a fantasy, though. Love the bass “boom boom” background under Van Dyke’s already deeper-than-usual vocals.

## 157 [Eep Opp Ork Ah-ah](https://youtu.be/rZoWTh9thuE) by Jet Screamer (1962)

Hanna-Barbera cartoons are generally marked by solid music, with *Josie and the Pussycats* especially being [filled](https://youtu.be/QXBx2yzaNwA) [with](https://youtu.be/v6b7tZglKGU&t=32) [banging](https://youtu.be/YOrblU5Ttyk) [pop](https://youtu.be/JQZ7kZ_or0Q) (check those links, too!)—but before Josie, it was jazz, specifically jazz written by Hoyt Curtain. *Jonny Quest* probably had the best incidental jazz score, but everyone remembers [the](https://youtu.be/955rFSoAE48) *[Jetsons](https://youtu.be/955rFSoAE48)* [theme](https://youtu.be/955rFSoAE48), which hit #9 on the Billboard charts in…1986? Whatever you’re into, radio fans.

[

![Jet_Screamer_and_dancers](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!nbSs!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6542d708-8cb9-473a-8451-127845192775_1000x563.webp "Jet_Screamer_and_dancers")



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!nbSs!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6542d708-8cb9-473a-8451-127845192775_1000x563.webp)

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hoyt_Curtin#The_Jetsons), in a hamfisted way I’d probably have to make an account to correct, thinks the #9 hit was this tune, the elegantly titled “Eep Opp Ork Ah-ah” (only played by Jet Screamer “in-world”—I assume it was really Howard Morris and Hoy Curtain). I guess maybe “Eep Opp Ork Ah-ah” *also* charted in 1986, by some miracle, but probably this is all just wish fulfillment, because “Eep Opp Ork Ah-ah” is 700X better than the *Jetsons* theme! It’s futuristic rockabilly with a midcentury modern esthetic, contemporary guitar licks, and beeps and boops. But it’s also (“nobody digs a word we say”) an old man’s view of 1962 rock ’n’ roll, at once ridiculous and juvenile: funny funny faces and ipso facto every nonsense utterance must be about love. It’s one of those rare songs that is at once a parody of the genre it exists in and a wonderful example of the genre it exists it (you know, like the songs from *Nashville*).

## 156 [The Bridge](https://youtu.be/b7nbNNxsnGQ) by Dolly Parton (1968)

Dolly Parton would come into her own in the 1970s and stay near the top of the music world for the next two decades (at least), but if her ’60s work doesn’t quite reach the heights she would later attain, it was often excellent in its own right. Especially memorable is this beautiful tragic ballad, sung with an uncharacteristic lisp (?).

Points off for the line “Our emotions filled the air”; but so many points extra for the boldest ending in all of country music.

## 155 [Sugar Babe, It’s All Over Now](https://youtu.be/sWyLnYGSKwc) by Mark Spoelstra (1963)

You listen to Mark Spoelstra’s smooth, confident voice and you might assume this song would fall in the top 50 on this list; then you hear that virtuoso guitar, and you might think top 20.

So why is it down here at #155? For me, at least, the vocals and the music just don’t work together. They sit awkwardly on top of each other, instead of blending into an organic whole.

Once again I find myself only saying negative things about a song I love. Why am I so mean?

## 154 [Always Something There to Remind Me](https://youtu.be/JoUi41ZktaY) by Lou Johnson (1964)

Another former gospel singer—his group, The Zionettes, had the best name since [The Fabulons](https://haljohnsonbooks.substack.com/i/176981939/connie-by-the-fabulons)—Lou Johnson here sings the most Bacharachy of Bacharach compositions, and single-handedly salvages the song from mediocrity. The opening horns are too “tasteful,” the opening vocal too slow and measured…but then it builds to the belt-it-out chorus, and everything is forgiven.

## 153 [Mama’s Doing the Twist](https://youtu.be/E764F2UAsdE) by Linda Hopkins (1962)

I know [earlier](https://haljohnsonbooks.substack.com/i/176981939/meet-me-at-the-twistin-place-by-johnnie-morisette) I was [talking big](https://haljohnsonbooks.substack.com/i/176981939/twist-and-shout-by-the-isley-brothers) about the best Twist songs, but I had forgotten all about this delightful number! Despite a sixty-year career, Linda Hopkins never really had a hit single; she did once reached #21 on the R&B charts, which is something, but she’s probably better remembered for her work on Broadway.

This B-side, a cover of an Eve Boswell song I have never heard and cannot find, is not necessarily typical of the Hopkins sound, but it’s a great tune right from the opening “Ma! Hey, Ma!” shout. What follows is a top-drawer twister with a rocking beat and fun vocals, but it’s also the story of how an easy and family-friendly dance temporarily closed the generation gap; it would reopen, of course, but somewhere along the line rock ’n’ roll would have to come to grips with the idea that it was not just for teenagers. In part this was because the teenagers were growing up, but in part it was because the beat was just so infectious.

Like, remember how in Ferris Bueller’s father can’t resist doing a little bit of the Twist in his office?

## 150 [I Resign from Your Love](https://youtu.be/1Hp9dX2KtVc) by Syl Johnson (1963)

That wild animal yell is one of the best openings to any record! Syl Johnson is better known for his later, funkier recordings, but for my money nothing can beat this tight number. It would be a solid rocker even with the yell, *but it also has the yell!* What a way to resign!

Don’t confuse it with Johnson’s [other very similarly titled single](https://youtu.be/IUcOqDqFk08)!

## 149 [Two Lovers](https://youtu.be/oa4MqpN-A2k) by Mary Wells (1962)

You liked the Twist, didn’t you, music fans of 1962? Well here’s a twist for you!

Mary Wells’s scandalous little number belongs with “[The Naughty Lady of Shady Lane](https://youtu.be/JGpR6R3a1D4)” (or even “[Shaving Cream](https://youtu.be/sKVCCcnHFw8?)” and “[Darbytown](https://youtu.be/Hf5Hgbynykk)”) with it’s “we’re singing something naughty—whoops! no we aren’t!,” except it is completely self-serious. That self-seriousness could be unbearable, but Wells sells it.

I assume you know the twist, but if you don’t—well, I’m not here to spoil it for you.

Another Smokey Robinson composition; another Motown hit.

## 147 [Remember Then](https://youtu.be/Rgqzb-u6Q0c) by The Earls (1963)

The nasal and almost sarcastic “re-meh-meh, re-meh-meh-member” is so irresistible that it’s easy to forget all the other great components of this doo-wop classic: the barbershoppy “then, then, then, then, then” rising, an opening riff that sounds like CCR’s “Looking Out My Back Door” (I am not crazy), and especially Larry Chance(?)’s sublime vocals on the bridge, a high point in a song full of high points.

## 146 [I Know a Place](https://youtu.be/7fFmg2pmtTs) by Petula Clark (1964)

We all think of the British Invasion as being made up of rock combos, but there’s more than one way to British-Invade, and here comes Petula Clark, already an international star of two decades’ standing in Europe, riding the wave of swinging London’s hep status to finally start charting in the US. “Downtown” was a #1 hit in 1964, and “I Know a Place” is her very similar follow-up. On another day perhaps I might have preferred one of her other great hits, but I’ve always had a soft spot in my heart for this number, a rather ridiculous and vague description of a music venue. Perhaps it’s located downtown.

As befits a pan-European star who recorded in multiple languages, Clark made her own French version of the single: “[Viens Avec Moi](https://youtu.be/NL9ypn-phL4).”

## 145 [Tossin’ and Turnin’](https://youtu.be/ghFBvBmXv4E) by Bobby Lewis (1960)

An orphan, slowly going blind, runs away to join the carnival circuit and works his way up to recording, improbably, the most popular pop song of 1960. Improbable but true.

“Tossin’ and Turnin’” is a step above the usual R&B singles of the time (many of which, o.c., were excellent) because of the vocals: The gospel-infused background vocals, sure, but mostly Bobby Lewis, himself, with that inimitable hitch in his voice when he sings “just-a thinking of you.”

Not even [this Almond Delight commercial](https://youtu.be/4xgB3KQ6HC8) can ruin the song.

## 144 [Lizzie Borden](https://youtu.be/kdF428jC6vw) by The Chad Mitchell Trio (1962)

The Chad Mitchell Trio weren’t specifically a novelty act, the way Tom Lehrer, say, was, but when I think of them, it’s always a song like “John Birch Society” or “Super Skier” or this hilarious experiment in black humor. Maybe the folk scene of the time just had a tongue often in cheek—even arch-squares like The Kingston Trio covered “[Charlie on the M.T.A.](https://youtu.be/7Tc1GUXxr2o)”—but in any event here Chad and his pals riff off the most famous quatrain in the annals of American crime to create a satire on another folk staple: the murder ballad.

(The murder ballad it reminds me the most of is Hazel Dickens’s “[The Yablonski Murder](https://youtu.be/6stAMgnPiGY),” but clearly this 1962 single is not satirizing a crime that happened in 1969; so never mind about that.)

While The Trio always had a way with words (and their John Birch song linked above is only one notch below Lehrer himself), come “Lizzie Borden” they outdid themselves with its frenzied grotesque humor. “They said, Lizzie, cut it out, / So that’s exactly what she did.”

One gag that I didn’t understand in my youth (and, judging by their muted response, that the live audience in 1962 didn’t get either) was: “Such a snob! I’ve heard it said / She met her pa and cut him dead.” Etiquette books have since taught me that to “cut him dead” merely means to snub him. So I offer that explanation for those unfamiliar with the slang that was apparently already obsolescent in 1962.

## 142 [Surfin’ Bird](https://youtu.be/Qgmh0BjLKVM) by The Trashmen (1963)

The genesis of rock ’n’ roll was, like many geneses, also an apocalypse, an apocalypse for the pop music of the past. Songs had once had lyrics by mature professionals, so in “[Cocktails for Two](https://youtu.be/VhBlsRzSo8A)” Howard Phillips could rhyme “cigarette” with “chansonnette” and “serviette,” while the catty wittiness of “[Tangerine](https://youtu.be/q-JDUnZv1N0)” was practically Algonquin. “[Tutti Frutti](https://youtu.be/NnIIvWnpaBU),” “[Wooly Bully](https://youtu.be/KZJiGu6Gz8E),” or “[Hanky Panky](https://youtu.be/DCnXFePGGmU)” are all great songs, but lyrically they come from another planet, a primitive planet of barbarous cavemen.

And that’s just the lyrics, which are of course subservient to the actual music. In *On Chesil Beach*, set in 1962, Ian McEwan has his classical music aficionado character complain:

> When the tunes were so elementary, mostly in simple four-four time, why this relentless thumping and crashing and clattering to keep time? What was the point, when there was already a rhythm guitar, and often a piano? If the musicians needed to hear the beats, why not get a metronome?

*Why not get a metronome? You are so dumb!* No argument there, but this was part of the great rock experiment: to see how dumb music could get and still stay music. As I’ve said before, punk would try some of the same things business in a later decade…but before there was punk, there was “Surfin’ Bird,” a partially improvised cover of a song The Trashmen had heard second-hand the night before.

I once read (probably in some liner notes) that the most punk rock moment in musical history was when Jonathan Richman decided to do another chorus of his childlike bubblegum jingle “Ice Cream Man” in front of hostile, incredulous, booing punks at CBGB. Before that the most punk rock moment in musical history had been “Surfin’ Bird”—The Trashmen had put such a period on this method of contempt that Richman had to turn things in the opposite direction to outpunk them.

Obviously professional lyricists (Brill Building, etc.) still flourished in the rock era, as did pre-rock pop forms ([Peggy Lee hit Billboard #11](https://youtu.be/eMsXjKBZM74) in 1969); furthermore, rock ’n’ roll would get smarter, not always to its advantage. But we had to tear it down to build it up, and “Great Balls of Fire” or “Louie Louie” did their part, but no one tears it down (or tears it up) like “Surfin’ Bird.”

## 141 [The Horse Wrangler](https://youtu.be/9wBWcx5vKAM) by Roger Welsch (1965)

[

![Sweet Nebraska Land](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KOtz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcd9a4fc7-c3dc-446e-8f6a-bd114d375139_649x700.jpeg "Sweet Nebraska Land")



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KOtz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcd9a4fc7-c3dc-446e-8f6a-bd114d375139_649x700.jpeg)

Roger Welsch was an academic folklorist at Nebraska Wesleyan University who, in celebration of Nebraska’s state centennial,[1](#footnote-1) recorded an album of traditional Nebraska folk songs that had been “collected” decades earlier as part of an FDR-era WPA project. The plum of the lot is this wry cowboy ballad about the hard lot of the cow puncher.

In Nebraska, Welsch was apparently known as a humorous commentator, a kind of local Andy Rooney type—Wikipedia doesn’t even mention his musical endeavors beyond a reluctant discography as a kind of appendix. Welsch is actually an excellent guitarist, but the whole glory of the song lies in his bemused delivery. “And in the morning—if you don’t die…” The song is funny without being a novelty tune; it’s cowboy exaggeration, which is always fun even when it’s not funny; and Welsch sings it like a cowboy—not the lonesome type, but the tall-tale type. “Then cut your throat with a butcher knife, ’cause it’s easier done that way.”

In context, it’s funny.

[1](#footnote-anchor-1)

The actual centennial would not come until 1967; don’t blame me.
