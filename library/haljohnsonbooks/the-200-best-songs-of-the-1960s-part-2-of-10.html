Remember the rules: One cut per artist. That’s the main rule. Part 1 (#181–200) is here, part 3 (#141–160) here.

180 Cry like a Baby by The Box Tops (1968)

A very weird rock ’n’ roll song made weirder by the fact that it also pure pop, and a little bubble-gummy. It also has everything thrown in, an electric sitar (!), an opening John-Cale-y organ drone, a backup girl-group (but only for the second half), a muted breakdown section that they may have just forgotten to fill in, and a gruff “rock” vocal curiously low in the mix. That’s too many ideas for one song! But the gestalt works, because the bubblegum poppiness is so very poppy. The electric sitar sounds pretty sweet, like something from a Men without Hats album. An opening organ drone is **always cool**. By the time the horns and strings come in, their excess just sounds natural.

179 It’s My Party by Lesley Gore (1963)

Now that’s good melodrama! This track combines busy and complicated production values (Quincy Jones produced) with the raw emotion of a genuine teenager: Lesley Gore, unlike most of the people on this list, was an actual Baby Boomer, and only sixteen when she knocked this one out.

Early rock ’n’ roll is powered by pure punk teenage emotion; by the ’60s people (esp. but hardly exclusively Phil Spector) had learned to couple that raw emotion with the kind of emotional background that is anything but raw; eventually music “grows up,” which is a weird thing for music to do, and a teenage party will sound like a ridiculous topic to write a song about. But that’s too bad. It was a glorious moment, when a song like this could make sense.

178 Meet Me at the Twistin’ Place by Johnnie Morisette (1962)

By 1962, all of America had fallen in love with, become disenchanted with, and then fallen in love all over again with the Twist! That’s when Sam Cooke wrote and produced this single for Johnnie Morisette, and, like…who’s Johnnie Morisette? No one knows, because not even the combined powers of Sam Cooke and the Twist could keep this single from obscurity, which is UNJUST! Because this song is great! “Man, I sure wish I had somewhere to go,” it starts, stating a problem before offering the solution: the twisting place! Johnnie Morisette’s gritty R&B vocals contrast splendidly with the squeaky-clean, much whiter backup singers, and that’s the twist in a nutshell. This might be the best song ever recorded about twisting that is not also about shouting.

177 Those Were the Days by The Limelighters (1962)

Glenn Yarbrough’s creepy-ass voice is seared into my brain because he played the minstrel of Gondor in the Rankin-Bass TV special Return of the King: “Frodo of the nine fingers…”. But here he is singing not about a ring of doom but about the terrifying passage of time.

The song is (they say) and old Russian folk song, and it certainly presents as one, with its haunting minor chords. I know nothing about Russian music beyond, you know, Tchaikovsky or Mussorgsky, but this sounds plausible—certainly the music is Slavoiserie, if nothing else. And the lyrics are as bitter as bittersweet gets, a Carmina Burana dirge to lost youth. That’s all good. Whichever Limeliter is singing lead—presumably Alex Hassilev, a Russian-Jewish emigre—is fine, and the song is haunting, and then the chorus comes and—

—and it’s Glenn Yarbrough, with a voice from the time before time (Middle Earth, I guess). Those were the days, he sings, and he’s only part of the harmony, but he takes over and it all sounds so long ago. Those were the days.

This is the reason The Limeliters’ version is better than Mary Hopkin’s. Hopkin didn’t have Yarbrough singing backup.

176 Rice Is Nice by The Lemon Pipers (1968)

The Lemon Pipers were one of those ’60s bands that did not want to play the music the industry demanded of them, and yet they managed, however reluctantly, to produce the absolute finest moment of bubblegum pop (um…depending on your definitions) of the decade. It’s 1968 and someone is still putting out a love song celebrating marriage (metonymized through thrown rice)!

175 Hello Muddah, Hello Fadduh by Alan Sherman (1963)

Of all the things mainstream music has lost since the ’60s (or perhaps the ’70s), the most unjustly unmourned is the loss of a sense of humor. Sure, “Weird” Al is great, but he didn’t have a top ten hit until 2006—only his second top forty single! I guess Biz Markie got to #9 in 1990…that’s something. Fill in your favorites here, but they’re all fringe productions.

Meanwhile, back in 1963, Alan Sherman could bring a nineteenth-century opera tune with barely sung lyrics full of goofy camp jokes to #2 on the Billboard Hot 100. For shame, modern world!

I’m not damning with faint praise: This song is great! Goofy jokes are good jokes! And the simplicity of the tune makes it perfect for singalongs—this is one of those songs that people who have never heard the recording may have learned at a summer camp. One wonders what “kids these days” make of a lyric like: “And the head coach wants no sissies / So he reads to us from something called Ulysses.”

174 Sittin’ on Top of the World by Roscoe Holcomb (1964)

You can kind of see how the music world was prepped, in the mid ’50s, for rock and roll. The music of the time, while not bad, was soft, safe, decadent: just a bunch of late-period Romans sitting around waiting for the barbarians to come.

This is, I think, the mainstream take, the rockist take, but it obscures the fact that before the ’50s music was strange, dangerous, and whatever is the opposite of decadent (acandent?).

We’re not here to talk about Greil Marcus’s “Old Weird America”…we’re here to talk about the 1960s…but here comes Roscoe Holcomb and his banjo, singing the old songs in the old way, and he sounds practically primeval, like a track from the Harry Smith Anthology.

“I killed a man / With a borrowed (?) knife / Got (?) 99 years / [unintelligible] life.”

The ’60s folk revival brought all manner of folk music back into American homes, and it’s easy to think of The Kingston Trio or Bob Dylan. There’ll be older performers on this list, but none of it will sound older. None of it will be this alien. Dylan once said, “If I told you what my music was about, we’d all be arrested,” but nothing he sang ever sounded as dangerous as Roscoe Holcomb, borrowed knife or no.

173 I Was Gone by Eddy Clearwater (1961)

Another gospel performer drawn to the blues, Eddy Clearwater (his stage name is a play off Muddy Waters) is remembered for his six (!) decades-long career playing Chicago Blues, but this track is from an earlier period, when he was still under the spell of Chuck Berry. Cincinnati’s Federal Records (the label that is hardest to Google, because just try) put out a lot of great 45s in the ’50s and ’60s, but this is one of the best, a rollicking story of reckless driving (“I moved up to one-two-oh” (i.e. m.p.h.)) that is propelled both by its Berryish R&B guitar and its unusual call-and-response structure with the mocking backup band: “Are you crazy?” a Greek chorus asks Clearwater; “stay gone,” it demands when he brags, “I was gone.” Maybe to escape them was the whole reason he was going. They’re merciless.

As a bonus, the cop who pulls the narrator over has his siren invoked/imitated by a great irritating sax riff.

Clearwater’s uncle, the Rev. Houston H. Harrington, owned the Chicago Atomic-H label, which is how a Mississippi lad like Eddy Clearwater ended up playing Chicago blues in the first place. The Chuck Berry sounds, though, is pure fandom.

172 Lost My Job by The Del-Airs (1960)

Don’t confuse the Del-Airs with the Del-Aires, the rock band from Horror at Party Beach (also a great band)! Oh no, these Del-Airs are a doo-wop group from Philadelphia, where the Del-Aires’ pendleton shirts were presumably illegal. In fact, they’re a great doo-wop group! They only put out two or three (sources differ) singles, but one of them is “Lost My Job,” which combines rock ’n’ roll instrumentation and a rockabilly breakdown with the bum-bum-bum backgrounds and wailing falsettos from acapella doo-wop. None of that is unique, or even unusual—it just works great here! The mild griping (“why oh why do I try?”) lyric is clearly harkening back to earlier (and even better) songs: The Coasters’ “Yakety Yak” or The Silhouettes’ “Get a Job”—losing a job is hardly an existential threat here, just a mild inconvenience. Now the singers have no “pay,” “bread,” OR “dough.” The whole thing is in fun.

Incidentally, the site “White Doo Wop Collector” claims this track is from 1962, but Discogs says 1960, so I went with the latter.

171 Mountain of Love by Johnny Rivers (1965)

Is it just me or does no one talk about Johnny Rivers any more? The dude had nine top ten hits! I guess most of them are only okay (including his overwrought chart topper), but whatever; maybe I’m misreading the room, but I feel like we’re treating this guy like a one-hit wonder, when actually he cranked out hits for years.

Anyway, this is the only Rivers recording I really love. Here he took Harold Dorman’s excellent 1960 single, removed the strings, added a harmonica, and sped it up slightly, improving it 100%. That should be enough for any career, but, like I said—Rivers put out the hits! Choose your poison.

170 Carrie Anne by The Hollies (1967)

Don’t you hate it when your girl runs off with a prefect? Man, the ’60s were so British.

But the good thing about being British is you get British pop, and The Hollies deliver with a catchy tune, steel drums, and a chorus that both rocks and harmonizes, which people don’t usually try simultaneously.

169 Colours by Donovan (1965)

In 1995, Adrian Tomine put out a comic book about a hippie who named his two daughters after musical legends: Dylan and…Donovan? How did he get in there?

Isn’t that what’s annoying about Donovan? They guy is always punching above his weight. And then there’s the whole sensitive half-fey persona that I don’t even know is Donovan’s fault, but it clings to him like a miasma. Don’t you want to hate him?

But what can I say? This song is beautiful.

168 Connie by The Fabulons (1963)

The late ’50s and early ’60s actually had quite a few mixed-race musical groups, even if, Del-Vikings aside, such compositions were forbidden among the more successful. Where would they play? What labels would release them? I guess it upset the whole musical applecart, which was predicated on two kinds of music, white and “race,” even if everyone listened to everything, at least on the sly.

But as long as you were not successful, things were more loosey-goosey. Bring on The Fabulons (great name!) from Stamford, Conn. In fact, The Fabulons’ name is so choice that a Florida party band has claimed it, making it hard to look up much of anything about the original group. But the AUTHOR IS DEAD, so let’s just worry about this great track! Built around a tasteful piano riff, with gentle “ahh ahh” background vocals and a somewhat inane (“You’re my guiding light / Each and every night”), this piece sounds like something from six years earlier. Of course, music was still great six years earlier, so that’s no complaint! Bert Person’s vocals are so smooth and the song drives along with the secret implicit in so much pop of the era: that talking about love always sounds inane, which is why the music is there to convey the meaning for you.

“Teach me of the birds and bees. / Oh love me, won’t you please?”

167 I’m a Nut by Leroy Pullins (1966)

Leroy Pullins’s sole hit is this bonkers little novelty tune that combines borschty gags (“only forest fires prevent bears”), self-deprecating celebrations (?) of how impoverished Pullins is (“if it took a dime to go ’round the world, I couldn’t get out of sight”), solid guitar work, and this…noise that Pullins can make…presumably with his mouth? Remember when yodeling was big and country singers would all yodel?—I mean, I don’t remember it either, it was like a hundred years ago, but I know it happened. Anyway, I think Pullins was trying to create something new, like yodeling, that could take the country world by storm.

In some alternate history, everyone would be making that noise. But Pullins turned out to be a one-hit wonder: He left music to become (Wikipedia says) a fireman, and this track is the only immortality he and that noise will share.

166 Part-Time Love by Little Johnny Taylor (1963)

Little Johnny (not to be confused with Johnnie Taylor, who is also good) Taylor’s biggest hit is this wonderful slow piece, its creepy music—a noodling blues guitar over truly ominous drones—matching the morose, defeated vocals. “People in the cemetery / They’re not all alone.” Unlike, presumably, Little Johnny Taylor. Creeptastic.

This might be the first (but will hardly be the last) time that my one-per-artist rules really hurt me in the application. On another day I would have picked a different Little Johnny Taylor song.

165 Bye-bye Birdie by Ann-Margret (1963)

It’s hard to separate the song “Bye-bye Birdie” from its music video—I mean the opening (and closing: treat it as a two-sided single) credits of the film of the same name, with Ann-Margret on an unseen conveyor belt that perpetually threatens to carry her away from the camera as she scurries to stay in one place. She also mugs shamelessly and makes that weird obscene hand gesture from Friends, all against a blank blue background. It’s unforgettable.

But we’re not here for music videos! “Bye-bye Birdie” works blind, and it works because of Ann-Margret, who mostly refuses to sing (although she could really sing), preferring to belt out a brassy, over-breathy (Birdie = Bird–heee) pout. None of this should work, but it sounds like nothing else and is, like that conveyor belt, unforgettable.

The rest of the movie doesn’t hold up, though.

164 In Dreams by Roy Orbison (1963)

Yeah, it’s hard to separate this one from its music video, too.

161 Twist and Shout by The Isley Brothers (1962)

I feel like Dave Barry spends enough time defending The Isley Brothers (against the Beatles) that the rest of us can just shut up about it. Anyway, it’s true: The Isleys’ version is better. Dance party indispensable.

162 Israelites by Desmond Dekker and the Aces (1968)

I don’t want to come across as a guy who loves reggae, because, you know, I’d be a type. I’d have to get a whole new set of clothes; change my identity; it sounds like a lot of work.

But this song, this song is great! Even for 1969 (when it charted in the US) it must have sounded different from the rest of the charting records—and lots of records sounded different from each other in 1969! Dekker just comes across so cool as he croons, “I don’t want to end up like Bonnie and Clyyyyde.”

161 Barbara by The Temptations (no, not those ones) (1960)

What do you want from me? There were more than one band called The Temptations in the 1960s (although apparently not simultaneously). I don’t know anything about this earlier group except that 1. they were from New York, and 2. one of them would “climb the highest mountain” to get Barbara to “wear [his] high school ring.” That kind of bathos is what I’m all about. That’s what the early ’60s were great at, and that’s why we’re here.

Also I love the plink plink plink of the opening, whatever it is they’re plinking. What a great song!
