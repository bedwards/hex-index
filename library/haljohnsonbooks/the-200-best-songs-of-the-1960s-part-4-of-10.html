(Parts one, two, three)

140 Ode to Billie Joe by Bobbie Gentry (1967)

I was going to put “Harper Valley PTA” here, because I sing it every time the parents at the playground talk about the local PTA, but then I remembered it’s the same song as “Ode to Billie Joe,” so this one gets in on precedence.

139 I Know by Barbara George (1961)

People don’t sing in church choirs any more and that’s why we don’t have any real singers? Is this true or just a conspiracy theory?

Anyway, Barbara George was another chorister who became a great R&B singer. She was a teenager, married for three years, and a mother of one when she wrote and recorded her first and only album. Driven by a simple, catchy piano from its opening seconds and featuring George’s heartfelt, brassy vocals, this single deservedly became a crossover hit…and then George faded from music history as fast as she rocketed in. The ’60s, like the ’80s, were a time of one-hit wonders, and George will neither be the first nor the last on our list. I hope that, as the writer of this track, she actually got a payout.

138 Alice’s Restaurant Massacree by Arlo Guthrie (1967)

I wouldn’t want every song to be like this, but I’m glad one is.

Of all second-generation musicians, Arlo Guthrie had the biggest shoes to fill (sorry, Julian Lennon), and while is output is usually competent, he had one moment of genius. Woody Guthrie played a mean talking blues, but Woody Guthrie never wove a shaggy-dog story like this—not even in those CDs of endless Alan Lomax interviews: A cute little anecdote (about littering) that blossoms into an anti-war song that potentially blossoms into a movement. What a portrait of the times!

Incidentally, I assume that it is pure hell having your signature song be over eighteen minutes long, and you barely even get to jam.

137 Do You Love Me? by The Contours (1962)

A song famously earmarked for The Temptations, who were at church (!) when Berry Gordy came looking for them. The Contours were at the Motown studio, though, so Gordy tried them out, and the rest is dance party history.

“Do You Love Me?” may be the purest encapsulation of 1960s pop of any song not produced by Phil Spector (which is perhaps weird because this is an R&B song?). The lyrics literally could not be dumber—it takes zero skill to Mash Potato or to do the Twist. Look at a printout of the words and try to piece the song together by reading them:

Work, work!
Well, I’m gonna drive you crazy.
Work, work!
You are getting kind of cold, now.
Work, work!
With just a little bit of soul, now.

It’s not even English! Even the premise (set forth in the introduction) is silly.

And yet the music is not stupid. The music is about how important it is to dance the Twist. The fade out and return defeats (and what else in music does?) The Isely Brothers. That weird opening tremolo guitar (?). And…how many ’60s songs are this quotable (I know I just said the lyrics are dumb, but…)? “Now I’m back to let you know I can really shake ’em down.” “Watch me now, hey!” “Work, work!” Why is it impossible not to chant “work, work”?

Could The Temptations have pulled this off?

136 Ramona by The Blue Diamonds (1960)

The Blue Diamonds were two brothers, Indonesian emigrants to the Netherlands who were, apparently, popular in Europe, but in 1960 they had a modest US hit with this rocked-up version of an old standard. A crack at the US market makes sense: If the Everly Brothers had not yet, in 1960, been paralyzed by the triple blow of military enlistment, drug addiction, and a lawsuit that prevented them from writing their own songs, they soon would be, and the Hot 100 biome was ready for something Everlyish to fill their eco-niche; and if there’s one thing The Blue Diamonds were, it’s Everlyish.

Nevertheless, stateside stardom eluded them, but we’ll always have “Ramona,” a pretty but sleepy melody converted into a still mellow but nevertheless drum-crashy (I mean drum-crashy for 1960) rocking single. It supposedly moved a million copies in Germany; a shame it didn’t catch on here.

135 I’m Tore Down by Freddy King (1961)

Chicago blues is usually not my kind of blues, but at this point in Freddy (as he spelled it then) King’s career, a side could be like 2/3 R&B with only 1/3 Chicago noodling, and that turns out to be the right ratio. And what a great lyric!: “I’m tore down / I’m almost level with the ground.”

134 Different Drum by The Stone Poneys (1967)

“Wow, that’s a lot of harpsichord for a Stone Poneys record,” you may be thinking. It’s actually a lot of harpsichord for any song. It does work, though. I don’t usually think of myself as a Linda Rondstadt fan, but here she delivers one of the great vocal performances of the decade, so go figure.

A Mike Nesmith composition rejected by The Monkees (or their zookeepers); I mention this because I think it is hilarious that this is the one Stone Poneys song where the Poneys don’t play their own instruments.

133 Never Comes the Day by The Moody Blues (1969)

The Moody Blues are a pickle, by turns beautiful and terrible (you know, like the morning and the night). Some of the recited poetry is unendurable, not so much for its content as for Mike Pinder’s ponderous delivery. The pretentious orchestral interludes are a common enough vice magnified to 11. Even the album covers are usually cheesy (skull/baby)…while also looking kind of cool (skull/baby).

Because when they’re not making you writhe in agony, aren’t the Moodys kind of…good?

YMMV on things like concept albums and hippie visionquest trips, but there are enough solid three- (or, you know, five-) minute rock songs in their ’60s catalog to make a selection a challenge. I settled on this lovely little folk-rocker with its gentle, stringy opening and an irresistible swell into hand-clapping party time. Is it possible this song should rank higher and I let some kind of anti-Moody prejudice keep it from its proper place? Nah, it’s just that so many 1960s songs are so good!

I assume Roger Waters was thinking of the lyric “If only you knew what’s inside of me now / You wouldn’t want to know me somehow” when he came to write “The Final Cut” (“and if I show you my dark side” etc.).

132 Truck Drivin’ Son-of-a-Gun by Dave Dudley (1965)

This song is a good reminder (as “A Boy Named Sue” is not) that grown-up adults used to be able to say things like “son of a gun” unironically.

It’s a “girl-in-every-port” song, like “Travelin’ Man” or “The Wanderer,” although Dave Dudley’s blue-collar bass sounds more plausible as a callous roving heartbreaker than sensitive Dion or sissy Ricky Nelson—which of course means those songs are better than this one. Too on point, Dudley!

Still, if this “kiss-stealin’, wheelin’, dealin’, truck drivin’ son-of-a-gun” can’t compete with those teen heartthrobs, he is operating on a different level for a different audience, and in that way he’s hard to beat, with a boogie-woogie country piano and a sardonic delivery that (gun or no gun) has persuasively been there. Years later the Teamsters made Dudley an honorary member for his devotion to truck driving songs, and you can kind of see it, can’t you?

131 Only Love Can Break a Heart by Gene Pitney (1962)

What am I doing? I don’t even like this kind of music! Who put all those strings in there? This was the music rock ’n’ roll was invented to overthrow!

But Pitney’s voice is always one-of-a-kind, and it’s at its most heartbreaking in this song. I might have chosen a more endurable production like “Liberty Valance,” but Pitney’s performance here beats them all. Maybe it wouldn’t even work as well without all the kitsch blaring behind it. We’re left with what we got, and the song, despite itself, is great.

130 Saginaw, Michigan by Lefty Frizzell (1963)

This tale of class-based resentment and revenge is delivered in such a gentle and appealing way as to sound as unthreatening as your standard toffs vs. yobs story, despite the fact that here the narrator has committed literal fraud and will certainly be in financial if not legal trouble from which no amount if “serves him right” (as the lyrics assert) will extricate him. It’s a working man’s rebellion fantasy, the kind that would blossom in the 1970s with “Convoy” or “One Piece at a Time,” here made strictly personal because it is (as the law requires) a love song. He’s only defrauding his father-in-law! That’s how to impress a lady!

So all of that is fun. The father-in-law freezing in the Klondike is fun. But I think the best part of the song is the euphony of the repeated phrase “Saginaw, Michigan”—the state’s greatest gift to music since “Kalamazoo.”

129 Angel of the Morning by Merrilee Rush & The Turnabouts (1968)

Chip Taylor wrote both this song and “Wild Thing”—that’s the alpha and omega of pop music! That’s all of pop music! Chip Taylor has won pop music, and he won it in 1968!

128 Any Day Now by Chuck Jackson (1962)

I think I spend a lot of time making fun of Bruce Bacharach, but this is a solid tune. Former Del-Viking Chuck Jackson really sells the heartbreak, and the rhythm is pulsing, and that hammond organ is beautiful. “Any day now / When the clock strikes go.” No complaints.

127 Nag by The Halos (1961)

The 1960s as a cultural era has a definite beginning (JFK’s election), but musically it’s rather arbitrary. You can pretend that ’50s music ended with the 1959 payola investigation or the 1959 Beechcraft Bonanza plane crash, but while rock ’n’ roll proper may have waned temporarily, music chugged along into the new decade blissfully (as they say) unaware anything had changed (until The Beatles, 1964, of course).

This is fine, because the late ’50s were a particularly fun and fecund era in music. Take “Nag,” a cross between Eddie Cochran’s “Summertime Blues” and The Coasters’ “Yakety Yak” (by way of “Lost My Job”) complete with basso asides. The result is so juvenile I hadn’t realized until this writeup that the titular nag is a spouse and not a parent. But late ’50s music excelled at being juvenile, and here we are in 1961 still not grown up. Just dig the simple rocking drum kit and the semi-novelty bilabial falsetto gobbling (I don’t how else to describe it)! Who would want to grow up if juvenility rocks this hard?

126 The Night Has a Thousand Eyes by Bobby Vee (1962)

Bobby Vee famously got his musical start filling in at age fifteen for a newly dead Buddy Holly; apparently the surname Velline was too long, challenging, or ethnic for the public at the time. He sang pleasant songs and generally lived as a mid-tier early ’60s performer, the kind that got buried under the weight of the British Invasion.

(Actually, I guess he had an excellent 1967 comeback single, too.)

But there was nothing mid-tier about this particular song, Vee’s main claim for immortality, a song so nervous and carnivally (I can’t really say carnivalesque) right from its early high-speed drumbeat that blooms into a chorus simultaneously brash, paranoid, jovial, and insane.

Plato once wrote, “I wish I were the night, so I could watch you sleep with its thousand eyes,” but Bobby Vee knows his girl’s not sleeping, and yet he sounds just as excited as Plato.

125 Springtime for Hitler from The Producers (1967)

Art should be, in some sense, dangerous, a fact that we have either forgotten or fully embraced by ramping up the danger. People went to court (and lost!) for printing Ulysses in 1921 and Boiled Angel in 1994, but the arc of our courts has generally bent towards increasing laxity: While laxity in the court of public opinion seems to have bell-curved: Songs like “Angel of the Morning” had been controversial at the time; then very briefly everyone mellowed out; then people learned it was more fun to melt down about art. Recent conniption fits over art that may or may not endorse political violence seem weird to anyone who can remember that there were whole genres of punk music explicitly endorsing political violence. Pink Floyd (NOT AN OBSCURE BAND) made an entire concept album and the concept was “wouldn’t it be fun to kill a whole bunch of politicians, starting with Margaret Thatcher?” The only controversy I’ve ever heard expressed about the album was the fear that it might be racist against the Japanese.

The ebb and flow of taboo mean that this song (from a mainstream 1967 movie) is perhaps unlistenable-to today, but it was once and perhaps will be again someday the funniest three minutes in all of ’60s music. “Well! Talk about bad taste!”

124 When I Lay My Burdens Down by The Oak Ridge Quartet (1966)

In another ten years, the Oak Ridge Boys (as the Oak Ridge Quartet was already calling itself sometimes) would be the greatest band in the world (and the mere fact that I believe something so outre is why I am not making a best songs of the 1970s list), but in 1966 they were not there yet. The signature Oak Ridge Boys sound is based on two novelties:

1. They transported Southern gospel harmonies to country music, and

2. Although plenty of bands have had great harmonies, no one has had a bass as rich and deep as Richard Sterbin’s to bottom out the harmony and give it a whole new sound.

But Richard Sterbin hadn’t joined the band yet in the 1960s, and the Oaks were still doing gospel, so instead of striding confidently somewhere into the top five, they’re here. But here is still great, and these guys were an amazing group even if (as in this album) only William Lee Golden from their “classic” lineup is present.

123 Play Those Oldies, Mr DJ by Anthony & The Sophomores (1963)

Don’t worry, Potsie! It’s never too soon to get nostalgic about the 1950s!

Maybe when Little Caesar and the Romans sang “Those Oldies but Goodies” in 1961 they were talking about songs (you know, like “Ramona”) that were actually old (hard to prove), but the oldies that Anthony and the Sophomores crave are quite explicitly tunes like “Tears on My Pillow” by Little Anthony and the Imperials (1958) or “Earth Angel” by the Penguins (1954)—songs, Anthony sings, from “just a couple, couple years ago.” In context, the Mr. Sandman-style call-and-response (“ye-e-e-sss?”) is presumably thematic.

In fact, the song was originally titled “Sing Those Oldies, Mr. Bass Man,” but Johnny Cymbal’s “Mr. Bassman” (a song so good it probably should have made the list, except I messed up; I messed up a lot) came out first, so they made a quick switch to the DJ.

My only complaint: Anthony and The Sophomores perform perfectly competent South Philly doo-wop, and their energy on this song is infectious; but they’re no Penguins, and…

Ah, but no spoilers. What is to come must not be revealed.

122 Pretty Little Angel Eyes by Curtis Lee (1961)

Very early Phil Spector doesn’t quite have his sounds in a wall yet, but he gets some nice effects with that opening percussion and the overall rocking busy near-chaos—even before the breakdown.

Curtis Lee does a fine job, but he’s probably the least important part of the track, behind the production and the background singers (who are, by the way, our friends The Halos from five slots away). Wikipedia summarizes the tragedy of the one-hit wonder succinctly: ”Without Spector’s influence, Lee’s hits dried up. He went into the construction industry with his father in 1969. He died from cancer on January 8, 2015, in Yuma, Arizona, aged 75.” He didn’t even get to do the revival circuit?

121 Having a Party by Sam Cooke (1962)

This was the A side, “Bring It On Home to Me” was the B side, and yet the B side gets all the love. I AM CHANGING THAT TODAY. Not because “Bring It On Home” isn’t great—it’s great—but because “Having a Party” is Sam Cooke’s finest hour.

Is he having such a good time (as he alleges)? This is the crux of the song for me, because no one (this is a paradox) has ever sounded as morose as Sam Cooke while also being in a strange kind of bliss. Othr listeners don’t seem to hear the melancholy in this song, which I can’t believe—this is the most downtempo party anyone’s ever thrown! But he is also having so much fun, and this I cannot deny. For me, the tension in the entire song. To bad it’s a fallen world…but at least I’m dancing with my baby.

Cooke, of course, suffered an ending more tragic than Curtis Lee’s obscurity, shot dead while naked and perhaps enraged in a motel. Johnny Ace has been called rock’s first martyr, and then there was The Day the Music Died, and then the ’60s as they spilled into the ’70s would generate its own martyrologies: I’m not here to pinpoint which death was the most tragic, but let’s have a moment of silence for Sam Cooke, who died (like Alexander the Great, like Cuchulainn, like Christ) Æ 33.
