120 Let’s Make the Water Turn Black by The Mothers of Invention (1968)

Zappa fans are a breed apart, and I can’t usually pick up what they’re laying down. There are a few exceptions, though, such as this irresistible little song about the strange pastimes of two young brothers. Apparently some killjoy (named Frank Zappa!) went ahead and decoded all the mysterious lyrics, but I prefer a little mystery here: In my mind, Kenny and his buddies are literally taking water and adding various disgusting household items to it until, you know, it turns black.

I guess the part about burning poots is pretty unambiguous and clear.

119 Trail to Mexico by Peter LaFarge (1963)

A genuine rodeo rider and folk singer, Peter LaFarge died young but left behind a legacy…perhaps mostly of songs he wrote (many of which, including “Ira Hayes,” his friend Johnny Cash later covered) but also of songs he recorded, like this great bitter cowboy ballad. In the great stoic cowboy tradition, LaFarge sounds equanimous despite his hardships, climaxing in a faithless love.

Well, damn your gold and the bullets too!
God pity a woman who can’t prove true.
I’m heading back where the bullets fly,
And stay on the trail till the day I die.

Maybe I’m bad at reading people’s tones (empirically true), but LaFarge makes the experience sound so light-hearted and pleasant. Sign me up!

118 The Shoop Shoop Song by Betty Everett (1964)

I don’t know how had the idea of naming the song after the background nonsense syllables and not for the chorus hook, but it’s a genius move. The shooping is the best part of an already great song! The whole song is so up-tempo that it kind of rattles my nerves, but it really ratchets up in the…is that the bridge? It happens twice…are there two bridges? Well anyway, Betty Everett may as well be defusing a bomb the way I’m on the edge of my seat. I mean that as a compliment.

The background singers (the uncredited Opals, apparently) are really the co-stars of this record, and half its charm comes from the call-and-response, and Everett’s growing frustration: “You’re not listening to all I say!” Overall, the lyrics are superior to what you’d expect from a hit song of 1964: “That’s just his arms” is, in context, a genuinely funny line.

Note that the album this single derives from is, in fact, titled It’s in His Kiss.

117 We’re a Winner by The Impressions (1967)

Eventually Curtis Mayfield would take R&B down paths I couldn’t follow—too funky; too soulful—but before something goes too far there is generally a place where it goes just far enough, and that’s “We’re a Winner,” a rousing anthem complete with crowd cheers and weird grammar. Are we not winners? But surely this is a political message of unity.

The lyrics are some kind of postmodern game, insisting as they do, that:

We’ll just keep on pushing
Like your leaders tell you to

when of course it was The Impressions who told us, back in 1965, to keep on pushing. Meanwhile, the lyrics say that “we’re moving on up,” and indeed a solo Mayfield would “Move on Up” one year later. Maybe, if I had a more encyclopedic knowledge of the Impressions’ catalog, I’d be able to spin more connections out.

But, honestly, although I’m aware I’m supposed to listen to Impressions songs for the lyrics, they’re not Frank Gallup, so I don’t. I’m sure I’m missing a lot of political subtext (or, you know, text), but great art transcends politics. Great art is just soulful enough.

116 Higher Ground by Sarah & Maybelle Carter (1966)

You know these two, right? They’re cousins (who married brothers) from The Carter Family, that seminal and indispensable country group from the 1920s and ’30s. Everything about country music (except the Nudie Suits) that did not spring autochthonously from the soil of America came from The Carter Family. I can say without exaggeration that Maybelle Carter is if not the greatest guitarist in American music history (she’s probably more like #2 or 3), she is nevertheless the most influential.

Well, that was along time ago, and what are you going to do for us now, Carter Family? By 1966, one third of the original Carters was dead, but the other two thirds are back, baby! They were middle-aged to old, and honestly sounded older than they were, but they still sounded great; and they won’t be the last old-timey musical figure returned to the recording studio by the 1960s’ passion for old folk songs.

115 theme from Spider-man (1967)

I don’t really think of the 1960s as the Golden Age of TV theme songs, although The Andy Griffith Show, Gilligan’s Island, Bewitched, It’s About Time, and Scooby-Doo, Where Are You? all make a good argument I’m wrong. (I didn’t include the theme from The Brady Bunch on that list because the iconic version doesn’t appear until season two in 1970; sorry, Peppermint Trolley Company.) But I couldn’t just clutter this list up with TV theme songs, could I? I mean, It’s About Time is really tempting; yet I held strong.

Except when I caved; that blast of a jazz-horn opening leading right to the excited group chanting (from anonymous or semi-anonymous CDC session singers) of that unforgettable couplet. He does whatever a spider can!

I think this song succeeds because the anonymous or semi-anonymous CDC session singers almost make it sound like a real song, but not quite—it has a little of the Golden Records sound about it still. It’s like a juvenile trembling on the cusp of regular pop.

Note that the Bob Harris credited with co-writing this song is not the same as Bob Harras, alleged “nasty S.O.B.” and erstwhile EIC of Marvel Comics.

114 You Can’t Hurry Love by The Supremes (1966)

I think I undervalue The Supremes because, unlike some other major girl groups of the era (The Crystals, The Ronettes, even the Shirelles) they have no one or two transcendent songs, no single that just blows me out of the water. Nothing truly top notch. But what The Supremes do have is a deep catalog of second-notch songs, and they’re all really good and they all sound different! After the Ronettes burn through their best half-dozen songs, the quality starts to drop, but the Superemes’ repertoire, um, keeps hanging on. (Sorry.)

Anyway, which of their songs to choose is pretty arbitrary, and I kept changing it. Probably I can’t go wrong.

113 Matty Groves by Fairport Convention (1969)

A traditional folk song, sung to the tune of a different traditional folk song, all to tell the tale of a lover not a fighter, and what happens to him when he meets a fighter.

Honestly, this song is so good it would have been higher up were it not for the interminable jam session at the end (a common Fairport Con. weakness). Does it go on for fifteen minutes? No, but it sure as heck feels like it does.

My preferred alternate traditional lyrics for the ending bit:

“A grave! A grave!” Lord Donald cried,
“And a stone to place above.
“But bury my lady at the top
“For the days we were in love.”

If you ever have an opportunity to cover the song, try these lyrics instead.

112 How Glad I Am by Nancy Wilson (1964)

A good example of a song that is 110% only successful because of the performance. Nancy Wilson overcomes the cloying instrumentation and especially annoying percussion to craft an amazing vocal performance, especially on that “you don’t know you don’t know you don’t know you don’t know.”

“How Glad I Am” won a Grammy in the R&B category, which, I will not be the first to point out, is a little strange because the song is more pop than R&B; it did much better on the pop than the R&B charts. But Wilson has always been hard to categorize, and her performance here owes as much to jazz as it does to pop vocalizations. And the single charted during the (brief) time when Billboard threw its hands up and proclaimed that no one could tell R&B from pop any more. Billboard recanted (and Cash Box had never given up its separate charting), but the fact remains that this track, at least, is sui generis. If it was just pop it would suck, but it’s not, and it’s great!

111 Do You Know the Way to San Jose? by Dionne Warwick (1968)

I will admit that as a rule I can’t stand Dionne Warwick, her weak breathy voice, or her songs’ cheesy arrangements. But this side, for reasons I don’t even want to try examining, haunts me. Even the weird organ behind “parking cars and pumping gas” is great! Yeah, this one works.

110 Georgie and the IRT by Dave Van Ronk (1961)

Much like a Weird Al number, “Georgie and the IRT” is funnier if you know the original it is parodying: the grisly train disaster song “Engine 143” ( by the Carter Family (!), although of course in the custom of folk songs the song is older than their recording). “Engine 143” is more explicit than most train disaster songs (“The Wreck of the Old 97,” “Casey Jones”), with lyrics like:

Upside down the engine turned and Georgie’s breast did smash.
His head was against the firebox door, the flames were rolling high.

(That looks like it should be the end, but Georgie survives for two more bloody stanzas)

but it has nothing on “Georgie and the IRT”!

The IRT is Interborough Rapid Transit, one of the companies that used to run NYC’s subways (the “numbered” lines). The distinction between the IRT and BMT (or even IND) lines no longer matters, but you still can hear old-times in NY casually drop the terms. If I were traveling from Times Square to Flatbush Ave. (and Georgie does) I’d take the B or the Q, which are not IRT lines, but presumably things were different sixty-odd years ago.

Anyway, this song is about the day Georgie gets decapitated (alternately: bisected) by the train doors, his body parts traveling different routes. It’s played for laughs. In “143” Georgie’s last words are: “Nearer my God to thee,” but in “IRT” they’re: “Screw the IRT.”

His body, it lies between the ties, amidst the dust and dew,
And his head it rides the IRT to Flatbush Avenue.

Dave Van Ronk, “The Mayor of MacDougal Street” (as per the title of his posthumous memoir), was a long-time Village folkie who seems to have always been Forrest-Gumpliy stumbling through the paradigmatic life of a folk singer. He arranged songs for Bob Dylan (who was sleeping on his couch). He was almost one-third of Peter, Paul, and Mary (but Albert Grossman decided he was too unkempt, Red, unmarketable, or all of the above). He got arrested in the Stonewall Riots (he has just been wandering by and joined in). He (supposedly) inspired the character Llewyn Davis. Perhaps he never got the fame he deserved, but many Trotskyites, including Trotsky, had worse lives.

This song is really a duet with Dick Rosmini, who also didn’t get the fame he deserved; I barely even credited him!

109 Little Black Egg by The Nightcrawlers (1965)

Of all the inexplicable ’60s lyrics, the little black egg with the little white specks, desideratum of this song, is perhaps the least explicable. Supposedly written for an Easter concert, which might explain the egg part…nope, still doesn’t track. It almost sounds like a children’s song (with that lovely hypnotic guitar), but it’s also a little ominous. Why does everyone want to take his egg?

A minor sleeper hit in its own era (it charted in 1967, two years after release), it slept even longer before inclusion in the Nuggets compilation, which is where I, and presumably many others, first heard it. Another great find, so, um, thanks, Nuggets!

108 The Fairest of the Seasons by Nico (1967)

Surely Nico has the weirdest beautiful voice in music, accented and low, low like someone on the phone pretending to be the president. The high point of her career was surely her one album with the Velvet Underground (specifically, and this is very specific, the way she sings the word clown on “Femme Fatale”), but her solo album Chelsea Girls would be an enviable high point of most careers. Famously, Nico hated it, hating all the background strings (and especially the flute!). She wanted to rock!

Some of Nico’s later work, presumably closer to her artistic intent, can be, if not hard to listen to at least strange to listen to. I feel like I’m betraying something deep within me when I say I prefer the overproduced 1967 stuff.

An album full of great songs, but my favorite is this melancholy (is Nico ever not melancholy?) number.

107 Black Nights by Lowell Fulson (1966)

Lowell Fulson fans probably either think of his ’50s material or his funky 1967 hit “Tramp,” but for my money his finest hour is this comeback song, a melancholy as the blues should be. The way he hits that “it used to beeee” is heartbreaking.

106 This Train by Big Bill Broonzy (1962)

Does this even count? I don’t mean Big Bill Broonzy in general, a towering figure in blues, folk, and country for decades. I mean, can this be one of the best songs of the 1960s when Broonzy died in 1958?

But “not long before his death” (as the liner notes say), Moses Asch recorded a mortally ill Broonzy, and those recordings were not released (AFAIK) until 1962. Is that not a ’60s record?

The album that session was finally released on (which also contains unrelated live tracks) was the Folkways issue Big Bill Broonzy Sings Folk Songs, which is a strange title because it makes it sound like Broonzy is leaving his comfort zone, you know, like The Country Side of Esther, when Broonzy has sung plenty of folk songs in his life, before and after his bluesiest period.

Broonzy was quite literally dying when this song was recorded, his face (the liner notes again) a rictus of pain as he sang. It’s a song (like many a train song) about the inevitable trajectory of life towards death, and Broonzy gives it his most poignant performances.

105 Palisades Park by Freddy Cannon (1962)

We had a couple heavy ones in a row, but the neat thing about music is that a song doesn’t have to be serious or even particularly intelligent to be great. Cue Freddy Cannon, who went to a theme park and met a girl.

Maybe you think it’s unfair of me: Fulson sings about the end of love and Cannon sings about the beginning of love, and I immediately characterize the latter as juvenile. But what could be more juvenile that “Palisades Park”? If your heart goes up like a rocket ship it must go down like a roller coaster (etc., I assure you)! This song, Cannon’s last top ten hit, is great because, like early rock ’n’ roll, it captures both lyrically and in the raucous music, a uniquely teenage experience.

The Beach Boys covered “Palisades Park” in the mid ’70s, not altogether successfully; they got closer to the joyous spirit of Freddy Cannon in their 1965 recording “Amusement Parks U.S.A.”; but closer doesn’t match it. Not even Brian Wilson’s manic laughter can touch Cannon’s anarchic glee.

104 Madame George by Van Morrison (1968)

Joining Sister Ray and Brother Mary in the triumvirate of ambiguously gendered oldies characters comes Madame George, in a long, rambling, and exceptionally Dylanesque exercise in surreality. Van Morrison, the quintessential garage rocker and quintessential blue-eyed soul singer must have surprised people with this gentle, nostalgic, hard-to-understand portrait of…something? Beautiful; it does not wear out its welcome over its nearly ten-minutes’ length.

I assume “the love that loves to love” is a Ulysses reference.

103 Got a Woman Cross Town by Pink Anderson (1961)

Pink Anderson, unwitting eponym for the band Pink Floyd, had a long career as an entertainer, but almost all of it is lost to time—hardly a unique situation, as most vaudeville performances, and up until the late twentieth century even most live performances, went unrecorded. Anderson’s habitual venue was unusual, though: He toured with various patent medicine peddlers. Occasionally recorded by blues-hungry folklorists, he was filmed in 1962 by a documentarian singing an album’s worth of material, which was only released some ten years’ after his death (and long after the 1960s; a sort of reverse Bill Broonzy situation).

Either a loose adaptation of the Ray Charles number or a song built on similar traditional tags, “Got a Woman Cross Town” is one of those songs that sounds a thousand years old, which in mid-twentieth-century terms just means it sounds forty years old.

102 Public Execution by Mouse and the Traps (1965)

Did I say “Madame George” was Dylanesque? “Public Execution” is practically fraud!

Still, even fake Dylan is good. Another Nuggets find.

101 North to Alaska by Johnny Horton (1960)

Johnny Horton’s “Battle of New Orleans” has to be the most educational pop song, in the sense that no regular joe would remember when the Battle of New Orleans took place or between whom without it; or maybe I should say it’s the best educational pop song; but it’s 1959, and not going on this list. We’re here for Horton’s follow-up hit, the beautiful chugalong “North to Alaska.” Apparently the theme song to a movie of the same name; the single dropped before the movie did.

Johnny Horton would die (car accident; Æ 35) during the brief interval between the release of the single and the release of the movie.
