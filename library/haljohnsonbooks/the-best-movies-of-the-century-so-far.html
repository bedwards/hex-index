People keep making these lists and they’re all foolish and embarrassing blunders, so I thought I’d make my own blunder, as foolish and embarrassing as it may be. I let the year 2000 count as this century (because I don't believe in math), and I was open to letting 2025 count as well, but no current movie, not even the ones you like so much, cracked the top 25, which is where I stopped.

I limited things to one entry per director, for reasons unclear to me. This post is of course too long for an email, so click through to find out what is number one (“…will surprise you”).

As always: FIGHT ME!

25 Boyhood (2014)

I love a good gimmick, and spending a decade slowly making a film (you know, like they did with Stranger Things ha ha) is even better than a Tingler. Epic in the way that’s hardest to fake.

24 Another Year (2010)

Perhaps not the best Mike Leigh film, but his best of this century, a quiet, pleasant, melancholy year in the life of a couple and their friends. Compare this movie to the frenetic, unpleasant, implausible Four Seasons mini-series. Back in 2010 people could make movies about a worthwhile life that wasn’t overstocked with signifiers of wealth.

23 Samaritan Girl (2004)

Like many a Kim Ki-duk film, this is partially a horror story, partially a portrait of strange psychology, and partially…just weird. But unlike in most movies—not most KKD movies, I mean, but in most movies—the characters’ insane actions gel into something more than just the arbitrary requirements of the the three-act structure.

22 Primer (2004)

I’m not sure most movies should require a viewer to take so many notes, but maybe it’s good at least one does. Time travel without the nonsense.

21 Apocalypto (2006)

There’s a story beat I love, when the barbarian from the hinterlands enters Rome (or fantasy-Rome-analog) and looks around with that just where on Earth am I? only it doesn’t quite work because for viewers few things are less alien than Rome (or fantasy-Rome-analog). Well, Apocalypto squares that circle because the city our barbarians come to is nothing like Rome or (for most of us) like anything previously encountered.

Everyone got mad at this movie when it came out because Mel Gibson is a bad guy (although perhaps not the worst guy to direct a film on this list) and because our particular fantasies about particular times and places were not being consulted. I don’t care about any of that, and what I like about this movie is that it shows a persuasive (not necc. realistic, but movies are not supposed to be realistic; they must always be persuasive) stone age people as, you know, people, as opposed to positive or negative cliches, repositories for our fears or desires. The opening is fascinating, and better than any depiction of such a people I have ever seen. And then the coming to the city is even better! Of course, the movie descends after that to being a perfectly serviceable action flick, which is too bad, but it pulls up in the end with a truly apocalyptic closing shot. And note that the shot, like the whole movie, is still from the POV of Jaguar Paw and his people. You thought you’d seen it all, guys? You thought you’d lived through apocalyptic trouble? The worst is yet to come. It’s a terrifying ending, and a rare use of dramatic irony that doesn’t feel pandering and cheap. I’ve said too much.

20 Princess Raccoon (2005)

I once read an interview with Seijun Suzuki where he said, concerning his later films, something along the lines of: “I’m eighty years old; I’ll do what I want.” Well, his stagy, crazed Pistol Opera was Suzuk-y enough, but not even that could match the strange, beautiful, surreal quality of this tanuki fairy tale.

19 Shaun the Sheep Movie (2015)

Everything an animated feature should be: kinetic, kid-pleasing, and, measured in pure laugh units, arguably the funniest movie of the twenty-first century. The Aardman studio has put out some great films, but this is the jewel in their crown.

18 Capturing the Friedmans (2004)

Just a documentary about a family either implicated in or (more likely) witch-hunt railroaded into a child-abuse plot, only with clowns. The tragedy is that some of the best parts are relegated to the generous DVD extras, such as the part where (we are told) Elaine Friedman watches herself speaking on screen and insists, “I never said that.” I guess canting critics say it’s a one-sided portrait, but what documentary is not, and, come on—a middle aged man has only so many orgasms he can conjure in a day and he’s going to waste one (as prosecution witnesses insist) on ookie on the cookie? Wholly implausible.

17 The Prestige (2006)

People gripe at this movie for discarding an admirable vaudeville narrative in favor of a daffy science fiction narrative, but pulp has got to pulp, and the only way to succeed is to dare more excess (this is right there in the movie, folks!) than any one would believe. The ridiculous Tesla appendix is the stuff of nightmares.

16 The Man Who Wasn’t There (2001)

Like several Pynchon novels one could name, TMWWT is both a loving homage to pulpy noir and a portrait of a time and place, in this case postwar (aka midcentury) America. “Your honor, I plead Modernism” (not an actual quote).

15 The Act of Killing (2012)

An insane documentary idea, getting mass-murderers to reenact their murders, and in almost any other context an unforgivably cynical marketing ploy; but somehow The Act of Killing comes across a humanist film. The mass-murderers here are, of course, often officially on the side of right and justice, and by invoking the word “murderer” I am asserting power over both them and to some extent the Indonesian government; I’m also talking out of my hat, as everything I know about Indonesian history I learned from this film—but this is the power of the film to persuade you, the viewer, that you have learned something about both Indonesian anti-communism and humanity.

Also, the reenactments get really (of course in a good way) weird.

14 Christine (2016)

I sometimes praise actors to make myself sound like a sympathetic soul and not a the monstrously inhuman critic that I really am, but the truth is I barely understand acting or any artistic endeavor that requires looking at another human, especially in the face. The exception is Rebecca Hall in her portrayal of infamous newscaster Christine Chubbuck, as she manages to look uncomfortable (as they say) in her own skin for every moment of this movie’s two hours. It’s painful to watch, which, in a biopic of Christine Chubbuck, is of course thematically intentional.

Naming your film after a much more famous one, especially when yours is ambitious and serious, while the other is about a homicidal car—yeah, that’s a gutsy move.

13 Mother! (2017)

Simultaneously a retelling of the Bible, an ecological fable, a portrait of a troubled relationship, and a Kafkaesque nightmare, Mother! (I’m supposed to decapitalize the M, but to heck with that) is as busy and excessive as its exclamation point. That could be a problem if there was only a little excess, but there’s plenty of excess, and nothing exceeds like excess. Has any movie been this self-serious and yet this playful since Buñuel’s catalog?

The scene where partygoers break a sink is as tooth-gnashingly frustrating to watch as anything filmed this century.

12 Match Point (2005)

I think I’m supposed to like Crimes and Misdemeanors (to which this film is some kind of homage) better, but give me the thirsty class advancement and pure noir quicksand of Match Point any day. The film is just as slick as it needs to be, and that exceedingly gauche tennis-ball metaphor of the opening pays off so well. You can psychoanalyze why Woody Allen would be the master of the overcoming-guilt narrative, but I’m just here for the movie!

11 Barb and Star Go to Vista Del Mar (2021)

Kristen Wiig’s accent is sufficiently hilarious to get this film at least a nomination for this list. A buddy comedy in the tradition of Wayne’s World or Romy and Michele, both movies that famously go off the rails, but Barb and Star goes so much further off the rails, and then further still. I assume this is a cult classic now, or will be soon.

10 Get Out (2017)

A great B-movie premise (like The Atomic Brain’s) somehow given emotional and moral weight without getting flattened. It helps that the screenplay (unlike, you know, most) feels thought out, and that the movie believes in its world. Hitchcock called the inconsistencies in his movies icebox scenes, but surely the better icebox moment is when you realize, hours after finishing a film, that actually the story made more sense than you anticipated. “So that’s why she fought the cop about checking ID; so there’d be no record he was visiting! It all makes sense!”

Also: Ever sense I saw that UBC sketch I watch for titular lines in movies, and Get Out has one of the best.

9 Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon (2000)

In a way a harkening back to the old adventure films of the distant past, like Gunga Din or a Fritz Lang silent, but also of course a love letter to the very different tradition of wuxia—CTHD has enough melodrama for three films and enough action for twelve. The wirework is breathtaking, but the miracle is that this is also a character piece, with great actors—Michelle Yeoh is the standout, but hardly alone here.

Best of all, and unlike all but a very few fantasy films, CTHD takes place (or of course seems to take place) in a world with a rich history that is hinted at but rarely explained. This is doubtless due to its being adapted from a much longer serialized story (which I’ve never read, so I cannot complain, as I can with Jackson’s LOTR films, when something gets screwed up).

8 Damsels in Distress (2011)

Whit Stillman’s most challenging and philosophical movie. I’ve ranted elsewhere at some length on the film’s meaning, so let me just say here that few movies are more fun; that this is Greata Gerwig’s finest role; and it’s insane to me that it did not make Carrie MacLemore, the funniest player in the film, into a star.

7 Grand Budapest Hotel (2014)

The only thing Wes Anderson has been missing all these years is gravitas and this elegy for a departed world of prewar elegance has it in spades. I once claimed that Edith Wharton’s Age of Innocence was about not the gilded age but rather the innocent time before the Great War, and this movie, too, feels like it harks back to the First World War while reveling in its connection to the Second. Love those frame narratives, as balanced and nesting as an Anderson shot composition.

6 Parasite (2019)

One of the best tone-shifts in film is this nutty grifter comedy that blossoms into something more serious on two different levels. It helps that the grifter comedy was a really good grifter comedy!

5 There Will Be Blood (2007)

I undervalued There Will Be Blood for a while, but I had a reason. On my first viewing, after the first ten minutes or so, I thought, “This is the greatest movie ever made.” Then, after the first hour, I thought, “Okay, it couldn’t maintain that tension; but it’s still the greatest movie of the last ten years.” But of course it couldn’t maintain that tension either, so by the end I was reduced to considering TWBB one of the top three movies of the year.

But that was all contrast! It was like jumping from the hot tub to the pool—of course the pool feels cold! When I was ready for the downward arc in quality, the contrast didn’t seem as great, and, sure, it’s a drop, but it drops from an empyreal height!

4 Inglourious Basterds (2009)

In the same way that Ian McEwan cheapened the “twist” of Atonement by repeating it in Sweet Tooth, Tarantino may have compromised the success of Inglourious Basterds by reusing its own twist in a perfectly serviceable but lesser film. But nothing can wash away the rush of watching Basterds when it first came out—“How are they going to get out of this one?” you asked, before the incredible catharsis of realizing they weren’t going to. Christoph Waltz is the man you love to hate, even in a movie full of men you love to hate.

A rocketship ride from the incredibly tense opening scene to the “masterpiece” ending. “I’ve been chewed out before,” is the Tarantino line I quote the most.

3 The American Astronaut (2001)

A rock-and-roll space western musical with a budget so low the spaceships are just chalk drawings may not sound like the most auspicious set-up for a movie…or it sounds like the most auspicious set-up possible. The soundtrack (by The Billy Nayer Show) rocks, the jokes are solid, the low-fi vibe is utterly charming, and (whether this sounds auspicious or in- will depend on your own esthetics) the whole thing is the slowest, trippiest, most pornographically (?) lingeringly beautiful movie since 2001.

2 Up (2009)

In the 1973 Japanese revenge fantasy Lady Snowblood, Yuki [SPOILER!] comes face to face with the man she has sworn to kill, but the man grabs Yuki’s love interest (to whom she owes her life) and used him as a human shield. What are you going to do now, Yuki?

That man Yuki has sworn to kill? Yuki stabs him right through the love interest. She stabs them both! Yuki has a mission, and nothing is going to distract her from her mission.

I mention this because of all the things there are to love about Up—the melodramatic opening; the post-pulp esthetic; the peak-Pixar humor—there is also this: Carl has a mission at Paradise Falls, and although he is tempted away from his mission to help save a child from mortal peril he does nothing until he achieves his Paradise Falls goal. This is one of the most hard-core moments in all of American cinema.

Sure, sure. The rest of the movie is boss, too.

1 2046 (2004)

A sequel to more than one Wong Kar-wai film, 2046 is a busy shaggy-dog portrait of a man in despair. It’s also at times a science-fiction epic that mixes kitschy retro-future costumes with execrable CGI. None of this should work, and if things had been a little different it wouldn’t have, and the whole house of cards would have come tumbling down. But it works, and 2046 is the best film of the last twenty-five years.

Based on my experiences watching WKW’s My Blueberry Nights, a lot of this melancholy would come across as insufferably ponderous if intoned in English. Obviously I have no idea how it sounds in any of the several languages it was recorded in…but the slight distance provided by reading this stuff makes it sound beautiful.

The cast is wonderful throughout, but the real hero is Tony Leung, who manages to take a character who is dull and odious on paper and make him fascinating to watch. I don’t want to sound crazy, but the real hero here is the set design of the Oriental Hotel, especially that uncanny shade of green. Would I have even liked the movie if it hadn’t been for that green? Probably, but not as much.
