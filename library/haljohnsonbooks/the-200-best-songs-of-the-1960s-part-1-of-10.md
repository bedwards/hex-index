**(Part [two](https://haljohnsonbooks.substack.com/p/the-200-best-songs-of-the-1960s-part-35a), [three](https://haljohnsonbooks.substack.com/p/the-200-best-songs-of-the-1960s-part-93d))**

My basic attitude towards the ’60s is more or less like my basic attitude to the early years of Modernism: It’s mostly bad and dumb, but it must have been exciting to be young then. But then there’s the music…

Not [Modernist music](https://youtu.be/3KtRsTR0qyE); I mean the music of the 1960s. When has music ever been as exciting at the 1960s? When has music been as consistently good across such a wide spectrum of genres? When has music *changed* so much in such a short time? Maybe you could pitch the 1920s as an answer for two of those questions, but not all three. Everyone knows Sturgeon’s Law is “ninety percent of everything is crap”; but in the ’60s about 75% of music was at least pretty good.[1](#footnote-1) It’s not quite an inverse Sturgeon’s Law, but it’s close.

So of course I thought I’d ruin everything by quantifying it. I mean by *pretending to quantify it*. I have ranked the 200 best songs of the 1960s, and I will stand by my rankings with religious fervor, but I have scarcely assigned them scores in different categories and then run them through some database compiling…engine? I don’t know words for things, and I have certainly misused every musical term I bring up in the following piece, including easy ones like “country and western” or “drums.” Anyway, I just put the songs in the order of how much I liked them.

I decided to limit spots to one song per artist after I saw how many Jan & Dean tracks were on the list. “No one is going to respect me if they see all this Jan & Dean,” I said silently to myself. So there’s only one Jan & Dean pick. I tried to be accommodating with people who were in more than one band, or who fielded solo careers, largely because I didn’t want to have to pay attention to who was in The Drifters when. If you see someone pop up more than once, don’t shoot me. It'll happen, and don’t shoot me.

In general, you should not be angry that I have snubbed your favorites. First of all, I hate most music, so statistically speaking I probably hate whatever you like, even in an inverse-Sturgeon era like the 1960s. Second of all, I’m an idiot. Third of all, there are plenty of good songs and artists sitting in #201–300, and I just didn’t get around to them. And, you know, last: I cleverly arranged the list in reverse order and then doled it out piecemeal, so you can keep pretending your missing favorites are higher up, towards number one, and by the time you learn the truth, weeks or months from now, we’ll possibly all be dead.

Feel free to provide threats, recriminations, broken links, and predictions.

## 200 [I’ll Be Doggone](https://youtu.be/eDqxyR4jpBc) by Marvin Gaye (1965)

From the prolific pen of Smokey Robinson comes my favorite Marvin Gaye moment of the ’60s, and Gaye’s first #1 R&B hit. Supposedly, Robinson wrote this the day after “Tracks of My Tears”—but he must have been churning out six hits a month, so it’s probably not so remarkable. Anyway, Marvin Gaye is excellent, and I know people are going to be mad about the fact that he clocks in at #200, but *that’s how good the 1960s were*.

Also, the combination of raisins and that interminable CCR jam have ruined “Grapevine” for me, so that song wasn’t about to chart. It had to be this one.

## 199 [For What It’s Worth](https://youtu.be/80_39eAx3z8) by Buffalo Springfield (1967)

Sure, sure, this song is good, but it’s up on the list at #199 because of its guitar solo, the greatest guitar solo of all time. The solo starts at 2:34, and you’ll notice it’s only two seconds long. Why didn’t more people follow this lead?!?

## 198 [Alley-Oop](https://youtu.be/vcNSGFeUIV0&t=40) by The Hollywood Argyles (1960)

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qp5w!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fde58ea15-145b-4241-8226-57eb04aea120_1340x990.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qp5w!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fde58ea15-145b-4241-8226-57eb04aea120_1340x990.png)

I’ve never heard the term “caveman rock” bandied about \[ETA: Actually [George Starostin just used it to describe The Pretty Things](https://onlysolitaire.substack.com/p/review-the-pretty-things-get-the)\], but if I ever hear it I’ll know what it means: “Louie Louie” or “Wooly Bully” or something like that. Wrong, idiot! I am wrong! It’s “Alley-Oop”! He’s a literal caveman!

“Alley-Oop” revels in its own stupidity, mixing beatnik slang with just made-up prehistoric (?) neologisms: “A mean motor scooter and a bad go-getter.” The whole thing grinds along at a pace that seems to indicate moving faster would tax The Hollywood Argyles’ neanderthal brains. Also: It’s a paean to a comic strip. In other words, this is the most punk song ever recorded, or it was in 1960. Better examples may follow over the course of the decade.

## 197 [Silver Dagger](https://youtu.be/x3ge9vDZfTM) by Joan Baez (1960)

Certainly not the best rendition of this folk song (that would be Dolly Parton’s [1999 cut](https://youtu.be/-nEOjMI4p78), of course), and perhaps not even Joan Baez’s best song (take your pick from the early ’70s), but this one’s great, with lovely fast picking and a vocal that switches between beautiful and chatty. And in case you’re wondering why Dolly sings “all men are *fools*,” which makes no sense in context—Baez sings “all men are *false*,” and what’s oral tradition without misheard lyrics?

## 196 [Homecoming](https://youtu.be/blO8u7wuyWQ) by Tom T. Hall (1969)

Tom T. Hall is known for telling stories in his songs; I mean, that’s his *job*; of course he’s good at it. I think he got even better in the following few years, but 1969 had several sweet tales, including this one about an alienated musician and his brief trip to see his family after his mother’s passing. The entire song is a monolog, but like a Bob Newhart telephone gag, with responses to inaudible questions. And the whole thing builds up to a quiet moment at the end: The monolog, we learn, has been a delaying tactic as the narrator works his nerve up to say the thing he came here to say.

Maybe this song lacks the catharsis of “Turn It On, Turn It On, Turn It On” or the whimsy of “The Monkey Who Became President,” but it’s a heartbreaker on its own. “And by the way, if you see Barbara Walker, tell her that I said hello.”

## 195 [Yonder Come the Blues](https://youtu.be/9NML2nl1_MU) by Odetta (1962)

[

![Odetta - Odetta And The Blues - Amazon.com Music](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!IAG2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8336f6ae-11fa-4d27-84ee-7a8024a6953c_226x223.jpeg "Odetta - Odetta And The Blues - Amazon.com Music")



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!IAG2!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8336f6ae-11fa-4d27-84ee-7a8024a6953c_226x223.jpeg)

We think of Odetta as a folk singer, and indeed she brought her acoustic guitar to the 1963 March on Washington, but (like Cass Elliot after her), her voice was too vast for conventional *folk*, and it must have seemed natural for her to branch out: This track has her singing blues with a dixieland jazz band, and I’ll take it over her arguably more famous “If I Had a Hammer” or “There’s a Hole in the Bucket.”

For extra trivia: Odetta is the only person on this list to make a cameo in *[The Education of Robert Nifkin](https://haljohnsonbooks.substack.com/i/163393688/the-education-of-robert-nifkin-ya-novel)*.

## 194 [Simple Song of Freedom](https://youtu.be/sbRWiWcPnMQ) by Tim Hardin (1969)

One of the glories of the ’60s is that even in what we may think of as a marginalized style—folk music—so many varieties of folk music could flourish. *Cf.* this folksy cover of a Bobby Darin song to the tracks by Joan Baez or Odetta above or The Rooftop Singers or Doc Watson \[etc.\] below. Hardin jazzes it up (compared to Darin’s [even folksier version](https://youtu.be/ytsYjMPyGm8); Hardin’s was released first) and gets some L. Cohen-y backup singers, but it’s his own sleepy, druggy vocals that sell the piece.

Too bad he drops Darin’s Solzhenitsyn reference. Pop music needs more Solzhenitsyn in it.

## 193 [A Losing Battle](https://youtu.be/YRL26PpxlwQ) by Johnny Adams (1962)

Church choirist Johnny Adams was overheard singing (says the NYT obituary) in the bathtub by a neighbor, and that neighbor just happened to be the woman who cowrote “Tutti Frutti” with Little Richard. I guess she had contacts. So thin walls and Dorothy LaBostrie got Adams to record some R&B sides, and he kept performing and recording until the very end (hence the NYT obituary). “Losing Battle” was his only real hit in the ’60s, and here Adams mixes his trademark smooth vocals and occasional falsetto with sardonic lyrics for a real winner.

## 192 [Road Runner](https://youtu.be/ap8JtQZG73M) by Bo Diddley (1960)

[

![Bo Diddley | Songs, Guitar, & Rock and ...](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aTUA!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F378ab6b6-3e36-4f0f-b65a-e615d12b2847_212x238.jpeg "Bo Diddley | Songs, Guitar, & Rock and ...")



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aTUA!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F378ab6b6-3e36-4f0f-b65a-e615d12b2847_212x238.jpeg)

Bo Diddley is one of those 1950s legends whose star had dimmed by the ’60s. If he didn’t enter the decade as gracefully as Elvis or Chuck Berry, well…he was doing better than Buddy Holly, wasn’t he?

My favorite of Diddley’s ’60s output is this casually strolling blues rock piece. The contrast between the novelty “beep beep” background and Bo Diddley’s bragging talk—somewhat reminiscent of 1958 hit “[Say Man](https://youtu.be/45ZdKCFFR3I),” but less, you know, hostile, while still being pretty hostile—is irresistible.

## 191 [The Pusher](https://youtu.be/3XqyGoE2Q4Y) by Steppenwolf (1968)

Second-generation (his mother wrote “Heartbreak Hotel”) songwriter Hoyt Axton penned “The Pusher” back in 1963 and played it in his live gigs; some of the musicians-who-later-became-Steppenwolf heard him and released their version five years later (which is seventy years in 1960s-music time). Axton eventually made [his own recording](https://youtu.be/3xInEkw9KwQ), but, and I hate to say this…Steppenwolf’s is better, because they really damn that pusher. I mean, that is some serious damning. They hit it hard. That damning is the whole song to me. God *damn*.

Axton was dead broke when the song appeared in *Easy Rider*, and from that point on he was no longer broke.

## 190 [Bad Boy](https://youtu.be/JegLBhcRj7o) by The Donays (1962)

In a screenplay-ready moment, a quartet of high school friends from the suburbs of Detroit put out a lone single—and the Beatles [cover the B-side](https://youtu.be/IGe-jfFrxCk)! And after that…nothing happens. The Donays never recorded a third song. This girl group disappeared into the vacuum of history. “We’ll always have the B-side.”

Even better than that B-side, though, as custom dictates, is this A-side, the off-brand–Motown “Bad Boy,” with its “cheat cheat” background vocals. “Popcorn” Wylie (freshly off the Motown label after a feud with Berry Gordy) produced, which may explain the familiar sound; regardless, this is a single that deserved more than it received (i.e. nada).

## 189 [Just a Little](https://youtu.be/dYneeXVOAyA) by The Beau Brummels (1965)

Great little early folk rocker from a band probably more famous now for being included in the *Nuggets* box set. According to wikipedia, the “folk” part of this folk rock was accidental: The band wasn’t skilled enough to keep their songs from sounding the same, so in desperation they switched to acoustic instruments. “Mighty events turn on a straw; the crossing of a brook decides the conquest of the world” (Carlyle).

## 188 [Son-of-a Preacher Man](https://youtu.be/oAZLgsDRUv4) by Dusty Springfield (1968)

Aretha Franklin would release [her own version](https://youtu.be/PHWy0baK-NE) two years later, but this time, and only this time, Dusty Springfield gets the palm. It’s like when Shelley and Keats each wrote a poem about Egypt, and Keats produced “[To the Nile](https://www.netpoets.com/classic/poem/037039),” and Shelley produced “[Ozymandias](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/46565/ozymandias),” and posterity has ever after been “Shelley beat Keats how???” It’s like that.

## 187 [Indian Giver](https://youtu.be/DnhUULkU-xo) by The 1910 Fruitgum Co. (1969)

The 1910 Fruitgum Co. is the perfect name for a late-sixties pop band, and even more perfect is this driving bubblegum piece with it louche Indian theme. The central metaphor (a girl who took back her heart) is backed with a tom-tom beat. The whole thing is irredeemably offensive, all the way to the faux Native American costumes on the album cover, but it’s still 1910FCo.’s best single, and one of the catchiest of the decade.

Just to make everything weirder, the [B-side of the single](https://youtu.be/8zX5fFh4ohw) is merely a different 1910FCo. song (about [Howdy Doody](https://youtu.be/1mWUGvpi0lE) of all things, and itself a bonkers track) *played backwards*.

## 186 [Mama Don’t Allow](https://youtu.be/UL5BgPj_oe4) by The Rooftop Singers (1963)

Like some kind of bizarro-world Peter, Paul, and Mary: I give you Erik, Bill, and Lynne, the Rooftop Singers. Their version of this classic may not be the best ever recorded—that would be [this one](https://youtu.be/Yo-gLC6C_Yk&t=273)—but it does capture perfectly the combination of anarchy and harmlessness inherent in a song that models guitar-playing as the apotheosis of rebellion.

## 185 [Leader of the Pack](https://youtu.be/t5vFOpVGjVc) by The Shangri-Las (1964)

Absolute peak ’60s tragedy porn. The precise peak comes when the Leader’s girlfriend staccato-shouts: “Look out! Look out! Look out! Look out!”

## 184 [The Train That Carried My Girl from Town](https://youtu.be/29MgHwBSmWU) by Doc & Merle Watson (1967)

Doc Watson had recorded this old folk song before (for example in 1963), but this version (with his son Merle, just a teenager at the time) moves the fastest. The faster the picking, the faster the train, I suppose.

The ’60s folk revival reinvigorated the careers of a lot of half-forgotten old masters and launched the careers of some newbies (plenty of whom will appear in later installments), but Doc Watson is an unusual case: Not quite “authentic” (despite being authentically blind; go figure) Watson decided one day to un-Dylan himself, dropping his electric guitar and playing, you know, the authentic instruments with genuine authenticity. And here we are.

## 183 [Now That the Buffalo’s Gone](https://youtu.be/nnZVIluWuq4) by Buffy Sainte-Marie (1964)

Buffy Sainte-Marie sounds like a cross between Joni Mitchell and Johnny Rotten, just the absolute angriest person imaginable expressing herself sweetly and with maximum vibrato. The effect has always been kitsch, as kitschy as “Leader of the Pack,” but perhaps it was the kind of kitsch we’re supposed to feel bad about enjoying because it was, after all, about serious and important matters.

Except, of course, it turns out that it was all fake. Not the centuries of injustice part, but the part where Buffy Sainte-Marie pretended to be an Indian. Imagine singing:

> Can you remember the times  
> That you have held your head high  
> And told all your friends of your Indian claim,  
> How your great-great-grandfather from Indian blood sprang…

and the whole time you’re just no more an Indian than the 1910 Fruitgum Co.! This is amazing!

It wouldn’t work if the song wasn’t so good, but Sainte-Marie is a great songwriter (check out [this cover](https://youtu.be/N_mGdSKIubM) of her 1972 side “Moonshot”). This is good kitsch, and good kitsch should be fake and Buffy Sainte-Marie is fake and now the world makes sense (except for the injustice part).

## 182 [I Enjoy Being a Boy](https://youtu.be/wGpWExEVANY) by The Banana Splits (1969)

The early years of the record biz saw a host of fake bands, I mean bands (orchestras, we used to call them sometimes) recording side after side and changing their name each time. Who would know? If you don’t want to buy thirteen different 78s by one band, we can make believe you’re buying thirteen different 78s by thirteen different bands, and everyone’s happy.

Factors such as the cult of celebrity and appearances in film (and later television) killed the idea of musicians being faceless widgets that could be replaced at a whim—and of course record companies were furious! Possible solutions included just rewidgetifying your bands (famously no members of the Crystals appeared on their [sole #1 hit](https://youtu.be/VlEOTKwoO0E)), inventing [fake bands](https://youtu.be/QaG2Acg8n60) from whole cloth, or reducing bands to animated cartoons. Everyone thinks of The Archies in this regard, because they had the #1 hit, but perhaps more tantalizing were The Beagles, because the songs from the cartoons could be played live by actors cavorting in dog costumes. Get several dog costumes, and The Beagles could play multiple concerts on different coasts simultaneously (you know, like mall Santas). Everyone wins!

Well, The Banana Splits were not even cartoons, although they *introduced* cartoons on TV. They were three guys in animal costumes, and the various actors in various copies of their animal costumes could tour the country. Another difference: The Archies were bubblegum pop and The Beagles were (as the name implies) British Invasion pop, but the Banana Splits actually rocked! Their album is surprisingly *loud*.

Perhaps it is no surprise that they were better when they were not rocking, though. The Banana Splits’ infectious ([tra la la, la la la la](https://youtu.be/KRnmMk7tQZI)) theme is great, but even better were some other very poppy sides, like “[Long Live Love](https://youtu.be/f5UPrACWMXE)” and especially this one, my choice for #182 on our list, “I Enjoy Being a Boy.” By 1969, the ’60s were almost unparodyable, but these opening lyrics (“I live in a cucumber castle on the banks of a cranberry sea”) give it a good shot; they would be unendurable if they were not being sung by literal animals. Sung by literal animals, the song becomes sublime. TMBG did [a cover](https://youtu.be/M-xsivOz0Fg), BTW.

## 181 [Hava Nagila](https://youtu.be/iFelEcx7RaM) by Dick Dale & the Deltones (1963)

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!q-mN!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fde5ad157-c1c4-4d31-855e-6fddd9a6d58f_280x283.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!q-mN!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fde5ad157-c1c4-4d31-855e-6fddd9a6d58f_280x283.jpeg)

Look, surf music is great, and probably a sober analysis would put Dick Dale higher on this list and the Ventures, the Surfaris, the Chantays, etc. peppered throughout it (just as [The Royal Fingers](https://youtu.be/biZbzNiqFhY) belong on any best-of list of the early 2000s). But no ’60s ranking should be sober, and surf music is like Gregorian chants: I love it when I hear it, but I can never remember which song I’m listening to. So here we are, surf fans.

And I picked the song that you won’t confuse with any other. “Hava Nagila” blends seamlessly into the surf sound, which actually makes sense, giving surf’s Eastern-Mediterranean roots: Dick Dale is half Lebanese and “Misirlou,” a song so good that even [Carl Wilson’s 1963 chops](https://youtu.be/5aKTHBzgKg8) couldn’t ruin it, is famously a Greco-Armenian folk song set to reverb. By the ’90s, the cool kids spun this record at their bar mitzvahs.
