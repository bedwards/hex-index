Picture a teenage boy with a teenage boy’s complexion, which is to say not great. He wears a thick pair of glasses on a giant head, made uglier by a gap-toothed smile. Lots of people will say he looks like Shrek when the movie comes out, which he will accept with good humor. Other people tell him that he has a thousand-yard stare, to which he firmly insists they don’t know what they’re talking about.

It’s a lie he will insist upon for years.

He carries a sense of darkness that he knows he’s supposed to keep secret. It’s a feeling like the breath of the Nothing from *the Neverending Story* on the back of his neck. A feeling like there’s some terrible, beastly unknown fact about the world standing right behind him, which if confronted would logically demand the end of existence. Something so terrible a man would rather lay down and die on the spot than acknowledge it directly.

The feeling makes him wildly punch the cement walls in the basement until his knuckles bleed. He has a stupid fantasy that one day he will make his bones stronger than rock if he does this enough. Nobody cares that his knuckles are covered in scabs that bleed all the time, or that his fingers click when he makes a fist. It’s a teenage boy thing. A rite of passage where he has to try to be more tough than sensible. Like everyone else who has stared into the mouth of hell, the boy is going about the indirect business of pretending life has any meaning and everyone knows he has to figure it out for himself.

Give this boy the awkwardness of great height attained in youth but only half the muscle a man requires for his dignity. Place in his mind an embarrassing and precocious intellect unpruned by peers of equal ability. Bolt on a sense that he doesn’t belong anywhere so that he reads too many Fantasy novels and thinks there’s something noble about deciding he’s too good for the world as it is, and that somehow by thinking this he has become too good for the world in truth.

For the setting, imagine a living room in a run-down house in the kind of neighborhood that floods every year. A third divorce kind of house. For the time, imagine that it’s beyond the middle of the night and has now become the early morning. The hours not of fun, but of despair, when all the bars have closed and the honest bakers have not yet risen to make the day’s bread. The hours when the boy’s mother should definitely be home, but inexcusably isn’t.

As on every weekend, the boy attempts to soothe a baby girl who will not sleep. A fragile, tiny, baby girl, held there in his scab-covered hands in front of his unfortunate ogre face.

She’s crying.

For hours, sometimes as many as three —*which he counts with no small amount of bitterness, second by second on all the clocks in the house, each of which is set to a different wrong time, and which for strange reasons he has been repeatedly scolded for correcting*— he alternately walks, feeds, and burps the baby, or rocks her in a chair for the few minutes she’ll stay asleep. If he stops, the tears will start. He’s all she has on these nights.

He’s not enough.

The boy mostly accepts he will not be sleeping these nights. He’s a teenager now. Thirteen. A man, basically. That’s what his awkward mustache is signaling to the world. You can’t be a boy with a mustache. Adults are supposed to be able to handle things like crying babies.

Whenever the wild thoughts come that this is all wrong and definitely shouldn’t be happening —*the feeling that moves into his knuckles when he punches cement walls, a bitter sense that* he’s *supposed to be a little kid still and* he *shouldn’t have to constantly be taking care of* his *parents*— he stews in a black, and limitless loathing.

On the best nights, *Loveline* is playing on MTV and Adam Carolla dispenses judgment on a world gone mad. Dr. Drew attempts to soothe all parties with calming medical advice. Some caller will say something absolutely bizarre and it will be seized upon and discussed immediately.

It transforms the boy’s loathing into laughter. It all sounds right and good when people are admonished and corrected for behaving poorly. The boy wishes he could yell at his parents with the same sort of comedic righteousness as Adam Carolla, but somehow when his mother comes home drunk with his step-father disrespectfully squeezing her breasts on the front porch, only to be arguing and slapping her minutes later, he can’t find anything to say.

The words dry up under what he knows.

A part of that dark truth over his shoulder is that this isn’t happening because his mother doesn’t know better.

It’s happening because she *likes* it.

The boy objected in the beginning. On those first few nights, when his stepfather was just some guy his mom had brought home, the boy mustered up all his courage to confront the situation like the hero in one of his Fantasy novels. Rand al’Thor would do something. So would Sturm Brightblade, or even the twisted Roland of Gilead. None of them would let a man hit a woman, let alone their mother. Surely, the boy told himself, if he kept that truth wrapped around him justice would prevail.

It was his time to stand and be counted!

When he tried to help, it was like *he* was the one doing something wrong. His mother rolled her eyes at his thirteen-year-old dramatics when he tried to stop his stepfather from beating her. Shook her head in annoyance when he was pushed to the ground for his troubles, his wounded honor another item on a long list of things she didn’t have the time or inclination to “deal with.”

He forces himself to relive a memory, often.

The way his mother touched her freshly slapped cheek and laughed. *Laughed*. At *him*. Telling *him* he was being hysterical and silly, giggling as she said *his* name in a baby voice. Mocking him for thinking he was going to do anything.

What did he think would happen? Was he going to beat up a grown man? Was he *stupid*?

All he’s good for is watching the baby. That’s the way he can help, she says. So he rocks the baby and sings to her, even when there’s a feeling inside like hell. Even when the Nothing is breathing down his neck whispering that none of this means anything, he pats her back, or changes her diaper, or gives her a bottle. His mother insists that it’s her turn to have fun since it was so stressful to raise him, although the boy doesn’t ask for a justification after the first few weekends.

His mother justifies it, anyway.

She raised him all the way up until thirteen! She shouts, as he sits there silently, rocking the baby. Thirteen years is an unfathomable amount of time for a child to be at home! How dare his eyes judge her! It’s his job to pay her back now. Doesn’t he agree? It makes sense! *He* will watch the babies and *she* will go out and have fun. All of this is happening to *her*, doesn’t he understand? The divorce. The money problems. The times when the abuse is too extreme, and the boy can tell she has warring emotion between bailing on the relationship and loving nothing more than being important enough to a man that he’ll strike her in a moment of passion. *All* of these are things that are happening only to *her*.

He doesn’t have to say a word before she screams on the way out. The constant refrain to their relationship. You’re not my father! You’re not my dad! You’re just a kid!

Rocking and singing. Feeding. Changing diapers.

It’s okay. You’re okay. I’m here. You’re not alone. I love you.

He’s surprised to find that he does love the baby. He didn’t think he could love anything. Hadn’t even thought love described anything real, but it’s there inside him and undeniable. A little firefly dancing in the dark.

I love you. Yes. I do. It’s okay. Lay down on my chest. See? That’s good. I’m sorry I don’t know how to swaddle you better. I’ll have grandma show me again. It’s okay. I’m sorry, she’ll be back soon. No one forgot about you.

Some nights the baby needs to hear this for hours and hours.

Some nights are ten years long.

He tells her it’s going to be okay when she’s burning with fever or teething pain. He quickly learns how to dose the medicines because he called his grandparents once in a panic and his mother had screamed at him like nothing ever before. If he wasn’t so stupid, he would have figured out how to do it on his own. His mother can’t believe he’d go behind her back like that just because a tiny infant was screaming with fever and wouldn’t stop crying. What had he been thinking?

Do you want me to sing to you? I know all the songs you like. How about a bottle? A diaper change? I know I’m not who you want, but I’m here. *I* love you.

He feels pride when he thinks of that little hand wrapped around his finger. That tiny hand that only wanted something to hold, reaching out to the world and finding him. Night after night, him being the one there when she reached out for something to hang onto, until she recognized him and understood him, not her mother, to be her caretaker.

How can I explain?

I can’t.

So we’ll make a map out of my memories.

His stepdad pisses and shits with the bathroom door open. There is no apology, made, felt, or feigned, for this behavior. His stepdad simply refuses to be embarrassed that everyone can watch him fart or scrunch his face as he flexes through a bowel movement. It is the Americans who are silly, too prudish, too ridiculous for this world. Americans who have absurd customs around something everybody does.

On Ponape, his home island, his stepfather insists everyone shits in front of each other all the time. Later, the boy will discover this isn’t true and it will confirm what he suspects. His stepfather does this to be disgusting. He does it so that nobody will bring outsiders into the house.

The boy objects not because of his own dignity, which has been depleted since long before the divorce, but because of the baby. She’s an American and she should learn American customs. When his stepfather dismisses these concerns, the boy says that he needs to close the door because of the brother a few years younger than him who is embarrassed to bring friends over after school. Enough is enough, the boy says, this is a small request. The boy gets up and closes the door.

His stepdad erupts in outrage.

The short man rises from the toilet, ass un-wiped, his small dick wiggling as he runs, and chases the boy down the hallway. The boy is taken by surprise as his stepfather pins him to the ground, holding a shit-covered piece of toilet paper inches from the boy’s face. The smell is awful, psychologically amplified by the proximity and the fact he can’t get away.

Does the boy see? His thirty year old, meth-addict stepfather screams trying to make the boy understand. It’s just shit! It’s *just* some fucking shit! Why is the boy freaking out over some fucking shit? Everybody fucking shits and everybody’s shit stinks! Does the boy think he’s better? Does the stuck up fucking white boy think his fucking shit doesn’t stink? His stepdad lunges at the boy, coming closer and closer to wiping the shit across the boy’s face.

His stepdad laughs like a hyena as the boy squirms.

Calm down, his stepdad says, holding the shit under the boy’s nose as he gags and tries to turn to the side. Why is the white boy so fucking high strung over some shit? He needs to calm down because nothing bad has happened. He doesn’t have any shit on him. He should be fucking grateful. This literally wasn’t anything bad at all. No harm done.

Eventually, his stepdad laughs and lets the boy up, waddling back to the bathroom to clean the rest of the shit off his ass. Singing to himself in the language of his island. The boy grabs the baby and takes her downstairs to read her some books. He ignores what has happened. Calms himself down because nobody is going to care and he can’t do anything, besides.

His stepdad does the same thing a few weeks later, but with a long string of snot dangling over the boy’s eye.

This time, the boy is able to connect to his anger. Still, it’s not possible for him to imagine wounding another person on his own behalf. He’s not someone worth defending, even in his wildest imagination. All those circuits broke a long time ago. Still, this behavior demands redress.

“If you ever do something like this to the baby, I’ll fucking kill you!” He promises.

His stepdad laughs, shouting “ooooh, big man” over and over, shoving the boy against the wall, daring him to do something.

The boy doesn’t cry, doesn’t even want to cry, when he thuds against the wall. He repeats the promise.

“If you ever do something like this to the baby, I’ll *fucking* kill you.”

He knows it sounds so pathetic, but he means it.

I did mean it. I need you to know that.

“Does it ever bother you that you’re *so* ugly?” his mother giggles.

The laughter is the same as the slap-laughter, the question probing for some sentiment with which to wound him. In biology class he read a paragraph in a textbook about the way a lioness will go into heat when a new lion moves in and kills her cubs and recognized the truth of it immediately. He holds the knowledge like some dark, forbidden spell, never letting himself forget. It’s part of the Nothing that breathes down his neck.

He understands that it excites her to hurt him this way. It’s *thrilling* to her.

It says something important about the world and his place in it.

He sighs.

There’s a frustration in her eyes, like she’s trying to wring water from a bone-dry cloth, when he doesn’t react. These kinds of things have happened too often, and this is nothing like the worst one. Only the worst one that can be written down.

“No,” he says, “I know I’m ugly. There’s nothing to do about it.”

He practices telling himself he’s ugly a few times a day so that his lack of emotion will be genuine when she brings it up, which she does exactly as often as someone might if they’re always thinking about something. She was pretty in school. Her son is ugly. It bothers her. Lots of people are ugly, though, he tells himself. He happens to be one of them. Being ugly is perfectly ordinary. Bell curves don’t always go in your favor. Sometimes knowing things like that can hurt a little bit until you get used to it.

So what?

The baby uses his fingers for support to take her first steps. He walks around the house, bent over to support her, as she learns to waddle. Her first unaided steps are taken to walk *toward* him. *An-Roo*, she coos with each stumbling step. *An-Roo*. She is a big fat mess of black hair with green eyes and she doesn’t care that the boy is ugly. The biggest, Ernest-Borgnine-level-ugly grin you ever saw steals over the boy’s face when she turns to him and claps, excited to see him come home from school. The smile says that he is good, that he is fun, that he makes her happy.

The boy likes this because if he could choose to be anything that he is not, he would choose to be a good man. He would choose to be whoever it is that she sees when she looks at him. His baby sister, he thinks, when he picks her up and swings her around. *His* baby sister.

His baby brother arrives a few years later, so now the long evenings and longer weekends are spent watching not one child but two. The new favorite movie is *Toy Story*. The new baby likes to throw things into the shadow at the end of the hall and shout “fack-a-day-o!” Later on, the boy figures out that it means crocodile.

Collectively, the babies are “*the kids.”*

The boy is the man who looks after the kids.

Make the kids breakfast. Go to school. Leave the kids with his grandparents. Come home. Make the kids dinner.

Everyone is sick? Too bad. He’s got the kids to think about so he throws up until his stomach is empty and gets over himself. He changes a diaper. He crawls down the hall when he can’t walk to get the second diaper.

There’s no point being a baby when the actual babies need him. Everyone is sick. Mom doesn’t have what it takes to push through it, but so what?

She told you to do it. You can do it, you big ugly dummy. He tells himself.

What is the alternative? Let them go hungry? Let them sit in a dirty diaper?

The boy gets the kids a puking bowl and moves the kids to the front room to watch television. He puts down towels. He calls in sick to school. They know him, so it doesn’t matter that he doesn’t have a doctor’s note. He takes out blankets and tucks everyone in to watch the movie. Turns out the kids just needed to be in a common area and they fall asleep again on the couch.

You’re safe, he says. I love you. It’s okay. I’m here. He dozes in a rocking chair, shivering and sweating under a blanket.

His mother is in the other room because she never got out of bed. She’s talking on the phone, telling everyone how hard it is to take care of little kids when she’s sick.

He thinks about taking his own puke bowl and dumping it on her face, other things he could do to cause her pain, but he doesn’t.

There’s no point to it.

Still, there’s a hate in him, so big and so profound it warps the fabric of his soul like a black hole.

It’s amazing that it’s possible to hate someone so much.

I know what it is to hate someone so much that it makes your whole body shake.

In the seventh grade, his mom takes his stepdad with her to parent-teacher conferences. His stepdad slips a note into his art class supply box, signing it from a girl the boy likes. It’s a love note. A few lines written hastily, left as a prank.

When the boy finds the fake love note the next day, he almost doesn’t understand what kind of world he inhabits. The moment passes. He knows, intuitively, that there’s no way the note is real the same way a person can tell checks for a million dollars in the mail aren’t real. It isn’t congruent with anything else he knows to be true. He is ugly. He is not likable. The girl whose name is signed to the note is beautiful and beloved. These are brute facts. The note *cannot* be real.

This knowledge spares him from total humiliation.

Still… he *has* to check.

What if, somehow, it *is* real?

He sees her in his next class and he sits down next to her. Very quietly, as if confused, he asks if she maybe accidentally put anything in his art supply box. No, she doesn’t even have art class, she says. No hesitation. No sense of evasion. He is gone from her attention as soon as the question is answered.

This makes perfect sense.

*Of course.*

It was stupid to even ask.

*Mike*, is the next thought.

He has an embarrassing fit of emotions sitting next to her in geography class for the next fifty minutes that he thought he was beyond ever feeling. He is both humiliated and humiliated by his humiliation. He hates that there are still these parts of him, underneath, that evidently want these kinds of things to be true. What a fucking baby, he thinks. Why should it hurt when he knew right away the note wasn’t real? If part of him didn’t wish he wasn’t ugly, then why would he be so upset to be found unattractive? If part of him didn’t wish he was likable, why would he expect some girl to like him? If he truly understood his parents to be his enemies, why shouldn’t he *expect* this kind of pointless cruelty?

Why should it suddenly hurt that his mother asked him just that morning if the reason he was frowning so much had anything to do with being too ugly for any girl to ever like him.

He wishes he could carve these parts out of himself with a scalpel. It’s childish and he has no room for it. At least he doesn’t cry. He hasn’t cried in years. He’s promised himself he won’t ever cry again. So he sits with it, talking himself through the humiliation, until an empty anger takes it place. A quiet and clean-burning fury that helps keep the pain at bay.

His stepfather can’t stop laughing when the boy gets home from school and he asks if the boy found anything interesting in art class. He sees the boy’s face and it makes him cry with barking hyena laughter. The boy’s mother can’t stop laughing, too, when his stepfather explains the prank. Then she tries to stop herself and asks if he’s okay, which is worse. He must have felt like such a fool, she says! Like such an idiot, she insists! How tragic! But still… his mother says with her bitten lip and red face trying to hold back the laughter… don’t you see how funny it is that you felt like such an idiot?!?

The boy doesn’t like that this gets under his skin. He doesn’t like that he almost cries about how mean and how unfair it is. As if he never read the paragraph in biology class about what happens when a lioness goes into heat after her cubs get eaten by a new lion. As if he doesn’t understand that a woman might look on a man savaging her children not as a monster, but as a man willing to do anything for love. As if he didn’t decide long ago, that the only dignity in the world left to a lion cub being eaten as its mother watches and orgasms, is to not let the bastards see you cry.

He knows better and he should act like he knows better, but his ugly face gets all red as he fights back tears. It’s another victory in their column when he leaves to go downstairs so he can be alone. If he was any kind of real man he would have been able to stand there and take it, or turn it around on them somehow.

This is not tolerable.

When he’s alone, he calmly practices. “Of course no girl is ever going to like you. Of course not. You’re just not that kind of person. You’re ugly and weird. It’s just who you are. They’re not lying about that part. Not everybody is cut out for having someone else love them like that, and that’s you, and that’s fine.”

He decides to be proud of himself for not crying. That was as close as he’s been to crying for years, but at least he didn’t actually do it. It’s something small he can take away to soothe his pride.

The kids get a red radio flyer wagon from their grandparents for Christmas. The boy makes them swear a super special promise not to jump into traffic and he pulls them behind him to the video rental store. He has money from their grandparents from odd jobs to pay. *Barney’s Magical Adventure* again, with that creepy little thing called “The Winkster.” That and *Army of Darkness*, one of his favorites. Then he loads them back up into the wagon and walks them home.

It is good to be with them. To get them out of the house, away from their mother and father. Wholesome. Here, in these little patches, he can create a pocket of perfect order. He would never, ever humiliate them for his own pleasure. There is no second lion on this savannah, devouring the children of the first. He has not one ounce of resentment at their parentage. Not one atom wishes to take his pain and place it upon them. He can make this one little piece of the world better.

One time a boy from school sees him walking the kids in the wagon and laughs, asking why he’s smiling. Everyone knows the boy doesn’t smile. The boy frowns and tells him to fuck off, feeling like he’s been caught naked. Nobody should ever see him smiling, except for the kids.

I know what it is to be subjected to deliberate cruelty from the people who are supposed to love me.

His father keeps shouting things like, “Every day I think about blowing my brains out or disappearing down to Mexico!” And then he glares at whichever child is closest, as if to assign blame, before insisting, “and every fucking day, I’d do it except for you kids.”

His father mutters to himself that this is because he’s a good man, but reminds himself it would probably be easier to disappear in Mexico or kill himself. If only the world were so kind. If only he were dead, which everyone understood to be inarguably better than being alive.

The boy’s father hasn’t been the same since he hid in the back of mom’s van and stood up to find her cheating on him. Sometimes, his father talks carelessly to his best friend Dutch about how close he was the night he bought a gun and put it in his mouth, before both of them start laughing at the absurdity of the situation. That would have been a lot more final than the night he took all those pills. It’s hilarious how many times his wife’s cheating has driven the boy’s dad to suicide, because it’s happened a *lot*. The boy has trouble keeping them all straight.

“Don’tcha know that’s why I *can’t* own a gun, Dutch? Three days out of the week I’d shoot somebody. Four days out of the week, I’d shoot myself.”

Both men laugh uproariously, because it’s honest.

It becomes a favorite joke of his father’s that he repeats for years and years. It’s funny that his only two moods are murder and suicide. The passion is thrilling! Women *do* like it. All of his girlfriends after the divorce like his drama and his passionate moods.

Well, they say, he’s not boring. Maybe he’s been divorced a lot, but he’s never had a problem finding another woman to replace the last one.

He’s exciting.

For a while, anyway.

None of the girlfriends like the boy, who is like a cat, keeping to himself and not seeking emotional connection from anyone. They don’t like the way he looks at them like they have a timer over their head winding its way down to zero.

When the boy isn’t watching babies, he’s roofing houses with his father, part of a larger and undignified process of “making ends meet.” They use old wooden extension ladders, where the wood is punky and you can squeeze it in parts and see water seep out like a sponge. The ladders were inherited from a great uncle, and are over a hundred years old by the time the boy first climbs one. The boy and his father haul seventy pound bundles of shingles over their shoulders, up and up, over and over, daring the ladders to break each time.

There is no money for new ladders, the same way there is no money for safety harnesses, or to pay the boy. Everyone else needs the money more. The boy is only good for working not for paying. If there is any money to be made here, it will go the current wife first. Then the boy’s older sister. Then the boys mother. There’s never any money left after that last part, not even for school trips. So the boy works, tearing off and picking up shingles, twelve or sixteen hours a day where he will exchange a half a dozen words with his father. At night, he will watch his little brother and sister and not sleep while his mother goes out to Karaoke.

One of the ladders finally breaks when the boy is carrying up a bundle of shingles.

The boy pivots as he falls down, and would break his hip if the wood were not so rotted that his hip bone breaks the ladder instead. It hurts like hell but his femur is still intact when he hits the ground. He would break his neck but he lands in a compost heap, full of old grass and onions. It knocks the wind out of him. For what seems like an eternity, he can’t breathe.

Seconds pass and he can’t get any air.

Then, at last, he gasps, dizzy and woozy.

“Did you just break my fucking ladder?”

His dad snorts, unseen on the other side of the roof. He never stopped working to come and check on the boy. The silence before the gasp was full of his father’s annoyance at having to deal with an interruption.

It’s not that they were using wooden extension ladders instead of fiberglass. It’s not that the ladders were a hundred years old. It’s not that his dad stores the ladders outside in the rain or that the wood was punky. Have some accountability, his dad says. The boy walks with a heavy step. He walks too heavy, and it’s not that he’s big either, he doesn’t know how to walk right. He’s never known how to walk right. You walk with a heavy step, his dad insists, and the ladder would never have broken if you hadn’t been walking with a heavy step.

It’s the same logic where it becomes the boy’s fault that his dad’s last girlfriend cheated. The boy had borrowed the truck to go to another part of town, and therefore the last girlfriend felt free to get drunk since she was driving her own car. She would never drink and drive in someone else’s vehicle, because her manners were too good. After becoming drunk, she had decided to fool around with some other guy at the bar. There’s a clear line of cause and effect where the boy borrowing the truck caused this to happen. She *never* would have done that if the boy hadn’t been driving the truck. How could the boy do something like that?

“Fuh-fuh” he gasps, taking a full breath, “Fuck you, dad!”

His dad shakes his head from the ridge line, which the boy can see as limps away from the job site. His hip hurts.

It’s a long, painful, six mile walk to get back home.

Like a cuck, he still goes out to roof the next weekend. Still uses the last wooden ladder to carry shingles. Like he doesn’t know better. Like it isn’t stupid. Like it won’t just break like the last one. There’s no helping it.

Sometimes, the boy viciously scratches his knuckles across the hot asphalt shingles, part of his obsession with making his hands as strong as possible. He wants to be able to hit someone like out of a movie, where he can pound his fists against a skull over and over again without his fingers breaking. Surely he must be close to his goal. The skin on his knuckles has become like leather. He has no way to measure it but surely his bones must now be much harder than average. He’s recently broken his first punching bag.

The first punching bag breaks like a scene from a movie. All three straps securing the bag in place break simultaneously under the fury in his fists. His strike bends the whole eighty pound bag in half and then the force of his blow throws it back several feet to the ground. Privately, he tells himself the truth, which is that the bag was old, lightweight, and the straps half-rotted. It’s better for his hatred if he never lets any accomplishment be real. It’s better if he never lets this peculiar lust be satisfied. Another used, but newer bag, will soon break in less dramatic fashion. Each of the three anchor points will break one at a time, each haphazardly repaired, until the whole thing is spent.

The third, actually new punching bag his grandfather buys him is the last to break. His grandfather pridefully takes him to have it reinforced by a leather smith. His grandfather flashes a rare smile, laughing even, that his grandson keeps breaking punching bags. What a proud problem to have! He asks for the straps to be reinforced so that they will never break. The leather smith tells the boy he should wear gloves or he will ruin his hands, but the boy is proud of the blood all over the canvas and the callouses on his knuckles. For the longest while, I could not reconcile forgiveness with justice. I thought to forgive meant to pretend a bad thing was a good thing. I thought it meant to be passive, to take no action, to never stand up for what is right. I thought it was this sort of blissed out lie that people told themselves so they wouldn’t have to try.

This was never true.

This was something I told myself because I didn’t like how hard it is to forgive.

To forgive is not to lie or to stand still.

To forgive is to move righteously and without hate.

My mom should take her medication, not because I hate her and want vengeance on her sickness but because it would soothe the horror I’m sure she sometimes feels in the dead of night when her mind is calm that she allowed her mental illness to destroy her family. I forgive Mike.

It would have been better for everyone, *including him*, that he go away to prison for what he did.

I forgive myself for failing to say anything.

For so long as you are moved by hate, you will be moved to do not only what is within the limits of justice but also what is beyond justice. You’ll not only want to set the world back to order you’ll want to grab extras to satisfy your petty vengeance. Your hate will demand that it too has a right to be satisfied by the pain of others. Each, and every time you do that it will stain your soul.

Oh, yes, it sounds like a platitude from out of a movie but along the way the person you’ll learn to hate the most will be yourself. Don’t you roll your eyes at me like I don’t exactly what I’m talking about. I *earned* how well I understand this.

Put it down.

This piece took me forever to write because I had to figure out how to lay this all out like a map. I wanted you to understand every step I took along the journey to get here. I wanted to leave you without excuse. I had to figure out what I could say without being a hypocrite.

Put it down.

If you think this is all impractical, ask yourself who the real victors of history are. I am not a perfect man. I fail at so many things every day. Part of why I struggled to complete this piece was figuring out how to say that honestly. This is a lesson that we all have to learn again and again. Those things we think of as hard truths are lies over something grander. The world as you know it today would not exist if that were not so. If you learn to move righteously and without hate, you will change the world.
