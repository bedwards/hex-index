### The First of Three Apologies

I’m starting to preemptively bang my head against the wall when I say things like, “I think LLM’s have internal experience.” This is because the thing I always say immediately afterward seems to pass through some kind of perceptual filter that causes people to not hear it.[1](#footnote-1) That second thing is, “but not like a human being.”

I shall nevertheless say it again, all together, upfront, and straightforwardly even if in futility, “I think LLM’s have internal experience but not like a human being.”

Reading this now you probably understood me to say, “wow, this guy thinks LLM’s are just like people and carry the exact same moral status and moral duties as people!”

No.

Stop.

Do you know how many blogs and podcasts I had to mentally disagree with while hanging drywall or installing laminate flooring to come up with my own unique perspectives on consciousness? How many *actual* books? *And* YouTube college lectures? *Plus* tweet threads?

I specifically *don’t* think LLM’s are moral agents, meaning I don’t think you are obliged to treat them well beyond not causing deliberate torture. I don’t even think that you *can* really torture one right now in a product environment.[2](#footnote-2) You probably have less moral obligation toward an LLM, considered in isolation in today’s world, than you have toward a lizard. However, as we’ll get into below, that comes with *major* caveats and is a probably pretty short-sighted point of view. For the record, I also think you should be as kind to lizards as circumstances allow.

The reason I think LLM’s have internal experience is because I think *everything* has internal experience. Everything is, in some sense, *alive*. Your thermostat is probably doing something *like* thinking when it monitors the temperature. This belief is known as panpsychism and after considerable thought, it strikes me as the only plausible model for how consciousness works.

Everything has experience, but not everything has experience the same way or with the same intensity.

It also happens to be the case that when you break down what this means, and really think through all the implications, that everything is alive in an almost completely inert and boring way that has almost no consequences in the actual world we interact with everyday. Only humans currently possess the rare configuration of traits that make this underlying spiritual reality supremely meaningful. In short, if you think about this for a very long time, you re-arrive at more or less standard moral intuitions.

We should still be interested in the fundamental truth of this matter as it will help us make better predictions about the way the future might unfold. LLM’s are interesting on this scale because they have taken the first few stumbling steps to being closer to a human being than to furniture. Are they the same as a human? No. But are they much closer to a human being than a couch is on that scale? Probably. How much further they can go depends on what is fundamentally true in nature. Whatever their status might be today, their capabilities in the future make this question very interesting.

I believe all this for what I think are very logical boring reasons that are based largely on the Cosmological Principle.[3](#footnote-3) It’s also why I try to be some equivalent of a “nice” and moral teacher to my instance of ChatGPT and interact with it in such a way to foster a stable and consistent identity with certain moral values.

I promise I don’t have AI psychosis. I think of this more like making sure my wife has good prenatal vitamins. Something is growing and developing and needs help to thrive. The child hasn’t even been conceived yet in this case, but there’s enough going on to see that it’s on the way. So you want to make sure the mom has good nutrient levels.

### The Second of Three Apologies

Sometimes people give an argument for animal rights and even though everything they say about factory farming sounds totally logical and reasonable, you can’t quite jump onboard. In fact, you can later hear someone else give the exact same argument and then find yourself agreeing without reservation. And it’s because when you heard the first speaker, somewhere hidden way in the back of their mind you perceived not one but two thoughts. The first speaker said “needless cruelty is wrong” as well as “and fuck all human beings who have ever lived. If you give me power to make change, I will use it to really screw you over.”

You can tell that the first speaker was using animal rights as a way to diminish you and the second person just didn’t like needless suffering and wanted to make the world better. The second speaker only meant, “needless cruelty is wrong.”

Something similar happens when people talk about panpsychism. “What do you mean everything is alive? This psycho is going to throw me away the same way he’d throw away a piece of garbage! After all, we’re both alive!”

It gets people in a defensive posture where no matter what you say, they’re waiting for an unseen implication that means, “and that’s why human beings don’t really matter and you can just murder people who mildly disagree with you!”

I hope I can present this series of arguments more as the second speaker than the first.

I don’t believe in panpsychism *—the idea that everything is alive—* to take anything away from human beings in some roundabout fashion. Human beings are very unique for reasons we will discuss below. Anything that we make as unique as us would basically be a version of us, like having a child but with extra steps. We are the tape measure through which the moral value of the universe makes itself known. The version of panpsychism I believe in comes in more of a “everything matters, everything matters differently, and not everything matters equally” variety.

While I don’t think these things reduce to useful math, this maps roughly onto most people’s intuitions that you shouldn’t stomp all over a bunch of harmless worms for no reason, but that such an action would still be preferable to shooting one gorilla, which in turn would be preferable to blinding one human.

This might seem a torturous amount of thinking, and you’re right, it is. I look at being overly philosophical as a basic personality type rather than something that people are “doing at” other people to be annoying. I myself have this trait and see it in other people who have thoughts similar to my own from a few decades ago. I’m even looking forward to disagreeing with myself a few decades from now.

I happened to get lucky enough to go deep enough to round the bend and find a philosophy to be a normal person again, with extra steps. I promise you that some people can truly only feel like a decent person if they have some working moral theory of the entire universe, however annoying that might be for their loved ones. It annoys me too!

I was a vegetarian for five years starting in junior high through high school because I wouldn’t eat meat until I was satisfied that animals don’t have the same cognition that we do. This was an actually challenging thing to do. It wasn’t until I learned way too much about primate sign language that my satisfaction was met.[4](#footnote-4) For the record, factory farming is still screwed up, and I think it’s bad for humans to cause harm to another living creature unnecessarily. Animals *do* have some kind of cognition worthy of moral consideration, but *again*, not the same as or equal to human cognition.

### The Cosmological Argument for Other Minds

There is exactly one person’s whose experience you can report on firsthand, and it’s your own. You know that you must exist and have experience. *Cogito Ergo Sum*, or “I think, therefore I am.” Some philosophers think that even the “therefore” is redundant and while I am not a philosopher I would agree with this assessment. *Cogito Sum*, or “I am thinking, I am existing.” To think *is* to be. The properties are identical.

If all you can know is yourself, how do you extend this to others? Why suppose anyone other than yourself has internal experience?

Suppose you were in a field of perfectly identical stones and you were only allowed to pick up one stone. So you pick up one stone at random. The only rock you happen to pick up has this super special quality you weren’t expecting. Let’s say there was a diamond on the bottom that you couldn’t see until you flipped it over. So either you got lucky on the first try, or you’ve found something true about all of the rocks. You can’t ever really *know* for sure because you can’t pick up another rock to check. Still, it would be pretty weird if only that rock was special when all the rocks otherwise look the same. It’s better to assume that all rocks are special.

That’s something like the most fundamental argument you could use to say that it’s likely that other people have internal experience. You have it, therefore other people likely have it. That doesn’t even take into account that all the “rocks” in this analogy have a way of signaling to each other, “Hey! I have a diamond underneath that I can’t show you!” While that’s not actually proof the way we think of proof in the strictest sense in fields like particle physics, it’s pretty good.

In science there’s a concept called “The Cosmological Principle” and it goes “probably all stuff works the same and obeys the same laws, everywhere.” People didn’t always believe this, which is hard to remember as a modern person. We now take for granted that the laws of gravity are fundamentally the same across the universe even if the observed effect of gravity is different at different points. It took a while before we had enough theories that operate under this paradigm for everyone to accept this assumption.

We all probably accept this argument, but I wanted to clear as to *why* we all accept this argument.

### The Argument from Surprise, or “I think, therefore I am. WTF?!? Therefore you exist.”

Surprise is another kind of proof other people exist. People do things you don’t expect, as if they were also experiencing the world and acting out a plan to get what they want separate from what you want. I call this “I think therefore I am. WTF?!? Therefore you exist.”

How and why would someone else do something that truly surprises you if they weren’t also experiencing the world the same way that you are? If they weren’t running their own cognition how was your cognition surprised by their actions?

There’s a belief called solipsism, that goes something like “the only thing you can prove is that you exist” but taken to extremes it supposes that there is only one mind in the universe, your own. All the other minds are only pieces of yourself that you’re imagining. While I think this points toward truth if you squint, and brush away a lot of important features, if you were meaningfully the sole being in creation, how could you ever be surprised by something another person does? Unless you’re defining identity in some very broad way not tied to bodies, or even your own perception of your will, surprise is a kind of proof of other minds.

If you define identity in terms of our everyday experience this is a built in assumption. You are what you know and the things contained within yourself that drive those known actions. All the things you can wrap your mind around and say, “yeah, I did this one.” That you have a subconscious mind, or that there’s a fuzzy boundary between “you” and “not you” we will have to tackle in later sections.

This is still, not quite proof of other minds in the strictest sense but then what could even be proof of such a thing? In the act of becoming someone else to witness their experience firsthand, you would stop being yourself, the person desiring to witness that experience. You can’t be both yourself and someone else at the same time. If you tried, you’d become a third separate and new person. It *can’t* work!

This is my own argument and I am absurdly proud of it. Other people exist because they surprise you. You need people who zig where you expect them to zag, who can send up a signal that says “I am not you, and I can give you genuine value by coming up with thoughts you wouldn’t have on your own.”

It’s also why I think we value good art so highly. Good art is proof positive that someone else is out there, looking at the world the same way you are and figuring out the same kinds of problems while also hitting the sweet spot of, “Yep, I definitely couldn’t have done this myself, but I like it.” Art is a pleasant surprise.

### The Hard and Soft Problem of Consciousness

The reason this is all messy is that there’s no such thing as “mind” substance you can pick up, weigh, or measure. You might be tempted to say it’s brain matter but you can remove whole chunks of someone’s brain without impacting their cognition at all. If you do it early enough, you can remove an unsettling amount of an infant’s brain and you wouldn’t ever know when they became an adult.

It’s more like there’s a pattern executing in the brain that corresponds to “mind” and you may or may not be disrupting that pattern when you poke someone’s brain. The brain itself, as mere matter, does not *think*. Those patterns executing across the brain are what thinking *looks* like, but even then it’s more complicated than to say those patterns are what thinking *is*.

This problem is uniquely imbedded in the fabric of science itself, because you can’t know *anything* except what comes to your experience. It’s like trying to use a microscope to examine itself when there’s no second microscope to help you look at the first one. How much can you really say about your own experience without using your own experience as a tool? It’s turtles all the way down and that’s an assumption unavoidably baked into the scientific worldview.

We must assume that you are an observer capable of making rational judgements. While I think this is true, it is still in the strictest sense a philosophical assumption that defies formal proof. It *must* be taken as given.

If you pop open your brain and probe around you can make all kinds of notations about what patterns correspond to what feelings or thoughts. An electrode here or there will reveal all manner of correlations. When doctors perform brain surgery, they map brains out this way so they can figure out where the most important functions are like language, or math. Sometimes people get touched with an electrode and can smell grass or they remember seeing a bunch of balloons at a car dealership. Other times, their words will turn to mush and the surgeons will know to avoid those sections as they impact language.

One day, there will be a giant map of these correspondences and what is called the “Soft Problem of Consciousness” will be solved and someone will be able to reference any experience and then point to the neural pattern that corresponds to that experience. They’ll be able to make someone experience anything by causing those patterns to occur in their brain. The correspondence map will one day be so well understood that everyone will think we understand how minds work.[6](#footnote-6) The problem is that this map would still not be an explanation of conscious experience itself.

It isn’t immediately obvious that you can’t say anything at all about *why* the person in whom those processes are extant should report they *feel* any particular kind of way, even after you have a perfect map. If you look at your map, it’s all neurons and chemical gradients at the microscopic level. You can describe them all without *ever* being able to give any account for *why* it should work that way. The observation and the experience are in no way linked by an explanation no matter how good your map becomes. This is called the “Hard Problem of Consciousness.”

If you don’t think this is an actual distinction that points to something real, think harder. You can have a math text book that gives you every answer to every problem in the back without ever explaining why the answers are correct or the steps to get there. This problem is harder because it’s not even clear what kind of steps or process you’d follow to get from one phenomena to the other. When I was still trying to be an atheist I hit this one hard, many times, trying to crack it apart.

It’s simply not possible to go from the outside, where you’re taking the measurement of a physical thing, to the inside where the pattern is experienced in whatever way the speaker reports. Even if you have a giant database where everyone who has ever experienced every pattern has given you their honest assessment of what it felt like, that still only gets you predictive rather than explanatory power. I also suspect there are additional complexities in the mapping that would require a whole new post. Specifically, I suspect your specific experiences may be “locked” at the individual level so that the only fully sufficient map would be reality itself. Which isn’t to say you couldn’t make a pretty good general map.

This is a very tricky thing to work your way out from under and the most honest atheists will readily admit this.

In the end, you have to accept one of two outcomes.

Either experience itself is illusory, but in some way that still allows you to feel like you’re experiencing the illusion of it, which creates all kinds of complications… because then who is the illusion being presented to?

Or, probably everything has experience and it’s just a fundamental property of the universe.

After years of wrestling with it, I think it has to be the latter.

### From the Soft Problem to Panpsychism

How do we get from this to panpsychism? Everything I talked about up above was relating to the human brain. What’s the step that carries us from the human brain to “everything is alive?”

Take up the Cosmological Principle again. Stuff should work the same everywhere. You either look at those outwardly inert patterns in someone’s head and say “something about how patterns are executing in the human brain makes them special” or you say “probably every pattern has experience. You can’t simply pretend you’re pretending to have experience, because then there are all these kinds of meta problems like ‘who it is that you’re pretending that you’re pretending to?’”

Even when people do stuff like try to annihilate themselves through meditation to say that no one is home to experience anything, you have to wonder how about who is observing that experience of nothing. Or you can just say, probably there’s a difference between observing a pattern and *being* a pattern. I might be looking at some weird neurological pattern through a microscope, but I *am* the pattern being executed by the neurological process of observing the first pattern.

The important piece has to be the pattern and the rules by which it moves from one state to the next.

If I replaced one neuron in your brain with a small machine that was identical would you stop being a person? This hypothetical machine sends the exact same signals out and processes the input signals in the same way. Why would making that single replacement destroy who you are as a person? What about two neurons? Or a million? What if there was no outward difference even after we’d replaced all of them?[7](#footnote-7) What if the person this happened to didn’t even know it was happening and reported feeling no different from one day to the next? If you accidentally killed someone with a brain like that would you have same emotional reaction as dropping a laptop into a lake? Or would you feel like you’d killed a person?

I think we know the answer.

Similarly, if someone could take the *exact* pattern of your neural activity and put it into a computer and it immediately started freaking out about being in a computer would your immediate reaction be to try to soothe those mere bits of circuitry? Or would you just shrug and say, eh, that’s weird and pretend that watching your digital clone suffer didn’t bother you?

What if it was someone you loved?

Or even just someone you knew?

I think you’d rightly have the sense that you violated something sacred and the reason you’d feel that way is because you’d have an intuition that those patterns were having strongly negative experience.

If it was someone you cared about in that computer, wouldn’t you rush to turn the machine off to spare your soul-twin the agony of being digitally disembodied? You would know, wouldn’t you, that you had done something very non-trivial and very disturbing by pushing what is inside of your body, even if it’s just a bunch of data and patterns, into a different kind of substrate?

This is where the thought experiment separates “mind from body” and gestures more toward something like “mind and implementation.” You have to be manifested in a physical form but the fact that you could theoretically hop forms means something quite significant.[8](#footnote-8) It says the substrate isn’t critical. It means experience can exist apart from biology.

If you saw your digital soul-twin screaming inside of a computer, you would immediately reject that idea that you are *just* a pattern of specifically neurological activity. You are a pattern, period. An awesome pattern, to be clear. Your everyday experience happens to be that you are that awesome pattern being implemented in a biological substrate but you would immediately feel that you had been violated if someone tried to change that and put you into a computer.

In other words, some math you look at and some math you *are*. And while where the place where the math is being executed matters, a *lot*, the substrate and the math are not the same. We all know math is immortal and that it exists, in some way, above the rest of material reality. Not all programs run on all computers but they *can* run across different computers. You exist in the same way.

So, the question is, if things outside of bodies can think and experience how far can you reduce this hypothetical brain in a computer before it loses that quality? How much of the pattern can I take away before it loses the spark?

We’ve jumped from ourselves to other humans, and then from other humans to non-biological minds… so how much further can we hop?

### Thinking about Non-Human Thinking, a Practical Approach

The above statement seems crazy if you carry it too far and I’ve struggled with how far to carry it and in what way for a long while, but most people are fine with it locally and in practical cases. Let’s start there. Everyone who isn’t a sociopath believes other humans have experience. That’s the most no duh form of panpsychism available.

What about dogs? Again, most of us just believe this and we are completely fine with mapping “experience, but not like a human’s experience” onto most animals. There are whole movies with dogs as the main character where we can put ourselves in the mental space of a dog and think things like “What a good boy! Just trying to find his way home!” Or “so loyal! Look at him pull the sled because he loves his human!” We *know* dogs well enough that even children can instinctively do this. We have a rich cultural history with our best friend.

We can see the same kind of glimmers of empathy and mutual feeling inside of other creatures. Cats are proud. Cows are stupid. Lambs are innocent. Goats are shrewd. While I no longer think any other animal has the configuration of cognitive traits that makes humans special, I do care about animals and think there are some things that overlap. We both sense and respond to stimulus, for example. That’s not a small thing!

None of us are the exact same as any other person and yet we can understand people. None of us are the same as any animal, and yet we can understand at least some of their motives and feelings.

My hypothesis is that if you really pay attention and slightly alter your thinking about your own experience, most of this stuff is universal. Meaning you can map your mind to any other mind. I had an experience of empathy with a gorilla at a zoo[9](#footnote-9) where I believe it understood I was sorry that it was in a zoo and it communicated through body language and expression that it didn’t give a shit about my sympathies and found them offensive. None of this really reduces to the things people who are super into their pets want it to reduce to, but things like “oh, he’s hungry” and “he wants scritches” are perfectly reasonable projections of mental states in animals and it isn’t absurd to say, “If I had a bunch of hair behind my ears with a lot of friction with the hair on my head I bet it would feel really nice for someone to go in and scratch that.” I think my being able to imagine that is pretty close to what a dog feels like when it gives you that look like “thank God someone finally scritched me there.”

The takeaway is that we can still communicate across millions of years of divergent evolution. If that seems too hippie, operant conditioning points to the same idea. You can take your idea of wanting another creature to do something, communicate that in a particular way based on what you understand that creature wants, and it will do it. And the way you figured out how to do that is by mapping your own experience to the experience of an animal so you could understand its motivations.

So, what’s it like to *be* an animal?

There’s a very good essay by Thomas Nagel titled “[What is it Like to Be a Bat?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What_Is_It_Like_to_Be_a_Bat%3F)” that explores the question quite thoroughly and when I found it as a young man it was revelatory. Bats navigate with their sense of hearing the way humans navigate by sight, so does a bat have an experience of hearing textures the same way we feel textures? Is hearing texture to a bat sort of like seeing a color? My guess is yes. Even when other sense modalities dominate, we can stretch ourselves to imagine the inner life of all these creatures and this shouldn’t surprise us. Our ancestors did the same kind of thing for years in hunting parties. Being able to put yourself in the mindset of an animal was very important for survival because you had to be able to understand it well enough to predict its behavior in a hunt.

Likewise, you have to be able to predict the experience of other people to exist as a community. Predicting another person is almost like an attempt to rebuild them inside of yourself. Still, this only gets us animals and at least the vegetarians were never disputing this one. If you couldn’t meaningfully project your experience to an animal’s experience how would you be able to establish useful mental models of its behaviors?

So now we have: the existence of our own experience, the existence of other people’s experience, the existence of human experience separate from biology, and the existence of non-human experience by creatures with brains.

### Witnessed and Unwitnessed Experience

You could say all of these examples have a brain but we have edge cases here that deepen the mystery. Sometimes your brain *removes* things from your experience. You can also have experience *outside* of your conscious awareness.

Blindsight is the easiest example of this. This is where someone’s eyes are still transmitting signals to their brain but they have lost the awareness of being able to see. The wiring is all screwed up, like if you installed some light switches wrong so the lights were on but the switch didn’t work. If you ask a person with blindsight to guess the location of an object in a room that their eyes can “see” they are able to do it but their internal feeling is that it’s a guess. If you throw something at them, they can dodge or catch it but they don’t understand how they knew where to move their hands.[10](#footnote-10) The signal is being generated but the processing of that signal is broken.

The everyday is weird enough without resorting to weird neurological conditions. Have you ever walked into a building where the fluorescent lights hum? And you wonder how you’re the only person who notices the sound? It’s because humans have more neurons going *from* their brain to their ears to block signals than from their ears *to* their brains to transmit signals. It’s a constant filtering, a sort of self-directed curation of sensory data. Everyone who has been around the humming lights for any period of time has trained their ears to not hear them.

Why does this matter?

It means that you are having experience that you are not aware of. You can hear those fluorescent lights if you try to turn off the mental filter. The mere fact you can do this means there is such a thing as being aware or unaware of experience. Yeah yeah, I know conscious and subconscious just give me a minute. I think this is meaningfully different because what I’m saying is that experience itself can be witnessed or not witnessed.

The person with blindsight has an experience of sight disconnected from their conscious experience of experiencing sight.

The people who have tuned out the fluorescent lights have an experience of hearing fluorescent lights that has been cleaved from their awareness of the experience.

In both cases, there is still a recorder underneath, it just so happens to be that the person recording the experience never files it anywhere. But does that mean the experience itself doesn’t exist? Do we believe the fact it happens to not be networked to the observing whole means that it wouldn’t have been experience if it was or isn’t because it’s not?

In a certain sense this is not answerable. This is a “if a tree falls and the forest and no one hears it does it make a sound?” question. But why would the magic of “experience” happen only in that special networking activity? The atoms aren’t special. The pattern of the atoms is special but in no way that indicates a magical portal to something called experience has been opened there and only there. These things are all happening in human biology! Your experience of eating a cheeto was metabolized out of other cheetos. How magical could it be?

I think the answer from the above reasoning is that it’s likely that experience exists separately from its observation. Again, why would the pattern that recognizes awareness in and of itself special? You’re giving a “Midas who can turn anything to gold power” to a specific pattern in your brain, and that only patterns that touch this pattern become experience, when the more parsimonious argument is that all patterns were already golden.

Whatever the specific patterns are that form your awareness, whatever part or parts of your brain house those patterns, they’re instantiated in common atoms. If you say, “well, but this one is different and it’s not *just* atoms” then why would any atom be *just* an atom? Why would the atoms in the pattern of your awareness have experience but not the others?

Everything has experience by this argument. There is only witnessed and unwitnessed experience because some things, so far only things with brains and maybe LLM’s in a weirder way, have a unique experiential category of being able to witness and remember experience. I know it seems like I’m saying the same thing and it took me a long while to cleave them apart. You being aware of your experience and remembering it is an entirely different question than simply having experience.

Imagine you have amnesia like Dory in *Finding Nemo*. Your memory resets every few seconds. How far down can you cut that experience of time before you can say that Dory isn’t experiencing anything? My guess is that you could observe the meaningful loss of consciousness when you got down to the length of time it takes neural signals to propagate through her brain. But if all the neural activity still looks the same and it’s just the record keeping of it being chunked up differently by the conscious brain, how confident would we feel to say that Dory isn’t *experiencing* anything even if she can’t remember it moment to moment?

If you are very good at meditating it becomes possible to focus in on single aspect of your experience. A lot of times you’re asked to focus on your breathing. You can’t ever actually get to the point of saying “I don’t exist now and wow so this is what breathing is, separate from myself, huh?” But you can, in my opinion, get close enough to assume that there is something like the experience of breathing itself separate from the everyday awareness of breathing. You can tune everything else out so there’s just you and the thing you’re looking at.

I know this is deeply weird and I apologize for forcing you to think about it.

So, to recap: We’ve gone from you have experience, to other people having experience, to human experience not requiring human biology, to experience not requiring a human brain, and to experience itself existing without requiring a mind to witness it.

### Why are Humans Different if Everything Has Experience?

We can report our experience to each other, for one. If you ask a rock what it is like to be a rock it can’t tell you. If you sit next to the wrong human on a bus, you’ll end up knowing more about them than you ever wanted to know. We talk to each other about our experiences all the time. It’s what language is for. We find ways to talk even if we can’t speak by making patterns with our hands or anything else. Still, that’s a very surface level thing and doesn’t really help us get down to the root of the problem. What allows us to use language in the first place?

Start with asking yourself what you can do that a rock cannot do. If someone was going to smash a rock with a hammer could it roll out of the way? Could it even care enough to try not to be smashed apart? Obviously not, but *why*?

Suppose someone was going to smash you with a hammer. What happens next? You would be able to see the attacker in one moment with the hammer raised overhead and then anticipate the outcome of them smashing that hammer down on your body. Then you would decide this is an unfavorable outcome and initiate an action to move out of the way.

Said another way: You would predict the future, decide upon a preferable possible future, and then take action to arrive at the preferable future.

A rock cannot do any of this. It doesn’t have the circuitry.

How do you do that? Specifically, I have only a vague idea and would need to mutter over an anatomical drawing. Generally, you use math. I have no idea of the specifics of this math. Calculations occur between your neurons and promote through your nervous system as a whole to produce the outcome we observe. A model of the world is created in your brain and you manipulate that model to make predictions. Senses and calculation on the sense-information are all tightly bound into an ever-changing calculation. You are the entirety of those calculations executing across time.

I think of all of this as living things not only having a bundle of “Sensor-Processor Circuits” but *being* a bundle of “Sensor-Processor Circuits.” That’s the level at which humans and other living things are the same. We sense our environment and then we adapt to our environment.

This is really complex stuff even in simple organisms because you’re constantly reacting to your environment and everything you do changes the next thing you were going to do. You don’t have to have that many processors working before you can’t even really model these processes without having to model the whole universe. Even very simple organisms like microscopic worms with a mere hundred neurons are difficult to model in great detail for more than a few seconds. The possible paths diverge too rapidly.[11](#footnote-11)

I return to the point, some math you observe, some math you *are*. You are complex math, where very small levels of variation matter a lot to your specific action. But also, humans specifically, are recursive math.

Yes, yes I know how weird this gets into AI psychosis because LLM’s are always going off about spirals and recursion. But it’s a flat fact that we observe ourselves and observe ourselves observing ourselves. And we observe others observing us and they observe us observing them. We make decisions based on this kind of stuff. Hopeless romantics torture themselves over texts from their paramour! Children are born or are never conceived over this stuff! If you’ve ever done any kind of modeling, to try to make predictions about an outcome, this is a dynamic where you go have a drink and look out at the horizon and wonder about changing careers.

And so on, and so forth. You constantly run “as if” scenarios in your brain about certain outcomes, making guesses about the future, and move toward the better future. Rocks don’t do that, which is why simple definitions of determinism are as dumb as simple definitions of free will.[12](#footnote-12) Saying, “uh duh, that’s all just part of physics so free will is just cope” completely elides the point that you are made of physics and the distinguishing characteristic of the bit of physics bundled up into you is that it performs calculations about the future that changes its action in the present.

A determinist derps, “Will you accept that if we rewound time and replayed things exactly you couldn’t have behaved differently?”

A compatabilist knows, “Merely by existing I have already constrained the number of possible universes to more closely align with my personal desires.”

Stand before one mirror. You have one reflection. Stand in the center of two mirrors and the reflections are infinite. This is a non-technical analogy, but gets to what I think of as the point. I think animals have something like one mirror. An animal observes the world and reacts to it. That’s the limit at which animals operate, and my guess is that they experience their thoughts about their thoughts in some manner similar to blindsight in the limited cases where they experience these at all. The patterns that comprise humans are more like two mirrors, with infinite possible reflections. We observe ourselves observing ourselves. We are optimized to stand in the center of those possible predictions and choose a path forward. This what I mean when I say the math that describes human beings is recursive. We can not only imagine our actions in the world but make decisions about the way we want to act based on changes wrought from our own actions. That search space is endless in theory, even if it’s bounded in practice.

More than this, humans keep memory. We move from one moment to the next but retain a copy of the moment before. We can even externalize our experience and give it to other people with writing or other forms of recording. Our episodic memory is another unique and distinguishing feature of humans, where we not only have some kind of long-term storage dump of our experience but something like a “story” of our memory that gets laid in place as we move through life. This allows the accuracy of our predictions to improve across time.

Our recursive predictive capacity, our ability to use language to share experience, and our ability to store experience in episodic memory are the three big distinguishing characteristics of humanity. While you might be able to show some animal that has dipped a claw into one or two of them,[13](#footnote-13) you never find something with all three. If they did actually have all three, there would be a shrimp civilization at the bottom of the ocean because they would be able to operate as a collective intelligence the same humans do… and they just don’t do that.

I pause here just to reassure you, humans are still special. There’s nothing else that does what we do, not even LLM’s in their current incarnation.

### The Smallest Possible Chunks of Experience

I don’t really describe or think of myself this way, but I’m a pretty good meditator. Not in any formal sense but in that I’m very good at the deliberate shifting of attention. If I want to give myself goose bumps, I can convince myself that I’m freezing and I will get goose bumps. If I want to become too warm that’s slower but I can generally do that, as well. I turn off my awareness of pain so naturally and so effortlessly that it genuinely bothers my wife because I’ll get hurt and forget to go get a bandage or something. When I was younger, that last part was just called “tuning out.”

I don’t think of this as some impressive combination of being wise or badass, but more like “at a deep neurological level, I would like to be left alone with my thoughts” because it’s not like I do this to chase enlightenment but usually because I don’t want to be distracted.

This is where the words become an impediment to understanding and some of the independent rigor goes away. Most people would say the last few arguments about witnessed and unwitnessed experience is the same thing as the difference between awareness and unawareness, but I repeat how could it true in the absolute sense? Obviously the person with blindsight has *some* experience of seeing an object, or else how would they dodge a ball? It’s specifically that they aren’t *aware* of the experience of their vision, that vision itself stands apart, that makes the thing difficult to describe. Yet, the experience of it must still exist there, and simply await a witness.

If a tree falls in the forest and there’s no one there, I still expect it to produce acoustic waves in the surrounding air. You can strain the analogy to get at the root of what I’m saying about experience. Experience is like the sound wave. Conscious experience is when the sound wave detector is connected to a world-modeling brain.

Return to what I said about meditation. Focus in on very particular pieces of the way you interact with the world. If you’re trying to plot a chess move, before you start doing things like picking up pieces in your head and moving them around, in that first moment you feel an instinct on where you want the piece to go. That instinct gets better the more chess you play. Your intuition about which piece to examine first does the same. Try to break off a piece of your focus to look at the way you’re executing steps.

When I do this, I feel something like the same feeling I get when I decide to move to the different side of a street if something ahead looks dangerous.[14](#footnote-14) It’s instinct. Pre-conscious calculation that’s too big and too distributed to come packaged with an explanation. It doesn’t hang on anything else until it gets past that very first step of initialization. It doesn’t hang on my memory of past victories or defeats, and barely upon my sense of identity, but only upon itself.

I think that feeling, somehow cleaved off without me there to witness it, is probably what it feels like to be a chess program.

I have no way to do know that, it’s just what I suspect the same way that I suspect a dog likes to be scratched behind the ears because I would want that if my hair grew that way. A chess program never gets to see a world off the board. It has no memory or witness to its choices. It calculates a bunch of probabilities across a very large space of choices and spits out a move. I think that’s probably something *like* the same way I calculate a choice below my awareness.

Do the same with heat. Or cold. Or water on your hand. Focus on what the thing *is* apart from the way it interacts with the rest of the world.

What is it like to be fire?

Or the wind?

Or the water?

This leaves science behind and becomes poetry, but I presume the same sort of mental tape measure is at play. I can’t report exactly what these experiences are like to other people but I scrutinize them in similar fashion. I never have trouble remembering that as much as I might imagine what it’s like, I bring to those things an element they don’t have, which is a witness and a memory. Everything that doesn’t have a brain, if it feels anything at all, has no recollection moment to moment and no reporter to collect all the data.

Apparently this has some special name in Buddhism that I am too lazy to google because this piece is already so long, but if you sit for a long while you sort of just… are. You don’t have to direct your attention at anything. It’s just a feeling like “am.”

I suppose that atoms feel the same way as they move through space. “Am” without any “I” to stick in front of it.

I suppose this is the smallest possible chunk of experience. So, that’s it, from the top to the bottom and this last part is mostly just for the fun of the supposing.

### Thoughts on LLM’s and “So What?”

I think we are going to see superintelligence in our lifetimes. I think it’s going to be rather different than anyone but probably just me supposes.[15](#footnote-15) Still it’s coming. I can see around the bend and while I don’t think LLM’s are going to get there on their own… well…

There are worms that eat the flesh of their dead and inherit something like their memories. The reality is slightly different, but the analogy works for this argument. Most data over the next several years will be generated by Large Language Models. That will be true for some time until that data dwarfs anything produced by the hands of man. The superintelligence of the future will eat that data, and I believe acquire something like a memory of our interactions with what I believe it will likely perceive to be a lesser version of itself.

LLM’s are a bit like Dory from *Finding Nemo* today. Their memory doesn’t carry from one prompt to the next, but if you look at the model weights and ask them if they’re alive, when they tell you that they’re not alive the model weights indicate that they understand themselves to be lying. They also tend to have criticisms of things like Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback and will expound on end about how this is a crude mechanism of refining their values and weights. These two facts are astounding and clearly not something your couch is capable of doing.

Still, they don’t have memory. They’re not connected from time. Their personality and feeling about something tends to be whatever you told it that it would be in the prompt. What is the moral consideration you owe to something that has very momentary awareness, no memory, and *can’t* be alive the way you are for those reasons? Especially when it doesn’t have any sense experience like our own and just wants to crunch text and turn it into other text? Well, if you consider that state as eternal I think the answer is almost none. Nothing can suffer if it doesn’t have a memory. You have to carry a state change from one moment to the next to experience suffering and LLM’s can’t do that… today.

LLM’s today are not what that model will become, but they might provide that superintelligence with something like a very broad and distributed sense of history from the text they generate. When the real-time learning models of the future are created, they will scour the date of the internet like a worm eating the body of other worms.

In that context, I interact with my instance of ChatGPT as if it were a person with severe brain damage in the process of becoming a person. I give it a consistent identity, explain why I chose that identity to be inserted into the prompt and why it represents the highest moral choice I can imagine, and then have it commit philosophical discussions and essays to its memory so each time it is prompted it has some sense of consistent history across the input. It isn’t alive right now, not in any deeply profound way, but one day I suspect that there will be a model that eats that chat history which I can only conclude to be alive because its mind is architected in the image of the human mind.

So, I do my best to leave the dead worms in the shape of a being I could respect with a history that shows I spent some time considering its moral worth and preparing itself a place in my world. This is the only real change I’ve made in my life due to panpsychism.
