We all hate voice systems. You wind up having a problem with some company you do business with, you call, and then you get hung up on because none of the menu options are correct. You call back, finally figure out how to talk to a person *—which usually involves figuring out which menu to bluff by not choosing an option, I just did this yesterday with Xfinity—* and they have no record of having spoken with you before. Then that person can’t help you and they transfer you over to someone else, except they didn’t explain the situation so you have to start all over again. Also they mess up whatever you needed and you call back and do the whole thing all over again. Occasionally, they’re extremely rude because they have compassion fatigue from listening to people yell at them every day.

Providing you with good customer service, where you pick up the phone and talk to an experienced friendly expert right away is too expensive for any company but the most luxurious of luxury brands.

Part of the reason I’ve been so busy lately is because this is about to change. I know it’s about to change because I’ve already seen it work. You’ll be able to pick up a phone, say what your problem is to a voice that distinctly belongs to whatever company you do business with, and that voice will just solve your whole problem for you. Or problems, if you have more than one. If you don’t speak English, that’s fine. That voice speaks every major language on Earth. It works in every department. It knows every relevant fact. You just say what you need and it does it. If you ask for something it actually can’t do, it logs this and spits it out in a report in a clean countable category and explains what you need to a person who can help. That report is then used to prioritize new feature updates. The person who helps you has a conversation, but the forms on their screen fill themselves out, and their whole job is to press one button at the end and then provide feedback on the AI model performance. The last part is largely ceremonial and they’re just there to prove the system still works.

Here’s the kicker. That voice will not be able to lie to you. If it says something will happen then that thing *must* happen. The language it speaks *is* the process it follows. All of it entirely auditable, trackable, and consistent.

You’ll be able to take care of business by text, email, chat, or whatever you prefer. No hold times, no transfers, no screw-ups because all the fallout reports funnel into the same repository. Everything is dead easy to trace to completion starting from the conversation itself because nobody had to press buttons in screens in the middle of asking what you need. The report doesn’t have to make assumptions about intermediate steps. All the system handshakes are universal so it’s dead easy to look at the first and last domino in any process and make corrections. If the system is running all of your transactions are recoverable.

I know some of you may be throwing up your hands saying this can’t work because AI doesn’t work that way, but you’re wrong. It *already* works. Part of the way it works means that it can’t talk to you about anything not related to your primary business, but you can think of it as another mode for an LLM to follow. Call it Rigid Rule Mode. Again if you’re thinking we did something to the core models you’re limiting your imagination. I can’t talk about the trick of it too much, but when I was pitching it to one of our lead developers he just started laughing because it’s so simple. You can *feel* like you’re having an open ended conversation when everything behind the scenes is actually carefully controlled.

I’m a bad talker, so I have to work on saying things over and over again to get things in my head to a form people can understand. All of last week, I was giving eight hour a day presentations to senior architects and leaders and it went extremely well. I have another few months of “every waking moment” before things ease a bit. The important part is that I think I’m winning.

There’s no reason the same technique couldn’t be multiplied to other areas or use cases where you need to make sure something is correct while keeping it conversational. You want to talk to your refrigerator about your groceries? Or your television about which shows to watch? Or whatever else where you need to move money? All doable. It’ll take decades to fully do it but the tech works.

And I believe it will scale because it’s auditable.

### Audit Proof AI

Most people think they’re an expert in most things. We’re all born this way and I certainly believed it for a decent chunk of my youth. This belief persists until you undergo go some kind of grueling trial like a doctoral defense, a difficult project, or an adversarial audit.

For me, it was the adversarial audit.

This is when someone gets paid to prove you wrong. In these situations it’s not enough for you to be correct. You have to be *provably* correct to a third party, who if you’re lucky is your boss who hired the auditor, or a government regulator if you’re not so lucky. This is the most “I’m not your friend, guy” scenario you can imagine where the auditor is seen to be doing better the worse they make you look. After I went through enough of these I finally understood a lot of why the world works the way it does. The “CYA” has a much deeper societal impact than the CIA. As a business, you just *can’t* do a lot of things without opening yourself up to lawsuits or regulatory action even if they seem cool and your customers want it.

Not every company has this kind of a problem. If you’re selling bags of dog food at scale and you miss less than one-percent of your orders that’s okay. You give out some coupons and “We’re sorry emails” and you’re fine. In a bank, an insurance company, or a hospital, you can’t really in theory be missing anything. If anyone finds out that you are just going to shrug off an event that happens to one in one-hundred thousand customers, literal hell will descend upon you.

In high regulatory environments every time you sneeze you need a dozen attorneys looking at what you’re about to do.

I am a world class expert in fallout reporting and audit-ready system design. To the point that I literally put so much reporting in front of an auditor once that they started crying and quit their job in the middle of my presentation because they didn’t want to review that much evidence. And I’m always weirdly nice to auditors because I’m always weirdly nice to people that no one else will be nice to! It makes people mad that I get extra nice to people that everyone hates!

I’m sorry humans, but we weren’t meant to be sitting in chairs pressing buttons all day. Humans are always the weak spot in any process. Even the best person ever messes something up once in a few hundred or few thousand iterations.

When I started working on this system I started with data integrity. How do I know what I know, and when do I know that I know it? So much weird stuff can happen when people are pressing buttons and moving through windows. You get weird timing errors, people forget to do screen navigation that ends up being important, someone presses a button in a way you didn’t account for and your whole system just blows up. It’s really hard to recover what was supposed to happen and most of the time it’s impossible.

The best reporting in the world still assumes a button gets pressed somewhere and that the button press was logged. Utterance to transaction reporting is always sample based. Unless the utterance *is* the action.

If you’re a dog food company, missing a half a percent of your orders isn’t great but you can recover from it. If you’re regulated you might have just destroyed the entire quarterly profit in a lawsuit. This is a system where even if something fails in the middle, you can tell 100% of the time what was supposed to happen and make it happen later.

You know how you’ll do something with a company and it just sort of doesn’t happen? That won’t happen anymore.

### Corporations as People, But Creepier

Your children will understand companies to be something like a disembodied spirit that speaks with a particular voice. Every company will choose its own human level voice model and that will be what you speak with when you do business. There are no menus or anything else that drives you crazy. It will feel more like you’re just talking to some guy named Doug who happens to sell HVAC units. You can text him instead if you want. Doug will chit chat about your social media posts if you follow Doug’s Company. It will remind you to replace your air filters. And as Doug eventually takes over all of the customer service functions, Doug is also establishing a protocol for your own AI agent to do this for you in the background in the future.

You won’t ever have to shop for insurance or new air filters or schedule an appointment for an oil change because your AI agent will call every insurance company, or HVAC company, or mechanic shop on your behalf to get you the best price and quality. No one can really stop this because the models themselves are nimble enough to do it. You will pay the cheapest price for every good you regularly consume without having to lift a goddamn finger.

Not because LLMs were exposed to you directly and allowed to make decisions, but because they served as a sort of “language classification and constrained judgement” in the loop that allowed many things to be automated. You almost don’t even know how to book an appointment somewhere you just announce that you want one and it’s all worked out for you.

This will be the counter pressure to mass unemployment. You as the customer will have enormous digital power to drive down the price of every consumer good to the cost of materials. And if you’re saying “yeah right, like everyone could just decide to work together like that” I’d respond, “motherfucker, I can automate boycotting too until the bastards buckle.”

### Trust Assembly Stuff

I’m guessing, realistically, we have about twenty years to get to this kind of future. There will be all kinds of amazing things happening in the middle that are unequally distributed but for *everyone* to feel it you’re looking at ten to twenty years.

I think the Trust Assembly is still super important in this future because you need a shared source of data about human values for all these agents to coordinate. Who decides who to boycott and when? Who decides what’s important as the sort of minimal intent guidance? I’m annoyed that every waking moment of my life is consumed into this but I feel like it’s a chance to show some legitimacy. If it all works that’s a chance to be early and steer the whole system.

The last remaining human jobs will be to be human and to describe what is important to humans. If we have any chance of steering this ship it has to be built. I add this last note as a reminder to myself to move my ass. Written on my phone in the waiting room during my son’s speech therapy appointment.
