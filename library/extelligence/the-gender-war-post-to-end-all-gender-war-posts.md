“We used to say ‘you’re not in traffic, you *are* the traffic.’ That’s what the Gender War was like. Everyone complaining about the opposite sex in public. Blaming everyone else but themselves. Well, I almost think-pieced the mother of my children.” I’m standing on a dais in front of the Gender War Memorial, only a single withered Millennial in a long line of withered Millennials who have come to confess their part in the conflict. I cough, trying to focus on the words even as my hands shake.

“It was back in the twenties and I was trying to be funny. Everyone was always trying to be funny. The whole concept of the essay was that I would lie and reverse the household duties. I would pretend that I was a stay-at-home dad and that she woke up every morning at three o’clock to start her corporate job. Then I’d talk about things like how I had to stay on top of her about her weight and force her onto various diets while I became addicted to buying expensive novelty pajamas for our children. You get the point. I wanted to demonstrate that her life was easy and mine was hard and that if you reversed the genders it would be obvious. Never-you-mind that every life has its own challenges, or that she was up with the baby all night. We told ourselves it was normal to air all of your most intimate relationship details on the internet as long as you loosely attached it to an overarching societal trend, disguising the almost unimaginable violation of trust. Thank God I asked her if it was okay before I posted it. I was so close. My name should be there on the wall of accusers.”

I point my wobbling old man finger at the wall. I’m looking down at my shoes by the end. There are tears in my eyes. I almost don’t hear the polite chime signaling the end of my time. A chorus of young people call out the ritual chant, after my confession —as they will chant with all the others— “this is what happens when you don’t have friends in real life or a community! It’s okay to keep some things to yourself!”

I nod at the wisdom in that.

I step off the dais to make room for the next public confessional. My family waits below to greet me. My two boys. My knees hurt as I go to embrace them. God how I wish their mother was still here to get after me about my diet. I miss her every day.

“How are you feeling, dad?” My oldest boy asks.

Both boys grab my shoulders for support. The grandkids are distracted and didn’t bother to hear my confession. To them this is all self-serious. Nobody died in the Gender Wars, at least not officially. They’ve grown up with restored institutional guardrails to assist in their development. They’ve never known the horrors of situationships, body count discourse, or been the subject of a Think Piece. They don’t have time to listen to their grandpa explain the difference between a Chad and a Stacy or a Cat Lady and an Incel. For all of five-minutes, there was a social trend called “Gram-Glamming” where schoolmates would find thot pictures of their friends grandmas archived on OnlyFans and send them around, but their elders shut it down immediately. The matter was resolved in less than a few weeks. They never lived in a world where adults just said, “well, what can we do about this?” They’re too busy reading all the names on the Gender Wall and laughing in incredulity at the whole thing.

Some of the girls are lining up to take selfies with the giant statue of Cartoons Hate Her. Some of the great writer’s acolytes are wearing cartoon masks and handing out bound copies of her essays titled, “Sometimes Men and Women are Different and Sometimes the Same and Maybe Don’t Panic About This or Listen to Weird People on the Internet, Otherwise I Don’t Know What to Tell You.” They are the earliest writings that signaled the shift, their author the Thomas Paine of the Gender War Revolution. I nod to the statue, respectfully, but do not bow.

“Did you want to read any of the names on the wall, dad? There might be a few you recognize from the olden days.” They lower me into a wheel chair, and I get confused. Eventually, they just do what they want, which is walk me along the Gender Wall. It’s a beautiful day. I’m wearing a leather jacket with a blue Mars symbol stitched on the left breast pocket. I pass by old women wearing the same jacket but with a pink Venus symbol.

All the names our civilization can scour from the internet have been recorded on a three mile long slab of marble. The victims of the Gender War. The two boys who were thoughtlessly think-pieced by their own mother in the pages of the New York Times because they were teenagers and to her great shock, disobedient. The wife who wasn’t trad enough and went viral on social media. The wife who was too trad so it looked like she was faking for the camera. The dad who was too tolerant of clutter and was publicly shamed. The dad who was too strict and tidy and was accused of controlling his family. Name after name, an isthmus of gender slop as far as the eye can see. It’s amazing we didn’t drown in it.

“I remember a little bit from when I was young,” my oldest boy says. “Mom used to listen to videos of common myths about being stay at home mom. Negative stereotypes. Then she’d accuse you of whatever was said in the video even if you hadn’t actually said any of it.”

I nod.

“Happy Wife, Happy Life. Disney Princesses. Colleen Hoover.” I shudder. Terrible memories. As if it was the role of women to become dictatorial if their every demand wasn’t immediately met. As if they alone should hold the burden of measuring the rightness of existence. As if women could only become aroused when they were seducing the husband of a comatose author, who looked liked them but less attractive, and then watched her have sex with the husband while only faking to be comatose, because the fake-comatose writer had also murdered one of her children?

“People were entirely unable to conceive of being wrong in gender matters. It was all personal. None of us could be objective. We had no intimate communities. Everyone of us was an atom, even in our marriages. It was everywhere. The way we talked, it was like we were trying to become separate species. We forgot how much we needed each other.”

“How did it end, grandpa?” One of my grandchildren asks. She’s like a little angel, like her grandma in heaven. I don’t know what I did to be so lucky. I don’t deserve this sweetness.

“Sex robots. Men and women united against a greater enemy,” I said.

“Dad! She doesn’t need to hear that!”

We pass a tall banner, preserved from the old days of Louise Perry holding a boy and a girl child in each arm, underneath the words “No Clankers Shall Kiss Our Cuties!” At her feet, she trampled victoriously upon destroyed robotic machinery. We pass another banner of a genetically modified and incredibly muscular Scott Alexander destroying an army of gothic vampire mommy chat waifus with a flame-thrower underneath the words, “It Seems Like There Should Be a Couple of Rules About This.” Another image blurs by, this one of JK Rowling giving her life to detonate the EMP Blast that saved the world from being overtaken by a super-intelligent nanobot Gender Fluid. Her banner had only five letters, “For Jo.” Lastly, Jordan Peterson, crying as ever, lowering the entire literary contributions of Colleen Hoover into a mountain to be buried under five miles of concrete. The words, “Let No AI Optimize This Murder Porn.”

“Whole cohorts of young people were consumed. It finally got bad enough we did something. Parents banded together and pushed back against the Clankers! And when the war was done, social change followed. Whenever someone said something that wasn’t true about statistics, every single cellphone or Alexa, or intelligent listening device would go on a long, mandatorily excruciating, fifteen minute lecture about how they didn’t know what they were talking about and you have to judge every individual on their own merits, while also still holding people individually accountable so as not lie to them or others to balance out the appearance of an average person that doesn’t actually exist. We stopped trying to create naive equality and started asking people what they truly wanted, and tracking their success at achieving it.”

There is a statue of the average person in the darkness, fair of face, but one-breasted and one-testicled, a demon holding a pitchfork. The Average. He is surrounded by the actual people, men and women, who had been combined to make him, except now they held shields keeping him held at bay. If you looked closely, the Average had slightly less than two eyes, ten fingers, and ten toes. How many had suffered trying to appease this false deity?

“We created designated Meet Cute Mondays, Wednesdays and Sundays, in Aisle eleven of any store you could go and meet someone. One and one, making two, get it? A single woman would wear pink. A single man, blue. And if you liked the person you would ask them where to find something and if they liked you they’d offer to go help you look. If they didn’t they’d say they didn’t know. You could always pretend they weren’t in on it. Because we recognized, and this is extremely important, that it’s super fucking weird to just sort of like throw yourself out there for immediate binary evaluation like you’re a PowerPoint slide someone swipes on, and the junior high system of having your friend approach the friends of a prospective partner to feel things out is actually a super advanced civilization tool. So, we created rituals like that where people could pretend to plausibly not be putting themselves up for rejection. Wheelchair semaphore, where handicapped people could display their relationship status and preferences depending on where they tied up and positioned their legs. Things my parents couldn’t have even imagined like Arranged Gay Marriage, where the parents of gays and lesbians trade genetic material on the cheap with turkey basters to produce grandchildren. Top minds got involved in the creation of children. Top minds.”

One of my other grandchildren, or is it the same one, climbs into my lap. “Were you a hero in the Gender War, grandpa?”

I rub a tear out of my old eyes.

“No, but I wrote about myself like I was a hero.”

We arrive at the wall where my sweet wife’s name is displayed. I smile, remembering her hilarious shit posting in various facebook groups while breastfeeding our children. I pay a kid for some flowers and lay them there and look up at the sky, smiling. There are so many children running about, having fun, laughing. None of them would have been possible if we hadn’t learned how to get along again. Every individual person facing and conquering some small part of their own darkness and bitterness. I wonder when I will get to see my wife again. We were never enemies, not even when she spouted her angry gypsy curses at me because I wanted to write weird substack articles that mentioned her.

I can see now the right way was to be grateful for the joy she brought to my life. For the unique points of view. For her being her. The Gender War was only a way to distract ourselves from the vulnerability of loving another human being. I love my wife, and I miss her. Soon, my fight will be over and we’ll see each other again.
