### The Short List of Things I am Succeeding At

I’m a good dad, or at least I hope so.

It feels like every facet of my life revolves around my children, so my fingers are crossed that’s the same thing as being a good dad. One thing I never got from my own parents was a sense that I was important to them personally, so I try to give as much of that to my children as possible. If they need something, nothing else is more important. For the eyebrow-raisers, I include in that things like, “needing to be told no and experience appropriate boundaries.”

I’ll move or cancel meetings with people who have super fancy titles without a second thought if the boys need me. This sounds like a bigger sacrifice than it is. What I’ve learned with business stuff is that there’s always a workaround, and very rarely does one missed meeting ever result in anything catastrophic. The pressure can be immense, but I suppose everyone in charge of a large group of people has to learn to do this and balance obligations. As this has increasingly become the case for me, it’s a lesson I’ve had to take on continuously.

I take on big work assignments and projects in order to build reputation so I can translate that into making more money for our family. I wake up early in the morning to dole out assignments for a work group in Chennai a few hours into their shift, and then I log off when the East Coasters are wrapping up. I’m doing my best to be available across two continents and it’s mostly working even if my brain feels like it’s melting after each daily machine-gun spray of back-to-back meetings. For my children it’s only 3:00pm when dad is suddenly available and that makes starting my day at 3:00am worth it.

My oldest boy is autistic, although we’re in the middle of the formal diagnostic process. His pediatrician believes he’s going to be type III, meaning that he will require very substantial support, although I’m thinking he might develop his way into type I, or high-functioning. Most of my time not working is spent on this. I take him to a few specialists each week to work on language development, although most of this comes in the form of playing with him and trying to interrupt whatever he’s doing so he has to deal with the existence of other people. If autism is a disorder in the economization of attention, forcing him to pay regular attention and respond to other attention-paying entities while his brain is still developing seems like the best therapy.

If I read him a story he gets interested or scared in all the right places. Same with movies. That’s why I think he might end up type one. A lot of the capabilities are there. It’s like there’s some sensory processing layer in the middle that needs some fine-tuning so he can coordinate with what he knows. Hopefully all of this stuff we do with him will help him fine-tune those knobs early so he has a better outcome than otherwise. Two days ago I was sick on the couch and he put a blanket on top of me, so I know there’s some social attention in there.

His brother is already running around the house shouting “Yes!” or “No!” at everything. He spouts out words for when he wants something. All those language milestones are right on track. I try to get them to play together but success on that front has been largely non-existent. The little guy is willing but Dutch is in a world of his own, and that world has no babies in it. Hopefully this changes as his brother seems like a huge fan.

Insofar as I can tell, I’m doing a good job here.

I also seem to be on the frontier of Enterprise AI automation. Much to my surprise I’m genuinely annoyed by the level of ignorance I encounter on the subject, which I guess makes me an expert. When I get into the same room as a developer who is an expert at creating AI models, I find that he[1](#footnote-1) has almost all the exact wrong intuitions for how to solve business problems and that much to my surprise if I review code I couldn’t write, I’m able to greatly increase accuracy and effectiveness. The Venn Diagram of people who can make AI models and people who can deploy AI models for business value is not the near perfect circle I thought it would be.

The way this usually works is that I will say seven or eight things about how the code has to work that drive the developer crazy, but then I explain reporting and audit responsibilities and he slowly believes and accepts that it’s all necessary. Then I rewrite some prompts and say the code needs to be in a different order, which he says isn’t going to do anything but then it works. And I explain some of the stuff I’ve written here about computer psychology which he will say is all wrong, except it keeps leading to the right intuitions to solve problems.

The whole corporate world is in in something like the period where the temperature of water reaches boiling but remains a liquid. A certain amount of input is needed before the particles can break free and become a gas. All the energy is going into upskilling developers en masse. There’s no such thing as the skillset to deploy LLM’s to automate tasks. It’s not taught in any schools. It’s taking a while for everyone to accept things like “yes, you really do have to do juries or ensembles or whatever you want to call it at all of these different steps” and “no, it’s not exactly the same as an API call and your prompt actually does have to explain the problem in a clear and direct manner unless you’re doing something that has many different examples on the internet” even the dreaded, “you actually need to explain this to a subject matter expert and make sure you’re thinking about this seemingly simple thing the correct way.” As always, data pipelines are a huge bottleneck that no one wants to invest in because it doesn’t have a sleek UI you can put in front of a managing director.

I also seem to have wholly invented a technique we’re calling “Fragments” but which will probably one day be called “Intermediate Language.” If you combine fragments with certain state machine architecture reliability becomes much, much higher. This was the innovation I made by walking around starting at the sky a lot, wondering how the hell I was going to make this thing work.

My already packed calendar of about 120 meetings per week has skyrocketed to 130 meetings per week, which doesn’t sound like much of a difference but it’s only because I declined the other 120, for which I am still responsible and delegated one of my direct reports to handle all of them. Don’t worry, she took the rest of my team too. I think she’ll get a promotion for it next year. I still handle all of the escalations. I haven’t seen the bottom of my inbox in six months.

It’s a sacrifice of time and sanity, but if this all works out I think I’ll have enough money at the end of ten years that I won’t have to worry about how my son will support himself if he stays around type III.

I wouldn’t say this keeps me up at night, but it’s definitely something I’m always thinking about in the background.

### The Long List of Things I Suck At

What do I suck at? Keeping on of this substack for one. I used to do all of my best writing for this place between three in the morning and six in the morning. I used to have a strong rule that I wouldn’t have any meetings before six in the morning. That rule is broken every single day now. I’m sorry for the lack of updates.

I don’t blame anyone who has canceled their paying subscription. I’m attempting to make changes in my situation that should produce a bunch of wins all around and give this time back to me and to you. I like writing on here. I just don’t have the hours to throw at it after work and family. If I sleep less than five hours a night, I’m not useful to anyone.

Another thing I suck at is the Trust Assembly. I keep trying to talk myself out of this one, and I keep not being able to. I don’t know what a good future for people looks like in the world of infinite AI slop and automation if something like this doesn’t get built. I don’t even know what a modern world without something serving as a communal memory and trust signal looks like in the long term. Unfortunately, every day when people tell me that, no, *this* idea is ridiculous and I’m not making sense it turns out that I’m correct. So even though the idea seems crazy to some people, I would have to be hyper-particularly crazy in this one particular way to be wrong. It’s unfortunate that I’m forty, work eighty hours a week, and have a child with special needs. Absolutely no progress has been made on it this year. Maybe over the holidays I can make some kind of education effort on it.

The other place I’m failing is my health. I’m feeling a bit crispy and know I’ll need to make some changes soon. A few weeks ago I realized I hadn’t drunk any water in the previous few days and had only had diet coke to keep me focused on my work. I was drinking around six diet cokes a day for the caffeine. If I can liken this to alcoholism for comedic effect, I’m clean now. A step on the scale also revealed I’ve gained about thirty pounds over the last few months because I’m sitting in a chair all day and not moving. So now everyone who reports into me is having to contend with their boss on a zoom call bobbing back and forth on a desk treadmill.

I get why leaders become so eccentric now. If you don’t have a spare moment to yourself, you have to find a way to just survive and all of your bullshit is going to leak out because you don’t have any place to hide. No wonder Elon is nuts because I’m feeling it and I’m only building four large applications.

I also haven’t been the best husband, because my wife and I’s joint time is all spent on the kids and it’s hard enough to find time for us that we generally don’t ever do this. Still, if you just don’t give up and hunker down, you’ll get through whatever comes your way. Inch by inch, move toward the daylight. It’s hard to find someone who can watch our oldest boy and not feel totally overwhelmed, but my wife’s mom is sometimes up to the task if we don’t go far. I’ll try to take her to more regular dinner dates next year.

But man, some days do I feel like a tube of toothpaste where someone has squeezed out all the toothpaste. Anyway, I take all the pain as feedback. I’ll figure it out and just won’t give up. You can learn and do a lot by just not giving up.
