Kurt Cobain made the world famous decision to kill himself over thirty years ago, when I was in the second grade. I remember this vividly because his much younger stepsister was my classmate. She stayed home from school that day, but something like a dozen news crews circled outside the building hoping to get a shot of her entering or leaving. All of us kids got our pictures taken by eager photographers in case we happened to be “the one.” That’s probably what Kurt’s mom was trying to spare his sister from, and she stayed home from school for a few months until the story cooled off. Kurt’s death shook the whole town and it’s still a decently popular topic of conversation.

Later, when my brother became a paperboy to Kurt’s grandfather, I saw what that kind of world-famous grief had done up close. I always accompanied my brother on collection days so he wouldn’t get robbed, and Leland would always try to pull us inside and give us Polaroids of Kurt as a baby. We never accepted, which I think was a sort of test, but before he paid his bill Leland would rant for however long we stayed that Courtney had been the one ultimately responsible. He wanted us both to know, as grade school kids, that Kurt would never have killed himself. Never. Courtney killed Kurt. He knew. He wanted to make sure we knew.

My brother was his paper boy for years and this was more or less every interaction. I don’t know that Leland ever really had a waking moment after Kurt’s death where he wasn’t thinking about his grandson.

This was the ugliest intrusion, into what has to be one of the most private pains, I have ever directly witnessed. It’s one thing to lose someone you loved. It’s another to have your tragedy be a global news juggernaut that extracts your private grief into public spectacle so you have to constantly explain it to strangers to satisfy their curiosity.

When I think about the assassination of Charlie Kirk, I think his family must be experiencing that same tension. You want loss to be like a movie. There will be a cut scene at any moment, you’ll be in a different setting, time will have passed, and somewhere offscreen you became “better” without having to live through all the pain. Except the cut scene never happens. Moments keep connecting and you keep having to slog your way through them one struggling heartbeat at a time. You have to figure out how not to dwell. For Charlie Kirk’s family I imagine it was a lot of commotion and confusion, a trip to the hospital, the knowledge that he’s suddenly gone, and then the surreal experience of the president and every other important person in the world trying to say something to you. I hope their loved ones are sheltering them from all the people celebrating. Movies about grief feel more real than whatever the sensation is of sitting at an iHop several months after your loss and wondering how the hell Belgian waffles connect to your personal pain.

There’s nothing to be said to such red hot pain that can make it go away. It would be like trying to drop a cold glass of water directly onto an iron skillet pulled straight from a fire. Silence, space, a few platitudes, the invisible doing of chores is about the best you can do. Yet their grief is so big and so visible the rest of public discourse orbits around it like dust around a star. I do pray and I prayed for Charlie’s wife and children. I encourage you to do so as well.

I know I hem and haw on politics but… well, I’ll be honest and direct on the things I feel most firmly. I think most politics is just kind of stupid. Consequential, don’t get me wrong, but stupid. Ridiculous. People show up politically in a way that they would never dream of showing up professionally. I don’t even really believe that most people have actual foundational beliefs, and I think most people are entirely unaware of this. A lot of politics boils down to to nothing more than “I would like things to be better and not worse. And no, don’t ask me how. I just want it to happen.” It isn’t at all surprising to me that someone might have a political stance than myself, and I detest the instinct to look up the worst things Charlie Kirk has ever done in a decade long career and apologize for them before condemning his murder. For all my other failings, politics is a place where I don’t have to try very hard to find an ocean of grace and understanding for other people.

Charlie Kirk was going about the business of talking through his ideas in the exact right way. He championed the free and open exchange of ideas. In this sense, I am a supporter of him unreservedly. You could not have asked for a better champion of this central idea. That is my most fundamental belief and the one I don’t have any problem being firm on. People have to be able to talk to each other honestly and not have their entire life destroyed because they’re trying to express their honestly considered moral view of the world. You can’t even have something like a sincerely held morality if you can’t do that in society. He was a common fixture in my household because my wife, who is much more interested in these sorts of political shows than I am, was a follower of his work. I’ve agreed with many of the things he’s said, but above all else his unapologetic championship of the free and open exchange of ideas. The fact that he was young, that he was a father to young children, and that he was a person putting himself forward to bridge the political divide makes his loss especially tragic.

I found myself becoming much more emotional at the images of him holding his wife and children, or the videos of him fathering his daughter, than the vulgar spectacle of him being shot in the neck. Everyone is somebody’s baby, and Charlie was also a husband and a dad. It’s bad when people get killed. The only time it’s remotely “okay” to kill someone is when they’re trying to kill you and there’s no other choice. Charlie Kirk wasn’t trying to kill anybody.

I can’t imagine what his children’s experience of this nation will be, growing up knowing their dad was killed in front of them and that parts of the country cheered that it happened. Whether or not those kids view their father’s death as a tragedy restricted to a certain period of time or an ever-present madness lurking at the heart of the country will tell us whether or not we are succeeding or failing as a people. I’d prefer a future where those children, as broken-hearted as they may forever be, are still proud of the way their country responded to the tragedy.

I hope the assassin is found quickly, but I do want to call out the degree to which this is the fault of the whole country. The part of you that shirks away in fear when an assertive lunatic invades your space on the internet is at root here. We’re living in the political fallout of people’s resentment of that fear. The part of you that sits down and says, “I don’t need to tell people who mostly agree with me that that they’re going too far” also allows extremism to take hold. We all want our quiet tidy lives where we don’t have to worry about what our neighbors are doing, but you can’t have a society unless you’re engaged with your neighbors. We’re all responsible for the way things are.

You all know my views on this. Without a large scale systemic solution for how we view content online that incorporates enlightenment principles, this will only get worse and worse. I’m doing my best to get something like that built. If it helps, I don’t even want to make money for it. I just want our country to work again. You should also want that, and you need to push forward your own ideas as well and be a vocal supporter of free expression.

I don’t know what happens next. My belief since the Trump assassination attempt and the assassination of the United Healthcare CEO is that we’re entering a period like the 1970’s where political violence will become more common. Even if people can’t articulate what they want, they clearly want “not this” and the craziest in the mob will feel emboldened to take the assassin’s veto. What we all need to do to end this, which is sit down and figure out what changes we need to make that will cause the world to make fundamentally more sense without violating anyone’s ethical code, hasn’t started happening yet. I’m going to push harder on some of my ideas, because as crazy as this sounds I think I’m correct. I think I personally know what we need to do to make the world better in a way that your craziest aunts and uncles would mostly agree with. In the spirit of Charlie Kirk, I’ll do more to put myself out there and talk through them.

I’m as heartbroken as you. There’s so much blood lately. One of the children killed at the Annunciation shooting had the same name as my son and I haven’t stopped thinking about him since it happened. Similar feelings on the subway stabbing which I don’t expect to lead to real policy changes. Or any of the other killings. In times like this it’s hard to say much that means anything to anyone. It’s like the sun went out.

But when things get dark, I think that’s when the Christian virtue of hope is most important. When you’re in the dark, you still have to make all the right steps like the moral light is still there, shining brightly with the path clear before you. You have to keep faith that rightness still exists. It is still there, only hidden by a cloud. God didn’t go away because you can’t see Him in a particular moment. All tradition is only an approximation of people and cultures following this moral dance through the dark, finding the next right step to move in time with the music. The good is still real. Love is still real. People still matter. Even when you can’t see it right away.

There’s an ugly demon making its way through the world and it wants blood. It’s our job to stand up and banish it. But light always banishes the dark, however hard it might be to remember when the lights go out. We’ve gone through the dark before and come out. We will find a way toward a future where things like this don’t happen.
