I’m writing this piece to tell you about one of the greatest literary accomplishments of the present generation. We shall start this journey together with the explicit absence of a joke. It was going to be quite a long joke having to do with Space Romans and a made up language called Old High Italian. I can’t bring myself to write it.

I am a naturally crooked and profane man, from a naturally crooked and profane culture. My favorite uncle is Italian. I have developed such a powerful Italian joke-making reflex over the years that suppressing it feels like forcing my leg to stay still after a doctor strikes my patellar tendon. My only explanation is that certain rules are transcendent. One does not belch in the company of a lady, or utter obscenities in front of a child, or produce flatulence at a funeral. The work I am about to describe makes the mere thought of indulging in some kind of cheap joke something akin to standing before a council of devil-worshippers and spitting on a picture of Jesus.

With solemn dignity, I now arrive on your screen to tell you about the Sun Eater series by Christopher Ruocchio. These books are not only a masterwork of science-fiction but what can only be described as good. Good as in reading them will not only entertain you and demonstrate mastery of craft, or provide you with meaningful moral instruction and scaffolding, but also that they will reignite in you the belief that our present generation is capable of creating greatness. In the seven volume series, Ruocchio has crafted a story that in hindsight will be understood as one of the first fully realized and worthy narrative considerations of moral philosophy in the age of artificial intelligence.

Christopher Ruocchio, the Italian American Author of the Sun Eater, is a young man. At the time of this writing, I believe him to be thirty two. In other words, he is a Millennial. He began writing the Sun Eater when he was only twenty-one. This is apparent in the series. The first book of the series, Empire of Silence, is merely serviceable. It’s not a terrible book by any means and many much older authors would view it as their finest work, but it’s only great if you remind yourself that it was written by a young college student.

I would argue the series as a whole is much better for this fact. The reality of this skill-curve in the text is that it allows you to experience two journeys as the story unfolds. Readers get to experience both the obvious narrative of a young man rising up to change the fate of the galaxy, and the visceral but much less remarked upon journey of a young author finding his voice. By the time Ruocchio hits the third volume of the series, you understand that you have been witnessing the slow birth of a literary genius. You were reading the Lord of the Rings before people understood it was “the Lord of the Rings,” or Dune before people understood it was “Dune.” What this means is invaluable. We all aren’t on our cell phones. We’re not all writing slop. Our generation still has the focus and ingenuity to achieve artistic depth. Our culture is still capable of producing a Tolkien-level talent. We can be assured, as every generation must be assured, that the making of fire has not been lost.

I intend to send this review to every high profile person I know. If you are such a person and don’t have time to witness this growth, start with the Demon in White and simply give Ruocchio a few pages. If you give him a few pages, you will give him a hundred, and if you give him a hundred you will be compelled to read the rest of the series. It will not have been a waste of your time.

There is not one “Big Question” that Ruocchio fails to meaningfully address over the course of the series, from the relationship of science and faith, to the meaning of life, to the heat death of the universe. Although the series has begun to grow in popularity thanks to glowing reviews on BookTube, it is still far less popular than it deserves. It is also differently popular than it deserves. These are more than just good books.

I do not believe the Sun Eater could have been accomplished in the way it has been accomplished if it had been fully supported. I say that as someone who is genuinely annoyed when artists have to struggle. Even its publisher, Penguin, went so far as to force Ruocchio to quickly split the fourth book into two volumes and subsequently canceled the series after the fifth book. It is only through the heroic intervention of the BookTube community that Ruocchio was able to gain a large enough readership to not only secure a new publisher but to lure Penguin back to the table for the final volume. I can’t imagine what it was like to create this work under those conditions. That Ruocchio succeeded in spite of this has me suppressing the instinct to grab people by the lapels of their jackets and shout in their faces that the curse of Rothfuss and Martin has been broken and the spring of science fiction and fantasy is upon us all.

I don’t know that a reader can truly appreciate the scope of Ruocchio’s accomplishment unless they are broadly familiar with the works of antiquity, classical literature, the major works of the golden ages of science fiction and fantasy, the Bible including a decent chunk of theological literature, let’s be honest Gene Wolfe, and a wide swath of Stargate related media. This familiarity, however, is by no means required and each quote or deliberate misquote is always placed in relevant context. The series is in open and deft conversation with every piece of science fiction that came before it. Ruocchio alludes without ever stealing, referencing common touchstones to help the media-laden reader arrive at startling conclusions and break new ground. Each concept he touches, Ruocchio changes enough to make his own and never surrenders plot, theme, or motivation in the juggling act. Each reference a sign post, to say “you’ve been here, now look over there.”

The Sun Eater follows the biography of Hadrian Marlowe, the prince of a far distant world, and the son of a cruel Archon whose chief ambition is to rise above the station of a lowly planetary lord whose domain is a mere continent. In an empire with billions of worlds and quadrillions of citizens, to be the absolute ruler of a mere continent is almost nothing. In this universe, the singularity and its horrifying conclusion is ancient history. Humanity learned the hard way that infinite unconstrained power is infinitely destabilizing, and so the remnants of society built its new empire around the goal of unending sameness. To that end, humanity revived something like the Roman Empire among the stars to prevent ourselves from ever doing something so dangerous ever again, and for tens of thousands of years and at the price of billions of deaths it has ruthlessly succeeded.

The ruling elite are genetically enhanced to be long-lived, but their genes have been edited in a manner so as to be cryptographically locked. It is impossible for a ruling “Palatine” to have a child naturally without the risk of severe mutation, requiring the direct intervention of the Empire to produce healthy offspring. In this way, the Sollan Empire holds billions of young royals hostage and ties every world to the throne. Hadrian is one such Palatine, who we meet on his home world of Delos before he is twenty years old. By the time the series ends, Hadrian is over a thousand years old. While no man has lived even two centuries, Ruocchio somehow creates an authentic-feeling experience of aging that spans over centuries. You will find yourself nodding along in places, as if in agreement, “Ah yes, that’s what it feels like to be five-hundred.”

Apart from this, Ruocchio’s accomplishment is to cut through the noise of politics and plot, never allowing himself to be so distracted by “Making a Point,” that he forgets to strike at the moral heart of humanity. As the series progresses, Hadrian encounters every possible kind of society and person and not one of them is ever shown to be “the best” or without downsides. Hadrian’s native empire is fascistic and controlling, but also the only possible structure able to keep humans human in the face of infinite technology. Likewise, the transhumanist extrasollarans who live between the stars are more “free” in a certain sense, but without structure are subject to unimaginable biological horrors. So do the Lothrians have “equality” but in a world in which science can make men equal in a way that is an obvious abomination. In each case we find, always, that the dividing line between good and evil lives nowhere but the individual human heart. For even those humans who have rejected their own humanity, like the fifteen thousand year old villainous cyborg Kharn Sagara, who has pretended to itself that a mere change in its biology is capable of changing the mortal condition, is still inescapably human.

Our hero begins life as an idealist. Raised primarily by a tutor, Gibson, Hadrian is a youth full of ideas. News comes to Delos of the terrible Cielcin, a race of nightmarish eaters, who travel on moon-sized warp ships from planet to planet, consuming all of its inhabitants before disappearing again into the dark. Hadrian’s first thought when finding out there is a race of aliens who have developed a taste for human flesh is that he can find a way to make peace with them. He goes so far as to be one of the few humans to learn their language.

As Hadrian ages and witnesses the horrors of the Cielcin firsthand, Ruocchio is brave enough and subtle enough to keep the reader from collapsing into simplistic notions of right and wrong even as no punches are pulled. Make no mistake, this is not a story where the hero encounters the villain and finds out the conflict was all a big misunderstanding. There is not some single simple thing Hadrian must do in order to reform the Cielcin race. The Cielcin know what they’re doing. There is no trivial error of understanding or translation that gives an easy out. What the Cielcin do is evil. Their entire culture, from top to bottom, is fanatical and nihilist. And yet…

As the stakes grow higher, Hadrian discovers it is his destiny to deliver something like divine retribution against the entire Cielcin species. Yet if Hadrian destroys the Cielcin, he will not only kill everything they are in the present moment, but everything they might ever possibly become. Everything that they were in history. How can this be the only way? How can this be righteous?

One is forced to recall a story of a man beseeching God to spare his city if he can but find ten righteous men. And as the reader begins to consider these things, the series switches from asking what mankind will become to asking who should be permitted to decide.

And here enters the God of Abraham, but into a setting where at first neither Hadrian nor the reader has any reference to the nature of the entity communicating with our protagonist. God and Cosmic scale are brought side by side in the books in a way that I have never seen. One day, this future Space Roman who believes he knows everything there is to know about antiquity, receives a vision of the future from God. In Hadrian’s vision, he fires a weapon into a star that destroys an entire solar system. Then, he hears a voice simply saying “this must be.”

If you were worried I have spoiled the series, this information is given on the first page. I cannot overstate how well this is handled. Even knowing the ending can’t pull the punch from all the various surprises. The appearance of God is by no means blatant or heavy-handed. It is not until you are several books into the series that you even understand you are reading something that touches on the divine. Every bit of the story is earned, so that by the time the revelation is complete, it is the only possible and appropriate answer. The Sun Eater is not only a speculation on the future of humanity, but a speculative continuation of the Biblical narrative itself. If God is real, then He is the God of stars and time and reason, and on matters of the survival of entire sentient species, God is not silent. When creation itself is at stake, God anoints a prophet. In the Sun Eater, that prophet is Hadrian Marlowe, even if he isn’t quite sure what that means.

All Hadrian knows is that he has seen something. An entity has spoken to him and lifted the veil of time. Something has shown him bits of the future. He presumes it to be some sort of advanced alien lifeform. I won’t spoil the many twists and turns the story takes, but at several points Hadrian witnesses direct intervention by a divine hand that never feels cheated or forced. It is simply weird. These divine interventions are at first as mysterious to the reader as they are to Hadrian. Even by the time the series ends, even as the voice of God quotes Job, Hadrian never truly understands he’s been communicating with the God of Abraham.

Hadrian eschews the cinematic protagonist mold, in favor of something more like the role of Moses. He is a man touched by fate, by history, but there is blood on his hands as the series concludes. Not because that blood was the intended will of the divine, but simply because Hadrian is doing his best to follow that divine will and is not fully adequate to the task. He knows this and knows that there is little he can do to change his fate. As Moses struck twice, as Moses was denied entrance to the Promised Land, so too is Hadrian only a flawed man doing his best, as are all men who change history.

There are, of course, things I would change about the series. I would change the Lord of the Rings to remove razing of the Shire, for that matter, and I would have told Tolkien he was making several large mistakes in the way he was wrapping things up. And I would be wrong in both cases. Art is art, in part because it is a sort of proof that minds exist other than our own. That other minds can be better than your own. I could not have written the Sun Eater, nor could you, nor could anyone besides Christopher Ruocchio. I could not be him, and I could not walk through the many challenges and battles he clearly walked through and won to deliver this final masterpiece.

If it was not already abundantly clear, I strongly encourage you to read it.
