By Jóna Ákadóttir

In July, 2024, China made a surprising move at a Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) preparatory meeting in Geneva. It dropped a 32-page report accusing the U.S. of violating the NPT through its long-standing nuclear sharing arrangements.

Though framed as a legal analysis, the report was political in nature: an attempt to weaken confidence in the U.S. extended deterrence in Europe and, by extension, to cast doubt on current and future U.S. nuclear guarantees in Asia. The problem is that the report gets the law wrong, the history wrong, and even the footnotes wrong.

What’s the Chinese Argument?

The Chinese report accuses the U.S of violating Articles I and II of the NPT through NATO nuclear sharing. These are the parts of the Treaty that prohibit transfer of nuclear weapons or control over them, to non-nuclear weapon states. The report also claims the practice undermines the Treaty’s credibility, is outdated, and raises risks. The accusations target the stationing of U.S. nuclear bombs in a handful of allied countries like Germany, Italy, and the Netherlands.

China’s core arguments:

  1. Parking U.S. nukes in NATO countries is a special form of proliferation.

  2. Treaty rules still apply, even to old arrangements.

  3. Some countries didn’t know about nuclear sharing when the NPT was drafted.

  4. The NPT remains binding in wartime, so any future transfer of control would be a violation.

  5. NATO nuclear sharing is outdated and damages the credibility of the NPT.

Debunking the arguments

  1. Nuclear sharing isn’t nuclear transfer

China argues that the stationing of U.S. nuclear weapons in allied countries is the same as handing them over.

Despite the name, NATO nuclear sharing doesn't involve transferring ownership of nuclear weapons. Instead, it refers to the strategic placement of these weapons across allied countries in Europe for their possible use in nuclear war. It’s about cohesion in the alliance, not control. Under NATO nuclear sharing, U.S. nuclear bombs stored in Europe are always under American lock and key. Allies may train on delivery systems but cannot launch nuclear weapons without direct U.S. authorization.

Proliferation is like handing someone the keys to someone else's car. It means giving another country nuclear weapons or the power to use them. NATO’s arrangements do neither. The U.S. is merely parking some of its weapons in Europe to fulfil U.S. security guarantees. It is not proliferation under any possible reading of the NPT.

The Chinese report calls this a “special form” of proliferation. But it is a form practiced by the two biggest nuclear weapon states, including the Soviet Union during the Cold War and more recently, Russia with its 2023 deployment of nuclear weapons in Belarus. Curiously, China’s report does not seem to find that particular form problematic.

  1. The Treaty covered nuclear sharing from the start

China argues that NATO nuclear sharing can’t be grandfathered in just because they began before 1970, the year the NPT entered into force. This is true in principle, but beside the point as no one is claiming a free pass based on timing.

China is arguing against a point no one is trying to make.

NATO nuclear sharing was not swept under the rug during NPT negotiations. It was explained, debated, and explicitly accommodated. Debates and criticism concerning which states should have nuclear weapons are valid, but from its very inception, the two co-chairs of the Treaty talks, the U.S. and USSR, understood that nuclear sharing did not constitute proliferation, and thus drafted the treaty with nuclear sharing in mind, building it into the language of Articles I and II. The practice was a feature of the NPT, not a bug.

China’s stance on this argument suggests an ulterior motive.

  1. Nuclear sharing was no secret

One of the stranger claims in the Chinese report is that some states signing the NPT - Sweden and Canada specifically - didn’t know about NATO nuclear sharing.

This is historically inaccurate. NATO nuclear sharing was public knowledge. It was debated in parliaments, dissected in the media, and scrutinized in U.N. committee rooms. From the NATO Paris Summit Declaration in 1957 to the establishment of the NATO Nuclear Planning Group in 1966, allies openly publicized and defended the role of U.S. nuclear weapons in the defence of Europe.

Newspapers from the era covered it extensively. Academic journals debated it. The United Nations recorded it. If Sweden or Canada or anyone else was truly unaware, it was not due to NATO secrecy. It was because they weren’t reading The New York Times.

The Chinese report’s core claims about Sweden and Canada rely heavily on a single article written in 2000 and were debunked long ago. This becomes obvious when looking at the footnotes of the report. It repeats the same typos, including citing the wrong volume of the U.S. State Department’s records, in the exact same, pre-internet format. China didn’t just copy the argument, it copied the footnotes without even checking them.

  1. The NPT does not apply in the case of World War III

China also tries to argue that if a nuclear war with NATO breaks out, handling nuclear weapons by the NATO non-nuclear states would violate the NPT. If nuclear war breaks out, the Treaty has, in a tragic sense, failed its main goal and has been rendered effectively void. It exists to prevent that scenario, not to regulate it.

During the Treaty’s drafting, this was widely understood. The U.S. and USSR agreed that the NPT would not bind states during general war, and the other nations in the negotiations understood. Article 10 even includes a withdrawal clause for countries facing threats to their “supreme national interests”. If nuclear war doesn’t qualify, it is hard to imagine what would. China knows this, but is directing attention towards NATO during a time of its own nuclear expansion.

  1. Nuclear sharing has prevented, not caused, proliferation

The final Chinese argument is that NATO nuclear sharing is obsolete and undermines the NPT. But if this is what treaty erosion looks like, we should all be so lucky.

Since 1970, the NPT has become the most supported arms control agreement in history. The NPT did not survive despite NATO’s posture, it thrived alongside it, expanding, enduring and helping stop more states from building their own nuclear weapons, more effectively than any other treaty or arrangement.

NATO nuclear sharing has been one of the quiet engines of non-proliferation. Countries like West Germany, Italy, Sweden, and South Korea all explored nuclear programs and backed down. Not because they lost interest, but because the U.S. nuclear umbrella gave them a credible alternative.

Since the establishment of NATO nuclear sharing, France is the only ally that went nuclear – and did so because the U.S. would not share enough, not because it shared too much.

Today, according to the Federation of American Scientists latest study, only half a dozen countries host nuclear weapons, down from more than 30 during the Cold War and NATO’s posture has been shrinking for decades, not expanding. In fact, the NPT has more members than ever. Not a single state has cited NATO nuclear sharing as the reason for leaving or staying out.

In short: NATO’s arrangements may be old: but that is not the same as broken. And if anything, the past 70 years show that they have done more to prevent proliferation than provoke it.

So why is China making this argument now?

Because this isn’t about NATO.

China is worried that the U.S. might extend NATO nuclear sharing to Japan or South Korea - and ultimately Taiwan. With North Korea expanding its arsenal and tensions rising over Taiwan, calls in Japan and South Korea for stronger U.S. nuclear guarantees are growing louder.

By targeting the U.S. nuclear arrangements with NATO, China is trying to prevent and discredit future arrangements that have not been proposed.

The timing is no coincidence. China is in the midst of an unprecedented and completely non-transparent nuclear buildup, all while refusing to enter into arms control negotiations or disclose its arsenal size. Shifting focus to NATO’s decades-old arrangements provides a useful distraction.

This is about narrative control. It is a calculated move to limit U.S. deterrence options in Asia without having to say so directly.

But if that is the plan, it is not a particularly clever one. Undermining nuclear sharing does not strengthen non-proliferation. NATO nuclear sharing has withstood legal, political, and strategic tests for decades. It was designed to prevent proliferation and it has done exactly that.

Trying to discredit it now does not weaken the model. Instead, China’s effort may risk increasing proliferation, and it definitely weakens the credibility of those repeating the argument.
