Throughout the 8th century CE, the land of England was divided into a patchwork of Christian kingdoms, including East Anglia, Mercuria, North Umbrea, and Wessix. Their rulers fought over land, wealth, prestige, and power, making and breaking alliances as it suited them. The most formidable of these kings was Offer of Messia, who ruled the Midlands and the Southeast with an iron fist.

Yet it was during his reign that pirates first arrived on England's shores. In 789, three ships came ashore at Portland in Wessex. When word reached Beeder, the Reeve of Dorchester, he rode out to inspect them. Assuming they were traders, he ordered them to follow him to the town to make their presence known.

Yet to Beer's astonishment, his orders were refused. He and his men were attacked and killed on the spot. This was the first act of violence in a long age of terror and bloodshed that would forever change Britain. Four years later, these raiders struck again, storming the holy island of Lindesvan and raiding its monastery, one of the most sacred sites in Britain.

Its relics were smashed up, its silver robbed, and most of its monks were either slain or dragged off in chains to the great slave markets beyond the sea. These terrifying raiders had braved the North Sea in shallow long ships from the forests, fjords, and fishing villages of Scandinavia. Scandinavia at the time was divided into a series of pagan kingdoms, eldoms, and chieftains. The region faced significant challenges.

In modern-day Norway, there was overpopulation and poor farming land. To the south, the Danes faced aggression from their mighty Christian neighbor, the Frankish Empire. These issues prompted young men, warriors, adventurers, and even powerful warlords to seek opportunities for wealth and reputation elsewhere. They set out on their long ships and sailed west.

They initially arrived in small groups, raiding the coasts of England, Scotland, Ireland, Frisia, and Frankia. Using hitand-run tactics, they struck at unprotected sources of wealth at coastal towns or monasteries. They returned to their ships before defensive forces could be raised. With each successful raid, the Vikings, as they were later known, grew bolden, attracting greater numbers.

By the mid 9th century, large fleets emerged from Scandinavia. Some raided the great southern towns of London and Canterbury, but others began to settle along the coasts they had once plundered in the Scottish Isles and the east coast of Ireland. After defeating local rulers, they built fortified coastal bases, established new trade routes, lived off nearby farmland, and collected tribute from the native population. This breed of sea kings was here to stay.

Ambitions to conquer the English kingdoms soon materialized as well. In the year 865, a vast fleet from Viking bases in Ireland, Frisia, and Frankia, as well as from Scandinavia gathered in the North Sea. To the Anglo-Saxons, it was known as the Marcel Hathan Hera, the great heathen army. It likely numbered between 2,000 and 3,000 seasoned warriors.

According to the Norse sagas, its leaders were Ivar the Boneless, Uber, and Haftdan, who were sons of the legendary Ragnar Lofbuk. We are told they were seeking revenge for their father's death at the hands of an English king. Their first target was the kingdom of East Anglia. But its ruler, King Edmund, negotiated peace.

To save his people, he offered food, silver, horses, and allowed the invaders to winter peacefully in his kingdom. To the north, the North Umbreans were fiercely divided. Two royal rivals, Osp and A, both laid claim to the northern throne. This created the perfect opportunity for the Vikings to attack.

In the winter of 866, the Northmen crossed the river Humber and conquered York.
