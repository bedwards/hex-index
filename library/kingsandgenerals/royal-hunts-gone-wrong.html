Our first hunting incident takes us to the golden realm of Baantium, the most politically complex empire of the medieval period.

Much like their Latin speaking ancestors, the Eastern Romans placed great importance on hunting, considering it a vital social pursuit for aristocrats and emperors. As a Christian empire, the Byzantines viewed nature as a creation of God and themselves as its custodians. One of their favorite ways to show this mastery of the natural world was through the staged fights and hunts of animals. When the Arab physician Al-Mazawi visited the Eastern Roman capital of Constantinople in the 12th century CE, he recorded his observations of numerous spectacles, including public fights between lions and bulls or cheetahs and antelopes.

These events were always the talk of the town, attracting nobles and commoners alike. However, the aristocracy and the peasantry within the medieval Roman Empire saw hunting very differently. For the former, hunting was a form of recreation and prestige. For the latter, it was a means of subsistence, especially for those living in rural areas.

Several Byzantine emperors are known to have been avid hunters, but the most famous of them belonged to the Comoi dynasty of the 11th to 12th century CE. It is one of these emperors who had a rather fatal hunting accident. John II Koanis or John the Good ruled over Baantium for 25 years and was known for his genocide of the Petune and constant wars with Arabs, Venetians, Hungarians, and Turks. The long reign of Emperor John ended in 1143 when the Byzantines were in the early stages of preparation for a campaign against the city of Antioch which was under the control of their Catholic crusader enemies.

John was out hunting in the nearby region of Silicia when he encountered a wild boar. The boar charged at him and he struck it with his spear under the chest. The animal went berserk and flung the emperor around, thrashing him about so much that his wrist was slashed by a poisoned arrow that he had equipped in his quiver. Immediately, his companions tried to stop the wound from bleeding with a bandage, but nothing could be done about the poison now seeping through the emperor's body.

Within a few days, Jon had begun to weaken from blood poisoning. His doctors tried to help him, but it was all for Nort. By April the 8th 1143 the Byzantine emperor assembled the army and urged them to accept his son Manuel Kenos as the new emperor. With that he passed away in the arms of his weeping heir who was dressed in imperial garb and proclaimed emperor of the Romans.

John Kenos was not the first Byzantine emperor to perish at the hands of wildlife. His predecessor, Basil I had also died in a hunting accident when his belt was caught by a stag's antlers which dragged him across rough terrain. The story of John reveals a great deal about how even the mightiest emperors can be brought low by some twist of fate. Our next stop on this excursion takes us back over a millennium to the time of ancient Greece in the 5th to 4th century B.C.E.

The ancient Greeks were passionate about hunting, as evidenced in their mythology and art, both of which are filled with rustic scenes of heroes and lovers pursuing wild boars, lions, and even more fantastical animals. The 4th century BCE, Athenian mercenary, Xenopon, even produced a text called the Syogeticus, a manual on how to hunt and properly manage one's hunting dogs. The kingdom of Macedon in particular appears to have placed great importance on hunting amongst its elites. Because the kingdom was run by an elaborate military aristocracy whose relations relied on friendship between landowning nobles, kings and their sanctioned friends often used hunting as a preparation for war and to cultivate relations with one another.

There is one hunting incident involving a Macedonian king, although it is one with dubious historical value. That is the tale of Alexander's ancestor, King Archelaus I of Macedon, who ruled from 413 to 399 B.CE. Archelaus built roads and fortresses, produced coinage of high quality, and also provided Athens with valuable timber to construct its mighty navy. He also invited many Greek intellectuals to his court like the playwright Uripides.

Throughout his busy reign, Archelaus was also said to have been a man who deeply enjoyed hunting, a hobby that ultimately proved fatal for him. According to the traditional account, Archelaus went on a hunt with Catos, his faithful page and sometimes lover, only to be suddenly and fatally struck by the man with whom he was in a situationship. There are three versions as to why this murder happened. One tale says that Cataros did this to become king of Macedon himself.

Another says that he was promised the hand of one of Archelaus's daughters in marriage and then was denied her, making him angry for revenge. A third version merely says that the whole ordeal was an accident and that Cataros did not mean to strike Archalos with his weapon. In some versions of the tale, Catateros was working with two Theelians, Damnos and Helenocrates, both of whom were also lovers of the king. Damnos is said to have sought revenge on account of being flogged by Uripides on the permission of Archelos, with the playwright doing this because of the pages bad breath.

Whatever the case, the king of Macedon perished on a hunt in a story so fantastical as to almost be a Greek myth itself. The third story about hunting hy jinks takes us to the iridescent lands of India during the period of the Mughal Empire spanning the 16th to 18th century CE. The subcontinent has long been a land of hunting. But the Mughal monarchs who ruled it introduced some notable innovations to the practice.

The Muals were a Mongol descended dynasty which had embraced Persian culture when they first emerged in central Asia. Upon conquering parts of northern India in the 16th century, they merged Persian, Turo Mongol, and native Indian hunting traditions to create the hunting grounds known as Chicago, large fields and estates adorned with small palaces for Mughal elites to lounge in when they went on large hunting tours. The Mughal Sha Shars often took long holidays with their noble companions and armies. They participated in hunts of cheetahs and elephants in events known as kamaga or enclosure hunts.

These events were highly theatrical with servants managing hunting scenarios and preparing the animals for their ritualized slaughter at the hands of their royal hunters. After a long performance, the hunted prey would be surrounded by the nobles and their servants. Eventually, the Sha and Sha would come to the animal and slay it, claiming his victory. Apart from their recreational role, the Chicago hunting grounds were also early attempts at curating nature, making them a form of proton nature conservation program and demonstrating the Mual's great achievements in combining nature and fun in their hunting pursuits.

Reigning from 1556 to605, Emperor Akbar was one of the Mugal Empire's most prolific hunters. He would often go on long Kamaga hunts and bask in the elaborate performance of the capture and murder of animals. He also used hunting as a political tool. An example of this was found in 1561 CE when Akbar's foster brother Adam Khan was plotting a revolt against the Sha Sha.

Adam Khan had seized Malwa province for Akbar, but had used his control of the newly conquered region to establish his own center of power in defiance of central Mughal authority. To counter this threat, Akbar took his army to the city of Sarangpur in Malwa. He pretended to merely be passing by on a hunt, going after leopards and other animals. However, upon reaching the city, he launched a sudden assault upon it.

Adam Khan was utterly captured by surprise and immediately surrendered. Many other such stories appear in tales of Akbar's life like putting down spontaneous revolts while on hunts. In these stories, Akbar is usually praised for his hunting prowess and also for his love of nature and magnanmity towards his subjects. These all show the highly theatrical connection between the Muals and nature.

The fourth hunting incident we will discuss comes from the ancient civilization of Persia, specifically in the Cisanian period. Hunting is an ancient tradition in Iran where as early as the Akeminid period, princes were required to participate in the slaying of wild animals as part of their equestrian and marshall education. According to the Greek historian Xenopon, the Akeminid kings would often take many young aristocrats on their hunts to train them in the use of bow and arrow. In these instances, the sha and sha was always first to strike a beast as he was meant to take precedence.

This tradition continued in the Henistic and Paththean periods spreading to Armenia and other parts of the Middle East. In the Cisanian period, kings and their noble companions conducted large opulent hunts in their massive garden estates. On these occasions, large numbers of falconers, servants, and horses accompanied both elite men and elite women in their excursions in the wilderness of Iran. Many of these servants even carried saffron, which they used to perfume the air for the king and the princes who accompanied him.

All these events are depicted in various hunting panels from Cisanian art throughout its reign, spanning the 3rd to 7th century CE. They showcase a culture rich in elaborate rituals and equestrian values deeply ingrained in Iranian society. Many hunting accidents are recorded in Akimened and Persian history. For instance, Heroditus of Hakonasus, the so-called father of history of ancient Greece, mentions one such story.

Heroditus claims that the Persian king Darius I once dislocated his ankle while on a hunt. Despite the best efforts of Egyptian physicians, they could not set the bone back in place. So, Darius spent an entire week in excruciating pain. Salvation came in the form of the enslaved Greek doctor Deamosides of Croton who managed to fix his foot.

Another story happened centuries later in the Cisanian period. This event like the story of Archalos is of dubious historical reliability and involves the death of Baraham V, the Sha and Sha of Iran who reigned from 420 to 438 CE. According to the Persian epic known as the Shaame, Baham was a pivotal warrior who defeated two lions to demonstrate his capacity to rule. He also apparently died in a hunting accident.

Some historians say that he died while chasing an onager, a half donkey animal. He was out hunting one day when he spotted the fierce animal which immediately began to run away. Baram chased after the onager until he followed the beast into a cave and disappeared forever. In another version, the chase led him to a swamp where he became stuck and drowned.

It should be noted that this is not the story that appears in the Shaame where he is said to have died in his sleep. Regardless, the story of Baraham is typical of many Persian royals deeply ingrained with hunting. Our final hunting incident takes us to the center of Europe to the illustrious realms of the Franks which illuminated the history of Western Europe. Frankish dynasties such as the Meravenians and the Carolinians were a product of the merging of late Roman and Germanic traditions which included hunting as a recreational and noble pursuit.

Both Germans and Roman nobles engaged in hunting for prestige. For the Frankish and Holy Roman nobles who inherited parts of both legacies, hunting was an expression of masculinity and royal splendor. Similar to the Muals and the Keminids we discussed previously, the Frankish rulers of the Holy Roman Empire instituted offices that managed royal parks where hunts took place. These functionaries ensured that the parks maintain their forested status and remain suitable for the kings to hunt boar and other large beasts.

Hunts were political affairs where politics was on full display and where many conspiracies and murders took place. This phenomenon preceded the establishment of the Holy Roman Empire. For example, King Childder II, who was king of the Franks between 673 and 675 CE, was murdered alongside his wife and 5-year-old child by enemy nobles near the city of Lonier. Others, like the famous Holy Roman Emperor Charlemagne, were more fortunate as they spent long lives hunting in peace with their companions, remaining so until old age.

One rather famous accident from the Holy Roman Empire is the death of King Louis V, the do nothing, the last emperor of the Carolinian dynasty, who reigned in the West Frankian kingdom from 986 to 987 CE. He ascended to the throne during a time of turmoil for the empire as the Archbishop of Rhymes, Adalberon, and his wife, Queen Emma, were quarreling over whether the Franks should reestablish ties with the Saxon Atonian dynasty. Louie reigned for only a year and did not manage to resolve these struggles, for he died in a rather gruesome hunting accident. Here is how the contemporary chronicler Ra of St.

This was an unceremonious death, marking the end of the Carolingian dynasty in West Frankia, which was then replaced by the Capesians.

This brutal death marked more than the death of one king. It was the end of the Carolingian era.
