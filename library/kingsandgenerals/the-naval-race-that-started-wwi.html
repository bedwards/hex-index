The second half of the 19th century was a time of rapid change in the technology of the warship in which sailpowered wooden vessels began being phased out in favor of steamowered ships with hulls of metal. In 1859, Britain laid down her first ironclad warships, the HMS Warrior and Black Prince.

Naval gunnery from 1859 to 1892 saw a gradual increase in the caliber size of onboard firepower, allocating for greater range as cannon broadsides were replaced with rotating turrets or barbettes, while muzzle loading rifles in the hands of shipboard marines gave way to the more efficient breach loading rifle. By the 1870s, wooden ships of the line disappeared as plate armor was outfitted to them, but the substantial weight cost them speed, maneuverability, and range. At first, the British were reluctant to relinquish their sails and masts for engines, for steam engines were prone to failure, and coal stocks were not yet globally abundant. Later, engineers began designing long hulls with narrow beams.

And by 1892, naval architects settled on a lengthto- width ratio of 5:1. That year, Britain settled on a balance of power, protection, and propulsion, requiring 14,000 tons of displacement for a new type of ship, the HMS Royal Sovereign. designated as a pre- dreadnaugh. Another major development had occurred back in 1890 when Alfred the an American naval officer and historian published the influence of sea power upon history.

Mayan asserted that sea power was the key factor that enabled strong nations to prosper and exert their influence over weaker ones advocating that the best way to achieve naval supremacy was through large-scale fleet battles. Mayan's book became deeply influential and made its way into militarymies around the world. Consequently, what became known as the Mayan doctrine deeply influenced navies across the globe. Despite a minor Anglo French naval arms race in the late 19th century, British maritime supremacy remained relatively unchallenged in the decades after the Napoleonic Wars.

That was until the divided states of Germany united into a single nation in 1871. Chancellor Otto von Bismar had masterfully forged Germany's foreign relations so as not to be attached to any other European power. This all changed when a young Kaiservillehelm II took the German throne in 1888 and sacked Bismar as his advisers believed the great diplomat would overshadow him. Wilhelm held great ambitions to elevate Germany to the status of a world power.

Wilhelm broke with Bismar's vision and drew Germany deeper into a triple alliance with Italy and AustriaHungary while simultaneously not renewing the reinsurance treaty with Russia, prompting her to gradually ally with France. Wilhelm and his advisers had done this due to the belief that it would improve Anglo-German relations. After reading Mayan's work in 1894, Wilhelm began pursuing a policy of providing Germany with a navy that could match her growing status. By 1895, Wilhelm pressured the Reichag to fund the construction of two pre- dreadnots, but only received funding for four of the 36 cruisers he sought.

Frustrated, Wilhelm recalled Rear Admiral Alfred von Turpetsz of the Imperial German Navy from the Far East in June 1897 to lead the Imperial Naval Office. A staunch advocate of Mayan's doctrine, Turpetsz insisted Germany must build a fleet capable of offensive naval battles to secure global influence. In his first meeting with the Kaiser, Turpitz proposed his Riku Gadena or risk theory, he argued that by building a fleet twothirds the size of Britain's and narrowing their battleship advantage to no more than 50%, Germany could exploit the Royal Navy's global dispersal. If Germany's navy reached a strength where Britain could not defeat it without crippling losses, Turpitz believed Britain would be forced to negotiate with Germany as an equal, potentially abandoning its splendid isolation policy to join the Triple Alliance.

To realize this vision, Philhelm and Turpitz pursued five naval laws. However, Britain was an island nation whose economy was dependent on her ability to import and export goods via the sea. Likewise, according to one source, by 1900, 58% of the caloric intake of the average British citizen came from overseas. Prior to the German naval challenge, British political and military leaders contemplated the potentially disastrous economic, social, and political ramifications if the Royal Navy failed to ensure Britain's freedom of action.

Concerns over Britain's capacity to defend itself became central to the invasion literature genre, significantly influencing public opinion. This led Britain to enact the Naval Defense Act of 1889, whereupon she formally adopted her two power standard, mandating that Britain's Royal Navy match the next two largest fleets combined. Turpitz's naval buildup scheme and Wilhelm's incoherent foreign policies began threatening Britain, resulting in a naval arms race. Germany's first naval act of 1898 set an annual construction target of three battleships, one cruiser, six light cruisers, and six torpedo boats.

Between 1901 and 1906, [music] this legislation authorized five pre- dreadnots and five armored cruisers. These later pre- dreadnots featured 11in quickfiring turret guns and upgraded 6.7 in secondary batteries, marking a significant step in naval firepower. Despite these advancements, the British Admiral T took little notice, continuing to focus instead on the naval buildup of France and Russia. During the Second Bore War, British cruisers seized three German male steamers off southern Africa, provoking public outrage in Germany.

This incident enabled Turpets to persuade the Reichag to enact a second naval law on June 14th, 1900, which committed to doubling the fleet size of the Imperial German Navy from 19 to 38 battleships by 1917 and positioning Germany as the world's second strongest naval power. While Britain maintained superiority in battleships and armored cruisers, Germany gained a significant advantage in light cruisers, oceangoing destroyers, and pioneered the development of surface torpedo vessels. In contrast, Germany's first submarine U1, a coastal type, was not built until 1906 due to Turpets's opposition to submarine warfare. By this point, the ratios between Britain and Germany stood at 51% for pre- dreadnots and 280% for armored cruisers, giving Britain a 164% margin in heavy ships.

The second naval act had alarmed the British admiral, which in 1902 continues to uphold the two power standard, but advocated for the construction of an additional six battleships. Britain shifted away from its splendid isolation policy by forming the Anglo-Japanese alliance and in 1904 it signed the Antant Cordial with France adding Russia to the agreement in 1907 effectively encircling Germany. In 1904 Admiral Sir John Fischer became first seaord and initiated a radical overhaul of the Royal Navy. He scrapped ineffective ships and limited new construction to four types.

battleships, armored cruisers, large fast destroyers and submarines. Fischer standardized 12-in guns for battleships and 9.2 in guns for armored cruisers with both classes powered by steam turbines. The next pre- dreadnots would feature 10 guns in five turrets, two wing turrets and three on the center line, allowing a broadside fire of eight guns. Armored cruisers would carry eight guns with two amid ships turrets arranged on echelon between the second and third funnels.

Fischer redistributed the entire fleet to concentrate its most powerful ships against Germany. The home fleet was renamed the Channel Fleet, operating near the English Channel, while the former Channel fleet in Jibralta became the Atlantic Fleet. To bolster the Channel Fleet, four battleships were transferred from the Mediterranean and five from China, increasing its total to 17 battleships. The Atlantic fleet's eight battleships could be maneuvered north towards the British Isles or east into the Mediterranean.

In the wake of the destruction of Russian sea power during the Russo-Japanese war of 1904 to 1905 and the recent diplomatic realignments, the Admiral T and the public began debating how specifically to deal with the threat of a German naval buildup. On January 1st, 1907, Fischer established a new policy, stating that the Royal Navy should maintain a force twice as powerful as the German Navy, positioned just hours from Germany's coasts. After analyzing the results of the Battle of Sushima, Fischer convened a committee to design a new super battleship designated the Dreadnaugh. In February 1906, amid extensive publicity, the HMS Dreadnaugh was launched, astonishing Germany.

The HMS Dreadnaugh featured an 11-in thick armor belt between her lower and middle decks with an overall armor thickness of 8 in. The Dreadnaugh was the first capital ship powered by steam turbines, which were capable of 21 knots, and had a uniform main battery of 10 12-in guns in five twin turrets, allowing a broadside of eight guns. For secondary armorament, she carried 27 3-in quickfiring guns with a rate of fire of 20 rounds per minute alongside five submerged 18-in torpedo tubes capable of holding 29 torpedoes. Turpitz was deeply troubled by the debut of the HMS Dreadnaugh.

He recognized that Germany [music] had no choice but to respond despite the high costs associated with larger ships and the need to enlarge fixed works like the Keel Canal. It was Tupperitz's erroneous belief that Britain had made a mistake in building her new expensive dreadnots and armored cruisers, and if Germany did not flinch, they would deescalate. To that end, Turpets advocated for the development of German dreadnots and a 35% increase over the second naval act to enable the construction of two dreadnots and one armored cruiser per year. However, the bill faced significant opposition due to budget constraints and the regax's reluctance to raise taxes.

Then in April of 1906, the German team at the Alisera's conference failed to break the ant cordial. It seemed to many in Germany proof that Britain was working in concert with Russia and France to encircle them and there was a fear of a preemptive strike akin to the battle of Copenhagen in 1807. The Russo-Japanese war likewise spread rumors that Britain's new ally Japan might join in a war against her. Fear gave into nationalist sentiment and right-wing hawks encouraged by the navy and industry promoted escalation while left-wing doves encouraged more peaceful foreign relations.

Luckily for the hawks, the first Moroccan crisis in April 1906 outraged the German public, allowing turpets to enact the third naval law. In both nations, an evolving naval industrial complex was emerging, using politicians and the press to promote their interests. Public enthusiasm and media coverage centered around the dramatic presentation of naval power through events such as fleet reviews and ship launches, which became theatrical displays of strength and deterrence. These exhibitions directly fueled Anglo-German hostility due to their showy nature, their connection of national identity, the sea, and military might, and the unique rhetoric and choreography of rivalry they embodied.

The quest for maritime supremacy became a central theme of political mobilization, public discourse, and nationalist identity politics. By May 1907, Germany's Imperial Navy Office concluded that the latest designs of foreign dreadnots, not only from Britain, but also Japan and the United States, would require further increases in displacement and cost. To address this rise in expenditure, Turpets formed a Navy League to promote naval expansion within the Reich. On November 18th, 1907, a new bill was introduced seeking to increase production to three battleships and one battle cruiser per year.

The fourth naval law was passed on March 27th, 1908. In April 1907, Admiral Lord Charles Berisford was appointed commander-in-chief of the Channel Fleet and criticize Fischer's policies for neglecting the construction of adequate light cruisers and destroyers for operations along the German coast. The resulting 1908 to 1909 minimum program included two dreadnots, one armored cruiser, six light cruisers, and 16 destroyers. Despite including a full-size battle cruiser in Britain's 1908 to 1909 program, the ratio of authorized dreadnots remained 12-9.

When Britain slowed its construction rate in response to Germany's growth, Germany unknowingly surpassed its target of a 3-2 ratio for capital ships. By 1908, both nations faced growing criticism regarding the cost of the naval arms race. In August, King Edward IIIth visited Phil Wilhelm II in Kronburgg with a document outlining British concerns, but ultimately refrained from addressing naval spending, believing it would spoil a congenial mood. Tensions escalated [music] during the Bosnian crisis, prompting Chancellor Bernhard von Bulov to question Tuppets's strategy, which had alienated Germany diplomatically and doubled its national debt since 1900.

Bulof concluded that Germany could not afford to maintain the largest army and the second largest navy in Europe. However, Turpets argued that too much money had already been invested in the naval arms race to abandon it. Now, similarly, Britain's liberal government demanded a reduction in naval expenditure to increase spending on social programs. By 1909, the Admiral T reported that they were failing to maintain the two power standard and noted that the latest German designs were significantly more powerful than the older British ships.

Additionally, AustriaHungary planned to construct four dreadnots and Italy had just laid down its first. Alongside this, Germany's fourth naval law and the British government's intelligence errors led to the British Navy scare of 1909. Terror struck the British public who believed Germany intended to use their navy to blockade them into tyranny. This fear led the British public to demand that their political and military leadership accelerate the naval buildup.

However, the Liberal government had just proposed the people's budget, introducing unprecedented taxes on the lands and income of Britain's wealthy to fund new social welfare programs. To counter this, the Conservatives, the Navy League, the British Arms Industry, and King Edward IIIth pushed for eight more dreadnots under the slogan, "We want eight and we won't wait." The House of Lords rejected the people's budget in November 1909, and by January 1910, the Liberals lost their majority government. A compromise was met in April of 1910, seeing the people's budget enacted alongside a commitment to mobilize whatever financial resources were necessary to remain ahead of Germany in the naval arms race, even at the expense of a constitutional crisis. The Germans could not hope to match this escalation.

Raising new revenue to cover the ever escalating costs for the naval buildup was being met with elite political opposition and public condemnation. They knew Britain was financially superior and could simply outbuild them. Moreover, the diplomatic repercussions of the naval arms race had isolated Germany and seemed to be leading to war with Britain. Both the German elites and public began advocating for Germany to refocus on land power rather than naval expansion.

Despite the hysteria, the second Moroccan crisis of 1911 gave Turpetss and Wilhelm II the opportunity to push for a fifth naval law. Increasing production to three dreadnots, two light cruisers, 12 destroyers, and 17 diesel-powered overseas submarines over the next 6 years, which would achieve Turpets's coveted 3:2 ratio. The financial suffering and diplomatic instability led the elites and public of both nations to demand a deescalation of the naval programs. However, in Germany, Admiral Turpitz held a veto power that enabled him to derail any attempts at reduction.

In Britain, the interest in an arms agreement stemmed from a desire to stabilize naval competition at the lowest possible level, thereby minimizing the financial burden of maintaining clear British superiority. By 1912, the government under Chancellor Tibbalt von Besman Hulvek faced isolation, Russian military expansion, and a proposal for a fifth naval law that was deemed far too expensive. Thus, Hve pursued a Rustang vendor or armament's turning point policy, shifting focus on the army and opening up negotiations with Lord Chancellor Viccount Richard Haldane. This led to the Haldane mission brokered by Ernest Castle and Albert Balin where the British made three proposals to Germany on January 29th.

If Germany allowed Britain to maintain naval superiority, Britain would not interfere with German colonial expansion and would refrain from joining any aggression against Germany. After reviewing the proposal, Hulve and Philhelm II agreed to curtail naval expansion amidst intense protests from Turpets on the condition that Britain would remain neutral in a war during which Germany was not perceived as the aggressor. However, Haldane's visit to Berlin proved fruitless. Wilhelm II demonstrated his characteristic diplomatic finesse by introducing Turpets's naval amendment bill to the Reichtag just a day before Haldane's arrival.

The British refused to accept the proposal for unconditional neutrality as it would allow Germany to take calculated risks in a conflict with France and Russia and they believed they had already secured naval superiority. After 1912, Britain laid down nine battleships and showed no intention of slowing down their yearly programs. On March 18th, 1912, Sir Winston Churchill publicly acknowledged Britain's goal to maintain a 60% margin over Germany, meaning Britain would lay down 56 dreadnots to Germany's 35 by 1918. Realizing Britain would not reduce its naval expansion, Turpets announced his acceptance of the British 60% standard before the Reichag in February 1913.

The following year, Germany shifted its focus to building submarines instead of new dreadnots and destroyers, effectively abandoning the naval arms race while keeping this policy secret, which prevented other powers from following suit. The naval arms race strained Anglo-German relations and contributed to a broader militarization across Europe. By the outbreak of World War I, Britain achieved a 62% margin in dreadnots over Germany and dwarfed her in terms of number of ships, capital ships, servicemen, and tonnage. Ironically, while Turpets and Philhelm sought a fleet in being, their numerical inferiority allowed Britain to achieve a strategic victory during the Battle of Utland in 1916, whereupon the high seas fleet would sit at port until the war's end.
