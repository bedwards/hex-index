This may sound contradictory, but such a decentralized system could only operate properly if the central authority was powerful and respected. In the absence of a powerful and functioning central authority, provincial elites were more reluctant to follow the rules of the state, tended to misappropriate collected taxes and engaged in infighting with their neighboring rivals. And this is exactly what was happening in the Sappvid Empire in the 18th century. Unlike in the apex days of the Sappovid dynasty when its leaders were military, political and even religious leaders of their realm and were trained in these matters through practice [music] as the rule of the dynasty solidified.

But thousands of kilometers away in the dangerous northeastern border area of Kurasan, the disorganization was more readily felt. In 1698, a boy named Nedagulu was born into the family of Imamulu, a herdsman of the Afarid Azabjani speaking Kisbash tribe. His exact place of birth is unclear, but many sources indicate that it was Dascer, a village located northwest of Mashad. As a member of the militant and pneumatic Asherish tribe, Nadair is said to have been a good rider and hunter as early as the age of 10.

This was a dangerous region, regularly plagued by raids from the Turkmen tribes, and young men were trained to be ready to fight at a very early age. Nadair's defiant character was to an extent shaped by the troubles of his childhood and early adulthood. His father died when he was still very young, condemning Nadair to a life of poverty. He is said to have been subject to ridicule for growing up in poverty without a father, but this experience apparently hardened his character instead of bringing him down.

Moreover, this experience is said to have fostered distrust towards those in privileged positions, including nobility and mulas. Around the age of 15, Nadair entered the service of the governor of Abivad, Baba Albec Kusa Amedlu as a musketeer in his army.
