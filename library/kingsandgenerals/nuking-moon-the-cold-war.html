So the moon escaped the bomb. Space itself, however, did not. In the early 1960s, both the US and the Soviet Union conducted nuclear detonations at high altitude to see what would happen. The results were both spectacular and terrifying.

The American program was called Operation Fishbowl, part of a larger series of tests held in 1962. The most famous shot was Starfish Prime, a 1.4 megaton hydrogen bomb detonated 400 km above Johnson at the Pacific on July 9th, 1962. The effects were unlike anything seen before. In fact, Starfish Prime's radiation created artificial belts around the Earth that disabled or degraded several spacecraft in the following months. Other fishbowl tests followed with smaller yields designed to study EMP effects and auroras. But the lesson was clear.

Nuclear explosions in space had unpredictable wide-ranging consequences that could not be controlled. The Soviets carried out similar tests under the code name project K in 1961 and 1962. Both sides quickly realized that nukes in space were too dangerous to continue. The 1963 partial test ban treaty banned nuclear explosions both in the atmosphere and in space and effectively ended high altitude testing. Of course, not all nuclear schemes were military. Some were pitched as peaceful uses of atomic power.

Nuclear as a tool rather than a weapon. The American program was called Project Plowshare, launched in 1958. Its mission was to find civilian applications for nuclear explosions. Proponents argued that atomic blasts could move Earth, create harbors, stimulate natural gas mining, and even build canals faster and cheaper than conventional methods.

One of the most ambitious proposals was Project Chariot in Alaska. The plan, championed by none other than Edward Teller, was to detonate a series of hydrogen bombs to carve out a brand new harbor on the coast of Cape Thompson on the north slope of the state on the Chukchi Sea. The idea was to open access to coal reserves in northern Alaska, stimulating economic growth. Local Inupia communities and scientists protested, however, warning of fallout and environmental damage.

The plan was canceled in 1962 before any bombs were detonated. Plowshare did carry out real tests, though. In 1962, the Sedan test buried a 104 kiloton device at the Nevada test site to see how much earth it could move. The result was the largest man-made crater in the United States, 100 m deep and nearly 400 m wide, with 12 million tons of soil being displaced.

This was never disclosed to the local populace and once it became public 30 years later the government was forced to remove and clean the contamination among much public outrage and condemnation.
