Before we tell the story of the Catalan company, we must understand the cultural and geopolitical context of the Mediterranean in which they operated. The origins of the company come from Catalonia in the eastern shores of Iberia. The crown that ruled modern-day Catalonia was that of Araggon, a union of the principality of Catalonia and the Kingdom of Araggon. The Araggonese crown was a maritime powerhouse that possessed the third largest fleet in Europe at the time with which it established control over parts of modern-day Italy.

During the war of the Sicilian vespers from 1282 to 1302 CE, the royal house of Aragan established control over the island of Sicily as the kingdom of Trinacria, dislodging the previous Norman state established there. Moving on to the eastern Mediterranean, we find an even more complicated geopolitical landscape. The lands of modern-day Greece were still bearing the consequences of the Latin sack of Constantinople and the dismantling of the Eastern Roman Empire in 1204 CE. Various Catholic states had emerged there, such as the Duche of Athens, often ruled by Latinsspeaking elites oppressing the local Greek and Albanian Eastern Orthodox peoples.

The reconquest of Constantinople by the Byzantine Kingdom of Nika in 1261 CE had revived the Eastern Roman Empire. But this resuscitated state was unable to prevent the various Turk bicks of Anatolia from eating away at its territories in Asia Minor. The Byzantines often used mercenaries to supplement their forces against the Turks. This is where the Catalan Company came in.

But before we discuss their history, let us first provide an overview on how they fought. The Catalan company was composed predominantly of light infantry forces called the Al-Magavas through versatile asymmetrical light infantry tactics. The Catalans often managed to defeat opposing forces which far outnumbered them. The unique style of the Al-Magavas stemmed from their participation in the reconista against the Islamic powers of Iberia.

In fact, the name Al-Magava is itself of Arabic origin. In terms of equipment, they carried two javelins and swords as well as spears and their own shields. Their light equipment enabled their shock and ore tactics, making them even more effective. The Catalans were also very good at psychological warfare, terrorizing their enemies, striking flint against their swords to create vivid sparks.

They accompanied this show with yells like, "Light iron, it's time to kill."
