It’s not that the left forgot how to win; we seem to not know how the win looks like because we have no idea what the fight is anymore. And the most clear evidence of that is the fact that since the neoliberal revanch has begun, the left on both sides of the Cold War divide has remained constantly on the back foot: since the 1970s the left has been continuously ceding the ground to the advancing conservatism, which over the next 50 years since then wiped out large parts of the socialist/social democratic developments so much so that with economic conditions of the 1920s also returned the politics of the 1920s, with the public voting fascists in power all over the place, including the United States.

Since the 1970s, conservatives were successfully doing revolutionary politics of pushing the Overton window (backwards), while the left was only catching up and reacting to relentless conservative initiative. Such total, all over the world except for the communist 5 (China, Cuba, DPRK, Laos, Vietnam) and consistent, over four decades, political reversal could mean only one thing: collapse of the left theory. Since at least the 1970s, the left didn’t have a working theory of change, flying essentially blind, yet remained in denial about that, because earlier that century, the left, armed with the theory, had literally changed the world.

But any idea can only be as powerful as material conditions allow. Right idea means “right for the circumstance”: not the one that sounds right or considered to be right, but the one that works. “Practice is the criterion of truth.” The theory, the bulk of which originated in the XIX century, did work in practice up until the middle of the XX century, and allowed for change to humanity on a civilizational scale. It worked to the extent it could, and by succeeding in changing the world, rendered itself inadequate to the new material conditions it brought. Such is the dialectic of change.

But because the Western left hasn’t done its XX-century history homework, it still clings to that era’s time—at least thirty years late for the political present. Any successful left political praxis in such a situation is necessarily done, to some extent, in defiance of the theory. Which creates the problem that is twofold:

  1. resistance of the orthodoxy, unable to evaluate what works and what is needed because they live in the previous political era

  2. timidity of the leaders whose ideas succeeded, but without the adequate theory might not understand what exactly worked, and how far in what direction it could be pushed.

And both issues became very prominently evident in Zohran Mamdani’s victory in the New York mayoral elections: his campaign and victory became a global political sensation on the left, but supporting Mamdani activists-organizers, who made this victory happen and celebrate it, felt compelled to moderate expectations—cautiously, even apologetically: ‘Yes, Mamdani isn’t a revolutionary; this isn’t a revolution, but...’ Meanwhile, his orthodox ‘revolutionary’ detractors held no such restraint, branding the campaign a failure and Mamdani a traitor, collaborator, and opportunist—before he’d even taken office.. But it is indicative that, as seemingly the opposite, they united in their contribution to discourse that Mamdani’s victory is not a revolution, differing only in the anger levels of their delivery. At the same time, excitement, global interest, and vibe on the broader left are a mismatch to both of them: it’s giving revolution.

1. This is the New York revolution

If, minding the necessity to question the political validity of any ideas about how left politics is done that we might have, we go back to the first principles and look at the Mamdani movement, campaign, and victory from a class perspective—that is, apply the dialectical method to political analysis—then, in the most fundamental sense, in New York, actually did take place a political revolution.

A New York-based Working Families Party and the Democratic Socialists of America-led movement did in 2025 something actually rare in history: it directly introduced masses, people themselves, to politics, as the side of their own in a struggle for power.

The most guarded principle of the class system in any society is that politics is the prerogative of the ruling class. Although populism, i.e. the recruitment of the lower classes by different competing sides of the elite for power, has been practiced in politics for as long as politics has existed, any attempt by the lower classes to enter a struggle for power directly, representing their own, not somebody’s from the elite, interests, under any system from monarchy to liberal democracy, is mercilessly suppressed by the united ruling class, as they rightly recognize in this challenge to their class political power as such, i.e. a revolution against the class system.

From Caesar to Trump, a “champion of the people” should either come from the ruling class or, in cases of a powerful bottom-up movement, be coopted from its leadership, which gets adopted into the ruling class, on the other side of the class line and thus separated from the masses. The power flows across the class line only in one direction: the masses cannot take somebody of the elite as allies, can only enter into their service, transferring their agency and power to the leader. E.g. Trump and the base: the more power the base wins for Trump, the stronger Trump gets and the less control over him the base has left.

  • Actual revolutionary movement has its leaders entirely on their side of the class divide, which means that they do not have any material interests across the class line, on the side of the ruling class, and rely in their well-being entirely on people below the class line, e.g. exclusively grassroots fundraising, no relationships of political or financial patronage across class line etc.

  • The political and/or financial dependence of a movement’s members—especially its leaders—on elite support from the other side, compromises them and the movement according to the degree of their dependence and power in the movement.

Situations where a completely grassroots movement enters political struggle and wins power before it develops any material dependencies on the ruling class or their leadership get coopted are exceedingly rare—this is why actual revolutions are rare.

And this is what happened in New York:

  1. Mamdani’s campaign—with Zohran himself an activist, manned and led by activists—is as grassroots as it gets in its origin,

  2. Financially is independent from the influence of capital and relies on small donors only,

  3. The Democratic Party establishment’s refusal to endorse Mamdani’s candidacy, and thus to count on his gratitude, turned out to be an accidental gift to the campaign, completely freeing it from even the customary across class line commitments.

The entirely bottom-up nature of the New York movement, organized on the basis of their shared class-specific material interests, i.e. affordability, which unites everybody outside of the ruling class and separates them all from the ruling class at the same time, and won the mayoral office while maintaining its class integrity (led by political newcomers, funded by small donors) also makes the movement behind Mamdani a successful example of the political awakening of the masses, aka developing political consciousness, aka turning from “a class in itself” to “a class for itself”—something that the revolutionary left in theory supposed to fight for, yet completely missed it happening in New York.

This is a genuine local revolution: led by the political vanguard of the PWF and the DSA, politically awakened masses dethroning the ruling class on a city scale—New York 2025 is Paris Commune meets the 1917. And though it is similar to many historical events from the XIX-early XX century, it is the first of its kind in the XXI century. In other words, in 2025 in New York, the revolutionary XXI century has finally begun.

Sure, it doesn’t look like those revolutions looked. And Mamdani did not seize power in the country, nor even declare New York’s independence.

But who said that revolutions in the XXI century must look like those of the XX? In fact, the revolutionary XXI century will not be like anything that we know from the past precisely because of the past revolutions and the changes they brought, which have consigned to history the historical circumstances that created them, and ensured that the battle for future generations will look different—with each step of progress, political violence will be less useful and political consciousness more necessary to take the next step. So, the “radical left” vigilant watch awaiting the return of the glorious revolutionary struggle of the past is neither revolutionary nor progressive, but another case of generals preparing for the last war.

We need to separate revolutionary aesthetics from revolutionary (material) dynamics, and clear the meanings of the concepts used from the distortions of the associations stuck to them. We must take stock of what we have learned from the XX century, and overcome the schisms that we still carry from there in order to finally enter the revolutionary XXI century. And the New York victory is a very good place to start.

2. The movement perspective vs “leaderism”

At least part of the reason why the left so often tend to conceptualize politics, even the left politics, in terms of personalities, must be that human language itself (which means, thinking too) heavily biased towards anthropomorphism. It’s just easier to speak about anything in anthropomorphic terms, whereas more precise language takes more effort and oftentimes sounds very awkward. And it is easier to make sense of a collective dynamic by associating groups with certain individuals. But it must be done, because we cannot afford any more confusion about what left politics is and who’s it for: left politics is a movement politics for the political empowerment of the masses. This is a collective politics in its goals and its method.

The whole point of the left movement has always been to politically empower the masses, to expand ruling class to all people under its rule and turn governing into self-governing.

But in modern left politics, this collectivist arch-goal collides with an entrenched habit of personalizing the perception and understanding of politics. Among the post-Soviet countries, it’s likely to be the residue of the famous “leaderism” that was characteristic of the communist movements of the XX century, whereas among NATO countries it may rather be the result of a theatrical, reality show-like character of democracy in a bourgeois state. Either way, it remains one of the unlearned lessons of the XX century, homework for the left to do. Until then, it only harms the movement: lack of the movement perspective, unjustified, unnecessary, erroneous focus on personalities poisons the left discourse and left politics in general, with the latest example being the discourse around the campaign and victory of Zohran Mamdani in New York.

Although the materialist rejection of the concept of “great men” acting in history has been overwhelmingly accepted, the materialist perspective and understanding of political/historical processes to replace it in practice has only been half-adopted by the Western left: many leftists have developed a strong materialist intuition, when it comes to analyzing current political conditions and their development, but still mostly personalize political outcomes.

For example, with Bernie Sanders:

  • when it comes to his appeal, rapid growth in popularity, scale of the movement behind him, ability to challenge the party elites etc. the left always talk about him and the movement in materialist terms, in a broader economic context, in the context of class war.

  • however, when it comes to assessing the course and the results of the 2020 presidential campaign, the movement suddenly disappears from the picture, which gets compressed into a narrow perspective of the personal political achievements instead, reduced to and encapsulated in Bernie himself (despite his campaign slogan literally being “not me, us”). “Bernie lost” becomes “the movement lost,” and even his ideological shortcomings outside the campaign context—on migration or Israel and Palestine—are projected onto the movement.

What this perspective switch does is turn criticism against Bernie Sanders into anti-movement criticism. It doesn’t allow for constructive criticism, making it self-destructive. And it shows, that by losing the focus on the movement being the purpose and means of left politics, the perspective itself becomes anti-left. Personality-centered political outlook comes at a cost, it takes away from the left political perspective: focus on personalities in itself makes the political outlook more reactionary. And with Mamdani the pattern continues: suddenly, it’s all about Zohran, him governing, him delivering etc.

Meanwhile, Bernie and Zohran together can actually illustrate how nonsensical personalist perspective in politics—left politics especially—is, and why the movement perspective works much better analytically:

  • those who overly identified the Bernie movement with Bernie himself perceived his defeat in the 2020 election, and the subsequent end of his participation in future elections, as a defeat and retreat of the entire movement… which in 2025 returned, as if it never went away, in fighting form with Zohran Mamdani.

  • Whereas from a movement perspective it would be clear that it actually never went away, and moreover, that Mamdani’s campaign in New York is a continuation of what was built over Bernie’s two presidential campaigns.

A focus on personalities promotes a biased perception of historical dynamics and political processes, making it harder to recognize underlying material forces in action, whereas a focus on the movement makes the left, the movement, the activists, supporters, and voters much less vulnerable to the narrative: “progressive candidate overpromised but underdelivered.”

Even worse offenders than the Western “democratic socialist” left are the Western post-Soviet ML left: if demsoc left tends to get overly attached to the personalities in today’s movement, MLs by default hate every contemporary left politician, seeing in them only traitors in the making, that will betray the left, or losers, who are so useless and insignificant that their betrayal isn’t even worth anything, because ultimately the MLs are deeply stuck in the last XX century. And their reflexive rejection of modern left-wing leaders is largely just a rationalization of their expectation that their favorite moments of revolutionary history will repeat themselves, and that the Bolsheviks will return with them, with Lenin, so young, leading them. And of course, they are waiting for the revolution (in the “armed overthrow of the government” sense), just as it was typical about 100 years ago, in some places, and the return of the guerrilla warfare in others. It’s not only loser behavior, reliving glory days of history in their fantasies, dreaming of their return, without thinking through that we know now how they end, and that it is exactly the glorious beginnings in the left history of the XX century that lead to their current loserdom from which they are escaping into fantasy.

At the same time they are missing not only a completely different historical circumstance precisely because the October 1917 Revolution happened, and led to enough change to make repeat both pre-1917 circumstances and itself impossible, but also the fact that new Okhranka (secret police) is also aware of what happened in 1917, and, as it happens, prepares for yesterday’s war, determined to make sure that anybody remotely resembling Lenin or Bolsheviks would not have a future, political or otherwise. Following their attempts must be very unnerving for those who seriously believes that the future is repeating itself just because. But as pathetic as it is for Okhranka looking backwards to miss at the end again from which side future came, for the left such a failure is malpractice.

Questions like “Will Mamdani (as a person or campaign/movement in his name) have real leverage in New York?” are just the wrong question to ask outside of literally discussing matters of local politics and life in New York, which is a matter of local politics and Mamdani’s biography, but not something that is relevant to the movement around the world just because it concerns a city where Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles are from.

Both Western and post-Soviet revolutionary left are committing the same methodological mistake, losing materialist focus in their perspectives and the entire movements behind the personalities.

3. The left for the XXI century

The job of the XXI century left is to serve once again as a socialist vanguard of the postcapitalist future, but this time with a stronger emphasis on a movement rather than leaders. On the path to self-governing humanity, this century’s iteration of the left should make a step in that direction, relative to where it was in the last century:

  • the movement should become more decentralized in its structure

  • the leadership, the decision-making process, should become more collectivist

Left politics remains revolutionary in the sense that it pursues a fundamental, essentially revolutionary transformation of society. But at the same time, this revolutionary spirit should not and will not resemble the revolutions of the 20th century in any way, just as the 1917 revolution was unlike the French Revolution. And Zohran does not and should not resemble Lenin or Mao, in order to resemble what revolutionary leaders should and will be like in the XXI century.

Speaking of collective leadership: it is important for the left movements, especially after getting in power, avoid “bottlenecks” of singular leadership and promote collective decision-making. And Mamdani in New York could provide an example, presenting after assuming the office a “collective Mamdani” instead of himself as a singular decision maker—sort of Politburo.

In a different historical conditions, Lenin isn’t necessary, but we need more others like Mamdani: though Lenin was a unique figure, one of a kind, and it is undoubtedly nice to have him by your side, the democratic mass movement couldn’t and shouldn’t been built around any exceptional personalities in any way. They must empower everyone, which Lenin himself would agree with: “Every kitchenmaid should learn how to govern the state.”

As by 2025 the world is in many meaningful ways different from 1917 (in many ways thanks to the USSR), and if in the XX century Russian revolutionaries had little agency in the ways the world treated them, in the XXI century the international left has much more agency potentially available to them, and the way this century will go for the left and for humanity is in many ways depends on how capable the left today will be.

In this sense, the fact that Mamdani cannot technically run for president is actually for the better—this rule only makes him more averse from the very attempts to try to follow the “leader” path again. And Lenin’s principle “we will go another way” continues to apply in terms of not following the Lenin’s path. The fuhrers must be left to the fascists.

So far the Mamdani campaign in theory and in praxis corresponded to the arch-purpose of building a vehicle of mass mobilization, and something from his victory speech alone can be utilized as a guiding principle for the left movements everywhere:

We won because we insisted that no longer would politics be something that is done to us. Now it is something that we do.

Does this mean a world revolution is beginning in New York? It doesn’t depend on New York, and it shouldn’t. A world revolution means a New York-like awakening throughout the world. Its accomplishment means a political awakening of the masses throughout the world, like New York’s, on a scale large enough to transform quantity into quality. New York has spoken.

It is now up to the left:

  • in New York—to embody this principle together with all New Yorkers

  • around the world—follow this example in their cities.

Whether this becomes an example or a model for the entire world—time will tell. But the first page of the left history of the XXI century is now open.

And since outside the communist five this is the first victory of a grassroots movement in a political struggle where it is only possible to participate and win with the sanction of the elites, this event can be considered the finally arrived beginning of the long-awaited, long-delayed revolutionary XXI century.
