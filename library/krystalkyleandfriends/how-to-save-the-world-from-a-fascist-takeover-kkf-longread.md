The current global rise of fascism was anything but unexpected. Originally, fascism emerged in the 1920s in very specific historical circumstances, shaped by 1) the conditions of late-stage capitalism with its runaway inequality and rapidly increasing poverty, which was making the conditions of the working poor ever closer to the conditions of the unemployed poor, on the one hand, and 2) the successful October Socialist Revolution in Russia in 1917, followed by the Bolshevik’s victory in the Civil War and the establishment of the first worker state, the USSR, in 1922, on the other.

In other words, the power of economic coercion under capitalism was weakening, as late capitalism no longer left the working masses any hope of material improvement in the future, at the same time as an alternative to the entire capitalist system just emerged, for all the workers of the world to see in the form of the USSR.

And the clock was set on repeat almost 50 years ago, in the 1970s in the US, when the American big business utilized Lewis Powell’s memo and Milton Friedman’s new wrapper for capitalism—that became known as neoliberalism—for the purpose of undoing whatever moderate progress was achieved by the social democratic reforms in Western Europe and the New Deal in the US in taming capitalism after 1929. The rollback of regulations introduced after the Great Depression did the only thing it could do: it returned the conditions of capitalism to the very state that led to the rise of fascism and the Great Depression in the 1920s. The fact that this time America itself is playing the role of not only the main economic instigator, but also the main villain, became obvious during Trump’s first term, and turned into a question that has haunted me ever since: how will a world without the USSR be able to defeat fascism this time? And it only became more acute when Trump’s victory in 2024 did just that, completing the transition from the US Weimar Republic to the US Third Reich.

## The anti-colonial revolution of 1917-1975

The anti-colonial world revolution began in 1917, when the October revolution of the Bolsheviks led to the fall and decolonization of the Russian Empire, the first of the world’s colonial empires to crumble. Over the next 50 years, Japanese and all the European colonial empires lost almost all their colonies around the world and disappeared from the world map, with the culmination of the revolution in the year of Africa, in 1960, when 17 African countries declared their sovereignty.

But declaring independence does not in itself constitutes decolonization necessarily, so with the completion of the imperial collapse, and colonies replaced with nation-states by 1975, decolonization had just begun.

**What decolonization is really about**

The real issue of colonialism is exploitation:

1.  human relations are inherently reciprocal. The universal human ability to enjoy helping others allowed humans to collectively build a power strong enough to become the first animals to leave the food chain (or at least move ourselves on the top of it)
    
2.  conversely, the power imbalance introduced in human relations takes the humanity out of it. The force of coercion removes the need for reciprocity in a relationship to continue, turns human cooperation into one-sided exploitation, and destroys the humanity of the relationship on both sides: on the part of the exploiters, allowing the humanity of the exploited to be ignored, and on the side of the exploited, robbing them of the joy of being able to help voluntarily. The more human cooperation relies on the power of coercion rather than human willingness to cooperate, the less human it becomes.
    

The ultimate form of coercion, slavery, is also the ultimate form of dehumanization in interpersonal relationships.

By adding a physical dimension to social relations of exploitation, **colonialism perpetuates social inequality in space, making inequality not only socially, but also geographically distributed.** Colonial exploitation embodies the physical limits of the possible dehumanization of human relationships: it turns not just social classes, but whole ethnicities, cultures, civilizations and entire parts of the world into a source of wealth for other people of entirely different origin.

Conversely, **it makes decolonization the problem of decolonizing social relations as much as geographical decolonization.** The anti-colonial revolution of the 20th century, having thus achieved geographical decolonization, could not be completed while maintaining the class system, since with the preservation of class inequality, decolonization only changes its form, turning direct cross-border colonial exploitation by foreign rulers into distributed neocolonial exploitation by local elites.

**The lie of the liberal idea of decolonization**

As the first wave of the anti-colonial revolution passed by 1975, Western liberals did what liberals always do with emancipation movements: hijack and co-opt them, reducing the meaning of the struggle to the external features of emancipation in order to obscure its material demands.

Internationally it coincided with the worldwide spread of neoliberalism, by the end of the XX doing for global relations the same neoliberalism did to societies: it shifted the focus from from social freedoms to individual independence. By conflating the means of achievement social freedom (independence) with ends, liberalism has thus contributed to universal atomization and growing insecurity, making entire countries as well as individual citizens vulnerable to predatory interests:

-   Liberalism promotes the false dilemma of “imperialism vs independence” in order to convince the world that fight for freedom and decolonization is over when independence is achieved.
    
-   Reducing freedom to independence masks the endless predatory fragmentation of the world into smaller and smaller pieces in order to maintain the colonial relations system of international inequality and exploitation under the guise of anti-imperialist struggle.
    

In fact, fragmentation and the deliberate splitting of former colonies and zones of influence into competing halves have become a tool for maintaining former colonies in constant conflicts with each other—consequently, constantly weakened, maintaining an imbalance of power at the heart of exploitation, in favor of the West, which, on the contrary, was consolidated under the hegemony of the United States in the second half of the 20th century. Almost all permanent “conflict zones” on the planet—such as the Israeli-Palestinian, Iraqi-Kuwaiti, Korean, Indo-Pakistani, Chinese-Taiwan, and Ukrainian-Russian conflicts—did not exist before 1917-1945, and were intentionally manufactured in order to make international inequality self-perpetuating. Having shifted from direct domination prior to 1917 to domination through “divide and conquer” by 1960s, and substituted the idea of ​​peoples’ liberation with the fragmentation of states, the West preserved the system of global inequality and global exploitation at the core of imperialism.

But just as class inequality and exploitation create a revolutionary situation within societies, continued international inequality and exploitation create the conditions for the continuation of the anti-colonial revolution, demonstrating that the disintegration of colonial empires alone is not enough to overcome colonialism.

**Incompleteness of the anti-colonial revolution: independence without freedom**

Understanding why the independence of countries did not make them free returns to the specifics of the [Western understanding of freedom as a property right](https://krystalkyleandfriends.substack.com/p/the-sinister-origins-and-actual-logic) i.e. the right to deny access to whatever constitutes property, which David Graeber wrote about, in contrast to the historical understanding of freedom as ability to establish and maintain social (moral) relations:

> Originally freedom meant ‘not being a slave’, and so referred to people who had social relations. In fact the word ‘*free*’ in English traces back to the same root as ‘*friend*’ – free people are, as noted before, people who can make commitments and promises to others, which of course slaves cannot do.  
> *David Graeber, **“[Debt, Slavery and our Idea of Freedom](https://davidgraeber.org/interviews/debt-slavery-and-our-idea-of-freedom/)”***

The following decades after the geographical decolonization of the planet was completed, and especially after the collapse of the USSR made the United States the sole and indisputable global hegemon, demonstrated this difference in the practice of international relations, when the United States used the power of the petrodollar to dictate to independent states around the world with which countries they can and cannot enter into cooperative relations. The freedom of the United States to prohibit third countries from doing business among themselves has demonstrated that none of the more than 190 “independent” states on the world map is free in their relations with each other. States, therefore, cannot be free if they are not free in international relations, and the United States, by increasingly prohibiting relations with other countries, is thereby claiming ownership of the entire world.

### **The neocolonial complicity of postcolonial elites**

To be fair, former colonies and zones of influence—now nation states—cannot be credited for the impulse to assume their own responsibility for maintaining international order.

Within the framework of the class system—that is, the collective exploitation of the majority by and in the interest of the organized minority—”sovereignty” means a monopoly on exploitation within a given territory.

As long as the overarching system—the collective tyranny of an organized minority exploiting a subjugated population—and the role of states as means of it remains:

-   as conduits for the interests of the ruling class, new “nation states” inherit the priorities of the old colonial empires: maintaining the exploited masses in subjugation;
    
-   the change of hands wielding sovereignty from an overseas colonizers to local overlords does not change the exploitative and oppressive characters of this relations for the subject population.
    

Power in exploitative systems are threatened from two sides: being overthrown by revolution and being lost to competition. The former challenges the very notion of wealth acquired through oppression of many by few, thus excluding a possibility of compromise, whereas the latter is only a question of dividing it up in comparison, which makes it open to collusion.

Therefore, for national elites, decolonization is truly only a question of state independence. Having seized power within a certain territory, further antagonizing the former metropolis by challenging it internationally, where the colonial extractive system of international exploitation, consolidated after the World War II under the US hegemony, still reigns supreme, does not bring any other prospects but losing it, while maintaining the predatory nature of the international order leaves the prospect of complicity in the maximum possible extraction of wealth from the “liberated nations.”

Thus, any liberation struggle, if successful, is doomed to reproduce the relations of economic plunder in one form or another, institutionalized.

As the 20th century demonstrated, even clearly anti-capitalist, humanist revolutionary movements—aimed at and prepared to truly fight for social liberation, the elimination of class barriers in all forms from social relations, and the abolition of economic exploitation at the core of political oppression—cannot escape this logic. Left-wing revolutionary movements proved to be the only political force in history capable of actually challenging this logic at all, waged the struggle internationally and resisted for decades. However, the necessity of existing in a struggle for survival led them to reproduce repressive systems from the opposite side.

A revolutionary spark can ignite a flame of struggle that will burn for years, illuminate an entire generation even. But no revolutionary enthusiasm can overcome the generational barrier:

-   The generation born after the revolution will be shaped by different conditions.
    
-   The attempt to artificially institutionalize revolutionary fervor creates a system that extinguishes it.
    

Thus any nationalist liberation forces, unless they are anti-capitalist, are doomed, if successful, to find themselves on opposite sides from the “liberated peoples” and on the same side from yesterday’s enemies, within the framework of the new neocolonial order that has been and remains in their interests.

Further hostility with them now threatened the preservation of sovereignty over the at their disposal. On the contrary, an alliance with the West within the new neocolonial order was and remains in their interests. The class logic made the ruling classes of all newly independent and post socialist countries by the end of the XX century organically interested in partaking in and maintaining the neocolonial world order, profiting off keeping their populations docile and subjected to the same relations of international exploitation to the benefit of the West that were established during colonial era.

Basically, colonial elites turned post-colonial elites transitioned from imperial colonial employees into “independent contractors” who, for a higher share of the profits, took it upon themselves to keep their populations from Kuwait to Russia, from Congo to the Czech Republic, from Chile to Uzbekistan, from Vietnam to Ukraine obedient to the exploitation system, removing the cost of “security” from the former metropolises and making the system of international exploitation less visible.

Through the turmoil of the twentieth century, which began as the world of colonial empires proved inadequate for the needs of maintaining class order and exploitation, by the end of the century, the international order had stabilized in a new equilibrium of the world of nation states.

The first stage, the collapse of empires made national elites independent in a world that remained under the Western rule. ### **United Nations vs the West**

One of the most toxic influences of the US on global discourse has been the demonstratively disdainful attitude toward international law, the international community, and its institutions, such as the UN, WHO, ICC, UNRWA, and so on. Every “free thinker” around the world repeats American right-wing arguments about the futility and meaninglessness of these institutions in the same tone they repeat American right-wing arguments about feminism and queer activism hindering the labor movement.

The practices of international institutions cannot be separated from the context of capitalism, Western imperialism, and US hegemony in which they exist. And comparing the UN, WHO, UNRWA, and The Hague should not be done with abstract ideals, but with a world without them: a pandemic without the WHO, the occupation of Palestine without UNRWA, genocide and war crimes without The Hague, and so on.

The fact that

• the World Health Organization (WHO) has become the enemy of the right-wingers and anti-vaxxers since the pandemic,

• the United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees in the Near East (UNRWA) is the enemy of Israeli Nazism,

• the global hegemon, the United States, is also the main abuser of the veto power and, by a wide margin, the record holder for refusing to ratify UN conventions, often the only country in the world,

• the courts in The Hague have been viewed as a threat to the United States since the 2003 Iraq War,

validate the reality of their opposition to American hegemony, Western imperialism, and the rule of force in global politics.

There is no other institutional force that would oppose Western imperialism and the renewed threat of fascism emanating from the West. The mocking attitude of the United States and its vassal regimes (aka the collective West) toward international law and institutions will force the rest of the world to take responsibility for the world (in every sense)—and this is happening.

The West has no monopoly on human rights, humanism, progress, or civilization. Today, the West’s primary role in human history has been firmly established as that of those who, while attempting to enslave the entire world, helped the world unite and recognize its unity.

### **The UN and future of sovereign states**

Maybe somewhat paradoxically, yet the idea of a sovereign state will only benefit from the strengthening of the UN, because a sovereign state is the building block of the international community, without which the UN and all other institutions of that sort would be meaningless.

Strengthening international institutions is a very state-friendly idea, because it requires the existence of states, not the technocratic corporate fiefdoms dreamed of by Silicon Valley oligarchs, for example.

As for borders, open borders should not be confused with their abolition. In a legal sense, a state is a territorial jurisdiction, and as long as they exist, there are boundaries where the jurisdiction of one government ends and another begins. Another issue is that the movement of people between jurisdictions must be absolutely free—this is what open borders are, and the binding nature of this right is precisely an expression of supranational jurisdiction, because a ban on closing borders is a limitation of state sovereignty.

In this sense, international law is a natural advocate of human interests in the face of the capitalist state, and opponents of international law, in fact, advocate for the unlimited freedom of violence of the capitalist state against the individual.

## **A better world on the other side of fascism**

No matter how close the current moment comes to the 1930s, fascism will be defeated: fascism is an inherently self-defeating project. The question is how the inevitable end of fascism will come — and at what human cost. The prospect of fascism coming to power victory in the militarily strongest and most aggressive country in the world, even in its “liberal” era, has always been a frightening expectation in this sense. And now the image of a way out of this dangerous moment in history, which is now finally beginning to take the form of an international community, also known as the “world minus the USA”, the “united nations” in every sense, for the first time gives me reason for optimism:

1.  The possibility that at some point the violent and destructive behavior of the United States will meet with organized resistance from all countries of the world gives reason to hope that the cost of meeting fascism in human lives this time will be less than in World War II. Even the United States cannot confront or threaten the entire world at once. In this sense, the more solidarity and organized the international community is in opposing the United States, the less human tragedy will unfold — and, perhaps, the liberation of humanity (including the US population) from the dictatorship of the last empire will also be the most bloodless.
    
2.  The humanity that is able to organize itself for the collective overthrow of the world hegemon is the humanity that is able to prevent the emergence of the hegemon. We have so thoroughly were trained to never expect anything to really change and not to hope for anything significantly better. However, following the logic of the scenario “the world minus the United States is collectively rapidly putting an end to American hegemony,” I can’t help but see all the ways in which the consequences of this unfold beyond US hegemony in the future into a radical change in life for all of humanity for the better in our lifetimes.
    

If the entire world organizes itself sufficiently to defeat fascism and American hegemony, it will be a powerful step toward communism, despite the fact that within each country there are regimes of varying degrees of reaction. This unification will change historical circumstances, reorganizing the world from a space of competition to a space of cooperation, creating the conditions for further progress—whereas the victory over fascism in 1945, on the contrary, split the world in two (thus allowing fascism to return).

The idea of a revolutionary transformation of the world as a result of the revolutionary transformations of individual countries was tried and ultimately failed in the XX century: progressive transformations in some countries simply triggered reactionary transformations in others. The October Revolution in Russia triggered the emergence of fascism in Europe, and ended up splitting the world into the Cold War.

**The path of world revolution lies not through revolutions in different countries, but through a revolution at the international level: a revolution in international relations itself** — which, in fact, is what the victory of the “United Nations” over the “Collective West” united by American hegemony will be: from international relations, defined by the division of the world into exploited and exploiting countries, to international relations of equal participation and cooperation.
