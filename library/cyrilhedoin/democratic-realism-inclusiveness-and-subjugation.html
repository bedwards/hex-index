Very short summary: This essay explores a neglected aspect of the debate between deliberative democrats and democratic realists regarding the possibility and relevance of more inclusive democratic institutions. I reformulate the debate in terms of the subjugation costs due to a lack of inclusiveness. Hélène Landemore’s open democracy argues that demands for more inclusiveness justify non-electoral democratic mechanisms. Reasoning in terms of subjugation costs makes it possible to evaluate this argument. I argue that subjugation costs depend on the stakes of politics. From this perspective, a polycentric (rather than open) democracy could lower the stakes of politics – thus making demands for inclusiveness less pressing, but also making inclusiveness less costly to implement.

There have been a few significant paradigmatic innovations in the theory of democracy over the last couple of decades. One is the so-called “epistemic turn” in deliberative democracy, which acknowledges that the justification of political institutions largely depends on their epistemic properties for tracking the truth.[1] The other is the emergence of the “realist” account of democracy, which urges us to reconsider our normative expectations about what a democratic regime can and should achieve, especially in light of what we know about voters’ competences.[2]

The interaction between these two innovations can lead to unexpected outcomes. In particular, democratic realists can easily raise skepticism about democratic mechanisms’ ability to produce good outcomes or to uncover the truth. It is then only a small step to the more radical conclusion that some undemocratic regimes are epistemically superior to democracy.[3] [3] Epistemic democrats have countered by arguing that “diversity trumps ability,” meaning collective decisions made by a diverse electorate through democratic mechanisms tend to track the truth better than a more competent but less diverse committee of experts.[4] Also, screening for competence can inadvertently (or, for the most cynical, purposively) introduce social biases in the electorate, as socioeconomic and demographic characteristics are likely to be correlated with competence, however the latter is defined.[5]

I must admit that I’ve never been much impressed by the deliberative democrats’ epistemic counterargument, as I’ve already explained on this newsletter. More generally, the epistemic aspect of the debate between deliberative democrats and realists is not the most interesting. More relevant is that this confrontation prompts a reconsideration of the nature of democracy and its normative justification. Realists point out that an appropriate account of voters’ behavior disqualifies any “populist” understanding that locates the justification of democracy in its ability to express the general will. On the one hand, voters’ behavior expresses an inconsistent mix of interests and vague considerations related to the public interest that are largely shaped by political, socioeconomic, and epistemic factors. On the other hand, democratic decision rules are themselves prone to inconsistency and are likely to disregard minority opinions.[6] In line with a long tradition of political economists (Schumpeter, Downs, Buchanan) and the political epistemologies of Walter Lippmann and Raymond Aron,[7] realists locate the legitimacy of democratic institutions in their ability to tame and harness political power by putting would-be rulers in competition with one another, while protecting individuals against various sources of tyranny. This is not a perfect regime, and it can indeed fail spectacularly at times. In particular, (representative) democracy is likely to give rise to a democratic oligarchy that fails to respond to the interests and values of a large portion of the citizenry. Some improvements at the margin are possible; in any case, it is the best we can have.

Unsurprisingly, deliberative democrats are not happy with this deflationary view of democratic legitimacy. In her book Open Democracy, Hélène Landemore regards the realist paradigm with considerable contempt.[8] While she grants realists important empirical insights regarding voters’ behavior, she points out the fallacy in deriving normative conclusions from them. This derivation is essentially based on the essentializing of voters’ behavior, as if that behavior were not responsive to the particular democratic institutions in place. Landemore’s open democracy is a thorough rejection of the idea (that she mostly associates with the U.S. federalist political tradition) that democracy can only be representative, electoral, and embedded within a liberal constitutional framework. Or, in other words, democracy is not the problem; rather, the problem is our inability to conceive it in any way other than through the regular organization of elections. Elections perform poorly by democratic standards because they fail to really empower citizens and, in practice, are insufficiently inclusive. Realists are right that behavioral aspects limit what electoral democracy can achieve. They are wrong to assume that these behavioral aspects are inherent and independent of the democratic institutions in place.

Landemore doesn’t argue that we should fully give up elections, if only for pragmatic reasons. But she claims that the legitimacy of democratic institutions depends on their ability to foster better representation through inclusiveness. While elections are rather bad at this task, Landemore points out that other democratic mechanisms such as sortition (sometimes called “lottocracy”) and mini-juries would be an effective complement—if not a substitute—to truly make democracy open to all. She offers case studies of the French climate citizen convention and of Iceland’s 2010–2013 constitutional process (that tasked randomly selected citizens with writing a new constitution) as empirical illustrations of what an open democracy could look like.

It’s not my purpose here to discuss Landemore’s open democracy in any detail, though there’s surely room for criticism.[9] My interest rather lies in the role she grants to the democratic value of inclusiveness. Rather than the “folk populist” view of democratic legitimacy being grounded in the expression of the general will, a more plausible (but still “populist in a way”) account demands that democratic collective choices be responsive to people’s interests, values, and beliefs. Political institutions are responsive insofar as the collective decisions they produce change when the interests/values/beliefs of individuals change (or new ones are added). Inclusiveness and responsiveness are clearly related, with the latter a plausible proxy for the former. A genuinely inclusive democracy permits the representation of all perspectives, not only by letting them be expressed through voting but also by fostering their insertion and discussion in the public realm. Randomly selected mini-juries are likely to be more inclusive than elections because they leave more space for open-ended deliberation.

Inclusiveness is valuable because it is a necessary condition for political equality. It also has intrinsic worth. Though Landemore doesn’t explicitly address this point, I would argue that lack of inclusivity entails subjugation, i.e., the coercion of individuals to abide by rules or decisions they regard as unjustified and would prefer to ignore. In a polity where slavery is legal, slaves are completely subjugated because they have no chance to voice their preferences, though they would obviously prefer to be free. Subjugation, albeit on a smaller scale and scope, is possible in democracy too. It generates subjective costs that we would ideally want to minimize.

If more inclusivity reduces subjugation costs, it arguably creates costs of its own, including resources allocated to deliberation and the increased possibility of persistent disagreement. This concern was indirectly expressed by the Founding Fathers and resurfaces in realists’ writings. It’s unclear to what extent deliberation effectively favors the convergence of views, let alone produces “good” decisions. An early concern about modern forms of democracy was that it could give rise to collective instability by feeding the collective decision process with a diverse range of inconsistent passions, even the most irrational ones. Crucially, the realists are not arguing that some categories of voters are more prone to irrationality or inconsistency than others; rather, the more the deliberation process is open to a diversity of views, the more difficult it will be to reach a sensible decision.

Everything else equal, the best is to opt for the degree of inclusiveness that minimizes the total cost. Deliberative democrats are likely to consider deliberation and disagreement costs low relative to subjugation costs, and realists’ assessment will probably differ. In any case, we have a framework to capture part of the debate between deliberative democrats and realists.[10]

Without taking a stance in this debate here, let me finish by pointing out an interesting potential development. Subjugation is especially costly when political decision-making touches on aspects of human flourishing or domains of social life that individuals regard as private. It is less objectionable when individuals don’t grant too much importance to the issue that is decided without their input. Meanwhile, while deliberation and disagreement costs are likely to increase with the stakes of politics, it is plausible that this increase is more moderate. Hence, the higher the stakes of politics, the more inclusive politics should be.

A complement (or eventually, an alternative) to Landemore’s open democracy would be a polycentric democracy where democratic decision-making is differentially scaled depending on the issue at stake. Also, in a polycentric democracy, citizens would have the possibility, for relatively low cost, to move from one jurisdiction to another in case they are not satisfied with collective decisions. Some (democratic) collective-decision-making would still be necessary at the level of the polity, but ideally most of it would take place at the local level.

Now, I suggest that in a polycentric democracy the stakes of politics would be lower because, on many issues, individuals have an exit option. If subjugation is too costly, they can change jurisdiction, at least for a large range of issues. By lowering the stakes of politics, polycentric democracy plausibly makes the demands of inclusiveness less pressing, except for decision-making at the “constitutional” level of the polity.[11]

In such a system, most political decision-making would be at the local level and about local issues. It is plausible that in this context, most individuals broadly agree on the general ends to be pursued and only disagree on means to reach them. In this case, recent studies suggest that collective deliberation can effectively foster convergence and agreement. This reduces the costs of deliberation and disagreement compared to subjugation costs and, therefore, increases the optimal degree of inclusiveness.

It is impossible to know which effect is likely to dominate. The effective degrees of inclusiveness at the various levels of the polycentric polity would, in particular, depend on how costly mobility between jurisdictions is. The point of polycentricity is also that it facilitates experimentation with collective decision-making procedures. Rather than assuming that we know subjugation, deliberation, and disagreement costs, the institutional competition between jurisdictions can serve as a mechanism to reveal them. A plausible possibility is that high degrees of inclusiveness are warranted for global/constitutional decision-making and at the most local levels. Less inclusiveness—and thus, elections—may be justified in between these levels, e.g., when choosing a regional or state governor. However, again, rather than fixing these rules a priori, it is best to let experiments develop. Here, open and polycentric democracy converge.
