Part 1: Liberalism and Normative Individualism

Part 2: The Liberal Man and the Mass Man

Part 3: Mass Societies and the Liberal Man’s Predicament

Summary: I address in this three-part essay a constitutive tension in liberal thought between liberalism’s normative individualism on the one hand, and the fact that individualism and liberalism largely helped the emergence of a mass society. In the latter, individuals’ agency and values that are so relevant from a normative point of view barely count as anything in the complex web of causal relations. This creates the risk of a dissonance between individuals’ publicly stated and self-conceived normative significance and the observed and felt disempowerment in multiple domains of social life, starting with the economy and politics. The first part argues that normative individualism, rather than tolerance, pluralism, or public reason, is the core ideal of liberalism. In the second part, I claim that this normative postulate grounds the distinction between the liberal man and the mass man that has often appeared in liberal writings. The third part identifies the liberal man’s predicament, as his self-governance abilities are undermined in mass societies. This creates the conditions that can turn the liberal man into the mass man. The inability of contemporary liberal democracies to respond to this predicament may account for the rise of populist politics.

Looking at the history of liberal thought, one can single out a wide range of candidates for the title of “foundational liberal ideal,” i.e., the ideal on which all other liberal ideals, values, principles, and institutions are derived from. Tolerance is one of the most significant candidates. The origins of liberalism are very often located in the Lockean religious tolerance that emerged in the 17th century. Although this historical reconstruction is disputed and ultimately doubtful,[1] it remains that the ideal of toleration is central in “live‑and‑let‑live” versions of liberalism according to which a liberal society is one where a peaceful modus vivendi allows everyone to live according to their own values and conceptions of the good life. A good contemporary example is Chandran Kukathas’s liberal archipelago where tolerance, alongside freedom of association and conscience, is elevated to a principle.[2]

Individual rights, sometimes interpreted as “natural” rights, also very often serve as the normative primitive of liberalism. Robert Nozick’s libertarian account of the minimal state builds on individual rights interpreted as “side constraints” that strictly restrict what individuals can do in their interactions with each other.[3] Once ascribed to individuals, rights are assumed to fully determine what is permitted, independently of the consequences (e.g., the distribution of resources and wealth) that result from the free exercise of those rights.

Pluralism is another major contender for the title of foundational liberal ideal. This version of liberalism is often associated with Isaiah Berlin and Joseph Raz.[4] Value pluralism means that values and ideals, whether “liberal” or not, cannot in general be compared or traded-off. Hence, there is no decisive reason for individuals and society as a whole to grant priority to some values and ways of life compared to others. Freedom (in the negative sense) is a plausible and sensible answer to this observation. Since there is no reason to impose a way of life on everyone, each individual should be free to choose to live as they see fit, without interference from others.

Finally, the ideal of public reason has been increasingly invoked as the foundation of liberal thought and society.[5] Liberalism would be unique in its endeavor to guarantee that all rules of social morality, from constitutional laws to social norms and conventions, that individuals follow (sometimes because they are coerced to) are appropriately justified to all of them. Liberal institutions are publicly justified and are such that all relations of authority between individuals that develop in society are themselves justified by these institutions.

This is probably not an exhaustive list and, indeed, I will argue below that there is one additional and stronger contender. Before that, it is useful to quickly explain why these different ideals are unlikely to serve as foundations for liberalism. Pluralism and public reason are plausibly compatible with non-liberal principles and institutions. The philosopher John Gray has argued for decades that value pluralism is compatible with illiberal ways of life.[6] If values and ways of life really are incommensurable, then there is no decisive reason to prefer a form of (negative) liberty to another (positive) one. Along similar lines, contemporary populist politics, especially when it bends toward national conservatism, quite often appeals to value pluralism.[7] Post-liberals, for instance, don’t need to deny value pluralism when they argue that negative freedom destroys traditions and is therefore not an important concern compared to other considerations (religion, family). On the other hand, critiques of public reason liberalism have recently argued that the ideal of public reason is fully compatible with communitarianism and other forms of illiberal social morality.[8]

If successful – and I think they mostly are, though addressing all the arguments and counter‑arguments would take considerable space – these claims strongly suggest that neither pluralism nor public reason are sufficient to ground liberalism, though they may perfectly be necessary – and I do believe they are. What about tolerance and individual rights? To entirely ground liberal principles and institutions in tolerance is problematic for several reasons. First, as Karl Popper famously pointed out, tolerance can easily be self-undermining.[9] If tolerance goes as far as tolerating intolerant attitudes and ways of life, the liberal society is prone to instability and fragility. There must be other normative considerations that justifiably limit the range of tolerance. Also, as Ryan Muldoon convincingly argues, there is something potentially illiberal in mere tolerance because it can readily go alongside treating others with contempt and disrespect.[10] From a liberal perspective, it is not enough to tolerate others in the mundane sense of the word. You have to be willing to set aside some of your most entrenched beliefs and values to find ways to cohabit with other ways of life; that means that you need to view others as being worth your respect and your effort to accommodate your normative beliefs. And, of course, you will not make such effort if you don’t think that others are willing to do the same. In sum, a liberal society cannot tolerate everything and whatever it tolerates, this is not sufficient to ground genuinely liberal attitudes.

Individual rights come the closest to properly grounding liberalism. All versions of liberalism recognize individual rights and there is no liberal society without constitutionally guaranteed individual rights. However, what justifies those rights? The question is especially relevant when various liberal rights conflict with one another (e.g., when property rights conflict with political rights) and when other considerations (e.g., social welfare, distributive justice) clash with some individual rights. Even in a Nozickian perspective it is acknowledged that individual rights can be occasionally trumped by other normative considerations.[11] Pretty much all liberals, with maybe the exception of “jusnaturalist” libertarians, acknowledge that individual rights are not absolute. That suggests that rights must be justified, as well as their occasional exemptions.

If we want to search for liberalism’s first principle, I suggest that normative individualism is the right direction to look to. By normative individualism, I mean the idea that individuals‑qua‑persons, in virtue of their agency capacities, are the locus of justification and value. Or, in other words, normative individualists contend that whatever is mandatory, permissible, or forbidden is ultimately grounded in individuals’ abilities to form intentional attitudes (desires, intentions, beliefs) and to interact with their environment. That implies, in particular, that the normative status of laws and, more generally, social rules should be evaluated with respect to their relations with, including their effects on, individuals. Therefore, normative individualism rejects social and political moralities that make the tribe, the nation, religion, or any other non‑individual entity the source of justification for constraints imposed on individuals. For instance, justifying conscription by referring to the “nation’s good” or patriotic values is in contradiction with normative individualism, as these reasons are not plausibly related to considerations that individuals must necessarily endorse.

When combined with normative individualism, pluralism and public reason unambiguously lead to liberal conclusions. Normative individualism also permits qualifying the kind of tolerance that liberalism entails. Finally, it grounds individual rights while also accounting for their eventual restrictions. Normative individualism is a necessary condition for liberalism and provides a fertile normative soil for the recognition of other liberal ideals like pluralism, public reason, and tolerance.

A proper understanding of normative individualism also helps to account for its ethical and political appeal. In his article “Individualism: True and False,” Friedrich Hayek laments – with reason – that the concept of “individualism” has been deprived of its meaning due to many different usages, most of them inappropriate.[12] For instance, in the second volume of Democracy in America, Alexis de Tocqueville notoriously singled out individualism – which he distinguished from mere egoism – as “a feeling which persuades each citizen to cut himself off from his fellows and to withdraw into the circle of his family and friends in such a way that he thus creates a small group of his own and willingly abandons society at large to its own devices.”[13] Tocqueville directly linked the growth of individualism to the democratic equalization of conditions.

Tocqueville’s account of individualism has inspired “post-liberal” critiques of liberalism.[14] Now, independently of whether it is accepted that Tocqueville has identified a real mechanism at play in liberal societies – more on this in the second part – what he calls individualism cannot be the positive foundation of liberalism. Instead, following Hayekian lines, I suggest that normative individualism can be characterized and justified in light of two related features of modern society: the division of labor and the dispersion of knowledge. A society can only reach a minimal level of prosperity through specialization of productive tasks. Specialization permits individuals to exploit their comparative advantages and increases overall wealth. This has two effects. First, it creates more interdependencies between individuals and groups of individuals, thus increasing social complexity. Second, it makes knowledge more and more dispersed, specific, and tacit. Division of labor permits individuals to acquire specialized knowledge, while making their knowledge “deeper” but not necessarily “wider.” They therefore increasingly depend on what others know.

Hayek argues that precisely because personal knowledge in complex modern societies is deeper but relatively narrower, we must embrace normative individualism and its political implications: “From the awareness of the limitations of individual knowledge and from the fact that no person or small group of persons can know all that is known to somebody, individualism also derives its main practical conclusion: its demand for a strict limitation of all coercive and exclusive power.”[15] There is simply no plausible alternative to normative individualism, at least as soon as society has reached a minimal degree of complexity, because there is so much that one single mind can know. Any attempt to “collectivize” social relations, for instance by coercing individuals to accept a particular allocation of resources, is doomed to failure. There is no end that transcends individuals because these ends depend on the unavoidable dispersion of knowledge across society.
