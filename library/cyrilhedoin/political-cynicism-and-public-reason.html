Very short summary: This essay examines a paradox at the heart of liberal democracy: while we design political institutions assuming the worst about human nature, this very cynicism threatens to undermine democratic self-governance. Drawing on public choice theory and public reason liberalism, I explore how we might resolve this tension—and whether it's possible in an age of rising populism.

Social scientists, especially economists, are torn between two general behavioral models. Following the economist Samuel Bowles's terminology, let's call these models the "Machiavellian" and the "Aristotelian," respectively. On the Machiavellian model, individuals are self-interested and self-regarding. Self-interested individuals exclusively pursue their own ideals, values, and goals. The self-interested individual is the archetypal utility maximizer of economic theory, their behavior described by an objective function that reflects preferences over outcomes. However, a self-interested individual is not necessarily self-regarding. They can be altruistic if their utility depends on others' fate, or they can attach importance to conforming to what they perceive as prevailing social norms. An individual will be fully self-regarding if they care only about what happens to them, as in the standard economic model of the consumer. Let's call an individual who is self-interested and entirely self-regarding "Machiavellian."

It is easy to imagine a self-interested but partially other-regarding individual. Such an individual cares about what happens to others and values following social norms, even at personal cost. In some cases, they may even be willing to expend resources to punish norm violators without receiving any personal benefit in return. Of course, no individual can be exclusively other-regarding. In the economic language of utility functions, the choices of these individuals reveal a marginal rate of substitution between self- and other-regarding objectives. I call these individuals "impure Aristotelians." What would be a "pure Aristotelian," then? That would be someone who is not (fully) self-interested in the sense that their behavior is not entirely described by an objective function reflecting their goals, whatever they are. These are individuals who possess an ability to commit to goals that are not their own, for instance, the goals of a collective (a political party, a team) that do not directly align with their own preferences. These models are ideal types. That means we should not expect to find real-world individuals strictly conforming to one model or the other. It's possible that some of us are more Machiavellian and others more Aristotelian, but we are all a blend of these different categories, including potentially what I've called "pure Aristotelian." Besides casual evidence, this is confirmed by experimental research showing that, in specific settings, most individuals display behavioral propensities to sacrifice their welfare for other persons or collectives.[1]

However, this doesn't settle the question: which behavioral model is most appropriate to study social phenomena? Let's be even more specific: what behavioral model should we use to study political behavior and institutions? This question is not only relevant from a purely intellectual perspective. Self-governance —that is, giving ourselves rules that regulate our interactions in making collective decisions and using coercion to implement them—cannot be realized if our behavioral assumptions are false. A society that falsely assumes its members are angels may conclude that enforcing property rights is useless. Similarly, if we believe that political leaders are pure Aristotelians, constitutional checks and balances will appear redundant. On the other hand, real-world individuals are too complex and diverse to adjust the rules of self-governance to specific cases. Even if it's not ideal, we can't avoid the "one size fits all" approximation.

It is from this perspective that we can interpret David Hume's famous remark: "In constraining any system of government and fixing the several checks and controls of the constitution, every man ought to be supposed a knave and to have no other end, in all his actions, than private interests."[2] In my typology, Hume is asking us to assume that we are all Machiavellians when designing our constitutional system of self-governance. Hume didn't have to wait for 21st-century behavioral economics to know that this assumption is false, strictly speaking. What justifies such cynicism, then?

In their book "The Reason of Rules," Geoffrey Brennan and James Buchanan provide an interesting defense of the cynical Machiavellian hypothesis in the study of politics.[3] Brennan and Buchanan are among the most preeminent contributors to the intellectual tradition that is most representative of political cynicism, public choice theory. They explicitly acknowledge that individuals may not be Machiavellian. They may display other-regarding preferences, and in practice, many of them do. They nonetheless argue, in Humean fashion, that it would be wrong to design our constitutional rules based on any other behavioral model. They offer three arguments for this claim:[4]

· Even if the majority of individuals are (impure) Aristotelians, the presence of a minority of Machiavellians can encourage the former to adopt more self-regarding attitudes. Above some (possibly very low) threshold of Machiavellians, it simply doesn’t pay off to display other-regarding concerns.

· The rules of politics favor the self-selection of Machiavellians. There is no guarantee that political rent-seeking will allocate offices to the most devoted, public-minded, individuals. Quite the contrary, we may expect that Machiavellians are willing to sacrifice more resources to achieve their self-interested goals, as they don’t face the same trade-offs as Aristotelian individuals.

· Even if we suppose that there are broadly as many Aristotelians as Machiavellians, the appropriate behavioral model must reckon with the fact that the effective harm inflicted by Machiavellians (who behave “worse” than the average) will be higher than the benefit brought by Aristotelians (who behave “better” than the average). In other words, we should be “risk-averse” because the expected value of the opportunity costs of excessive cynicism is lower than those of excessive optimism.

The first two arguments are relatively intuitive. The first reflects the fact that even Aristotelians are not unconditional cooperators. In an environment where there are too many cheaters, they will stop cooperating at their own expense. The second expresses a "Gresham's law of politics." The nature of political competition tends to favor selfish and ruthless individuals. Of course, the two arguments can be combined to explain why, over the long run, Aristotelians have few chances to survive. The third argument is more difficult to explain in nontechnical terms. In a nutshell, it's related to a convexity assumption standard in economics, according to which marginal costs are increasing. Because of that, to measure costs, you should not look at the average (political) behavior but give more weight to the relatively costly behavior—here, the cost generated by Machiavellians under inappropriate institutions.

These are surely strong arguments, especially the third one. They license a form of cynicism that goes a long way toward explaining why many liberals display a general contempt for politics. As my fellow Substacker Eric Schliesser noted a couple of years ago, this is not without problematic implications:

“Among many so-called classical liberals, politics is near synonymous with rent-seeking. Ironically, this accepts the Marxist interpretation of capitalism.) The problem is that if you turn your back on politics you end up being tempted to put your faith in transitional enlightened dictators or a technocracy that disguises the sectorial interests they promote behind the jargon and the authority of science as a means to silence others.”

If you think that politics is nothing but the ruthless promotion of personal interests through force behind a façade of good intentions, then the temptation is great to regard this whole domain of social life with disdain. Your preference will go to market-based spontaneous, and coercion-free interactions and, when not possible, to technocratic rule. The problem is that the former needs a political framework, and the latter is all about politics.

Brennan and Buchanan are neither libertarians nor proponents of technocracy, and they realize that their cynical account of politics raises another danger. Self-governance involves setting and enforcing rules that are responsive to the appropriate behavioral model. This activity, however, is itself a public good from which individuals will not receive important private benefits. As I’ve discussed in a previous essay, Buchanan elsewhere recognizes that this carries the risk of “rational indifference” where most of us simply opt to disengage from the political domain.[5] More than a century before, intellectual liberals like Constant and Tocqueville had already identified similar dangers, though from significantly different perspectives. While many equate public choice with the cynical Machiavellian behavioral model, most forget that some public choice theorists are well aware of the necessity to transcend it. The penultimate paragraph of Brennan and Buchanan’s book could not be more explicit about this:[6]

“In more general terms, this book is an expression of the hope that a new “civic religion” is on the way to being born, a civic religion that will return, in part, to the skepticism of the eighteenth century concerning politics and government and that, quite naturally, will concentrate our attention on the rules that constrain governments rather than on innovations that justify ever expanding political intrusions into the lives of citizens. Our normative role as social philosophers, is to shape this civic religion, surely a challenge sufficient to us all.”

In the broad liberal tradition, I think that the most significant efforts to elaborate on this “civic religion” over the past two decades come from public reason liberalism. Public reason liberalism is itself a broad church, and not all of its chapels have the same “classical liberal tilt” as Brennan and Buchanan’s. A thread common to all variants of the public reason idea, however, is that political morality must transcend the ruthless pursuit of personal interests. A purely Machiavellian political morality would be self-defeating, for the reasons just identified. The idea of public reason precisely encapsulates the Aristotelian behavioral model, which recognizes individuals’ ability to commit to goals that transcend their interests and private judgments.

Public reason theorists face two major challenges in this context. The first, which is essentially theoretical and academic, is to provide a coherent account of the articulation of the Machiavellian and Aristotelian behavioral models in politics. The second, which is more urgent, is to face the possibility that the very idea of public reason can be rejected by a large number of individuals, including political officials. These individuals conceive politics through nothing but a Machiavellian lens. Brennan and Buchanan's arguments discussed above then come back into force. In the populist era, political cynicism is likely to be a self-fulfilling prophecy.
