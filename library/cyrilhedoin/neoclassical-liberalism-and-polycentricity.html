Very short summary: This essay discusses the recent surge of interest in the concept of polycentricity in liberal thought. I clarify that polycentricity is different from a stateless or anarchistic governance system, though the liberal account differs from the Ostromian one. I suggest that two main considerations explain the attraction of the concept: the limits of political knowledge and the irrationality of the political.

This summer, Eric Schliesser published a series of posts on “neoliberalism” addressing various topics, such as polycentricity, (polycentric) philosophy of science, and AI. I wanted to reflect on the first topic, as it overlaps with my current work. I plan to address the third one in the near future.

In his post on polycentricity and neoliberalism, Eric addresses the reinvention by a new generation of classical liberals through their appropriation of the (Elinor and Vincent) Ostroms’ Bloomington School work on polycentricity. The most interesting aspect of this appropriation is that, as Eric rightly points out, it’s not (only) motivated by the updating of old liberal obsessions with competition and secession under a new framework. It also signals a new willingness to reflect on systems of administrative governance. As I’ve been reading and writing on polycentricity extensively lately, I have some thoughts to offer that are hopefully complementary to Eric’s.

My first point concerns the extreme malleability of the polycentricity concept. In what I take to be the foundational paper for the renewed interest in polycentricity from a broadly classical liberal perspective, Paul Dragos Aligica and Vlad Tarko associate this concept with three features.[1] First, multiple partially autonomous decision centers that can freely pursue various ends or, at least, choose means to achieve imposed ends. Second, an overarching system of rules that organizes interactions between decision centers and determines conditions of entry/exit from the polycentric system. Third, a spontaneous order that results from the decentralized choices of decision centers within the boundaries of the system of rules. This figure illustrates the great diversity of governance systems that fall under the label “polycentric.” This reflects the concept’s history. Michael Polanyi used it to contrast a monocentric and centralized organization of scientific research with a mode of scientific governance emphasizing autonomy, experimentation, and diversity.[2] Polanyi also included the market system and the (common) law as notable examples of polycentric systems. In an early article on metropolitan governance, Vincent Ostrom and economist Charles Tiebout argued for a decentralized and competitive organization of public services where public goods would be provided locally by autonomous public agencies.[3] This article largely built on previous work by Tiebout on the local provision of public goods.[4] Finally, in the work that would give rise to the Bloomington School, the Ostroms used the polycentricity concept to characterize the diversity of community-based governance systems that help manage common-pool resources and more generally address collective action problems.[5]

In the same article, Aligica and Tarko make the interesting claim that polycentricity refers simultaneously to an object of study and a framework of analysis. Combined with the malleability of the concept, this largely explains its attractiveness from a liberal perspective. There is great diversity in polycentric systems that realize the three features to various degrees and in different forms: multiple decision centers, an overarching system of rules, and spontaneous order. Moreover, this conceptualization is helpful for analyzing coordination and collective action problems in a variety of governance systems, including those that fall short of being polycentric. Thus, polycentricity simultaneously exemplifies what many regard as an ideal type of liberal governance system and provides a tool to address governance problems in general.

However, I should note that while Eric is right that (neo)liberals are using polycentricity to study systems of governance, Aligica and Tarko’s conceptualization is biased toward a (liberal) emphasis on competition and spontaneous evolution, especially because of their third feature. Ostromians and people working within the tradition of the Bloomington School may not accept this liberal understanding of polycentricity.[6] As Aligica and Tarko acknowledge, this makes their understanding of polycentricity biased toward positive studies of anarchy. Indeed, they even maintain ambiguity when writing that the concept of polycentricity provides the theoretical branch of positive anarchy studies “a comprehensive way of modeling the boundary between peaceful anarchy (i.e., polycentricity) and chaotic and violent anarchy” (my emphasis).[7] In this sentence, Aligica and Tarko seem to equate polycentricity with peaceful anarchy, while it is clear from their own conceptualization that anarchy is merely one form of polycentric governance among others.

Confusion can easily arise because the spontaneous feature of polycentricity may be understood as requiring that there is no central control at all. Polycentric governance would then merely consist of free (market) competition between decentralized and autonomous decision centers, resulting in fully unplanned outcomes. This is not the correct understanding of polycentricity, and I don’t think this is what Aligica and Tarko are suggesting, notwithstanding some isolated confusing statements. By definition, outcomes in a polycentric governance system are not controlled by a single, centralized entity. However, this doesn’t mean that outcomes escape any form of central control whatsoever, if only because changes in the system of rules can be purposefully introduced to alter outcomes. In this sense, even if the notion of spontaneous order sounds Hayekian, liberal polycentricity more closely follows the footsteps of James Buchanan’s constitutional political economy. This still raises the question of how this relates to the Ostromian account of polycentricity (or how the so-called Virginia and Bloomington Schools are related), but I leave that for more academic venues.

The bottom line is that even a society with a strong state can count as polycentric, for instance if its economy relies on a free market system. Following Vincent Ostrom, Aligica and Tarko speculate that polycentricity in one domain of society is likely to trigger a dynamic favoring more polycentric relations in other domains, so that “market polycentricism seems to entail judicial polycentricism, judicial polycentricism to entail political polycentricism, and political polycentricism to entail constitutional polycentricism.”[8] Though there undoubtedly is a minimum degree of polycentricity required in any single social domain to permit polycentricity in other domains, I’m skeptical that this conjecture holds in general. Modern societies are arguably polycentric, but with highly varying degrees within and between them, including sometimes a state with a strongly centralized administrative organization, as in France. On the other hand, as I argue in a paper, the concept of polycentricity is useful for showing that some libertarian and (quasi-)anarchistic views of a free society fail to properly account for its polycentric character because they neglect the importance of the overarching system of rules.[9]

Let me conclude by explicitly identifying two reasons why polycentricity is such an important concept for 21st-century liberal thought. First, polycentricity is particularly appealing for addressing the issue of limited knowledge, and more specifically the limits of political knowledge. Thinkers as diverse as Hayek, Walter Lippmann, Raymond Aron, and Gerald Gaus have emphasized that the limits of what we can possibly know set constraints on the political organization of society. From this perspective, I think the value of polycentric governance comes especially from its ability to contribute to eliciting what Buchanan and Gordon Tullock call “social interdependence costs.” [10] Buchanan and Tullock’s main thesis is that the political organization of society should minimize those costs. Depending on the kind of decision at stake, this may entail leaving individuals to make their own decisions and eventually make voluntary exchanges through market mechanisms or use collective decision mechanisms such as majority rule. They deliberately make the simplifying assumption that this constitutional choice is unanimous, which implies that everyone assesses social interdependence costs the same way. But social interdependence costs are opportunity costs, and the latter depend on individuals’ preferences, skills, and other determinants that are not publicly known. Polycentric governance is the only way to peacefully reveal those costs without recourse to coercion and without the risk of conflicts and chaos.[11]

Second, polycentric governance is especially attractive in light of what I’ve been calling the “irrationality of the political.” In a society where a form of peaceful and moderate liberal politics prevails, disagreements can be addressed through various mechanisms of collective deliberation. This includes traditional representative democracy mechanisms but may also include other forms of democratic deliberation, such as mini juries. More generally, as a form of life, democracy relies on a public political culture that, as I’ve noted recently, is well captured by the idea of public reason. When politics responds to the requirements of public reason, we may expect liberal principles, institutions, and policies to largely prevail. Polycentricity can naturally be conceived as a safeguard for openness and freedom when politics is practiced along more illiberal lines. Polycentricity then guarantees a right to exit that is both economically and politically valuable. In turn, an effective right to exit will help strengthen individuals’ ability to voice their claims and concerns and make political authorities properly account for them.[12]

I think these two considerations largely account for the recent liberal interest in polycentric governance systems. Clearly, polycentricity is not a magical answer to all concerns related to these two points. Polycentric governance entails specific social interdependence costs that result from miscoordination between decision centers. Even more concerning, it’s unclear how much we can expect polycentricity to be sustainable in a society that rejects liberal principles and the idea of public reason. The justification of polycentricity is also tied to the recognition of the value of toleration and diversity. So, polycentricity presupposes liberalism at least as much as it is a way to safeguard liberalism in an illiberal world.
