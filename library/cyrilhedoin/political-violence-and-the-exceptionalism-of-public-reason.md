*Very short summary: In this essay, I defend the central importance of the idea of public reason in liberal social and political morality. It is incompatible with political violence in all circumstances. The idea of public reason is however a late discovery of humanity and is fragile. It can quickly unravel as soon as some members of the population suspect others to be unwilling to meet its demand.*

\---

A recurrent theme discussed in this newsletter is the idea of public reason. It's not only because it refers to a research program – public reason liberalism – with which I identify and that largely informs my recent academic work. The idea of public reason also has a particular echo in our politically disturbed times where a lot of things that we have been taking for granted regarding the organization of our society and how to live together are suddenly struck with uncertainty.

The idea of public reason is complex and resists any simple characterization. At the most general level, it expresses the requirement that moral and political rules should be justifiable or acceptable to all persons over whom those rules are assumed to have authority.[\[1\]](#_ftn1) In one of the very first attempts to make the idea explicit, John Rawls presented it as an encapsulation of our democratic political culture.[\[2\]](#_ftn2) It expresses at the same time a commitment to certain democratic practices, a way to “do politics,” and a more substantive assessment of values, principles, and beliefs that are acceptable and can legitimately be introduced in the democratic collective deliberation.

As a way to do politics, the idea of public reason expresses at once the willingness to listen to others' arguments, a restraint from bringing views and demands that we know others cannot accept, general sincerity in the search for a consensus or at least a compromise, and – of course – the rejection of any form of violence to coerce others into submitting to one's views. As Rawls put it, in this sense, the idea of public reason entails a "duty of civility" for all citizens, or at least for political actors who, in one way or another, are making rules that will have authority over everyone. In particular, public officials who don't follow this duty, for instance by continually appealing to religious reasons that disregard the religious beliefs of a part of the population, or by threatening to jail a political opponent, adopt practices that go against the ideal of respectful politics. It's not only that such a way of doing politics clashes with democratic political culture. It's also self-undermining: sectarian and threatening behavior is unlikely to lead to the adoption of rules that everybody has reasons to accept… except for the fear of being punished for not doing so!

Even if the idea of public reason is not tied to a specific moral or political doctrine, it also has more substantive implications, once again resonant with our democratic political culture. It sets moral and political requirements that are broadly “liberal,” at least when the idea is combined with other related liberal principles and ideals.[\[3\]](#_ftn3) The idea of public reason is tied to the fundamental liberal postulate that persons are morally free and equal. Free because by their very nature, they have the capacity to form intentions, beliefs, and desires and, based on them, to interact with their environment. This capacity can be restricted by others, but that seems to call for a justification. In other words, [there is a presumption in favor of liberty](https://cyrilhedoin.substack.com/p/is-there-a-presumption-in-favor-of?utm_source=publication-search). Because we prima facie all enjoy the same liberty, any restriction must be imposed by appealing to reasons that are equally valid to everyone, or at least be acceptable to everyone.

The idea of public reason doesn’t entail the rejection of authority but demands that claims of authority that some persons make over others be appropriately justified to them. That definitely eliminates moral views that assert the authority of groups over individuals, and political systems that subjugate the majority to an illusory common good. The idea of public reason calls for broadly democratic societies where collective choices are as inclusive as possible, where political life is peaceful and respectful, and where individuals enjoy the freedom to choose the kind of life they want to live. That doesn’t mean that authority and coercion are eliminated but that the former is justified, and the latter minimized.

I started by observing that the idea of public reason echoes political events. This is so, insofar as those events are the genuine *negation* of the idea of public reason. Consider political violence. Political violence is the ultimate transgression of the idea of public reason. Its use signals that we have given up on the idea of convincing others by bringing *reasons*. It signals that our objective is to force others to submit to our views by instilling fear and eventually by simply eliminating those who disagree with us. Needless to say, there is no civility in this. An important question is what triggers political violence. The first possible trigger is that the politically violent entertain *unacceptable views*, that is, views that others cannot accept because they infringe on their freedom. They have no other way to push for them than by using violence. The second possible trigger is that the politically violent themselves face a repressive political system that ignores their rights.

In practice, the two triggers may be difficult to distinguish. Public reason is an idea but also an *ideal*. Like any ideal, it will never be fully realized in practice. All political and social moralities are partially sectarian. Relations of authority are never fully justified. Political actors always imperfectly abide by their duty of civility. Finally, many views are located in what I would call the "grey zone of public reason." These are views that are not obviously unacceptable, but they are met with resistance in the population. These views are based on reasons that are intelligible to everybody (e.g., religious reasons) but that some individuals have (counter-) reasons to reject for equally intelligible reasons. In such a situation, we are in a stalemate. Public reason is just silent about what rules can or cannot be used.

These “imperfections” make political violence difficult to completely eliminate, even in societies that broadly meet "liberal" and "democratic" requirements. Let's be clear: political violence is *never* acceptable because, by definition, it entails the ultimate disrespect of at least one person, i.e., the victim of the violent act. However, social and political morality rest on reciprocal relations. Public reason is a demanding idea. You will stick to it only if you believe that others are doing so. Hence, a society where the demands of public reason are imperfectly satisfied can quickly unravel into violent political chaos because each individual, or each group, gets the impression (correct or not) that others are no longer making the required efforts.

The assassination of Charlie Kirk has been followed by press articles investigating whether political violence is on the rise and whether some views are more prone to or supportive of political violence. There is [evidence that the answer is positive](https://www.economist.com/graphic-detail/2025/09/12/is-radical-left-violence-really-on-the-rise-in-america) in the U.S. regarding the former question, though at levels far from the 1960s. Regarding the latter question, the U.S. Vice-President J.D. Vance has claimed that data show that people on the left “[are much likelier to defend and celebrate political violence.](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cn0r5y33pj5o)” Actually, data suggest a [more complicated picture](https://www.economist.com/graphic-detail/2025/09/17/is-the-american-left-really-more-supportive-of-political-violence). The measurement of the disposition to justify the use of violence to achieve political goals seems to be sensitive to framing effects, i.e., how the question is asked. The expressed degree of concern with political violence is also affected by recent events and the identity of the targets of violent attacks – republicans are more likely to express concerns when the victim is a Republican and reciprocally for democrats. Maybe the more interesting – and concerning – insight is that individuals in each political camp highly overestimate the support for political violence by people from the other political camp.

The survey of empirical evidence gives at least two lessons. First, there is a relationship between the two dimensions of public reason I've discussed above – public reason as a set of norms about how to do politics and public reason as a set of substantive moral and political views. If you see others as free individuals and your moral equals, you will tend to refrain from using any form of violence to achieve political goals. There is no indication (in the U.S. at least) that progressives or conservatives are more likely to use, justify, or support political violence. But progressive and conservative views are diverse and more or less endorse liberal ideals, values, and principles. For instance, I would conjecture that progressives and conservatives who reject individualism (groups matter more than individuals) are also more likely to endorse a conception of politics as a radical conflict between irreconcilable views. In other words, the question is not about right or left but whether one accepts values that lead them to perceive others as individually valuable moral beings to be respected or primarily as opponents.

Second, beliefs about political violence are partially self-fulfilling and performative. I said above that the idea of public reason demands reciprocity. That means that if one thinks that others are unlikely to stick to the demands of public reason and are eventually willing to use violence to achieve political ends, then they are themselves less likely to stick to those demands. The anticipation of political violence not only triggers political violence, but also encourages people to adopt illiberal moral and political views, views that themselves reinforce the support for political violence. It is difficult to say how close we are to falling into this downward spiral. Even relatively peaceful and liberal societies cannot avoid a modicum of political violence. The point is not to cross the threshold.

It is easy to forget that we are living in exceptional times. Political violence has been the norm for human societies for centuries and even millennia, until some of them found the right circumstances and the appropriate institutions to turn it into something *abnormal*. The idea of public reason is a modern invention that, as Rawls rightly identified, reflects a political culture that took time to emerge and consolidate. As much as the free market economy, it is at the roots of our prosperity.[\[4\]](#_ftn4) But its historical exceptional nature is also an indication of its fragility. Being aware of this should convince everybody that political violence is a dangerous poison that calls for condemnation in *all* circumstances.

But involuntary or not, they commit a fallacy because they implicitly suggest that public reason is independent of other liberal principles and ideals, such as individualism and moral equality.
