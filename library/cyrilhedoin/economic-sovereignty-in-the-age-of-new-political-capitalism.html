Very short summary: This essay argues that the emergence of new political capitalism makes the notion of economic sovereignty more relevant than ever. New political capitalism is characterized by deep collusion between economic and political elites to secure domination at both national and global levels. By destroying the global rule-based order, new political capitalism raises concerns about economic sovereignty in many countries. However, considerations of economic sovereignty risk creating opportunities for further collusion between economic and political elites.

Sovereignty is a concept often used in academic and popular discourse. There is first individual sovereignty, i.e., the idea that individuals should be free and able to make autonomous decisions based on their own assessments of the ends to pursue and the means to achieve those ends. There is then political sovereignty, i.e., the ability of a people (a nation) to self-govern. Finally, there is economic sovereignty, i.e., the control that a people (a nation) has over decisions regarding its economic affairs and policies.

Economic sovereignty represents a component of political sovereignty. Self-governance means that a group of individuals is individually and collectively in control of its destiny. That surely implies that this group should be able to make independent economic decisions. More specifically, economic sovereignty depends on considerations such as resource control, fiscal and monetary policy autonomy, trade policy independence, and regulatory autonomy. A more controversial condition of economic sovereignty (at least from an economic perspective) that is often mentioned in popular debates is the extent of control over supply chains and industries. Talks about “reindustrialization” betray the belief that a nation cannot be economically sovereign if most of the goods and services it needs are produced elsewhere by foreign economic agents.

Globalization has made the very idea of economic sovereignty look anachronistic over the past decades. Economic sovereignty makes less sense in a global economy where national markets are largely interconnected. International trade and division of labor mean that there is no advantage in producing a good that other economies can produce more cheaply. Goods can be bought instead of being produced. Of course, there are economic consequences from losing one’s industries and turning into an economy of services. Jobs are destroyed, and those who lost their jobs will not necessarily find a new one in a completely different economic sector. In the same way, there is a cost for a national economy in losing part of its regulatory, fiscal, and monetary autonomy.[1] But the widespread assumption has been that this was more than compensated by the benefits brought by globalization.

This assumption can surely be questioned.[2] However, the point is that even if it is true, the process of globalization has been put to a halt. Indeed, a defining characteristic of the past 10-15 years is the emergence of what I would call new political capitalism. By political capitalism, I refer to a society where the national economy and the political system are deeply intertwined. Economic and political elites develop promiscuous and mutually advantageous relations through which they can pursue their aims. The economic elite uses the government and the state to achieve, protect, and strengthen economic advantages over competitors – when competitors are not simply eliminated. In return, the economic elite provides financial and possibly other kinds of support to the political elite to help them preserve their positions and status.[3]

Political capitalism is not a new phenomenon. Max Weber already used the concept in a similar sense, in contrast to “rational” or “modern” capitalism, to characterize non-Western societies such as ancient China.[4] The economist Randall Holcombe argues that it applies to fascist Italy and Nazi Germany during the interwar period.[5] However, as Holcombe’s discussion of the concept within the framework of public choice theory clearly suggests, capitalism has a more or less pronounced political dimension in virtually all societies. Public choice theorists, but also political economists like Joseph Schumpeter or Mancur Olson, have offered multiple arguments suggesting that in many contexts, we should expect economic and political elites to collude. As an illustration, a typical Olsonian argument is that because the group of the economic elite is small compared to the rest of citizens, it is significantly easier for them to cooperate to influence the political elite, leading to regulatory capture. This kind of phenomenon is generally recognized well beyond the circles of public choice theory and is pointed out as a major impediment to economic development.[6]

Still, some societies have been able to avoid the most serious harms caused by the excessive politicization of capitalism. World-leading economies have been able to flourish thanks to systems of rules – economic and political “constitutions” – that maintain independence between economic and political elites, while giving the rest of citizens the ability to monitor the political elite and enjoy the benefits of the economic wealth they helped create.[7] The key here is to create and maintain an “open social order” characterized by significant economic and political competition and a sustained associational life.[8] At least for societies that were already endowed with such economic and political institutions, globalization itself has contributed to the containment of the politicization of capitalism. The creation of an integrated global economy has presumably reduced the ability of the political elite to influence economic outcomes and, therefore, weakened the incentives for the economic elite to buy the favor of the political elite of their country.

“The sleep of reason produces monsters,” Francisco José de Goya (1799)

Nonetheless, political capitalism is making a return. The difference from original political capitalism is that it is now widely observed in “liberal” societies that had precisely succeeded until now in limiting politicization. Today’s United States is a case in point. The presence of all the major U.S. economic tycoons at Trump’s presidency inauguration, seated in the front row, is a symbol of the extreme politicization of the U.S. capitalist economy. The fact that politicization is happening while we witness a return of cultural and economic nationalism is not mere coincidence. Because the natural extension of political sovereignty remains the nation state, the politicization of capitalism can only occur under a nationalist outlook. Hence the typical populist and nationalist rhetoric that there are distinct and well-identified national interests, that those national interests are also those of the country’s citizens, and that political leaders embody them.

However, what we observe in the U.S. now is the emergence of a new form of symbiosis between political and economic elites that extends the idea of economic sovereignty beyond national borders. Trump’s tariff policy is largely part of an attempt to reaffirm U.S. economic sovereignty while simultaneously politicizing international economic relations. New political capitalism is deeply intertwined with a modern spirit of conquest. It is the playground of those whom the Italian-French writer Giuliano da Empoli calls “predators,” i.e., politicians and businessmen who aspire to take over the world by destroying any form of rule-based order at the national and global level.[9] What fundamentally distinguishes new political capitalism from the old one (which was at times imperialistic and thus extended beyond national borders too) is the economic and technological context. Economies have never been so integrated with high levels of wealth concentration. Control over digital infrastructure creates new forms of sovereignty that don’t map onto traditional national boundaries. The politicization of capitalism is now global.

By attacking the global economic order, Trump’s America is pushing other major economic powers to reconsider the necessity of asserting their economic sovereignty. This is particularly clear in Europe. Europeans are becoming obsessed with guaranteeing their independence and sovereignty in sectors such as AI and digital platforms because they understand that, under new political capitalism, their dependence on American products and technologies could make them politically vulnerable. This is likely to favor in Europe new forms of entanglement between economic and political elites as well to counteract American dominance, with the risk that, ultimately, this mostly serves personal interests. A genuinely competitive European federalism would be a good way to mitigate this risk. By distributing political power vertically and horizontally and enforcing competition between jurisdictions, federalism makes collusion between economic and political elites less stable. Federalism would also make Europe better able to resist the strategies of conquest of other political and economic powerhouses. However, for reasons I developed in a recent essay, this is unlikely to happen.

The most plausible scenario is that new political capitalism will inevitably spread and will further strengthen nationalist obsessions. Economic sovereignty risks becoming again an excuse for harmful abuse by economic and political elites. There is a sad irony. Populist politics has been grounded on a systematic attack on the elites of the liberal order. However, not only will populism not make elites disappear, but it will empower them with new opportunities to capture power at the expense of the rest of the population.

[2] Quinn Slobodian’s intellectual history of neoliberal globalization argues that the neoliberal project was driven by the aim of disentangling political and economic sovereignty by creating a rule-based global economic order. Nation states were meant to remain politically sovereign over their territories while national economies were fully integrated under a single set of rules. This leads to the coexistence of two separate (sets of) orders: dominion (the world economy) and imperium (governments and states), with the former taking the upper hand over the latter. See Quinn Slobodian, Globalists: The End of Empire and the Birth of Neoliberalism (Harvard University Press, 2018). The failure of the neoliberal project so characterized might then be related to the fact that political and economic sovereignty are deeply imbricated.
