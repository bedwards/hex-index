*Very short summary: This essay addresses two claims made by the historian of ideas Quentin Skinner regarding republican liberty, according to which to be free is to be non-dominated: first, that this ideal can only be realized within democratic institutions; second, that one must choose between republican freedom and alternative conceptions, such as negative freedom. I agree with the first claim but with some caveats. As for the second, I argue that freedom as non-domination and freedom as non-interference are different values that can enter into conflict but are also complementary. Public reason liberalism provides an illustration of the way these two liberties are intertwined.*

\---

A few days ago, I listened to [a podcast](https://creators.spotify.com/pod/profile/theihpodcast/episodes/Liberty-as-Independence-with-Quentin-Skinner-e35peqp) with the great historian of ideas Quentin Skinner. The conversation centered around Skinner’s last book *Liberty as Independence: The Making and Unmaking of a Political Ideal*.[\[1\]](#_ftn1) Skinner discusses the conditions under which the ideal of liberty as independence, sometimes also called “republican freedom,” emerged, and how this ideal has been progressively superseded by the alternative conception of freedom as non-interference. In this essay, I want to focus on two claims that Skinner makes toward the end of the conversation: first, the ideal of liberty as independence can only be realized within democratic institutions; second, one must choose between liberty as independence and alternative conceptions such as negative or positive freedom.

Before discussing these two claims, it might be useful to quickly recall what differentiates republican and negative freedom. Negative freedom, as famously characterized by Isaiah Berlin in his essay “Two Concepts of Liberty,” is a “freedom from” type of liberty. To be free in this sense means not to be interfered with in the pursuit of one’s goals. It’s negative in the sense that it is not assessed in terms of an individual’s ability to do something or even to have a large range of choices open to them but rather in terms of the condition that no effective interference from other individuals restricts one’s choices and actions. Rather than non-interference, republican freedom emphasizes the importance of *non-domination*. Domination is exemplified by the relationship of master to slave or servant. Philippe Pettit, one of the most prominent contemporary proponents of the republican conception of freedom, writes:[\[2\]](#_ftn2)

> “Such a relationship means, at the limit, that the dominating party can interfere on an arbitrary basis with the choices of the dominated: can interfere, in particular, on the basis of interest on the basis of an interest or an opinion that need not be shared by the person affected. The dominating party can practice interference, then, at will and with impunity: they do not have to seek anyone’s leave and they do not have to incur any scrutiny or penalty.”

This points toward a clear distinction between domination and interference. B is dominated by A to the extent that A has the capacity to interfere with B based on A’s interests or beliefs, even though A may never actually interfere. The point is that, even with no effective interference, the fact that A (the master) has this ability and that B (the servant) is aware of this ability is enough to constrain B to doing things that B may not want to do otherwise. Hence, domination without (effective) interference is possible. Less obviously, interference without domination is also possible. It occurs when interference is not arbitrary, i.e., not based on one’s interests, but based on general rules that constrain the interfering party and that the person interfered with has accepted (or has good reasons to accept). Republican freedom is then the condition of being non-dominated; in other words, it is the ability to live independently of others’ arbitrary will.

This definitional work already sheds light on Skinner’s two claims. The master-slave view of domination obviously implies that republican freedom is incompatible with arbitrary political power. Hence, political institutions that concentrate political power in the hands of a single or small group of persons disregard the ideal of freedom as independence because they put people at the mercy of the will of a few. Neo-republicans like Pettit typically argue that this conception of freedom entails the political institutions of constitutional democracy, including the separation of powers, the rule of law, and checks and balances.

Two remarks are worth making here. First, it is interesting (and slightly disappointing) to notice how two significantly different conceptions of liberty lead to virtually the same conclusions regarding which political institutions are justified.[\[3\]](#_ftn3) There is virtually no difference between the views of neo-republicans and classical liberals, at the constitutional level at least. That may be an indication that the republican and negative conceptions of freedom are not so mutually exclusive, as Skinner suggests—but I return to this below.

Second, some intricacies emerge when we look further than constitutional generalities. In the podcast, Skinner insists that republican freedom requires majority rule, while acknowledging that something like the tyranny of the majority can compromise individuals’ freedom-as-independence. That is, of course, precisely why we want a *constitutional* or *liberal* democracy, rather than a mere electoral one. But that’s not enough. Independence and non-domination require the ability to pursue our ends without being arbitrarily interfered with. That requires adequate political, but also, adequate *economic* institutions. In particular, there are good reasons to think that economic planning is incompatible with freedom as non-domination. The reason is twofold: first, economic planning requires that some (the minority) make tradeoffs between ends on behalf of the rest (the majority); second, economic planning cannot proceed on the basis of general rules; it necessarily requires significant discretion. The bottom line is that republican freedom may entail a commitment not only to constitutionalism, but also to polycentric political and economic institutions, including the market economy.[\[4\]](#_ftn4) Non-domination requires giving people opportunities to pursue their ends and to exit relationships that put unjustified restrictions on them. This is nothing but [the ideal of self-governance that classical liberals also endorse](https://cyrilhedoin.substack.com/p/the-ideal-of-self-governance).

The fact that republicans and classical liberals largely converge regarding which political and economic institutions are justified or required suggests that the relationship between freedom-as-independence and freedom-as-non-interference is more complicated than is usually thought. This is also suggested by the fact that some classical liberals endorse a conception of freedom that has overt republican tones. Friedrich Hayek, for instance, defines freedom as follows in *The Constitution of Liberty*:[\[5\]](#_ftn5)

> “the possibility of a person’s acting according to his own decisions and plans, in contrast to the position of one who was irrevocably subject to the will of another, who by arbitrary decision would coerce him to act or not to act in specific ways. The time-honored phrase by which this freedom has often been described is therefore ‘*independence from the arbitrary will of another.*’”

Strictly speaking, Skinner is of course right that the two conceptions of freedom – as stated above – are mutually exclusive and cannot both be correct. However, this strongly suggests that the difference between (neo-)republicanism and (classical) liberalism lies less in endorsing two mutually exclusive conceptions of freedom than in how they articulate two different values associated with what we call “freedom.”

This viewpoint is perfectly exemplified by Eric MacGilvray in his book *Liberal Freedom* (reviewed [here](https://cyrilhedoin.substack.com/p/the-antinomies-of-liberty)).[\[6\]](#_ftn6) MacGilvray convincingly argues that republican freedom and negative freedom are considerations (or values) that mostly operate in two different domains of social life. Negative freedom is fully expressed within market relations and, more generally, social interactions that depend on the existence of jurisdictional rights. These relations are based on the possibility of excluding others from interfering with one’s activities. Republican freedom is the value that reflects the importance that individuals ascribe to the fact of being able to self-govern and be seen as responsible for their decisions. As I have noted above, self-governance entails non-domination because it requires that people control their individual and collective destiny.

When defined as values, republican freedom and negative freedom can enter into conflict. However, this conflict reflects more a disagreement about how to organize social relations (through private individual choices or collective decision mechanisms) than a fundamental contradiction between radically conflicting conceptions of freedom. Republicans may acknowledge that the freedom typically reflected in market relations is valuable, while liberals are likely to accept that collective decision-making is sometimes required and should not depend on the arbitrary will of some individuals. Where they may disagree is over whether private and independent decision-making based on jurisdictional rights should be preferred over collective decision rules that impose a kind of conformity on everyone. For instance, public choice theorists have developed a framework to think about this kind of issue,[\[7\]](#_ftn7) but this framework is itself based on assumptions and considerations that reflect a particular tradeoff regarding the relative importance of each kind of freedom.

Public reason liberalism captures in its own way the delicate articulation between the two kinds of freedom. Public reason liberals place great importance on negative freedom, at least those who assume that there is a “[presumption in favor of liberty](https://cyrilhedoin.substack.com/p/is-there-a-presumption-in-favor-of?utm_source=publication-search).” In this, freedom is clearly understood in the negative sense. But they also acknowledge that this presumption can be defeated. When and how it is defeated are determined in the domain of public reason. Negative freedom is not absolute and must be balanced against other considerations. The idea of public reason expresses the requirement, in social and political morality, to publicly justify to everyone the principles and institutions that govern their lives – including when and to what extent negative freedom is relevant. Public reason is one way to capture the demands of self-governance. The master-slave type of domination is clearly incompatible with the idea of public reason, as the latter presupposes that individuals are morally free and equal. The freedoms of the “members of the public” (i.e., the individuals who reflect on the public justification of principles and rules) and of private individuals who are protected from interferences by their rights are of two different kinds.

Admittedly, public reason liberals are not representative of all liberalisms, and some strands do emphasize negative freedom while ignoring freedom as independence. But the plausible forms of liberalism do recognize the value of freedom as independence and propose ways to articulate it with negative freedom.
