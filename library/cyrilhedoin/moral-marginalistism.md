*Very short summary*: *This essay examines the extension of marginalism to morality and politics. The marginalist reasoning principle holds that past decisions are irrelevant when assessing the rationality of current and future choices. Applied to moral and political matters, this would imply that history is irrelevant for determining what is just. However, marginalism cannot be easily exported outside economics because history provides normative reasons that underlie moral and political disagreement.*

\---

Among the first principles that economic students learn is the marginalist reasoning principle. This principle states that rational decisions should only consider the marginal costs and benefits that a decision will generate. A key implication is that rational choice-makers should ignore sunk costs—costs that have already been paid or will be paid regardless of the decision made. Consider two classic textbook examples:

\- You bought a concert ticket six months ago. The concert is tomorrow, but you're no longer keen on attending—you don't like the band's new album, or you partied the night before and feel tired (though that would have never happened in your young days!).

\- You're the CEO of an aeronautics company. For the past decade, you've invested significant resources in developing space tourism. However, you now realize that demand may be too limited to justify further investment.

Marginalist reasoning dictates that attending the concert simply because you've already paid for the ticket would be irrational. Similarly, continuing to invest in space tourism based on past expenditures, rather than future profitability, would be equally misguided. The millions already spent are irrelevant to the decision. In both examples, basing decisions on past expenditures would be to commit the sunk cost fallacy. Rationality is forward-looking: rational choices are independent of past decisions and their consequences.

A close cousin of the marginalist reasoning principle is the concept of *sequential rationality*. Sequential rationality applies to strategic interactions where individuals make multiple choices in a sequential game. Technically, the “path” through which a specific node in a game has been reached (i.e., the successive decisions that have led this specific player to have to choose between these specific options) is irrelevant to assess the value of the different alternatives available, and hence to determine the rational choice. When a player makes a decision, sequential rationality requires evaluating it based on what rational choices the player would make at future decision points. Players cannot justify current irrational choices by committing to future irrational choices.

The marginalist reasoning principle, when applied to economic decisions, is relatively uncontroversial. Some could object: "What if I value keeping with my initial plan?" The formal (if somewhat absurd) response is that keeping to one's plan constitutes a marginalist benefit. A more practical response is that such preferences often lead to economic ruin.[\[1\]](#_ftn1) Sequential rationality may lead to seemingly absurd conclusions, such as the irrationality of kidnapping (since executing threats costs more than releasing hostages) or nuclear threats (since nuclear war costs exceed the benefits of compliance). However, most often these conclusions arise not because players are sequentially rational, but because we assume that sequential rationality is common knowledge. Regardless, sequential rationality prevents that players from committing to future moves that are not best responses.[\[2\]](#_ftn2)

There would be a lot to argue here, but I’ll just suppose that we have sufficient reasons to accept the marginalist reasoning principle and its cousin, sequential rationality. My interest here is whether the marginalist reasoning principle also applies outside economics, especially in political and moral matters. Consider for instance [a recent op-ed by the influential economist Deirdre McCloskey](https://substack.com/inbox/post/172413745). She starts by reminding us the marginalist reasoning principle:

> “Marginalistism says that in making a decision today about the future we should look forward. Pretty obvious, eh? Think about deciding today how many engineers to work on artificial intelligence in your innovative factory in São Paulo. Obviously, the correct way to decide is to imagine what would be the contribution of the *next* engineer you hire, in addition to what you already do and know. It’s called her “marginalist product.” Then compare it with her wage. If the marginalist product is greater than her wage, hire her. Then do the same for the next one, and the next. Stop when another would be stupid. That way you hire in the correct, efficient amount—by looking forward “marginalistly,” step by step.”

Few paragraphs below, she uses it to strike a blow on economists (Mariana Mazzucato) and politicians (Barack Obama) who ignore the principle to justify taxation and redistribution:

> “Mazzucato never imprinted the discovery of the 1870s on her heart, as a real economist does. So she says things like ‘prices of innovative products do not reflect the collective contribution to the products concerned.’
> 
> Of course they don’t, and should not. ‘The collective contribution’ is the whole society, the past, the sun. The past is the past. It’s given, the place from which your decision in your factory should begin. Not finish. Yes, roads should be built and education should happen. Some of it should be paid for by taxes on you and me.”

Let me unpack. McCloskey is responding to an intuitively plausible argument often attributed to Mazzucato. Most of our productive assets (including culture, tacit knowledge, and other intangibles) were created in the past. The society we inhabit, with its public goods, is the product of past decisions and investments. Therefore, what we achieve today is made possible by past decisions and their associated opportunity costs. Under this logic, profits from current productive activities should be redistributed to reflect this “collective contribution.” This reasoning fits within a loosely Marxian framework but, more significantly, was central to the social philosophy of late 19th-century French solidarism.

McCloskey has no difficulty showing that this goes against the logic of marginalistism. Her own reasoning is however close to commit a *non sequitur*. The marginalist reasoning principle applies to the rationality of economic decisions – economic in the specific sense that they are concerned with the optimal use of resources given one’s objectives. McCloskey’s critique is valid only if we assume that considerations about redistribution and more generally fairness should respond to the very same principle. But should they? There is prima facie no obvious reason, which doesn’t mean that there is definitely none after careful reflection.

If we try to transpose the marginalist reasoning principle and its strategic close cousin to the domain of distributive ethics, we obtain something like James Buchanan’s claim that the allocation of property rights should be made against a baseline corresponding to the current status quo.[\[3\]](#_ftn3) According to Buchanan, social and economic justice consists entirely of mutually advantageous exchanges of rights. This is determined by the disagreement (or “deadlock”) point – what happens if bargainers fail to agree; it's determined by contingent circumstances. It is neither fair nor unjust. It just *is*.

Buchanan and other political philosophers who defend this approach[\[4\]](#_ftn4) tend to allude to a practical justification. Since economic and political relationships occur within a given context, rather than arguing infinitely about contextual fairness, we should focus on opportunities to improve everyone's situation. This makes justice and fairness *path independent*: how we arrived at our current state is irrelevant and does not determine future arrangements. History sets limits on possible mutually beneficial arrangements but has no normative bearing on which specific arrangement should be chosen.[\[5\]](#_ftn5) Since people engage in sequences of rights-trading “bargains” throughout their lives, this mirrors sequential rationality: past decisions don't matter for what individuals should choose now or later.

Interestingly, not everybody agrees with this idea that the disagreement point has no moral value, even among liberal philosophers. Two notable examples are David Gauthier and Robert Nozick.[\[6\]](#_ftn6) David Gauthier argues that individuals’ starting position in a bargain *does* matter to assess the justice of the outcome:[\[7\]](#_ftn7)

> “\[I\]t is both rational and just for each individual to accept a certain constraint on natural interaction, and on the determination of his initial endowment, as a condition of being voluntarily acceptable to his fellows as a party to co-operative and market arrangements – to social interaction.”

This requirement means that the state of nature produces justice only when individuals' bargaining positions are not too imbalanced. Gauthier explicitly rejects Buchanan's claim that it's always rational and fair for individuals to accept agreements that improve their situation, especially when the starting point was achieved through coercion or other unfair means. Gauthier associates this requirement with the Lockean proviso as characterized by Robert Nozick. In Nozick's entitlement theory of justice, the proviso states that owners are entitled to their holdings acquired through voluntary trades only if the initial process of appropriation by current or past owners didn't worsen others' situation. Hence, history matters.[\[8\]](#_ftn8)

Both Gauthier's and Nozick's theories of justice imply a rejection of the marginalist reasoning principle. What is fair today partially depends on past decisions. Justice is path dependent. The broader implications are significant. If, like McCloskey and Buchanan, you believe that the past is normatively irrelevant, then you should ensure we remain in situations where mutually beneficial social interactions are possible. As long as everyone can improve their situation through interaction, there should be no conflicts about justice. This logic promotes what I would call a Pareto-expanding society—a society organized around the overarching goal of always offering new opportunities for mutually beneficial interactions. Justice is all about that, and nothing else.

Reintroducing history makes things messier. Gauthier's, Nozick's, and Mazzucato's views hold that you cannot judge whether a situation is just without examining the past. Even in Gauthier’s and Nozick’s abstract theories, determining whether the proviso is met or whether the disagreement point reflects past coercion proves difficult. There is no clear-cut criterion for assessing whether the current bargain starts from a fair situation. This becomes even more complicated when we leave abstract philosophical reasoning for real history and its complexities. History can be viewed from multiple, often conflictual and incommensurable perspectives. As Raymond Aron emphasized in his epistemology of history, what we call "History" is not a set of objective facts but rather a set of beliefs infused with values and interests.[\[9\]](#_ftn9)

[History thus becomes a source of normative disagreement](https://cyrilhedoin.substack.com/p/forgetting-history?utm_source=publication-search) because it offers normative reasons to justify actions and institutions. Two persons who perceive history differently are likely to disagree on many normative issues because their normative views are intertwined with their understanding of history. This might seem to support McCloskey's, Buchanan's, and others' claim that the past doesn't matter. If it's true, the prospect of finding agreements that everyone views as fair is improved. However, I fear there is no straightforward argument for this claim. Marginalism works in economics but it's far less evident that it applies to morality and politics. More importantly maybe, insisting on ignoring history would mean denying our historical condition – that who we are as individuals and collectives is determined by how we conceive our history.

In saying this, I'm not endorsing Mazzucato's or the solidarists' claim that because, ultimately, we are not fully responsible for the outcomes of our actions, we necessarily owe society. Nor am I specifying to what extent we might do. Saying that history matters is not saying that individuals aren't responsible for their actions. However, it invites more careful and nuanced thinking than merely transposing an economic reasoning principle to a completely different domain.
