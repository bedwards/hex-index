Part 1: Liberalism and Normative Individualism

Part 2: The Liberal Man and the Mass Man

Part 3: Mass Societies and the Liberal Man’s Predicament

Summary: I address in this three-part essay a constitutive tension in liberal thought between liberalism’s normative individualism on the one hand, and the fact that individualism and liberalism largely helped the emergence of a mass society. In the latter, individuals’ agency and values that are so relevant from a normative point of view barely count as anything in the complex web of causal relations. This creates the risk of a dissonance between individuals’ publicly stated and self-conceived normative significance and the observed and felt disempowerment in multiple domains of social life, starting with the economy and politics. The first part argues that normative individualism, rather than tolerance, pluralism, or public reason, is the core ideal of liberalism. In the second part, I claim that this normative postulate grounds the distinction between the liberal man and the mass man that has often appeared in liberal writings. The third part identifies the liberal man’s predicament, as his self-governance abilities are undermined in mass societies. This creates the conditions that can turn the liberal man into the mass man. The inability of contemporary liberal democracies to respond to this predicament may account for the rise of populist politics.

I characterized in the previous part an opposition between two kinds of characters, the liberal man and the mass man. As I pointed out, these characters are ideal types that should not be granted too much normative significance. If the liberal society really was inhospitable to any type of character that doesn’t at least approximate the liberal man, then it is doubtful that it could pretend to the form of universality that it aims to manifest. Still, liberalism is more than a political doctrine with legal and constitutional implications. It also consists of an anthropology that promotes particular ways of life.[1]

Post-liberals like Patrick Deneen and Adrian Vermeule have been arguing that liberal institutions and their underlying anthropology are self-undermining.[2] The emphasis on autonomy erodes over the long run traditional institutions and values on which liberalism has slowly built over the previous centuries. The emergence of what John Gray labels the “hyper-liberalism” of the progressive left may be seen as the ultimate concretization of this thesis.[3] Even if it was, there is solid ground to doubt that this is the necessary result of the endogenous dynamic of the liberal society. More generally, post-liberals mostly reassert an old conservative concern that many liberals have also expressed.[4] What can be admitted is that liberal thinkers have been at times displaying sectarian tendencies, precisely because they have reified the liberal/mass man distinction. In turn, liberal states’ public policies have, on many occasions, displayed a lack of consideration for the members of society who were less able to benefit from the many opportunities offered by liberal freedom and autonomy. But, overall, there is no reason to think that the post-liberal thesis of the self-destructive character of the liberal society is anything more than a fantasy.[5]

Here, I want to advance a different kind of thesis that points toward an inner tension both in liberal thought and in liberal societies. While the liberal man remains an ideal type, he nonetheless expresses real tendencies at work. The rise of the liberty of the moderns has given individuals greater control over their private life and affairs. It has made them sovereigns at their local, individual, level. This would not have been possible without the division of labor and the dispersion of knowledge I discussed in the first part. As knowledge was dispersed and labor divided, the political control of individuals’ lives became increasingly difficult, ineffective, and inefficient. Human societies naturally resist the synoptic ambition of leaders who want to organize them from the top, and this resistance increases as they become more complex.[6] The consecration of the liberty of the moderns and recognition of individual rights is an almost logical follow‑up of this evolution. If individuals can no longer be controlled, they will as a matter of fact successfully claim control of themselves. Individual rights are the normative formalization of a state of affairs that can hardly be changed.

This evolution came at a cost, however. As individuals were successfully claiming rights, liberal societies transitioned from aristocracies to mass democracies. The liberty of the ancients, where individuals (at least a significant number of them) are directly involved in the governance of the polity, had to be traded off for private freedom. Increased social complexity also meant a “massification” of societies in which there increasingly prevails the impression that individuals and their actions are less and less causally relevant. This massification has several concrete manifestations. At the political level, while liberal societies became democratic and progressively enlarged the franchise, they created a new political aristocracy, leaving most individuals unable to directly participate in the governance of public affairs. At the historical level, it leads to the disappearance of “great men,” putting the historical fate of men and women into the hands of impersonal forces.[7] At the epistemological level, individuals became increasingly unable to have a direct acquaintance with the world they are living in, and had to rely on indirect sources of information, which helped fuel the rise of broadcasting and mass media.[8]

In mass democratic societies, while individuals have become impure forms of the liberal man, their agency is literally “flooded” into an ocean of entangled causal effects mediated by a no less complex institutional nexus of impersonal rules. The liberal man has an unprecedented number of opportunities to interact with other individuals and to benefit from these interactions. In the meantime, most of these interactions are anonymous, distant and very often indirect, and maybe less valuable or significant because easier to replace. Of course, as long as these interactions fall into the domains of personal sovereignty, they matter a lot for the liberal man. But as soon as they are related to less personal and more public considerations, they become more distant. In the public sphere, no particular individual is in charge, which can lead to the false impression that nobody is in charge.

As democratic societies had yet to massify, Tocqueville diagnosed that “[a]s social equality spreads, a greater number of individuals are no longer rich or powerful enough to exercise great influence upon the fate of their fellows.” They think that they “owe nothing to anyone and, as it were, expect nothing from anyone.” That makes them prone to “willingly imagine their destiny as entirely in their own hands.”[9] This individual retreat into private spheres is conducive to political disengagement. One of Tocqueville’s most serious concerns with mass democracy is that political disengagement opens the door to new forms of tyranny, whether from a passive but nonetheless effective majoritarian social opinion or from charismatic leaders.

Hannah Arendt’s influential analysis of the rise of totalitarianism pushes this concern a step farther. She views totalitarianism as the ultimate expression of a mass phenomenon permitted by political apathy and individual isolation.[10] In Arendt’s interpretation, the highly atomized mass society turned the liberal man into a mass man succumbing to the siren song of violent nationalism:[11]

“The truth is that the masses grew out of the fragments of a highly atomized society whose competitive structure and concomitant loneliness of the individual had been held in check only through membership in a class. The chief characteristic of the mass man is not brutality and backwardness, but his isolation and lack of normal social relationships. Coming from the class-ridden society of the nation-state, whose cracks had been cemented with nationalistic sentiment, it is only natural that these masses, in the first helplessness of their new experience, have tended toward an especially violent nationalism, so which mass leaders have yielded against their own instincts and purposes for purely demagogic reasons.”

Tocqueville and Arendt convey, in different ways and in different contexts, two similar ideas: the liberal man, in his pure form, is an impossibility. At least, if he exists, his retreat toward his personal sphere of sovereignty cannot be complete. Either through civic associations (for Tocqueville) or social classes (for Arendt), he willingly puts part of his destiny into the hands of fellow men and women and in return expands his own control of social events. Social forms such as associations and classes are a needed response to what can be viewed as the liberal man’s predicament in mass societies: the impossibility of achieving by himself the self-governing ideal that is at the root of his own individuality. The absence of these forms turns the liberal man into an isolated atom, generating frustration, resentment, despair, and ultimately anger.

Nowhere is this predicament more visible than in the never‑ending debates about merit and deservedness. Liberal and capitalist democracies are routinely characterized as “meritocratic.” In mass societies, even liberal ones, merit is however mostly a myth that, when taken too seriously, generates the kind of reactive attitudes that betray the feeling that one has been wronged. Contemporary critics of meritocracy rightly point out that the meritocratic ideal is flawed and harmful in societies that are structurally unequal and, by and large, unfair.[12] We should go farther: merit is normatively meaningless and irrelevant in all circumstances, as acknowledged – from different perspectives – by liberal thinkers as diverse as Rawls and Hayek.[13] In complex societies, the belief that individuals have (to quote Tocqueville again) “their destiny as entirely in their own hands” is self-serving for the lucky few who enjoy social success, and a source of personal humiliation for the rest. The liberal man is just one cog in a gigantic non‑deterministic machine, a cog that is not responsible for its place in the machinery. The liberal man is taught to be autonomous, free, and to bear responsibility for his choices. He can easily become a mass man when he realizes that this is an ideal that cannot be achieved.

Debates on merit are also instructive because they suggest a way out of the predicament. While rejecting meritocracy (i.e., a society systematically organized around the meritocratic ideal), we may also grant that merit is a useful social fiction, as long as it is understood as such. As Rawls points out,[14] what we call merit is essentially an institutional construction, not something that can be assessed in absolute terms without any context. For instance, we may just conventionally agree that someone who earned a large fortune by making risky investments on financial markets “deserves” their wealth. In this case, “merit” just becomes synonymous with “fair” and is subordinated to institutional conditions, such as the definition of individual rights. The value of this arrangement is that it helps to reconcile the character of the liberal man with the crude reality of complex mass societies.

The stability of the liberal society depends on its ability to strike the right balance between mechanisms that effectively confer self-governing abilities to individuals and social constructions that justify mass outcomes over which individuals have barely any control. Contemporary liberal democracies surely fail on both counts. As the case of meritocracy illustrates, they have institutionalized a dissonance between what people believe and expect and what they can actually enjoy in terms of autonomy and responsibility. On the other hand, the legitimacy of their political institutions is increasingly disputed because they systematically fail to be responsive to individuals’ preferences and values – the core of what we call self-governance.[15]

On this account, a plausible interpretation of populist politics and its systematic success in the Western world is that, triggered by the resentment created by the mismatch between liberal promises and expectations and the reality of the mass society, more and more individuals are reclaiming control of their individual and collective destiny. On this view, populism is the political expression of a demand for self-governance. The risk of the perfectionist characterization of the liberal man and the related Ortega y Gasset’s and Oakeshott’s – pejorative – accounts of the mass man is to make populism the sole result of a character failure.[16] While we cannot exclude that such failure has some responsibility in the rise of populism, liberals would be well‑advised to consider first the explanations of the lack of self-governance and how narratives around individual autonomy, responsibility, and merit are inappropriately articulated in our mass societies. Liberal elites, many of whom have succumbed to populist sirens, in particular, should acknowledge their own failures in this respect.

********************

I have identified in this essay an intrinsic tension in liberal thought and societies between the figure of the liberal man and the nature of the complex mass societies that liberal institutions give rise to. This tension is related to, but distinct from, old liberal concerns about mass democratization and conservative worries about the self-undermining nature of the liberal society. As technological changes are pushing massification to a new stage, the tension keeps on growing and, plausibly, accounts for part of liberal democracies’ crisis.

It’s easier to diagnose a disease than to provide a remedy, especially when the disease has multiple and independent causes. At the political level, the main objective should probably be to revise the mechanisms of self-governance so that individuals regain a fuller control of their destiny. Ideas and proposals exist, from polycentricity to new forms of “deliberative” or “open” democracy.[17] There are perspectives to experiment with new forms of democratic governance in a polycentric context, eventually taking advantage of new opportunities offered by artificial intelligence. At the intellectual level, besides the fact that liberal thinkers should consider issues that are viewed as important by citizens (e.g., national identity, immigration) more seriously, it’s the figure of the liberal man and its place in mass societies that should be fully reconsidered.
