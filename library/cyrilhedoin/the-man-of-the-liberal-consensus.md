*Very short summary: This essay reflects, based on a recent biography, on the intellectual life of Walter Lippmann. Lippmann exemplified the post-WWII liberal consensus in all its diversity and contradictions. Lippmann twice confronted a crisis of liberal society in his career. His writings and career bear instructive lessons regarding how to address the current one.*

\---

Since the end of WWII, the Western world has broadly lived under what can be called a liberal consensus. At the global level, the liberal consensus takes the shape of a rule-based order where relations between countries are mediated through international institutions such as the UN, the WTO, or the IMF. This rule-based order consists of trade agreements, international treaties, and collective decision mechanisms aimed at fostering cooperation and containing the risk of conflict. At the national level, it translates into the principles and institutions of liberal democracy, as well as a range of acceptable public policies in the context of a market economy. Public officials’ decisions are supposed to reflect the majoritarian views of citizens, but this relationship is constrained by and channeled through constitutional safeguards that limit the risk of tyranny from the few as well as from the majority. Economic freedom is granted equal importance to civic and political freedom, which implies that the state’s economic role is necessarily limited.

It is no exaggeration to say that the liberal consensus is now under stress. At the global level, cooperation is increasingly difficult as some major countries now reject the principles of the rule-based order. In many countries, the idea that constitutional safeguards should limit the popular will is increasingly contested, and public policies that deliberately discriminate between people are being enacted. The contestation comes simultaneously from parts of the population who consider that liberal society has deprived them of their ability to control their own destiny and made their lives worse, and from political and economic elites who claim the people have been betrayed by those they call the “elite.”

This current crisis of the liberal consensus has some unique characteristics and origins that make it historically singular. But it’s not the first time in contemporary history that something similar has happened, as you will realize by examining at the life of the celebrated American writer and political commentator Walter Lippmann (1889-1974). The new intellectual biography of Lippmann recently published by historian Tom Arnold-Forster offers the opportunity to (re)discover Lippmann’s career and ideas.[\[1\]](#_ftn1) As with any good biographical work, we learn as much about Lippmann’s intellectual life as about the surrounding economic, political, and intellectual context. It makes a relevant, if indirect, contribution to our understanding of the current crisis by showing how the liberal consensus has previously been shaken while still recovering.

Lippmann is indeed an intellectual incarnation of the liberal consensus that prevailed from the end of WWII until the end of the 1960s, when it was seriously jeopardized, especially during the U.S.’s engagement in Vietnam War. More generally, Lippmann’s writings throughout his career have offered a multifaceted reflection on the nature, possibility, desirability, and limits of liberal society. Depending on their background, some will mostly know Lippmann for his 1920s skeptical views of popular sovereignty, which led him to endorse a technocratic conception of democracy.[\[2\]](#_ftn2) Others will know Lippmann for his early contribution to the (re)birth of (neo)liberalism, epitomized by his book *The Good Society* and the subsequent Colloque Lippmann organized in Paris in 1938.[\[3\]](#_ftn3) Many will know him for his political journalism and opinion pieces that cover almost five decades of American and international political affairs. In these different contributions, Lippmann consistently scrutinized different aspects of the liberal consensus, while speaking from within it, very often as one of its most zealous if critical defenders.

Unsurprisingly for someone whose writings engaged with complex, changing, and pressing daily political and economic issues, Lippmann’s views evolved over time, and not necessarily linearly. Arnold-Forster articulates well Lippmann’s progressive evolution from left social liberalism to a quasi-Tocquevillian pessimistic and conservative liberalism, passing through a short neoliberal period before WWII where Hayekian and Misesian themes recurred in his writings,[\[4\]](#_ftn4) followed by his endorsement of Keynesian militarism during and after the war. This ideological instability can surely be partly attributed to a form of opportunism, something that is hard to completely avoid for the daily political writer. It surely also reflects the rapidly changing political and economic circumstances, marked by the uncertainty and trauma of a deadly war. But it’s also an illustration of the liberal consensus’ versatility. The liberal consensus is compatible with a broad range of views. This diversity is at times a strength (it’s an expression of freedom and favors social experiments), at times a weakness (it’s a source of disagreement and eventually of conflict). It definitely sets liberalism and the liberal society apart from ideologies and political projects that are less tolerant of diversity and dissent.

Lippmann lived through – and his writings reflect – two periods when liberalism was hotly contested. First, during the interwar period. Like many liberal contemporaries of this time (most of them participating in the Lippmann colloque), Lippmann was aware that 19th-century *laissez-faire* liberalism was largely responsible for the widespread rejection of liberal political and economic principles in the 1920s and 1930s that directly led to the rise of totalitarianism. Lippmann nonetheless retained classical liberalism’s typical skepticism toward mass democracy, though he gave it a psychological foundation that was then essentially new.[\[5\]](#_ftn5) For Lippmann, as for other liberals, the stakes were high. It was to establish the justification of liberal political institutions, liberalism’s skepticism regarding mass democracy, and qualified support for free-market principles along new ideological lines. In *The Good Society*, Lippmann was clear that old liberal recipes would not be acceptable to populations. At the same time, he insisted that liberalism was the only plausible response to the unavoidability and desirability of the division of labor – the purest expression of modernity. Indeed, in this book, Lippmann repeatedly characterized liberalism as “the philosophy of the division of labor.”

Lippmann’s engagement with the neoliberalism of thinkers like Hayek or Wilhelm Röpke was short-lived.[\[6\]](#_ftn6) This was partly due to the circumstances of war, pulling Lippmann toward other priorities. It surely also reflected deeper ideological disagreements that were already present in the 1930s.[\[7\]](#_ftn7) But this shows that with liberal society on the verge of being wiped out from the Earth’s surface, liberal thinkers of the time were able to insist on what united them and put their disagreements aside – something to be meditated upon in light of today’s context.

The 1960s witnessed another serious crisis that shook what was now the established liberal consensus, especially in the United States. The Vietnam War largely contributed to the polarization of American society. Meanwhile, the rise of the New Right of William Buckley and others consecrated an early form of illiberal conservatism that is now fully on display. Lippmann was one of the major targets of those conservatives who used anti-elite populist language, as the intellectual incarnation of the liberal consensus to be destroyed. In this polarized context, political violence rose to unprecedented levels. It was around this period that Lippmann mostly retreated from public life, though he occasionally wrote for major outlets until his death in 1974.

Lippmann’s last book, *The Public Philosophy*, published in 1955, was heavily influenced by the threat that McCarthyism posed to liberal democracy. Arnold-Forster suggests that it falls within the tradition of Cold War liberalism, building on the then common idea that “popular” conceptions of democracy are at the roots of totalitarianism and drawing a distinction between the constitutionalism of liberal democracy and the totalitarian conception of law that ignores individual rights and human dignity. McCarthyism threatened to push America toward the latter. The book expresses more generally a conservative liberal view of democracy where moral authority is located in institutional traditions and culture.

With all the caveats needed to account for the different political and economic context, Lippmann’s intellectual career reminds me somewhat of French philosopher and sociologist Raymond Aron – a participant in the Colloque Lippmann in 1938. Unlike Lippmann, Aron was originally an academic but was also a full-time journalist for almost two decades after WWII. [Both developed a liberal political epistemology](https://cyrilhedoin.substack.com/p/aron-lippmann-and-liberal-political?utm_source=publication-search), though along different intellectual lines. Both were actively engaged in debates over a major war for their country, Vietnam for the U.S. and Algeria for France. Both developed after the war a skeptical form of liberalism, aware of the difficult choices that politics involves and asserting the need for a political ethic of responsibility. Both pointed out the dangers of a conception of political legitimacy that relied too much on popular sovereignty and minimized the role of constitutional safeguards. Both were acute commentators of daily political events. In short, both were major contributors to a strand of liberalism, sometimes called Cold War liberalism, with a deep understanding of the fragile foundations of liberal society, at a time when the threat of totalitarianism was still real.

There is one major difference between Lippmann and Aron, though. From the end of WWII until the end of his career, Lippmann was clearly a major intellectual representative of the liberal consensus. His writings reflect the struggle to maintain this consensus in the face of its internal contradictions[\[8\]](#_ftn8) and its external threats. Aron’s position was less comfortable, as illustrated by the popular saying “mieux vaut avoir tort avec Sartre que raison avec Aron” (”better to be wrong with Sartre than right with Aron”). Though post-WWII France was a liberal democracy, Aron’s Cold War liberalism was never mainstream. The ideological strength of communism in France, the remains of the Bonapartist conception of political legitimacy (felt in the constitutional design of De Gaulle’s Fifth Republic), the war in Algeria, and the cultural uprising peaking in May 1968 were all factors that contributed to making Aron’s views controversial.

Looking at the lives of liberal warriors like Lippmann and Aron is instructive. It shows that even when there was an established liberal consensus, it was never uncontested. It also points toward intellectual and political strategies. But of course, times are different. Illiberalism is far stronger today than it has ever been since WWII. The intellectual crisis of liberalism has already turned into a political crisis. Almost a hundred years ago, it was the combination of a strategic ideological program, started with the Colloque Lippmann, and a deadly world war that planted the seeds of the 20th-century liberal consensus. What about the 21st’s?
