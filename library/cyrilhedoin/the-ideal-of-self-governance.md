*Very short summary: In this essay, I characterize the ideal of self-governance and show why it is connected to the notion of polycentricity, and more specifically, democratic polycentricity. Self-governance is about ensuring that individual and social preferences are appropriately related. This cannot be systematically guaranteed through purely private (market relations) nor purely public (state coercion) institutional arrangements. The frontiers between the public and the private are constantly and unexpectedly moving, and polycentric governance is the best way to permit the required adjustment.*

\---

I’ve [written recently](https://cyrilhedoin.substack.com/p/neoclassical-liberalism-and-polycentricity) about the surge of interest in the concept of polycentricity in contemporary liberal thought. Today, I want to embed this discussion within a broader intellectual context. More specifically, I shall argue that most recent developments in liberal thought are related to what I call **the ideal of self-governance**.

The ideal of self-governance is best expressed by the U.S. Constitution’s first three words, “We the people.” These words state that the ultimate justification of government is to serve its citizens. Similarly, the third article of the 1958 French Constitution states that “national sovereignty belongs to the People who exercise it by means of their representatives and by referendum.” Self-governance refers to this idea that, as free and equal individuals, we should be in control of our destiny. Either we make choices ourselves, or, if not possible or too complicated, we delegate this responsibility to representatives that are endowed with the task of pursuing our interests and abiding by our values when in office.

Attentive readers may have noticed that the title of this essay uses “ideal” rather than “idea” of self-governance. In our less-than-ideal societies, self-governance is never fully realized. For reasons I’ll develop below, it may just be impossible to achieve. In some cases, it may even be undesirable. However, the fact that an ideal cannot be achieved or should sometimes not be realized doesn’t imply that it is irrelevant and should be ignored. Considerations of feasibility and desirability are obviously relevant, especially in the political domain. That means that pursuing the ideal is not the only aim that matters. Nonetheless, insofar as we agree that realizing self-governance is feasible and doesn’t have adverse consequences, it is a relevant consideration to justify our political institutions and choices.

What distinguishes self-governance from other political ideals is that it is largely shared by otherwise very different moral and political stances. Indeed, even leaders whose decisions actually undermine people’s ability to self-govern tend to use this ideal as part of their rhetoric. Such broad acceptance and use create the risk that the ideal is actually empty. It may just be a façade to justify the illegitimate exercise of political power and the coercion of individuals in the name of a greater good that they are supposed to endorse.

From this perspective, one could argue that the *genuine* ideal of self-governance is tied to democracy and democratic regimes. When Alexis de Tocqueville marveled at the young U.S. democracy, it was largely because he saw in it the realization of the ideal of self-governance in the context of modernity.[\[1\]](#_ftn1) This was, for Tocqueville, the true revolution – a revolution that would inevitably submerge the modern world. However, precisely because not all revolutions actually lead to self-governance, we cannot equate the ideal with democracy. From this perspective, what sets liberals apart is their early recognition that democracy is not a sufficient condition for self-governance. Tocqueville foresaw this when he lucidly described, albeit dramatically, the potential side effects of the egalitarianism and individualism that the democratic form of life carries with it. As I have argued several times in this newsletter, contemporary populist politics largely confirms Tocqueville’s fears, as it builds on political indifference, passiveness, and disenchantment, and the corresponding individuals’ retreat into their private sphere. The very success of populists who falsely appeal to self-governance is proof that modern democracy has failed to realize this ideal.

This analysis suggests the ideal of self-governance cannot be understood and pursued by considering only the strictures of democratic regimes – again, an early Tocquevillian insight. Indeed, self-governance is nowadays closer to being realized in the private domain. Though this will sound counterintuitive to many, market relations *are* the expression of self-governance. They result from individuals’ freedom and autonomy to pursue their goals without other limitations than their capacity to offer something of value to the rest of society. Moreover, the existence of market institutions, especially market prices, benefits everybody. Prices inform and incentivize; more specifically, they inform by creating agents’ incentives to make rational tradeoffs and adjust them when needed, and they incentivize agents by providing them information about agents’ preferences and production costs. In a market economy, individuals can freely pursue their interests and achieve their goals without undermining others’ freedom. Quite the contrary, in the ideal case, their (negative) freedom to make choices without interference enhances others’ (positive) freedom to achieve their own personal values and ideals.

Surely, what we can call market freedom doesn’t guarantee the realization of self-governance. First, people’s self-governing ability in the market is directly determined by their capacity to produce value for others. This capacity itself depends on a range of factors that individuals don’t control: their skills, many of which are acquired at birth; the social environment in which they have been educated and raised; and brute luck with respect to events that may significantly and definitively affect the course of their life. Market mechanisms allocate resources based on individuals’ willingness to pay, and we know that this willingness is affected by wealth, and hence by people’s capacity to produce wealth. Arguably, unskilled and unlucky individuals will struggle to achieve self-governance through market relations alone.

Second, some goods essential for the realization of self-governance may not be properly produced and consumed through market relations. Collective goods—goods whose use cannot be restricted to a subset of the population (i.e., those who paid)—cannot be priced properly. The use of common pool resources or the provision of national defense, arguably major conditions for self-governance, are likely to require different institutional arrangements. Similarly, the provision of the required conditions for the healthy development of market relations (e.g., sound money, well-defined and enforced property rights, trustful information) also has, to some degree, the characteristics of a collective good.

In these cases, the realization of the ideal of self-governance may seem to require again an appeal to the political power of the democratic state. If self-governance cannot be achieved through private channels, then we should use public ones. However, one of the fundamental insights of modern (liberal) political economy is that the distinction between the private and the public is blurred and changing. Economic students learn (or at least, they should!) at some point that the distinction between private (or club) and public (or common) goods is not set by their intrinsic characteristics. First, it is relative to the technological state. Second, it depends on formal institutional rules, such as the constitution and the law. Third, it is affected by cultural norms and values. Hence, while music was a private good for most of the 20th century, it turned into a genuine public good when the digital economy rose at the turn of the 21st. Nowadays, given most consumers’ practices, it is mostly a club good that is consumed on streaming platforms that charge an entry fee and then provide a good free from consumption externalities. Interestingly, while the technology still makes the exclusion of non-paying consumers difficult, consumption habits signal that most individuals are willing to pay to benefit from an “augmented” product (no advertising, good quality, no risk of catching a virus).[\[2\]](#_ftn2)

Thus, self-governance largely depends on what happens in the grey area between the fully private (i.e., market relations) and the fully public (i.e., regulation through state coercion). The private and the public are two ideal types—two ways to understand the relationship between what people want and value (individual preferences) and what society is collectively doing (social preferences). In the fully private domain, what society “does” is just the spontaneous result of what individuals do to achieve their objectives. There is no “social welfare function” per se.[\[3\]](#_ftn3) In the fully public domain, individuals are coerced to abide by the decisions made by a “benevolent dictator” based on social preferences. In a democracy, these preferences are supposed to reflect individual tastes and values, though how they are established is unclear.

These two ideal types are largely fictional. Even for private goods, market relations are not the mere expression of independent and stable individual preferences. Individual preferences are shaped by social influences, including considerations about the “common good.”[\[4\]](#_ftn4) The idea that the democratic state acts based on social preferences that reflect individuals’ preferences is the fiction that supports the “populist” version of the ideal of self-governance, a vision that welfare economists have historically fed. Public choice economists have largely undermined this idea, at the cost sometimes of promoting a kind of [political cynicism](https://cyrilhedoin.substack.com/p/political-cynicism-and-public-reason) that is itself detrimental to self-governance.

Political economists like James Buchanan or Elinor and Vincent Ostrom have rightly insisted on the necessity to look at institutional arrangements that don’t fit the rigid private/public mold.[\[5\]](#_ftn5) This leads us back to the notion of polycentricity and its relationship with the ideal of self-governance. More specifically, a *polycentric democracy* is attractive as soon as we acknowledge that the relationship between individual and social preferences cannot be reduced to a systematic formula (or set of formulae) known in advance.

First, information about individual preferences (or other relevant considerations, such as production costs) is typically dispersed and local. It’s a well-known virtue of market mechanisms to reveal and aggregate this information through the price system, but as we have seen, they are not always effective.

Second, not only do individuals have conflicting preferences, but they also disagree about the common good (the appropriate “social welfare function”). The naïve answer is to say that what constitutes the common good should be settled by a democratic vote. But that neither guarantees that everybody’s preferences will be duly considered, nor that those enjoying political power will make choices in favor of the common good.

Third, there are many potential institutional arrangements to make collective choices, but it is difficult to know what Buchanan and Tullock call the social interdependence costs associated with them.[\[6\]](#_ftn6) Most of the time, they can only be evaluated ex post, once we have experimented with them.

Fourth, circumstances are continuously changing: new technologies unexpectedly appear, people’s tastes and values evolve due to multiple influences, and new social practices with unknown costs and benefits emerge. These changes affect social interdependence costs and the divide between the private and the public.

These considerations suggest that self-governance is not a state but a process. Polycentricity is the social organization that naturally responds to this observation. As Michael Polanyi remarked in his pioneering discussion of polycentricity, this is why polycentric organizations of science and the economy are preferable to monocentric ones.[\[7\]](#_ftn7) This was also the argument of Vincent Ostrom, Tiebout, and Warren in their discussion of polycentricity in the case of municipal governance.[\[8\]](#_ftn8) Finally, this provides strong support in favor of federalism at the level of national politics.[\[9\]](#_ftn9)

The justification of polycentric democracy is to provide the constitutional and social framework for favoring social, economic, and political experiments in search of the best way to align individual with social preferences in different sets of circumstances, while minimizing the external costs that such experiments may have on individuals who are not participating in them. This is what the ideal of self-governance is all about. As I said, the ideal is likely to never be fully achieved, and other, at times more urgent, considerations may weaken the case for political polycentricity. However, if you value self-governance, you must argue for more polycentric forms of democracy than those that currently prevail.
