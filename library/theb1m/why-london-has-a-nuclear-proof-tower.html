This is the GO tower or what we now call the BT tower. This strange giant cylinder would connect a country that had been shattered by World [music] War II. [music] The London of the 1950s and60s was very different to the London of today.

Decimated by the Second World War, entire districts and neighborhoods had been flattened by bombing, and the infrastructure that had survived was outdated and creaking under the demands of a rapidly modernizing society. The tallest building was still the 111 m St. Paul's Cathedral, which had remarkably survived the Blitz. Despite this, rebuilding London actually presented an incredible opportunity, especially as the city was undergoing a telecommunications revolution.

Once reserved only for the wealthy elite, telephones were quickly becoming essential for everyday Londoners. On top of that, television, still a novelty during the war, was now a national obsession. As such, signals had to travel further and faster. They also had to be more reliable than ever before.

And all of this fell at the feet of the General Post Office. The GO oversaw not just all of Britain's postal services, but its telephone and television services, too. And it was buckling under the pressure. Until this point, the UK had relied on traditional copper cables laid underground, but these were limited.

Amplifiers were required every few kilometers, and laying new trunk routes through a city as dense as London was next to impossible. Engineers were desperately in need of a better solution. Microwave radio transmission changed everything. It meant that telephone calls and television pictures could be carried wirelessly all across the country.

But there was just one problem. Microwave signals travel in straight lines, meaning lines of sight were needed between each transmission mast. That meant that in a sprawling metropolis like London, engineers needed a tower that was tall enough to rise clear above the rooftops. In 1955, Britain began planning a new trunk microwave network.

Relay towers would be constructed across the country in Birmingham, Manchester, Leeds, Bristol, and beyond. And in London would be a central node tying it all together. This node would house not only dishes, antennas, and offices, but to help recoup the £2.5 million build cost, equivalent to around £60 million today, it would also hold a restaurant. At the time, engineers and architects alike knew that what they were designing was more than just public infrastructure.

By the very nature of its location and scale, it would have to be a new landmark, a modern building for a modern Britain. Enter Eric Bedford. He was the chief architect of the Ministry of Public Building and Works and was entrusted with the design of this tower. He was also the youngest person ever to hold that position.

His proposition was daring, a cylindrical shaft of reinforced concrete 177 m tall, [music] vastly dwarfing anything that had ever been built in London or the UK before. Unlike square or octagonal towers, this circular form offered the best wind resistance, ensuring that gusts would sweep around its surface rather than battering flat walls. You see, this wasn't just about style. It was [music] about precision.

For microwave dishes to function, they needed accuracy. A structure that swayed, even slightly, could cause connections [music] to fail. Bedford's circular concept was really the only answer. It was designed to shift no more than 25 cm in winds of [music] up to 150 km an hour.

To stabilize it, Bedford designed massive foundations. [music] Unfortunately, surveyors found that the hard chalk that was needed to support them was more than 52 m below London, too deep to be of any use. Instead, engineers sank a square concrete raft 27 m wide by 27 m long and 1 m thick, reinforced with six layers of cables. A concrete pyramid was then built on top of this raft, which would support the immense weight of the tower that came in at more than 13,000 [music] tons.

Inside, the tower's core housed two high-speed lifts. Around them, thick concrete walls bore the vertical load. Every few meters, protruding platforms would hold out the heavy microwave dishes. Work began in June 1961, and Londoners soon noticed the strange cylinder rising steadily above Fitz Rovia.

The construction method was cutting edge for its time. It was built using a combination of a climbing crane and a specialized climbing formwork system to construct its core, a method that was a predecessor to the modern systems we use today. A crane at the very top lifted materials and climbed with the tower. While for the concrete core, once a section hardened, the formwork was jacked up and another pour began.

This allowed the tower to rise quickly and safely, a weekby- week ascent that became a spectacle in itself. By 1963, the structure had reached its full height. Standing above the rooftops, it was already the tallest tower in London. But the work was far from done.

Steel work for the upper levels was lifted into place by cranes perched hundreds of feet above the ground. A truly terrifying task in the days before modern safety harnesses and regulations. The microwave dishes were installed on cantalvered platforms. Each one was up to 4 m across built from aluminium with protective covers.

They were precisely angled to connect with relay towers right across Britain forming a national web of microwave beams. The central node was complete. At its peak, the tower bristled with more than 50 dishes, giving it the appearance of something out of the future. The post office tower was officially opened on October the 8th, 1965 by Prime Minister Harold Wilson.

Its dominance of the skyline was unmatched, visible from across London. It became an instant landmark, surpassing even the chimneys of Batisy Power Station. >> First time I see it for ages, I thought it was all right. >> I think it's ugly.

>> You know, it's one of them things. It's stuck there. I've never been up it. That's the only thing.

>> I think it looks pretty magnificent, actually. >> They're not going to knock it down, are they? >> For engineers, it represented the future of communications. Television pictures, telephone calls, and data all flowed through the tower.

It was in every sense the beating heart of Britain's new communications network. But for the public, it was the revolving restaurant that captured the imagination. Located on the 34th floor, the restaurant completed a full rotation every 23 minutes, offering diners panoramic views of London. It quickly became one of the most glamorous venues in the entire city.

But behind the glamour, there was a darker reality. The post office tower was also a piece of cold war infrastructure. Its microwave links were vital for defense. In the event of war, signals from governments, military, and emergency services would be routed through the tower, which made it a prime target.

So, the tower couldn't appear on any official maps, and parliamentary questions about it were stonewalled. It was even an offense under the official secrets act to photograph it in certain context. The tower had other secrets, too. Unlike many skyscrapers of its era, it was built with an unusual brief to withstand the unthinkable.

a nuclear strike. Its narrow cylindrical form, apart from reducing wind loads, was also designed to lessen the impact of a blast wave. The heavily reinforced concrete shaft at its core would remain standing even if much of the structure around it was obliterated. Communications equipment was tucked away deep inside, shielded by concrete and steel, ensuring that Britain could stay connected in a time of crisis.

Of course, this is all relative. A direct hit would reduce even the toughest of structures to rubble, but the BT tower's design meant it could endure the peripheral shock waves of an attack better than most other structures. Inside the tower itself, life was more functional than glamorous. Floors housed racks of equipment.

Microwave transmitters, amplifiers, switching gear. Engineers worked around the clock to maintain this incredibly complex system. The lifts were among the fastest in Europe, taking staff and visitors to the top in under 30 seconds. Power was supplied by multiple feeds with backup generators to keep communications running in case of an emergency.

People who worked there described the tower as a world of its own. Its resilience was tested on the 31st of August 1971 when a bomb planted by the provisional IRA exploded in the restaurant toilets on the 31st floor. The blast caused significant damage and injured several people. And while the restaurant did reopen briefly, the sense of risk remained.

In 1980, it closed its doors forever. From then on, the tower's role as a public attraction faded, and its communications function became its primary identity. But the very reason this tower was built soon became its biggest problem. By the 1980s, new technology was emerging.

fiber optic cables could carry far more data more reliably and without the line of sight limitations of microwave signals. Within a generation, the tower's original purpose was obsolete. The dishes were stripped away, leaving a cleaner silhouette, but also stripping the tower of its purpose. Today, it's far from London's tallest building.

Yet, its height is still impressive compared to its immediate surroundings. What's unique about it is that unlike the city or Canary Warf, Bey Tower stands remarkably alone. There's no cluster of skyscrapers here. Planners have resisted high-rise development in Fitzrovia to avoid blocking sight lines to and from the tower.

If tall buildings had been constructed nearby, they would have blocked its signals. [music] For decades, that alone was enough to limit development in this area. Even after the dishes were removed in the 2000s and these lines of sight were no longer needed, the planning legacy has remained. [music] Traveling up the tower today, you can see some truly remarkable uninterrupted views of London, which makes it perfect for its new purpose, a hotel.

BT sold the tower to MCR Hotels, an American company that's partnered with Heatherwick Studio to transform this icon. Heatherwick is well known for its ambitious and impressive work. The studio designed New York's vessel and Little Island, and its reimagining of BC Tower will no doubt be just as impressive and unexpected. Their design team is of course going to face numerous challenges, not least because BC Tower is now grade 2 listed, meaning its historical and architectural character is protected.

Any major changes have to respect that heritage status. The tower can't be easily deconstructed either. If it can survive a nuclear blast, then it can survive modern London developers. It can't just be taken apart to make way for a shiny new skyscraper.

But maybe that's a good thing. Modern cities can be quick to write off old buildings, especially those made in the middle of the 20th century that dare to be modernist or, god help us, brutalist. However the future may shape it, this indestructible cylinder is a towering monument to postwar Britain. and its indestructible spirit.
