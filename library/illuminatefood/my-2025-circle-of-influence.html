2025 will always be the year when things started to fall into place, and it all kind of made sense.

I was with my husband’s niece over the holidays, and she told me about the 2020 Taylor Swift song "Invisible String" as we talked about two people who met, felt a spark, but then their paths veered in different directions. Still, years later, they ended up together thanks to the invisible string connecting them.

I don’t think this concept only applies to romance. Every decision leads you somewhere new, but if you believe in the string, it will take you where you need to go.

2025 was a year of uncertainty for me. What was I doing with my career? What should I be writing about? Should I go to culinary school? How will I make money? Endless questions and frustrations.

Nothing is ever set in stone, but many of those questions have finally been answered. I said yes to a hunch that zeroing in on beans made more sense for me than writing about food systems, and to giving Instagram a real shot by consistently creating content. This choice was met with a big YES after a simple carousel post sharing my story went viral, confirming my belief that there are people on Instagram who want to learn and make changes for a better future, not just doomscroll.

Then my career started to fall into place, finally choosing a job that would be a creative outlet and not just something I should do. I took a risk and chose freelance over a 9-5, and I feel like my true self again.

I kept cooking. The more beans I made, the more confident I was about sharing recipes with people. I don’t need to make fancy recipes. Telling someone you can mash beans with olive oil, hot sauce, salt, and pepper and spread them on toast instead of avocado can be valuable, too.

Some of this change came through meditations and visualizations, or through ideas in the shower, but some of it came through talking with friends and mentors, and through expanding my world.

I wrote this post last year, and I still believe the word "influencer" carries an unnecessarily bad reputation. My rant is that some people provide value and do more than post aspirational content that makes us feel inadequate. One of the best messages I received this year was from of This Needs Hot Sauce, telling me I’m a good influence (to eat more beans), or when people send me photos of the beans they are eating, or tell me they felt inspired to buy a bag of beans.

I am honored if I’ve influenced you to eat more beans, to think about where your food comes from, or to consider the impact of what we eat on the planet. If so, I am doing what I aim to be doing.

As the year closes, I like to take some time to reflect on all the people who have positively influenced me - both as a way to mark growth and to highlight how important it is to stay close to folks you admire.

My 2026 Influencers

The Bean Community: Leaning all the way into beans this year changed my life. Beyond having a bean post go viral, the most fun has been connecting with others through the lens of beans. Imagine any other hobbyists finding each other online - the bond is real! I have gotten to know Madeline of , who originally grew her account by only eating beans for 30 days and the Foodprint team who organizaed a campaign to Eat More Beans (this podcast episode is amazing,) a new friend Emma who has a chickpea flatbread business, Faina, and The Beans is How group run by Paul Newman - and we all instantly bonded over beans! I am grateful to have found my people, and yes, we are occasionally called the leguminati.

Rachel Karten - Thank god for ! Now that I work in marketing, she watches all the chaos that is social media and somehow zooms out to identify trends, what type of content is working, and why. She brings an academic lens to social media and helps me articulate some of the bigger ideas and the psychology behind it. Also, a personal highlight of the year is when she mentioned my viral bean post in her newsletter here.

Freelancers Everywhere - Thank you to every freelancer I met, talked to, or read their content this past year. I could never be a freelancer without all the stories of how challenging it can be, yet also how often it's worth it in the end. It doesn’t really make sense, but even if I work 10-5 pm most days and a couple of hours in the evenings, getting to take off an afternoon every couple of weeks, or to decline a call because I have something else going on, gives me a huge thrill. It completely shifts the power dynamic, and yes, it’s more work, but it’s also more freedom. Also, shout out to my mom, who paved the freelance road ahead of me.

My Acupuncturist - I recently started seeing an acupuncturist, and she has given me many tips and tricks for how to improve my health. She is very focused on keeping my cortisol down, which spikes when my body is stressed or in fight-or-flight. Now whenever Theo and I get into an argument, I say “CORTISOL,” and it’s our sign to stop. Life-changing.

Espresso machine- major influence on my life. We have always been a serious coffee household, and Theo is a devoted Moccamaster customer, but I have often longed for an espresso machine. I am a sucker for steamed milk and love the taste, and have been excited to wake up every morning since Theo got it for me for my birthday in September. I am still working on my latte art skills, but I like the challenge.

Theo - not to be too corny, but my husband amazes me. When we first met in 2021, he had a cushy tech job, but always knew it wasn’t the job for him, and he actually had the courage to do something about it. Fast-forward almost 5 years later, and he completed his 2nd Master’s degree, this time from the Silberman School of Social Work at Hunter College. He passed his licensing exam & got a job as a therapist at Weill Cornell Medicine, where he works to combat loneliness & depression in aging adults. AND he’s building his private practice. His commitment to service and connection with his clients inspires me every day.

Dana Cowin - A mentor, friend, and the founder of Progressive Hedonist, a platform to spotlight ways to find joy and connection in tackling one of the seemingly impossible challenges of our lifetime: reversing climate change.

Beyond the clarity of her mission, Dana is far ahead of the curve in helping people gather in person and move beyond our virtual connections. I was lucky enough to attend a handful of her themed gatherings this past year, which brought like-minded people together over incredible food. She also hired me for one of my first content gigs, documenting her events for social media, which significantly shaped where my career took me.

Shannon McKinstrie - Shannon is a social media guru and has been a big influence on me to create new types of content. She is so positive and a big cheerleader for those of us who create content online. She breaks it down in ways that seem so easy (though it’s not always), and I often listen to her podcast, Good Content, and walk away with new ideas I want to try. Thank you, Shannon.

Spices - I started working for Burlap & Barrel in August, and I now own over 50 of their spices, with about 20 in constant rotation. The more I experiment, the more I use them, and my food is better for it. Not only is it a company worth supporting, but its products are of higher quality and contain no caking agents. As many have said before me, the Royal Cinnamon is like no other Cinnamon and a great place to start. These are the spices I use most: Purple Shallot Powder, Purple Striped Garlic, and Ground Bay Leaves in every pot of beans; Silk Chili and Black Urfa Chili sprinkled on many meals; and Berbere to season tofu or cauliflower.

My Grandma - the only person that made it from last year’s list to this year. My grandmother is now 97 and amazes everyone she meets. She is still active in our family movie club, asks pertinent questions, and expresses gratitude for her family all the time. She is generous, loves to learn, and has maintained a joy for living even as so many around her have passed. Love you, Grandma!

Aspirational: These are things that have slightly influenced me this year, but I want more.

Atmos - all of their content reminds me of humanity’s connection to nature, which is something I want to be immersed in every day. Their podcast or newsletter is something I want to consume and support more consistently.

Treadmill - I got a treadmill for under the desk on Black Friday. I have used it a couple of times and have been able to read articles and have a phone call with my mom without getting too out of breath. As I sit for most of the day, I am excited to have this option to move. I despise Amazon, but sometimes I have no choice but to use it for something like this.

Reading: 2026 is the year!! The harder life is, the more I need to zone out in front of the TV. There is nothing wrong with that, but I’m hoping to read more this year. After giving up on finding new podcasts, I’m fully embracing audiobooks, and in an attempt to stop giving money to Amazon, I found Libro.fm, which supports local bookstores.

My sibling Emmy (another huge influence in my life) has been reading nonstop, and after I asked how they can read at night instead of watching TV, they said, “A good story takes care of you.”

The best books I read this year were Elizabeth Gilbert’s The Signature of All Things and Naomi Klein’s Doppelganger.

Thanks for being with me through 2025. Though this newsletter has changed from weekly to about once a month, I am grateful to be here and be in touch with all of you.

Here’s to eating more beans and reading in 2026.
