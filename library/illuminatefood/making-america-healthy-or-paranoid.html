The Make America Healthy Again Movement (MAHA) is making waves in the food industry, but is it surface-level marketing or real change?

The food industry often responds to cultural changes by tweaking its products to match current trends. Remember the low-fat and sugar-free obsessions from the 1980s and 1990s? Or who could forget the Atkins diet-led low-carb boom of the 2000s? Now, added protein is everywhere, and seed-oil-free is on the rise.

These zig-zagging trends are largely driven by the desire to lose weight and by America’s ongoing identity crisis around food and health. Unlike countries with thousands of years of culinary tradition to draw on, we don’t have a deeply rooted food culture to guide us. That absence makes us especially vulnerable to fads, as people chase the next promise of “eating healthier,” all while it’s really the heavily marketed, ultra-processed foods that are undermining our health in the first place.

The MAHA movement is an odd combination of RFK and his skeptical eye of Big Pharma and Big Food, and MAHA moms, led by a woman named Vani Hari, aka the FoodBabe, who are exposing the dangers of the food industry to “protect” their children.

So far, their number-one target has been artificial food dyes, something many companies have already pledged to phase out. In reality, this is more of a marketing play than a sweeping health shift, since most processed foods don’t rely on neon colors. Still, cereals like Trix and Lucky Charms have become the poster children of the campaign, and perhaps we’d all be better off without them, as cute as they may be.

From a recent article, There’s Money to Be Made From ‘MAHA.’ Food Companies Want In.

And by year’s end, Pepsico plans to rebrand Lay’s and Tostitos chips as free of artificial dyes and flavors. (Those versions already exist in a line of snacks labeled Simply.)

During a recent call with investors, the company’s chief executive, Ramon Laguarta, said the goal was elevating “the real-food perception” of the brand. “If you think about the simplest and most natural snack,” he said, “it is a potato chip. It’s a potato, it’s oil and it’s a little bit of salt.

What remains interesting to me is when the demands of the MAHA movement come up against the foundation of how our food system operates.

In addition to artificial dyes, the MAHA moms are very outspoken on pesticides, but our food system can’t function without pesticides because our food is grown on industrial monocrop farms, and we are now reliant on them. I imagine the CEOs of Big Ag are crossing their fingers every night that the anti-pesticide movement doesn’t get much louder or they are in big trouble.

Seed oils are another hot-button issue for MAHA, but as someone genuinely concerned with our food system, this one doesn’t alarm me. MAHA influencers have spun seed oils into a full-blown conspiracy, making baseless claims, including the idea that cutting them out means you no longer need sunscreen……

For me, the tension is that, unlike the other food fads which stemmed from popular diet books, this wellness focus is political, and it’s a delicate rope to walk. Do I think it’s good that there is a light being shown on the food industry and the ingredients in our food? Yes, but in no way will I associate with MAHA as it’s an offshoot of MAGA.

What I wrote earlier this year remains true for me, which is that the fear-mongering of our food led by the MAHA movement only creates a wider gap and starts to align with other conspiracy movements like anti-vax. I want change in the food system, but these are not my leaders. The change we need is systemic, not performative, and we want change that is accessible, not reliant on expensive groceries and stay-at-home moms making everything from scratch.

Sungold Creamy Pasta w No Cream ✨

You MUST make this before the end of the summer.

I can’t explain it, but the orange/yellow cherry tomatoes have a special place in my heart. Look for a box of just those colors or mixed.

INGREDIENTS

  • 1 pint yellow or orange cherry tomatoes

  • 1 shallot

  • 1 clove garlic

  • red pepper flakes

  • 1 cup raw cashews

  • pasta of your choice

INSTRUCTIONS

  • Slice and saute the shallot until soft, add garlic and stir for two more minutes

  • Slice the tomatoes in half and add to pan

  • Pinch of red pepper flakes and salt

  • Stir until the tomatoes burst, about 7-10 minutes

  • Meanwhile, make your pasta and cover your cashews with boiling water in a bowl

  • Once the tomatoes are saucy, take them off the heat and let them cool for about 10 minutes

  • Move to a blender and add your soft cashews (not the water)

  • BLEND

  • Add back to the pan with 1/2 cup of pasta water and mix with your pasta

  • Top with basil and parm
