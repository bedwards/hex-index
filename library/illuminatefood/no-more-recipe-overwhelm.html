We are all drowning in recipes. Every day, I see dozens of new ones posted online, and every week, there are new cookbooks out in the Universe. I love recipes and particularly enjoy seeing the photos of them, as well as which ones go viral. However, based on my own behavior and conversations with others, I've found that few people are actually making these recipes.

Part of it is overwhelm, as we are inundated and there is no good way to keep track of recipes, part of it is the entertainment not functional role of social media, these recipes are meant to get you excited for 1 second and then move on with your life, and part of it is the reality of making food everyday, few people have the time or energy to deal with a recipe which often invovles a trip to the grocery store.

I feel for the recipe developers and food personalities who have to come up with new and viral-sounding recipes almost daily. It’s draining on them, and honestly, it’s draining for us. Dubai Chocolate Overnight Oats or Protein Packed Sheet Pan Freezer Meals are trying to be helpful, but if you’re like me, they can sometimes make me feel worse. These videos are aspirational, but perhaps not actually helpful.

I felt inspired this week to start a series about things I’ve learned or tried that actually change the way I cook and eat. Many of them are little things, some are bigger things, and many are a work in progress.

As I’ve written about before, there was very minimal cooking in my home growing up and therefore no food traditions that got passed down to me, so I’ve had to figure out my own.

If you’ve been with me a while, you know Illuminate Food was once a farm box delivery business that closed because people didn’t have the time to cook and use the produce. This is what has motivated me ever since. How can we go back to a time when we start the week with a fridge full of seasonal veggies and staples and cook our way through?

My spouse, Theo, was reflecting the other day about how raising kids in a queer home with anti-capitalist beliefs is a radical act, and I was reminded that this is how I feel about cooking. While everyone is trying to sell you something, you actually hold the power to muddle through all the noise and make the food you want to eat.

So, this is a long-winded way of saying I want to break free of single-use recipe culture. It’s like single-use plastics, you use it up and throw it away. Instead, let’s invest in longer-term practices and grow our skills so we are not dependent on recipes all the time, we can feed ourselves and our families from a place of confidence and creativity.

So here is my first in the series: Bulk Sauerkraut 💛

Why Sauerkraut, you might ask? I’m sure you’ve heard a million times about gut-health and probiotics, but what the marketers don’t tell you is that one of the best sources is plain-old fermented cabbage.

There are many resources out there about the importance of fermented foods, but here is why I eat saurkraut. Fermented foods are made through a process where microorganisms, primarily bacteria, digest sugars and starches in the food. Those same live bacteria are what we call probiotics. Probiotics help maintain a healthy balance between beneficial and harmful bacteria, aid digestion, support immunity, and reduce inflammation. If you want to learn more, this interview with the celebrity fermentation guru, Sandor Katz, and Dr Tim Spector might be a good place to start.

Supplements, like the popular Seed pills, or all these products with probiotics added, are just not the same. Packaged foods often have probiotics added back in after processing, but these are usually limited to 1–3 strains chosen for stability and shelf life, not diversity. Some are pasteurized after fermentation, which kills off the natural live cultures, leaving only the added ones.

Sauerkraut, on the other hand, because it’s raw and unpasteurized, the bacteria are still alive when you eat it, and this is what we want! Sadly, the krauts on the shelf aren’t alive and don’t offer the same health benefits, but they are still tasty.

So, how do I eat sauerkraut every day?

I have tried making saurkraut multiple times and was not happy with my results.

I was ordering the Hawthorne Valley Sauerkraut, but I would go through it so fast, and it’s $$$.

Recently, I found my solution:

I have mixed feelings about Costco, which I wrote about previously, but when you need to consume items in bulk, it’s a lifesaver.

I found this sauerkraut by Wildbrine, and it’s in a massive container (50 oz!), and it’s incredibly good. It’s tangy, crunchy, salty, and satisfying.

So here is the hack. If you are hungry while you are cooking, pull out your giant jar and take a few bites before dinner. This is the optimal time to eat fermented foods anyway. OR add it to your salad. It’s a wonderful topper for plain greens, offering flavor and texture.

So, if you are a saurkraut fan, I highly recommend this brand and keeping this size in your fridge. If you have never incorporated fermented foods into your diet, know that every country has a fermented food they eat: kimchi, curtido, kvass, Kefir, Injera, etc., so this is not a fad health trend.
