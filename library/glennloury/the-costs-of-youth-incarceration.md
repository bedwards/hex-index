Over the summer, I published my friend Robert Cherry’s essay [“More, not Less, Prison May Improve the Life Chances for Youthful Offenders.”](https://glennloury.substack.com/p/locking-them-up-for-their-own-good?utm_source=publication-search) I thought it was an incisive and rather alarming intervention in debates about crime reduction and recidivism. Robert is a distinguished economist, a professor emeritus at Brooklyn College and an adjunct fellow at the American Enterprise Institute, and I’ve long known him to be sincerely committed to coming up with practical solutions to problems affecting black communities. So I take his argument very seriously.

Youth diversion programs targeting at-risk boys and young men, Robert argues, have largely failed. So have “Raise the Age” laws that keep even those youthful offenders convicted of serious crimes out of prison. Youth offending, recidivism, and long-term unemployment remain tenacious problems. Rather than reducing or eliminating prison sentences for young offenders, Robert says, we should look to prisons as effective vehicles for rehabilitation. He argues that prison often provides youthful offenders with skills and discipline they can use after release, as well as services to help them reenter society. It’s a forceful case for remedies that the author himself recommends only “reluctantly,” amid what he sees as an absence of better solutions.

Since I published the essay, I’ve been casting around for responses to this provocative take on youth offending. I finally found some takers: Vincent Schiraldi and Lael Chester, who have decades of experience between them working with young offenders. They provide a vigorous rebuttal to Cherry’s recommendations, arguing that prison produces dismal outcome for incarcerated youths. Cherry’s reply to their rebuttal is below their essay.

### The Costs of Youth Incarceration: A Response to Dr. Robert Cherry

By Vincent Schiraldi and Lael Chester

Sixteen-year-old Kalief Browder is accused of stealing a backpack and spends over 1,000 days in New York City’s notorious Rikers Island jail, 700 of them in solitary confinement. While there, he is repeatedly assaulted by other youth and staff alike. Two years after his case is dismissed and he is released, [he takes his own life](https://www.newyorker.com/news/news-desk/kalief-browder-1993-2015).

That same year, 2015, city officials entered into a consent decree over violence in Rikers, empowering a judge to monitor and enforce an agreed-upon plan to improve conditions there. Although the city has been under federal court monitoring since then and spends more per capita than any other jail in the country, topping [$550,000 per person annually](https://comptroller.nyc.gov/newsroom/comptroller-stringer-cost-of-incarceration-per-person-in-new-york-city-skyrockets-to-all-time-high-2/), violence has *worsened* since Browder’s death and subsequent federal court monitoring, with [a dozen people dying in custody](https://www.thecity.nyc/2025/09/04/rikers-deaths-judge-remediation-manager/) already this year. After a decade of pain and violence, the judge overseeing the consent decree recently decided it’s time to take matters into her own hands, ordering a court receiver to manage Rikers earlier this year.

New York is not alone. A [federal receiver took over operations of Jackson, Mississippi’s jail](https://www.themarshallproject.org/2025/10/01/mississippi-hinds-county-jail-receivership) on October 1. Like Rikers, the Hinds County Jail had been subject to court monitoring for a decade, with violence and conditions continuing to worsen. Six people have died in custody so far this year in Hinds’ jail, which has about one-ninth of Rikers’ population.

This summer, after 30 facility staff were indicted for encouraging 69 fights involving 143 different victims in Los Angeles’s two juvenile detention facilities, numerous youth overdosed (resulting in one child’s death), and the county was out of compliance with 75% of a consent decree’s provisions, California’s attorney general moved for the facilities to be taken over by a receiver. He [lamented,](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/attorney-general-bonta-asks-court-place-los-angeles-county-juvenile-halls) “For four-and-a-half years, we’ve moved aggressively to bring the County into compliance with our judgment—and we’ve been met with glacial progress that has too often looked like one step forward and two steps back. Enough is enough. These young people deserve better.”

These are just the most extreme examples of lengthy—but ultimately failed—court oversight in jurisdictions from coast to coast. But many more correctional facilities are under court oversight due to unconstitutional conditions. As of 2019, [227 of the nation’s 1,677 adult correctional facilities were under court monitoring](https://bjs.ojp.gov/content/pub/pdf/csfacf19st.pdf)—more than one in seven.

So we were surprised that the American Enterprise Institute’s Robert Cherry painted such a rosy picture of U.S. correctional facilities in his recent guest essay for this Substack. Cherry recommended, albeit “with a heavy heart,” that the U.S. increase its [record-breaking incarceration rate](https://www.nationalacademies.org/our-work/causes-and-consequences-of-high-rates-of-incarceration) by adding *more* young people to our prisons. His reasoning is that “prisons no longer have the same downside they had in the past,” and that “\[t\]oday, most prisons substantially focus on preparing inmates for reentry.” Perhaps most shocking of all, Cherry says that “prison imposes demands similar to that of ordinary jobs.”

Cherry’s contention that incarceration prepares young men for productive lives is contrary to both the research and our collective 80 years of experience working with young people. This is not just because conditions are too often violent and debilitating—and they are—but because the very nature of institutionalization robs young people of the normalized challenges that they must grapple with in order to mature into productive and healthy adults.

[Aizur and Doyle](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w19102/w19102.pdf) used the random assignment of judges and their differential rates of youth institutionalization to assess the impact of youth incarceration, following 35,000 adolescents for 10 years from a large, urban county. They found that, when compared to similar youth granted probation, institutionalized youth experienced large decreases in the likelihood of high school completion and large increases in the likelihood of adult incarceration. In stark contrast to Dr. Cherry’s contention that the structure of incarceration assists with future employability, Aizur and Doyle concluded:

> Incarceration can negatively influence human capital and increase the likelihood of future criminal activity through two potential channels. The first is by encouraging the accumulation of ”criminal capital” and hindering the accumulation of social capital that can aid in job search, lowering the probability of employment. \[…\] The second way in which juvenile incarceration can negatively affect human capital accumulation is by interrupting high school completion and reducing years of schooling, thereby greatly reducing future labor market wages and increasing future criminal activity.

Similarly, analyzing data from 14,344 adult participants in the National Longitudinal Study of Adolescent to Adult Health, [Barnert et al](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5260153/#:~:text=What%20This%20Study%20Adds:,than%20any%20other%20developed%20country.) found that “cumulative incarceration duration during adolescence and early adulthood is independently associated with worse physical and mental health later in adulthood.”

As bad as incarcerating young people in youth facilities can be, imprisoning them alongside adults is even worse. In 2007, the Centers for Disease Control conducted a [systemic review](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/pdf/rr/rr5609.pdf) of published scientific evidence concerning the effectiveness concerning transferring juveniles into the adult criminal justice system. They found a 34% increase in felony recidivism, on average, from the practice in the studies they reviewed.

Donna Bishop[\[1\]](#_ftn1)— whose research was cited in the CDC report and who interviewed 50 youth in adult and juvenile facilities—quoted a young person who had been in both, offering his explanation of these outcomes, “When I was in juvenile programs, they were telling me that I am somebody and that I can change my ways and get back on the right tracks. In here, they tell me I am nobody and I never will be anybody.”

In a 2023 [cohort study](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2806838?widget=personalizedcontent&previousarticle=0) of 8,951 youths, Silver, Semenza, and Nedelec, found that incarceration in an adult correctional facility before the age of 18 years was associated with a 33% increase in the risk of mortality between 18 and 39 years of age.

Being incarcerated as an adult can literally kill you.

Perhaps the most damning evidence against Dr. Cherry’s argument—but hopeful for young people and society in general—is the profound youth crime and incarceration changes that have occurred over the past two decades, which fly directly in the face of arguments urging more incarceration.

There has been a massive decline in locking up young people in both juvenile and adult facilities over the last two decades. Between 2000 and 2022, there has been a [75% decline](https://www.sentencingproject.org/policy-brief/youth-justice-by-the-numbers/) in the number of young people incarcerated in juvenile facilities, with youth incarceration plummeting by double digits in red and blue states alike.

Likewise, between 2000 and 2022, [half of all states](https://eji.org/news/23-states-have-removed-children-from-adult-court/) passed laws reducing or eliminating mandates to try young people as adults. From 1997 to 2022, there was an [84% decline](https://www.sentencingproject.org/policy-brief/youth-justice-by-the-numbers/) in the number of youth confined in adult jails and prisons nationally.

Further, the burgeoning field of [Emerging Adult Justice](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5c6458c07788975dfd586d90/t/6595add3c7d19b67c7c92d8f/1704308180648/EAJ+Americas+Recent+Attempts+to+Apply+Research+to+Policies+and+Practices+by+Chester-Shefner-Schiraldi+ABA+CJ+Magazine+Winter+2024.pdf) has produced some promising practices and policies that are beginning to divert young people ages 18 to 25 away from incarceration and into treatment and opportunities for success. Guided by research in the fields of neurobiology, developmental psychology, and sociology, [jurisdictions](https://jjie.org/2023/05/11/1442839/) are creating more effective and developmentally appropriate responses to these emerging adults. Instead of relying exclusively on negative consequences (e.g., threat of prison), justice systems are now using [positive incentives](https://justicelab.columbia.edu/sites/justicelab.columbia.edu/files/content/Designing%20an%20EAJ%20Track%20in%20the%20Manhattan%20ATI%20Court%20FINAL%206.22.23.pdf) that have shown to be much more effective at motivating behavior change. Recognizing that the pre-frontal lobe is the last part of the brain to fully develop and that emerging adults consequently struggle with executive functioning, justice systems are providing targeted support to prioritize and successfully complete court-mandated tasks while under community supervision. The goal of these innovative practices and policies is all about public safety.

If those suggesting that more incarceration equals more safety—either through incapacitation, deterrence, or the beneficial structure imprisonment purportedly provides—were correct, these data and evolving practices should spell disaster.

But contrary to tough-on-crime predictions from such “lenient” treatment, youth crime actually *fell* nationally by 80% from 1996 to 2020. It has also fallen *more* than it has for adults whose incarceration rate has declined more modestly. Data [analyzed](https://jeffreybutts.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/databit202502.pdf) by John Jay’s Jeffrey Butts show that youth arrests declined by 78% from 1995 to 2024, arrests of adults ages 25 and above have dropped by 19%, and emerging adults ages 18-25 experienced a 64% decline in arrests. The very populations Dr. Cherry’s essay focused on are experiencing the most significant declines in crime.

In 2014, the National Academies of Science, Engineering, and Medicine completed a watershed [consensus report](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/18613/the-growth-of-incarceration-in-the-united-states-exploring-causes) on the causes and consequences of mass incarceration, calling the U.S.’s imprisonment growth “historically unprecedented and internationally unique,” with massive collateral consequences, costs, and public safety outcomes that were otherwise “unlikely to have been large.” NASEM’s consensus recommendation was a significant reduction in our nation’s use of incarceration.

We’ve seen this movie before, and should all be very, very careful before endorsing a sequel, especially during these challenging and divisive times.

***Vincent Schiraldi** is a [Visiting Fellow](https://www.thepinkertonfoundation.org/staff/vincent-schiraldi-visiting-fellow) at the Pinkerton Foundation and former director of probation and corrections in New York City and juvenile justice in Washington, D.C. and Maryland. **Lael Chester** is a consultant and the former Director of the [Emerging Adult Justice Project](https://justicelab.columbia.edu/EAJ) at the Columbia University Justice Lab.*

### Reply to Vincent Schiraldi and Lael Chester

by Robert Chery

The response to my essay is underwhelming. It begins with sensationalized accounts of detention centers—Rikers, Jackson (MS), and Los Angeles—not the state and federal prison systems that I was describing. Schiraldi and Chester note the increase in violence at Rikers. As I [wrote](https://www.aei.org/op-eds/how-to-address-rikers-violence-work-with-correction-officers-and-isolate-dangerous-offenders/) about this two years ago, this reflected the changing demographics there. Prior to 2018, almost half of inmates awaiting trial [were](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2023429118#t01) charged with misdemeanors and only 15% with violent felonies. By early 2019, misdemeanor-charged inmates dropped to 9% while those charged with violent felonies had risen to 57%; and to almost 80% by 2023. With such a concentration of violence-prone individuals, increases were inevitable.

Next, they present two studies (Azur and Doyle; CDC) with data that are 25 years old on the impact of incarceration on those under 18 years old. Given that most of the shift in state and federal prison efforts to rehabilitation that I described are more recent transformations, and that my concerns are much more with the 18-to-25-year-old population, this information is irrelevant.

They do reference a 2023 article, but it too is irrelevant. Silver, Semenza, and Nedelec found that incarceration in an adult correctional facility before the age of 18 years was associated with a 33% increase in the risk of mortality between 18 and 39 years of age. Schiraldi and Chester can’t help moralizing: “Being incarcerated as an adult can literally kill you.” The problem is that this study compares those incarcerated to those who were *never* arrested, not to those arrested and sent to diversionary programs. Given the gun violence particularly in center-city black neighborhoods, this finding was predictable.

Schiraldi and Chester then go on to reference statistics on the decline in arrests of young people and associate it with their engaging in less crime. Given the decriminalizing of illegal actions, such as shoplifting less than $1,000, this claim is problematic. More importantly, though both authors are from New York, they ignored the material I presented on what appears to be the disastrous impact of “Raise the Age” legislation.

They never reference the *targeted* population that I am focused on: repeat offenders who have been out of school and out of work for an extended period of time and have been convicted of misdemeanors (like repeat store thefts) and nonviolent felonies (like auto theft). And, most telling, they never discuss the changing efforts in prisons to facilitate educational and occupational advancement. Surely, they are aware of the changes I described in New York and could have commented on them. But I guess that eighty years of experience—mostly in a past era—dominated their thinking.
