After years of being called “uninvestable,” China’s $19 trillion stock market is back in global portfolios. The reason isn’t real estate or manufacturing. It’s tech.  
AI, biotech, and semiconductors are pulling foreign investors back into the country, despite export bans and trade tension.

### Market sentiment shift

Foreign inflows into Shanghai, Shenzhen, and Hong Kong exchanges are climbing again. Indices are hitting multi-year highs after Beijing’s monetary easing and a fragile tariff truce with Washington. Institutional investors see Chinese tech as undervalued compared to global peers, especially as companies double down on AI and automation.

[Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/chinas-19-trillion-stock-market-once-called-uninvestable-lures-foreigners-again-2025-09-16/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) reports that global funds are selectively returning to Chinese equities, focusing on sectors where domestic policy support meets long-term demand.

### Domestic tech push

China is spending heavily to rebuild its tech base from within.  
A new data-center complex in Qinghai runs on locally made chips, part of a state plan to expand computing capacity and reduce reliance on U.S. hardware.  
[Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/media-telecom/china-spotlights-major-data-centre-project-using-domestic-chips-2025-09-17/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) shows how the project became a showcase for “Made in China” semiconductors.

Smartphone maker [Honor](https://www.reuters.com/technology/artificial-intelligence/chinas-honor-announces-10-billion-investment-ai-devices-2025-03-02/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) announced a $10 billion plan to develop AI-driven devices, from PCs to wearables.

Meanwhile, the government launched the **National AI Industry Investment Fund**, worth about $8.2 billion, to finance chip design, computing infrastructure, and AI applications.

The third phase of the **China Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund**—the “Big Fund”—is channeling billions into domestic semiconductor production and materials.

### China’s data-center buildout

China is pouring money into new data centers across inland provinces. Local governments offer cheap land and energy, and Beijing wants more computing power outside the crowded coastal regions.

Bloomberg showed how projects in places like Gansu and Qinghai are turning into large AI hubs, powered by domestic chips and linked to major cities through high-speed fiber.

Reuters reported that the Qinghai complex is now a showcase for locally produced semiconductors. The site handles training workloads for state agencies and private tech firms.

This expansion matters for two reasons:

First, it reduces dependence on foreign hardware by scaling local chip demand.  
Second, it supports the national strategy to spread AI infrastructure beyond Beijing and Shenzhen.

More capacity means lower costs for developers and a stronger ecosystem for AI startups.

### Biotech partnerships

While the West debates decoupling, foreign pharma is doubling down on Chinese biotech.

[AstraZeneca and Pfizer](https://www.ft.com/content/89285fd5-cd24-4772-a53d-0553cd37032d?utm_source=chatgpt.com) are signing licensing deals with local startups to co-develop or distribute early-stage drugs.

Many of these partnerships are driven by cost advantages and fast-track R&D in China’s biotech clusters.

The government’s [Foreign Investment Action Plan 2025](https://www.china-briefing.com/news/chinas-foreign-investment-action-plan-2025-implications/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) explicitly lists biotech as a priority sector for foreign collaboration.

### Policy and reform

Beijing’s policy tone has softened. The new action plan offers clearer rules, tax incentives, and easier access in healthcare, telecom, and education.

At the same time, the state is reinforcing the self-sufficiency message: foreign investors are welcome, but China will build its own tech backbone.

The dual strategy, open yet nationalistic, is shaping how capital flows back into the country.

### Risks and headwinds

The U.S. and its allies keep tightening export controls on advanced chipmaking tools.  
[The World Economic Forum](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/08/us-policy-chip-sales-china-semiconductor-global-tech/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) outlines how these restrictions still block China from accessing the most sophisticated semiconductors.

In response, Beijing ordered public institutions to source at least half of their chips from domestic suppliers, as [Tom’s Hardware](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/semiconductors/china-mandates-domestic-firms-source-50-percent-of-chips-from-chinese-producers-beijing-continues-to-squeeze-companies-over-reliance-on-foreign-semiconductors?utm_source=chatgpt.com) reported.

That protectionist turn brings new risks: oversupply, inefficiency, and potential asset bubbles in state-funded projects.

Transparency and IP protection remain weak spots. Analysts warn that dependence on government funding may hide underlying competitiveness issues.

### Outlook

If reforms hold, foreign participation in China’s tech sectors will grow, especially in biotech and AI.

Semiconductors remain vulnerable to external pressure and overcapacity.

“Made in China 2025” delivered partial success—reduced import dependence and faster innovation—but high-end chips and medical devices still depend on foreign know-how, according to [Rhodium Group](https://rhg.com/research/was-made-in-china-2025-successful/?utm_source=chatgpt.com).

For investors, China’s tech is no longer off-limits, but it’s far from risk-free.  
The opportunity lies in spotting where state ambition meets real competitiveness.
