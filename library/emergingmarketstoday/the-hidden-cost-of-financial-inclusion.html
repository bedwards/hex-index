Bright yellow cards. No annual fees. A smooth digital app that promised simplicity in a country where banking is often intimidating.
The mission was clear: bring lower-income Brazilians — especially those in the C and D income brackets — into the formal financial system.

And it worked. Millions joined. For many, that little yellow card was their first credit product. A symbol of trust and access.

Behind the scenes, Will Bank was expanding fast. The fintech, backed by Banco Master, built a portfolio heavy on consumers with thin or no credit history. Every swipe, every installment plan, every late payment carried weight on its balance sheet.

Then the numbers started to tell another story.

According to Valor Econômico, Will Bank now lists R$ 5.1 billion (roughly US $900 million to US $1 billion) in receivables from payment transactions, a figure that caught the attention of Brazil’s financial press and regulators. It wasn’t just the amount.

It was the question behind it: who owns the risk tied to those receivables?

Was it the issuer — Will Bank itself?
The acquirers handling card payments?
Or the merchants and fintech partners downstream?

The lines weren’t clear. And that lack of clarity has reignited a debate that goes beyond one digital bank.

Brazil’s card ecosystem has long thrived on inclusion and innovation but much of it rests on complex partnerships and funding arrangements. When defaults rise or liquidity tightens, everyone feels the strain.

The Will Bank case has become a real-time stress test for how well the country’s financial inclusion model can hold up when credit risk scales faster than the safeguards around it.

The cost of inclusion

Financial inclusion is often celebrated as progress. More people in the system. More access.
But inclusion built on credit can easily become debt with a nicer interface.

This is something Emmanuel Daniel warned about in our Emerging Markets Today episode, Financial Inclusion: The Good, The Bad, and The Controversial.

He argued that when fintechs onboard the unbanked into venture-capital-driven models, they aren’t empowering users.
True inclusion, he said, happens when financial systems are built from the ground up — as Grameen Bank once did in Bangladesh — keeping both assets and liabilities local. Emmanuel added:

“For so long as we think of financial services as something that needs to be put on platforms and monetized, the whole idea of financial inclusion is a lie in that model. Many of the players in financial inclusion are not empowering people — they’re bringing the unbanked into the same system that created exclusion in the first place.”

In other words: financial inclusion without shared responsibility creates fragility.

Patterns across emerging markets

Brazil isn’t alone. Similar stories are emerging across other developing fintech ecosystems.

In Africa, Union54 — a Zambian fintech that issued virtual dollar cards for users across the continent — was forced to suspend its card operations after facing an attempted chargeback fraud scheme reportedly worth over US $1 billion.

Users disputed transactions they had actually made, triggering massive losses for merchants and the issuer itself. The case became one of the most high-profile warnings about how fragile digital payment infrastructure can be when fraud detection lags behind growth.

In India, the trend looks different but the outcome is similar. Credit card default rates among younger users have climbed steadily since 2023, according to TransUnion CIBIL. Defaults now sit near 1.8 percent, while delinquency in small-ticket unsecured loans has passed 3 percent.

Much of this exposure comes from first-time borrowers in smaller cities, using credit for e-commerce or short-term BNPL purchases. The Reserve Bank of India has already tightened rules for fintechs and non-bank lenders, warning that unsecured consumer credit could become the next systemic risk.

Across Southeast Asia, the same pattern is visible — fintech wallets and micro-lenders onboarding millions of users with little credit history. Inclusion is growing, but oversight isn’t keeping pace. Fraud prevention remains patchy, and loss-sharing agreements between banks, fintechs, and merchants are often unclear.

Each of these cases reflects the same pressure point: fintech inclusion expands faster than the systems that protect it.

Financial inclusion has value, but it also has a cost. As Emmanuel Daniel said on the podcast, bringing millions into the system without changing how that system works only shifts the risk around. The Will Bank case is a reminder that inclusion needs more than good intentions — it needs structure, transparency, and a safety net strong enough to hold the weight of progress.
