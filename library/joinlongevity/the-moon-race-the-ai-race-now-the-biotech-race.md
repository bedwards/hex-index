The biotech field is still recovering from the global pandemic. Recently, regulations, political decisions, and new policies may be costing the United States its long-standing leadership in the sector. Meanwhile, new players are rising and the US is beginning to make nervous moves.

Just last week, we covered how a healthcare giant from the Emirates made a major biotech investment, boosting the region’s stance in global innovation. Today, we see even stronger moves from another direction: China is accelerating. And this time, it might be the start of a biotech power shift.

\---

## How We Got Here

After the pandemic, biotech needed stronger support. Instead, the US saw regulatory freezes, funding cuts, key resignations, and a broader political uncertainty that began to weigh heavily on the sector:

**Investor Anxiety:** During the pandemic, a rush of generalist investors flooded into biotech, eager to capitalize on the “gold rush” of innovation and COVID-related market opportunities. But as the pandemic waned and volatility increased, many of these investors exited just as quickly, creating an artificial boom-and-bust cycle and leaving a vacuum in biotech funding. Meanwhile, ongoing concerns about Health and Human Services Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s stance on biomedical innovation have left investors and innovators worried that new policies could slow progress rather than accelerate it.

**Key Leadership Loss:** The removal of Peter Marks, a highly respected FDA official who supported faster drug approvals, deepened concerns across the industry.

**The Biosecure Act:** Intended to protect US interests, this act aimed to sever ties between American and Chinese biotech industries. This, however, further isolated the US while encouraging China to find new partners elsewhere.

## From Copycat to Competitor: China’s Biotech Surge

For years, China’s biotech industry was primarily known for producing generics and serving as a manufacturing hub. That narrative has evolved. Today, China is not only partnering with global players, but also innovating and exporting its own biotech breakthroughs.

-   **BeiGene**, once a domestic-focused company, is now on track to post its first operating profit a significant milestone in the biotech sector.
    
-   **Jiangsu Hengrui** has secured billion-dollar licensing deals, including a notable heart drug partnership with Merck.
    
-   China's share of major licensing deals has risen from 5% in 2022 to 42% in 2025, highlighting its growing influence in the global biotech arena.
    

\---

## China’s Advances in Oncology, Genomics, and Advanced Therapies

China is making especially significant strides in oncology and advanced therapeutics:

-   **mRNA Vaccines:** While China was late to deploy mRNA vaccines for COVID-19, it has since built robust mRNA platforms. Chinese mRNA vaccines are now in international trials and are being exported to new markets.
    
-   **Genomics:** BGI Genomics is now a global leader in sequencing capacity, generating vast amounts of genetic data from diverse populations. But the real significance is not just the volume—BGI and other Chinese institutions are amassing massive and diverse biodata sets. By pairing these resources with rapidly advancing AI, Chinese researchers are uniquely positioned to decode complex biological processes and accelerate the discovery of advanced therapeutics, including those that could directly impact the biology of aging itself.
    
-   **Cell and Gene Therapies:** Chinese companies are launching cell and gene therapy trials at scale, sometimes benefitting from a more flexible regulatory environment. Major players like Takeda and Eisai (Japan) are now co-developing cancer drugs, building joint R&D centers, and licensing Chinese discoveries to global markets.
    

The numbers tell the story:

-   The Hang Seng Biotech Index, which tracks the 50 largest biotech companies listed in Hong Kong, has surged 32% this year.
    
-   By comparison, the Nasdaq Biotechnology Index has dropped 15% under the weight of funding cuts and tariffs.
    
-   Licensing deals are China’s new catalyst for success:
    
    -   In 2022, China made up just **5%** of major biotech licensing deals (with upfront payments of $50M+).
        
    -   In 2025, that number has skyrocketed to **42%**.
        

Global pharma companies are no longer just sourcing from China, they’re paying big upfront checks for Chinese-invented medicines. This intention to invest heavily before these medicines even reach the market is a sign of China’s strong stance, potentially turning China into a global biotech powerhouse.

## The US Response

Signs of a US response are starting to emerge, but whether it will be fast enough remains an open question.

**A new FDA commissioner, Dr. Martin A. Makary, was appointed in April 2025.** Dr. Makary, a well-known surgeon and health policy expert, was brought in to lead the agency through a period of rapid change and to restore public trust after months of leadership uncertainty.

Dr. Makary’s mission is clear:

-   Accelerate drug approvals, especially for rare diseases.
    
-   Cut down the traditional 10-year development timeline.
    
-   Move away from methods like heavy animal testing, embracing computational models, organ chips, and organoids to test monoclonal antibody therapies and other drugs.
    
-   Reduce pharmaceutical industry influence over FDA decision-making.
    

Efforts are also underway to fix past mistakes.

The “pill penalty,” a rule imposed by Joe Biden discouraging the development of pill-based drugs, is being reformed. A new executive order aims to make it more attractive again to innovate in oral medications, unlocking another area of biotech potential.

The US is trying to accelerate, and biotech stocks have shown small, fragile signs of life. Companies like Intellia Therapeutics, Avidity Biosciences, and Regenxbio experienced brief rallies earlier this year but, overall, the sector remains volatile and lasting recovery is still uncertain.

## Replacing Animal Testing with Organoids: Hope and Risks

As part of efforts to speed up drug development, the FDA is now pushing for new testing methods that move away from heavy animal testing.

Technologies like computational models, organ chips, and organoids are being embraced to test new drugs faster and more cheaply, especially antibody-based therapies.

For antibody development, this could be a revolution:

-   No more relying on primates costing tens of thousands of dollars each.
    
-   Faster trials.
    
-   Lower development costs.
    

But the shift brings risks too.

If investors and the market start favoring only cheaper, faster-to-develop drugs, it could discourage investment into more advanced and complex therapies, like genetic or cell therapies, which are harder to model in organoids or chips.

## The Longevity Perspective: The True Race

This concern is especially relevant for those of us following the longevity biotech scene, as the most advanced therapeutics are going to be crucial in truly modulating the aging process. By focusing predominantly on streamlining processes for antibody drugs and other “easier” modalities, the U.S. may inadvertently divert attention and investment away from the most transformative therapies, like gene editing, cell therapies, and regenerative technologies.

For those following the longevity field, this is a pivotal issue. It’s these advanced therapeutic platforms that hold the greatest promise for actually modulating aging and achieving meaningful gains in healthy human lifespan. If the innovation ecosystem tilts too heavily toward the quick wins, there’s a real risk the U.S. could fall behind in the true race: the one to unlock radical longevity.

Meanwhile, China’s biotech sector is positioning itself aggressively in these frontier areas, investing in everything from mRNA and gene editing to large-scale clinical trials in cell therapy and regenerative medicine. If the momentum continues, global talent and capital may increasingly follow accelerating progress, and possibly making China the birthplace of the next generation of longevity breakthroughs.

Currently, the longevity biotech industry is highly concentrated in the US, with leading companies, capital, and talent centered around Boston, San Francisco, and San Diego. But how long before we see China emerging as a major hub for longevity biotech? After all, applying advanced therapeutics to extend healthy lifespan is the ultimate test of what this industry can achieve for public health. And with China’s rapidly aging population, a legacy of decades of the one-child policy, the pressure to find solutions for age-related disease and the burdens of an aging society is immense. It’s not just about global competition, but about which country will first deliver on the promise of longevity for its people.

## The Biotech Race Has Begun

The United States, long the symbol of biomedical leadership, now faces an urgent challenge.

China, in contrast, is forging alliances, innovating, and exporting biotech breakthroughs to the world.

And it’s not just China. As we highlighted last week, even the Emirates are stepping into biotech, investing heavily, hoping Abu Dhabi becomes the new longevity hub of the world.

Biotech is becoming a competitive race. Who owns health owns the economy, and the big players know it. We’re seeing healthcare companies investing in preventative measures and giants appearing in the biotech field.

And as with AI, the winners will be decided by speed, investment, and the ability to adapt.

The question is no longer whether biotech will define the future, but...

**Where will that future be built? And who will lead it?**
