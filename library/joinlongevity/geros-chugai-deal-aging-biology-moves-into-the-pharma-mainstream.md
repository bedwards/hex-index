# **Gero’s Chugai Deal: Aging Biology Moves Into the Pharma Mainstream**

**AI drug-discovery company Gero has signed a licensing deal with Roche-owned Chugai. In a crowded field of AI startups, it was Gero’s commitment to aging biology that made it stand out.**

While most coverage focused on the financials, this story is bigger than a headline. It signals that aging biology is reshaping early-stage drug discovery—and that pharma is no longer just watching from the sidelines.

In a behind-the-scenes interview with **Alex Kadet**, the business developer who led the commercial effort, we unpack how the deal came together — and what it means for the field.

\---

## **Modeling Aging to Treat Disease**

Gero treats aging as a **dynamic system** that can be measured, modeled, and intercepted. Using physics-based analyses of longitudinal human data, the company pinpoints how biological resilience erodes over time, then targets the mechanisms driving that decline.

That systems-level lens is what first drew Chugai’s attention, according to **Alex Kadet**, Business Developer at Gero.

> "The first conversation with Chugai happened in mid 2021, just after we had raised our round," Alex told us. "We had something that stood out—not just AI, but a new way of thinking about biology as a physical system."

But the conversations truly picked up after Gero’s 2023 partnership with Pfizer, which gave the team access to larger-scale human datasets.

> "Once we started showing traction on both technology platform development and industrial partnerships leveraging human data – the interest from Chugai got more serious. The Pfizer partnership was a key unlock."

This two-year journey from first in-depth conversation on the potential contact to deal signing underscores a key lesson for biotech founders: **pharma moves slowly**, and partnerships are built through consistent, long-term engagement.

\---

## **From Human Data to a Licensed Target**

The assets Chugai licensed— undisclosed **age-related-disease targets**—followed Gero’s “human-first” pipeline:

1.  **Discovery in real-world people:** Large-scale human datasets revealed a vulnerability tied to loss of resilience.
    
2.  **Physics-based prioritization:** Gero’s model flagged specific mechanisms as a high-leverage intervention points.
    

> "Pharma wants human data. That’s what helps to get the conversation started," Alex explained. 

Gero’s approach—predicting in humans, then proving in animals—demonstrates a translational model that can resonate with pharma's need for risk mitigation.

\---

## **The Deal: Structure & Milestones**

Gero tells us the agreement with Chugai includes:

-   A **confidential upfront payment** Up to ~**$250 million** in development and commercial **milestones**
    
-   **Tiered royalties** on future sales
    
-   An **exclusive global license** for Chugai to develop and commercialize antibody therapeutics against the targets
    

In pharma, **upfront payments** are more than just cash—they’re a signal of conviction. These payments are the **hardest to secure** and indicate a partner's belief not only in the underlying asset but in the platform and team behind it. Chugai didn’t sign an option or pilot—they bought in.

> "So basically, it's the boldest move, in my view, so far from pharma, maybe beyond what was done in 2008, when Sinclair sold his company to GSK, which unfortunately didn't go further after they tried to validate animal data. But since then, I don't recall any deals where pharma acquired specifically anti-aging assets... The target we partnered with Chugai is related to aging itself."

\---

## **A Pharma Mindset Shift**

While pharma has flirted with aging biology for years, industry partnerships have been few and far between since the early 2000s. According to Alex, the landscape is now shifting.

Several forces are converging:

-   **Maturing science**: More technologies are entering the clinic and showing signals in humans, not just mice.
    
-   **Validated hypotheses**: Human data is beginning to confirm long-held assumptions in the longevity field.
    
-   **Patent cliffs**: Many blockbuster drugs will soon lose exclusivity, pushing pharma to scout for new therapeutic paradigms.
    

> "We’re seeing the field cross a maturity threshold," Alex told us. "Five years ago, pharma didn’t take aging seriously. Now they do—but they want proof. That’s what this deal delivers."

The Gero-Chugai deal may be one of the first in this new wave. It likely won’t be the last.

\---

## **What This Deal Reveals About Pharma Partnering**

This deal also tells us something about **what matters to pharma** when evaluating a partner:

### **Aging can be a strategic wedge—if framed correctly**

Gero didn’t try to hide its focus on aging. It led with it—but grounded it in predictive modeling and therapeutic relevance.

> "There’s a right way to talk about aging," said Alex. "If you’re just hand-waving about healthspan, pharma tunes out. But if you tie aging mechanisms to actionable targets, you get their attention."

### **Human-first discovery is gaining traction**

In contrast to target discovery pipelines rooted in animal models, Gero’s approach starts with human data and then moves to validation. That order of operations helped de-risk the biology early.

### **Traditional validation still matters**

Biotech may be evolving, but certain gatekeepers remain in place.

> "We always say: predict in humans, prove in mice. That’s the only way to bridge the old world and the new."

\---

## **For Founders and Translational Biologists**

Whether you're building in aging or adjacent fields:

-   **Lead with mechanistic clarity**: Abstract promises won’t cut it. Be explicit about how your target addresses the root cause of a disease.
    
-   **Show cross-species proof**: Especially if you discover in humans, validate in animal models—and make the translation visible.
    
-   **Understand pharma psychology**: What gets pharma to write a check isn’t always what excites academics. Learn to bridge that gap.
    
-   **Play the long game**: Pharma relationships can take years to mature. Start early, stay consistent.
    
-   **Engaging with pharma early—so that when the assets are ready, the communication channels are already open—can be a strategic move.** It brings commercial validation and traction, which can often mean the difference between making it or breaking it when bringing new therapeutics to market.
    

> "If you’re a founder in this space, remember: pharma is conservative," Alex advised. "They want to know what happens in the body, not just on your slides. Build trust by showing the biology step-by-step."

## For a Deeper Dive: Our Q&A with Gero's CBO **Alex Kadet**

**Q: Gero just signed a licensing deal with Chugai, a Roche subsidiary. Why is this a meaningful milestone for Gero—and for the field?**

**Alex Kadet:**  
This deal validates something we’ve believed for a long time: aging biology isn’t just scientifically interesting—it’s commercially investable. It signals that pharma is no longer just watching the longevity space with curiosity. With this licensing agreement, Chugai is making a commitment to an aging-targeted therapeutic. That’s a paradigm shift. For Gero, it’s a strong validation of both our platform and our translational strategy. We’re not just doing aging science for its own sake—we’re building real assets, aligned with pharma standards, that can move into the clinic and eventually to market.

**Q: What made Gero attractive to Chugai compared to other AI-driven platforms?**

We approach drug discovery differently. Most AI platforms aim to optimize efficiency within traditional discovery pipelines. That’s valuable—but incremental. Gero’s platform goes a step further. We use physics-based models to quantify and predict how biological resilience erodes over time using longitudinal human data. That gives us a systems-level understanding of aging and disease risk—letting us pinpoint targets that drive the loss of function before symptoms appear.

This view—aging as a dynamic, quantifiable process—resonated with Chugai. We weren’t just using AI for speed; we were redefining what should be targeted and why. That depth of insight helped us stand out.

**Q: What was the timeline from initial contact to signed deal?**

We first engaged with Chugai in 2022, shortly after raising our round. The early conversations were exploratory—they were intrigued, but the field was still considered high-risk. Things accelerated after our 2023 Pfizer collaboration. That gave us access to larger datasets and allowed us to show how our models perform at scale. By mid-2023, we had compelling platform outputs and an increasingly validated process. Over two years, that consistency built credibility. Ultimately, it was a mix of scientific progress, platform maturation, and demonstrated commercial traction that brought the deal to the finish line.

**Q: Can you walk us through the asset Chugai licensed and the structure of the deal?**

The licensed target is focused on a mechanism tied to age-related loss of resilience. While the specific indication remains confidential, it represents a new category of disease-modifying intervention grounded in aging biology.

The deal includes:

-   A confidential multimillion-dollar upfront payment
    
-   Development and commercial milestones up to multi-billion
    
-   Tiered royalties on net sales
    
-   Exclusive global rights for Chugai to develop and commercialize antibody therapeutics against the target
    

This is not a pilot or exploratory option—it’s a fully committed license. In this space, upfronts are the hardest component to secure. That kind of structure reflects true conviction in the science, the platform, and our team.

**Q: What’s unique about how Gero discovers and validates targets?**

We start with people. We mine large-scale longitudinal datasets—tracking how health metrics change over time—and apply physics to model the tipping points when resilience begins to fail. That’s where aging becomes biologically actionable.

Once we’ve identified those inflection points, our system flags molecular mechanisms likely responsible. From there, we prioritize interventions based on systems-level leverage. Then—and only then—do we move into animal models to validate the target and effect size.

That “predict in humans, prove in mice” model flips the traditional discovery process and helps de-risk targets early. It’s also what pharma wants: human-relevant biology with clean translational logic and measurable effect in preclinical systems.

**Q: What does this deal say about pharma’s evolving view of aging biology?**

We believe this is the first licensing deal in over a decade where a top-tier pharma company committed to an asset explicitly tied to aging biology. Most industry interest until now has been adjacent—focused on diseases of aging, but not the underlying drivers.

That’s changing. Several forces are converging:

-   More high-quality human data
    
-   Increasing evidence that aging pathways are druggable
    
-   Strategic pressure on pipelines as blockbuster exclusivities expire
    

In that context, aging isn’t a niche anymore—it’s a potential engine for durable, high-impact therapies. This deal shows pharma is beginning to think that way.

**Q: What does this mean for other aging biotech startups or investors exploring the space?**

Aging is now a credible wedge into pharma’s pipeline planning—but only if framed correctly. Our advice to others building in this space:

-   **Be mechanistic**. “Healthspan” and “wellness” are not sufficient. Show how aging biology drives disease in a way that’s therapeutically actionable.
    
-   **Ground your pitch in translational logic**. Pharma isn’t just buying a mechanism—they’re buying a path to clinic. That means human data, animal validation, and a clear indication strategy.
    
-   **Plan for a long relationship cycle**. This deal took two years. Trust-building matters. So does technical readiness when the partner is ready to move.
    

**Q: What are Gero’s next steps following this deal?**

We're scaling. This partnership gives us both validation and resources to expand our pipeline. We’re advancing additional aging-related targets through preclinical development and are preparing for clinical entry. We’re also raising a new round of capital to support these efforts.

Our long-term goal is to be the first clinical-stage company to bring aging biology-based therapeutics to patients—driven by physics-informed, human-first discovery.

**Q: Last thoughts—for investors watching the longevity and AI drug discovery space?**

This deal is a signal. Aging is no longer just the frontier—it’s becoming part of the core. The companies that will define this field are those that can bridge rigorous mechanistic science, validated AI platforms, and pharma-grade execution. That’s the bar. And that’s what we’re building at Gero.
