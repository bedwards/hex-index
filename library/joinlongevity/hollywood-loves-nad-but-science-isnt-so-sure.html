NAD+ (Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is one of the most talked-about molecules in the longevity world. It plays a critical role in energy metabolism, DNA repair, and mitochondrial function. As we age, NAD+ levels decline, and that decline has been linked to fatigue, muscle loss, and slower recovery.

This led to the rise of NAD+ boosters like NMN and NR, marketed as anti-aging interventions aiming to restore what time takes away.

But what if we’ve misunderstood the relationship?

New Research: Muscles Function Normally Despite NAD+ Loss

A groundbreaking Cell Metabolism study (2024) put the NAD+ theory to the test. Researchers genetically deactivated Nampt, a critical enzyme in the NAD+ salvage pathway, in adult mice, causing an 85% reduction in NAD+ levelsspecifically in skeletal muscle.

The results were surprising:

  • Muscle strength and endurance remained normal

  • Mitochondria continued functioning effectively

  • No acceleration in biological aging (measured via DNA methylation clocks)

  • Metabolic health markers like glucose tolerance were unchanged

Despite such a severe depletion of NAD+, the muscle tissue was remarkably resilient.

This challenges the idea that NAD+ decline is a primary driver of muscle aging. Instead, it may be a byproduct, a downstream effect of deeper dysfunctions like inflammation, mitochondrial impairment, or cellular stress response failure.

Downstream vs. Upstream: Where Should We Intervene?

Think of NAD+ as fuel. Supplying more of it via NMN or NR is like topping off the gas tank, but what if the real problem is a failing engine?

NAD+ supplementation is a downstream intervention: it temporarily raises levels of a molecule whose decline might reflect upstream breakdowns, like inflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, or impaired stress responses. It doesn’t restore the body’s innate ability to maintain NAD+, nor does it repair the underlying systems that rely on it.

This is why a growing number of researchers now advocate targeting upstream mechanisms, particularly mitochondrial health and inflammation, as more effective levers for promoting longevity.

Enter Urolithin A: A Mitochondrial Makeover

One of the most promising upstream interventions is Urolithin A — a postbiotic compound produced by gut microbes from dietary ellagitannins (found in foods like pomegranate and walnuts). Urolithin A doesn’t raise NAD+ directly. Instead, it restores mitochondrial health by stimulating two essential processes:

  • Mitophagy — the clearance of dysfunctional mitochondria

  • Mitogenesis — the creation of new, efficient mitochondria

As we age, mitochondrial turnover slows, leading to accumulated damage, reduced energy output, and increased oxidative stress, all of which can contribute to systemic inflammation and NAD+ decline as secondary effects.

Urolithin A, in both animal and human studies, has been shown to:

  • Improve mitochondrial health and density

  • Enhance muscle performance in older adults

  • Support longevity pathways independent of direct NAD+ manipulation

In short, it addresses the root, not just the symptom.

But What About the Recent Nature Study Showing an NAD Precursor Improves Muscle Function?

This is where things get interesting.

A recent paper in Nature Metabolism (2024) reported that an NAD+ precursor improved muscle strength and reduced fatigue in older adults. At first glance, this seems to contradict the earlier finding that NAD+ isn’t essential for muscle performance.

But here’s the twist: the compound wasn’t NMN or NR, it was Trigonelline, a plant-derived NAD+ precursor found in coffee and legumes.

And unlike NMN/NR, Trigonelline operates through a different biological mechanism.

Yes, it contributes to NAD+ biosynthesis, but it also appears to activate mitochondrial pathways directly. Studies suggest Trigonelline stimulates PGC-1α, a master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis and mitophagy. That means its benefits may stem less from NAD+ repletion, and more from its ability to restore mitochondrial quality control.

So while Trigonelline is technically an NAD+ booster, its therapeutic effects likely come from upstream modulation of energy metabolism, not just from raising NAD+.

That’s not a contradiction, it’s a clarification:

Not all NAD+ precursors are created equal, and their effects depend on how broadly they impact cellular health systems.

The Takeaway: It’s Not Just About NAD+

These studies suggest we may need to reframe the role of NAD+ in aging, particularly in muscle tissue. Simply topping up NAD+ may offer limited benefit if the upstream systems that regulate mitochondrial health, inflammation, and cellular stress response are already impaired.

Instead, we should:

  • Target upstream dysfunctions — like mitochondrial decay and chronic inflammation — which may cause the downstream drop in NAD+

  • Recognize that not all tissues are alike — skeletal muscle may adapt well to low NAD+, but the brain or liver may be more NAD-dependent

  • Understand the distinction — NMN and NR are downstream NAD+ precursors; Trigonelline is a precursor with upstream effects; Urolithin A is not a precursor at all, but a compound that restores mitochondrial quality through autophagy and regeneration

  • Focus on multi-layered interventions — such as exercise, mitochondrial support, inflammation control, and gut health optimization for broader and more sustainable impact

Where Longevity Science Goes Next

Rather than asking who should take NAD+ boosters, the more important question might be:

What’s the most effective way to restore mitochondrial function and maintain cellular resilience with age?

Here are some of the frontiers researchers are exploring now:

Tissue-Specific Strategies — Skeletal muscle may tolerate NAD+ loss, but other tissues like the brain or immune system may not. We need nuanced, tissue-informed interventions.

NAD-Independent Energy Systems — How do muscles sustain performance with such low NAD+? Researchers are looking into backup systems like the malate-aspartate shuttle and creatine kinase cycle.

Upstream Longevity Pathways — Compounds that restore mitophagy, reduce systemic inflammation, and optimize mitochondrial biogenesis may play a bigger role than simple NAD+ repletion.

Synergistic Therapies — Combining exercise with mitochondrial-targeted interventions (like Urolithin A or Trigonelline) may yield benefits greater than either alone.

Bottom Line:

NAD+ decline may not be the fire, it may be the smoke. To extend healthspan and preserve muscle function, we should stop obsessing over the NAD+ tank and start fixing the engine.
