*Welcome to the first issue of Ecopolitics newsletter!*

> We are a living part of a living planet. -[Joanna Macy](https://youtu.be/LwlXTAT8rLk?si=P8F2CaJS4ix3kZA3)

I will start with the grim facts, but I won’t stay there. The Earth is barreling toward an unrecognizable future due to the political failure to address climate change; there was a [73% drop](https://wwflpr.awsassets.panda.org/downloads/2024-lpr-executive-summary.pdf) in the average size of wildlife populations between 1970 and 2020; [millions](https://www.unicef.org/press-releases/air-pollution-accounted-81-million-deaths-globally-2021-becoming-second-leading-risk) of people die each year due to air pollution. Our political-economic system is cannibalizing the natural world that humans and other living beings are all a part of and rely on to survive and thrive.

The startling reality of the moment demands a new politics—or, perhaps better put, the renewal of an older form of politics rooted in the environment.

My hope is to keep the politics of this newsletter open-ended rather than prescriptive and following from formula. But I think it is useful for this first issue of the newsletter to sketch out one vision of an ecological politics.

The crises of structural injustice and environmental degradation can be thought of as failures of moral consideration. We have not managed to create a political and economic system that affords equal moral consideration to all people, let alone non-human animals and the natural world.

But to form a grounded and rooted politics, we can actually begin with the individual body. To gain an expansive moral consideration, you can start with you. The body (and with it the mind, spirit, etc.) is what is sacrificed in a political-economic system that squeezes the environment and human labor. Because of this, self-love, nurturing, and acceptance of the body is politically meaningful.

However, the self is only a starting point because the self is inextricably connected with the rest of the world. The circle of moral consideration can and should expand from the self to the immediate community, a larger community, human beings as a whole, non-human animals, and the entire living world. This form of politics hinges on a recognition of interconnection and, as a result of this interconnection, demands a complete solidarity.

This approach implies an expansive view of both environmentalism and politics. Environmentalism is not just about protecting a patch of land here and there or instituting specific regulations but rather caring for an ecological system as a whole, which involves both humans and non-human beings. In this view, the environment and economics are closely linked, with the Earth and human labor seen as the foundation of economics.

An expansive view of politics means seeing both ‘official’ politics (such as elections and lobbying) and everyday life as political. To draw in part on comments by Peng Yu in my [interview](https://youtu.be/IqXMz_LenIc?si=Pr-dndtY_OyaKy7y) with him about the ancient Chinese philosopher Zhuangzi, the politics of everyday life involves matters such as a person’s relationship with friends and family, non-human animals, food, and their presentation of identity. The social world is not static but instead re-created every day, and that re-creation is a collective choice that can be either maintained or disrupted.

My ecological politics—which, again, is in flux and subject to change—draws from many sources. These sources include ecosocialism, green politics, liberalism, the model of The Great Turning as theorized by Joanna Macy, and the philosophical and religious traditions of India, China, and Indigenous Turtle Island (North America). As I write and research for this newsletter, I hope to deepen my understanding of these perspectives and learn new ones.

As heavy as political theory may seem and as grim as our present ecological and political crises are, I hope that this newsletter can be creative and slyly playful. In an effort to be true to the world we live in, *Ecopolitics* will be honest about losses and sorrows while also celebrating beauty and resilience.
