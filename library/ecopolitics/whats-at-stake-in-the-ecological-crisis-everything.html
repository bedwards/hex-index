Let’s face it: environmentalists want to take away your tasty cheeseburger, your cushy SUV, your suburban McMansion. You might think that we want to take away prosperity itself. I want to argue that this conception, while understandable, has things backward.

In my view, prosperity comes from the natural world refined by human labor.1 The natural environment is ultimately the source of both our necessities and our luxuries. And so a natural environment in crisis threatens whatever society values. In the collapse of the planet’s life-sustaining systems, everything is at stake.

The environmentalist Guardian columnist George Monbiot writes that we tend to think of environmental crises in discrete “boxes” such as climate, biodiversity, and deforestation among others. These are indeed crises, but for Monbiot they should not be addressed as stand-alone issues: “Nature recognises no such divisions. As Earth systems are assaulted by everything at once, each source of stress compounds the others.” This gives a sense of the magnitude of the ecological crisis as a whole, as does a statistic cited in Monbiot’s article from a 2021 scientific paper: “only 3% of the Earth’s land surface should now be considered ‘ecologically intact.’”

The gist of the environmental picture is that the systems that sustain life on Earth—yes, that includes you and me—are breaking down. Monbiot identifies a simple culprit: “the sheer volume of economic activity. We are doing too much of almost everything, and the world’s living systems cannot bear it.”

I want to emphasize that this is not a question of either/or. It’s not either we can have an economy that produces things such as SUVs, McMansions, and (a lot of) cheeseburgers or we can have a healthy biosphere. Instead, if the systems that sustain life fail, economic prosperity will come to an end too. Environmentally devastating forms of economic production will halt one way or another, the question is whether they take down the planet with them.

For now, I want to sidestep the question of whether a growth-based economic system (such as capitalism) can be made compatible with thriving ecosystems.2 Instead, I am content here to make two basic points: first, that it is vital to repair the damage caused to the planet and restore ecosystems and second, that we need to make the economic changes necessary to accomplish this goal, whatever those changes end up being. Radical change need not be a preference, but it may be a necessity.

Environmentalists should want economic prosperity. However, economic prosperity must be compatible with ecological well-being in the long-term because the natural world is the source of prosperity! Prosperity based on cannibalizing the Earth’s resources is unstable. In contrast, prosperity rooted in ecological well-being would be secure.

Finally, I want to make an aesthetic point: the types of production limited or eliminated on environmental grounds should be aesthetically value-neutral. This is not a culture war. Environmentalists should not be saying that tofu is cooler than cheeseburgers, just that we can (I think) produce more tofu than cheeseburgers within ecological limits.3 Moving to a sustainable way of living should be thought of as a political shift, not an aesthetic one. Green culture is not necessarily aesthetically superior to consumer culture, although it’s not necessarily inferior either.

Should the end of the world come, who knows what you may miss the most. At the end of the climate change allegory Don’t Look Up (2021), as the life-destroying shock of a meteor impact is rippling through the planet, the main characters are sitting at a dining room table, talking about apple pie:

Marshall Mindy (Conor Sweeney): “I’m definitely gonna have some more of this apple pie. It’s so good.”

Kate Dibiasky (Jennifer Lawrence): “It’s actually store-bought, but you really can’t tell.”

Dr. Teddy Oglethorpe (Rob Morgan): “If I’m to be completely honest, which at this moment, why not? I actually like the junky taste of store-bought better than homemade.”

Dr. Randall Mindy (Leonardo DiCaprio): “You gotta be kidding me.”

Dr. Oglethorpe: “No, I’m not kidding.”

June Mindy (Melanie Lynskey): “You know what? I know what he means. It’s like a childhood memory thing.”4

If we avoid apocalypse and align our economics with ecological imperatives, there may no longer be SUVs, McMansions, or even cheeseburgers. But there might still be store-bought apple pie.

Just before the dining room collapses, Dr. Mindy reflects, “Thing of it is, we really… We really did have everything, didn't we?”

What’s Out There

Data: A project is tracking the human cost of the Trump administration’s funding cuts to the US Agency for International Development (USAID). The “Impact Metrics Dashboard” on impactcounter.com tracks statistics such as “additional adult malaria cases” and “additional child deaths from malnutrition.” Taken as a whole, the tracker showed 98,290 adult deaths and 205,096 child deaths due to the cuts as of May 30.

Impactcounter.com also estimates the human cost of potential cuts to Medicaid in the U.S. Should Medicaid be cut, the tracker estimates 28,721 additional deaths annually and 750,857 additional Americans will face “catastrophic healthcare costs” annually.

The project was initiated by Brooke Nichols, an associate professor of global health at Boston University. Read more about the project in this piece, written in March, on the university’s website.
