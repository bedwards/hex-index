*This essay is based in part on [a piece](https://politicscolumn.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/green-growth.pdf) I wrote on growth and the environment in 2022.*

Debates about the environment and the economy are usually between whether environmental protection will slow economic growth or whether it will not. However, there is a third perspective: that aiming to grow the economy is itself the problem.

Those who argue that we should not focus on economic growth belong to movements including ‘degrowth’ and ‘doughnut economics.’ Here, I will refer to all these thinkers as ‘growth critics’ except when specifying a particular movement. In this piece, I will present some of their arguments, the counter-arguments by supporters of ‘green growth,’ and even bring in to the conversation two of my economics professors from college.

Most academic papers are published without much notice. However, every once in a while, there is the rare paper whose findings should be considered by just about everyone. One such paper, [“Is Green Growth Possible?”](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/59bc0e610abd04bd1e067ccc/t/5cbdc638b208fc1c56f785a7/1555940922601/Hickel+and+Kallis+-+Is+Green+Growth+Possible.pdf) by Jason Hickel and Giorgos Kallis (2019), may change the way you think about the economy.

‘Green growth’ is an attempt to sustain economic growth (using the metric of Gross Domestic Product, or GDP) at the national and global levels while also ensuring the health of the natural environment. Hickel and Kallis characterize green growth as based “on the assumption that absolute decoupling of GDP growth from resource use and carbon emissions is feasible (e.g. Solow 1973), and at a rate sufficient to prevent dangerous climate change and other dimensions of ecological breakdown.”

So what is decoupling? In a 2024 [essay](https://aeon.co/essays/how-to-think-about-the-prospects-of-truly-green-growth) for *Aeon*, researcher and writer Ville Lähde summarizes: “The basic idea is that economic growth can continue and literally decouple, or part ways, with material growth and environmental degradation.” Roughly speaking, if an economy is decoupling ‘relatively’ that means GDP is growing quicker than material use although material use continues to increase; if it is decoupling ‘absolutely’ that means resource use is actually decreasing even as GDP grows.

Hickel and Kallis’ “Is Green Growth Possible?” is a review paper, surveying empirical studies to determine if absolute decoupling is possible and happening quickly enough. Their answer is not encouraging:

> extant empirical evidence does not support the theory of green growth. This is clear in two key registers. (1) Green growth requires that we achieve permanent absolute decoupling of resource use from GDP. Empirical projections show no absolute decoupling at a global scale, even under highly optimistic conditions. While some models show that absolute decoupling may be achieved in high-income nations under highly optimistic conditions, they indicate that it is not possible to sustain this trajectory in the long term. (2) Green growth also requires that we achieve permanent absolute decoupling of carbon emissions from GDP, and at a rate rapid enough to prevent us from exceeding the carbon budget for 1.5°C or 2°C. While absolute decoupling is possible at both national and global scales (and indeed has already been achieved in some regions), and while it is technically possible to decouple in line with the carbon budget for 1.5°C or 2°C, empirical projections show that this is unlikely to be achieved, even under highly optimistic conditions.

In other words, Hickel and Kallis argue that evidence shows that a growing economy will, first, use more natural resources than is sustainable and, second, likely fail to make the transition away from carbon emissions quickly enough to meet the Paris Agreement targets to address climate change.

While anyone who can bear to read an econ paper should read Hickel and Kallis’ work in full, I will pick just one study they referenced to give a sense of how even ambitious attempts to reduce resource use are projected to fail should GDP continue to grow:

> Schandl *et al.* (2016) use a model based on 3 per cent average annual global GDP growth and explore three scenarios between 2010 and 2050. The reference scenario, with no significant change to environmental policies, shows that global resource use grows from 79.4 billion tonnes in 2010 to 183 billion tons in 2050… with slight relative decoupling. The ‘medium efficiency’ scenario, with a carbon price of $25 per ton of CO2 (rising by 4 per cent per year), shows that global resource use still grows steadily over the period, but at about half the rate of global GDP, reaching 130 billion tons by 2050. The ‘high efficiency’ scenario, with a carbon price starting at $50 (rising by 4 per cent per year to $236 by 2050) plus a doubling in the material efficiency of the economy (from historical average improvements of 1.5 per cent per year up to 4.5 per cent per year), shows that global resource use still grows steadily, but at about one-fourth the rate of global GDP, reaching 95 billion tons in 2050…

Does resource use equal environmental strain? Not quite, but Hickel and Kallis reference a study finding “that while the mass flows of individual materials are not indicative of their ecological impacts, and while impacts vary as technologies change, at an *aggregate* level there is a high degree of correlation (0.73) between material throughput and ecological impacts.”

There are some caveats worth mentioning. The authors note that the global material footprint surged at the beginning of this century, signaling a move in the wrong direction. However, according to a [2024 report](https://unstats.un.org/sdgs/report/2024/Goal-12/) on the UN Sustainable Development Goals, there has been recent good news:

> The global material footprint, representing raw materials extracted to meet final consumption demands, increased by 71 per cent from 57.4 billion metric tons in 2000 to 98.0 billion metric tons in 2022. Its compound annual growth rate declined from 3.9 per cent in 2003-2012 to 0.9 per cent in 2013-2022.

A decrease from 3.9% to 0.9% yearly growth is big, although I don’t know enough about the field to say how significant of a problem this is for Hickel and Kallis’ argument.

The authors also say that green growth seems plausible “at very low GDP growth rates, i.e. less than 1 per cent per year—significantly lower than historical trends and projected pathways.”

On climate change, a key issue is whether it is appropriate to employ bioenergy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS). Hickel and Kallis write that BECCS “entails growing large tree plantations to sequester CO2 from the atmosphere, harvesting the biomass, burning it for energy, capturing the CO2 emissions at source and storing it underground.” The authors reference arguments that BECCS is a reckless gamble, unproven and potentially damaging for people and the planet.

A possible nail in the coffin for green growth is a 2016 paper (Ward *et al.*) referenced by Hickel and Kallis that argues improvements in resource efficiency will eventually hit a wall:

> As the physical limits of resource efficiency are reached, continued GDP growth drives resource use back up. Ward *et al* conclude that ‘decoupling of GDP growth from resource use, whether relative or absolute, is at best only temporary. Permanent decoupling (absolute or relative) is impossible for essential, non-substitutable resources because the efficiency gains are ultimately governed by physical limits….’

The best case scenario is that we are a long way from hitting physical limits. Hickel and Kallis admit, “We cannot rule out substitutions or technological breakthroughs that will push such limits so far into the future as to render them irrelevant (e.g. nuclear fusion, 100 per cent recycling of materials fuelled by fusion or solar power, etc).”

However, this is a gamble, and even if massive technological advances in efficiency occur, that does not guarantee that sufficient decoupling will follow.

**Jonathan and Raja**

When I was in college, I was surprised to find that I enjoyed my Intro to Economics class. Still, I became queasy when we had to complete problem sheets for homework that involved math. Because of my math aversion, I ended up taking Earlham College’s Intro to Econ three times, dropping it twice before finally passing on the third try. One class was with Professor Jonathan Diskin, one with Professor Rajaram (Raja) Krishnan, and the third was a combined class with both Jonathan and Raja. (I will observe Earlham’s egalitarian Quaker ethos of using first names for professors here.) Jonathan and Raja were a good pair to learn economics from. Both were enthusiastic professors whose interest in the discipline inspired students to become interested as well. And the two seemed to represent opposing views on the subject matter—Raja a more orthodox, pro-capitalist economist while Jonathan is more heterodox and sympathetic to socialism. In the class they co-taught, Jonathan and Raja explored these opposing views in a collegial way.

So, given the limitations of my economics prowess, I reached out to Jonathan and Raja once again to discuss growth and global economics. Broadly speaking, Jonathan is sympathetic to growth critics while Raja is supportive of green growth. I will introduce Jonathan’s comments here and turn to Raja’s perspective later.

Jonathan says, “I think the idea of changing the way we think about growth and getting people to rethink how we evaluate economic success at a global level and at the level of country policy is really valuable.”

However, Jonathan also notes benefits that we have received from growth:

> one thing you can say for it is that for all the brutality and inequality entailed in the rise, development, and promulgation of global capitalism over the last several centuries, more people live longer than ever before and have access to more kinds of goods and services, including medical care, than ever before.

Jonathan also raises some questions about the meaning of growth:

> Growth doesn’t always mean consumption of more material goods. Growth often takes the form of people becoming educated and skilled in new ways….
> 
> The idea of us being a creative species—of doing new things, of having new needs, of developing new desires and new modes of doing things, new ways of doing things, more efficient ways of doing things, sometimes it consumes far fewer resources. So I think this idea of development, we’re always going to be, we should always be, a species that is seeking the new in various ways….
> 
> I’m predisposed to value a movement that is concentrating on challenging the primacy of GDP maximization. Absolutely…. That leads me to a series of questions about ‘well, how do we refer to and think about the kinds of dynamics and changes and improvements in our lives that we want and seek? If we’re not gonna call them growth, then how do we talk about those things?’ And some of them may use some resources and we may need to scale down other resources we use.

**Empirical Questions, Value Questions**

The question making up the title of Hickel and Kallis’ paper—“is green growth possible?”—can be given an empirical answer. We can utilize economic models to tell us something. However, in his [essay](https://aeon.co/essays/how-to-think-about-the-prospects-of-truly-green-growth) for *Aeon* mentioned earlier, Ville Lähde argues that focusing on the issue of decoupling may be a mistake:

> the whole toing and froing about decoupling may itself be a confusing dead end. It anchors us to the abstraction of GDP, to any idea of aggregated and undifferentiated growth and, conversely, it seems to invite an equally abstract environmental metric as a counterpoint. Decoupling A from B. It plays havoc with contextuality.

While I think that Hickel and Kallis do a good job being specific in their discussion of decoupling, Lähde’s concern about abstraction is worth considering.

In addition to the empirical question(s) about growth, there is also a question of values: is green growth *desirable*? Or, more broadly, should GDP growth be our economic goal?

In a 2020 [article](https://www.opendemocracy.net/en/oureconomy/green-growth-vs-degrowth-are-we-missing-point/) for *openDemocracy*, Beth Stratford criticizes “growth dependence”:

> The spectre of shrinking or stagnating GDP has not only been invoked to block environmental policies. It has also been invoked to block food standards, labour rights, and most recently to justify the lifting of COVID-19 restrictions in workplaces, in spite of the risks to public health. Fear of the consequences of economic contraction has been a major impediment to the containment of a pandemic, just as it has proved a major impediment to effective climate policy.
> 
> Our dependence on growth is thus a dangerous straitjacket. When certain forms of economic activity imperil our health and wellbeing, or the living systems upon which we depend, our governments must have the confidence to scale back those activities — without fear of triggering an economic crisis. That confidence will only be found if we escape our growth dependence.

In this article, Stratford plays peacemaker between green growthers and growth critics. She argues that given the rapidly deteriorating environment, we simply don’t have time to settle this economic debate. Instead, those from both camps should “agree on a basic set of demands…”

The policy problem resulting from dependence on economic growth is two-fold: first, the environmental degradation resulting from growth-driven extractive practices and second, the human cost of economic recession should growth stall. Stratford and Dan O’Neill identify [four “strategies”](https://goodlife.leeds.ac.uk/related-research/doughnut-shaped-recovery/) to mitigate both problems in the context of the UK:

> 1.  Safeguard basic needs, by strengthening the social security net and expanding access to free basic services
>     
> 2.  Empower and protect workers, to make working-time reduction a feasible solution to the threat of unemployment
>     
> 3.  Reduce our exposure to debt crises, to help achieve economic stability
>     
> 4.  Tackle rent extraction, to prevent rising inequality
>     

The transition necessary to avert environmental collapse is in some ways a matter of less: less material production in destructive industries and perhaps less production overall. However, insofar as this transition moves economies away from a dependence on growth, it is also a story of more: more time for any activities not focused on boosting profit and economic productivity. Growth critics can plausibly promise a stronger social safety net, better public services, and fewer working hours. Shifting away from growth dependence also broadens the space of democratic politics—if we are not gearing policy toward the narrow metric of GDP growth, we can more adequately consider other priorities.

Stratford and O’Neill’s report builds on the model of ‘doughnut economics’ pioneered by economist [Kate Raworth](https://www.kateraworth.com). The “doughnut” is the zone where human needs are met without surpassing the boundaries for a healthy environment. Stratford and O’Neill’s four strategies reduce an economy’s (in this case, the UK economy’s) growth dependence, which in turns enables action to be taken to protect the environment. In effect, these strategies aim to be shock absorbers in the event that environmental policies slow or reverse growth. As the economy is currently structured, a recession is a calamity; growth critics argue that restructuring the economy can change this dynamic, making it possible for the economy to remain sound if it shrinks.

**Objections**

I can imagine objections to growth critics from two directions. From the left, it could be argued that the global economy’s problems are deeper than growth dependence and progressive economic moves by governments will not be sufficient to create a just system. At some point I would like to explore the question of how deep the political rot goes and what this implies for how to engage in politics. But for now I will focus on the opposite objection: that green growth remains the most political feasible and economically just solution.

In my conversation with Raja, he argued in favor of this view. He says that green growth is possible and “the right question is, how do you get good governance?” saying it is possible to internalize costs and externalities.

One point by Raja speaks to the question of physical limits to sustainable growth raised by the 2016 Ward *et al.* paper referenced by Hickel and Kallis. Raja says, “There are people who actually believe that there are biophysical limits to growth and… the entropy law suggests that you will now hit up against a point where you can’t use material resources anymore. I just don’t buy that stuff, it’s a literature I’m very familiar with.”

While this is *not* a literature I am very familiar with, it seems to me that there are physical limits to how efficient production can be. Hickel and Kallis quote Ward *et al.*:

> For non-substitutable resources such as land, water, raw materials and energy, we argue that whilst efficiency gains may be possible, there are minimum requirements for these resources that are ultimately governed by physical realities: for instance the photosynthetic limit to plant productivity and maximum trophic conversion efficiencies for animal production govern the minimum land required for agricultural output; physiological limits to crop water use efficiency govern minimum agricultural water use, and the upper limits to energy and material efficiencies govern minimum resource throughput required for economic production.

This seems reasonable to me. However, as Hickel and Kallis note, what is relevant for the prospect of green growth in the short-term is how close we are to hitting these limits.

Raja does not see a conflict between shifting economic production to be more environmentally sustainable and growing production overall. He makes an analogy to the U.S. war economy of the early 1940s—in that case, what was produced changed “but there was plenty of growth.” He says, “To do good things for the environment, you might need to grow. You might need to use resources.”

Another endorsement of green growth comes from the economist [Paul Krugman](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/02/17/opinion/economic-growth-green-degrowth.html). Although his argument seems reasonable on its face, Hickel and Kallis’ review paper cast doubts on Krugman’s claims. Krugman goes so far as to say, “there is no necessary relationship between economic growth and the burden we place on the environment.” This seems to fundamentally miss the connection between the economy and the planet: there will always be at least *some* material basis for economic prosperity, and we have to determine how much of the Earth’s resources we can safely utilize.

Now to political feasibility. Jason Hickel is a supporter of ‘degrowth,’ which makes the case that wealthy countries should reduce their GDP while developing countries may continue to grow. Some argue that either degrowth will always be a losing battle in wealthy countries or, while it may eventually attract enough support to be politically possible, this consensus will not be built in the vanishingly short amount of time we have left to address climate change. Spencer Bokat-Lindell in the *New York Times* [quotes](https://www.nytimes.com/2021/09/16/opinion/degrowth-cllimate-change.html) Eric Levitz of *New York Magazine*:

> “Americans might well find themselves happier and more secure in an ultra-low-carbon communal economy in which individual car ownership is heavily restricted, and housing, health care, and myriad low-carbon leisure activities are social rights.” But, he adds, “nothing short of an absolute dictatorship could affect such a transformation at the necessary speed. And the specter of eco-Bolshevism does not haunt the Global North. Humanity is going to find a way to get rich sustainably, or die trying.”

In my conversation with Raja, he also saw degrowth as a political impossibility, arguing that prioritizing the environment in economic production cannot be legislated: “those are social transformations that have to come from the grassroots socially, politically—and you have to convince people it’s desirable.”

Jonathan was skeptical that Westerners would accept a dip in growth while other countries continued to grow: “I think the political systems are so sensitive to grievance, to relative position of one people, one country, one group relative to another…” However, “Change should not be driven by what’s feasible in the next year or decade. Long-term change has to be pursued over a long time.” The problem is that we don’t have a long time before existing environmental crises become calamities.

Another argument against degrowth is that due to the interconnectedness of the global economy, economic contraction in high-GDP countries would hamper economic growth in low- and middle-GDP countries. In a *Vox* article, Kelsey Piper [writes](https://www.vox.com/future-perfect/22408556/save-planet-shrink-economy-degrowth):

> the pandemic offered a taste of how a sudden drop in rich-world consumption would actually affect the developing world. Covid-19 [dramatically curtailed Western imports and tourism for a time](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/799701589552654684/pdf/Costs-and-Trade-Offs-in-the-Fight-Against-the-COVID-19-Pandemic-A-Developing-Country-Perspective.pdf). The consequences in poor countries were devastating. Hunger rose, and child mortality followed….
> 
> Hickel’s perspective is that most trade between rich and poor countries is extractive, not mutually beneficial — and that maybe when that dynamic ceases, poor countries will have the chance for the catch-up growth they merit. That’s one take. But it means that degrowth’s case for not crushing the poor world is predicated on a speculative take on how those countries can grow — one that democratically elected leaders in those countries largely don’t share.

Raja argued that we don’t know what effect reduced consumption in wealthy countries will have on developing countries:

> Say tomorrow the United States of America cuts its consumption by 30%. There will be short-run disruptions, but then… \[other countries\] will all transform themselves in very, very interesting ways. And where the new path will be, I think it’s a mug’s game to sort of figure out what will be the exact effect. Because what we’re not taking into account over there is endogenous changes. These are not just exogenous factors, systems transform themselves, systems change. And when systems change we’ll end up with new paths and new equilibria will work themselves out.

However, Raja also emphasizes the crisis of global poverty:

> we will do so much better on the environmental front, we will do so much better on intergenerational environmental issues if we start paying a lot more attention to intragenerational poverty issues.

Raja continues:

> \[There are\] very nice ways of sort of tying together intragenerational poverty issues with intergenerational environment issues. Because what we do know is, once you become richer and wealthier you start caring about the environment a lot more…. Caring about the environment is a luxury even though a bad environment screws the poor a lot more than it screws the rich.

So, who is right? Will a move away from growth save the planet while empowering workers and undermining neocolonial economic systems? Or will any stall in growth harm people’s economic security, with the most vulnerable hurt the most?

In another issue of *Ecopolitics* I will attempt—with more insight from Jonathan and Raja—to take a first pass at a broader view of global economics.

**What I Think**

After looking into the debate between green growthers and growth critics, my tentative view is that maintaining a growth-dependent economic structure is a very risky bet. The ecological transition is not happening thoroughly enough or quickly enough to prevent myriad environmental disasters (climate change, biodiversity loss, etc.) and it is not clear that things will change in the future if we keep the current model. We could double down on growth, do our best to advocate for green policies, and hope that things work out for the planet. Or we could restructure our economic systems to prioritize other things besides growth. I think the second option is the better one.

There is a chance that technology will change the game, and breakthroughs in artificial intelligence may give us a better-than-usual chance of achieving breakthroughs in material and energy efficiency as well. However, it is my understanding that at present AI is putting ever greater strain on the environment and gobbling up resources from human beings. Further, AI may turbocharge economic growth, compounding the challenge of decoupling. Relying on a technological *deus ex machina* is another risky gamble.

The challenge then is a political one: winning the kind of economic shifts that will enable the economy to weather contraction should necessary environmental policies reduce GDP.

Moving beyond growth also tests our moral considerations. If the transition is to be just, it must empower developing countries rather than choke off capital and leave them to fend for themselves. It is these economic considerations for the working majority—both in wealthy Western countries and everywhere else—that temper my enthusiasm for a shift away from growth.

If a post-growth model can find better solutions for global economic security, that would be momentous. Can we create a more just and democratic economic order with environmental and human well-being at its center? I would like to think so.
