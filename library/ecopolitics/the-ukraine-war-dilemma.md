When thinking about the war in Ukraine, I try to keep two things in mind at once:

1.  Russia is waging an imperialist war against Ukraine and has no right to Ukrainian territory.
    
2.  The war has been a bloodbath for both Ukrainians (soldiers and civilians) and Russians (mostly soldiers) and this human cost makes ending the war morally urgent.

The problem with these two points is that there is some tension between them. The first point implies the political importance of victory for Ukraine while the second implies the humanitarian importance of a speedy ceasefire absent such a victory. To put it simply, the Ukraine war is a situation where, at least in the short term, peace and justice are incompatible.

The cost of the war to Ukrainian civilians is startling. The Council on Foreign Relations’ [summary](https://www.cfr.org/global-conflict-tracker/conflict/conflict-ukraine) of the war states, “Fighting and air strikes have inflicted over 40,000 [civilian casualties](https://ukraine.ohchr.org/sites/default/files/2025-01/Ukraine%20-%20protection%20of%20civilians%20in%20armed%20conflict%20%28December%20%202024%29_ENG.pdf), while 3.7 million people are [internally displaced](https://www.unrefugees.org/emergencies/ukraine/#:~:text=There%20are%20nearly%205.1%20million,\(as%20of%20May%202023\).&text=More%20than%206.2%20million%20refugees,\(as%20of%20July%202023\).&text=Approximately%2017.6%20million%20people%20are%20in%20need%20of%20humanitarian%20assistance%20in%202023.), and 6.9 million have fled Ukraine. 12.7 million people [need](https://www.unrefugees.org/emergencies/ukraine/#:~:text=There%20are%20nearly%205.1%20million,\(as%20of%20May%202023\).&text=More%20than%206.2%20million%20refugees,\(as%20of%20July%202023\).&text=Approximately%2017.6%20million%20people%20are%20in%20need%20of%20humanitarian%20assistance%20in%202023.) humanitarian assistance.” According to [Encyclopedia Brittanica](https://www.britannica.com/event/2022-Russian-invasion-of-Ukraine/Costs-and-casualties-the-price-of-Putins-war), Russia “carried out the forcible transfer of between 900,000 and 1.6 million Ukrainian citizens (including some 260,000 children) to Russian territory…” When Russian soldiers were pushed out of the areas around Kyiv shortly after the full-scale war began, war crimes committed by Russia against Ukrainian civilians became apparent.

Total numbers of military casualties are disputed. A piece by [The Kyiv Independent](https://kyivindependent.com/a-very-bloody-war-what-is-the-death-toll-of-russias-war-in-ukraine/) notes:

> The true military death toll is the subject of intense media debate. In December 2024, \[Ukrainian President Volodymyr\] Zelensky disputed reports that as many as 80,000 Ukrainian soldiers have been killed during the full-scale war.
> 
> Zelensky's comments likely referred to the Wall Street Journal's claims published in September that **[80,000 Ukrainian soldiers had been killed](https://www.wsj.com/world/one-million-are-now-dead-or-injured-in-the-russia-ukraine-war-b09d04e5)**, with a further 400,000 wounded. The outlet estimated Russia's losses at as many as… 200,000 killed and 400,000 injured.

It is tempting to offload the moral weight of Russian war casualties by saying that these soldiers deserve what they have coming to them. I find this morally dubious even if all the soldiers made the free and fully informed decision to fight, but it becomes even more difficult to maintain when you learn more about Russia’s military.

According to a finding from earlier in the war, men from Buryatia, where there is a large number of ethnic minorities, were 75 times more likely to die fighting in Ukraine than those from Moscow. Natalia Arno of the Free Russia Foundation said, “When Putin's Russia attacked Ukraine, it's definitely a genocide, a very tragic genocide of Ukrainians. Simultaneously, inside Russia, there is an ethnocide of ethnic minorities.” ([PBS Newshour](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/russias-ethnic-minorities-disproportionately-conscripted-to-fight-the-war-in-ukraine))

Russia is willing to sustain huge military casualties, and last fall’s military offensives by Russia were particularly bloody. A December 2024 BBC News [article](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c0ewneynypwo) states:

> Russia is estimated to have lost about 125,800 soldiers \[killed or injured\] over the course of its autumn offensives, according to the Washington-based Institute for the Study of War (ISW).
> 
> Russia's “meat-grinder” tactics, the ISW says, mean that Moscow is losing more than 50 soldiers for each square kilometre of captured territory.

Finally, the war has had numerous spillover effects beyond the physical harm caused by bombs. Russians have felt the effects of economic sanctions, the global economy was shaken by the war, and as the Council on Foreign Relations [notes](https://www.cfr.org/global-conflict-tracker/conflict/conflict-ukraine), global food insecurity increased.

When Russia launched its full-scale invasion in February 2022, Ukraine shocked the world by repelling Russia from the Kyiv area and preventing an overall takeover of Ukraine. However, Russia has maintained an occupation of “roughly 20 percent” of Ukraine, [according to](https://www.cfr.org/global-conflict-tracker/conflict/conflict-ukraine) the Council on Foreign Relations. Military support by the U.S. and its allies during the Biden presidency bolstered Ukraine’s ability to defend itself, but the U.S. never lent its full military might to Ukraine. This was largely due to the fear of triggering a potentially disastrous direct conflict between the U.S. and Russia, two nuclear-armed superpowers with the ability to destroy the world. I do not wish to downplay this concern but there is no doubt that the nuclear card has helped enable Russia to unleash immense (non-nuclear) destruction in Ukraine and previously in Syria.

As months and then years of war have dragged on and the human cost mounts, this does not look like a war Ukraine can win outright—at least without a dramatic shift in U.S./NATO policy toward unfettered military support.

Charles Kupchan of the Council on Foreign Relations [puts the situation](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/what-the-u-s-is-offering-and-demanding-in-latest-ukraine-ceasefire-proposal) like this:

> The principle is that Ukraine should restore territorial integrity and get back every inch of its country. In reality, Ukraine can't do that because it doesn't have the military capability to do so.

As with peace and justice, principles and pragmatism go best together but are sometimes incompatible.

The political big picture shows a battle between democracy (Ukraine and its allies pre-Trump) and authoritarianism (Russia and Trump’s increasingly authoritarian America). But politics is not just about the big picture. It’s also about the individual lives of Ukrainians and Russians affected by the war.

When peace and justice cannot both be immediately achieved, the question becomes: what is the next best outcome? It seems to me that at the level of elite politics (the decisions of political leaders) the best outcome is a ceasefire as soon as possible.

A ceasefire will stop the bleeding but it won’t heal the wounds of Russian occupation in parts of Ukraine’s south and east. Life under Russian occupation cannot be described as a state of peace. A ceasefire also will not heal the wounds of authoritarianism and racism in Russia.

These wounds may best be healed at the grassroots level, by the work of Ukrainians and dissident Russians. Progress toward justice will likely be slow, if there is progress at all, but it is justice that can ensure a lasting peace.

*I approach this article with humility both because foreign policy is not the subject I am most comfortable writing about and because my argument here is at odds with where many progressives, including Ukrainian activists, stand. I welcome all feedback on the piece.*
