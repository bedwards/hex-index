*Although* Ecopolitics *usually appears on the first Sunday of the month, I’ve published this issue a week early because of the timeliness of its subject matter.*

In New York City, socialist State Assemblymember Zohran Mamdani has almost certainly defeated former Governor Andrew Cuomo to become the [Democratic nominee](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2025/06/24/us/elections/results-new-york-city-mayor-primary.html) for mayor in the June 24 election.

Mamdani won 43.5% of first-choice votes to Cuomo’s 36.4% (with 93% of first-choice votes recorded). Comptroller Brad Lander, the only other candidate to reach double-digits, won 11.3%. New York City has ranked-choice voting, allowing voters to rank up to five candidates in order. Mamdani is expected to build on his lead once all the rankings are tallied because supporters of Lander and some other candidates are more likely to rank Mamdani than Cuomo. Cuomo has already conceded.

After nearly all polls showed Cuomo winning the race, Mamdani’s victory is surprising—and the margin of victory is shocking.

Mamdani, a member of the Democratic Socialists of America (DSA), campaigned on [progressive proposals](https://www.zohranfornyc.com/platform) including a rent freeze for rent-stabilized apartments, making public buses free, free child care for children six weeks to five years old, and creating city-owned grocery stores aimed at reducing costs for shoppers. On the revenue side, Mamdani plans to raise NYC’s corporate tax rate and add a flat 2% income tax for those making more than $1 million a year. A catch is that these taxes require approval from New York’s state government, according to a *[Politico](https://www.politico.com/news/2025/05/27/zohran-mamdani-policy-pitches-new-york-00369756)* [article](https://www.politico.com/news/2025/05/27/zohran-mamdani-policy-pitches-new-york-00369756) that is critical of Mamdani’s plans to increase revenue.

Because this is *Ecopolitics*, I want to take a look at Mamdani’s [environmental plans](https://www.zohranfornyc.com/platform). Mamdani says he will build green energy on public lands and he has an ambitious plan related to schools, pledging to “renovate 500 public schools with renewable energy infrastructure and HVAC upgrades, transform 500 asphalt schoolyards into vibrant green spaces, create 15,000 union jobs, and build resilience hubs in 50 schools that provide resources and safe spaces during emergencies.” More broadly, an affordable New York City with extensive public services is an environmental win in itself; living in cities and using [common infrastructure and facilities](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2017/may/31/private-wealth-labour-common-space) is highly resource efficient.

Mamdani has proven himself to be a megawatt political star, taking the mayoral race by storm with his campaign videos and turning in impressive performances in the two debates. As the writer [Ross Barkan](https://rosselliotbarkan.com/p/the-collapse-of-the-professional) put it, Mamdani answers the question, “What if Barack Obama were *actually* left-wing and Muslim…”? (Barkan previously ran for office himself; Mamdani was his campaign manager).

Mamdani is only 33, but the mayoralty is near the peak of where he can reach in politics—born in Uganda, he is constitutionally ineligible to be president. If the immigrant story of a Ugandan-American of Indian heritage sounds familiar, you may be thinking of the film *Mississippi Masala*, directed by Mira Nair. Nair happens to be Mamdani’s mother. Mamdani is now writing his own immigrant story in this bid to lead a city that includes [more than three million](https://www.nyc.gov/assets/immigrants/downloads/pdf/MOIA-2024-Annual-Report_4.4.25.pdf) immigrants.

Should Mamdani win the general election, it would be the most consequential socialist electoral victory in U.S. history, with the possible exception of Bernie Sanders’ successful U.S. Senate runs. New York has long been a place of tension between left-wing and right-wing impulses, both America’s financial capital and the heart of the country’s socialist movement. Its mythos captures this tension, the city at once emblematic of a capitalist-leaning version of the American Dream (“[If I can make it there](https://genius.com/Frank-sinatra-theme-from-new-york-new-york-lyrics), I'll make it anywhere”) and a left-leaning version that emphasizes inclusivity (“[Give me your tired](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/46550/the-new-colossus), your poor,/Your huddled masses yearning to breathe free…”). Mamdani emphasizes the latter vision of New York City (and America), promising that with the right political leadership, the dreams of all working-class New Yorkers can be realized right there in New York.

Dreaming demands solidarity. And when I look out at this room and out onto the midnight skyline, that is what I see.

(Zohran Mamdani, from his [victory speech](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yxyXXVoi514))

**The Context**

The Democratic primary occurred amid a crisis of leadership in New York City. Mayor Eric Adams was indicted by the Biden-era Department of Justice for alleged corruption. Then, in February, “the US Department of Justice moved to drop criminal charges against Adams, in what many see as a blatant quid pro quo for getting Adams onboard as a political ally” of Donald Trump, according to a *[Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2025/feb/18/eric-adams-donald-trump-corruption-case)* [article](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2025/feb/18/eric-adams-donald-trump-corruption-case). Adams’ apparent indebtedness to Trump, along with his own hostility to immigrants, has put undocumented New Yorkers at risk during Trump’s mass deportation effort.

Adams was elected in 2021 as a Democrat but is running for re-election this year as an independent. Although that made the Democratic race an open primary, a clear front-runner emerged: Andrew Cuomo.

While Cuomo has high name ID and retains goodwill from many Democrats, he resigned as New York governor in 2021 “after the attorney general of New York found he had sexually harassed roughly a dozen women.” (*[NY Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/06/24/nyregion/andrew-cuomo-sexual-harassment.html)*) Cuomo has mostly denied wrongdoing and, as the *Times* article also notes, he has “used millions of dollars in state resources to mount a scorched-earth legal campaign against many of the women who accused him of mistreatment…”

Cuomo campaigned on his experience in government and presented himself as a competent moderate. According to polling, none of the numerous candidates in the race rivaled Cuomo—except for Mamdani, who picked up steam as the contest went on.

Cuomo had the backing of outside spenders on the race, such as Fix the City PAC. According to a *[City & State New York](https://www.cityandstateny.com/politics/2025/06/pro-cuomo-pac-responsible-nearly-half-all-outside-spending-across-all-nyc-primary-elections-year/406209/)* [article](https://www.cityandstateny.com/politics/2025/06/pro-cuomo-pac-responsible-nearly-half-all-outside-spending-across-all-nyc-primary-elections-year/406209/) published on June 20, $13.5 million in outside spending had been spent in support of Cuomo and $6 million had been spent in opposition to Mamdani. However, Mamdani’s campaign raised a significant amount of money directly, and he had a formidable [DSA-backed field operation](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/06/24/nyregion/nyc-primary-day.html) that turned out the vote for him.

In addition to the organizational strength of the DSA and his own political talent, Mamdani was helped to victory by left unity. Third-place finisher Brad Lander, who is to the left of Cuomo but somewhat to the right of Mamdani, was a [key ally](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/06/26/nyregion/brad-lander-zohran-mamdani.html). Lander scorched Cuomo in the debates and Mamdani and Lander cross-endorsed each other (in an [amusing video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GYH_zerPE3M)). The two campaigned together in the closing stretch of the race. Lander, who is Jewish, also helped defuse accusations of anti-Semitism against Mamdani. The Lander-Mamdani collaboration demonstrates that there are times when socialists and progressive liberals can succeed when they work together. Mamdani also benefited from the key endorsement of Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez and the support of the Working Families Party, which endorsed a slate of candidates including Mamdani ranked #1.

We can be free and we can be fed.

(Zohran Mamdani, from his [victory speech](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yxyXXVoi514), arguing that people need not look to authoritarianism for economic security)

**What’s Next?**

It appears that November’s general election for mayor will be a five-person race: Mamdani for the Democrats, Republican nominee Curtis Sliwa, and three independents: Adams, Cuomo, and Jim Walden, a lawyer. While Cuomo has retained ballot access, it is not yet clear if he will actively campaign (sources: [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2025/06/26/politics/cuomo-mamdani-mayor-november-ballot) and *[NYT](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/06/25/nyregion/nyc-mayor-election-november.html)*).

Unlike the primary, the general election is not ranked choice. With Mamdani crowned as the Democratic nominee and the anti-Mamdani vote potentially split, I see him as the front-runner. While the left unified enough in the primary to allow Mamdani to win, the question is whether business forces will unify enough in the general election to stop him. The stage is set for a socialist vs. capitalist battle in November.

There is some reason to temper expectations should Mamdani enter office as mayor. After all, he would be the executive of only one site of power, city government, which is interconnected with multiple other sites of power. It’s not certain that his policies will pan out for the good. Well-intentioned plans can have unintended effects. What I can say with greater confidence is that Mamdani would be a leader with character and the right vision for a different kind of politics. Already, he has joined Bernie Sanders and AOC in making democratic socialism a viable political path in this country. The space has opened for a politics beyond Trumpian fascism and the unpopular pre-Trump status quo.

Perhaps the previous apex of socialist politics in New York City was the [1917 mayoral run](https://jacobin.com/2024/12/mamdani-hillquist-nyc-mayor-socialists) of Socialist Party candidate Morris Hillquit, himself an immigrant. Hillquit finished third in the general election with around 22% of the vote. This time around, the socialist will probably win.
