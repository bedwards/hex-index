At once Inuit, North American, European, and Danish, Greenlanders hold many identifications. Now, Donald Trump wants to scrap the latter two and add a new one: US American.

Greenland—an over 2 million square kilometer large island home to 57,000—has become central to the wrangling of great powers for reasons that are linked to climate change. A Guardian article notes, “Greenland’s enormous ice sheet is losing an average of 30m tonnes of ice an hour due to the climate crisis…” The melting ice is revealing numerous minerals that are crucial for advanced technologies, including renewable energy. Infuriatingly, melting ice may also make it possible for Greenland’s oil and natural gas reserves to be tapped. Finally, melting ice in the Arctic Ocean around Greenland has increased the viability of using this area as a shipping route for global trade. The rush to take advantage of a new, speedy trade route and natural resources has led to jockeying for influence between Russia, China, and, increasingly, the United States.

As things stand, Greenland is a semi-autonomous territory of Denmark. But pro-independence sentiment is strong, and Greenland’s Prime Minister Múte B. Egede has called for independence. He said in a speech this year, "Like other countries in the world, we must work to remove the obstacles to cooperation – which we can describe as the shackles of colonialism — and move forward.”

A Politico article notes that should Greenland become independent, the island may pursue ‘free association’ agreements with a larger state, such as Denmark or perhaps the US—or both. Greenland’s leadership has also signaled support for greater US economic investment in mineral mining.

All in all, Greenland is engaged in a delicate dance between regional and global powers, striving for sovereignty while also trying to secure economic and military support from those who can provide it.

Greenland’s government, led by the pro-independence, environmentalist Inuit Ataqatigiit (IA) party, is attempting a similar tightrope act on the environment. A Reuters article on IA’s 2021 election win states, “The party campaigned against a controversial rare-earth mining project at Kvanefjeld in the south of the island…. Kvanefjeld contains a large deposit of rare earth metals, used to make wind turbines and electric car batteries, but also radioactive uranium, which many fear will harm the country's fragile environment.” Once in power, the government banned uranium mining but supports other forms of mining. Crucially, the current government also suspended new oil exploration.

For the environmentalist left, it is important to defend Greenland’s right to independence from Denmark and oppose infringement on its sovereignty by other countries—such as our own. It is interesting to note how Greenland is trying to chart its own course and secure its own interests amid the competition of larger powers. And Greenland is a reminder of the centrality of climate change in creating a new physical, and political, world. Global heating has led to thawing that could open up new fossil fuel resources—new fuel to burn the planet. It has also enabled mining of minerals that can be used to boost the transition to clean energy, although perhaps at a local environmental cost. Aaja Chemnitz Larsen, a Greenlandic representative in Denmark’s parliament from the IA party, told The Guardian, “Climate change is affecting everything in Greenland, we are seeing land becoming exposed and also new species in the sea… For us, climate change is real, there’s no doubt that it’s here and will become more difficult for us in time.”

From a US perspective, President Trump’s belligerence toward Greenland holds clues as to what his broader foreign policy will look like. ‘America First’ is shaping up to be a peculiar mixture of isolationism and expansionism. Trump eschews multi-lateral alliances led by the US such as NATO. And even though the institutions of the post-WWII global order were heavily molded by the US to serve its interests and some remain largely controlled by the US, this is insufficiently nationalistic for Trump. He is not interested in a (nominally) rules-based US-led global order. Instead, Trump wants a powerful United States that flexes its muscles on its own, forging ad-hoc deals and gaining whatever it can wherever it can. America’s power is underpinned by its economic and military strength, but it is often exerted through Trump’s blustery rhetoric at press conferences and in Truth Social posts.

The prospect of annexing Greenland—as well as returning the Panama Canal to US control and even incorporating Canada into this country—can be thought of as a return to an old model of US colonialism. Ian Ward writes on the appeal of Trump’s expansionism to the New Right political movement:

Some on the New Right argue that acquiring Greenland would represent the opening of a new American frontier, reviving the “frontier mentality” and “settler spirit” that imbued the American pioneers largely of the 19th century. Others argue that Greenland could serve as a sort of spiritual and technological staging ground for more ambitious feats of American expansion, including the eventual settlement of Mars.

During his presidential campaign, Trump himself proposed building new cities on federal land to “reopen the frontier…”

With Trump, there is a return of Manifest Destiny as an ideal, promising the glorious expansion of America no matter what or, indeed, who is in the way. Unremarked upon is the reality that US expansion on this continent involved the genocidal mass displacement and killing of Indigenous Americans.

Trump’s departure from recent US foreign policy norms may not be all bad. Trump played a key role in sealing the current Gaza ceasefire deal, although this seemingly good deed comes with a litany of caveats. It is also plausible that Trump will achieve an end to the Ukraine war quicker than a Harris administration would have and with no fewer concessions to Russia—we’ll see what happens.

Critics of Trump may be tempted to support a return to the global political system as it was before. Conversely, opponents of the global status quo may be enthusiastic about Trump’s wrecking ball. But we can do better than either a global order disproportionately controlled by and benefiting the West or an international system of Trump-style nationalisms. The left can present a third option.

Left-wing internationalism should be based on anti-imperialism, real democracy, and an egalitarian, Earth-sustaining economics. What exactly this looks like and how we further this approach is a harder question. It’s something I’m still thinking about.
