Last week I [claimed](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/is-blue-state-ed-reform-hopeless) that ed reform is currently hopeless in blue states because elected Democrats have no incentive to buck the teachers unions. I heard five responses:

1.  It’s not hopeless because the unions support some (blue-tinged) reforms.
    
2.  It’s not hopeless because some elected Democrats are tiring of the union position.
    
3.  It’s not hopeless because a progressive approach to reform can overcome union resistance.
    
4.  It’s not hopeless because regulatory agencies—which are somewhat insulated from politics—can drive reform in blue states.
    
5.  It may or may not be hopeless, but education policy in red states is a mess, too.
    

**1\. Unions support some progressive reforms.**

Some folks wrote me privately to argue that the unions are not the boogeyman I make them out to be. The NEA and AFT and their state and local affiliates are on board with school funding reforms, for example, that drive more resources to the neediest schools—as well as efforts to diversify the teaching profession and implement universal pre-K. In all three of these cases, they say, it’s stingy Republicans (and their business backers) or ideological conservatives (with their bugaboos about any race-conscious policies) that are the problem. And they have less power in blue states, so progress is possible there.

**2\. Some Democrats are showing more willingness to break with the unions.**

**Marguerite Roza** is hopeful that Democratic leaders are starting to show some courage again:

> My oversimplified take: In the last few years, blue state leaders and unions were closely aligned on the narrow push for more money for K–12. With lots of cash in the mix, both were happy. But more money is going to be hard to come by going forward and that alliance starts to fray. As push comes to shove, the unions will want to protect different parts of the pie than would some of their allies (I’m thinking of a program with great results for Washington K–12 homeless youth that just got cut).
> 
> Plus, the NAEP scores left some state leaders (not just in blue states) feeling a bit duped, which might also be driving a wedge in that alliance with some blue state leaders now wanting better outcomes. Couple of examples:
> 
> -   Oregon’s **Governor Kotek**: “I don’t believe in writing a blank check” where “the [disconnect between spending and results](https://www.wweek.com/news/schools/2025/03/10/kotek-on-education-spending-i-dont-believe-in-writing-a-blank-check/) has emerged as a hot topic in Salem this legislative season.”
>     
> -   After years of dismissing test scores, Washington’s state superintendent **Chris Reykdal** is turning his focus to improving math scores (albeit by asking for another $10M from the legislature).
>     
> 
> So I’m hopeful. Here’s what could help: **NAEP every year** (off years could be a lower-cost skinny NAEP without as much detail). Governors are competitive.

**3\. A full-throated progressive argument for reform can win in blue states.**

**Conor Williams** writes that the reason ed reform seems hopeless in blue states is that too many proponents are making arguments that appeal to the center or the center-right rather than the left. **Conor**’s entire post is in the “Raise Your Hand” section below, but here are some key excerpts.

First, he argues that we don’t need to frame reform entirely as a project in “moderation”:

> There are voters out there working in and around reform who are demonstrably, verifiably progressive—not moderate. Major reform projects can also be legibly presented in demonstrably progressive terms—the problems with neighborhood schools are the same problems that [the environmental justice movement](https://www.nrdc.org/stories/environmental-justice-movement) has been wrestling.
> 
> My point here is that reform’s path forward with progressives has to be about re-centering the substantive problems reform is actually trying to solve. A reform movement that targets the essential inequity of neighborhood schools and gerrymandered school districts and unfair K–12 funding systems has political legs. The move has to be about simply deciding not to frame the argument—especially the public argument—in reference to other political groups or some mushy and inconstant “moderate” spot.

Furthermore:

> It’s worth noting that the history of reform belies efforts to redefine it as a moderates’ project! NCLB’s progressive leaders, as we all know, were Ted Kennedy and George Miller, guys whose careers get filed in the “Liberal Lions” section of the political library. Why not give that—a full-throated progressive account of reform—another try?

**4\. State boards and/or departments of education can drive reform in blue states.**

Another possibility is to focus on regulatory actions instead of legislative wins. That’s been a recipe for success in Massachusetts, argues **Heather Peske**, who served in a senior role in the Bay State’s Department of Education (see her full post below, too):

> We got a lot done in Massachusetts through regulation and by being smart about how we used our authority at the SEA, with respect to:
> 
> -   curriculum and professional learning reform in math, science, and reading;
>     
> -   regulating charters so they are strong and many hugely outperform their district counterparts;
>     
> -   educator licensure reform;
>     
> -   revising standards and accountability for ed prep programs;
>     
> -   educator evaluation that *is* better than it used to be, even if not the major reform we had hoped;
>     
> -   adult learning;
>     
> -   innovation in teacher compensation in the state “takeover” districts;
>     
> -   support for diversifying the teaching profession;
>     
> -   standing up a teacher apprenticeship model (results not out yet); and
>     
> -   supporting teacher residency programs.
>     
> 
> Much can be done if you have capacity at the SEA and courageous leaders (who are supported by the governor, who, at the time I was there, was Charlie Baker—a Republican not beholden to the teachers unions).
> 
> Is it hopeless? No. It’s just hard. So let’s roll up our sleeves and do the work.

**5\. Red states are a mess, too.**

Finally, let’s hear from **Morgan Polikoff**, who is not buying the argument that red states are kicking blue states’ butts:

> I am no defender of a “do nothing” approach to education reform, and I’ve been talking about the lack of Democrats’ affirmative agenda on this topic for a while. But it’s hardly the case that Republican-led ed reform is going swimmingly. I’ll take your Mississippi/Louisiana and counter with Oklahoma, where the reform agenda involved [watching p&rn on the job](https://oklahomavoice.com/2025/07/25/probe-needed-after-reports-of-nude-women-on-state-superintendents-office-tv-oklahoma-speaker-says/) and [trying to buy Donald Trump bibles](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/10/04/us/oklahoma-schools-trump-bible.html) for every classroom. Or Texas, where the state seems poised to adopt questionably-accurate [history standards](https://www.texastribune.org/2025/11/14/texas-sboe-social-studies-redesign-conservative-advisers/) that white-wash the past (the jury is out, but I think we know where this is headed). Or the relentless culture-war nonsense (some of which is fair game, a lot of which is not) that is clearly demoralizing educators and driving some out.
> 
> The truth is there are bad ideas left, right, and center in education right now. Unaccountable voucher schools that can legally discriminate are bad. Virtual schools and shortened school weeks are bad. Too much technology in the classroom is bad. Demolishing the Department of Education is bad. And yes, Democrats have plenty of bad ideas too—gutting testing and accountability, lowering standards in the name of equity, and resisting curriculum-driven reform, for instance. \[Shameless plug\] You can soon read more about this in my in-progress book on all the bad ideas that pollute our education policymaking. But there’s a reason that Democrats still have the edge in polling about education (see **Matt Barnum’s** [recent article](https://www.the74million.org/article/have-democrats-lost-voters-trust-on-education-not-according-to-most-polls/))—it’s because voters can see plain as day that the Republican agenda remains mostly oriented toward hollowing out public education, and voters don’t want that.

\---

Those are pretty good responses! But I’m still not persuaded. For example, where do we go with **Conor**’s argument that “gerrymandered school districts” are the problem? Does anyone think that district lines are ever going to change? I don’t. It’s also telling that **Heather** and her Massachusetts colleagues made so much progress…when a Republican was governor! And does “universal pre-K” really deserve to be considered part of the reform basket, when it’s really just about spending more money?

But then again, I’m a center-right guy, so of course I would have these takes. More important is the notion that blue state reform is possible—but we have to acknowledge that it’s going to look different. And that’s OK.

-   The worm is turning! After a decade of being down, out, and ignored, education in America is finally getting attention again, as opinion writers and journalists discover just how terribly our students are performing. As **Andy Rotherham** [puts it](https://www.eduwonk.com/2025/11/houston-we-have-a-problem-actually-no-houston-is-one-of-a-few-places-at-least-trying-dramatic-reform.html), “The past few days have seen a flurry of articles from writers who are not traditional characters on the education beat. And they point up a culture clash that isn’t R and D, left or right—it’s more about who thinks we have a serious problem and who thinks the erosion of standards isn’t a big deal, or is acceptable in service of other goals.”
    
-   One of those articles is [this one](https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/us/how-public-education-failed-in-the-liberal-enclaves-that-care-about-it-most/ar-AA1QEZgm) by **Andrew Rice**, titled “How Public Education Failed in the Liberal Enclaves That Care About It Most.” Discussing our recent (and depressing) NAEP scores, he quotes **Carol Jago**: “You can’t believe how low ‘below basic’ is… The things that those kids aren’t able to do is frightening.” Also name-checked: **Chad Aldeman**, **Tom Kane**, **Michael Hartney**, **Chris Cerf**, **Tim Daly**, **Carey Wright**, **Vlad Kogan**, and **Laura Overdeck,** plus the public schools of well-heeled districts like Montclair (NJ), Montgomery County (MD) and Ann Arbor (MI). It’s a long and compelling case for what he calls “the Declining Standards Hypothesis”—declining standards that are wreaking havoc everywhere.
    
-   A [much pithier take](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/2025/11/23/high-school-students-college-preparation/) on the same educational malaise came over the weekend from **Megan McArdle**. “Instead of rectifying disparities in preparation and achievement,” Megan writes, “people decided it would be simpler to adjust the measurements. Parents opposed standardized testing, got their kids disability diagnoses that allowed them extra time on tests, and lobbied teachers to change bad grades. Exhausted teachers responded with grade inflation, which also helped conceal that low-income and minority kids weren’t doing as well as their richer and White peers…. Colleges dropped standardized testing requirements, in part because that made it easier to diversify their student body.” And that’s how we got to a point where one of the best public universities in the nation—UC San Diego—had to introduce a remedial math course pegged at a grade-school level. Ugh!
    
-   So the terrible results of our broken education system are coming into stark relief. But how are we going to fix it? Let’s turn to eternal optimist **Joe Viteritti**, who [hopes](https://www.vitalcitynyc.org/articles/mamdanis-charter-school-dilemma) that **Zohran Mamdani** will be savvy enough to embrace NYC’s super-effective charter schools. “Such a shift,” Joe argues, “would give economically disadvantaged parents the real choices they deserve and enhance the future fortunes of district schools, charter schools and maybe even the Democratic Party.”
    
-   Speaking of **Joe**, fellow New Yorker **Ray Domanico** [reviews his new book](https://www.city-journal.org/article/joseph-viteritti-radical-dreamers-progressives-school-choice-education) (see other reviews we’ve covered [here](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/all-worked-up-about-workkeys) and [here](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/should-we-let-cte-students-opt-out)). “*Radical Dreamers* is a well-written and important history of the educational choice movement,” Ray writes. “It reminds us that improving schools is possible and that reasonable voices from both sides of the political divide can contribute to that effort—if they have the courage to do so.”
    
-   Now from the “two things can be true at once” department: Our current educational achievement levels are terrible, but as **Freddie deBoer** [reminds](https://freddiedeboer.substack.com/p/was-the-united-states-once-a-global) us, that doesn’t mean that there was a Golden Age when America’s schools were crushing the competition. “We have never, ever done well in international educational comparisons,” he argues. In particular, compared to other OECD countries, “We’ve always sucked, more or less.” Yup! Though let it be said that we did have a [Golden Age of Educational Progress](https://www.the74million.org/article/when-was-the-golden-age-of-american-k-12-education-and-how-can-we-tell/) not so long ago—and not just for achievement gains but for [high school and college attainment](https://www.the74million.org/article/the-remarkable-educational-attainment-gains-of-the-school-reform-era/), too.
    
-   On Friday we [covered](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/the-dismembering-of-the-department) reformers’ reactions to the dismembering of the Department of Education. Now **Lisa Guernsey** [weighs in](https://www.the74million.org/article/downgrading-education-is-a-disastrous-misreading-of-what-our-country-needs/). “This week our national leaders decided that education isn’t something that the United States government needs to care about, let alone nourish and strengthen,” Lisa argues. That feels a little over the top to me, given the extremely limited federal role in K–12 education, ever before Trump ‘47. But I’m with her on this one: “It is not hard to predict chaos and confusion for the next several years as government officials try to figure out how these interagency agreements will work.”
    
-   **Nat Malkus** also [weighs in](https://www.aei.org/education/six-questions-about-the-restructuring-at-the-department-of-education/), in his typical understated style. He asks six good questions: 1) Is this restructuring, or merely subcontracting? 2) Will these partnerships “Break Up Federal Bureaucracy,” or just “break up the federal education bureaucracy?” 3) How big are the transaction costs for these government agencies? How about for the states, districts, and universities that work with them? 4) Is the Department of Labor well suited to take over the duties of ED’s Office of Elementary and Secondary Education (OESE)? 5) Will this restructuring last? 6) Is this the necessary intermediate step to closing ED for good? Nat, let us know when the Trump folks give you some answers!
    
-   Speaking of dismantling the Department, **John Katzman** [thinks](https://hechingerreport.org/opinion-it-will-take-patience-and-courage-to-fix-k-12-education-without-the-department-of-education/) it gives us an opportunity to rethink governance of the American education system—at all levels. He wants to create a center to study the myriad rules and regulations that impede progress, which would lead to a governor allowing “volunteer schools” to operate within a leaner, meaner regulatory regime. I like it, John! But may I introduce you to a 30-year-old idea called “charter schools”?
    
-   **John** made his name and fortune via tutoring, which is the topic of a [two](https://informedchoice1996.substack.com/p/the-new-era-of-tutoring-q-and-a-with)\-[part](https://informedchoice1996.substack.com/p/the-new-era-of-tutoring-q-and-a-with-e8c) Q & A series between **Paul DiPerna** and **Liz Cohen** on the occasion of the release of her [new book on the subject](https://www.amazon.com/Future-Tutoring-Lessons-District-Initiatives/dp/B0DZGKPL4Q). Liz is clearly bullish on the potential for tutoring done right—in part because of the impact of best-in-class providers like SAGA, but also because we might be “reaching the limits of how much can be achieved in our current classroom model.” I’m not sure about that, but I do agree with Liz (and Paul) that the future of tutoring lies with education savings accounts and the federal tax credit initiative—for better or worse.
    
-   Some point to AI-driven tutoring as having great potential, but others are ready to give up on ed-tech writ large. Count **Emily Cherkin** among them, who [joins](https://ifstudies.org/blog/the-edtech-enmeshment-problem-and-how-to-fix-it) the [chorus](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/16/opinion/laptop-classroom-test-scores.html) of [critics](https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/commentary/next-curtail-chromebooks) whose worries go far beyond phones in schools to the “EdTech enmeshment problem.” As she writes, “today, most teaching tools are digital: grading portals, student information systems, digital curricula, parent communication systems, assessment tools, and more.” This not necessarily a positive development! “It is confusing to advocate for phone bans in school,” **Emily** writes, “but hand children iPads on which to ‘learn.’ It can’t be one and not the other.”
    
-   Finishing out today’s roundup on a similar theme: **Rick Hess** [tackles](https://www.nationalreview.com/magazine/2026/01/ai-is-educations-next-big-test/) the AI-in-education question for National Review. He finds the enthusiasm for AI in schools ironic, given that “at this very moment, we’re scrambling to combat the toxic influence of cellphones in schools and reduce the astronomical amount of time our kids spend on devices.” He’s not calling for a full-on ban, like **[Andy Smarick](https://thedispatch.com/article/artificial-intelligence-education-learning-moratorium/)**, but an understanding that “when it comes to education, AI poses a raft of trade-offs amid a sea of uncertainty.”

In response to my question of [whether blue state ed reform is hopeless](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/is-blue-state-ed-reform-hopeless):

**Conor Williams**

> In your last newsletter, you follow most people in framing the political state of reform along a left-right axis, with “centrists” of each side joining arms in the past to constitute the reform movement. That’s not a wholly false way to analyze the political situation (past or present). Many recent reform figures—guys like you and **Andy Rotherham**, et al.—are absolutely towards the middle compared to others in your respective parties. At some level, a lot of folks in reform have built their entire political orientation in relief, that is, by defining their views in contradistinction to some other political figure they use as an anchor point, e.g. “I disagree with Rick Santorum in a direction that I perceive to be towards the center and away from the John Birch Society,” or “I disagree with Lina Khan,” or “I disagree with \[insert perceived radical here\] in such a direction that I *must* be a ‘moderate.’”
> 
> But that framing is also incomplete—largely for being static. That is, the center’s always a moving target. Read through moderate-framed books like [Geoffrey Kabaservice’s](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/rule-and-ruin-9780199768400) *[Rule and Ruin](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/rule-and-ruin-9780199768400)* and you find that moderate-ish guys like Bob Dole were radicals before political churn and party strategy enveloped them into something like a new center. Last year’s Young Guns are this year’s RINOS. Just ask Eric Cantor. Or Paul Ryan. Or Kevin McCarthy. Or NCLB’s John Boehner! Particularly for those of us who work outside of party politics, a “moderation” that’s always about triangulating in between the (fluid) position of each political party isn’t a concrete position to build upon...it’s contrarianism or milquetoast iconoclasm dressed up as a theory of political progress.
> 
> Again, we don’t need to abandon that framing of reform’s political situation entirely. But I think we’d make more progress if we supplemented it with other options. To wit, 1) it is a fact that the modal charter school teacher at high-performing campuses in big blue cities in big blue states...has political convictions falling somewhere between Elizabeth Warren and Che (see also the modal TFA corps member). 2) It is further true that the core problem reform is trying to solve (as **Liz Cohen** points out [in the piece you linked](https://www.educationnext.org/fairer-framework-for-evaluating-choice-same-demands-voucher-programs-should-apply-all-education-models-traditional-public-schools/)) is about breaking the link between accumulated material capital and access to high-quality educational opportunities. That is, the problem is people of means always buying better public goods via their privileged power in the housing market. 3) It is also true that polls reliably show stronger support for reform priorities from progressive voters of color—who hold a variety of political orientations, not all of which are easily reduced to “moderation.” 4) It is also—and urgently—true that standardized benchmarks for assessing student progress have been a central component of a civil rights agenda for holding public systems accountable to serving marginalized communities better. 5) It is true that inflexible teacher training and credentialing systems produce uncertain and unreliable gains for students—but nearly always reduce teacher diversity.
> 
> Etc. This is a grab bag of examples of the sorts of things the “reform is always the moderates’ project” framing misses. There are voters out there working in and around reform who are demonstrably, verifiably progressive—not moderate. Major reform projects can also be legibly presented in demonstrably progressive terms—the problems with neighborhood schools are the same problems that [the environmental justice movement](https://www.nrdc.org/stories/environmental-justice-movement) has been wrestling.
> 
> My point here, right, is that reform’s path forward with progressives has to be about re-centering the substantive problems reform is actually trying to solve. A reform movement that targets the essential inequity of neighborhood schools and gerrymandered school districts and unfair K–12 funding systems has political legs. The move has to be about simply deciding not to frame the argument—especially the public argument—in reference to other political groups or some mushy and inconstant “moderate” spot. That is, a new progressive reform agenda can’t define itself in terms of political allies perceived to be radicals, whether they’re teachers unions or [members of Congress](https://www.the74million.org/article/williams-on-testing-meritocracy-and-educational-equity-how-exams-forced-alexandria-ocasio-cortezs-school-to-see-beyond-stereotypes/) or whatever. It can advance in directly progressive terms—e.g., recognizing that neighborhood schools are part of a systemically racist and classist system, or insisting that school quality shouldn’t be left to trust and needs verification by academic assessments.
> 
> I’m not suggesting that this will be a simple project, or that the only thing blue state reform needs is a message refresh. Some progressive groups will oppose some-to-most of any updated agenda. But that is/was also true of efforts to reframe housing development into a YIMBY v. NIMBY frame from its old “wealthy developers” v. “environmental protection and ‘preserve’ our community” frame. Politics is hard, and we shouldn’t require all new approaches to a policy problem to bat 1.000. And finally, it’s worth noting that the history of reform belies efforts to redefine it as a moderates’ project! NCLB’s progressive leaders, as we all know, were Ted Kennedy and George Miller, guys whose careers get filed in the “Liberal Lions” section of the political library. Why not give that—a full-throated progressive account of reform—another try?

**Heather Peske**

> Weighing in from Massachusetts... Two weeks ago, the Massachusetts House unanimously passed “right to read” legislation (155-0) after several years of working to gain a foothold. The Senate has yet to vote the companion bill out of the ed committee and to the full Senate; we are optimistic. When I testified (along with ~25 other people in 5 hours of testimony) in September, the major opponent to the legislation was the Mass Teachers Association. Their arguments rested on teacher autonomy (leave them alone and let them teach!), unfunded mandate, and downright inaccurate and misleading information on the reading research from someone they claimed is a psychometrician. The AFT-MA recently weighed in to oppose the legislation (despite the Shankar Institute’s great piece on the strength of IL and MA’s proposed bills. When I asked if the local AFT-MA has read their own union’s research, that question was not well-received). It has been frustrating...and YET, there is a powerful [Mass Reads Literacy Coalition](https://www.massreads.org/), led by (mostly) centrist-leaning democratic education leaders (almost all of whom are women, I will note. Shout out to **Mary Tamer**, formerly DFER-MA, who leads Mass Potential). We’re optimistic, though this fight has been too hard and too long (NCTQ just estimated that if we persist on our current trajectory, MA will collide with Mississippi in 2028 on our 4th grade NAEP results—Number One, for some, and falling fast).
> 
> Additionally, we have suffered two disappointing losses in recent years: the removal of the requirement for students to have to pass the 10th grade state assessment (MCAS) and the slap-down on expansion of charters. Both of these things happened through ballot initiatives (and we would have likely lost a similar ballot measure aimed at preventing us from using the common core standards, but for the fact that the language of the ballot measure was deemed by the courts to be illegal). Ballot initiatives: In my estimation, the worst policy mechanism ever (why elect representatives if we are going to send policy decisions to the whole general electorate?). And it was the unions that paid millions of dollars to campaign to get the measures passed (or denied) to align with their positions. Argh.

> HOWEVER, as a former state ed department leader, we got a lot done in MA through regulation and by being smart about how we used our authority at the SEA (e.g., curriculum and professional learning reform in math, science, and reading; regulating charters so they are strong and many hugely outperform their district counterparts; educator licensure reform; revising standards and accountability for ed prep programs; ed eval that IS better than it used to be, even if not the major reform we had hoped; adult learning; innovation in teacher compensation in the state “takeover” districts, support for diversifying the teaching profession, standing up a teacher apprenticeship model (results not out yet), and supporting teacher residency programs). Much can be done if you have capacity at the SEA and courageous leaders (who are supported by the governor, who, at the time I was there, was Charlie Baker—a Republican not beholden to the teachers unions).
