Happy Giving Tuesday, education peeps. I hope everyone had a wonderful holiday weekend.

Today I turn over SCHOOLED to Ben Austin, who makes the case for Democrats to embrace education reform again, both because it’s good for kids but also because it’s good for winning elections. Then I round up the edu-opinions from the past week, including those from Crystal McQueen-Taylor, Dan Buck, Anna Low, Robert Pondiscio, Ashley Berner, Rick Hess, Marc Porter Magee, Sharif El-Mekki, Heather Kirkpatrick, Lindsey Henderson, and Dave Kung. And Christine Toribio Pitts offers her perspective on the blue-state debate from her perch in Oregon.

It probably feels like an eternity, but two weeks ago I [teed up a debate](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/is-blue-state-ed-reform-hopeless) about whether blue state education reform is hopeless. Last week, [several folks argued that it’s not](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/blue-state-ed-reform-is-different), for five reasons, including the teachers unions’ openness to some important reforms and the feasibility of making change via regulatory agencies.

Now **Ben Austin** adds his voice to the conversation, arguing that Presidents Clinton and Obama proved that education reform could be a winning issue for Democrats. I agree and would add that those presidents made it easier for Democrats at the state and local levels to embrace ed reform, too, and harder for the teachers unions to oppose it whole hog. I see no reason why that couldn’t be the case again—if an ed reformer won the presidency in 2028. As Dale Chu [wrote](https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/commentary/ed-28-playbook-democratic-education-reformers) a few months ago, maybe the country needs an Ed in ’28 campaign!

Those of us reformers on the right should remember that presidential leadership—and an aggressive federal role—has been a force for good in the past and could be again. Maybe red states don’t need the political cover from Washington, but blue states certainly do. Something to remember as the dismembering of the Department of Education continues apace, and as we wonks respond to [Treasury’s plans for regulating the new federal tax credit scholarship program](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/treasury-irs-request-comments-on-implementation-of-the-new-federal-tax-credit-for-individual-contributions-to-scholarship-granting-organizations-under-the-one-big-beautiful-bill) (released last week).

Without further ado, take it away, **Ben**!

**Ben Austin:**

> Thomas B. Fordham Institute President Mike Petrilli recently posed an [existential question](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/is-blue-state-ed-reform-hopeless) for education advocacy that needed to be asked out loud: Is blue state education reform dead?
> 
> I agree that it’s currently on a trajectory to drive off a political cliff. We are long past the Roaring ‘20s of blue state education reform—when progressive advocates across the country were experimenting with novel ways to reimagine public education for the children of today and tomorrow. Back then, I was organizing parents to transform their failing neighborhood schools through California’s landmark [parent trigger law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parent_trigger) and challenging the constitutionality of low-quality public education with the *[Vergara v. California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vergara_v._California)* civil rights litigation.
> 
> That era was not a coincidence. It was the result of nearly a quarter century of bipartisan support for education reform, spanning the presidencies of Bill Clinton, George W. Bush, and Barack Obama.
> 
> That bipartisan consensus created the ability to forge unusually powerful coalitions—which is especially critical in education politics because kids can’t vote and parents don’t have lobbyists. California’s parent trigger law passed in December 2009 with unanimous Republican support and just enough Democrats of color willing to defy teachers unions on behalf of the parents and children they represent to pass by one vote in both legislative bodies.
> 
> That kind of bipartisan consensus sounds like a fairy tale in today’s morass of tribal politics.
> 
> I don’t think blue state education reform is dead, but it’s on life-support. As long as Democrats continue to view it as fundamentally oppositional to their narrow political interests because teachers unions oppose it, blue state education reform will continue to die on Petrilli’s vine. No amount of pleas to “do the right thing for kids” will change that dynamic—even though the end of the Obama era of federal accountability and leadership on education directly coincided with a [dramatic decline](https://www.the74million.org/article/interactive-see-how-student-achievement-gaps-are-growing-in-your-state/#:~:text=NCLB's%20accountability%20pressures%20had%20been,and%20all%20across%20the%20country.) in American student achievement.
> 
> The path back to relevance does not go through a “status quo lite” rebrand, where many advocates in blue states have even abandoned the term “education reform” because it implies the system needs change, inherently offending teachers unions and district bureaucrats.
> 
> Instead, the path forward is all about winning.
> 
> My old boss Bill Clinton, as well as Barack Obama, both won in part because they had the courage to challenge party orthodoxy on behalf of the American people. [Kamala Harris](https://thehill.com/people/kamala-harris/)—along with every other nominee since [Walter Mondale](https://thehill.com/people/walter-mondale/) who lost in the general election—ran effectively as an avatar of party interests and ceded education policymaking to teachers unions.
> 
> One reason Harris lost is because she and Biden lit the traditionally strong Democratic education brand on fire. My daughters didn’t go to school for a year and a half because of blue state pandemic school closure policies. The whole country and an entire generation of young American adults are now paying the price for Democratic political cowardice.
> 
> The Orwellian response from our local teachers union at the time was: “[There is no such thing as learning loss](https://lamag.com/education/cecily-myart-cruz-teachers-union/)”—which went virtually unchallenged by the Democrats supposedly in charge of our children’s education. *Nothing to see here folks!*
> 
> Trump’s new opt-in voucher program [forces this issue into stark relief](https://thehill.com/opinion/education/5403289-education-choice-expansion-politics/). Voters now face a clear color-coded national split-screen. In red states, you get free money for the school of your choice. In blue states, you get what you get, and you don’t get upset.
> 
> If Democrats are serious about winning back the White House and saving democracy, they must learn lessons from the only two-term Democratic presidents since FDR and elevate the interests of parents, children, and citizens above the interests of teachers unions and other narrow party interests.
> 
> To meet this moment, Democrats must offer solutions that solve problems for parents and children—regardless of special interest politics. To begin, they’ve got to end the untenable hypocrisy of supporting choice and freedom for everything, except schools. And it’s long past time for Democrats to translate “high-quality public schools” from a soundbite into a [civil right](https://edcivilrightsnow.org/) for every child in America.
> 
> To paraphrase [Mark Twain](https://www.dictionary.com/browse/the-reports-of-my-death-are-greatly-exaggerated), the death of progressive education advocacy is greatly exaggerated—and is far from preordained because of a unique and synergistic political symbiosis. Democratic presidents need the political cover of blue state reformers to challenge party interests on behalf of the public interest. And blue state reformers need the political cover of Democratic presidents to challenge special interests on behalf of parents and children.
> 
> If funders and advocates make the affirmative choice to abandon blue state education reform, they will be abandoning more than “just” half the parents and children in America. They will also be undermining the Democratic Party’s path back to power. This is a moment that demands courage—from reformers, funders, and Democrats who claim to represent families. The time is now for blue state education reformers to find the courage of their convictions to fight for parents, children, and the future of American democracy.

-   As I mentioned up above, over the holiday weekend the **Treasury Department** released a [request for comments](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/treasury-irs-request-comments-on-implementation-of-the-new-federal-tax-credit-for-individual-contributions-to-scholarship-granting-organizations-under-the-one-big-beautiful-bill) on what it calls “implementation of the new federal tax credit for individual contributions to Scholarship Granting Organizations \[SGOs\] under the One, Big, Beautiful Bill.” To my eyes, Treasury does not appear open to allowing governors to add their own rules that SGOs must follow, though it would enable the SGOs themselves to choose to limit scholarships to, for example, only low-income families. In a bit of good (or bad?) timing, **Dan Buck** and **Anna Low** [published their take](https://www.aei.org/research-products/report/making-the-federal-scholarship-tax-credit-a-success) yesterday on what the Treasury regulations should entail (and why), skewing to a less-is-more approach. “There’s a colorable case to be made for allowing states to place purely logistic, value-neutral regulations on SGOs,” **Dan** and **Anna** write, “prioritizing limited funds to low-income families or setting accountability for outcomes, for example, but that sets a precedent for state regulation that welcomes further public meddling.” I [disagree](https://www.wsj.com/opinion/democrats-school-choice-dilemma-budget-bill-party-division-teachers-union-5e1a08a4?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=AWEtsqdhi33M9AyROoZ5ELVBF7gKakA9KjWoE_I3tv7ks00T_mhpblDH8KVapO1c_iE%3D&gaa_ts=692dbb11&gaa_sig=lUIliIetNSriUEPERR29FGbV_Cy_2O_MdeOPRb-JtDad10rX-5aFOWAgfXr3euoq9CcRQ1VCFtPm0S8-p7vgdw%3D%3D), but it looks like **Dan** and **Anna**’s team is going to win this one.
    
-   Meanwhile, **Dan** and **Anna**’s AEI colleague **Robert Pondiscio** [argued](https://thenext30years.substack.com/p/the-most-important-civics-lesson) last week that schools shouldn’t teach kids that “the world is against them.” It’s not that teachers need to lecture children on how good they have it—teenagers will roll their eyes at any sermon coming from authority figures anyway—but schools need to avoid a curricular approach that is all doom and gloom. Even if educators want to inspire their students to work to make the world a better place, **Robert** writes, an overly pessimistic message doesn’t inspire, it demoralizes. The “bleak narrative doesn’t produce activism. It produces lassitude, even despair. It’s not preparing young people to enter civic life; it’s nudging them toward exhaustion, withdrawal, and retreat.” Try optimism, attachment, and patriotism instead, **Robert** says.
    
-   Let’s make it a hat trick from our friends at AEI with **Rick Hess**’s [take on the takes](https://www.educationnext.org/sorting-through-reactions-to-the-department-of-eds-big-breakup-trump-administration-return-education-to-states/) about the breakup of the Department of Education. Among other judgments, he rules [my “nothing burger” depiction](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/the-dismembering-of-the-department) an underreaction. “The longer the new arrangements persist,” **Rick** writes, “the harder they’ll be to unwind. An old rule of government is that it’s hard to reverse new arrangements once they’ve become familiar. That usually trips up Republicans seeking to dismantle new programs; in this instance, it could work the other way. A Democratic administration might decide the payoff just isn’t worth all the time, hassle, and energy. We shall see.” I certainly agree that if the arrangements persist into the 2030s—because the GOP wins the presidency again in 2028—they could be hard to undo.
    
-   Actually, make that four from AEI, thanks to this [back-and-forth](https://www.edweek.org/policy-politics/opinion-should-states-mandate-student-testing-for-choice-programs/2025/11) between **Rick** and **Ashley Berner** about “assessment choice.” As in: Should the growing school choice and education savings accounts programs require that participating students take tests, and if so, which ones? They both come down on the side of public accountability for these taxpayer-funded efforts. Cue **Rick**: “When families use taxpayer funds to attend a school, taxpayers have a legitimate interest in how those dollars are spent. At a minimum, this entails policing waste and fraud. It also means asking whether public funds are delivering meaningful educational benefits.” Woot woot! But that doesn’t mean policymakers should force kids in these private-school and ESA programs take the state tests. **Ashley**: “I would love for all choice participants—not necessarily the schools serving them—to take a nationally normed assessment and report the results to the state. The state could then publish aggregate scores when a particular program or school network hits a predetermined threshold of participants.” That’s where I’m at, too. But **Ashley** would go even further, allowing districts (and charter schools?) to opt into curriculum-aligned assessments—even if they weren’t implemented statewide. [That’s not where](https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/commentary/welcome-lake-wobegon-oklahoma) [I’m at!](https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/resources/should-all-state-funded-schools-take-state-standardized-tests-episode-982)
    
-   **Marc Porter Magee** has not one but two high school seniors in his house, so he knows as well as anyone that it’s college application season. (One kid going through the gauntlet is plenty, says I!) He uses the occasion to [dig into the depressing evidence](https://50can.org/the-new-reality-roundup/week-298/) that test-optional policies are making elite universities less welcoming to low-income children, turning the equity argument on its head. “The elite schools that stand apart from the crowd in not having a bias towards wealthy families—like Carnegie Mellon and MIT,” **Marc** writes, “are exactly the ones that put more of a focus on standardized testing in admissions.”
    
-   Speaking of test scores, enrolling middle school students who score at top levels into advanced math courses is gaining ground nationwide, and now **Lindsey Henderson** and **Dave Kung** [make a strong case for it](https://www.the74million.org/article/when-every-student-is-guaranteed-a-chance-more-reach-advanced-math/), or what they call “guaranteed access to advanced math.” Notably, this approach is more equitable than universalist policies that place *all* students in Algebra I by the eighth grade, since not everyone is ready to succeed with such challenging content by then. “Only Nevada, North Carolina, Texas, and Washington now have statewide guaranteed access policies,” **Lindsey** and **Dave** report, with Indiana and Virginia joining during the 2025–26 school year.” Come on, laggards, get with the program!
    
-   Another key policy is reforming—and funding—teacher preparation programs. So [argues](https://hechingerreport.org/opinion-funding-high-quality-teacher-preparation-programs-should-be-the-highest-priority-for-policymakers/) **Sharif El-Mekki** and **Heather Kirkpatrick**, pointing to recent wins in California and Pennsylvania, along with the promising Teaching Academy model. “Funding high-quality teacher preparation should be as nonnegotiable as funding other vital public service professions,” **Sharif** and **Heather** write.
    
-   As Zohran Mamdani prepares to take office, **Crystal McQueen-Taylor** [offers a strong defense](https://www.the74million.org/article/mayor-elect-mamdanis-first-test-keeping-our-schools-accountable) of mayoral control in NYC. “Real democracy in a city of 9 million people and a school system serving 1 million students isn’t about diffusing power: It’s about making responsibility clear,” **Crystal** writes, pointing to the educational chaos in the Big Apple in the days of elected school boards. “No one was clearly responsible, so everyone passed the buck. Student performance stagnated, and inequities deepened. Children in wealthier neighborhoods got attention, while kids in lower-income communities were left behind.” Yes, yes, yes, and yes.

**Christine Toribio Pitts**

> I have to be determined to keep ed reform alive in Oregon because I cannot sit by and watch our leaders center systems over children over and over again.
> 
> I was recently asked to sit for a [hotseat interview](https://www.wweek.com/news/state/2025/11/13/christine-pitts-has-spotted-one-key-reason-oregon-kids-arent-learning-to-read/) and found myself unpacking Oregon’s history of entrepreneurship and our slow slide back toward protecting the status quo of K–12 schooling. We *do* have a governor’s race coming up. We have a strong secretary of state who has been willing to hold our social services to account. What we are missing is an advocacy infrastructure backed by business and community, willing to take on the wall of labor and special interest groups. We have to be comfortable with adults feeling uncomfortable ([look how that played out when Marguerite did her presentation on ROI](https://www.wweek.com/news/schools/2025/02/05/dramatic-increases-in-school-spending-have-not-improved-outcomes-for-oregon-students/)).
> 
> I [agree with Marguerite](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/blue-state-ed-reform-is-different) that we are seeing some Dems frustrated by the lack of progress on student outcomes under progressive/socialist leadership. The reality is that, in Oregon, we will not move until our politicians and senior leaders are willing to run and lead, as if they will never run or lead again. Their agendas cannot be driven by diplomacy because of the relationships that they want to protect. Their agendas cannot be set with the goal of winning a future campaign or achieving a new notch on their leadership belt. Their goal *must* be to achieve broad, straightforward goals in teaching, learning, and belonging—urgently, now, with immediate behavioral changes.
> 
> It is OK for blue-state ed reform to be different. I do not think that progressives in Oregon are even willing to think about reform. In Oregon I’ve witnessed my community rehashing debates that other states had 20 years ago: fully fund schools, my neighborhood school is the only option (period, I don’t care about your kid or their needs), the kids are fine (right?) because my kids are fine. I am not hopeful, but (like Heather said) I am willing to do the hard work.
