On Tuesday, I asked how the feds should respond to Indiana’s waiver request under the Every Student Succeeds Act, and today we get answers from Nicholas Munyan-Penney, Brenda Dickhoner, Joshua Kumler, David Nitkin, and my new Fordham Institute colleague Brian Fitzpatrick. Then I round up the week’s best takes on ed reform, including Jonathan Chait and Vlad Kogan pushing back on pessimistic progressives, plus posts by Checker Finn, Nina Rees, Matt Barnum, Liz Cohen, Robert Pondiscio, Danyela Souza Egorov, Chad Aldeman, Robert VerBruggen, and John Kristof. And we say goodbye to a great American, Secretary of Education Rod Paige.

As I [explained earlier this week](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/how-should-the-feds-respond-to-indianas), Indiana was the first (and so far, only) state to ask Secretary of Education **Linda McMahon** to waive some of ESSA’s accountability requirements. I asked y’all how **McMahon** and her colleagues should respond, understanding that a firm no was likely out of the question.

Several folks weighed in to explain why the question marks about Indiana’s still-under-development system are so problematic. Let’s start with **Nicholas Munyan-Penney**, who hammers home the concerns he and I discussed on Tuesday regarding how subgroups would factor into Indiana’s new system, as well as its squishy targets for high school students.

> Saying no to everything certainly *is* an option—consider that the Department publicly pushed back against Oklahoma’s request and seemingly had an influence in Iowa substantially scaling back its original request. While certainly unlikely in the case of Indiana, **McMahon** *could* be willing to put some guardrails in place related to Indiana’s accountability system, particularly if pressured to do so by conservative voices with sway in Trump World.
> 
> There are two main guardrails that I’d recommend. First, the department must insist on clear information about how IDOE will identify schools for improvement, including based on student group performance. While we’ve heard anecdotally that identification will continue as normal, there’s nothing about this in the waiver proposal. This is essential to ensure schools can get access to resources and supports. Second, the department should require that scores for each indicator include the performance of all students, rather than the “choose your own adventure” menu approach Indiana is proposing (see [Tuesday’s post](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/how-should-the-feds-respond-to-indianas) for my concerns about this).
> 
> Alternatively, ED should be clear in any approval about which specific provisions Indiana needs waived based on how the new system is incompatible with ESSA requirements—and limit the flexibilities to these specific provisions. And regardless of what flexibility is approved, ED should require clear public reporting on the data being used to calculate new school ratings, so advocates and community members can see where schools may be leveraging the new system to hold some students to lower expectations.

**David Nitkin** has some similar worries, even though he’s enthusiastic about developing non-traditional measures at the high school level.

> I wanted to share one thought on the Indiana waiver: the big gap between their stated (and admirable!) aspiration for measuring communication and collaboration and what’s actually currently technically possible.
> 
> All of Indiana’s messaging about the waiver foregrounds their intention to include communication and collaboration (e.g., “[Indiana’s proposed accountability model elevates academic outcomes and skill development, including communication, collaboration, and work ethic](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1H8_cFVZoRntQsOfsa3IX9GkCObej8sq5/view).” “Collaboration & Communication” is one of the five key characteristics in their [proposed GPS dashboard](https://www.in.gov/doe/files/Indianas-Future-Accountability-Model_Overview-1.pdf), etc.). But in all their materials, they only have a single slide ([slide 8](https://www.in.gov/sboe/files/10.15.25-October-SBOE_Accountability-1.pdf)) with any information about *how* they will do this, and of the three sentences on that very spare slide, two are questions. They cite their partnership with Carnegie and ETS to pilot new measurement approaches, but ETS will be the first to tell you that those tools are still in the very early stages of development and validation.
> 
> If Indiana and other states are serious about including skills like collaboration and communication in their accountability frameworks, then they need to make a commitment to R & D to pilot, validate, and prove out new measurement approaches. Technical validation of new measures is nontrivial, and the field has not solved even basic comparability issues ([more on this here](https://www.nciea.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/Assessing21stCenturyCompetencies-Report-FINAL.pdf) from the irreplaceable Center for Assessment). New advances in AI offer really exciting possibilities for tools that might soon exist, but there’s a big difference between “might soon exist” and “are currently ready for prime time.”
> 
> I say all of the above not to be a naysayer, but instead to commend the Indiana team for their ambition and urge them to be equally ambitious in their investment in R & D to make their vision possible. The ALI Coalition, Education Reimagined, and Transcend recently published a state R & D [brief](https://www.alicoalition.org/statebrief/) and [playbook](https://www.alicoalition.org/playbook/) with some ideas for how states like Indiana can get started.

Another place Indiana could look to is Texas, says **Joshua Kumler**.

> I will leave the question of what the Secretary of Education should do to the experts in federal education policy, but I did want to provide you and your readers with an example of how my home state is thinking about “gain\[ing\] a more complete picture of student success” within existing federal law rather than outside it.
> 
> In Texas, grades for high schools and school systems include a metric for how many graduates are considered “[college, career and military ready](https://email.mg-d0.substack.com/c/eJxM0M2K8yAUxvGribuIHqNNFi666W28-HFspEkUPek7ufthKAyzffjBA__gCJ-lXfbs2MaGdbtYtEa5pCNDK29Gq3m6ScVWO_kU05K0WmbldNQ-BK-WGQKqYOS0sGxBgJYghTRqVorPCbz26JNGAHDLMIn9OUbB--k7ufDioexssytR7YO6D_AY4EHoJvcOrlMrnNDxTo6Q0xc_-wCP-_iDYtldPsZy4OjHEPbGa0yslk7_crRylsIYA-Kz0FXRHvi_b0iEjTXbw1rKhnGYRCotrm7PR6dMJyEv7cn66T8Pv9Jfe35hRWp52zKjv8XeFr4DAAD__8Bvbdg).” This definition currently contains, to borrow **Nicholas Munyan-Penney**’s words, a “menu” with which “schools can get the same amount of credit for indicators of wildly different levels of rigor.”
> 
> I’d argue our “menu” is still stronger than Indiana’s in that nearly all options are tied to credentials with direct relevance to higher ed and/or the workforce (industry-based certifications, level I & II certificates, dual credit, etc.), and Indiana’s, in my admittedly limited understanding, appear to be [tied mostly to high school electives](https://email.mg-d0.substack.com/c/eJxMkEGurCAURFcDMwxc0LYHDP7EbfyAXJU0CoFrG3f_0unk5U1qUKlUTs7sCNdcb3s2rKJiSTcPdtBu6QNHqx5Dr0fzUJpv1j2x1zqYp1Zg5h4Wb_wyGBxNeEijAo8WJPQKlFSDHrXuxgV879EvPQKAezIj91UE2bXTN3Lzq5vzzpPdiEpj-h-DicF0XVc3by69PDrqcl0ZTPEI0R2OwQQSDINJwScUg-nAS4RYUt5dE0uuYovrJtq85ZxEozPgQU24Ump-Y2Aw8ZIb_Y_BqlHJYRhAfhu6C9oDr5aQCCuv9nuCgRm55Bo2t8ejUaST8MPF2-lD3l08fpf-3uMLC1KNKUVOf72-LfwEAAD__wmyeZQ). (Also you, uh, actually have to *pass* the AP exam to get college readiness credit for it.)
> 
> But we’re not resting on our laurels here. The fact remains that, to use one particularly egregious example, earning an associate degree in high school takes a great deal more effort on the part of the student, and resources on the part of the district, than merely earning three hours of dual credit, and our accountability ratings should reflect this.
> 
> That’s why, as part of the [A-F Accountability Refresh](https://email.mg-d0.substack.com/c/eJxkkE2OqzAQhE-DdyD_ACELL94m13hq2w1YsTFyN5lw-9Ek0mikLPvrUqmqPDAupZ72IKxtxT2dItjRwDwEgVZdxsFM_UUZsVqcXK_MDGFUDuV0GdwVvHGTVGrwCFpEq6UelFZSjWYypptm7QaHbh5Qaw3Xppd5aYPs6HDE4O-dL1kkuzLv1Jh_jb41-sYIHeMTqFvK43U_gVryaymJGn0D78uxMbiYIp8vAAFz9O3HZ8c6l5ph8_hTrlSO2_Jh0dJJjLkN-MBU9owbi70Q_4_BqknJcRy1fBM-d7QbflFCZqyi2ncuDE0v51LDCjluxJEPxq7URdDhQskQt1-lO3O8445cY0pR8N_tH1Z_BwAA__8kEYvn) currently underway, my organization is strongly supportive of a proposal to “tier” these CCMR outcomes based on their relationship to long-term success in higher education and/or the workforce. Our mutual friends at Texas 2036 are currently conducting research to arrive at a data-driven framework for these metrics, but you can see an early suggestion of what this could look like on [slide 11 here](https://email.mg-d0.substack.com/c/eJxkkM2O3SAMhZ8m7BKBye-CRTf3NSoDJkETQgTOdPL2VeZKVaVZns-W9fk4ZFpzuc1VqbSFzv0W3owaw-AFGTWNg577SWmxGafHSUrb-2Fa5iUsU4B-AanADYDUSxENSBgUKKlGPWvdzQHsYMmGgQAAl6aXaW297OplK6P76FxOYjcb81kb_auBVwMvJuyYvrB2a_78zl9Y2-q2nPfawAudy9fBaOMe-f4G6ClF1_6YnFRCLgkPR89zuXA81uck4tomoie2Ce_2Ee9OH8SZK_-O3qhZyXEcQb4J3yeZg_7UnZipiGLePuSbXoZc_IYpHpUjX0xdLquol_U5YTz-bdo7xQ86iUvc9yj4_84_DfwNAAD__26IheQ).
> 
> The TLDR: To use the words from Indiana’s waiver, “unique experiences like work-based learning... matter to a student’s future success,” but not all unique experiences are equal in terms of how much they ultimately benefit students, and states should be willing to take a hard look at the efficacy of these experiences in terms of higher education persistence, job attainment, and wage earning ability if they’re going to incentivize them through A–F accountability.

My new colleague (and a Hoosier himself) **Brian Fitzpatrick** argues that accountability based on inflatable credentials is worse than no accountability at all.

> Indiana just proposed to hold schools accountable based on easily inflated student GPA, ill-defined “soft” skills accumulation, and accumulation of credentials that are often industry-recognized only on paper. In truth, this would be just one more mechanism with a moral hazard built in. The lesson we must learn from this era of grade and credential inflation is to stop trying to hold schools accountable using metrics over which schools can exercise control. If we don’t, [Goodhart’s Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_law) will prove to be just as inevitable as Newton’s Laws.
> 
> Ideally states would hold schools accountable based on metrics outside of schools’ control, hence the value of standardized testing. If there is not enough political will to enforce standards that cannot be gamed, then we shouldn’t bother to rely on centralized accountability systems at all. The in-between option will set the worst sort of precedent for all states who follow Indiana’s lead in applying for changes to their accountability system.

Still, for all the concerns, one education reformer wrote in to support Indiana—**Brenda Dickhoner**:

> I do hope that the waiver is successful at least in part, and that Indiana can lead the way on showing how waivers are not necessarily a bad thing and can be used for good. One can be both an accountability “hawk” and also be open to reducing red-tape and senseless compliance activities to free up space for states to innovate. I’m excited by both Indiana’s education innovation grant proposal and the unified state/federal accountability system. As Indiana noted in their memo about federal school improvement funding: “Across all grades and models, no significant impact was found \[from billions of dollars of SIG\] on student math or reading scores, high school graduation rates, or college enrollment.” 🤔 Maybe we should try something different?
> 
> My understanding of their waiver is that they will still identify comprehensive support and targeted support schools but are seeking flexibility in how the students from those schools are supported. I especially love their student-centered focus: “Removing rigid eligibility requirements allows Indiana to invest in supports and opportunities that follow the student, not just the school. This could mean offering services at the high school level for students who attended an underperforming middle school, or engaging a neighboring LEA or public charter school to serve students zoned to attend an underperforming school when the local district is unable or unwilling to do so.” (see page 15 of the request [cover letter](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1ajZHlHhhJQzGVNlz1GdM8Nx3hd2uTm5V/view)). To that I say heck yes! We need fresh thinking on school turnaround and ways to break down residential-based attendance zones. I’m excited by Indiana’s proposal and hoping Colorado can follow their lead!

So what does that all imply for what Trump officials should do with Indiana’s waiver request? Like **Brenda**, I believe it’s time to try some new approaches, especially given the disappointing results of the past ten years under ESSA. But I agree with **Nicholas**, **Joshua**, **David**, and **Brian** that there are some specifics in Indiana’s plan that should be nailed down, improved, or dropped before the feds say yes. In the meantime, the Trump team might want to encourage some other states—those whose accountability systems have been around a while—to go first.

-   We lost a great American hero this week with the passing of former **Secretary of Education Rod Paige** at age 92. Here are **[Checker Finn](https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/commentary/remembering-rod-paige-great-american)** and **[Nina Rees](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7404276063037460481/)** with lovely tributes. (**Nina’s** has some great photos, too, reminding us that we were young once!) Personally, I was very saddened to lose **Secretary Paige**—for whom I worked at the Department of Education, and then for the 18 years he served as a trustee at the Fordham Institute. I was also saddened by his New York Times obituary, which went out of its way to recount various scandals (tame by today’s standards) but failed to note that his landmark achievement, No Child Left Behind, was associated with significant improvements in student achievement and other outcomes. Thank you for everything you did for America’s children, **Secretary Paige**!
    
-   Speaking of being sad, **Chad Aldeman** is [glum](https://www.chadaldeman.com/p/a-weak-federal-education-law-turns) about NCLB’s replacement, the Every Student Succeeds Act, which turns ten this month, and which has been associated with a *decline* in student achievement and other outcomes.
    
-   One of the themes of SCHOOLED since its launch in September is the [growing](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/10/09/opinion/democrats-education-failure.html) [number](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/2025/11/23/high-school-students-college-preparation/) [of](https://www.slowboring.com/p/american-students-are-getting-dumber) [elite](https://nymag.com/intelligencer/article/public-education-failure-american-test-scores-trump-pandemic-liberals.html) [opinion](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/archive/2025/10/education-decline-low-expectations/684526/) [writers](https://www.theargumentmag.com/p/illiteracy-is-a-policy-choice) who are awakening to America’s educational malaise and urging policymakers to do something about it. (Compared to the past ten years, when education reform has been in the political wilderness.) Now it’s **Jonathan Chait**’s turn, [taking the left to task](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/2025/12/school-reform-progressives/685179/) for giving up on narrowing the achievement gap. He recounts the recent dust-up over the Mississippi Miracle, as well as other examples of the extreme pessimism of progressives (including **Diane Ravitch**, **Richard Rothstein**, and **Freddie deBoer**) about whether schools can make much of a difference. “Though it is certainly true that some apparent success stories have involved statistical meddling or outright cheating,” **Jonathan** writes, “these cases don’t prove the impossibility of improving schools any more than a list of corporate fraudsters would prove the impossibility of running a profitable business.”
    
-   Speaking of taking on progressive pessimists, **Vlad Kogan** [spends 2,500 words](https://www.educationnext.org/debunking-deboers-education-reform-pessimism-critics-absolutist-appraisal-ignores-clear-evidence-progress/) pushing back on **Freddie**’s claims that “nothing works” in education. Among **Vlad**’s important and commonsensical points:
    
    -   Genetics are not academic destiny.
        
    -   Upward mobility is possible.
        
    -   Test scores may fade but benefits remain.
        
    
    “Real progress in closing achievement gaps is possible,” **Vlad** writes. “Realizing it requires difficult, incremental, and often politically unpopular efforts that must be sustained for many years.”
    
-   And if you’d like more high-quality debunking of the Mississippi Miracle debunkers, check out **[Robert VerBruggen](https://cityjournal.substack.com/p/mississippis-hard-to-debunk-reading)**[’s contribution](https://cityjournal.substack.com/p/mississippis-hard-to-debunk-reading). “I’m still skeptical of the ‘miracle,’” **Robert** writes, “and I’d still like to see a very thorough investigation from a researcher who has full access to individual-level student data (and not just the broad summary statistics that get released publicly). But now I’m even more skeptical that retention is anywhere near a full explanation for what’s going on.”
    
-   All of this debunking-debunking is having an impact! Columbia stats professor **Andrew Gelman**, whose post questioning the Mississippi Miracle went viral, [issued a retraction](https://statmodeling.stat.columbia.edu/2025/12/09/more-on-school-reform-this-time-new-orleans/) this week. “Selection has to be explaining some of what is happening in Mississippi—if you hold kids back and give them the test later or manage to exclude them from the tested population entirely, the average scores of the remaining students should rise—but it’s hard to say how much, and at some point you have to go with the data in front of you.” Um, yeah! He also prints a response from **Doug Harris** about a related claim that progress in New Orleans was entirely due to poor families moving away after Katrina. “In the end, to my own surprise,” **Doug** explained, “it became clear that the reforms really did drive substantial improvement in a wide range of education outcomes—elementary/middle school test scores, high school graduation, college entrance exams, college attendance, and college graduation.” The conventional wisdom is that we’re in a “post-truth” moment, but I don’t buy such nihilism. Setting the record straight matters. Good work everyone!
    
-   Meanwhile, **Matt Barnum** [pushes back](https://cbnewsletters.chalkbeat.org/p/what-the-internet-backlash-over-remedial-math-at-uc-san-diego-misses) on the conventional wisdom on the UC San Diego remedial education report, arguing (with the support of some convincing studies) that it’s still better for marginal students to be admitted to elite schools than not. That may be true for those students, but what about their peers? And what about the downstream effects on the K–12 system when the message is that even kids with weak math skills can get into top-tier colleges?
    
-   Put **Andy Kessler** on [Team Optimism](https://www.aei.org/education/ai-has-come-for-k-12-education/) when it comes to AI. [Maybe even Team Utopian](https://www.wsj.com/opinion/is-ai-making-us-dumb-21ea8e39). “AI is a productivity powerhouse,” he argues. “Rather than shielding youth from the future, make education as fun and addictive as technology, social media, and AI.” Hmm. Good luck with that!
    
-   Back in the real world, **Liz Cohen** [is worried](https://www.the74million.org/article/beware-the-smart-use-divide-in-ai/) about more practical issues, especially a potential smart-use divide around AI in the classroom. There are already signs that it’s happening. “Affluent students are using tools to augment learning while lower-income peers are using tools to learn the material in the first place,” **Liz** argues. She’s right to worry, but I’m not convinced that we know what effective AI use looks like in the first place.
    
-   Some wonks, including **Tom Loveless**, are skeptical that American teachers now use “their classrooms performatively as platforms for their politics or anxieties,” as **Robert Pondiscio** has argued, so **Robert** [promises to bring the receipts](https://thenext30years.substack.com/p/how-trauma-informed-teaching-rewired), going deep into the move toward trauma-informed teaching. “Within one professional generation,” writes **Robert**, “American education has adopted a working assumption that most students are carrying invisible wounds that explain their worst behaviors—a shift so sweeping and so entrenched that it amounts to a sea change in how the field views and interacts with children.” Maybe. **Robert** is right that lots of states have adopted policies on trauma-informed teaching, including mandates that educators be trained on the idea. But as **Robert** himself has long argued, there’s a lot of distance between the state and the classroom. Let’s at least see some survey data indicating that American teachers buy into these dubious notions before declaring it a crisis.
    
-   “Mamdani Should Collaborate With Charter Schools.” So [argues](https://www.city-journal.org/article/zohran-mamdani-new-york-city-charter-schools) **Danyela Souza Egorov**. In related news, I am hoping Santa Claus will bring me a top-of-the-line Ford Bronco for Christmas, but that’s not going to happen either.
    
-   Finally, **John Kristof** [makes a strong case](https://informedchoice1996.substack.com/p/choice-programs-should-differentiate) for differentiating funding levels in education savings account programs. Kids with special needs require more money to educate; so do students growing up in poverty. Hear, hear!
