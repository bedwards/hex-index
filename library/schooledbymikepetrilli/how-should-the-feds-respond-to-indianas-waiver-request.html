Whether or not you think the Trump administration’s effort to dismantle the Department of Ed is a nothing burger, surely we can all agree that Indiana’s ESSA waiver request is at least a something burger. The Hoosier State is the first to propose that Secretary Linda McMahon use her waiver authority to allow major changes to federal education policy related to accountability. (A separate issue is Indiana’s request for funding flexibility, which I will leave for another day, and which is somewhat akin to what Iowa has proposed, as well.)

Given its ed reform bona fides and the strong reputation of state superintendent Katie Jenner, the feds’ response could open the floodgates to imitators.

Back in August, a coalition of ed reform organizations urged Indiana to deep-six or fundamentally alter its waiver request, to no avail. (Those included All4Ed, Education Trust, National Parents Union, and UnidosUS, among others.) Now it’s up to Secretary McMahon and Assistant Secretary Kirsten Baesler to decide how to respond.

Personally, I think it’s naïve to expect Trump officials to reject the waiver proposal outright. They have been insistent (if not consistent) in their desire to return power to the states, and have welcomed waiver requests, so they will surely find a way to get to yes. The question, then, is what exactly they should assent to—and what pieces they should reject.

That’s where you come in. What would you recommend that McMahon and Baesler do? What parts of Indiana’s proposal can reformers live with? And where should bright lines be drawn?

How would Indiana’s new A–F accountability system work?

At the heart of Indiana’s waiver request is its new accountability system. To get up to speed, it’s worth reading Indiana’s request, its latest proposed regulations describing its new accountability system, and the All4Ed/Ed Trust/etc. response. The state board is expected to vote in January on Indiana’s new system. Here’s how Chalkbeat describes its key provisions:

The proposed grading system assigns points to students based on their academic mastery, measured through test scores, and gives them additional points for other knowledge, skills, and experiences that state education leaders say students need for future success. That includes earning workforce credentials and skills like work ethic and communication and collaboration.

Each student’s overall score is then averaged with other students for a school’s overall grade. Schools are not docked additional points for students who don’t pass tests or master specific skills. Schools receive a letter grade based on a scale of zero to 100 points, with 90 points and above graded as an A and 59 and below as an F.

It’s hard to figure out exactly what this will look like, given how different it is from existing state accountability systems. But groups on the left like Education Trust have expressed concern that it could translate into lower expectations for disadvantaged kids. In a public comment on the proposed A–F system, Ed Trust writes, “While the proposed system includes both traditional academic and skill-based outcomes, it doesn’t adequately account for both. Instead, it allows some outcomes to be prioritized for some students and different outcomes for others.” For example, low-performing students can still earn their schools points if they exceed their growth targets, attend school regularly, and earn high grades.

What’s in Indiana’s waiver request?

Katie wants Indiana to use its accountability system for federal identification purposes, rather than having two separate systems. Many states would love to be able to do the same!

But, as All4Ed et al. point out, Indiana’s system fails to meet many of the requirements of ESSA. That’s not a problem legally. Secretary McMahon has the statutory authority to waive those rules. But it understandably makes folks nervous. Specifically, the proposed Indiana system fails to include:

  • A standalone indicator for high school graduation rates.

  • A standalone indicator for English language proficiency for English learners.

  • An explanation of how the performance of student groups will be factored, if at all, into school ratings or identification.

  • An explanation of how it will identify schools for “comprehensive support and improvement” and “targeted support and improvement” (though I hear that schools that get Fs will also be labeled CSI)—or what it would do to support such schools.

Regarding the last point: It proposes a new “education innovation grant” instead to drive funding to districts or charter schools that could better serve students than the low-performing schools they currently attend—an idea that sounds pretty good to me.

Nicholas Munyan-Penney of Ed Trust is especially worried about accountability for high schools, which, he told me, “can earn full or nearly full credit for supporting a student without a single test-based measure (just attendance and credit accumulation/course grades), which is, of course, concerning given rampant grade inflation.”

He’s also concerned that:

…the student-based “menu” approach that Indiana designed could be gamed by school and district leaders, allowing them to hold some students to lower expectations than others, since schools can get the same amount of credit for indicators of wildly different levels of rigor, while making it harder to understand what letter grades actually say about how well schools are serving all students.

What should Secretary McMahon and Assistant Secretary Baesler do?

Now it’s your turn. How should federal officials respond—again, knowing that saying no to everything is not an option? Should they, for example, make Indiana rethink the idea of giving schools credit for low-performing students’ grades? Force Indiana to incorporate a separate indicator for English language proficiency for English learners? What else, and why?

  • Can “reciprocal accountability” lead to a “truce in the accountability wars”? Heather Hough thinks so. “This new paradigm honors the expertise of local educators while also acknowledging that the state holds the ultimate constitutional authority for educating students, and so it must intervene when districts and schools lack the ability or will to improve,” Heather writes. “At the same time, we must create formal structures that hold the state itself accountable for setting the conditions for improvement—through clear priorities, aligned resources, and coherent policies.” That sounds pretty good, though I’m not sure who the “we” is that will “hold the state itself accountable.” And I wonder what Carey Wright would make of it, given that the Mississippi Marathon was largely about old-fashioned top-down, state-led micromanagement—and it worked.

  • Then again, the story of educational success across the pond sounds a lot like “reciprocal accountability,” with the Brits’ “high accountability, high autonomy” mantra. So explains Natalie Wexler in her write-up of Sir Nick Gibb’s new book describing England’s impressive gains since 2010. “Wasn’t that the theory behind both No Child Left Behind and the American charter school movement?” Natalie asks. “Publicize test scores, highlight the schools that are doing well, sanction or close the ones that aren’t—or just rely on parents to vote with their feet—and voila, you’ll have a functional education system. Except it didn’t work out that way here.” To which I would say: Come on, Natalie, it did work, especially in the charter sector, and in the public schools, too, at least for a while, and at least for math. But she’s right that we’ve never made much progress on the reading front, and here she’s onto something. “On the reading side, these tests are supposed to measure abstract comprehension skills like ‘making inferences.’ That has led schools to focus inordinately on those skills while downplaying or eliminating subjects like social studies and science, which generally aren’t tested.” Channeling Robert Pondiscio, David Steiner, and E.D. Hirsch, Natalie wonders what it will take for us to ditch the “reading” tests and embrace content-based exams instead—as the English have long done, to great effect.

  • Speaking of reading, Michael Horn weighs in on the small-group instruction debate kicked off by Mike Schmoker and Timothy Shanahan a few months ago when they argued that whole-group instruction is almost always better because it results in higher “instructional dosage” for the most kids. “This seems like a classic case of drawing a broad conclusion at too high a level,” writes Michael, “and ignoring anomalies on both sides to understand the circumstances under which one approach or another may work better.” What’s key, Michael argues, is how students in small groups spend their time when they aren’t receiving direct instruction from the teacher. If they are engaging in “cut, color, and paste,” as Mike and Tim put it, yeah, that’s a dumb strategy. But if they are working on improving their skills, perhaps through a motivating and individualized digital curriculum, that’s a whole different story. “This isn’t an argument for divvying students up based on flawed notions of leveled reading,” Michael says. “It is an argument for increasing active learning, ensuring students master the skills they’re working on, and providing the direct instruction they need.”

  • Michael is swimming against the tide, though, when it comes to his openness to digital education. The ed-tech backlash is here, as indicated by a splashy new article in the Free Press by Jared Cooney Horvath, “We Gave Students Laptops and Took Away Their Brains.” In this excerpt of his new book, Jared fingers digital devices and the time spent on them at school not just for declining student achievement, but for falling IQ scores, too. One fundamental problem, Jared writes, is that “reading from screens often triggers an unconscious shift from deep comprehension to shallow skimming—glancing, scrolling, and extracting instead of truly learning.” I’m skeptical of mono-causal explanations for anything as complex as student learning, but I agree that the “digitization of everything” was surely a mistake.

  • You know what else was a mistake: Tying teacher pay to education credentials like master’s degrees. So argues Chad Aldeman, quite convincingly, showing that the system encourages teachers to spend time and money on dubious degrees and wastes the public’s scarce dollars. If there’s a silver lining, it’s that the money could be re-allocated to much better uses, especially salary stipends for great teachers serving in hard-to-staff schools and subjects.

  • Tim Daly, meanwhile, wants the “affordability agenda” to include higher-performing schools. “Childcare and groceries are critical. But if we continue to see more students with very low academic skills, they will earn less and it will be impossible to subsidize goods and services for all of them, no matter how aggressively redistributive we become.”

  • It’s not just employers who want students to learn “durable skills” like critical thinking, communication, collaboration, and leadership. They are important to families, too. That’s the upshot of a recent poll fielded by EdChoice and America Succeeds and written up by Colyn Ritter and Aidan Schief. That’s no surprise. It’s hard to imagine parents saying they don’t want their kids to learn how to collaborate, think critically, etc. The big questions, IMO, relate to how to teach such skills without ignoring the academic side of the equation—and whether, say, math class needs to be given over to collaboration and the like, or whether that stuff can be better learned on the football field and the orchestra pit.

  • Like most issues of SCHOOLED, this one contains a whole lot of handwringing. But that’s nothing new. In “The Ever-Present Panic About America’s Schools,” Idrees Kahloon digs up articles from The Atlantic’s archives to demonstrate that we’ve never been happy with the state of American education. Take, for example, this complaint from 1939: “It wasn’t just that students were incapable of writing well… The problem was that ‘a great many high-school pupils are not able to discriminate between what is a sentence and what is not.’” Yup, sounds familiar!
