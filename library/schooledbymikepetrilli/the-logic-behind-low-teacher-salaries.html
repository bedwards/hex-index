On Tuesday, we teed up two questions related to teacher salaries. First, what’s the argument for and against paying teachers more instead of spending education dollars on other things? Second, if we want additional funding to translate into higher teacher pay, should reformers focus state advocacy on teacher compensation instead of school finance reform?

We’ll save the second question for next week, but see below for great comments about the first from Matt Barnum, Kevin Carey, Susie Miller Carello, David Griffith, and Stéphane Lavertu. Plus, we round up recent posts from the edu-sphere, including Nina Rees on her time working for Dick Cheney and Jed Wallace on the impact of Tuesday’s elections on charter schools.

If it wasn’t clear on Tuesday, my own view is that schools and districts would be smart to invest more of their available resources in higher teacher pay, especially for exceptionally effective teachers, those serving in high-need schools, and those teaching in shortage areas. That would mean allowing class sizes to rise modestly, but also means doing without so many collateral staff, such as instructional aides, literacy coaches, tutors, and mental health counselors.

Yet few schools—in any sector—prioritize their budgets in this manner. Why is that?

As several respondents argue, schools may be responding to consumer demand. They are giving parents what they want.

On Bluesky, Matt Barnum noted:

The same trend (greater investment in people than salaries) shows up in private schools. For better or worse, this may reflect schools’ response to what parents want.

Kevin Carey said much the same:

The expensive private schools that rich people send their kids to are full of moderate-salary teachers in very small classes.

Matt agreed:

Exactly, and many schools of choice actively market their small classes/staffing ratios. This trend appears to be a market response—which doesn’t necessarily mean it’s optimal!—as much as anything else.

It makes sense. While parents might tell pollsters that they think teachers should be paid more, when it comes to their own kids’ schools, salaries are invisible while class sizes and extra services are all too apparent. Who doesn’t want tiny classes for their children, and tutoring as needed, wellness centers, and the other offerings in the ever-expanding cafeteria of school services?

But it’s not just parents. Teachers value the support services, too—support for both them and their students. That’s particularly true for teachers in tough schools. Here’s how my colleague David Griffith (who once worked in a tough, dysfunctional D.C. charter school) puts it:

You’re discounting the non-pecuniary benefits of non-instructional staffing. After all, teaching is hard. But it’s less hard if you have an experienced teaching assistant for your toughest class, or a dean of students to deal with troublemakers while you keep teaching, or slightly smaller classes.

These things are a form of compensation, as are pensions and other benefits. And for a teacher who is fundamentally comfortable with his or her lifestyle and works in a challenging environment, some of these things may be preferable to what is effectively hazard pay—which isn’t to say that our current choices are optimal.

That makes sense, although, as Marguerite Roza has pointed out, the best surveys have found that most teachers would rather receive higher pay than get help from a shared classroom aide—if the options are cost-equivalent. (See the survey, from Dan Goldhaber, Michael DeArmond, and Scott DeBurgomaster here.)

And as Matt noted, strong unions nudge districts toward higher pay—reflecting that teachers, not surprisingly, care about salaries (among other things).

It’s not just help that teachers need and crave but also the professional learning opportunities that help them get better at their craft. So argues Susie Miller Carello, the legendary former head of SUNY’s charter school authorizing shop, who now leads the Florida Charter Institute:

Everyone loves to say, “Teachers should be paid more.” And they’re right. But here’s the problem: Paying teachers well without giving them the tools, coaching, and cognitive science grounding to be excellent at their craft is like giving a pilot a big raise and then cutting flight training. Appreciation without preparation is not respect—it’s abdication.

The truth is that teachers deserve both: professional pay and professional preparation. A salary that says, “We value you” and a system that says, “We will make you exceptional.” The super-successful charter networks already know this secret. They don’t just compensate teachers better; they coach them relentlessly, they study their lessons before they’re taught, and they debrief with surgical precision after each one. Their secret is not a secret at all: Teaching is an act of deliberate practice, not an act of inspiration.

If money alone created mastery, professional athletes wouldn’t need coaches. The world’s top jazz musicians wouldn’t rehearse daily. Greatness requires practice, feedback, iteration, and the willingness to work toward better, always. Without the scaffolding of coaching and study, practice, and review, over and over—without that, even talented teachers plateau. But with those supports? They soar.

So yes—raise teachers’ pay. Please. But don’t stop there. Pair every raise with rigorous coaching and Socratic questioning. Match every salary increase with a step deeper into cognitive science. Make professional learning as predictable and non-negotiable as payroll. Because the teachers who understand and can teach to the ways the brain learns are the teachers who truly change lives.

Is higher teacher pay the right choice?

There’s an empirical question at the heart of this debate. As Matt put it:

What is the evidence that higher salaries are a better investment than more people? I think the research is mixed and unclear.

The link is to a study by Jason Cook, Stéphane Lavertu, and Corbin Miller: “Rent-Seeking through collective bargaining: Teachers unions and education production.” From its abstract:

Districts facing union pressure increase teacher salaries and benefits, spend down reserves, and experience no student achievement gains. Conversely, districts facing less pressure hire more teachers (instead of increasing compensation) and realize significant student achievement gains.

That sure sounds like an argument for teacher quantity over quality! So, I asked Stéphane Lavertu (an Ohio State professor and Fordham Institute fellow) for his thoughts. He thinks higher teacher pay is the better choice—but that we have to distinguish between the short term and long term and make sure that it’s not just higher pay for everyone.

I suspect that what happened in my “rent seeking” study (the one Matt cites) is that teacher pay increases exacerbated fiscal stress in many districts (e.g., required putting levies on the ballot) and was not used to reward the most effective teachers.

Wisconsin offers lessons here, too, according to Stéphane:

E. Jason Baron had some work on Wisconsin’s Act 10 that showed the reform [of teacher collective bargaining] had a negative impact overall (in the short term), and combining his work with Barbara Biasi’s, it appears to be because districts that ditched step-and-lane salary schedules were able to draw effective teachers from districts that kept rigid salary schedules. Biasi showed that districts that paid effective teachers more benefited, but the system as a whole suffered in the short term.

The long term is different, though. Stéphane again:

As Matthew Kraft and his colleagues documented, the state of the teaching profession is poor. It draws few undergrads, and part of that has to do with pay and working conditions (e.g., see this cool paper written by Carolyn Tsao, an incoming Ohio State economics professor).

In the long term, though, both higher teacher pay combined with management reforms (e.g., roll back union influence and give superintendents discretion on pay and teaching assignments) can yield benefits in terms of a stronger labor force.

For example, Jason had a follow-up paper (that got little attention) that showed that Wisconsin’s Act 10 over time seemed to improve the quality of the teacher labor pool and led to a net-positive impact of the reform statewide. I think this account is largely right. But, as with anything, effects are highly context dependent!

Generally, I think higher teacher pay will take a while to affect achievement because it will take a while for the labor force to improve. And I suspect that, for more pronounced long-term effects, we need simultaneous increases in teacher pay and management reforms.

(We’ll dig into those reforms next week.)

The “political economy” of teacher pay explains why salaries are so low, given parental preferences, and why unions are the main ones pushing against the low-pay paradigm. (But they also care about other stuff, especially the priorities of their older, more engaged members, especially generous and highly subsidized health care benefits. Plus, of course, how many members they have!)

If we want higher, more differentiated pay for teachers, we’re going to need to do it via prescriptive state policy. As we’ll hear next week, Texas is showing the way.

Because the technology Chad et al. use is a regression line, showing schools that do better on reading proficiency versus what you would predict based on their free-lunch rates. I get it; I’m sure Chad and his editors wanted a measure that would work across states, but as any graduate student could explain, growth measures are better, fairer way to identify schools that are beating the odds. And that’s because they don’t rely on binary measures like proficiency and FRPL rates, can control for students’ previous achievement, and track progress for everyone, not just kids near the “proficiency” line.

  • Despite dealing with a bad cold, Stéphane Lavertu is having a big week—not just weighing in on teacher pay (above) but also authoring a new Fordham report on Ohio’s high-achieving, low-income (HALO) students (and appearing on our podcast to talk about it, too). My Fordham-Ohio colleague Aaron Churchill and I provide our take on his study, and the leaky kindergarten-to-college pipeline for HALO students, which can largely be explained by inequitable access to advanced learning opportunities in our public schools. Consider this: While HALO students are outnumbered 3-to-1 by their more affluent counterparts in the third grade, a decade later they are outnumbered 6-to-1 in four-year colleges and a staggering 10-to-1 in top colleges. The HALO kids who beat the odds took advanced courses in middle school and Advanced Placement in high school and/or attended school with other high achievers. As Rick Kahlenberg wrote on LinkedIn, interdistrict choice would help get more HALO kids to and through college.
