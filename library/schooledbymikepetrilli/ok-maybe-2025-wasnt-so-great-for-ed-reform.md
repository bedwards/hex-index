On Tuesday, [I weighed the pros and cons of 2025](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/was-2025-a-good-year-for-education), arguing that, on balance, the year was pretty good for ed reform, despite all the things that made it challenging writ large. Most of y’all were not convinced. Let’s hear some responses:

**Tim Daly**

> The measure of any year is whether it was good or bad for kids. It’s very difficult to call 2025 successful given the [NAEP results released in late January](https://www.nagb.gov/content/dam/nagb/en/documents/newsroom/press-releases/2025/naep2024-pressrelease-final.pdf). While some folks had long expected them, I think they were shocking and far from inevitable, given the financial resources available to schools and the return to normal post-Covid school operations by fall 2022. A collective acknowledgement—[most evident among opinion leaders](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/was-2025-a-good-year-for-education)—that our problems starter earlier than 2020 and require a real national response is positive. But when are we going to put actual wins on the board? That’s what I would call a good year.

**Van Schoales**

> Seems far darker than brighter to me, given the following list:
> 
> 1.  There seems to be no effort or vision for how to better support most low-income kids in the biggest school districts.
>     
> 2.  No major national foundations are seriously investing in improving schools in urban areas, and there are fewer now than the last two decades.
>     
> 3.  Tax credits, vouchers, and ESAs will help some but at best will be a marginal improvement and likely do nothing for those large public systems.
>     
> 4.  Research on impact is less fashionable these days, and the federal chaos will make it super hard to improve the research ecosystem.
>     
> 5.  AI, while great for those with privilege and for some without privilege to accelerate their learning, seems to be a powerful accelerator for exacerbating the divides by income and race. My nephews, one at Berkeley and one at an elite private high school in Los Angeles, have not only incredible networks and lots of good basic skills, but they are also whiz kids in terms of using AI to their advantage in all things. And if you add the anti-diversity funding on top, it adds fewer opportunities for the disadvantaged.
>     
> 
> I think it is pretty dark as I add these things up. Thankfully there is some great TV, film, music, and art...and 2026.

**Mike Goldstein**

> AI gobbled up lots of philanthropy. I would think teachers unions see that as a big win because that means less for every reform effort. Although maybe in absence of AI to fund, those donors would just shift more to other sectors, like climate and medicine et al.

**Doug Harris**

> It was certainly an eventful year but pushing in different directions. A lot of what’s happening (science of reading, attention to grade inflation, etc.) should help achievement. The federal voucher/ESAs will probably do the opposite. Choice/market pressures will encourage more grade inflation, not less. As you say, immigration policy is also bad for academic outcomes, but it’s hard to see academics as a primary concern on that one.

One reader saw a silver lining, though:

**Jonathan Plucker**

> Good list! I would definitely add the growth of [automatic enrollment policies](https://edtrust.org/rti/automatic-enrollment-policies-for-advanced-coursework/) as a big positive this year. Over the past couple of years, we saw a few more states pass new policies or expand existing auto-enrollment laws. We don’t have data on district auto-enrollment, but anecdotal information suggests the policies are growing at the local level in the absence of state policies. This is something to be excited about!

And **Robin Steans**, in [her own year-end reflection](https://www.advanceillinois.org/news-media/from-the-desk/in-the-face-of-uncertainty-working-together-for-illinois-students-and-children), acknowledges 2025’s changes, uncertainty, and hardship. Still, she celebrates progress for children in Illinois: protecting access to school regardless of immigration status, supporting student well-being, and building new data-driven accountability systems:

> SB0406 creates a new Early Childhood Integrated Data System (ECIDS) to collect, integrate, store, and report on early childhood data across programs and agencies. While it will take time for ECIDS to be up and running, it is badly overdue and will help anchor critical work going forward, allowing the agency to develop tools for parents and communities to access aggregated data. On the other end of the education spectrum, SB2039 calls for a new public-facing and centralized higher education dashboard to report on everything from institutional affordability to student enrollment, persistence, and completion, starting in 2027. These developments strengthen the state’s education data infrastructure and transparency, and they could not come at a more urgent time, as federal actions have slashed data and research support and infrastructure.

She ends by reminding us:

> Making sure that all children have what they need to live up to their full potential remains one of the most important responsibilities we all have.

These are all compelling arguments. We probably won’t be able to judge 2025 for a few years—once we know what follows. Do policymakers listen to the opinion leaders and start getting their acts together, especially in the blue states that have been falling behind in recent years? Do candidates for president put ed reform front and center? And does any of this start to move the needle on student outcomes?

Much of that, my friends, is up to us. Let’s get to work.

-   **Alexander Russo** [reminds us](https://alexanderrusso.substack.com/p/the-top-10-education-stories-of-2025) of 2025’s big education stories and best media coverage. He makes some great choices. Among my favorites were **Emily Hanford** and colleagues’ [deep dive into Steubenville](https://www.apmreports.org/story/2025/02/20/steubenville-ohio-reading-success-for-all), Ohio’s, reading success, **Kelsey Piper**’s [missive on the Mississippi Miracle](https://www.theargumentmag.com/p/illiteracy-is-a-policy-choice), and **Chandler Fritz**’s [long essay in Harper’s](https://harpers.org/archive/2025/09/the-homemade-scholar-chandler-fritz-empowerment-scholarship-account-arizona-schools/) about teaching in an ESA-funded private school in Arizona (listen to my podcast with **Chandler** [here](https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/resources/joyful-classrooms-zero-public-transparency-inside-esa-micro-school-episode-987)).
    
-   Here’s another “best of 2025” list, [this time from](https://www.educationdaly.us/p/farewell-to-2025) **[Tim Daly](https://www.educationdaly.us/p/farewell-to-2025)**. I had forgotten that **Tim** helped to kick off the blogosphere’s focus on the Mississippi Miracle with a [great take](https://www.educationdaly.us/p/mississippi-cant-possibly-have-good) that went viral, partly because it was reposted in the [Free Press](https://www.thefp.com/p/mississippi-cant-possibly-have-good). Good work, **Tim**!
    
-   Now let’s look ahead to the New Year. **Emily Freitag** has been touring schools and learning about their use of AI, and she’s [got some predictions for 2026](https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/commentary/some-predictions-about-ai-education-2026). Among them:
    
    -   The teacher autonomy tools will win the early adoption surge but will begin to lose market share to products designed for district-wide use.
        
    -   Existing curriculum, assessment, and intervention providers will start offering AI-powered features.
        
    -   Instructional super products will begin to emerge that transcend categories (e.g., serving as curriculum, assessment, and intervention with embedded professional learning all in one).
        
    -   Some districts, teachers, and parents will opt out of AI entirely.
        
-   **Matt Yglesias** offers a [smart take](https://www.slowboring.com/p/confessions-of-a-former-studies-say) on the limits of policymaking via evidence, pointing to the recent dust-up over the Mississippi Miracle as one example. The “[scout mindset](https://www.amazon.com/Scout-Mindset-Perils-Defensive-Thinking/dp/0735217556),” which he strongly endorses, encapsulates what I’m hoping to do with SCHOOLED: “In the army, a scout’s job is to try their best to see things as they are and report that accurately back to whoever needs to know. A soldier is supposed to defend a position, which requires a different way of thinking.” No more “war of ideas.”
    
-   I don’t tend to feel big emotions when reading education policy posts, but boy did this one from **Dan Buck** get me: “[Our Schools Are Failing Refugee and Immigrant Students](https://www.aei.org/op-eds/our-schools-are-failing-refugee-and-immigrant-students/).” **Dan** recounts his attempts to maintain peace between warring refugee communities in the Milwaukee middle school where he once taught. “You don’t quickly forget the sound of a 12-year-old gagging and wheezing,” he writes. I won’t quickly forget that either.
    
-   Don’t ban phones at the state level—let schools (or districts) do it instead. That’s **[Michael Horn](https://michaelbhorn.substack.com/p/let-schools-ban-phones-not-policy?utm_source=post-email-title&publication_id=35034&post_id=179466218&utm_campaign=email-post-title&isFreemail=true&r=3drha&triedRedirect=true&utm_medium=email)**[’s argument](https://michaelbhorn.substack.com/p/let-schools-ban-phones-not-policy?utm_source=post-email-title&publication_id=35034&post_id=179466218&utm_campaign=email-post-title&isFreemail=true&r=3drha&triedRedirect=true&utm_medium=email), and also where I landed a few months ago in a [SCHOOLED debate](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/schools-but-not-states-should-ban).
    
-   Speaking of debates, earlier this year we [covered the dust-up](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/how-should-the-feds-respond-to-indianas) [stirred by](https://schooledbymikepetrilli.substack.com/p/how-to-persuade-democratic-governors) **Mike Schmoker** and **Tim Shanahan** when they [argued](https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/opinion-small-group-reading-instruction-is-not-as-effective-as-you-think/2025/09) against small-group reading instruction. Now two titans of the instructional practice world [respond](https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/opinion-yes-small-group-reading-instruction-works-but-use-it-wisely/2025/12), with guns a-blazing. **Nell Duke** and **Claude Goldenberg** make the case that small group instruction works—if used wisely. Which might be true, but they don’t seem overly concerned with **Mike** and **Tim**’s main worry, which is what kids spend their time doing when it’s not their turn to be with the teacher. “Research supports having children engage in play, participate in paired or dyad reading, or practice writing,” **Nell** and **Claude** write. “Increasingly, artificial intelligence tools offer beneficial experiences for children while the teacher is working with other children in small groups.” So I guess **Nat Malkus** can add them to [Team Optimist](https://www.aei.org/education/ai-has-come-for-k-12-education/) when it comes to AI.
    
-   **Danyela Souza Egorov** [digs into](https://www.city-journal.org/article/new-york-city-public-schools-class-size) why New York City’s class size mandate “is wreaking havoc” in Gotham’s schools. Among the outcomes: skyrocketing costs, facilities challenges, school closures and consolidations, and greater inequality. Good work, Empire State! Seriously, what a terrible example of a big blue state shooting itself in the foot.
    
-   Speaking of big city challenges… While Indianapolis is pushing ahead with a bold move toward portfolio management (see “Pass,” below), Denver is trading out the strategy in return for top-down control. So [explains](https://boardhawk.org/2025/12/despite-resounding-election-loss-all-is-not-lost-for-denver-public-schools-students/) **Van Schoales**, who isn’t a fan of the micromanagement approach but acknowledges that it has worked in a few districts, both recently (in LAUSD) and in the past (in Long Beach, San Diego, and Miami). I would add DCPS to the list, but I agree with **Van** here: “If Denver’s new board and \[Superintendent **Alex\] Marrero** adopt the best versions of centralized systems while monitoring student growth, it may not become the disaster most reformers expect.”
    
-   Let’s make it a hat trick on the urban education beat. **Chad Aldeman** [explains](https://www.the74million.org/article/how-can-los-angeles-schools-have-a-looming-1-6b-deficit-with-19b-in-revenues) that Los Angeles’s enormous deficit resulted from too many staff and schools for the amount of students the district serves. A lot of districts are in the same boat, according to [analyses](https://www.schoolbusinessnow.org/articles/2025/august-2025/here-comes-the-big-shrink) by **Marguerite Roza**. Speaking of too many staff, **Chad** also [dug into](https://www.chadaldeman.com/p/education-jobs-update-december-2025) the latest (delayed) jobs report and found that education jobs keep going up, inexplicably. And, along with **Samuel Yi**, **Chad** [shows](https://www.educationnext.org/are-teachers-abandoning-teaching-data-show-teacher-turnover-remains-low/) with a bunch of charts that teacher turnover has remained remarkably stable over time and is much the same as what we see in similar professions. “Based on media accounts and the rhetoric of politicians, one might think teachers are fleeing their profession in droves, whether because they are underpaid, overworked, or simply dissatisfied,” write **Chad** and **Samuel**. “And yet, the objective data on teacher behavior tell a rather different story.”
    
-   **Alli Aldis** [reports](https://informedchoice1996.substack.com/p/your-kids-should-be-debating) on a recent [EdChoice poll](https://edchoice.mcdatahub.com/edchoice/Reports/Adults_Aug_Nov_25.pdf) finding that Americans (including parents) think middle and high school students should learn debate skills like “using evidence, taking turns in a debate, and learning how to argue both sides of an issue.” **Alli** also implies that she uses these skills at family dinners, surely in the service of crushing her dad **[Chad](https://fordhaminstitute.org/about/fordham-staff/chad-l-aldis)** in political debates.
    
-   Speaking of adolescents, **Kelsey Piper** [looks at](https://www.theargumentmag.com/p/how-getting-richer-made-teenagers) how modern economic growth transformed childhood into something much safer but far less free. “No one could possibly examine what it was like to be a teenager a century ago and soberly call for a return to it,” **Kelsey** writes. “Things are better now. Still, it would be a tragedy if the explosion of prosperity that freed our children from labor traps them under increasing supervision and diminished opportunities for meaningful choice and meaningful participation in society. Today’s child endangerment doesn’t come from dangerous machines, high mortality rates, or a lack of K–12 opportunities—it often comes from a lack of agency.”
    
-   Finally, **Jude Schwalbach** [dives into](https://reason.org/education-newsletter/school-buses-and-changing-transportation-needs/) the not very sexy but essential topic of school transportation. The function is facing challenges on all fronts, from lower student ridership (as more moms and dads drive their kiddos to school) to driver shortages (in a world with Amazon delivery, Uber, and Lyft) to exploding costs (related to those first two factors). It’s time to think beyond the big yellow bus, **Jude** argues. “Loosening regulations so school districts can transport many students with small passenger vans would be a good starting point.”
