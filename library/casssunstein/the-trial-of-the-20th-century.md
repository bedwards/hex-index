The Alger Hiss-Whittaker Chambers saga, not so widely known today, was high drama. Actually that understates matters. It was drama of the highest order. You couldn’t make it up.

It should be a movie. It almost has to be a movie.

(After the fact, the novelist Allen Drury tried, more or less, to make it up, about a decade after the relevant events. See his gripping novel, Advise and Consent, and the amazing Henry Fonda movie that was based on it. Inspired by Hiss and Chambers, Drury couldn’t match the real thing. Not close.)

The Hiss-Chambers saga is not only an enduringly fascinating drama, with unforgettable protagonists (and their unforgettable wives), a host of other unforgettable figures (including a president-to-be), and unanswered questions that persist to this day. (One word in that sentence will be taken, by some, to be fighting words. Do you know what it is? Right: “unanswered.”) There are plenty of mysteries here.

The drama set the stage for, and helps explain, a lot of what we are seeing today. It says more than a lot about the current right, and it says something, too, about the current left.

To get ahead of the tale: Hiss stood for the elite. He was seen, by a lot of the country, as a domestic enemy. He was seen, by a lot of the country, as disloyal. He was seen, by a lot of the country, as Harvard, and that was seen, by a lot of the country, as very bad.

But I am indeed getting ahead of the tale.

1

Alger Hiss was handsome, eloquent, and charming. He was tall and thin. He was boyish. He was winning, with a shy smile. He was smart as a whip. If there was ever a golden boy, it was Alger Hiss. Here he is:

(Reader, I knew him. Just a bit, but still. At the time, he was in his late 70s. He was handsome, eloquent, and charming. He was tall and thin. He was winning, with a shy smile. He was not boyish, but he was smart as a whip. A former model, his wife - his second wife - was in her mid-70s at the time, and she was elegant, charming, and beautiful. Actually she was a knockout. Was I smitten, in my twenties? I was smitten, in my twenties. Who wouldn’t be?)

Hiss’ resume was beyond belief. He graduated from Harvard Law School, where he was an editor of the law review, and a superstar. He seemed to know everybody, or at least everybody in elite circles. He clerked for Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr., and he served in influential positions in government during the New Deal. He worked in the Department of Justice and the Department of State.

He became U.S. Delegate/Adviser to President Roosevelt at the Yalta Conference. He played a major role during the founding of the United Nations. (As in, a major role.) He was Temporary Secretary-General for the San Francisco Conference, and he made a massively favorable impression in that role.

In his young forties, he was elected president of the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace. He was not a kid, but he looked like a golden boy.

Whittaker Chambers was short and overweight. He had terrible teeth. He tended to sweat. He was a mess. Here he is:

But the man was smart, really smart, and gosh could he write. He went to Columbia at a time when the university was filled with extraordinary students who became famous for their brilliance. A professor who taught many of them said that Chambers was the best of them all, the most brilliant of the whole lot. (Was he even more brilliant than Hiss? I think so.)

Chambers became a Communist around 1924. He became a Communist in the sense that he thought that Communism and Communists were right. But he also took action. He joined the American Communist Party. He became a spy. He worked on behalf of the Soviet Union. He betrayed his country. He was a Communist for about thirteen years. He did a lot of cloak-and-dagger stuff, without the dagger. His code name was “Carl.”

Chambers came to be profoundly disaffected by Stalin and the Soviet Union. He left the Party in around 1938. He became a devout Christian. This was a conversion experience. He thought that the world was in a life-or-death struggle between Communism and Christianity, and that Communism would likely win. He was terrified that he would be killed. He was baptized in 1940. He became an editor and a writer for Time Magazine, without saying much about his past.

3.

On August 3, 1948, Chambers accused Hiss of having been (and possibly still being) a Communist, in testimony before the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC). This was a bombshell. Chambers was measured and precise, and he offered plenty of details.

Still, his accusation did not seem credible. Hiss, a Communist? No way. But as a witness, Chambers himself seemed credible. It was all stunning, and quite baffling.

Hiss responded to the charge. He was a terrific witness. He was disarming and full of charm. He did not even know the name “Whittaker Chambers. “ He did not recognize him from a photograph. No, he had never been a Communist. No, Chambers’ charges were false. It was all a lie.

Thus Hiss: “I am not and never have been a member of the Communist Party. I do not and never have adhered to the tenets of the Communist Party. … To the best of my knowledge, I never heard of Whittaker Chambers until 1947, when two representatives of the federal Bureau of Investigation asked me if I knew him and various other people….I said \[to the FBI\] I did not know Chambers. So far as I know, I have never laid eyes on him, and I should like the to have the opportunity to do so.”

Did Chambers suffer from a mental illness? Was he obsessed with Hiss? Was he engaged in some kind of vendetta?  
  
It all looked embarrassing, not least for HUAC. An honest citizen, an upstanding citizen, a public servant, had been falsely accused of disloyalty to our country. Wow. What a farce.

It all might have died then, but a member of Congress was suspicious. He thought that Hiss might be lying. He didn’t like Hiss’ answers; he thought that they were cagey. Actually he took an intense dislike to Hiss. His name was Richard Nixon, and he was both smart and dogged. He convinced HUAC not to drop the case.

Relatively quickly Chambers and Hiss were brought to testify together. During their surreal in-person encounter, Hiss started by indicating that he did not recognize Chambers. But he chose his words carefully. He indicated that he *might* have seen him before, but was not sure, and would have to look closely. In particular, he would have to look at Chambers’ teeth. Actually he would have to look closely at Chambers’ teeth.

After a period of not-quite-denials, Hiss noted, a bit out of the blue, that in the relevant period (the early-to-mid 30s), he did know someone: one George Crosley, a journalist who subleased his apartment then and who turned out to be a freeloader, a moocher, a bit of a creep. He acknowledged that the man who now called himself Chambers might actually be Crosley.

(What was this? Was it possibly true? Had Chambers called himself Crosley? Or was it a brilliant improvisation on Hiss’ part? Was it preposterous? If it was false, a made-up tale concocted under desperation, when did Hiss think of it, and how and why did he think of it? Was Hiss a cornered rat?)

To know for sure whether Chambers was Crosley, Hiss needed to take a real look at him, and at those teeth. Ultimately he made an identification: This was indeed the man, the freeloader, that Hiss had known as Crosley. (The teeth were a bit different, though. And indeed, Chambers had had a lot of dental work done.)

Hiss was asked whether he could think of anyone who would have known Chambers as “George Crosley.” He seemed to panic, but quickly recovered, and said that he probably could not. It was so long ago.

Chambers was asked whether the man who had been testifying was Alger Hiss. His brisk, possibly bemused answer: “Positive identification.”

He did not remember ever going under the name of “George Crosley.” When he was a Communist, he went under the name of “Carl.”

Here is one exchange, revealing Chambers’ tone throughout:

Mr. NIXON. Do you have any other evidence, any factual evidence, to bear out your claim that Mr. Hiss was a member of the Communist Party?

Mr. CHAMBERS. Nothing beyond the fact that he submitted himself for the 2 or 3 years that I knew him as a dedicated and disciplined Communist. .

4.

Someone was lying, but who? It was prominently said at the time: “One of these two men is the greatest liar in American history,” You could make the argument. But again: Which one?

In subsequent testimony, Chambers was asked a host of questions about Hiss. (Hiss was not present.) He seemed to know a lot of details, consistent with his general claim, which is that they were close colleagues (comrades).

He knew about Hiss’ nickname for his wife, and about his wife’s nickname for him. He knew about Hiss’ stepson. He knew about Hiss’ interests and hobbies. He knew about Hiss’ living quarters. He seemed to know a lot more than an ordinary acquaintance, subleasing someone’s apartment, would.

Chambers testified that Hiss had been his closest friend, or at least his closest friend in the Communist Party. He also testified that when he himself left the party, he pleaded with Hiss to leave too - but that Hiss refused. The picture that Chambers painted, of their final parting, was moving and specific.

In another, separate hearing, Hiss was asked a lot of questions about himself. Nixon was the main questioner, and his strategy was to prove that Chambers must have known Hiss very well - far better than Hiss had acknowledged, and well enough to suggest that Hiss had been lying when he denied knowing Chambers. If he had been lying about that, well, maybe Chambers had told the truth about everything.

Hiss’ answers generally mimicked Chamber’s answers. So: How could Chambers know so much about Hiss, if he was not telling the truth? Hiss himself had something to say about that. He had indeed spent significant time with Crosley, and Crosley could have picked up all of the relevant information in that way.

Asked by Hiss, at a separate hearing, how he could explain his statement that he stayed in Hiss’ apartment without signing a sublease, Chambers responded: “Very easily, Alger. I was a Communist and you were a Communist.”

Many thought that Chambers was an especially agile liar, and that he was going after Hiss for some personal reason. With palpable emotion, Chambers responded:

“The story has spread that in testifying against Mr. Hiss, I am working out some old grudge, or motives of revenge or hatred. I do not hate Mr. Hiss. We were close friends, but we are caught in a tragedy of history. Mr. Hiss represents the concealed enemy against which we are all fighting, and I am fighting. I have testified against him with remorse and pity, but in a moment of history in which this Nation now stands, so help me God, I could not do otherwise.”

5.

From there, things got much more complicated, and I am going to compress about a million fascinating details.

Hiss sued Chambers for libel. During the suit, Chambers produced secret State Department documents that, he said, proved that Hiss had engaged in espionage. Some of those documents were in Hiss’ own handwriting. (No doubt about that.) Some of them were typed. (Who typed them?)

This was another bombshell, a big one, a huge one. Many people thought, and think, that it settled the matter. In their view: Case closed.

Was Hiss not merely a Communist but also a spy? (It was not a crime to be a Communist; but it was a crime to spy for the Soviet Union.) Chambers had testified that Hiss was not engaged in espionage. He admitted that in so saying, he had been lying. Why? To protect his old friend Hiss! He did not want Hiss to go to jail. (Can this have been true? It can have been true? Was it true?)

After disclosure of the documents, a federal grand jury indicted Hiss for perjury, evidently mooting the libel action. (The statute of limitations had run for espionage.) Hiss and his lawyers claimed that he had never been a Communist, never perjured himself, and never engaged in espionage. Hiss suggested that Chambers, or someone, had stolen the documents in his handwriting. He added that the typed copies were forgeries.

Crucial evidence turned out to be an old typewriter that had allegedly been Hiss’ - and that seemed, according to experts, to be the very typewriter that had typed those documents.

Hiss had some pretty good character witnesses, including Supreme Court Justice Felix Frankfurter, Adlai Stevenson (who would run for president), Supreme Court Justice Stanley Reed, and John W. Davis (who had run for president).In the first trial in 1949, the jury was hung.

Hiss was retried in 1950 and convicted of perjury. At his sentencing hearing, he thanked the judge and said, “I am confident that in the future the full facts of how Whittaker Chambers was able to carry out forgery by typewriter will be disclosed.”

6

The Hiss saga has continued to the present day. Since 1950, so many things are interesting. Here are just a few of them.

First: Chambers wrote a vivid, long, moving, emotional, unforgettable, beautiful book, called *Witness*, that helped define the modern conservative movement. It has an immense number of details about Hiss and what Chambers sometimes called the Hiss Case and sometimes called the Hiss-Chambers case. But it has much more than that. It’s worth reading. It’s an important book. You could argue that it ranks among the most important books of the 20th century.

Second: The question of Hiss’ guilt or innocence long split the country. Emotions ran deep. Many people on the left thought that Hiss was innocent, and that he had been railroaded as part of the Red Scare. Whatever the truth of the matter, there’s a lot to say here about motivated reasoning and the sociology of knowledge.

Many people on the right thought that Hiss was guilty, and that his guilt told us a lot about the magnitude of the Communist threat and also about the nature of liberalism and the left. Many people on the right thought that the liberal left and Communism were more or less *continuous*.

Third: For all of his life, Hiss insisted on his innocence. He tried hard to win in the court of public opinion. He tried to clear his name. He was so agile, and so clever, and frequently charming. To many people, he just did not seem like a liar. He was quick to smile and quick to laugh. He said, “I like understatement.”

He was clear and confident, always, about his innocence. The mask, if it was a mask, never dropped. To many people, his clarity and confidence were, and remain, contagious.

In some ways, he was even surprising. Asked whether his views had changed since the 1940s, he answered, “I would say if anything I feel a bit more radical.” (Wow.) He said that Chambers suffered from “impostership” and was “only a fantasist.”

If Hiss was in fact innocent, his protestations and doggedness would not be nearly so interesting, at least as a matter of human psychology. If Hiss was guilty, a host of questions are raised: Why did he become a Communist? When did he stop being a Communist? What on earth was in his mind? Why didn’t he move on? Why did he keep lying? How could he do that? Why did he keep focusing on the details of the trials? That’s interesting.

7

Was Hiss a communist? Was he guilty? I think so, with a high degree of certainty, as do most historians who have studied the evidence in depth. Even now, it’s kind of astonishing to think that Hiss was a communist and a spy. But in all probability, he was (I think).

On both sides of the historical debate (and this is part of what it makes it all so interesting), you can smell the whiff of the conspiracy theorist, connecting a zillion dots. That’s more true of Hiss’ supporters, I think. They are obsessed. But those who think that he was guilty also go down plenty of rabbit holes. They too are obsessed. They too connect a zillion dots. Have a look at *Perjury*, by Allen Weinstein?

Here are some reasons to think that Hiss was guilty:

a. He was so cagey and so careful, early on, about whether he knew Chambers. What was that about? He initially said that he did not know anyone *by the name*, which seemed to give him wiggle room to admit that he knew the man, just under another name. Why would he take that approach? What was he afraid of? Why did he deny knowing Chambers until he just could not deny it any more?

b. The George Crosley stuff seems all made-up. Hiss’ tale of a moocher, who subleased his apartment, to whom he gave a car, and whom he somehow got to know really well, does not fit well with everything else we know about Hiss and the case. It seems too weird.

c. How did Chambers get those documents, some of which had Hiss’ handwriting? You would have to tell a pretty complicated story to explain that.

  
d. Forgery by typewriter? Really? True, the typewriter issue is a real rabbit hole. But forgery by typewriter?

e. Chambers did lie a lot. But if you read *Witness*, you will find it difficult to think that he was lying about Alger Hiss. He offers too many details, and in general, they fit together too well. If Chambers was lying about all that, then he was even more of a genius than he evidently was.

f. Subsequent materials from the former Soviet Union and elsewhere strongly support the claim that Hiss was a Communist and a spy. (I will let you look for yourself, if you want.) Reasonable people think that they are conclusive. (But there are some rabbit holes there too.)

If you put all this together, and more, you will be very hard-pressed to think that Hiss was innocent. Still, you might have a little voice in your head saying: “I am not absolutely sure.” (I hear that voice, and I don’t like hearing it. I wish I was absolutely sure.) If there’s a movie about the Hiss Case (and again, there really should be), it would be most dramatic, and most intriguing, if the audience isn’t given complete clarity about whether Hiss was lying.

8.

What on earth happened to Hiss? What’s his real story?

We don’t know. I tend to think that Hiss got radicalized in the 1930s. I tend to think that he saw the New Deal as very radical, and that he saw radicalism as necessary to produce something like justice. I tend to think that he wanted peace between the Soviet Union and the United States, and that he was trying to play a role in producing peace.

See from 7:59 from here, where he seems to speak positively about Communism (“in the New Deal we didn’t really care if people were Communists or not”; “we thought of ourselves as radicals”; “we certainly didn’t mind being called Communists”):

Is that a kind of smoking gun? No. But it’s smoking.

9

A lot of people hated everything about Alger Hiss. He was Harvard, and he was elegant, and he wore fancy suits. He was charming, but you could be forgiven for thinking that he was full of himself. (I don’t think that’s fair, but still.) He wasn’t exactly a man of the people. He was a member of the elite, big-time. He wasn’t rich, but he sure did seem rich.

Many conservatives focused a lot, and for a long time, on Hiss and his guilt. There is no question that the Hiss Case helped to define modern conservatism. (Again: Read *Witness*.) That thought that his guilt confirmed a large point, or a series of large points, about liberalism, the left, godlessness, disloyalty, otherness, hating America, and patriotism. You could easily draw a straight line from the right-of-center conception of Alger Hiss, in the 40s, 50s, and 60s, to the right-of-center conception of the enemies-on-the-left today.

Now those on the left are not Communists, and they are certainly not Soviet spies. But they are being seen that way, more or less. The music of the Hiss Case, as we might call it, helps explain why.
