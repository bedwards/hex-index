A young red fox appeared outside my back door at the start of July. The mammal was not noticeably wounded, but it was short of breath, moving slowly, and trying to hide. There was a heatwave in London so I offered a bowl of tap water, but I felt like I was missing the point, and not just because foxes prefer rainwater.

Our Spanish octogenarian neighbour, who has seen everything before, said the fox appeared to be poisoned, looked ready to die, and there was nothing we could do except prepare to bury it well. There is no soon-to-be dead-fox modus operandi in my part of the world that I am aware of, if only because foxes exist in a civically ambiguous place. For some, they are exotic urban wildlife, cute undomesticated pets and spiritual allies. But they are also prolific excrementarians who squeal at night and ransack bin bags.

I would have helped if I had known what to do. I imagined myself wrapping the ailing being in a blanket and taking it by public trnsport to a vet, but that seemed vainglorious. I also wondered if we had a spade in the shed if our neighbour was right, and hesitated. The family got diverted by whatever was happening in the kitchen, and the fox momentarily shuffled away from view.

Within an hour, I went out to look, and found the fox a few metres away, lying on its side, no longer breathing. It was behind a bike wheel, near a brick wall that matched its fur. I am not sure I could have done anything to keep it alive, but I felt sad and confounded. Why did that happen? I was not concerned to know what the fox might have eaten or where it had travelled from. I was in a hermeneutic mode, sensing that what I was obliged to witness *meant* something. Did this enigmatic fox really show up on my doorstep to be seen to die, by me, at this particular juncture of my life?

Some readers may relate to that inquiry (thank you for being here) and there are poems and folklore about the symbolism of dying foxes. But I also know how ridiculous, anthropocentric, narcissistic, or proto-psychotic this notion could sound.

*It’s about the poor fox, you conceited fool, it’s not all about you! You think, with all its cunning, this ambassador for Vulpes vulpes, with mobile ears that can rotate independently, has nothing better to do than die at your feet?*

Indeed, I disavowed the meaning of the experience at the time. But it was such a gratuitous, vivid and discomforting event that it kept coming back to me. The fox *became* an emissary of some kind, a portent. Perhaps it was a warning that I should prepare for someone or something dying in my own life. The experience scared me, but I sensed some deeper wisdom was in play.

A few days later, something unexpected entered my life. I can only say that it was distressing and it dominated the summer. That encounter with an ailing fox on the cusp of death stayed with me over that time, and lingers still. I can explain it away, but I don’t have to, and I prefer not to.

While a sceptic might say the meaning of it is *all in my head*, that’s not the case. There was an objective occurence in a shared lifeworld, and I did not experience it as a message to read, a puzzle to solve, or a code to decipher. The meaning of the fox arose from my experience of the beguiling ambiguity of it and the gift-like gratuity of it. It was one event among billions happening at the same time, but it found a place in my psyche as a form of totemic communication from the abundant world beyond it.

To be clear, I do not believe the fox knew me or that it was sent by anyone who knew me. While I cannot rule out the possibility that we are all blessed with personal angels or celestial aunties and uncles operating on our behalf, I am keen not to confuse intellectual humility with intellectual humiliation. It is enough to say that the arrival (and departure) of the fox was profoundly meaningful at the time, and in hindsight, because it coincided with what I was about to go through.

The fox did not appear *be-cause* of anything; the explanation is not causal. I suspect meaning has its own ontological grooves that coexist with causation, rather than being reducible to it. In a mysterious world of processes and events, the living question for all of us is precisely what is coinciding, when, and how, and why. Who is to say there is not a poetic or narrative order to the world, and that *coincidences* are about the intersection of the causal and poetic orders becoming apparent to us. What if these kinds of encounter are cosmic grammar from a mother tongue that we have forgotten how to speak?

This is familiar terrain to those familiar with Jung’s notion of synchronicity - an acausal connecting principle, but we can also take it beyond rareified personal moments and consider what these forms of meaning signify for our collective capacity to survive and thrive in the 21st century. I explored these issues at the end of last year in *["Christmas is a Coincidenc](https://jonathanrowson.substack.com/p/christmas-is-a-coincidence)e.*" I hope to deepen my understanding of this idea over the next few months, and I have noticed a new (for me) term - ‘ontopoetics’, articulated most directly by the Australian deep ecologist [Freya Matthews](https://www.freyamathews.net/full-text-articles).

> Ontopoetics rests on the premise that there exists an inner aspect of reality which is expressed via a communicativity that coexists with but does not over-ride physical causality. If physics is the study of the causal order, then *ontopoetics* may be defined as the study of the poetic order, of the meanings that structure the inner aspect of being. If ecology has in recent decades defined the first phase of the re-negotiation of our modern Western relation to reality, ontopoetics might be integral to the second phase.

I agree with that last line, which is from a 2007 [paper](https://australianhumanitiesreview.org/2007/03/01/an-invitation-to-ontopoetics-the-poetic-structure-of-being/). Ecology is a necessary but not sufficient premise for reimagining our predicament. I believe in the more-than-human world, but I believe it is altogether more weird and wonderful than prevailing ideas of nature.[1](#footnote-1)

**The Forest**

The fox reassured me, as well as discomforting me, but without a forest I would not have known what to do with my disequilibrium. And this was a special forest, a poet’s forest, found in *Lost* by David Wagoner:

> Stand still. The trees ahead and bushes beside you  
> Are not lost. Wherever you are is called Here,  
> And you must treat it as a powerful stranger,  
> Must ask permission to know it and be known.  
> The forest breathes. Listen. It answers,  
> I have made this place around you.  
> If you leave it, you may come back again, saying Here.  
> No two trees are the same to Raven.  
> No two branches are the same to Wren.  
> If what a tree or a bush does is lost on you,  
> You are surely lost. Stand still. The forest knows  
> Where you are. You must let it find you.

The creation story of this poem ([First published in 1971](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poetrymagazine/browse?volume=118&issue=4&page=35)) is about a young boy in an indigenous community asking an elder what he should do if he is (literally) lost in the forest. The forest is also a metaphor of course, and this summer, whenever I felt confused, dismayed or bewildered, I would say to myself:

*“The forest knows where you are. You must let it find you.”*

What makes the climactic line poetic is that it is not *about* a forest; it is about the experience of being alive, especially in moments of precarious turbulence.

I confess I would not have known of *Lost* by David Wagoner if I had not stumbled on a video of David Whyte (Curiously, on the YouTube pages of Jonathan Reams, editor at [Integral Review](https://integral-review.org/)).

I deepened my appreciation for David Whyte’s work more generally this summer, and I am impressed by how he can retrieve hundreds of poems from memory.

I share with [Adam Robbert](https://secondvoice.substack.com/p/the-lost-art-of-memory) and [Ellie Robbins](https://eleanorrobins.substack.com/p/reviving-the-ancient-memory-arts) a belief that in an age of instant access to information and analysis, we risk forsaking memory as a form of self-possession and capacity for insight and imagination. Memory is a kind of fuel for all our capacities. It is precisely because we don’t appear to have to remember as much as we used to at a functional level, that we should make the effort to do so for cognition, culture, sensibility and even resistance.

**The Frogs**

Finally, the frogs. A few days after [Naduve](https://jonathanrowson.substack.com/p/listen-to-the-drumming-from-the-other), in India, which I described in my last post, I found myself in Cochin, Kerala. I had been invited to give a lecture at Chinmaya University. The night before, just after dinner, I was sitting in the associated ashram talking with my mother-in-law, when I noticed a heavenly sound, the likes of which I had never heard before.

It was the sound of a frog chorus, and it was just a few feet away, in a courtyard pond inside the ashram that is open to the elements from above. Again, there is some risk of projection, but it sounded so palpably like the frogs were overflowing with delight in the rain. My old iPhone can’t do it justice, but this clip gives you the idea:

There are poems and songs about the phenomenon in South India, because when it rains, so they say, the frogs cannot help but sing in celebration, even though they are thereby more likely to attract snakes, and die. No doubt singing has some adaptive value too, but this idea of “sing, even if it kills you” felt sublime.

We are all hunted in some way, if only by the inevitability of our deaths. No matter who or what is hunting us, we don’t have to hide. The frog chorus reminded me that joy is our nature, and life is as beautiful as it is short.

May we all gift each other the courage to continue singing in the rain.

It is more like a signpost than a sign; more like an epistemic party atmosphere that attracts related ideas, than a systematic theory that organises them. Within a week or so I hope to finish and publish a post [over on Perspectiva](https://perspecteeva.substack.com/subscribe) that gives some sense of the contours of ontopoetics from a range of different sources.
