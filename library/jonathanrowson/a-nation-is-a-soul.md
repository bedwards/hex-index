“A nation is a soul, a spiritual principle. Two things which, properly speaking, are really one and the same constitute this soul... One is the past, the other is the present.”

\- Ernest Renan, 1882

Welcome to December. While some American readers are still recovering from Thanksgiving, and Advent calendars open the runway to Christmas, I felt moved to mark a lesser-known moment in the calendar - a public holiday in my home country of Scotland called St.Andrew’s Day. (November 30th was a Sunday, so the public holiday is today).

I just enjoyed watching an earnest [BBC Newsround video](https://www.bbc.co.uk/newsround/20556587) suggesting five million Scots will all be eating Cullen Skink and going for Scottish country dancing tonight. The truth is that St. Andrew’s is mostly ignored, and certainly less observed than Burns Night or Hogmanay (New Year’s Eve). Some banks and Schools don’t even recognise St. Andrew’s Day as a public holiday.

And yet, maybe we should take these days more seriously. Who wants unstructured time? How many mere *Mondays* do we have to put up with? I like the alliance of gonzo history, makeshift mythology, and collective identity. Is it not human to make things up before making them real?

The Buddhist cognitive scientist Evan Thompson puts it like this:

> Although some illusions are constructions, not all constructions are illusions.

St Andrew’s Day is a construction, but not an illusion, because Scotland is a nation, and nations are real. More to the point, nations have a certain kind of reality that is worth attending to today, and every day. In my work for [Perspectiva](https://perspecteeva.substack.com/p/perspectiva-in-10-premises), we question the viability of the nation state, because it’s a modern institution called into question by the spiritual and material exhaustion of modernity known as the [metacrisis](https://youtu.be/IjOQB608ylQ?si=Y71mVpezbYJS8pam).

To survive the 21st century, we may need to transcend and include the nation-state, but that makes it all the more important to understand the nation as such. There is no intelligent doubt that nations exist, nor that Scotland is a nation, and most Scots, regardless of their views on independence, feel this to be true and important. What is legitimately doubted and contested, however, is how clear the idea of nation is, and how much nations matter - morally, culturally, educationally, politically, and legally.

[Third horizon thinking](https://perspecteeva.substack.com/p/deactivating-the-h2minus-vortex) features different patterns of polycentric governance, perhaps a technologically wise global commons, shaped by a planetary ethos and ethic, informed by cosmolocal practice, grounded in bioregional governance, and the best forms of cryptocurrency. No, I don’t know quite what that means either, but it is not at all clear that the best way to govern eight-plus billion people in a time of ecological crisis is with about 200 nation-states of varying size and influence and a dysfunctional United Nations. With that in mind, I am interested in the teasing apart of nation and state that Scotland speaks to, while also recognising that national identity is closely tied to access to political institutions. Somehow, and I am not sure how, we may need to better ground ourselves in complex and layered national identities within a planetary identity, to retain and build forms of collective solidarity and political agency that might allow us to resist the slow-burning hell of algorithmic capture.

The ‘national’ in national identity is not primarily geographic in nature, and it is rarely singular. For my part, I’m a Scottish Londoner. That’s the kind of Scot I am, and that’s the kind of Londoner I am. Scotland is my country, London is my city, and I love and belong to them both. They say that when someone from Ireland lives away from Ireland, they become more Irish. In recent years, it has felt to me that if you’re Scottish, the opposite might be true because the civic nationalism focuses on who lives in Scotland, rather than who comes from there or identifies with it. But I don’t dwell on that, not least because I’m [European](https://www.opendemocracy.net/en/opendemocracyuk/deeper-into-democracy-legitimacy-of-challenging-brexit-s-majoritarian-mandate/) and [Indian](https://open.substack.com/pub/jonathanrowson/p/listen-to-the-drumming-from-the-other?r=1686d&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false) too.

The point is that the things we can look forward to in terms of political hope are more likely to come to pass if we better understand the work that ‘nation’ does for us, which could well survive the eclipse of the current form of the nation-state.

In this sense, St Andrew’s Day is universal, and futuristic.

\*\*

When I grow up, I want to be a patron saint. Yes, I’m joking, but patron saints are the true tech bros. They offer an identity technology that turns geography into personality and signals excellence in both material (patron) and spiritual (saint) spheres of life.

St. Andrew is the patron saint of Scotland. As one of Jesus’s twelve disciples and Peter’s brother, Andrew is the source energy of the parallel Pope that time forgot. He never set foot in Scotland, and he is claimed by many other countries, including Russia, so the history surrounding his Scottishness is cheeringly nebulous.

Nonetheless, he is apparently our inspiration, guardian and protector. I paraphrase ever so slightly, but legend has it that in the year nine hundred oat cake, a king called Angus had a dream on the eve of a battle with England in which Andrew appeared and said, in effect: “Good Luck, wee man.”

History should not be confused with legend and myth, but Angus was more likely Óengus, a leader of Picts fighting with/as Celts, and not against England as such, but more likely a combination of Northumbrians and Angles. The claim is that the night before battle, an apparition of Andrew told Óengus to look for his sign, and the next day a white cloud shaped as a decussate cross appeared; and so the proto-Scots were roused to victory.

(I just learned the word *decussate* and wanted to use it immediately. It’s a word for X, derived from the Roman numeral for ten. How curious in the post-Twitter age.)

To define ‘nation’ is to commit a political act. The [etymology](https://www.etymonline.com/word/nation) relates to birth, and that permeates many of the attempts at definitions, though it also feels like the idea of nation gradually outgrows them all. In the history of political thought, we have nation-as-language, nation-as-geology, nation-as-ethnicity, nation-as-folk rituals, nation-as-historical event, nation-as-territory, and nation-as-constitution. All these definitions (and more) are part of the context of nationhood, and you need that context, but each of them alone is easily refuted by examples of nations that don’t fit a tight conceptual straightjacket.

Taken together, however, these social and material assemblages are part of *the setting* in the play of nationhood. But they are not the plot; they are not what the nation is *about*. The setting makes the plot possible, but it’s the plot where the nation finds and creates itself. A nation is a true fiction in which we can be ourselves in its setting and become ourselves through its plot.

This shift of emphasis makes the nation a kind of political incubator (Indra Adnan’s term) because the national polity is an expression of both our cultural belonging and our connection with the world beyond, and is therefore the arena in which our agency is most clearly felt and known. In recent years, the emphasis has therefore been on nation-as-soul, nation-as-social construction, and nation-as-practice.

In 1882, Ernest Renan said: “A nation is a soul, a spiritual principle. Two things which, properly speaking, are really one and the same constitute this soul, this spiritual principle. One is the past, the other is the present.”

Also well-known is Benedict Anderson’s idea of ‘imagined communities’, which suggests that we either receive or *seek out* collective identity for ourselves, and we can feel a kind of solidarity and fraternity with people in our imagined community that we don’t actually know.

In *Honey from the Lion,* Doug Gay extends on this idea to say that nationalism is ‘a claim to identity on behalf of a population’ and quotes Oliver Donovan, “to see ourselves as a people is an act of moral imagination”.

For these reasons, Rogers Brubaker’s contention “Nation is a category of practice, not (in the first instance) of analysis”, is a critical thing to grasp. Brubaker goes on: “To understand nationalism, we have to understand the practical uses of the category ‘nation’, the ways in which it can come to structure perception, to inform thought and experience, to organise discourse and political action.”

Nationalism is legion, often harmful, and it manifests in millions of ways over thousands of years. My point is not that we should all become nationalists, but there is intellectual dignity in the kind of nationalism that is premised on two reasonable ideas: There are such things as nations, and nations should govern themselves. The reason there are so many nationalisms, and not all of them are benign, is that people disagree about what exactly makes a nation real, and people vary on the extent to which the moral force of ‘*should* govern themselves’ moves them to action.

I remember hearing in primary school (in Aberdeen) that Scotland’s Saltire flag is a diagonal cross rather than a vertical cross because when he died, apparently in Greece, Andrew did not want to imply he had the same stature as Jesus, and chose the diagonal cross through humility. When he became our patron saint, this manifested as the symbol for our flag. There is evidence of a seal of the “Guardians of Scotland”, dated 1286, that shows the crucified figure of Saint Andrew on a decussate cross. St. Andrew is also mentioned by Scottish nobles writing to the Pope seeking international recognition for Scotland as a distinct nation in the [Declaration of Arbroath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_Arbroath) in 1320.

In that declaration, Andrew is described as the first apostle. This may or may not be historically true, but in the Gospel of John (1:35–42), the claim is that John the Baptist points out Jesus to Andrew and says, “This is the lamb of God”. Andrew then sought out his brother Peter to tell him about it. The other gospels don’t give Andrew this starring role, but Scotland’s founding document holds to this idea that our patron saint, Andrew, “heard it first”.

One can make too many associations on too many tentative premises, but insofar as Scotland has a distinctive national identity, there is something of the legend of St Andrew in it, perhaps his spirit of sacrifice, but mostly in the humility of our relatively egalitarian outlook. After all, as our national poet put it: “[A Man’s a Man for a’ that.](https://www.scottishpoetrylibrary.org.uk/poem/mans-man-0/)”

I’ll end by looking backwards. A full history of Scotland is well beyond our scope, even on St. Andrew’s day. But if we are going to allow ourselves to feel the reality of nations and their attendant cultural influence and political claims, it helps to appreciate there really is a *full* history. Feeling the enduring influence of those years, with time as a kind of marinade, serves to highlight both where borders and national distinctions come from, and why they can be at once real and yet also contingent and contested. That conversation matters as we think about the future.

I beg the forgiveness of proper historians for what follows, which is copied from my post on [Scotland’s Political Culture](https://jonathanrowson.substack.com/p/reflections-on-scotlands-political-5da) from 2023.

> The history of Scotland would ideally start with the big bang (our genesis) followed by the formation of the solar system (our celestial inheritance) the geology of planet earth’s surface (our islands), the effects of its periodic glaciations (our highlands and lowlands), and the evolution of life forms leading eventually to humans (our people). We’d need some archeology about nomadic lifestyles and early tool use, some anthropology about kinship and human group formation, early religious rituals, and tribal customs; then we’d want something very carefully evidenced about neolithic, bronze, and Iron age ethnicities, migration patterns, and - in modern parlance - hostile takeover bids and merger and acquisition stories relating to Gaels, Picts, Celts, Saxons, Britons, Angles, Romans, Vikings, and Normans. Then, just as you were running out of steam because it all seems too far removed from the present-day news cycle about the election of Scotland’s First Minister, you’d learn of places that came to be known as *Britannia* and *Caledonia* about two thousand years ago when the first contours of our modern-day England and Scotland distinction appear. There are many embattled lands at this point, mostly around Northumberland, Carlisle, and Berwick, and plenty of division, but there’s some unity and periodic stability too, including the establishment of The Kingdom of Scotland in AD 843 which predates the Kingdom of England in 927AD. Then there was something like a four-hundred-year stooshie (argument) followed by a decisive Battle of Bannockburn in 1314 (you may remember the line from the film *Braveheart*: “They fought like Scotsmen, and won their freedom”). [The Declaration of Arbroath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_Arbroath) in 1320 followed, and that was an important moment in the Scottish national psyche because it effectively says to the international community (not least the Pope) - *we are a distinct and independent nation*, and by 1328 Scotland had its own parliament. Then there were the Stuart monarchs, and The Protestant Reformation which is perhaps relevant for the latter (contested) claim that Scots have been encultured into being more egalitarian than the English; but the active encouragement to read the Bible *did* lead to the educational impetus for *everyone* to learn more generally. Then another big moment, the Union of the Crowns in 1603 when a Scottish King, James VI, inherits the English throne and the prefigurative contours of the UK appeared. There follows some more religious tension, the Wars of the Three Kingdoms (including Ireland) and Cromwell vanquishing Scotland. King Charles II, who was the precursor of present-day King Charles III(!) restored Scotland as fully independent in 1660. Then, after a few decades of James VII, Catholicism tried but mostly failed to reestablish itself, and there were Jacobite rebellions. Amidst all this religion, monarchy, and war it feels like narrative relief to encounter… economics. The Bank of Scotland was created in 1695 yet the notorious Darien Scheme in which Scottish nobility sought to create Scotland’s own colony in Panama needed and did not receive the requested investment from England, so capital was raised from within the Scottish population; but this proved to be a ruinous venture, leaving Scotland bankrupt and dependent on trade with England. Then, the biggest moment of all, The Union of Parliaments in 1707 was agreed upon by the Scottish parliament but with possible bribery and without popular consent. The Act of Union is therefore often viewed as a betrayal - a famous Burns-inspired song of lamentation includes the line “We’re bought and sold for English gold-Such a parcel of rogues in a nation!”. However, some saw it as the necessary political solution to a deep economic problem (sounds familiar!) and in that sense, you could say it worked. Scotland kept its own legal system and church and the union arguably created the socio-economic and institutional conditions that paved the way for the Scottish Enlightenment of the 1750s. And the union was really strong for a long time, held together through a common purpose and identity beginning with the British empire (Scots were British colonisers) and the industrial revolution (Scots were British capitalists) and then latterly the two world wars (Scots were British soldiers). In the post-war aftermath the UK was still a global power and the national story was told through institutions of the NHS and the BBC, while the nationalisation of industries and services created a sense of pan-UK enterprise.

I share this potted history to highlight that the competing claims of Scotland being a distinct nation and Scotland being an integral part of the UK are both legitimate, and also to indicate just how ridiculous the whole thing can seem.

In 2014, I was heavily involved in the Yes campaign for Scottish Independence, and outlined my case for an [article in The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2014/jun/09/scotland-refendum-yes-economy-confidence) where I wrote:

> Our psyches are strengthened when we can see our own hand in the world that is being built around us. In a politically independent Scotland, ideals of a better society may or may not be more credible, but they are literally more fashionable.

I am not certain I feel the same way now. In fact, I feel more intensely now something I wrote about that experience in [2023](https://jonathanrowson.substack.com/p/reflections-on-scotlands-political), where I reflected on the memory of coming to terms with Scotland’s decision to say no, or at least not yet, in the small hours after the vote.

> I walked back to my hotel room via a deserted and drizzly Royal Mile at about 4.30 AM. I was disappointed, but it also felt surreal. It’s not that I was pretending, but I was somehow performing myself to myself. Looking back, part of me was clearly swept up in a narrative much bigger than myself…I do not disavow the thoughts and feelings I had at the time, but I can’t say with confidence that I really knew what I was doing. I took the train home to London the next day.

On *BBC Question Time,* a few days later, the broadcaster [Lesley Riddoch](https://youtu.be/UyQdmVSQ-TI?t=1088) spoke for me and many others when she said she acknowledged the result but qualified that with a remark that went much deeper:

> The level of activism, the commitment, the imagination, the friendship, the camaraderie…*It was the best year of my life*; from the point of view of the humanity and optimism that was generated. If you were a part of that… It’s so precious, it’s so unusual, that you really feel you do not want to see that go. Particularly younger people, older people, people in estates, people who are not usually involved.

The campaign for Scottish independence can be seen in many ways, but I experienced it in terms of what Roberto Unger calls the need to [spiritualise politics](https://www.prospectmagazine.co.uk/arts-and-books/why-our-politics-needs-to-be-more-spiritual) by ‘diminishing the distance between self and society.’

The world has changed. I can’t imagine caring as much about Scottish independence as I did then, when there is so much else going on. But I deeply loved the experience of campaigning at the time, and it has shaped me forever, as indicated in two of my first posts on The Joyous Struggle.

Parts 3 and 4 are scheduled for whenever Scottish politics calls me again.

What I can say for now is that I recently sat with my sons at home in London, turned on BBC iPlayer and found BBC Scotland, where the live match of Scotland v Denmark was showing. It was a truly amazing game, with some extraordinary goals from Scotland, allowing the team to qualify for the World Cup for the first time since 1998.

I confess, I did not think about St. Andrew, but for a couple of hours, I was glad to be Scottish and to feel the nation as part of my soul.
