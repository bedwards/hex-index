If you want to buy a pet, you should go to a pet shop. If you want to buy a pet shop, you should go to a pet shop shop. And if you want to buy a pet shop shop shop…well, that’s just silly.

A comedian said this somewhere. I wish I could trace the source because it’s brilliant in its simple advice to refrain from getting carried away. The mirth arises partly from the absurdity of having pet shops in the first place; places where consumerism shapes the relationship between humans and the rest of nature by domesticating socially acceptable aspects of it, while hiding its shadow, for instance, with stacks of frozen chicken packed in the aisles of the supermarket next door. Pets, almost by definition, are denatured nature, which is not to say humans are not part of nature, nor that domestication is not a natural process, nor that my family shouldn’t get a dog (we’re thinking about it). And there’s a subversive dig at financial capitalism in there too, because doubtless there are investors in pet shop consortia, but they almost certainly don’t work in shops, as such. So the joke also speaks to the unconscious grief of delocalisation, the long arms of rentier capitalism, and the perennial story of how power hides itself.

The statement comes to mind now, on being invited to join the editorial board at Integration - The Journal of Big Picture Theory and Practice, hosted by the relatively new Institute of Applied Metatheory. I am grateful for the invitation, and earlier today I accepted it. This post is about ‘showing my working’, including my hesitation, and the title of the post is a good excuse for a nostalgic sharing of an old Brian May/Queen song: Too Much Love Will Kill You, which includes the lyric:

Too much love will kill you,
Just as sure as none at all,
It’ll drain the power that’s in you,
Make you plead and scream and crawl…

I believe the same might be said of metatheory.

**

We should not be too quick or too conclusive in our understanding of what theory is, because that’s part of the task of metatheory. In general, I’d say something like: a theory is a system of interrelated ideas that help us to perceive, describe, and explain a phenomenon. Those ideas might be concepts or frameworks, and they may include diagrams or new forms of language, but ‘ideas’ covers all of that; and this applies to science, social science, and practice. Theories are lenses that help us see patterns.

There is, of course, discipline-specific theory that has made its way into general awareness, such as theories of relativity and quantum theory in physics, the theory of evolution in biology, and cognitive dissonance theory in psychology. There are also domain-general theories like complexity theory, chaos theory, or systems theory that are informed by science but apply beyond it. In economics, theory is used to explain how markets work and fail; in politics, theory informs our understanding of how power, authority, and governance are organised and justified; and in sociology, theory explains how social structures (eg class, race, gender), meanings(eg the idea of happiness) and interactions (eg identity politics) shape human life. But theory is also invoked every day to explain the behaviour of friends and colleagues: “I have a theory about why X and Y don’t like each other”. This is all just scratching the surface, but the point is that theory is ubiquitous, whether you like it or not. Theory can be edifying and liberating, or dense and inscrutable, and it is often both. The variable that counts is probably how much you need it, choose to attend to it, understand it, and even feel affection towards it.

In Theory as Liberatory Practice, Bell Hooks wrote:

I came to theory because I was hurting... I came to theory desperate, wanting to comprehend... I saw in theory then a location for healing.

**

Theory is a way of seeing, a way of knowing, a way of reorienting, and therefore also a way of feeling. Our relationship to it can be existential, or a matter of survival, but it can also be valued as part of the curriculum of normal life, captured in the famous (if dated/gendered) lines of John Maynard Keynes:

Practical men, who believe themselves to be quite exempt from any intellectual influence, are usually the slaves of some defunct economist. Madmen in authority, who hear voices in the air, are distilling their frenzy from some academic scribbler of a few years back.

So it’s not enough for those concerned with good practice to say “you need a bit of theory” as if to appease the intellect and hope for the best. Theories are everywhere, often invisible, overlapping, competing, essential, practical, more or less consciously applied, and of varying quality. And theory is very hard to do well. Most people don’t make theory, and not everyone can make theory - it’s a form of expertise. Humans need theory to live almost as much as they need clean air and safe drinking water. That may seem far-fetched, but theory manifests in practice as ritual, myth, navigation, and farming practices.

Theory without action may be powerless, but action without theory is blind. Kurt Lewin’s statement that "nothing is as practical as a good theory" originates from his work in organisational psychology in the mid-20th century, as does the practice of action research, in which theory and practice are in a continuous, iterative loop (my hope for the antidebate). Feminism co-arose with feminist theory, and there have been several waves of it, and subtypes of it (Can Feminism be African?). Theories of learning shape education. Theories of health shape medicine. On it goes. Theory is less like a book on the shelf and more like the foundations and materials of the building. (I just remembered Vanessa Andreotti speaks of the house that modernity built).

Beware the person with one conceptual model. Note that Lewin said nothing is as practical as a good theory, not any theory at all, which begs the question of what makes for a good theory. Theories are not always helpful or appropriately applied, and they often have biases or blind spots. With numerous theories available from diverse domains and disciplines, and with theories created for all kinds of purposes, there is also a place for theories of theories. The idea is that just as theory is a system of interrelated ideas to perceive, describe and explain phenomena, sometimes - perhaps even often - the phenomenon in question is theory as such.

For example, if you ask: How do you explain why people appear to have different levels of intelligence? Your answer will typically be a theory of intelligence. That theory will define intelligence and may inform and be informed by a method to measure it. However, it will also be packed full of assumptions derived from academic conventions that are shaped by paradigmatic understanding - by which I mean the theory will be institutionally embedded in a way that does not allow it to see its own eyes. Hidden inside disciplinary conventions are worldviews, and those worldviews have tacit ontologies (what exists), epistemologies (what it means to know) and, even more subtly, but usually present, axiologies (what is valued and why).

So a theory might say: intelligence is brain-based, entirely genetic and fixed, and can be measured in a psychometric test to give you an ‘intelligence quotient’ score known as IQ; some may go further to say your IQ level will and perhaps should determine your station in life. But that kind of theory - Terman and Spearman style - is informed by reductionism as an explanatory method, likely a naive realist and materialist ontology, without any apparent reflexive awareness of itself in the sociology of science. So is that a good theory to inform practice if you are, say, a parent or a teacher in 2025? No, it’s far too limited and limiting. Over a century ago, the unkindly named Edwin Boring stated that IQ is merely “what the tests test”. In other words, intelligence often has a circular definition of being that which is defined by an intelligence test.

Then the question is why the test has some questions rather than others, and who put them there and why - and this is an example of the terrain where Minna Salami’s coinage Europatriachal Knowledge’ is pertinent - it’s not that there is no place for psychometric tests at all, but they serve some forms of knowing more than others, those forms of knowing serve existing power arrangements, and it’s ignorant to think the IQ prism, for instance, is the only way to grasp the much wider notion of intelligence, and naive to think it doesn’t matter.

So off we trot, and we may then find another theory that views intelligence - Piaget style - as an adaptation to different kinds of environments and that develops over time, another - Andy Clarke style - with a larger canvas for cognition in the mind-body-world interface, and another - Howard Gardner style - that says we do not have one intelligence but several, and another - Stephen Jay Gould or even Foucault style - that says the notion of intelligence is socially constructed, shapes power relations and scarce resources, and it is therefore unwise and unfair to have a canonical definition or method to assess it.

At this point, it would be normal to feel overwhelmed, and this feeling is not specific to intelligence theory. I could give other examples. For instance, a recent article in The New Scientist indicated there are 350(!) theories of consciousness, and that each of them implies a different kind of worldview, and even an implicit politics. Please see and play with the extraordinary resource called Landscapes of Consciousness, created by Robert Lawrence Kuhn and Alex Gomez Marin, to feel how a background theory shapes other things.

The point is that theory itself is a phenomenon. It is not just something we use to explain other things, but something we live and breathe, which itself needs explaining. There is theory-informed practice, practice-informed theory, there is the practice of theorising, and there is the impact of applied theory in practice, which is then theorised for future practice. Then there is the term ‘praxis’ which has a rich history but can be thought of as a kind of indefinite reflexive yo-yo that goes theory-practice-theory-practice-theory…but then if two things are so inextricably linked, at what point does the distinction between them break down?

There is a saying that there is no difference between theory and practice in theory, but there is in practice, but that’s not entirely true either - theory these days is wise to the predictable deviance of practice. And as David Jean Rook put it to me in a personal communication, the trouble with the often quoted saying that “the map is not the territory” is that the territory is full of maps.

It’s all a little confusing. I can see why people turn away. But some feel called to look closer into what it might mean to have theories of theories, to better inform practice.

Ken Wilber is perhaps the best-known metatheorist, and both A Theory of Everything(2000) and An Integral Vision(2007) are good primers. From my own reading, I can also recommend Zak Stein's book chapter Beyond nature and humanity: Reflections on the emergence and purposes of metatheories (2015) and Paul Marshall’s book A Complex Integral Realist Perspective (2016) as good places to start. Bonnitta Roy is also an excellent metatheorist, and from several possible references, I’d highlight one that is relatively applied - Releasing Complexity (2018) and relatively abstract - Why Metaphysics Matters (2019). I never met Roy Bhaskar and don’t know his work as well as I know Wilber's, but I remember Zak Stein telling me about Bhaskar’s presence and the gravity of his intellectual purpose, which made an impact on him. Bhaskar himself did not speak of his work in terms of icebergs, but they are used (for instance, in the illustration by B.J. Anderson in a paper by M.D Smith1) to illustrate it. In his early work, Bhaskar was most concerned with science:

The domain of the real is the realm of objects, their structures and powers, and the mechanisms they constitute. The domain of the actual consists of what happens when these powers are activated. The domain of the empirical consists of experiences and observations of these events. (A Realist Theory of Science, 1975).

That sounds simple, but it’s quite explosive. To distinguish between underlying powers, events, and measurements is already the basis for metatheory, because each of these things has different ways of being and calls for different ways of knowing. It’s a way of saying that since reality has layers, science has to have layers too. However, we might also say that while mere theory is concerned with making sense of the relationship between the actual and the empirical, metatheory is more invested in making sense of the real, and what follows from grasping that how we conceive of ‘the real’ has real effects. Indeed, in his middle period, Bhaskar links perception of the real, to being, to ontology, to collective liberation:

The world must be understood as an open, stratified, differentiated totality, in which possibilities and potentials are real and in which human beings, as causal agents, have the capacity to transform the structures that condition them.”
(Dialectic: The Pulse of Freedom, p. 4)

This statement is another way of articulating David Graeber’s famous statement in The Utopia of Rules (2015): “The ultimate hidden truth of the world is that it is something that we make, and could just as easily make differently.” One of Bhaskar’s major contributions is to see ontology and emancipation as two sides of the same (real) coin.

Freedom consists in the transformation of the structures that constrain us.
Emancipation is the process of absenting absences — of removing the constraints, blockages, and absences that prevent the flourishing of being.”
(Dialectic: The Pulse of Freedom, p. 133)

That is not so different from Campagna’s case for a new cosmogony, which I wrote about last week.

In his later work, Bhaskar developed a philosophy of metareality, which was an explicitly spiritual, even pointing towards something deeper than ‘the real’:

“Critical realism has always been a depth realism, affirming that reality is deeper than our experience of it. The philosophy of metaReality deepens this affirmation, recognising that the deepest level of reality is the ground of our being — the pulse of freedom, the ground of love.” - Reflections on MetaReality (2002):

The pulse of freedom, the ground of love, the hair. Who could argue with that?

I sometimes call myself an applied philosopher, but professionally, I am more like an applied metatheorist. I can see this trait at least as far back as my PhD thesis on wisdom, which I submitted at Bristol University in late 2008. I started by asking what ‘proto-sagacity’ in adolescence might look like, and naively planned a randomised empirical study, which I detailed well enough to get three years of funding from the ESRC. But then I noticed I could not adequately ‘operationalise’ wisdom in a way that would make sense of its precursors in young people. Compared to intelligence and creativity and knowledge, the concept of wisdom was (and still is) undertheorised. I began to see why; namely that the notion of wisdom and wise action goes beyond description and explanation towards tacit theories of the meaning of life; it pushes boundaries of prevailing ontologies, epistemologies and axiologies - of what there is, how we know, and why we care. I have yet to publish this work, but Integral Review gave it some attention in 2015 as a summary, and as an interview.

A little later at the RSA, I responded to the popularity of Nudge (behaviour change theory) and to ambient neuoromania (the use and abuse of neuroscience) with a report called Transforming Behaviour Change in 2011. It was not just another theory of behaviour change, but a critical inquiry into whether ‘behaviour’ is the appropriate unit of analysis, what kind of ‘change’ we really seek, and what, if anything, the brain has to do with it. The report includes the following box, which summarises evidence from a variety of disciplines for the theory that our brains are inherently ‘social’: I was already thinking in terms of theories of theories.

The public engagement project into spirituality (2012-2014) was a kind of tacit plea for applied metatheory - a way of asking the public policy community I was part of: what do you think you are doing and why? But let’s back up, and remember the invitation to join the editorial board of Integration, the journal of big picture theory and practice. I have two concerns.

I am joining an editorial board of ten fabulous thinkers and in recent months I have felt indebted to the excellent scholarship of Jason Ananda Josephon Storm and Matthew David Segall especially. Does it matter that we are all Anglo-Saxon white guys?

What?!? Yes! Of course it matters. It’s a kind of epistemic scandal. How can you possibly claim to be into big picture anything if everyone, or almost everyone, on your editorial board has a similar body, a similar training, and a similar history of power?

I nearly said no for this reason, but then I remembered a time, in the darkest depths of the pandemic, when Peter Limberg(er) invited me on the newly formed vitalising juggernaut that is The Stoa, and I responded by saying I was grateful, but wouldn’t that make me the tenth or so male speaker in a row? I don’t think Peter minded me making the point, but I did not make it well, and he rightly offered me a digital equivalent of a punch on the nose. We soon made up, and I went on the show, but I remember those mixed feelings.

I have written about this kind of challenge before and it tends to get me into trouble. Maybe this is not my issue to carry? Maybe I don’t have to virtue signal on this point every time, even if I do see the problem very clearly. Like maths and physics, as a domain of inqury metatheory leans heavily male, and perhaps also heavily white, but that probably means we are not doing it right. In any case, I decided that in this case I did not want to deprive myself the chance to work with people I like and admire, and geek out with the bros; nor to make my qualms a decisive reason not to support an endeavour I believe in. I will keep the details private, but I will be making the case for the incoherence of a big picture offering that begins from such a narrow range of epistemic sensibilities, no matter how sophisticated. And I am joining on the condition that serious and swift attention is given to the composition of the board.

Second, just as there is more than one theory, there is more than one metatheory, so I understand why people want to see how metatheories connect and inform each other. However, if we start going meta on our metatheories as if creating a premise for action rather than just enjoying an intellectual game, we risk living in the map and losing sight of the world. Sometimes, too deep is too much. Inferences are not always well-served by allowing them to be infinite. Good parenting is largely about knowing when to say no. It is inhibition as much as connection that makes the brain work well. Theory is for meaning and for practice, and metatheory is about ensuring we put theory in enough perspective to help it serve practical and ethical purposes, but let’s be careful. If our aim in life becomes the equivalent of buying a pet shop shop shop, the original desire, a relationship with another being, is lost.
