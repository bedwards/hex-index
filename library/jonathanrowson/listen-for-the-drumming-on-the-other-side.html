I know I said I wouldn’t write here until September, but if I apologise in the vernacular of a newfound friend, you might forgive me: sometimes words misbehave, they form a mutiny against their incarceration, congregate without permission in the fingertips, and become fugitive…

I am in Bangalore, India. I feel privileged to have been a founding participant in Naduve, which took place from July 12 to 15. Naduve means ‘in between’ in the local language here, Kannada. But I didn’t find it in between, I found it right on, and it felt like the centre of the universe for a few days.

I can share two versions of what I just experienced, the first in descriptive professional jargon, the second as personal testimony.

Naduve is an innovative retreat, training and networking initiative for scholars, activists and artists from the global majority. The first event in India was made financially possible by a response to Bayo Akomolafe’s call for paraphilanthropy, which is about supporting “fugitivity, a longing for the non-legible, and support for social experimentation within undercultures of practice”. The gathering was inspired by the theory, practice and teaching of the Dancing with Mountains Collective, Madhulika Banerjee’s scholarship on the Politics of Knowledge, and it was co-created/curated by Cambridge academic Tobias Müller, mission steward, network weaver and late night dancer Ishaan Aggarwal, and organised collaboratively by a youthful team of ‘rythmn keepers’. 1

The participants were civil society leaders from all over India and others with layered identities from the US, the UK, and South Africa. Civil society here can be understood as neither business nor government, but the shrinking spaces of education, media, ecology, culture, and activism, where people seeking a different kind of world can support each other. The gathering features a multi-modal programme of ideas, music, art, dance and ritual, but the schedule is fluid and emergent. The event took place at The School of Ancient Wisdom in Bangalore, which is an enigmatic place with theosophical influences, good rasam, thin cats, chubby fish, and a statue of a Prometheus-like light-bearer at the centre…

I’m now writing this from my the home of my in-laws, the day after, a few miles away.

I find myself asking: What was that?

For me, Naduve was an unexpected invitation twelve days before the event, and just enough familiarity with the place and some participants to see myself there. The process began with the creation of an altar upon which we were all asked to share an artefact that spoke to who we are and why we were there; I shared my “person of Indian origin” card and the strange experience of changing my name to get into a temple.2 Naduve was forty-two participants, including ten organisers and volunteers - as far as I could tell, with the possible exception of myself, they were all wonderful people doing exacting work. Each of us seemed seasoned by the experience of what Joan Baez once famously called “little victories and big defeats”.3

Naduve was lots of sitting in circles and a fair amount of dancing- some structured, some improvised, some bollywoodesque. It was one of those places where people share openly with strangers who don’t feel like strangers, and whatever we are going through, we momentarily feel like we are going through it together.

Naduve was also a charming constellation of presences. I cannot share them all, but they included the breeze in the trees, a visionary intellectual who made plenty of space for others, a sublime singer - Shruthi Vishwanath - who regularly stilled our minds and quickened our hearts, an irreverent improvisor, an artist sent by the moon, a friendly Brazilian giant, a shaman with a water dragon, a baby with a microphone, and much else besides.

It was…magical.

I notice I do not want to over-describe the experience of the event nor over-share photographs and videos, because that would denature the intimacy that made the event so precious, to me at least. The spirit of Naduve found us, and moved between us and for us, staying with us at the end, and now I imagine most of us carry it still.

The poet John Keats famously said that “Nothing becomes real until it is experienced” and something became real at Naduve, but what exactly?

**

The substance was part of the setting, not part of the plot. The increasingly authoritarian context of India loomed large, and while ecocide and genocide were only briefly mentioned by name, some spoke of the major flood risks to millions of people living in Mumbai and Kolkata, many shared the challenge of idealism in the context of Gaza, and we all spoke with shared knowledge of the world beyond our temporary sanctuary. Everyone seemed to be carrying some kind of wound, but there was ambient maturity too, and tears flowed without sentimentality.

Decoloniality was a major theme, and though I was warmly welcome, I could not help but occasionally feel implicated. There was a connection between ways of knowing that give rise to models of how we should gather, and Naduve’s desire to do it differently. I did notice some overlaps with The Realisation Festival that I am involved with, but this event was smaller, younger, and it speaks from and for a different part of the world.

Bayo apologised for giving a PowerPoint presentation, but I was glad of it at the time, because I confess there were moments where the mind was jealous of the soul. The event invitation mentioned pervasive “epistemic fatigue” and Bayo spoke of the crisis of knowledge, particularly in the context of large language models that are now ‘in the bloodstream’ of the world. There was a lot of talk about going to where the system is misbehaving and breaking down of its own accord, and lots of talk of finding the ‘cracks’ where insurgency occurs and hope arises. At one point, I was reminded of Tupac Shakur’s line:

Long live the rose that grew from concrete
when no one else ever cared.

*

Mostly, I was eager to join in with whatever was on offer. As a rule, I chime with the wish of the country singer Lee Ann Womack: “When you get the choice to sit it out or dance, I hope you dance.” But I confess that at one moment I grew weary of collective practices and decided to have a cup of tea and a Good Day biscuit on a bench outside, while listening to everyone else laugh inside. I told myself it was a different kind of dancing.

More generally, the event is ripe for satire, with one participant joking that it could be seen as an activist/scholar equivalent of Triangle of Sadness that satirises the indulgences of the super-rich. There were certainly moments where I looked at people lying down and zoning out, or giving each other massages, and I found myself imagining how this would look to someone in acute suffering or even someone with a more conservative mindset.

Was this progressive therapeutic indulgence masquerading as spiritual innovation?

Did we hug ourselves back into cosmic significance?

Were we all just high on oxytocin?

I don’t think so. I truly believe something important was revealed at Naduve, and here is roughly how I would build the case:

*

It starts with the closing scene from Don’t Look UP, where the asteroid, widely thought to be a metaphor for ecological collapse, is just about to hit planet Earth, and the character played by Leonardo De Caprio says: “We really did have everything, didn’t we?” (2.40).

In essence, Naduve was a reminder of what “having everything" can feel like, and the conviviality and solidarity in that dinner scene is close in spirit to how it felt while we were together. It’s a reminder that we don’t actually need that much, and when we have it we should not forget to value it. Much of social activism is simply an attempt to make the conditions of normal life more widely available, and not to lose the ecological conditions and political freedoms that make it possible.

I would also link Naduve to Roberto Unger’s idea of what makes for a political progressive, particularly his lucid definition in his Radio 4 Analysis interview:

A progressive is someone who wants to see society reorganised, part by part and step by step, so that ordinary men and women have a better chance to live to a larger life.

A larger life is a good lodestar for progress. It refers to “a life of greater scope, greater capability and greater intensity”, and that’s not so difficult to comprehend. We can all do and be more, with growing aptitude and wisdom, and experience life more fully and deeply as a result. Of course, that means we all need to have a place to live, work to do, and the education and time we need to do it, but crucially, those things are the socio-economic means to the experiential ends that ultimately matter.

Naduve did not yet really put people to work or build capability in the way implied in “larger life”, but the organising team clearly stretched themselves, and for all of us this event was an experiential glimpse of this ‘larger life’, and a reminder of what we are all working for. The sacred felt abundant and pervasive.

Thirdly, I would bring in ‘the fun’ part of my argument in The Flip, The Formation, and The Fun as an antidote to the metacrisis where I argue that in addition to a paradigmatic shift in consciousness and transformative education as a societal means and end, we also need to ‘throw a better party’, ie an alternative political vision.

Naduve was a great party! It was just 42 people in a protected place, not over eight billion on a precarious planet, but it was nonetheless very playful, and prefigurative of the kind of invitation to participation we need to make to take people out of their consumerist trances.

I feel I have said enough for now, but if I were to write about Naduve again, I would say more about the importance of aesthetics as an underutilised element in other social gatherings. The title of this post comes from one of the songs shared in English that I identified with, because I came due to feeling called by “the drumming on the other side”.

More generally, what we experienced at Naduve wasn’t just music; it was regular music in different languages, sung as gratuitous gifts in between everything else that happened. The event was mostly about sensibility rather than systems, but we could all feel the connection. And there was art, which included this beautiful charcoal etching with weathered colour imprints by Maduri Umashankar that I was blessed to take home with me.

I should also mention the form of love known as Philia. Bayo joked that if Naduve went well, by the end of it, everyone present would love each other. We laughed, but the comment was not completely ridiculous. I imagine most people present were pleasantly surprised by the widespread feelings of affection and care that arose rapidly, deepened swiftly, and feel like they will endure.

So, should we feel guilty about not even trying more explicitly to solve any world problem while we gathered?

Is it not more than enough that such events leave people who carry the weight of the world in their work feeling less alone, reconnected to life, and glad to be alive?

Is it not clear that such feelings will be sublimated for the greater good in the work we are all called to do?

Amidst ambient government failure and cultural drift, it is surely necessary to at least try to start from a different kind of place, a different form of feeling.

I know this for sure. I would go back in a heartbeat.

From Tasting the Pickle: “I am not sure I am worthy of a spiritual name because for the last decade I have lived a bourgeois family life in London and enjoyed my share of boozy dinner parties. Yet there is a corner of the world where I am officially Vivekananda, a name conferred on me somewhat hastily in 2016 at a nondescript temple that doubled as a religious office in Kerala, South India. I came up with the name Vivekananda myself, which is not how it’s supposed to happen. I liked that it means discriminating (vivek) bliss (ananda) because it chimed with my experience of getting high on conceptual distinctions; my wife Siva and my Indian in-laws agreed it was fitting, and I was sent to photocopy my UK passport in a nearby booth on the dust roads. I returned with a piece of paper that detailed what I was ostensibly about to surrender, and it happened a few minutes later on a cement floor with chalk drawings, where I sat cross-legged under a ramshackle plastic sheet protecting us from the heat of the sun. I don’t remember the priest’s features and he didn’t speak English, but I knew the fire he created would be our witness, and when he invoked me to chant, those ancient Sanskrit sounds would resonate beyond that day. I was undertaking apostasy. This act of spiritual sedition felt political because it so often goes the other way in India, and I still feel the solemnity of that moment in my body. I did not seek to flirt with the sacrilegious and nor did I wish to renounce a faith I never really had, but I was sure that faith as such would always be my own, as would my Christian name.

I am still Jonathan, but technically renouncing my presumed Christianity to become Vivekananda was the only means by which I could be initiated into Arya Samaj (noble mission) which is a reformist branch of applied Vedantic philosophy within the religious orbit known as Hinduism. This conversion was neither doctrinal nor devotional, but it was undertaken quite literally to get closer to God, whom I hoped might exist, understand, and perhaps even laugh. After several years of sitting it out in a nearby air-conditioned hotel, the certificate I received after the ceremony was the only way I could, for the first time, join my family and enter the nearby pilgrimage site at Guruvayur, which is strictly for Hindus only, and purportedly Krishna’s home on earth. Whatever the fate of my soul, family pragmatics meant that my upper body was needed to carry our second son, Vishnu, and his abundant baby paraphernalia. I was never asked for my certificate, and were my skin brown I would not have needed it. Yet it was only because Jonathan doubled as Vivekananda that the rest of his family could pay obeisance alongside thousands of other pilgrims. I watched them queue for hours to see idols bathed in milk, offer their weight in bananas to God, feed the temple elephants, and pray. At one moment, tired but grateful, I looked down at baby Vishnu in my sling, not yet a year old, and it felt like the temple’s host was smiling back. I was there under false pretences, but those false pretences were true.”
