As I’ve mentioned before, I’m not a huge fan of the sitting president. I think this second Trump term is *by far the worst term of a president in my life-time*, and that Trump will go down in history as among the worst presidents ever, if not as one of history’s major villains. But because the administration is a whirling vortex of non-stop insanity, it’s almost impossible to keep track of all his failings.

This isn’t typical. If you get five people who didn’t like Bush, Biden, or Obama in a room, and ask them why they opposed him, you’ll get pretty similar answers. In contrast, with Trump, there are just so many different kinds of blatant misconduct, that there’s surprisingly little overlap. I recently heard on the [Triggernometry podcast](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mBEu3Om1u4M) recount his problems with Trump, and learned about a number of misbehaviors that I’d never heard before.

Scandals that would destroy any other president are a daily occurrence. Daily, we see headlines like “Trump threatens Greenland with nuclear war,” “Trump is gifted a free jet from Qatar,” and “Trump gets billions of dollars in crypto from the UAE and then right after makes an AI deal with the UAE.” The last two, by the way, are real!

For this reason, I thought I’d lay out, simply and concisely, my five biggest problems with the Trump administration—and why [defeating him politically](https://thingofthings.substack.com/p/donation-recommendations-for-effective) should be a top priority.[1](#footnote-1)

# 1 Foreign aid cuts

The most immediately disastrous Trump policy has been cutting foreign aid. The death toll so far has likely been [between 500,000 and a million](https://www.cgdev.org/blog/update-lives-lost-usaid-cuts), more than the Iraq war. The future death toll from canceled contracts is likely greater. This means Trump’s foreign aid cuts are responsible for about *1% of global deaths*. They have also damaged our soft-power and destabilized the world, harming America long-term.

The defenses of this policy do not hold up. It isn’t justified to cut foreign aid based on reducing costs because:

1.  Foreign aid is a tiny sliver of the budget, below 1%.
    
2.  America benefits from foreign aid in that in makes the world more peaceful and stable, and it boosts American softpower.
    

The moral argument that a nation should only serve its own interests so we shouldn’t provide foreign aid is wrong because:

1.  It ludicrously implies that we’d have no moral reason to intervene in Rwanda to stop the genocide, where teenagers were being butchered with machetes and raped. Similarly, it implies that the fact that intervening in world war two helped put an end to Nazism abroad and to the holocaust was no reason to do it. The only reason to invade was to benefit America.
    
2.  It would imply that if, say, Canada could spend a tiny amount of money stopping a virus from killing tens of millions of Americans, they shouldn’t do it (assume there was no way of working out a deal where Canada would benefit from this).
    
3.  Foreign aid benefits America by making the world more stable and boosting soft-power.
    
4.  Obviously we have negative obligations not to harm other countries. It would be wrong to invade and enslave another country. But if we have a duty not to harm other countries for their sake, it’s odd that we have no obligation to benefit them for their sake.[2](#footnote-2)
    
5.  If I’m right that foreign aid cuts are [more like killing than letting die](https://benthams.substack.com/p/foreign-aid-cuts-are-more-like-killing), then we have an obligation not to terminate them so long as we have a duty not to randomly kill foreigners.
    

Even if Trump’s term was otherwise impeccable, causing millions of deaths makes it impossible to be a good president.

# 2 Threats to Democracy

After losing an election, the [sitting president](https://benthams.substack.com/p/trump-attempted-a-coup) tried to overthrow the government and illegally reclaim office. He created fake slates of electors and tried to get them approved unlawfully by state legislators and then the vice president. When the vice president refused to illegally cooperate with the attempted coup, he sent protestors to the capitol to protest, and sat around for hours doing nothing as they violently stormed the capitol, threatening the lives of everyone in congress.

When you point this out, lots of people say that it isn’t a big deal because Hillary Clinton made some statement about the election not being legitimate. These actions are not the same. One of them was idle talk, and the other was a months-long concerted attempt to launch a coup.

Now, during his second term, Trump has begun [defying court orders](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2025/mar/23/judges-trump-court-rulings). He has also seriously floated the possibility of [running for a third term](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cx20lwedn23o). He has engaged in obviously bogus political prosecutions of many of his [political opponents](https://benthams.substack.com/p/the-comprehensive-case-against-trump), including James Comey, Jack Smith, Adam Schiff, Liz Cheney, Letitia James. Eugene Vindman, and Alexander Vindman; he’s also [a pollster](https://www.thefire.org/research-learn/trump-v-selzer-motion-and-brief-support-motion-dismiss) for publishing a poll he didn’t like.

Trump’s also systematically asserted powers that presidents don’t have, unilaterally imposing tariffs, declaring a [bogus national emergency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Emergency_Concerning_the_Southern_Border_of_the_United_States#:~:text=After%20signing%20the%20spending%20bill,a%20longer%20period%20of%20time.), asserting authority over [independent agencies](https://www.opb.org/article/2025/03/04/trump-is-asserting-extraordinary-power-over-independent-agencies-is-the-fed-next/), and [more](https://www.aei.org/op-eds/trumps-impact-on-executive-power-eleven-months-in/). The prediction markets put a [15% chance](https://www.metaculus.com/questions/36389/us-no-longer-a-democracy-by-2030/) on the U.S. no longer being a Democracy by 2030, and [similar judgments](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/eJNH2CikC4scTsqYs/prediction-markets-and-many-experts-think-authoritarian) are held by other prediction markets and experts. I take this pretty seriously because [prediction markets](https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2025/10/prediction-markets-are-very-accurate.html) are very accurate.

So while I think Trump probably won’t end Democracy in the U.S., the odds he does are more than, say, 5%, and that is very alarming. A one in twenty chance of destroying Democracy is totally disqualifying.

# 3 AI doom

Artificial intelligence is advancing very quickly. It is reasonably likely that sometime within the decade, [we’ll have](https://www.forethought.org/research/preparing-for-the-intelligence-explosion) AI that can [do everything that people can do](https://blog.ai-futures.org/p/ai-futures-model-dec-2025-update), but better. Developing AI much smarter than us without a clear plan for controlling it is a recipe for disaster, and it [poses serious existential risks](https://benthams.substack.com/p/ai-might-kill-everyone?utm_source=publication-search). For this reason, it is alarming that the Trump administration has increased the probability of AI-enabled catastrophe. Specifically, [the administration has](https://benthams.substack.com/p/madness-in-washington?utm_source=publication-search):

-   Attempted to preempt state-level AI regulation.
    
-   Sold America’s best chips to China, vastly raising the likelihood that they win the arms race, and that by being on our heels, safety measures are bypassed.
    
-   Systematically undermined global cooperation of the kind needed for navigating the AI race, by ending long-standing free-trade policies, pulling out of international organizations like the WHO, and adopting aggressive and hostile policies towards other nations. Global cooperation is needed for any kind of AI-safety treaty, so decimating global norms makes muddling through much more difficult. This is especially concerning given the ways [AI could go wrong](https://www.forethought.org/research/preparing-for-the-intelligence-explosion) even if it is aligned, that require cooperation to deal with.
    

As Ozy [notes](https://thingofthings.substack.com/p/donation-recommendations-for-effective), one of Trump’s top advisors is Mark Andreesen—an AI accelerationist with a direct financial stake in AI succeeding, wholly opposed to AI safety. This would be a bit like if one of your major advisors on nuclear weapons was from the head of a company building nuclear weapons, and this person denied that nuclear weapons posed any serious risks.

Thus, I think Trump meaningfully increases the odds of AI-enabled catastrophe. This is a significant risk; we are rushing full speed ahead towards making something much smarter than us, with potentially alien motivations, and have no great plan for making it safe. That would be dangerous enough even if our leaders were behaving cautiously, rather than randomly destroying AI regulations, any hope of sustained cooperation, and selling our best chips to China, even as we use the Chinese threat as an excuse not to regulate AI.

# 5 Bad policies

I think most of the standard Trump policies are bad.

It is [bad to send ICE agents to rove through U.S. cities](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/trump-set-expand-immigration-crackdown-2026-despite-brewing-backlash-2025-12-21/), terrifying and browbeating random Hispanics. It’s bad to deport [millions of legal immigrants](https://www.foxnews.com/opinion/trump-targeting-nonviolent-legal-immigrants-americans-starting-notice). Even if you’re opposed to immigration—which, to be clear, [I’m not](https://www.cato.org/policy-report/september/october-2013/immigration-good-america)—it’s bad to deport people *once they’re already here,* when the alternative is going to some dangerous third-world hellhole. Similarly, it is bad to send innocent people applying for asylum to be [tortured in a foreign prison](https://benthams.substack.com/p/senseless-brutality-is-not-a-reasonable).

The tariffs are also very bad. Basically [all economists agree](https://tcf.org/content/commentary/economists-agree-trump-is-wrong-on-tariffs/) about this. It follows from fairly [simple economics](https://www.economicforces.xyz/p/econ-101-is-wrong-about-tariffs), and [studies have born](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25402) out the basic argument, confirming their negative effects. Tariffs reduce the amount of government [revenue](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/57a08ab9e5274a27b200071d/TariffReductions2011Cirerareport.pdf) because they’re so bad for the economy. Trump’s tariffs have been [higher on countries with lower tariffs on us](https://www.cato.org/blog/please-stop-calling-them-reciprocal-tariffs?utm_source), and they’ve been met with countertariffs, so the “impose tariffs to lower tariffs” justification backfires. And the tariffs have been so random and unpredictable that companies can’t even reliably respond to them.

Then, there’s the policy of [undermining medical research](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-04051-y) by imposing billions worth of cuts to university funding. Vaccines have been especially targeted, especially [MRNA vaccines](https://www.statnews.com/2025/08/07/mrna-cancer-vaccines-kennedy-future/), which are critical for developing [cancer treatments](https://www.noahpinion.blog/p/did-rfk-just-take-away-your-cancer). Policies have also been sufficiently fickle and sporadic that grantees don’t know if they’ll have future funding, which makes research very difficult.

Medical research has very [high return on investment](https://www.richardhanania.com/p/government-science-and-the-prestige). [Each dollar](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2025/03/nih-funding-delivers-exponential-economic-returns/) spent on it returns multiple dollars. Innovation is the primary driver of increased prosperity long-term, so actions that slow it are very bad. Innovation is a public good, so we should expect it to be underproduced in a free market.

Of the core priorities pursued by the administration, none pass any kind of sane cost-benefit analysis.

# Conclusion

I’m not against everything Trump has done. I think, for example, in foreign policy, he’s had an unexpected number of successes—I was surprised by [his success in stopping wars](https://benthams.substack.com/p/the-comprehensive-case-against-trump) (though his record on that front is hugely overstated by his boosters). I wouldn’t be shocked if this recent Venezuelan takeover turns out for the best. But overall, he has been a catastrophically bad president, both increasing near-term death and suffering and vastly increasing the odds of the sorts of catastrophes that might *kill everyone*.

He is personally a moral and intellectual black hole. He pursues policies that make the world worse in the short-run, disrupt institutions, and inch us steadily closer to doom. For this reason, is right: one of the best places to donate, at this point, is to [Democratic candidates](https://thingofthings.substack.com/p/donation-recommendations-for-effective). It turns out it is bad to have a thoughtless wrecking ball as president of the free world. Who could have guessed?

As always, if you’re a Trump supporter tell me: why am I wrong?
