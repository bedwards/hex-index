1 AI 2027

AI 2027 is a model written up by Eli Lifland, one of the world’s top forecasters, Daniel Kokotajlo, who made a series of extremely accurate early predictions about AI, Scott Alexander, who I’m told has a blog or something, and various others. Its aim is to forecast how AI will go. The core claim of the forecast is that there is a reasonable probability that we will get AI that can do everything better than humans fairly soon, potentially in just a few years. The most likely scenario was AGI in 2027, while the median scenario was AGI in 2028.

Now, maybe that sounds outlandish. But remember, there has been a consistent trend where AI’s maximum task length has been doubling roughly every seven months. What does that mean? Take tasks that it takes humans some number of hours to perform on average. Then ask: can AIs do that task? AIs have been able to consistently do longer and longer tasks, so that about every seven months, AIs get the ability to do most tasks that take humans twice as long.

If you plot out a doubling every seven months, then over the course of 35 months (about three years) you get five doublings. Maximum task length increases by a factor of 32, and now, instead of consistently being able to do things that take a few hours, AI will be able to do things that take days. Double that a few more times, and you get the AI being able to do things that take months and then years. By that time, the AIs can do more programming of AIs, and will have far surpassed us.

Now, the AI 2027 people have a more sophisticated model. But hopefully the argument I’ve just given illustrates that something in the vicinity of AGI extremely soon is reasonable, even though the exact details are debatable. AI 2027 seems like a pretty decent forecast by some smart people and ought to be taken seriously.

Lots of people on the internet have taken to dunking on AI 2027. Some of these criticisms are reasonable. In their initial forecast, they made some subtle mathematical errors which, when corrected for, push their timeline back a bit. Another reasonable criticism is that Daniel Kokotajlo is #problematic because as of 2017, he endorsed the (VERY FAILED) self-sampling assumption and has YET to make a public statement DENOUNCING his past views. Very concerning.

Similarly, Eli Lifland has behaved badly in that his blog is very good, but it is not on substack, making it so that I have to manually check when new articles come out, rather than them appearing in my inbox. Very disappointing behavior. Very sad!

But most of the criticisms, I think, are bad. I thought I’d discuss why.

2 Is AI 2027 like those who say abolish the police and then when pressed claim that they only support small changes rather than wholesale abolition?

“Abolish the police,” was a common 2020-era slogan. When you asked most people what they meant by it, they’d make some mealy-mouthed statement about how it was important to systematically reform the police. This was silly! If you don’t want to abolish the police, don’t have your slogan be “abolish the police.” Similarly, I saw someone on Twitter make the same comparison: if you don’t think AI is likely to come by 2027, don’t call your timeline forecast AI 2027! Especially when now the lead authors think AI is most likely to come after 2027, and their median forecast was always post-2027.

I don’t think this analogy is apt at all.

I mean, first of all, the authors think the odds of AI by 2027 is non-trivial. Their median at the time they published was sometime in 2028, though 2027 was their mode. 2027 was the scenario they wrote up in the most detail because it was the single likeliest scenario. There are some important differences:

  1. The AI 2027 scenario thinks there’s a substantial probability of AI by 2027.

  2. The AI 2027 scenario is the single most likely scenario, even though it doesn’t have more than 50% probability. If someone called for abolishing the police, in a report that gave many similarly radical options, and thought there was a 40% chance the police should be abolished, I wouldn’t think they were hiding the ball.

  3. The AI 2027 scenario says in the very first footnote:

We disagree somewhat amongst ourselves about AI timelines; our median AGI arrival date is somewhat longer than what this scenario depicts. This scenario depicts something like our mode. See our timelines forecast for more details.

And when the probability distribution given by each of their authors for the 25th percentile, 50th percentile, and 75th percentile is as follows:

Daniel: ???? / 2028 / ????
Eli: 2029 / 2033 / 2045
Thomas: 2028 / 2032 / 2040
Romeo: 2028 / 2035 / 2060
Scott: 2027 / 2031 / 2040

(Classic Daniel, always causing problems with his “????” predictions. Perhaps this is the kind of moral, intellectual, and spiritual decay that comes when you spend your years endorsing the self-sampling assumption).

So I think it’s very hard to maintain that the AI 2027 people were hiding the ball the way the abolish the police people were.

3 Are models good?

One of the major critiques that people have been levying is that we shouldn’t rely on models when things are so speculative. One representative version of this critique came from Titotal in his comprehensive piece criticizing the AI 2027 model:

Maybe this is because I am a physicist, not a Rationalist. In my world, you generally want models to have strong conceptual justifications or empirical validation with existing data before you go making decisions based off their predictions: this fails at both.

Now, the AI 2027 people have already responded to this critique, so I don’t have too much to add. I basically think what they say is correct. It’s often worth having models as part of one’s analysis. Broad general trend-tracking is often reasonably accurate. For example, if you were trying to figure out how much solar would cost at various points in the future, even though it’s hard to perfectly model technological change, looking at trend lines would still be useful.

This is something that superforecasters tend to do, and it’s one of the things that makes them more accurate. If it’s hard to have amazing evidence in some domain, it’s generally best to rely on a hazy average of okay models.

You can always throw up your hands and say that our models aren’t good enough. But that is neither helpful nor informative. Of course models are imperfect—no one denies that. But they’re just about the best evidence we have. Speculative forecasting shouldn’t be held to the standards of physics, because physics is a vastly more certain enterprise.

We often have to make decisions that depend on what timelines are like. The 2027 authors give the example of going to law school. If you think by the time you graduate law school, there’s a more than 50% chance that AI will have replaced lawyers, then it doesn’t make much sense to go. Thinking AI might come very soon is one of the reasons I’m trying to get a career influencing how AI goes. The models don’t have to be perfect. They just have to be the best game in town.

4 Does the fact that the AI 2027 team has changed their timelines mean the model is bad?

Imagine someone writes a report “Vance 2028, where they argue that there’s a 40% chance that Vance is the next president.” Then, some more events happen, and they go down a bit to 35%. Have they been owned? Does this mean their model is bad? No, obviously not.

John Maynard Keynes famously said “When the facts change, I change my mind. What do you do, sir?” Similarly, it is a good thing that the people who made the AI 2027 forecast have updated it in response to changing evidence. Perhaps if their probabilities changed massively, that would be evidence that the original model was bad. But slight changes are good and are what’s to be expected of good modelers.

Now, the analogy isn’t perfect as the AI 2027 model is a lot better than the Rethink Priorities model. This isn’t to comment on which one was crafted with higher skill, only to note that we have a lot better evidence for AI timelines than for the welfare of different organisms. So any model of AI timelines will be more accurate than a comparable model of welfare ranges.

In both cases, lots of people were flabbergasted at the seemingly extreme results of the model. But I was inclined to trust both models because they were supported by independent arguments. For this reason, I guessed that reasonable models would end up somewhat similar.

The reasonable independent argument for thinking AI will be really fast is the one I gave at the beginning: maximum task lengths have been doubling. Extrapolate that out for a few years, and you get AIs being better at everything than humans. This becomes more robust when you realize that pretty much every trend behind AI has been increasing multiple times per year. For example, the number of AI models that can be run has been going up 25x per year, algorithmic efficiency ~3x per year, and compute 4.5x per year. Thus, I’m not actually that surprised when a model tells me things will get crazy soon. Whenever an increase is fast and exponential, it shoots from pretty good to unbelievably insane rather quickly.

Similarly, with the Rethink numbers, I wasn’t surprised they were extreme. You get extreme numbers, in expectation, for the welfare of simple animals unless you are very confident that cognitive simplicity goes along with very muted experience. But I don’t think there’s any very strong reason to think that. Valence might be like sight, in that you can have it work pretty well even with a pretty simple brain. So as a result, I’m not too shocked by high expected values given major uncertainty.

All this is to say that you should trust models more when they match other arguments. The AI 2027 team’s model does, which is one reason I place stock in it. A lot of times when people criticize models, it’s because the models say things that they find surprising, but aren’t that surprising if you follow other arguments.

6 Conclusion

There are reasonable quibbles to be had with the AI 2027 model at the margins. It’s not crazy to disagree with their core conclusion. But I think clearly their model isn’t flatly insane and they are making serious arguments. The present level of snark, scorn, and dismissal is not warranted when some of the best AI forecasters take the time to make what is, by all accounts, quite a good model.
