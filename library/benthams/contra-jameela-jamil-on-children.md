, who acted in one of my favorite shows, *The Good Place*, is [on substack](https://jameelajamil.substack.com/). And unlike [a lot of celebrity writers](https://wollenblog.substack.com/), she actually writes quite well. I also faintly recall her reviewing some book I enjoyed, and giving a positive review—so let “I’m pretty sure she liked some book that I also liked,” be a major character endorsement. However, Jamil recently wrote an article called *[I don’t want children. I don’t want children. I don’t want children. I don’t want children](https://jameelajamil.substack.com/p/i-dont-want-children-i-dont-want).* You might be able to guess what it was about.

The article went insanely viral. And a lot of it consisted of arguments against having children, rather than mere descriptions of why she doesn’t want children. For this reason, I thought it would be worth responding to. My view is that having children is a very good thing. It’s probably the best thing most people do, rather like donating a kidney. So while it shouldn’t be compulsory or anything, it’s a good thing to do, and it should be encouraged. It gives life to a person, [which is a very good thing](https://benthams.substack.com/p/a-comprehensive-takedown-of-anti). It’s good to live a happy life. The best thing anyone ever did for you was creating you.

Jamil [described one criticism](https://substack.com/@jameelajamil/note/c-192824058) as “so embarrassingly white,” so hopefully my piece will avoid falling into that trap (despite my *embarrassing* caucacity). I’ll also avoid talking about the personal points she makes, like the fact that she doesn’t like kids. I just don’t really have anything interesting to say about whether Jamil likes kids. It seems to me that she’d be a better judge of that than me!

> I don’t want to voluntarily risk grave, life altering illness, or death for anything or anyone. An astonishing number of women encounter this during pregnancy, and that sounds fucking awful to me. The woman who lost all of her teeth due to pregnancy lives in my head rent free. This is before the aftermath of the 18 months of physical and psychological recovery, hormones falling off a cliff, immense relationship strain, and literal years of insomnia. No thanks! Sorry!

Risk of death or serious injury from pregnancy is fairly low. About 600 people die from childbearing [in a year](https://ourworldindata.org/maternal-mortality), in the U.S.. Your risk of dying from pregnancy is about as high as your risk from dying in a year of driving. That’s not nothing, but it illustrates that this isn’t some catastrophic risk. It’s akin to all sorts of risks that we bear all the time.

It’s true, of course, that having children comes with all sorts of bodily changes. But it also comes with a bunch of benefits. Among these is that there is a *brand new person in the world who loves you*, who you can watch grow up. The [overall impact on happiness](https://www.econlib.org/archives/2009/11/the_effect_of_c.html) isn’t super clear, though the evidence seems to imply that if people later in life have children, they exceed [their childless peers](https://www.betonit.ai/p/kids_and_happin_1html) in happiness.

The other point: sometimes it’s worth bearing costs in order to do something very good. Bringing a person into existence to live a good life is a [very good thing](https://benthams.substack.com/p/its-good-to-create-happy-people-a?utm_source=publication-search). So even if one took a small hit to their well-being to have children, it would be virtuous. Giving up a kidney probably isn’t fun, but it’s still worthwhile.

> I am not up for the inequity of child bearing and rearing of our world. The mental and physical load that is heaped on women, and how disproportionate even 50/50 parenting would be (in the near unheard of instance that it even exists) when I have given two YEARS of my body, health, and well being, and taken all of the pain, stress and risk, ASIDE from the parenting part. THERE IS NO 50/50. How can there be? The person who didn’t suffer the prolonged medical nightmare of pregnancy will always be about 250 steps behind equality. Fuck that. I am watching my friends have children and the imbalance is frankly astonishing. Even from their most well meaning, feminist-leaning partners.

I find this a somewhat baffling mindset. You’re not having children because of the inequality in child-rearing? Isn’t the relevant thing how much work you have to do, not the relative differential between the amount of work you do and the amount your partner does?

In any case, as [Bryan Caplan has noted](https://www.astralcodexten.com/p/book-review-selfish-reasons-to-have), there are lots of ways to hugely decrease the amount of work you spend parenting. Parenting doesn’t seem to have a huge effect on children’s life outcomes, and in any case, it’s much better to have an extra kid and not parent them enough than not to have them. Whether you exist at all is a fairly significant determiner of life outcomes. Existent people on average earn vastly more than non-existent people and are much likelier to graduate college (though crime rates are a lot higher, due to what social scientists are calling the existence gap).

In addition, if you have a kid, your kid can take care of you later in life. This will be at a time when your capacities are hugely diminished. This partially offsets the work you do in childrearing.

> I think the world is absolutely terrible. Parents in this country, are as we speak, reading our morning headlines, that it is very likely we may go to war with RUSSIA. They know that their boys especially will be not only called upon, but possibly forced to join our armed forces, if shit goes south fast enough.

We’re at the [most peaceful period in human history](https://ourworldindata.org/war-and-peace)! A global war with Russia isn’t very likely. Mutually assured destruction is a very effective deterrent. So is the fact that neither side really profits from war. The world is, by every objective metric, [better than it has ever been and getting better](https://benthams.substack.com/p/ways-the-world-is-getting-better?utm_source=publication-search) (well, the foreign aid cuts might temporarily reverse progress, but decades from now, things will likely be better). [And](https://smallpotatoes.paulbloom.net/p/life-is-good?utm_source=publication-search) [most people are happy](https://benthams.substack.com/p/a-comprehensive-takedown-of-anti). The conscription concern seems silly; we haven’t had conscription *since Vietnam*! Also, is conscription so bad that it makes your life *not worth living*? My sense is most people who fought in Vietnam think they had okay lives.

> People roll their eyes if I bring up how the incoming technocracy and looming digital ID pipeline to social credit, doesn’t inspire me to drag anyone, against their own will, into this dumpster fire. I know humanity has been through worse than these times, and that actually statistically things aren’t as bad as they were in say the era of globally warring empires, or barbarianism. I just think that’s a lower bar than what I’m up for really. I don’t think “well it’s better than the Viking era” really cuts it for me. I would want more for my children. I would want them to know the freedom I knew. This big brother era is suffocating and it is inhibiting the growth of teenagers today. There is no space for the epic fuck ups of the 90s. Your footprint doesn’t start in your twenties anymore. I despair of a life never free from being perceived. I can scarcely handle it as a fully formed adult, I don’t know how to navigate a young person through such a crisis of inhibition.

It’s not really against their own will if they don’t have a will yet and are statistically very likely to be happy to be alive if they come to exist. It’s true that teenagers in the modern age have some problems, like the presence of social media and the absence of epic 90s fuck ups (or so I’ve heard—I paid little attention to the goings-on in the 90s in large part because I didn’t exist). But, I mean, really, are these problems so bad that it isn’t good to exist? There are studies that look at whether most people rate their lives worth living. They [mostly do.](https://benthams.substack.com/p/a-comprehensive-takedown-of-anti)[1](#footnote-1)

> There are just too many people who want to have sex with children. More than we ever realized. More than we could ever have imagined. They are everywhere, at every level, and within every trusted institution. I know that’s dark. But a child goes MISSING EVERY TWO AND A HALF HOURS. Did you know that? I would be thinking about that every day.

This statistic is pretty misleading. Statistically, kidnappings are rare, mostly “parent takes child while under other parent’s custody,” rather than strangers stealing them, and kidnappings rarely succeed. [Quoting Scott Alexander](https://www.astralcodexten.com/p/book-review-selfish-reasons-to-have?utm_source=publication-search):

> What about kidnapping? Plenty of people are kidnapped, but it’s usually something like a relative stealing them away in defiance of a divorce custody agreement. The “traditional kidnapping” where a creep in a white van plucks a child off the streets is much rarer - only about [100 such incidents](https://www.timesunion.com/news/article/child-abductions-strangers-moreau-kidnap-rare-18404760.php) come to the attention of the authorities per year.
> 
> …
> 
> But it seems like most abductions [are navigated successfully](https://www.timesunion.com/news/article/child-abductions-strangers-moreau-kidnap-rare-18404760.php):
> 
> > Across the country, only 181 Amber Alerts were broadcast in 2022, including one in New York, according to the National Center for Missing and Exploited Children. In those cases, 180 children were recovered and only one was still missing as of March. Four of the children were killed.

But of course, bad stuff happens in the world sometimes! There’s sexual abuse of children, murder, rape, etc. So does good stuff. Your child’s odds of falling in love are a lot higher than their odds of being abducted. The important question, it seems to me, is whether future people’s lives are expected to be good. But studies that try to ascertain this tend to find most people’s lives are good.

> How can I risk raising a daughter in a world where porn hub exists? The way women are written about and treated in porn is terrifying. It’s all about disrespect, punishment, humiliation, violence and revenge.

This obviously isn’t good, but is the existence of porn hub really so astronomically terrible that it makes being alive not worth it? One could similarly argue against saving lives this way—how can you justify saving someone’s life in a world as terrible as this one?

> How can I risk raising a son who might hurt someone’s daughter? I am not in the driving seat in a world with so much external influence hunting kids in video games, on YouTube and on social media. Did you know boys are targeted with misogynist content within 90 seconds of logging on to a social media platform? And if they engage, even just out of curiosity, even if they disagree with what they are watching it, they are then bombarded by the algorithm, with more and more violent misogynist content?

It’s true, of course, that you might have a child who harms people in serious ways. But you might also have a child who benefits people. If you raise your child to be an effective altruist, even if there’s only a 50% chance you succeed, you’ll still save a [bunch of lives in expectation](https://benthams.substack.com/p/a-life-that-cannot-be-a-failure?utm_source=publication-search). Your child might be someone’s husband, making her life a lot better.

I also find it weird that the concern is that men will hurt women, and the concern is that women will be hurt by men. Most victims of violent crimes are men! As rightly notes, your primary concern, when you look at your brand new baby, [should not be](https://www.cartoonshateher.com/p/the-preemptive-distrust-of-baby-boys) “is he going to become a child murder torturer?”

Overall, then, I think the case for having kids is strong. You get to create a brand new person, who will probably live a pretty good life and be happy to exist. You can see a miniature copy of you grow up. It’s hard work, but it’s deeply meaningful, and might even make you happier, especially if you have children when you’re middle-aged. There is also something intrinsically good about increasing the number of people in the world that you love deeply. That number goes up by 1 when you have a child. Kids are also very cute. SO CUTE! Have you ever seen one?

All of these make having kids a very sensible default. There might be other more valuable life projects, like saving others’ lives or fighting against factory farming. But for most people, it’s the best thing they do. There’s strong reason to preserve child-rearing norms.

Why do I treat the relevant threshold for it being worth creating a person as whether their life was worth living? A number of reasons:

1.  Even if you think there’s some higher threshold, probably that threshold is met over the typical happy life.
    
2.  The argument I give [here](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/utilitas/article/sequence-argument-against-the-procreation-asymmetry/773D4C271F072ED88BCC45BD9F3A3963) establishes that the Pareto principle—the idea that it’s worth performing some action if it’s good for one person and bad for no one—implies that it’s worth creating anyone with a good life.
    
3.  Critical level theories face a [number of serious objections](https://utilitarianism.net/population-ethics/#person-affecting-views-and-the-procreative-asymmetry). They either imply the sadistic conclusion or an odd kind of incomparability.
    
4.  These views have also just struck me as intuitively whack. How could it be bad to create someone, for their sake, if their existence is a good thing?
