> As soon as certain topics are raised, the concrete melts into the abstract and no one seems able to think of turns of speech that are not hackneyed: prose consists less and less of words chosen for the sake of their meaning, and more and more of phrases tacked together like the sections of a prefabricated hen-house.

Orwell wrote the above in *[Politics and the English Language](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/)*, one of my favorite essays ever, which is primarily about why you should write clearly and concretely. I’ve spent a lot of time [criticizing](https://benthams.substack.com/p/how-continental-philosophers-argue) continental philosophy recently, primarily for its unclarity and muddled thinking. In continental writing, the concrete is almost universally replaced by the amorphous and abstract. But the problem is considerably broader than continental philosophy. In its nebulousness, continental philosophy differs from other writing in degree, not in kind.

Nebulous writing and speaking is very often used to defend the status quo. If some practice is enshrined and widespread, we will likely have respectable phrases to describe it. Those phrases maintain a level of lofty abstraction that does not require one to grapple with the specific wrongs of the practice. For instance, when a person takes a casual drive-by shot at animal welfare, they will typically use euphemistic phrases to discuss what is happening. They are aided in doing so by the fact that we have all sorts of terms to whitewash what occurs in the meat industry.

We use the word “cull” instead of the word kill. We say “pork,” instead of pig flesh, and “leather,” instead of cow skin. It is very easy to defend pork. It is a lot harder to defend gassing pigs to death. It is especially difficult to defend the pig killing industry when one takes note of the conditions of the pigs prior to their death. Instead of defending animals having their body parts sliced off with a knife, their babies stolen from them, and being forced to live in feces, they can stick to loftier questions like whether man has, in some broad sense, the sort of dominion that permits us to eat other animals.

People very often take casual swipes at shrimp welfare. A [recent piece by Jobling](https://benthams.substack.com/p/why-are-they-so-bad) was fairly standard in the way it discussed the issue in saying:

> A typical example of EA thinking is [Bentham’s Bulldog’s article](https://benthams.substack.com/p/the-best-charity-isnt-what-you-think) about why the [Shrimp Welfare Project](https://www.shrimpwelfareproject.org/), which prevents shrimp suffering, is the “best charity.” I’m sure many feel, as I do, that there must be something terribly wrong with a moral philosophy that leads anyone to privilege shrimp welfare above all other causes.

Shrimp welfare is an abstraction. When one hears the phrase, nothing very concrete comes to mind other than the silliness of those bleeding hearts who care about shrimp. Jobling’s sideswipe would have been less sharp if he’d remained specific; it is hard to make snarky remarks about spending a single dollar to prevent over 10,000 shrimp from slowly suffocating to death. It is harder to breezily dismiss efforts to prevent the crushing of the shrimp’s eyes—a standard breeding procedure to induce more births.

In analyzing the practices of a society, it is very important to remain focused on the concrete goings-on rather than on the higher-level abstractions. Every practice can be justified by way of abstraction. If we care to investigate whether some practice is defensible, it is best to think about what specifically goes on in the practice, rather than which euphemistic phrases can be used to describe it. Orwell put the point very well:

> Consider for instance some comfortable English professor defending Russian totalitarianism. He cannot say outright, ‘I believe in killing off your opponents when you can get good results by doing so’. Probably, therefore, he will say something like this:
> 
> > While freely conceding that the Soviet régime exhibits certain features which the humanitarian may be inclined to deplore, we must, I think, agree that a certain curtailment of the right to political opposition is an unavoidable concomitant of transitional periods, and that the rigours which the Russian people have been called upon to undergo have been amply justified in the sphere of concrete achievement.
> 
> The inflated style is itself a kind of euphemism. A mass of Latin words falls upon the facts like soft snow, blurring the outlines and covering up all the details.

Similarly, when criticizing effective altruism, people routinely ignore the specifics in favor of generalities. When they defend failing to donate, they crucially neglect the obvious—and salient—fact that donations to effective charities prevent children from dying. [Emile Torres’s](https://aeon.co/users/emile-p-torres) great gimmick has been panning EA in the popular press without mentioning any things the EAs do or reasons they think what they think. Instead, Torres will make hay about the unrelated ideological judgments of EAs or allegedly racist emails written by Bostrom 30 years ago.

It is easier to criticize a movement that does lots of good things if one ignores what is done and instead sticks to lofty and relatively formless ideological pronouncements—along with criticisms of other, unrelated judgments. This [almost surpassed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TESCREAL) self-parody when Torres took to grouping EA with six other random movements, many of them with ideologically opposed aims, declared them all part of the TESCREAL bundle (transhumanism, extropianism, singularitarianism, cosmism, rationalism, effective altruism, and longtermism), and [treated the group as a single homogeneous category](https://asteriskmag.com/issues/06/the-tescreal-bungle).

I [recently reviewed](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xVlTNK0h3Ak&pp=0gcJCTwKAYcqIYzv) several videos and articles criticizing effective altruism. The articles would normally only briefly discuss the things EAs do, usually followed by a bit of muted praise. The criticisms were typically that EAs did not pass unrelated ideological loyalty tests. For example, [in this interview](https://www.currentaffairs.org/news/2023/05/why-effective-altruism-and-longtermism-are-toxic-ideologies):

> **Robinson**
> 
> It’s inconceivable to me to think of effective altruists talking about colonialism. The philosophy is just so far from touching that.
> 
> **Torres**
> 
> Yes. It’s pretty appalling the extent to which these other considerations about colonialism or capitalism, for example, are not really even on the radar of a lot of EAs.

One of [the videos](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jvpGdoKTK_I) was especially notable in how little interest it seemed to have in discussing what EA does, preferring to talk abstractions like the “Frameworks of bourgeois liberalism…in our neoliberal age.” Though the video repeatedly mentioned utilitarianism, neoliberalism, random bits of EA writings, liberalism’s supposed addiction to science and faux objectivity, and even displayed repeated grotesque images of tongues, it never once saw fit to mention any of the things the EA movement *has done*.

If one makes an hour-long video arguing that EA is a destructive bourgeois movement that greases the wheels of neoliberalism, it seems worth mentioning, at least once, some of the things EAs do. Certainly this seems more apt than repeatedly displaying *tongues*. This would be like a polemic about Trumpism solely devoted to criticizing the conservative ideology of Edmund Burke, and never once mentioning any of Trump’s policies. The other popular trick is mentioning that EAs believe things that are counterintuitive, never mentioning the arguments for them, and sneering.

Similarly, politicians talk frequently about their belief that a nation should serve only its own interests, often when justifying foreign aid cuts. This is an easy principle to maintain when one is loftily musing. They can wax poetic about the social contract, rather than having to discuss the predictable consequence of the foreign aid cuts—many dead children.

It is easier to say “a nation should serve its own interest,” than “a nation should serve its own interest, so we should not spent 0.1% of the federal budget preventing a million children from dying.” It is similarly easier to say “morality is a game-theoretic cooperative enterprise for the pursuit of mutual gain via strategic general equilibrium,” than “morality is a game-theoretic cooperative enterprise involving mutual gain, and so if far-away children cannot benefit us, we haven’t any moral reason to save their lives.”

Our moral intuitions should be trusted more on subjects that we grasp better. It is very easy to grasp the morally important facts about a person being stabbed, and so we should trust our intuitions that being stabbed is a bad thing. In contrast, our grasp of the ethical weight of harms to shrimp is a lot dimmer. We have little idea what it’s like to be a shrimp, and we rarely spend much time to think about things from their perspective.

But even when our grasp of an issue is incomplete, so long as we try to be empathetic, we can get a pretty good sense of things. When you see a crab having its limbs sliced off with a knife, though it is hard to imagine exactly what things are like from its perspective, you can imagine that something bad is likely happening. If you would at some future point be turned into a crab, you’d care quite a lot about not enduring such a fate.

Our intuitions are weakest when dealing with large categories. We can picture in our head numbers like 3 and 6, but not numbers like a million or 10^30. When we hear claims like “the far future could have 10^40 people,” it registers the same as “the far future could have 10^38 people.” This is in spite of the fact that there are only 1% as many people in the second case as the first—that the second scenario is the same as the first, except roughly 99 in 100 people are never born, and 99% of all value is lost.

Ethical intuitions fail us when we try to intuitively grasp global problems where large numbers are involved. This is one of the reasons why, if you want to oppose animal rights in a debate, it is best to aggregate. If you ask people whether it’s better to save a human’s life or to spare chickens from having to spend 14,000 years in a cage, almost everyone will say that you ought to save the human.

However, if you ask whether it is better to extend a person’s life by a single day, or to keep an animal out of a cage for a year, nearly everyone will say it is better to keep an animal out of a cage for a year. These two are in conflict—saving someone’s life extends their life by about 14,000 days. If each extension of a day is less good than keeping chickens out of a cage for a year, then keeping chickens out of a cage for 14,000 years is better than extending a life 14,000 days.

Put another way, imagine that there are 14,000 remotes, each with two buttons, only one of which can be pressed. One is labeled “chicken,” the other is labeled “human.” Each time you press “human,” a person’s life is extended a day. Each time you press “chicken,” a chicken is freed from a year in a cage. It seems, on each remote, you ought to press “chicken.” But if you do that, you will end up saving 14,000 chickens from a year in a cage, rather than saving one person’s life.

Why this inconsistency? The answer is that our intuitions about big numbers are hazy. A single day of life, we recognize, is of only modest value compared to a year in a cage. But when we multiply a year in a cage by 14,000, our intuitions don’t track that it gets 14,000 times worse. So when you take the loss of a day, and multiply it by 14,000, we intuitively think that is much worse than when you multiply a chicken being trapped in a cage by 14,000. We know that it’s very bad for people to die; because we underestimate the badness of chickens being trapped for 14,000 years, we don’t see that it is worse.

Our negative judgment about the badness of chickens spending 14,000 years in a cage is only a bit stronger than of chickens spending 100 years in a cage. We hear both as simply “\[big number\] of years in a cage.” We can’t really picture either number. This is why people would spend the same amount to benefit [2,000 birds as 200,000 birds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_neglect).

This is why plain description is not always adequate. If an issue is described plainly, it becomes much harder to miss extremely obvious truths about it, which is one serious upside of plain description. This is one reason the criticisms of effective altruism almost always end up at the level of lofty ideals and ideology. The things effective altruists support are largely uncontroversial and the objections do not make very much sense. The concrete must become abstract.

But mere concreteness is not adequate when our intuitions about a subject are not, themselves, concrete. No matter how plainly one says “a .00001% reduction in existential risks secures, in expectation, 10^24 extra lives,” it won’t be emotionally or rhetorically gripping. Because a small reduction in existential risks is an abstraction. One can easily imagine what is bad about a person being in pain; you cannot easily get a mental image of why a world with 10^24 people is much less good than one with 10^30 well-off people. You can’t picture either.

This is not to say that it is impossible to make any judgments about the matter. Nearly everyone thinks, if asked, that a world where a trillion good things occur is less good than one where a quadrillion good things occur. We have intuitions about principles, not just cases. But it is to caution that when ethical judgments become more abstract and hazy, our intuitive grasp on them gets weaker. In such cases, we need to rely more on arguments from simple principles, because our intuitions about the case no longer pick up on what is important.

Abstract and non-specific words enable confusions in many domains outside of ethics. It is easier to feel less confused about consciousness when you say it [emerges](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/8QzZKw9WHRxjR4948/the-futility-of-emergence)—because emergence is a rather nebulous term that can serve as a vessel for one’s preexisting confusion. This is perhaps the single most common philosophical error made by people with a small amount of philosophical training. One will have some muddled idea in their head about a topic and will say things that do not make very much sense, because they treat the half-formed mental muddle as being a complete and satisfactory treatment of the topic.

So they will say some sentence like “you can’t do induction over random events,” without it being entirely clear what they mean or why they think that. Because that slogan lives in their head, and sounds vaguely plausible, they repeatedly double down. Each time they attempt to flesh out exactly what they mean, things get more muddled, and they repeatedly contradict themselves. Really what they are committed to is a slogan or a one-word explanation of a phenomenon—attempts to turn that slogan from the abstract to the concrete are met with fierce resistance.

It is like trying to describe a dream. Doing so is difficult because to describe something, one must precisely explain what was going on. And yet dreams do not have a specific narrative. They are instead a blur of sentiments, images, and ideas lacking a detailed plot. All too often our thoughts are that way too, but we aren’t aware of that fact.

A core part of effective altruism is thinking about the concrete and the specific. Rather than having one’s charitable donations be motivated by nebulous sentiments, one’s charitable donations depend solely on what they bring about. Instead of watching a sad advertisement and deciding that you want to do *something* about the problem, you reflect on which concrete actions are available to you and do whichever one does the most good.

Of course, in some cases the best thing to do is not something which has specific and identifiable beneficiaries. If you give your money to Longtermist organizations that very slightly reduce existential risks, there won’t be a specific person made much better off by your donation. Still, you are donating not because of broader ideology but because of the expected benefits it brings about. If asked to explain your donation, rather than pointing to a problem in the world or an abstraction, you will point to an expected effect of your action.

Specific and concrete writing does not just make one a better writer. It also makes one a clearer thinker. It is no surprise that the thinking of Derek Parfit was very sharp, and the thinking of Deleuze and Butler was not. Parfit writes clearly, Butler and Deleuze do not. Orwell put the point well:

> But if thought corrupts language, language can also corrupt thought. A bad usage can spread by tradition and imitation, even among people who should and do know better. The debased language that I have been discussing is in some ways very convenient. Phrases like *a not unjustifiable assumption*, *leaves much to be desired*, *would serve no good purpose*, *a consideration which we should do well to bear in mind*, are a continuous temptation, a packet of aspirins always at one’s elbow. Look back through this essay, and for certain you will find that I have again and again committed the very faults I am protesting against. By this morning’s post I have received a pamphlet dealing with conditions in Germany. The author tells me that he ‘felt impelled’ to write it. I open it at random, and here is almost the first sentence that I see: ‘(The Allies) have an opportunity not only of achieving a radical transformation of Germany’s social and political structure in such a way as to avoid a nationalistic reaction in Germany itself, but at the same time of laying the foundations of a co-operative and unified Europe.’ You see, he ‘feels impelled’ to write – feels, presumably, that he has something new to say – and yet his words, like cavalry horses answering the bugle, group themselves automatically into the familiar dreary pattern. This invasion of one’s mind by ready-made phrases (*lay the foundations*, *achieve a radical transformation*) can only be prevented if one is constantly on guard against them, and every such phrase anaesthetizes a portion of one’s brain.

Amorphous writing, then, has three effects. First, it tends to be unclear. If you are not saying anything very specific, it becomes very hard to track what you are intending to communicate. No one is confused by the sentence “George Washington was the first president,” because it is specific. Lots of people are [confused by the sentences](https://jan.ucc.nau.edu/~sj6/Spivak%20CanTheSubalternSpeak.pdf):

> The theory of pluralized ‘subject-effects’ gives an illusion of undermining subjective sovereignty while often providing a cover for this subject of knowledge. Although the history of Europe as Subject is narrativized by the law, political economy, and ideology of the West, this concealed Subject pretends it has ‘no geo-political determinations.’

Second, as discussed before, the more one deals in abstractions, the less one has to discuss the facts of the case that they are discussing. If you are forced to defend OJ on trial, it is better to muse about injustice than discuss the facts of the case. Third, it makes your thinking more muddled. The more you think in abstractions, the less you must think about anything specific, and the less your thoughts make any sense.

This is why it is best to be as clear as possible. Instead of speaking in hazy generalities, to whatever extent possible, you should speak in specifics. Sometimes attempting to speak precisely will make it hard to express a thought, but very often, this is because the thought did not make sense in the first place. We’d all do well to follow [Orwell’s rules for good writing](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/):

> i. Never use a metaphor, simile or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.
> 
> ii. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
> 
> iii. If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
> 
> iv. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
> 
> v. Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
> 
> vi. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

Following these rules will not automatically eliminate confused thinking. And those who try to hard to eliminate abstractions sometimes become like the logical positivists, thinking that no utterance can have meaning unless it makes a specific prediction about one’s future sense experience. This insight can certainly be taken to an extreme. But insofar as one can, one should try to deal in the specific and the granular, rather than the nebulous and abstract. If you do so, your writing and thoughts will make more sense, and you will be significantly less likely to drunkenly stumble into defending wickedness and cruelty.
