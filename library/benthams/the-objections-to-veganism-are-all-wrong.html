Let’s define ethical veganism as the position that it is wrong to eat animal products in typical circumstances. Maybe there are rare cases where it’s alright to eat them, like if you’re consuming milk from animals that lived great lives, eating roadkill, or eating non-conscious oysters. I eat oysters! But at least in the normal situations most of us find ourselves in—at grocery stores, restaurants, and so on—eating animal products is generally wrong.

It might seem like a cop-out not to discuss meat from animals that aren’t factory farmed. But seeing as about 1% of animals are in such conditions, we should mostly talk about the things that most people do multiple times per day, rather than weird edge cases. If you’d like to read my thoughts on eating happy animals, see this article.

The basic argument for veganism is very simple: almost all animals are factory farmed. Factory farmed animals undergo extremely horrendous conditions that everyone would call torture if inflicted on a dog—they’re locked in a cage where they can’t move, chronically sleep deprived, mutilated by debeaking and dehorning, overcrowded on stressful transport trucks, routinely starved, injured so on average they break bones multiple times, given no space to turn around, and killed by being dragged upside down by one foot, stunned, and then sliced in the throat. Nearly all the animals we eat are treated in ways that cause them extreme amounts of pain and suffering; they spend most of their lives in pain and fear.

Here is a plausible ethical principle: you shouldn’t cause others lots of pain and suffering for the sake of comparatively minor benefits to yourself. It is a bad thing to be in intense pain. It would be wrong to set a dog or pig on fire for slight benefit. It is wrong, for the same reason, to hurt animals a lot so that we can enjoy the taste of their flesh. The animals we eat can feel pain, and they suffer a great deal as a result of our consumption decisions. Each time you eat animals, you inflict hours, days, or weeks of intense expected suffering on animals for the sake of a benefit that is hundreds of times less great.

Here is an analogy: imagine that a person tortured puppies in their basement. They did it because they enjoyed the smell of dead puppies, and it was cheaper to keep them in miserable conditions than pleasant ones. Such a person would be a monster. But that is very much like we do when we pay for animals to be mistreated so we can enjoy their taste. There is not a morally relevant difference between smell and taste.

In other contexts, we recognize that animal pain is a big deal. It is a very bad thing to abuse a dog. Animals may not be smart, but the badness of pain doesn’t come from one’s intelligence. It is bad to hurt even if you don’t have the capability to have complex thoughts about your hurting. It’s bad to hurt babies for the same reason. Animals are smarter than some very mentally disabled people, they are more cognitively advanced than human babies, and pigs are smarter than dogs.

This argument is extremely simple and straightforward. It strikes me as clearly right. And yet virtually no one follows the conclusion of the argument. People don’t treat meat-eating as a big deal. They give a lot of arguments for this view. Most of these arguments, I think, do not hold up at all and crumble completely if you think about them for five seconds. I thought I’d address them comprehensively. I’ll start by quoting the objection and then discuss why I don’t buy it.

It’s natural to eat meat.

This first argument says that because eating meat is natural, it is alright to eat meat. The problem is that just because something is natural does not mean it’s good. It is natural to die of disease at a young age, but that is a bad thing. It is natural to have cognitive biases and ingroup prejudice and unnatural to have running water, but running water is good and dying of disease at a young age is bad.

Other animals eat meat

This argument goes: other animals eat meat, so why shouldn’t we? You wouldn’t condemn a lion for eating a gazelle, so how would you condemn us? But the fact that a lion would do something doesn’t mean we should do it. Lions often kill their cubs when they’re young, but that would be wrong for a human to do. Lions sometimes eat people, but you shouldn’t do that.

Maybe the idea is supposed to be that if animals eat animals, then they’ve sort of forfeited their right not to be eaten. But this isn’t plausible. Babies sometimes hurt other babies, because they don’t understand right and wrong, but that doesn’t mean we can hurt babies. Some severely autistic people might kill and hurt each other if put together, but it would be wrong for us to harm them gratuitously. We know better. They don’t.

Now, would I condemn a lion for eating a gazelle? Certainly I wouldn’t say it’s doing something immoral, because it doesn’t know right from wrong. Is it doing something bad? I think probably yes—it’s causing another sentient being to suffer intensely. Lions don’t tend to read blog posts, but if any lions are reading this, I would suggest they try to kill other animals humanely. Lions are also obligate carnivores—they have to eat meat to survive. We don’t, so you shouldn’t straightforwardly extrapolate from how lions should behave to how we should.

Plant agriculture kills animals

Often animals are killed in plant harvesting. Sometimes machines that pick crops kill some animals. So, it is argued, eating a plant-based diet is the same as eating an animal-based diet in that it kills animals.

The big problem with this argument is that way more animals are killed to produce animal products than plant products. Among other things, animals eat plants, so there are more crop deaths if you eat a steak than if you eat pasta. The following chart shows the number of deaths:

As you can see, it’s way more for animal products. The other big problem with this argument is that factory farming is a lot worse than just killing an animal. Raising an animal for weeks in hell is worse than just killing it. So eating animals causes a lot more suffering than eating plants, not just more death.

Vegans can’t get enough b12/Vitamin D/protein/be healthy.

Sometimes it is argued that vegans are inevitably unhealthy because they can’t get enough b12. In fact, however, vegans tend to have better health than non-vegans, even controlling for other factors. Adopting a vegan diet will likely improve your health.

Now, it’s true that plants don’t, by default, contain b12 and have low amounts of vitamin D. That’s why if you go vegan, it’s a good idea to take a b12 supplement and a vitamin D supplement, or make sure to have fortified foods. It’s easy to get enough protein on a vegan diet, even if you have an active lifestyle. In addition, even if veganism was a bit worse for your health, it would be wrong to torture thousands of animals to slightly improve your health. Especially when there is other less costly stuff you can do to improve your health, like get regular exercise.

The circle of life.

It is the circle of life for animals higher in the food chain to eat lower ones. But what ethical implications is this supposed to have? If aliens came down to Earth and factory farmed us, that would be wrong even though it was the circle of life. As already discussed, the fact that something is natural doesn’t make it morally justified. And factory farming isn’t part of the circle of life.

Plant products are shipped from far-off lands, thus harming the environment

One common claim is that because vegan products aren’t local, they’re worse for the environment. This is wrong. First of all, there’s no reason in principle for plant products to be less local than animal products. Humans evolved to eat meat. Eating meat was responsible for our larger brains

So what? We also evolved tribalism, prejudice, violence, cognitive bias, and brutality. The fact that we evolved to do something doesn’t mean we should do it today. If it turned out humans had—like ducks—evolved specifically to rape more effectively, that wouldn’t mean rape was alright. Also, we didn’t evolve to eat factory farmed meat.

Would you condemn indigenous people for eating meat?

If indigenous people eat meat from animals treated badly, then yes, I would condemn it. If not, then I’d still think it’s wrong, but vastly less wrong than eating meat from factory farms. But in any case, whether it’s okay for indigenous tribes to hunt is a very different question from whether it’s okay for you to buy a steak from a viciously mistreated animal at the supermarket.

Animals are bred to be eaten.

The fact that we breed beings for a purpose doesn’t mean it’s okay to use them for that purpose. If you have a child for the purpose of eating it, you’re doing something wrong. It’s similarly wrong to bring animals into existence in horrendous circumstances.

Animals don’t understand morality

Neither do babies! But it’s wrong to mistreat them. To be a moral patient (one who is entitled to moral protection) you don’t need to be a moral agent (one who knows right from wrong).

Animals aren’t smart

Neither are babies or some severely mentally disabled people. Nonetheless, it’s wrong to torture and kill them.

There’s no ethical consumption under capitalism.

Lots of products are sourced from conditions where people aren’t treated very well—e.g. sweatshops. If everything is as bad as eating meat, then there’s no special reason to cut out meat. However:

  1. I don’t actually think buying from sweatshops is bad. People work in sweatshops because its their least bad option—their other options are worse. You don’t make people better off by taking away their least bad options. However, buying from goods made by slaves is, of course, bad.

  2. When you consume animals, they undergo weeks of suffering for a small benefit. Your impact on global slavery is a lot more minor. And while it’s easier to eliminate use of animals, it’s harder to eliminate every single product produced in an unethical way.

  3. Would you buy this excuse for paying some guy to torture an animal in front of you because it made a nice sound? If not, you shouldn’t buy it here.

  4. In total the world has 28 million enslaved people doing forced labor. That’s very bad. But we torture and kill around 2 billion animals per week. Your impact on animal production is vastly greater than your impact on global slavery.

It’s a personal choice.

What does it mean to declare something a personal choice? If it merely means that it is something that people choose to do, then this is a property that meat-consumption shares with every single immoral act. It is thus not much of a defense. If it means a choice that is morally okay, and should be left up to people to decide whether they want to do, then it’s obviously question-begging. It shouldn’t be up to you whether to torture and kill others.

Animals taste good

They do indeed. But you shouldn’t hurt others just because you like doing so. Cannibals sometimes report humans taste good, but they’re still acting wrongly.

Without farming, animals wouldn’t exist

Without farming, animals wouldn’t exist. Thus, it is argued, we actually benefit them by eating them. However:

  1. Very few would accept this argument for humans. If you brought humans into existence and ate them, that would be wrong on almost everyone’s view. Now, I actually think this might be okay—I’m a weird kind of consequentialist—but probably you don’t (more precisely, I think it would be good to bring them into existence, wrong to eat them, yet better to do both than neither assuming it leaves them better off). And even I grant that it’s risky enough that you shouldn’t do it absent a good reason.

  2. Nearly all the animals we eat have lives much worse than non-existence. Their lives are constant suffering, so we don’t actually benefit them by bringing them into existence.

  3. If you’re concerned about lowering animal populations, then you should probably not eat animals given animal agriculture’s enormous environmental impact, causing lots of animals to go extinct.

Production isn’t sensitive to individual purchasing decisions, so by eating meat, you have no effect.

I’ve written an article about this objection, because I think it’s one of the objections that’s not on its face ridiculous. Read that if you want more detail, but the TLDR is:

  1. For this to justify eating meat, you need to be extremely confident that it’s right. You don’t get to seriously risk torturing others for a hamburger. But given the number of smart people who think this objection fails, you shouldn’t be very confident that it succeeds.

  2. It has to be that on average one extra item of consumption leads to one extra item of production. Suppose there are 10,000 instances of consumption and 10,000 units produced. That would mean that together the 10,000 instances of consumption lead to 10,000 units of production, averaging out one unit of consumption per unit of production. But if you know consumers, on average, by their purchasing decisions increase production by one unit, you should, in expectation, think you’ll increase production by one unit in expectation. Note: I’m not actually saying it’s likely that each time you eat a burger you cause more production—instead, every time you eat, say, 600 chickens, you lead to 600 extra chickens being produced.1

  3. Even if you probably have no effect, there are thresholds at which you have huge effects. Studies on this have generally found that if 900 extra birds are consumed, production will go up by about 900.

Why are you talking about factory farms? Everyone agrees those are bad.

It’s true that if you ask most people, they’ll say factory farms are bad. The problem is that nearly all the animals we eat are factory farmed. So even though mostly people say bad things about factory farms, they purchase their products multiple times per day.

But plants are alive!

  1. It is true that plants are alive. However, they have no brain or nervous system, and no analogous physical structure that could plausibly generate consciousness. It is thus very unlikely that they are conscious—that there’s anything it’s like to be them—and thus they have no interests.

  2. Animals eat plants before we eat them, so fewer total plants are eaten if you go vegan.

Why is this a big deal when people are suffering?

It is a big deal because we torture and kill 2 billion sentient beings a week. Tens of billions of land animals reside in factory farms at any given moment, subject to nonstop torture and abuse. We inflict more suffering on animals, in a few years of factory farming, than in humans throughout all of history. Even if you think that animals being in pain is only 1% as bad as humans being in pain—a belief that is very hard to justify—factory farming is about as bad as if we were torturing and killing 80 million people every year. That would be much worse than standard human atrocities; so is this.

Now, maybe you think that torturing and killing 80 million people every year would be bad mostly because it violates rights and animals don’t have rights. But a headache doesn’t violate rights either. If every year, 80 million people were afflicted with a very intense headache that made them feel like they were being tortured for several weeks, that would be the worst human problem. And that is a very conservative estimate for the badness of farming just of land animals—if you count fish, things get much worse.

When you realize that behind the veil of ignorance, you’d be much likelier to be tortured and killed in a factory farm than born a human—that you’d be likelier to be tortured and killed in a factory farm this year than born a human in all of history—the problem begins to look serious.

Eating meat is part of my culture.

The fact that something is part of a culture doesn’t make it alright. It was part of the culture of various tribes to engage in human sacrifice, but that was morally wrong. Colonialism was part of British culture but it wasn’t right. The fact that a practice is cultural is not a blanket defense.

Conclusion

The core argument for veganism is simple, straightforward, and powerful: you shouldn’t cause others lots of pain and suffering for minor benefits. However, you do cause others lots of pain and suffering for minor benefits whenever you eat meat. So it’s wrong to eat meat in circumstances resembling the typical ones. Most of the standard excuses about taste, culture, health, lions, plant agriculture, and so on do not survive even a bit of scrutiny. If you engage in the kind of typical breezy dismissal that is all too common—consisting of a snarky remark and a platitude about lions—you are at serious risk of both making a fool of yourself and continuing to fund a horrendous atrocity. As M. Frida Hartley poetically puts it:

For creatures of Thy making
Old laws assigned for use,
New freedom stands proclaiming
Their rights and our abuse:
From beasts we scorn as soulless,
In forest, field and den,
The cry goes up to witness
The soullessness of men.

Things get a tiny bit more complicated if you take into account the cumulative elasticity factor; if you abstain from meat, the price goes down a bit and someone else might eat the meat you’d have eaten. But that has only a small effect—cumulative elasticity factor is around 3/4, meaning that each individual who consumes one less chicken reduces the amount of meat purchased by about 3/4. However, production also exceeds consumption because some meat is wasted and some animals are killed prematurely, so taking both factors into account, increasing meat consumption by one will increase production by one, in expectation.
