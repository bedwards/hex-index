Congress will decide in the coming weeks whether to approve a $1 trillion military budget for 2026.

The House-passed National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) proposes [$893 billion](https://www.congress.gov/119/crpt/hrpt231/CRPT-119hrpt231.pdf#page=474) in military spending, while the Senate version proposes [$926 billion](https://www.congress.gov/119/crpt/srpt39/CRPT-119srpt39.pdf#page=414). Whichever total prevails, the White House plans to add another [$119 billion](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Fiscal-Year-2026-Discretionary-Budget-Request.pdf#page=43) from the [One Big Beautiful Bill Act](https://costsofwar.watson.brown.edu/sites/default/files/2025-10/What%20You%20Need%20to%20Know_Final_10.23.pdf). All told, next year’s pending military budget stands between $1.012 trillion and $1.045 trillion.

Money is policy. Should Congress approve such a historic sum, it would not only enable many of Trump’s dangerous and unjust policies — including [military occupations](https://theintercept.com/2025/11/11/cost-trump-national-guard-military-occupation/) of US cities, the [resumption](https://www.un-diplomatic.com/p/fresh-hell-unjust-nuclear-deterrence) of nuclear weapons testing, and rushing toward wars in [Mexico](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/national-security/trump-administration-planning-new-mission-mexico-cartels-current-forme-rcna241167), [Nigeria](https://inkstick.substack.com/p/critical-state-will-trump-launch), and [Venezuela](https://www.crisisgroup.org/latin-america-caribbean/venezuela/beware-slide-toward-regime-change-venezuela) — it would also trigger a historic redistribution of wealth from the public to private arms companies and their shareholders.

### **A looming half-trillion-dollar privatization of wealth**

More than half of next year’s trillion-dollar Pentagon budget would likely go to private companies. A recent [study](https://costsofwar.watson.brown.edu/sites/default/files/papers/Profits-of-War_Hartung-Semler_Costs-of-War_Quincy-FINAL.pdf) from Brown University’s Costs of War project found that over half of Pentagon spending from 2020 to 2024 went to for-profit contractors. For *Popular Information*, I expanded the report’s sample size by another 20 years (I should know how to do this, as I co-authored the Brown study.[1](#footnote-1)) Since 2000, 53% of Pentagon spending has gone to contracts.[2](#footnote-2) By that figure, the 2026 military budget would likely privatize well over $500 billion in public funds.

### **Four military contractors, ten years, $103 billion in taxpayer-funded buybacks and dividends**

More than a quarter of next year’s Pentagon contract dollars will likely go to just four companies: Lockheed Martin, Raytheon (now RTX), General Dynamics, and Northrop Grumman.[3](#footnote-3)

A review of each contracting giant’s [10-K filings](https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/form_10-k) over the last decade reveals what should be a scandal: Taxpayers are subsidizing arms company shareholders. Based on their share of revenue from sales to the US government, Lockheed Martin, Raytheon (RTX), General Dynamics, and Northrop Grumman used public funds to cover $103 billion in buybacks and dividends over the last decade. US taxpayers covered two out of every three dollars these companies spent enriching their shareholders.[4](#footnote-4) This is a conservative estimate.[5](#footnote-5)

Two main factors make these billions in shareholder subsidies possible. First, the extent to which these firms rely on public funds. Here’s how much of their 2015–2024 revenue came from US government contracts:

-   Lockheed Martin: 72%
    
-   Raytheon (RTX): 51%
    
-   General Dynamics: 64%
    
-   Northrop Grumman: 85%
    

Second, despite these firms effectively being public firms, US political leaders allow them to spend vast amounts of taxpayer dollars rewarding their shareholders. In 2024, each spent an average of $4.3 billion on [stock buybacks](https://hbr.org/2014/09/profits-without-prosperity) and dividends — well above the S&P 500 average of [$3.1 billion](https://www.oxfamamerica.org/press/largest-us-corporations-spent-nearly-16-trillion-on-shareholder-payouts-in-2024-triple-the-income-of-the-poorest-fifth-of-us-households/) — and collectively paid out $154 billion in buybacks and dividends over the last decade.

### **Class warfare**

To review, it’s likely that more than half of next year’s $1 trillion military budget will be privatized through contracts, over a quarter of those contracts will go to just four firms, and more than one in ten federal dollars these firms receive will go to their shareholders.[6](#footnote-6)

Military spending’s redistribution of wealth produces few winners and many losers. Winners include the top 1%, who own [50%](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WFRBST01122) of stocks and mutual funds, compared to the bottom 50%, who own just [1%](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WFRBSB50203).

The buybacks and dividends further enrich wealthy shareholders and [represent](https://www.politico.com/news/magazine/2025/10/27/lockheed-martin-strike-orlando-weapons-missiles-00514386) lost wages for arms industry employees. This is part of the reason why, despite surging Pentagon budgets, arms industry salaries are [declining](https://inkstickmedia.com/what-a-looming-1-trillion-pentagon-budget-means-for-jobs/) — [executive pay](https://www.stephensemler.com/p/bidens-farewell-foreign-policy-speech) being the exception.

The working and middle classes will bear the brunt of the trade-offs of the $1 trillion budget itself. To offset the cost of historic levels of military spending, social programs were cut, leaving most Americans [worse off](https://www.stephensemler.com/p/big-beautiful-bill-will-leave-most).

This is not a sign of a country on the ascent. Rather, it suggests a state in decline and addicted to war, two traits that often go together.
