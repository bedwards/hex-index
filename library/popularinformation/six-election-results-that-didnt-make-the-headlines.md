On X, Vice President J.D. Vance dismissed the poor showing by Republicans on Tuesday night as “[a couple of elections in blue states](https://x.com/JDVance/status/1986099131845136594).” Although Democratic victories in New York, Virginia, New Jersey, and California dominated the headlines, significant contests were held across the nation.

In purple states like Pennsylvania and Georgia, and deep red states like Texas and Mississippi, voters rejected the MAGA agenda. Here are six results from the 2025 elections that flew under the radar.

### **Pennsylvania county ousts sheriff who collaborated with ICE**

In Bucks County — Pennsylvania’s largest swing county, which Trump [narrowly won](https://www.cbsnews.com/philadelphia/news/donald-trump-bucks-county-pennsylvania-election/) in 2024 — Democrat Danny Ceisler was elected county sheriff after the Republican incumbent signed a deal to collaborate with Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) earlier this year.

In April, Republican Fred Harran signed his deputies up for a [287(g) program](https://whyy.org/articles/ice-bucks-county-sheriff-immigrant-rights/) with ICE, which allows local law enforcement to execute arrest warrants for immigration violations and perform other duties typically reserved for federal immigration enforcement officers.

Ceisler, who defeated Harran with more than 55% of the vote, described the ICE agreement as “[the big issue in this race](https://whyy.org/articles/elections-2025-pennsylvania-bucks-county-sheriff/).” Ceisler promised to suspend the agreement immediately after taking office and said that restoring trust with immigrants in the county was a top priority.

### **Progressives win control of Texas school board that censored books**

In Texas’s Cypress-Fairbanks Independent School District (CFISD), which is the third largest in the state, progressive candidates won all three open board seats, giving them a [4-3 majority](https://www.houstonpublicmedia.org/articles/education/2025/11/04/534968/cy-fair-isd-election-results-school-board-conservative-policies/). Since 2023, the board has been controlled by a 6-1 conservative supermajority, which has enacted a range of far-right policies.

In the past two years, the CFISD board has [removed textbook chapters](https://www.houstonchronicle.com/news/houston-texas/education/article/cyfair-isd-science-textbook-changes-politics-19509057.php) on climate change, vaccines, Covid-19, and diversity; fired over half of CFISD librarians, leading to school library [closures](https://www.houstonpublicmedia.org/articles/news/education-news/2024/09/11/499594/cy-fair-isds-libraries-are-frequently-closed-after-trustees-cut-librarian-positions-in-half/); restricted school library content; and created elective [courses about the Bible](https://www.houstonpublicmedia.org/articles/education/2024/08/13/496540/cy-fair-district-board-of-trustees-voted-unanimously-to-approve-bible-focused-elective-courses-for-2025-26-school-year/).

One of the members who lost her seat, Natalie Blasingame, first ran for the CFISD board in 2015. At the time, she wrote in an [email to donors](https://www.texasmonthly.com/news-politics/cy-fair-isd-board-christian-right/), “The Lord put on my heart that my agenda is to tear down the over-interpretation of the separation of church and state that has shut God out of schools.”

### **Georgia elects its first Democrat to a non-federal statewide office since 2006**

Two Georgia Democrats, Peter Hubbard and Alicia Johnson, won election to the state’s Public Service Commission, which [regulates electricity, telephone services, and natural gas](https://www.cbsnews.com/atlanta/news/georgia-psc-race-result-alicia-johnson-peter-hubbard-projected-win/). Both candidates won with over 60% of the vote, according to [unofficial results](https://results.sos.ga.gov/results/public/Georgia/elections/MunicipalGeneralSpecialElectionPSC11042025) posted on the Georgia Secretary of State’s website. The commission will now have a [3-2 Republican majority](https://apnews.com/article/georgia-public-service-commission-democrats-republicans-election-13064b8409c924571c4ebb5d356c5e15).

The commission [has not had a Democrat since 2007](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/04/us/georgia-election-utility-board-results.html), and this is the first time since 2006 that Georgia Democrats have “[won a statewide constitutional office](https://georgiarecorder.com/2025/11/04/democrat-alicia-johnson-appears-to-defeat-longtime-georgia-utility-regulator/).” Johnson will also become the state’s first [Black woman elected to a statewide partisan office](https://apnews.com/article/georgia-public-service-commission-democrats-republicans-election-13064b8409c924571c4ebb5d356c5e15).

The Democratic candidates ran on a [platform of affordability](https://georgiarecorder.com/2025/11/04/georgia-utility-races-seen-as-potential-bellwether-for-2026-midterms/). The commission [approved six rate hikes](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/04/us/georgia-election-utility-board-results.html) for electric utility provider Georgia Power in recent years. Rate increases approved by the commission have added around “[$43 a month to the average household bill](https://www.cbsnews.com/atlanta/news/georgia-psc-race-result-alicia-johnson-peter-hubbard-projected-win/),” which is now over $175 a month for the average Georgia Power residential customer, CBS [reported](https://www.cbsnews.com/atlanta/news/georgia-psc-race-result-alicia-johnson-peter-hubbard-projected-win/).

The upset could provide momentum going into the 2026 midterm elections, when Georgia will elect a Governor and U.S. Senator.

### **Mississippi Democrats break Republican super majority in the state Senate**

For the first time in [13 years](http://can-senate-supermajority-flipping-3-legislative-seats/), Democrats in Mississippi have broken up a GOP supermajority in the state Senate. This victory for Mississippi Democrats came after a panel of federal judges ordered the Mississippi legislature to redraw its state House and Senate districts to create more [majority-Black districts](https://www.mississippifreepress.org/court-approves-lawmakers-redrawn-mississippi-senate-district-map/). The court found that the previous districts diluted the political power of Black voters, in violation of Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act.

Democrats were able to flip two newly redistricted state Senate seats, reducing the GOP supermajority from 36 members to 34. Just one vote short of a supermajority, it will now be more difficult for Republicans to propose amendments to the state constitution and override the governor’s veto.

Whether the victory holds will depend on [an upcoming Supreme Court decision](https://apnews.com/live/supreme-court-voting-rights-arguments-updates) in a case challenging the constitutionality of creating electoral districts based on racial demographics to give minority voters more power.

### **Colorado voters increase taxes on the wealthy to pay for school meals**

In Colorado, voters passed a pair of ballot measures to fund free meals at public schools across the state.

[Proposition MM](https://www.coloradosos.gov/pubs/elections/Initiatives/ballot/contacts/2025.html) will raise taxes for households with incomes of more than $300,000 per year to fund free school meals, buy local ingredients in school cafeterias, and provide raises to cafeteria workers. Any excess funds raised by the tax increase will [support](https://coloradosun.com/2025/11/04/proposition-mm-results-colorado-election-2025/) the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP).

Proposition MM expands on Proposition FF, passed by Colorado voters in 2022, which “[limited income tax deductions for people earning more than $300,000](https://coloradosun.com/2025/11/04/proposition-ll-election-results-colorado-free-school-meals/)” to fund free school meals through the Healthy School Meals for All program. Colorado voters also [passed](https://apps.npr.org/2025-election-results/colorado.html?section=I) Proposition LL, which will [allow](https://www.coloradosos.gov/pubs/elections/Initiatives/ballot/contacts/2025.html) the state to keep additional tax revenue that was raised by Proposition FF and spend it on the Healthy School Meals for All Program.

### **Maine voters reject voter ID ballot initiative**

In Maine, voters overwhelmingly rejected a ballot initiative that would have created new voter ID requirements and made absentee voting less accessible, with over 60% of voters opposing the initiative, according to the [AP](https://apnews.com/projects/election-results-2025/maine/).

If passed, voters would have been required to [present photo identification](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/maine-rejects-anti-voting-ballot-measure-reaffirms-voting-access/) in future elections. The [initiative](https://www.maine.gov/sos/news/november-2025-referendum-election-ballot-order-referendum-questions-announced) would have also banned “prepaid postage on absentee ballot return envelopes,” prohibited “requests for absentee ballots by phone or family members,” and ended “ongoing absentee voter status for seniors and people with disabilities.” Additionally, the initiative would have limited the number of ballot drop boxes to only [one per town](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/maine-rejects-anti-voting-ballot-measure-reaffirms-voting-access/).

Opponents called the initiative a “[voter suppression bill](https://mainemorningstar.com/2025/11/04/mainers-reject-voter-id-proposal-opting-to-maintain-state-voting-laws/).” In 2024, about 45% of Maine voters cast ballots by mail, used drop boxes, or voted early, Maine Public [reported](https://www.mainepublic.org/politics/2025-11-04/mainers-reject-voter-id-absentee-ballot-restrictions-as-question-1-fails).
