What City Observatory Did This Week

The Interstate Bridge Project continues to fall behind schedule. In 2020, IBR told legislators they’d have a record of decision in the summer of 2023. It now appears that the earliest a record of decision will be released is April of 2026 more than two and a half years late.

These delays contribute to driving up project costs–and creating more billable hours for consultants. State transportation officials routinely miss their own deadlines, provide vague and misleading claims about being “on schedule”–never acknowledging that they’ve failed to execute their own plans, and that this is the reason for higher costs.

Metro's Climate and Transportation Plans are failing. Testifying to the Metro Council on September 18, Joe Cortright called out the failure of the region's climate plans. Transportation is the largest source of greenhouse gases in the Portland area, and is increasing, at a time when our plans call for GHGs to be decreasing.

Metro's Regional Transportation Plan (RTP) counted on congestion pricing to reduce driving and greenhouse gases, lower traffic congestion and help pay for roads, but is now headed for repeal in the Oregon Legislature. Repeal turns the Regional Transportation Plan into a dead letter.

Billions of dollars of highway expansion projects—the Interstate Bridge, Rose Quarter and Abernethy Bridge—are predicated on traffic forecasts that assume total driving in Metro Portland increases by 20-25 percent in the next two decades, when the Metro climate plan and RTP promise to reduce total driving by about 10-12 percent.

Metro needs to acknowledge, disclose and act on these facts: The region's climate plan and transportation plans are failing and need to be revisited immediately.

Must Read

Chuck Marohn of Strong Towns digs deep into a highway expansion project being peddled as an economic tonic for rural Minnesota. Here, the state highway department is proposing a massive, auto-oriented interchange to speed traffic around and through a largely suburban, big-box retail area.

As Marohn points out, the total cost of the project actually exceeds the value of the commercial property it serves.

The entire system of highway funding is treated like economic development, when in reality, it has become the single greatest source of wealth destruction in our local communities. The absurdity runs deep. In Baxter, Corridors of Commerce is spending $58 million to serve commerce on $40.8 million of property. It doesn’t have to be this way. . . . If Corridors of Commerce really aimed to foster economic growth, it would look very different. Slowing traffic for eight blocks in Brainerd, planting trees, improving bike and walk crossings — these are tiny investments with huge returns. A fraction of what we’re spending on the buttonhook would yield far greater economic impact in the core of Brainerd.

Our auto-dependent patterns of commercial development both create the need for continuing subsidies like this, and also serve the interests of people driving through a community, rather than the residents who live there. In addition, subsidizing auto-oriented development undercuts the market for traditional downtowns, like those in Brainerd. As Marohn argues, this destroys more economic value than it creates.

Is sustainable aviation fuel just greenwashing? A press release from Portland International Airport, Delta Airlines and Zenith Fuel brags about the pilot implementation of "sustainable aviation fuel" (SAF) in Portland. The fuel is actually a 50/50 blend of conventional fossil fuel and biofuels. And even that mix is vastly more expensive than conventional fuel:

Delta Airlines was the first commercial airline to use the fuel at PDX. But the cost to keep jets in the air isn’t cheap. Charlotte Lollar of Delta Airlines said the airline is working with different suppliers to manage expenses. “Today it’s two to three times more expensive than conventional jet fuel, so our ambition is to work through the supply chain to bring the cost down as much as possible,” Lollar said.

It's unclear that there will be any large scale use of such fuels at such prices because fuel is a major cost of flying. But touting the existence of such fuels helps mask the significant environmental impacts of air travel, which are a large and growing source of greenhouse gas emissions. In addition, environmental groups, like 350PDX, point out that even the "bio" portion of these fuels has dubious environmental benefits. According to KGW, 350PDX said:

When you add the word ‘sustainable’ or ‘renewable’ it sounds better, but it’s greenwashing at best. First, this is still a fuel and it still releases CO2 into the atmosphere. Second, the land use changes needed to produce this fuel can lead to increased pollution and a decrease in nature-based carbon sequestration solutions.

Writing at Bloomberg Business, Justin Fox looks at trends in work-at-home, and finds while more and more workers are returning to the office, that hybrid work remains a fixture in most places. Contrary to the naive view that work-at-home would simply propel rural development, much of the work at home, and especially hybrid work, seems concentrated in cities and urban neighborhoods.

New Knowledge

What casinos tell us about economic development. It's a widespread belief that a major project like a shopping center, sports stadium, or office building generates jobs, and that these increase the size of the local economy. But is that true? A new study, using the location of casinos as a kind of natural experiment looks at patterns of job growth and decline in response to a changed development pattern.

The authors look at the changes in employment patterns in areas where new casinos opened, and then compares them to the surrounding areas. In general, opening a casino increases employment at that location and very close by, but the employment effect turns negative as one goes further away. It seems very likely that projects like these simply re-distribute the location of economic activity within a very small area, rather than increasing total activity.

This seems especially logical if one recognizes that much or all of the revenue of casinos (like shopping centers or sports stadiums) comes from spending by local residents. The effect of a casino location is to change where people spend their money, not how much money they have to spend. And clearly there are "agglomeration effects"--i.e. if you operate another complementary business (a gas station, a convenience store, or say, a pawn shop), its better to be close to the casino (and its customers) rather than far away. The same effect is often observed with WalMart locations in rural areas: the only thing worse than having the WalMart open in your town, is having the WalMart open in the next town over--changing the center of retail trade.

This study of casino's shows all of the positive effects are within 5 minutes driving of the casino, and outside that area, the employment effect is negative. They conclude that the overall multiplier for the labor market may be as much (or more) that -1, meaning that the casino adds no net jobs to the local economy; each new job at the casino is associated with one fewer job elsewhere in the community.

Ari Anisfeld and Jordan Rosenthal-Kay, "How local is local development? Evidence from casinos," Regional Science and Urban Economics, Volume 115, November 2025, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0166046225000560?dgcid=rss_sd_all

In the news

The Portland Oregonian reported on Joe Cortright's testimony to the joint Oregon-Washington legislative committee on the Interstate Bridge Replacement.

But critics, including economist Joe Cortright, with the Portland policy think tank City Observatory, said lawmakers need to contend with escalating costs that he expects will be "squarely in the $10 billion range," telling lawmakers that would mean Oregon and Washington will each need to come up with another $1 billion in funding.

Cortright also questioned whether the traffic assumptions on which tolling estimates are based are inflated and whether it's prudent to count on the final $1 billion in federal funding, given ongoing cuts to the federal budget.

"Federal funding is very, very much in doubt," Cortright said, later calling it "reckless to embark on this project" when all the funding isn't secured.
