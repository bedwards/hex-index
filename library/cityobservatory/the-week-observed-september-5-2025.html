What City Observatory Did This Week

Ten things to know about the transportation package. Oregon's Legislature met in special session over the Labor Day weekend to develop a new transportation package, now known as HB 3991. The $4.3 billion bill is in limbo as the Senate struggles to assemble the votes needed to pass it. While everyone is waiting, we have a summary of the issues that this package raises.

  1. Cost Overruns - ODOT's financial problems stem from mega-project cost explosions, like the Abernethy Bridge tripling to $815 million and Rose Quarter quadrupling to $2.1 billion.

  2. No Maintenance Requirements - HB 3991 contains no language requiring new revenue go to actual maintenance and operations rather than mega-projects.

  3. Borrowing Against Future Revenue - ODOT can use debt to divert new tax revenue to fund mega-project overruns, having already borrowed $271 million this way.

  4. No Real Accountability - Proposed oversight measures mirror previous failed attempts like task forces and dashboards that didn't prevent cost overruns.

  5. False Maintenance Priorities - ODOT claims to prioritize maintenance over expansion but historically does the opposite after securing funding.

  6. Misleading Spending Comparisons - ODOT's claims that Oregon spends less on transportation than other states are fabricated and inaccurate.

  7. Diverting Existing Funds - ODOT has repeatedly moved operations and preservation money to cover mega-project costs like the Abernethy Bridge.

  8. Revenue Hasn't Declined - Motor fuel tax revenue actually increased $97 million over five years, contradicting ODOT's revenue decline claims.

  9. Hidden IBR Cost Estimates - ODOT is concealing new Interstate Bridge Replacement costs likely reaching $9-10 billion, with Oregon owing $1-2 billion more (see next commentary).

  10. Rose Quarter Deficit - The Rose Quarter project faces a $1.5 billion shortfall but ODOT continues construction despite state analysis showing completion may be impossible.

Watch out for a $10 billion price-tag for the. Interstate Bridge. Even as it moves forward with this $4.3 billion "band-aid"--legislators are due for yet another multi-billion dollar surprise, as IBR is once again delaying releasing a new cost estimate for the Interstate Bridge Project. It's an ominous sign that the cost is going to be much, much higher.

IBR leaders have known since January of 2024 that costs were going to be even higher--but repeatedly they've delayed releasing a new estimate.

In testimony to the Portland City Council project director Greg Johnson announced that there would be yet another delay, until late 2025 or early 2026-- in telling the Oregon and Washington Legislatures and the public how much the IBR project will cost.

The IBR cost estimate, which jumped from a maximum of $4.8 billion in 2020 to as much as $7.5 billion in 2022, has grown increasingly stale. Vintage 2022 ODOT forecasts have experienced 40-60 percent increases; a similar increase would easily put the Interstate Bridge Project in the $9-$10 billion range.

Even as the Oregon Legislature has debated, and now held a special session on transportation finance, ODOT and WDOT have held off disclosing the new higher cost of the Interstate Bridge Project. So much for "accountability."

Must Read

Does abundance help distressed communities? Tim Bartik, of the Upjohn Institute, is one of the nation's leading scholars on labor markets and economic development. In a new essay, he raises major challenges to the theory behind the abundance movement. Abundance, in his view, focuses heavily on the problems of encouraging and accommodating more growth (especially housing) in the nation's economically most vibrant metro areas. The theory is that by stimulating more job creation in these areas, and building the housing to accommodate more workers, we can restore opportunities for people to achieve economic opportunity by moving to these highly productive cities.

Bartik asks where that leaves distressed communities. His answer is that encouraging out-migration from these communities will actually make their prospects worse, and may do relatively little to help even those who leave. Bartik writes:

The abundance movement’s advice to residents of distressed places can be summarized in two words: Get out. To help residents of distressed places to leave those places, the movement proposes to make it easier to build new housing in booming places. Thus, more residents of distressed places will be able to find affordable housing in those places, and these migrants will increase their real earnings.

Encouraging out-migration from distress communities is likely to worsen their prospects for several reasons; out-migration will tend to depress local economies and worsen the fiscal plight of declining communities. In addition, the out-migrants are much more likely to be younger and better educated; the loss of this demographic would deprive communities of their best chances for entrepreneurship and make these communities less attractive to employers.

His solution is to focus more energy on job creation in distressed regions, and do a better job of training and connecting those who live in distressed neighborhoods to the job opportunities that exist.

Elements of case for the "abundance" make a lot of sense, particularly the observations about housing, and how we've throttled population growth and access to opportunity in the most productive places in America. But Bartik's thoughtful critique will remind us that much of the nation still faces different and difficult economic challenges that won't simply be solved by abundance.

Educational Attainment Maps. Kyle Walker—a master of R for statistics—has a powerful illustration of how this software tool can be used to generate detailed, informative and dynamic maps. This particular map shows the distribution of the US adult population by educational attainment. Different colored dots represent numbers of persons having completed different levels of schooling.

You see in an instant both the concentration of population and the educational attainment level. You can activate and deactivate different education levels to easily compare and contrast the pattern of habitation of different areas by well educated and less well educated populations.

Here are two maps of the Portland Metropolitan area showing the location of adults with a four-year degree or higher education (right) compared to those with only a high school degree or less (left).

In Portland, adults with a higher education tend to be strongly clustered in the center of the region, compared to those with less education who tend to be more likely to be found in the suburbs.

If you want to better understand the spatial structure and socio-economic composition of the nation's metro areas, this is a great place to be able to quickly draw a picture of how these differences in educational attainment of the population.
