What City Observatory Did this Week

Another Portland area highway mega-project has a catastrophic cost overrun. We’ve tried to like the Burnside Bridge project. Unlike other Portland-area transportation mega-projects, its pretty resolutely focused on just a like-for-like, but seismically updated replacement for the nearly hundred year old Burnside Bridge in downtown Portland—no “auxiliary” lanes, massive interchanges or elevated approaches. It should stand as an object lesson to OregonDOT that you don’t need to—and shouldn’t—expand highway capacity in urban centers. Alas, the project, which was supposed to have cost about $965 million has now doubled in price to as much as $1.8 billion

This is leading to the indefinite postponement (zombie-status) for the project. While Multnomah County officials want to blame the federal funding uncertainty, the bridge was never a good candidate for major federal funding, and the huge cost overrun is hardly the fed’s fault. Our best estimate is that on a price and quantity adjusted basis, the proposed Burnside Bridge is now three to six times more expensive than two recently constructed Willamette River bridges in Portland. It’s more evidence of the chronic problem of initial estimates “low-balling” the cost of a project to get approval, knowing that once some funding is committed and some earth is moved, political pressure will build to “get ‘er done” regardless of the cost. That appears to be what is at work here, as well.

An interview with Lars Larson. Conservative talk-show host Lars Larson asks whether the Interstate Bridge Project is “already dead.” There are plenty of signs that the project is in trouble: it has fallen more than two years behind the schedule it set in 2020 for completing its environmental review, has delayed producing a new cost estimate for almost two years, seems likely to grow in cost to the $9-10 billion range, is struggling to convince the Coast Guard to allow a relatively low fixed span structure that will impede river traffic, and stands to lose billions of dollars of federal money because of delays and Trump Administration hostility. On top of all that, its director for the past five years just announced he’s stepping down.

Larson and City Obervatory’s Joe Cortright discuss these issues, and whether the project will die. More likely, as did its doppelgänger, the proposed Columbia River Crossing, it will turn into a zombie project--still nestled in the plans of state highway departments, but lacking any resources to actually get built. Audio of the ten minute interview is available at City Observatory.

Must Read

Building a new freeway in LA: Alissa Walker, writing at Torched, points out that even as it spends billions to try to get more people to travel to Los Angeles International Airport, its also widening the access road into an expressway.

Yes, the very same airport where we just spent $2 billion — actually, more like $3 billion, plus another $900 million if you count Metro’s investment in the LAX station; so let’s just say $4 billion — to make cars go away. After all that talk about car-free connections for the transit-first games, LAX is dropping 5.8 lane miles of roadways in one of the most congested areas of LA.

The plan is to spend $1.5 billion on the airport freeway between now and 2028, a classic example of the muddled priorities and mixed messaging sent by “multi-modal” planning. The science of induced travel means that building more roadway capacity will increase traffic and pollution, and not incidentally undercut the viability of the public investment in mass transit. Hardly a gold medal performance in sustainability.

BikePortland calls Monday’s 3-1 vote by the Oregon Transportation Commission to advance the Interstate Bridge Project as ominous. One of four OTC commissioner’s voted against more funding for the IBR, citing uncertainty about the project’s design, and an utter lack of a current accurate cost estimate. IBR staff presented slides purporting to show that the project complied with state requirements showing a positive benefit-cost ratio, but Commissioner Jeff Baker pointed out that one couldn’t say anything accurate about the the benefit cost ratio, or whether the project on net had economic benefits, without an accurate cost estimate:

All of these [benefits] are formulated with cost, . . . and we don’t know what the cost is… so to try to rely on these numbers today for this presentation, I really struggle with it, because I don’t think they’re accurate, and they won’t be accurate till we get our new costs. I also struggle with the notion that we’re putting $2 billion forward here without having an answer on what this bridge is going to look like… I can’t support the request based on wrong information.”

Baker was not the only OTC member to raise serious doubts. OTC Chair Julie Brown asked about the outlook for a further $1 billion in federal grants that IBR is hoping to get from the Trump Administration to pay for the light rail component of the project. As Bike Portland noted, blue cities like Chicago and New York have seen big hits from the administration, clouding the outlook for light rail funding for the IBR.

In the News

KGW television interviewed City Observatory’s Joe Cortright for its story on the resignation of the long-time director of the troubled Interstate Bridge Replacement project, “Interstate Bridge Replacement Program set to lose project leader in December.” IBR is years behind schedule, has delayed releasing a new cost estimate that is likely to push the cost of the project into the $9-10 billion range, and hasn’t gotten needed Coast Guard approval. It quoted Cortright as saying:

“Johnson’s departure signals that it’s kind of unsettled, unsettled time right now. It’s not a propitious time to have to be changing leaders when we’re facing these critical decisions. . . . I know there’ll be a lot of finger pointing here, but I think the blame really lies squarely on the people who have designed a project that lacks financing and may not even be approvable by the Coast Guard.”
