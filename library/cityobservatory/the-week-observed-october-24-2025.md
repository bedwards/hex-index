### What City Observatory Did this Week

[The Oregon Department of Transportation’s worsening reign of error](https://cityobservatory.org/reign_of_error2025/). The cost of ODOT mega-projects continues to explode, blowing up the state’s budget. Despite claiming its becoming better managed and more accountable, ODOT megaproject budgets continue to spiral out of control.

Every major Oregon highway project routinely sees its costs double or triple. The latest instances are in Salem and Hood River:

-   The proposed Salem Center Street Bridge seismic improvement project has grown from an estimated $60 million in 2017 to $470 million now, a nine-fold increase.
    
-   A proposed Hood River Bridge across the Columbia River has doubled in price from $512 million to $1.1 billion.
    

Nearly three years ago, we described ODOT’s mega-project cost-excesses as a “[reign of error](https://cityobservatory.org/odots-reign-of-error-chronic-highway-cost-overruns/).” But in the succeeding time, even as it claimed to care deeply about accountability and efficiency, the cost of every single major project has continued to increase. These chronic cost overruns, and [not slow-growing revenues](https://cityobservatory.org/oregondots-real-financial-problem-isnt-revenue-its-mega-project-cost-overruns/) are the source of the agency’s financial problems--something they have yet to admit.

[Federal transportation re-authorization needs to hold states accountable for results](https://enotrans.org/article/advocates-responding-to-policy-changes-and-preparing-for-reauthorization/). The ENO transportation institute had a panel discussion about the potential up-coming re-authorization of federal transportation legislation. It heard from experts in transit, active transportation, and transportation policy. We were most struck by comments from Beth Osborne--[newly designated president](https://www.smartgrowthamerica.org/knowledge-hub/news/press-release-beth-osborne-announced-as-president-and-ceo-of-smart-growth-america/) and CEO of Smart Growth America and Transportation for America.

[Previously](https://thewaroncars.org/2025/02/25/episode-145-whats-happening-with-federal-transportation-dollars/), she’s pointed out that transportation legislation has tended to provide token categorical support for non-highway modes, while allocating most funds to state highway agencies with no accountability. The result is we get either nothing, or just more of the same for our transportation investment:

> When Osborne was asked whether the federal government should prescribe specific uses for transportation funds or allow states greater discretion in how they use federal funds, she responded emphatically and passionately, “We’ve been told that we were going to get safer roads. Wrong. Lied. We were going to get better roads and bridges and they would be brought into a state of repair. Lie. We were going to get less congestion. Another lie.
> 
> She reminded the webinar attendees that accountability was one of her primary concerns. “At some point,” she said, “We need a process that gives the people what they were promised or cuts off the funds. And we can do that by telling the states, ‘You go figure out how.’ But if you don’t produce the results, then you lose discretion, money, something.”

High on our list of priorities ought to be assuring a good state of repair of the massive investment in roads and transit, ensuring safety, especially for vulnerable road users, and reducing the climate impact of transportation. Simply writing checks and hoping for good things to happen hasn’t worked.

[The long arc: more driving and car-dependence in New York City](https://nyc.streetsblog.org/2025/10/22/heavy-traffic-driving-continues-to-rise-undermining-literally-every-effort-to-make-the-city-better). For the past nine months, we’ve focused on the huge strides that New York City has made in reducing congestion, improving safety, lowering pollution, and speeding buses, thanks to congestion pricing. But a new report summarized by Streetsblog New York shows that congestion pricing has been fighting a long-term pattern of increased driving and growing car dependence in New York.

> The number of miles driven within the five boroughs is increasing — a trend that both challenges efforts to promote more sustainable modes of transportation while it also undermines Vision Zero street safety initiatives, pollution reduction efforts and the traffic mitigation of congestion pricing, a new report makes clear. The analysis, “Hit the Brakes: Understanding and Reducing Vehicle Miles Traveled in NYC,” reveals that VMTs have risen by 16 percent between 2005 and 2023, even though the population of the city rose only 1.4 percent in that period.

Contrary to popular belief, the increases in driving having nothing to do with ride-hailing services like Uber and Lyft; for hire vehicles account for about the same share of driving now as two decades ago. The real issue appears to be increasing car ownership and more driving by households in the region. The report recommends a three-pronged approach for reducing VMT: investing in alternatives like bus rapid transit, changing incentives (like pricing congested roadways), and cutting public policies that subsidize private car use. Together these actions would make roads safer, reduce congestion, lower greenhouse gas emissions, and save households in the region money.

[Repairing roads creates more jobs than building new ones](https://ssti.us/2025/10/21/repairing-highways-is-better-for-the-economy-than-expanding-them/). A key rationale for spending money on roads is that it creates lots of construction jobs. Truth be told, spending money building anything produces employment. But within the field of transportation a new study confirms earlier work that money spent rehabilitating transportation infrastructure produces more jobs than new construction. Chris McCahill of SSTI reports:

> A new report from Good Jobs First finds that every dollar spent on rehabilitation creates more jobs than new construction. On federally aided highway resurfacing projects, each $1 billion generated 10,421 job-years of work, compared to 9,316 job-years for new highway construction.

Conceptually, that makes sense: rehabilitation work generally involves less material and design, and more on-site labor, which also means that more of the jobs accrue to workers living near the project. At a time when we’re near full employment, pouring more money into transportation actually tends to fuel inflation. If there is a rationale for road spending as a job creation program, its when the economy is in a downturn, and rehabilitation work makes much more sense because it can be organized and performed quickly, while new construction projects often take years.

[The false promise of “plug-in” hybrid electric vehicles](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/smoke-screen-the-growing-phev-emissions-scandal). One of the transition strategies that automakers have proposed to reduce carbon emissions is to produce hybrid electric vehicles that can be charged by plugging them into the domestic power grid. In theory, these vehicles can primarily rely on electricity, rather than their internal combustion engine, particularly if they are charged regularly and driven short distances. But in practice, few users of these plug-in hybrids rely much on grid charging, and instead most of the energy to propel the vehicles comes from their internal combustion engines. As a result, a new study from Europe shows that plug-in hybrids generate much more carbon emissions that predicted.

In theory, if they rely primarily on charging and electric batteries, plug-in vehicles should emit about 35 grams of carbon per kilometer traveled. In reality, they generate about four times as much, because they use their internal combustion engine much more commonly. The exaggerated benefits of plug-in hybrids is yet another example of why we should be cautious and skeptical of claims made about technical fixes for solving climate.
