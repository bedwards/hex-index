### Must Read

[Crime is down in supposedly “crime-ridden” cities](https://www.pbump.net/o/a-quick-reminder-that-crime-is-down-in-u-s-cities/). The Trump administration is using false claims of a surge in crime and violence as a pretext for sending armed troops to major American cities. While these builds on a widely shared--but again, patently false--right wing trope that cities are dangerous and crime-ridden places, actual data show that these cities are safe, and getting safer.

Philip Bump presents the latest data, covering the first half of 2025, showing how crime has fared in five cities singled out by the Trump Administration for military intervention. In every one of these cities, violent crime is down compared to a year ago.

[Jarrett Walker on weather, walkability and elite projection](https://humantransit.org/2025/10/nobody-walks-here-its-too-hot-or-cold-or-wet-or-dry.html). A common criticism of efforts to promote walking and biking is that not everyone will find it easy or convenient to walk or cycle on every single occasion. It’s too cold, too wet, too hot, too hilly for people to chose active transportation modes. As Jarrett Walker points out, this critique ignores the practical reality that many people do walk and bike despite these obstacles, often because they have no choice.

Deciding transportation policy for the comfort and preferences of those with abundant choices is a kind of “elite projection” that overlooks the need and opportunity to make transportation widely available.

> The functionality of a city, and of its transport system, arises from the sum of everyone’s choices about how to travel, not just the preferences of elites. When elites make pronouncements about what “people” will tolerate, while really speaking only of themselves, they mislead us about how cities actually succeed. They also demean the contributions of the vast majority of people who are in fact tolerating extreme weather to do whatever will give their lives meaning and value.

Walker argues that the bias of elite projection, which often plays a decisive role in shaping policy, prioritizes the views of those who are actually the least resilient and adaptable

> The human ability to adapt is the key to our spectacular success on this planet. Our problem is that the people who lead our public conversations, our elites of wealth and opinion, are often some of the least adaptable people on earth.

Public discussions and planning processes often give disproportionate weight to largely superficial and idiosyncratic objections to the utility and necessity of active transportation. We’d have better transportation policy if we thought more broadly about how these systems are used, and by whom.

[New York backs off plans for highway expansion disguised as a “temporary diversion structure.”](https://nyc.streetsblog.org/2025/10/09/state-dot-scraps-cross-bronx-expansion-road-on-highway-repair-plan) The Cross-Bronx expressway is one of the nation’s fabled highways that devastated urban neighborhoods to facilitate car commuting. New York State has been wrestling with how to maintain the aging structure. One idea NYDOT pitched, purportedly to ease construction delays was to build a “temporary diversion structure” parallel to portions of the roadways that would be reconstructed, then once the project was completed the structure would be transformed into a “community connector” that might include bus lanes, bike and pedestiran facilities, oh, and an additional car lane or two.

Calling it a “community connector” rather than what it really is--a wider highway or a car connector, is typical of the misleading greenwash employed by highway departments nationwide. As David Colon, writing at Streetsblog expansions, community advocates saw through this mirage:

> But [environmental advocates called foul](https://nyc.streetsblog.org/2025/06/06/gov-hochul-reconsidering-cross-bronx-expressway-expansion) at the prospect of building the highway-sized road over Starlight Park and the Bronx River, especially as it would add another two lanes of elevated highway-style traffic directly next to the Cross Bronx.

Apparently, an upsurge of opposition to the idea has led NYDOT to delete the proposed diversion structure from the project. If this holds, its a victory for freeway fighters.
