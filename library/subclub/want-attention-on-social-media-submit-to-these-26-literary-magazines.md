Honestly, did such an excellent job on that one that everything in there is still relevant today. And alongside that, there have been more…erm…shall we say developments in the social media world.

We’re living through what calls the demise of “Old Social.” As it pertains to the indie lit world, well…for years, when a magazine had, say 10K or 20K or whatever-large-number-you-can-conceive-of-for-lit-mags-K followers, one could reasonably expect that 5-10% of them would see any given post, often more.

### A Mini Case Study

I’ll take the example of *[ONLY POEMS](https://substack.com/@onlypoems)* (the lit mag I co-run with my husband, Karan). If we were around during the “old social age”, with our nearly 55K followers on Instagram now, that’s a guaranteed 2000-6000 people who’ve opted in to seeing our stuff *actually* seeing it every time we post and engaging with it also (because, remember, these are folks who’re already followers so highly likely they’ll engage in terms of liking, commenting, sharing, and the big one…actually tapping the link in bio to read!).

[

*Metrics for a viral ONLY POEMS post*

Today, with “New Social” in full force (Karten defines this as a recommendation-based feed as opposed to a follower-based feed), these numbers are still achievable, and wildly higher numbers are also achievable, BUT there’s a caveat. With what we’ve noticed at *ONLY POEMS*, because of how “New Social” works through nearly all social media algorithms, in order to reach “Old-Social” reliable numbers, we need to create content that is more, well, coded for viral-ness. I’m sure there’s a stupid-ass actual term for that, but I just…can’t be bothered. I hate that this is the case now, but that’s the world we live in and unless we all want to pack our lives up and head off-grid, we gotta try and learn the ropes just a little.

With *ONLY POEMS*, I feel we’ve been able to strike a decent balance. Thankfully, we still have great traction. And yes, part of it is that we *do* post stuff that goes viral—the algorithm-friendly content that gets us in front of new people. But we’ve made sure to keep sharing the poets we publish right alongside all of that, because we try to see the whole social experience as holistically as possible rather than just mindlessly chasing reels and viral moments. The viral posts help bring attention to the posts more core to what we actually do as a lit mag.

And look, we’re still figuring it out as we go. Every lit mag is, really. Because magazines have always been slow, thoughtful, resistant to the churn of trending topics—it’s natural that there’s either an ongoing struggle to figure this all out or, on the other hand (and I really do understand this), a kind of “fuck-it-we’re-done” kind of attitude.

### The Future

As you might know already (and may have done yourself), a lot of folks and mags have left X because well duh. I don’t think it’s happening right now, but I’m sure sooner or later Instagram will see a big indie lit exodus (especially with their ridiculous plan for an AI-only feed). I must say I do—for now!—appreciate Substack in this regard.

Yes it *is* social media (the [founder proudly admits it!](https://post.substack.com/p/substack-is-a-social-media-app)), but as of writing, it’s one that still feels less polluted and lobotomizing than the other apps. I’m sure, as with most digital media artifacts and apps, this too will see a degree of enshittification sooner or later (jury’s still out on whether it’s already started).

We might all be trying to stay afloat in a river of mass AI slop at that point though so perhaps we’ll finally decide to just do more physical, local events after all, I don’t know.

### 26 Lit Mags

Right now, however, I want you to feel less overwhelmed about trying to figure social media exposure.

So, below I’ve compiled a list of 26 lit mags that are worth pursuing if one of the things you’re after—yes, in today’s “New Social” age—is social media attention. It’s good to be honest with ourselves. Once in a while, every now and again, we all want some decent social validation. Getting your poetry or stories read by kindred spirits in the ether is hardly a bad way to go about it or seek it out!

Now, these mags below might not might not necessarily have massive follower counts (though some certainly do!) but they do have solid, consistent engagement all the while not trying to do some cringey version of viral videos or whatever. They also have editorial presence. Meaning, you can tell the people behind it care about the work they’re putting out, about the writers they platform, about those writers being read. And that’s something, I feel, we should all look for in the lit mags we submit it, whether or not we want to be literary social media stars!
