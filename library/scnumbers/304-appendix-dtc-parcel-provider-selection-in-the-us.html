In this week’s Supply Chain in Numbers newsletter, one of the numbers was from OnTrac’s announcement of a cheaper parcel offering. Here is what I wrote:

30% cheaper than nationals

OnTrac will launch two national services in early 2026: OnTrac Express (2–3 days coast-to-coast via ClearJet air) and Ground Essentials (1–2 days slower than standard ground, up to 30% cheaper than national services, with no residential surcharge). Early access is available, and pilots are underway. It’s also debuting “7-Day Play” with Fenix Commerce to show precise delivery dates at checkout, and reports domestic shipper volume up 40% YoY. These services will provide a stronger alternative to FedEx/UPS — especially for non-urgent DTC parcels — pressuring rates while improving landed costs and checkout conversion. [Supply Chain Dive]

Outside of USPS and national carriers like FedEx and UPS, options for coast-to-coast parcel shipping providers are scarce. This is where OnTrac is carving out a space. FedEx announced a 5.9% rate hike starting Jan 5, 2026, and also increased the residential surcharge. Based on the recent rate hike cycles of the past few years, UPS might also announce a similar hike.

For Direct-to-Consumer businesses, especially those with a smaller weight (say, less than 10 lbs), parcel costs are typically 50 to 75% of the total “order-to-delivery” costs (which mainly include DC handling, packaging, and parcel). Any savings in these costs will result in higher profits; hence, parcel costs are often a topic of discussion for DTC Managers.

Parcel carrier selection and rate card negotiations are an annual ritual for almost all Transportation Managers. This post discusses the preparation required and the comparative aspects of a career selection exercise. This is written for a Transportation Manager of a retail direct-to-consumer business with sizeable volume (1,500+ parcels per day on average).

Here are the datasets you need before you start this exercise:

  1. Baseline order history: Recent (last 6 months or 12 months) orderline level data along with origin and destination zipcodes, total package dimensions (length, width, height), total weight, order date, ship-ready date & timestamp, parcel pickup date, estimated delivery date, actual delivery date, number of delivery attempts, total parcel cost invoiced by the carrier (cost line level data would be great if available). Using this data set, calculate three metrics: (a) parcel cost per unit, (b) weighted average ‘pickup to delivery’ lead time, (c) delivery precision (i.e, % of parcels delivered on the estimated delivery date)

  2. Forecasts: create a forecast order dataset starting from baseline data, applying whatever granular level growth estimates you have. Forecasts may differ by region, product category, or customer segment, and should be used accordingly. In addition, it is important to remember that an X% increase in revenue growth may not necessarily mean an X% increase in the number of parcels — it might be more order lines, or more units per line, or more orders, or a higher price per order line. Now, recalculate all three metrics mentioned above. This serves as your reference for the remainder of the exercise.

Here are the five comparisons during the selection and rate negotiation exercise:

  1. Total Parcel Cost Per Unit: Your objective is to calculate this for every carrier option you are evaluating using the forecast order dataset. The Rate cards provided by the carriers contain excessive details and options. Fuel surcharge, residential surcharge, delivery area surcharge, and re-delivery attempt costs… There are too many of these. Best to stick to your order needs and apply them. Key factors that often differ among carriers include zone (origin-destination pair) definitions, dimensional weight factors, and surcharges (amount, scope, and timing).

  2. Pickup-to-Delivery Lead Time: Transit times vary by carrier and service level (e.g., air, ground, 3-day, etc.). Compare the daily pickup time cut-off for your DC locations. For example, a carrier might pick up all ground parcels as late as 9 pm, while another’s pickup is at 5 pm. Four extra hours means you can provide faster delivery for 15 to 20% of your orders (assuming orders are evenly spaced, which may or may not be true. Look at your order timestamps).

  3. Delivery precision or predictability: Fear of porch theft or the need to time the gift properly, more and more, consumers are demanding predictability and precision in delivery. Depending on your consumer needs, comparing carriers’ ability to provide an accurate estimate of delivery, their historical On-Time-Delivery rate (i.e., the percentage of deliveries made on or before the estimated date), and precision (i.e., the percentage of deliveries made on the estimated date) are essential.

  4. Parcel returns costs: One of the necessary evils of DTC business is the cost of returns. Check return parcel costs with the carriers and include them in the total cost-per-unit calculation. Some carriers may have better bundled rates.

  5. Other value-added service options: Each carrier offers a different set of VAS. You need to see the feasibility, consumer need, and revenue upside potential for each of these. Many of the VAS options may not be relevant to your business. Typical VAS to consider: pickup locations/locker facility, ability to hold parcels at a different location (e.g, UPS store) if requested by the end consumer, return drop off locations, tracking details, Proof-of-Delivery options, and undelivered parcel handling options

Parcel selection does not necessarily require a single carrier to be selected. It often involves multiple carriers, depending on your business concentration, distribution center network, consumer needs, opportunity costs of new service level options, and budget (of course). Taking a structured data approach helps you to benchmark effectively, run scenarios, and compare trade-offs.
