In the investment world, there are literally thousands of different products you can buy. There are those that most people have heard of, like stocks and bonds. There are safe products like CD’s and US Treasury Bills. There are risky products like call options and futures contracts. There are those “less understood by the general public” products like mortgage-backed securities and volatility futures. Then there are those “less understood by the general professional investor public” like variance swaps and worst-of Bermuda options. (What could go wrong in Bermuda?)

The one common thread among all financial products is risk. Every single product out there can be ranked by its potential for loss. This note will take you through the entire risk spectrum, in order of smallest to largest potential for loss.

### 100% SAFE (0 potential for loss):

Nothing at all. Not even cash. Sorry to say it, but it’s true. The world financial system is predicated on trust, which gets tested every 10-15 years, and comes to the verge of collapse every 80 years or so. Most recently in 2008. Prior to that, 1929 was pretty bad. 1873 wasn’t pretty either. The list goes on. If we lose trust in the system it’s all over. **\*Cryptoheads have entered the chat.\*** We will ignore them for now.

In our centralized financial system, cash is king. We will rate it at 99.9% safe. It will lose to inflation, but we’re going to ignore that too. We need to ignore a lot of things to understand the basics because the money system never stops moving. If it does, it will explode, and it almost did in March 2020 because of Covid.

### Extremely low potential for loss:

**CD’s** - Those brave souls willing to part from their cold hard cash and trust a bank to hold the money for an agreed upon period of time will be rewarded with an interest payment. Formally, this is called a Certificate of Deposit, or CD. Even if the bank goes bust overnight and doesn’t have the money to pay you back, you are insured by Uncle Sam [via the FDIC](https://www.fdic.gov/) for up to $250,000. CD’s used to be a safe source of income in the ‘80s and ‘90s. In 1981, you could buy an insured 3-month CD that would pay you 18%. Today they are equally safe, but provide virtually no income. The best 3-month CD pays an embarrassing 0.5%. Yes, that is zero point five per cent.

**UST’s** - United States Treasuries. If you don’t like private banks, you can park your money with the public bank, also known as the US Treasury. The Treasury will call your deposit a bill, note, or bond depending on how long you agree to lock it up. Could be 4 weeks, could be 30 years. The choice is yours.

UST’s don’t have explicit FDIC insurance like CD’s do, but carry an implicit backing by “the full faith and credit of the United States.” As long as the entire country doesn’t go tits up like the Soviet Union in 1991, UST’s will be safe. It’s hard to say whether treasuries or CD’s carry more risk since they both have essentially the same sugar daddy in Uncle Sam.

**Agencies -** A very important cousin to US Treasuries are government agency bonds like [Fannies](https://www.investopedia.com/mortgage/fannie-mae-loans/), [Freddies](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/freddiemac.asp), and [Ginnies](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/ginnie-mae-security/). These are backed by mortgage payments and represent a HUGE part of the overall money system. In theory they carry slightly more risk than Treasuries, and in practice they carry slightly more risk than Treasuries, but mostly because they are more complex, not because they are more at risk of going bust.

**Munis** - One tiny step up from USTs, Municipal bonds are government bonds that aren’t backed by the *federal* government, but rather by state governments and quasi-governmental agencies such as water districts, toll roads, airports, or even public golf courses. Typically, bonds issued by states will be safer than the those issued by small public golf courses, but as a whole, they are an extremely safe class of financial products. And people love munis because they pay tax free interest.

## Not quite risky, but not quite safe:

**Investment Grade Corporate Bonds** - In case you still aren’t quite sure, a bond is just a fancy word for a loan made by a public group of investors instead of a bank. A corporate bond is a loan to a corporation. An investment grade corporate bond is a loan made to an “investment grade” corporation. Not very creative with the naming.

An investment grade corporation is one that can be trusted to pay *all* of the money back *and* the interest owed *within* the specified time period written into the loan. There are no late payments or IOU’s accepted in the high grade corporate bond space. These are bonds issued by (loans made to) the strongest and biggest companies out there. Microsoft, Johnson & Johnson, Apple, Home Depot, etc. Since these companies can’t print money to pay you back (unlike the government) they will offer a slightly higher interest rate than a UST to compensate for that fact.

## Risky Assets:

**Junk Bonds** - If a once creditworthy company begins to look like it won’t be able to pay you back in full or in a timely manner, it will be downgraded to “non-investment grade”. Again with the creative naming. Non-investment grade bonds are nicely called “high-yield” or in slang, junk. Since these companies are financially weaker than investment grade ones, they promise to pay a much higher interest rate to entice lenders into letting them borrow. In strong economic periods, everything’s fine. In bad ones, they earn their derogatory “junky” nickname.

Many promising young companies are junk-rated because they don’t yet produce free cash flow, meaning they have to continually raise money to stay in business. But they are scrappy, and if everything goes well, they will be profitable in the future. Junk bonds are great for investors who like having legal contracts ([bond covenants](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bond-covenant.asp)) in place when making investments, but also have a tolerance for risk.

**Convertible Bonds** - This is a hybrid product between a stock and a bond. They’re often issued by high growth, low credit score companies with bright futures. They get away with paying a low interest rate by offering a much higher upside than a traditional bond. How do they do this? After a period of time, their bonds will be *convertible* into stock, which is dilutive. An example: there are 5 shareholders and 1 bondholder, and the bondholder converts his bond into stock. Now the 5 shareholders have to split profits 6 ways instead of 5. Companies that issue convertible bonds agree to give away a larger share of their future profits in order to get cheaper funding today.

**Stocks** - There’s an entire universe of stocks out there. Some are less risky and some are very risky. It all depends on what corner of the economy they call home. Safe stocks are large and in charge. These are called Blue Chips. They are consistently profitable and might pay you a quarterly dividend. Others and small, speculative, risky, and unprofitable. But they might have a huge runway for growth, and are expected to turn profitable in the future. The riskier the stock, the higher the potential reward. Most of these speculative companies will fail miserably, but a few will grow to gain the respect of investors worldwide. In the mid 1980’s, Apple was a small, innovative company forging its way into the nascent personal computer industry. Today it is the largest public company in the world and in 2020, did almost $275 billion in sales. Pretty impressive. If Apple were a country, it would be ranked the 8th largest, between France and Italy.

## Cowboy stuff, as risky as it gets:

**Options, Futures, and other Derivatives** \- These products can potentially go 10x in the morning only to be worthless by lunch. This is the Wild West of investing. Fortunes can be made and lost in a matter of hours. These are complex products that require a thorough understanding of markets, a need to be in tune with world news, and a strong grasp on human psychology. I will leave it at that.

## That’s it.

There are other financial products out there, but most of them fall somewhere within the asset classes discussed. It is extremely important to “know what you own” when it comes to investing in financial markets, and consulting with a financial advisor
