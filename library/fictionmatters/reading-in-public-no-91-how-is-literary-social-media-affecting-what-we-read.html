In October, I wrote about the potential pitfalls of comprehensive reading tracking in a Reading in Public essay. That comments on that post fascinated me because many of them leap-frogged over the idea of tracking to land on a different woe in the modern reader’s life: bookish social media. Many readers chimed in to suggest that it was all of the literary content they were consuming that was impacting their reading lives, and I was interested to learn more. I opened up a social media survey and almost 300 people participated. Consisting primarily of open ended questions, I’m still making my way through everything you all shared and will gradually be posting more about my findings in the coming weeks and months.

Today, I’m sharing some of the most interesting patterns I noticed in your responses to the question: How has social media impacted WHAT you read?1 The end of the year is personally a time when I think a lot about what I read—how I choose my books, what I did and did not get to this year, if my reading year felt satisfying, and how I want to make these decisions differently in the new year. I know I’m not alone in this so I thought this would be a good moment to think about how we as a collective body of readers are letting the internet shape our reading choices.

Of course, there is a massive disclaimer here that people who responded to this survey are a) readers of this Substack and therefore involved and invested in some form of online literary discourse and b) readers who cared enough about it to fill out the survey. This is likely still a niche group within the world of readers at large. Nonetheless, social media is having a major impact on books at large. Here’s how one reader put it:

Even as a non-SM user, you see it everywhere, in online reviews, blurbs, bookstores, what friends are reading, and of course what gets published. I’m oddly missing some of the older gatekeepers and worry it’s making our reading less diverse, even if it comes with nice advantages for finding audiences.

Here are some of the patterns that were most interesting to me. I’d love to know if any of these resonate with you, or what you’d add to these observations!

Engaging with the online book world has broadened what we read…

This was by far and away the most common positive response to how social media has changed what we read. It’s unsurprising, but still great to hear because I do think social media has the potential to push us into tighter and tighter reading niches. This may be happening for some people, but for the majority, being exposed to and interacting with a wide variety of readers continues to broaden what we read.

It has definitely broadened my scope—I have connected with many readers from all over the globe and provided me a range of recommendations that I may not otherwise have been exposed to.

Following a broader set of readers has opened my eyes to new authors and new genres I might have never found.

I follow many persuasive readers who convince me to try new things! The good news is that my reading has diversified and I’ve discovered lots of, for example, translated and mid-century fic I love. The bad news is my TBR is overflowing.

…but also muddled our personal tastes.

This theme was highly relatable. In being inundated new releases, books of the moment, and what other people are reading, some of us are forgetting how to trust ourselves and our own taste. Online book discourse can make us feel like we’re reading the wrong books or hinder our ability to tap into what we actually want to read. This is something I’d like to explore more in the new year because knowing our own tastes and being in touch with what we actually want to read is essential to feeling satisfied with our reading.

FOMO has made me pick up books that I might not otherwise, but I also feel that I have lost my own personal taste in reading. I’m not even sure what it is anymore because of taking in so many Bookstagram posts.

It causes me to feel FOMO of popular books; it gives me weird pressure to read more or faster to post more often; and it also leads me to read books to fit a certain niche which means I read less of what I want to read and more what I think I should be reading to keep up. This often results in more dissatisfactory reading.

It’s helped…and hindered. I find readers I identify with and follow their recommendations, which is great. It does make me doubt my own tastes sometimes and I feel like my focus can be more on the perfected curated bookshelf/read rather than just reading for me.

Social media is messing with our reading expectations.

We all know this one. Before social media was awash with readers sharing their reviews, rankings, and star ratings, we went into books with fewer—perhaps even zero—expectations. Of course we tried to read books we would like, but there wasn’t the same sense of disappointment and betrayal we sometimes experience when we don’t like a book every was raving about. I’ve said before that I do think it’s part of a reader’s job to resist letting expectations tank a book for us, but the online space makes that quite difficult.

It’s not that bad—but when a particular book is so hyped by many people that my expectations exceed reality, a book that is good still feels a bit of a letdown when it ought not.

I was succumbing to FOMO and being often disappointed.

It broadened my book awareness. Sometimes it causes expectations that don’t always work the same as what other people think.

Some books seem to be overly hyped and I’m disappointed after reading and feel let down. I try to avoid the publicity efforts to sell a book compared to a post because person genuinely likes book.

I’m reading much more hyped books that don’t actually hit.

It’s helped us get more serious…

The proliferation of “as seen on TikTok” tables at bookstores might make us think that social media is exclusively pushing readers towards easily digestible stories, but that’s not the entire story. Yes, there are certain bingeable books that get big on the apps, but social media is also encouraging readers to pick up books that might have previously intimidated them.

I now engage with more challenging texts.

It has encouraged me to be a more thorough reader. I’m reading more challenging books that need deeper thought. While I’m not sharing my thoughts, I keep a journal and am encouraged when I see others do the same.

I think I’m often encouraged to pick up texts I may have ignored due to difficulty.

…and loosened us up.

At the same time, some of us (myself included) feel freer to explore books we may have previously dismissed as silly or unimportant. The reading world on social media reminds us that there are many ways to be a reader and helps combat the “guilty pleasure” stigma.

I feel more free to read a romance or something silly than I would have prior, but otherwise, I’ve always known what I like/don’t like, and that hasn’t changed.

I’m willing to read more romantasy-type books.

We are hearing about and reading more new releases, for better or worse.

I found these responses fascinating, because I could echo all of these sentiments. Before the explosion of online book discourse, I never distinguished between new releases and backlist books in any substantive way. I often didn’t know or care about the publication date of my books, and I wasn’t anticipating forthcoming new releases. While I do enjoy being in the know about upcoming books, I am curious about the tip in the direction of the new. I’m also curious if any of this is born out in purchase data. Are people actually buying more new releases than in years past, or do we just feel that way because it’s what we see on our feeds? Are books that are 1-5 years old still being discovered by new readers, or does a book now have a shorter window in which to make a splash?

I read way more “front list.” I didn’t even know what that meant 5 years ago.

It clues me into new releases and backlist books that I may not hear about from friends or family or via traditional media.

I read a lot more new releases than I would like and they tend to be ones that I feel lukewarm about. I will also pick up a book just because everyone else is reading it but end up not liking it. Or even if I like it, I didn’t love it like everyone else.

It shifted my reading much more toward frontlist. I’ve had to really work to get back to rereading and doing backlist.

I trend much more towards newer releases than I did before getting into book social media. In some ways this is interesting and fun, and lets me engage with others who are reading similar books at the same time. But this has also made me less likely to challenge myself with backlist books or go down rabbit holes that I may find interesting but aren’t current.

We are hesitant to pick up books that haven’t been vetted.

This is the most disheartening find from my survey, and it’s reminiscent of what I’ve been getting at in many recent Reading in Public essays. Many of us have lost the ability to just pick up a book that calls to us and read it just because. We want the data first—Goodreads ratings, who’s recommended it, if it’s popular on Bookstagram. I understand how this has happened. We’re all more aware of how many books are out there that we’d like to read. We want to be sure we’re picking the “right” ones and not wasting our time so we look for information to help us decide.

But this becomes an underlying issue that affects everything else. We stop trusting our own taste and look to others for validation of what we ought to read. We let reviews and ratings affect our expectations and influence our reading experiences. So this observation comes with a challenge, sometime soon—whether it’s a December read or a goal for 2026—I want you to pick up a book you’ve heard little to nothing about and read it without getting any additional information. It might not be your favorite book of the year, but it will remind you that you can.

I am now reluctant to choose books that haven’t been “vetted” by a reader I follow on social media. I actually miss choosing books that speak to me, but I am so scared of getting a lemon I don’t do it.

If I haven’t heard social media “buzz” about a book, I’m less likely to pick it up.

I’m a lot more hesitant to pick up a book that I haven’t heard of, and feel pressure to read books that “everyone has read.”

I’m much less likely to choose to read a book if I haven’t seen it vouched for somewhere on the internet. I have always loved the thrill of discovering a book at the library or book store that I’ve never seen before, but I find that now instead of just reading these discoveries like I used to do, I have to look it up on Goodreads or whatever before I commit to actually reading it--even if the blurb sounds right up my alley.

If I am considering a book to read, I’ll check Goodreads and a negative review will influence my decision whether to read the book or not.
