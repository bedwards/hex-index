I had the best week celebrating books and their people in New York City this week. I attended ’s annual Off-Site party and met literary luminaries I’m obsessed with like Brit Bennet and Patrick Radden Keefe. I toured the offices of some of my favorite Penguin Random House imprints and got to talk with the editors, marketers, and designers who bring books into the world. And then I attended nerd prom, otherwise known as the National Book Awards after party. It was the best week, topped off by the fact it was the first time my husband and I have traveled away from Louise. We missed her, of course, but wow traveling without a kid is a luxury I’m so lucky to have experienced again! One of my goals for 2025 was to bring more of my work life into in person events and this was the most perfect way to cap off that dream.

In honor of the awards, I went back through fifteen years of the National Book Award for Fiction and discovered that I had read 38 of the shortlisted titles. I ranked them all here, which was a much more fun exercise than ranking more recently read books. I highly recommend a trip back through prize lists when you’re feeling inspired!

### This week in books.

***This week I read…***

***Sad Tiger*** **by Neige Sinno, translated by Natasha Lehrer.** This arresting memoir was shortlisted for the National Book Award for Translated Literature and came highly recommended to me by readers I trust. While difficult to read, I found this absolutely exceptional and I’m so glad I prioritized it. The memoir tells in straightforward tone and striking detail of the sexual abuse Sinno experienced at the hands of her stepfather as well as his trial years later. Throughout her story, Sinno weaves in literary analysis and cultural criticism exploring the ways we continue to tell, deny, and be intrigued by tales of abuse and incest. The way Sinno lays bare her experiences took me aback—I’ve never read anything quite so blunt about such a difficult topic. But the way she framed the narrative and through through her own pain and curiosity about her experience felt to me like it was adding something essential to these traumatic stories that have become their own genre. This isn’t a book I could recommend to every reader, but it’s one of the most beautiful, powerful, and memorable books I’ve read this year. [Bookshop](https://bookshop.org/a/1173/9781644214671) | [Libro.fm](https://libro.fm/audiobooks/9798855583748-sad-tiger)

***A Guardian and a Thief*** **by Megha Majumdar.** Majumdar’s sophomore novel is getting tons of buzz. It was an Oprah Book Club pick, a finalist for the National Book Award for Fiction, and a fan favorite for many online readers. This is the story about Ma and Boomba—one is a mother trying to get her daughter out of Kolkata to Michigan where her husband is living and the other is a thief desperate to reunite with his own estranged family. Over the course of the week their lives will intertwine and the desperation with escalate as they each seek to better their lives and save the people they love.

I absolutely get the enthusiasm behind this one, but it just wasn’t for me. This is a fast-paced book with high stakes, a well-drawn setting, and important themes, but it did a few things that are high on my personal list of reading pet peeves, and I couldn’t get over them. My primary issue was how heavy-handed the themes feel—this is a novel that spells out its thesis statement early and often. I don’t like when it seems like a book doesn’t trust me to get it, and this one insisted on hammering home its key takeaways. I also found the pacing frustrating and a bit manipulative. So much happened in such a short time space that there wasn’t room to breathe. Character interiority and complexity weren’t prioritized as the plot moved from gripping event to gripping event. Finally, I felt like the larger context of climate catastrophe was underdeveloped, leaving me with a lot of lingering questions. All of these factors combined meant this didn’t have either the emotional impact or thematic complexity I wanted and expected, especially from an NBA shortlisted book.

All of this pains me to say because I loved Majumdar’s debut novel *A Burning,* and I think she is a brilliant. A reader I really trust who did love this book told me it read more as a fable. I think they’re right and having that in mind makes me appreciate it more, but ultimately, the line between fable and realism was too murky for me to find this totally successful either way. I am very much in the minority opinion on this one so I highly encourage you to pick it up and give it a try, and I think its widespread success might make it an ideal book for gifting this season. I’ll also note that I listened to much of this on audio and the production was exceptionally well done. But overall while I’ll certainly read whatever Majumdar does next, this wasn’t a fit for my particular reading sensibilities. [Bookshop](https://bookshop.org/a/1173/9780593804872) | [Libro.fm](https://libro.fm/audiobooks/9798217174430-a-guardian-and-a-thief-oprah-s-book-club)
