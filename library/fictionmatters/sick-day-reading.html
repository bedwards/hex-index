Well, readers, unfortunately I am sick again. It’s been a very frustrating fall with at least one pretty significant illness per month, and I don’t know why I can’t catch a break! Luckily my husband and my mom have me totally covered on the childcare front, which I am so grateful for—parenting while sick is immensely challenging. But I’m sad! I hate having to miss fun things with Louise on the weekends. Now that she’s in school full time it feels particularly devastating to miss out on weekend activities. Please send healing vibes and let’s hope this is the last time I’m bedridden in 2025.

I have read some great books recently, but I couldn’t manage writing the reviews. I’ll catch you up on those books later, but for today, please enjoy some Links and End Notes.

End notes.

  • The Supreme Court issued an order to block SNAP benefits. I made a donation to Food Bank of the Rockies and highly recommend donating in your community if you’re able.

  • In addition to Disney soundtracks on repeat, Louise is pretty into any pop female vocalist—Taylor Swift, Chappell Roan, etc. As an experiment, I put Rilo Kiley on in the care the other day and to quote the great Jenny Lewis, it’s a hit. It is very fun to get to introduce her to things I genuinely love and have her love them too. Plus hearing a four-year-old sing, “ooh, it feels good to be free” is quite adorable.

  • Last weekend I went to a Mahjong party/lesson and I now understand the obsession. I’m not a big games person, but it is really fun! I haven’t played a real game since, but I have been enjoying playing against the computer during short breaks from work.

  • I’ve been cracing a good chatty podcast in my feed and dipped into Bad on Paper with and this week. I especially appreciated their conversation about competition and comparison from a few months ago.

  • Speaking of podcasts, I listened to the terrible Ross Douthat conversation about whether women are ruining the workplace. Why did I feel compelled to listen? I guess I’ve become afraid of not knowing what kinds of things these people are saying. As hard as it is to listen to, I find some degree of control in hearing what their insane arguments are and how they’re using rhetorical strategies to push their awful agendas. It’s not worth the listen—you know everything you need to know from the headline—but I find a sort of comfort in forming rhetorical responses to what I’m hearing, even if they’re only in my head or out loud to my husband.
