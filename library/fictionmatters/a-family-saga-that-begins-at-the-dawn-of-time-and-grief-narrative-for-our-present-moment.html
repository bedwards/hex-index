This week in books.

This week I read…

The Devil Three Times by Rickey Fayne. This multigenerational epic begins with a tale as old as time: the Devil’s expulsion from heaven. But from here, Fayne takes his own inventive journey depicting Jesus distraught at the plight of Africans being sold into slavery and enlisting the help of his brother, the Devil, to end the horrific institution for good. If the Devil can right this evil, Jesus will argue in favor of the Devil’s return to heaven. Over the course of seven generations, we see the Devil at work in the lives of the Laurent family beginning with bestowing captured and enslaved Yetunde the gift of communing with her deceased sister. Each generation is haunted, possessed, and called in singular ways, and over the centuries the Laurents attempt to heal from what has been done to them and what they have done to each other.

I loved this book. I had been waiting to read it until I had a bit more brain space and longer spans of time for reading before starting this, and I’m glad I made that decision. This is a demanding book with a lot of characters to keep track of and plot lines to follow—plus momentum can slow when every chapter feels like starting again. But this really is one, single, epic story and the way anecdotes and lives get finished, reinvented, and misremembered from generation to generation is beautifully done and so satisfying for this reader. I particularly marveled at the way Fayne was able to capture not just distinct narrative voices, but distinct ways of being in the world. Each character has a singular worldview informed by the time period in which they live, the traumas they have faced and inherited, and, yes, how the Devil has intertwined himself in their particular story. It’s remarkable to watch so many characters come to life and interact on the page and while (like every reader of this kind of narrative) I had my favorites, every single one compelled me to keep reading.

If you enjoy sweeping family sagas like Pachinko or Homegoing or love historical narratives that grapple with folklore and fate, this ought to be your next read. Bookshop | Libro.fm

What a Time to Be Alive by Jade Chang. At the beginning of this novel, narrator and protagonist Lola Treasure Gold’s best friend Alex dies in an accident. Lola then has a viral moment of off-the-cuff philosophizing after Alex’s funeral and this propels her into the role of a social media guru. Over the course of a year, we follow Lola as she grieves, makes questionable decisions, pursues romantic entanglements, and tries to figure out if this life in the spotlight is something she can and wants to make a living on. I loved the book’s willingness to put all of modern life on the page. I haven’t seen many books that give the literary treatment to an Instagram Live session, and even thought it’s a little jarring to see on the page, it’s such fruitful material! The willingness to get into internet allows Chang to explore someone trying to do something good out of their, while also examining the potential hollowness of our lives lived on screen and the way our identities bifurcate on social media.

The format of this book as a year in Lola’s life worked to varying success for me. I love an episodic story and, in this novel in particular, it allows for tangents and diversions that might have been cut in a more traditional rise and fall narrative arc—an ill-fated but redemptive appearance on a mid-tier daytime talk show was my personal favorite. It also leant to a few fits and starts in the pacing and while I generally found the book propulsive, I got bogged down in a few of the months. My favorite plot component of the book is Lola’s friendship with Celi, one of her few close girlfriends and someone who will unquestioningly root for Lola but also call it like she sees it. Their dynamic is that captivating blend of raw honesty and almost childlike silliness, and I enjoyed watching how each woman—and thus their relationship—evolved over the course of the year. I thought it was really effective to have Celi popping in and out of Lola’s life as a sort of yardstick by which we can measure what’s going on with Lola—but I loved their dynamic so much I missed Celi when she wasn’t around! Finally, ending with the close of the year also means that the story concludes without wrapping up every plot line. This might bother some readers, but I thought the ending was memorable, satisfying, and perfectly fitting for the story.

A lovely and ambitious exploration of grief in the internet age, pick this up when you’re looking for your next flawed but fascinating heroine to root for. Bookshop | Libro.fm
