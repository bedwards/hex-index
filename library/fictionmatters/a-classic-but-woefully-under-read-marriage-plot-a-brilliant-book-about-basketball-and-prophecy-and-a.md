### This week in books.

***This week I read…***

***Evelina*** **by Frances Burney.** Burney’s 1778 novel of a young, beautiful, and naive young woman’s first foray into London society is fantastic. The combination of social satire, farcical romp, and marriage plot make *Evelina* genuinely funny and delightful. This is a joyful and smart classic with pointed mockery of London society baked into a truly plotty plot with unforgettable set piece scenes and Bridgerton-style narrative twists. I had the pleasure of talking about this book with for Books that Matter and we’ll have much more to share in that January episode, so go read this now! As both a clear precursor to Jane Austen and a piece of art in its own right, this classic is well worth your time. [Bookshop](https://bookshop.org/a/1173/9780199536931) | [Libro.fm](https://libro.fm/audiobooks/9781482999372)

***Medium Rare*** **by A. Natasha Joukovsky (out March 3).** This is the story of an ordinary man named Phil who seems on his way to defying all odds—and winning a billion dollars—with a potentially perfect March Madness bracket. As game-after-game goes in Phil’s favor, his fame and opportunity grow, but of course there’s danger in flying too close to the sun. Told through the shrewd and knowing gaze of Cassandra—an omniscient first-person narrator with writerly ambitious and the power to foretell the future—*Medium Rare* follows Phil’s rise and its impact on the people around him while examining power and prediction. I loved this book. The plot is singular, fresh, and gripping (I read it in three sittings because I needed to know what happened), while also brimming with allusions to myth and classic literature. And as always with Joukovsky’s writing, there is much to chew on as you read. The novel’s themes are meaty and delicious, providing the reader a lot of room for investigation and interpretation, which are always the most pleasurable and satisfying reading experiences for me. If you tend to like my reading taste, this is a book to add to your preorder list for 2026. [Bookshop](https://bookshop.org/a/1173/9781685892470)

***The Wax Child*** **by Olga Ravn.** I was dragging my feet on reading this book for the silliest of reasons. I didn’t get an early copy of it and while I have no problem purchasing hardback books, I don’t like reading hardbacks as a tactile experience. But I suffered through because I have adored all of Ravn’s books so far and I was feeling the desire to conclude 2025 with a truly great new release. This one was worth the hardback frustration because it is excellent. The story is told from the omniscient perspective of a wax doll made by a 17th-century Danish noblewoman named Christenze Kruckow. When Christenze is accused of witchcraft, the doll is passed between parties observing and commenting on the nature of power and the ways in which female freedom strikes fear into the men around them. While this is a slim book, I took my time with it as Ravn’s language and imagery requiring a lot of processes on my part. You could fly through it, but you might miss some of the subtlety and the ways in which Ravn’s opacity force you to come to your own conclusions. I highly recommend this one and am so glad to have fit it in this year. [Bookshop](https://bookshop.org/a/1173/9780811238830)
