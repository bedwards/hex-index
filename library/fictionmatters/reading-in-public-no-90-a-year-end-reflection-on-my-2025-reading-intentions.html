#1 Read fewer books, better

I checked in last year I read 160 books. This year, I’ve read 118 so far so unless I manage to read over 40 books before the end of the year, I’m on track to have read fewer. As for better, I mostly feel good about that too. I can’t say I read every book on my 2025 read list well. There are still books I rushed through and titles I know I would have enjoyed more if I’d given them the time they needed. But for the most part, I read more slowly this year including a good number of books I read closely and deliberately, with the English major hat on that I’d been missing. I don’t think every book needs to be savored or annotated or pored over, but I am happy to have done more of those things this year. I’m particularly happy to have slowed down my Paperback Summer Reading Guide reading. Some years I barely remember those books by the time winter rolls around, but this year the guide books remain some of my favorites.

Here’s the more detailed plan from my original post, and how I fared.

The plan:

  • Read more books in print. Well this definitely happened, and now I’m wondering if I cursed myself into an audiobook slump with this intention. Part of why I read fewer books this year is because I didn’t listen to many. But I also believe that part of why I have fewer books I feel I only half read is I didn’t push through any audiobooks even as I was tuning out. I’m pleased with this, although I do hope I can get back into a listening routine soon.

  • Annotate without being precious. Yes! I’ve found title page notes and indexing to be what works best for me. I wrote more about this earlier in the year and it all stuck!

  • Read more literary criticism about the books I read. I did more of this in the second half of the year. I read a handful of more controversial new releases in the fall and spent a lot of time going down review rabbit holes after each one. I also read some literary criticism for each episode of Books That Matter that I’ve recorded so far. I think that’s going to be a great way to help me keep reading contextual materials.

  • Go down more reading rabbit holes. I haven’t gone as deep into my Richard III rabbit hole as I would like, but it’s there and I plan to keep exploring! I would also say that reading books authors choose for Books That Matter feels similar to a reading rabbit hole even though it’s a different thing—it still has the feeling of exploration and spontaneity that I wanted to create.

  • Mayyyybeee take a class?? I did it! My Paradise Lost class is still a highlight of my reading year.

#2 Be choosey with my book selections

This is difficult to assess at this point in the year because of the end-of-year book insanity that happens on the internet. I generally feel like I did a good job of being choosey with my selections, but the best-of-the-year lists have me doubting myself. I only read nine of the NYT’s 100 Notable Books! Does that mean I was being choosey? Or does that mean I foolishly let 91 of the best books of the year pass me by?! I may never know!

If I make a goal like this again next year, I want to create more of a system for myself that I can audit more regularly because right now I just feel unable to assess how this intention went.

The plan:

  • Make use of the huge spreadsheet of new releases I created. I totally stopped doing this by the end of the year. I’ve already got one going for next year but I don’t know if this is the right method for me. I don’t want to overplan my reading, but I don’t want to feel like I’m forgetting about books I would genuinely like to read.

  • Read books edited by and recommended by my favorite editors. Kind of! But not as intentionally as I would like. I have read many books edited by Elisabeth Schmidt and she remains my editorial North Star, so I’ll certainly keep looking for her work.

  • Read more books with third person narrators. Yes! I’m not necessarily sampling books to determine POV before I begin, but I have read more books with third person narrators this year as well as found myself abandoning books that are too voice-driven for my current preference. I’ve also realized I struggle a lot with present tense narration and have been very picky about this as well.

  • Focus more of my reading on books in translation and books from small presses. Yes and no. I think I did a decent job of this in my backlist reading, but it’s only now that we’ve arrived at the end of the year that I’m picking up more new releases that fit this qualification. It’s definitely something I’d like to focus on again next year.

  • Read classics and long books. I felt good about this in my halfway check-in and I feel good about it now. I’d love to fit in another long book in December, but otherwise this has been going great.

  • Read books from the Goldsmiths Prize lists. Still no.

  • Read more essays, craft books, and literary criticism. This fell by the wayside in the second half of the year, but some of my plans for 2026 will ensure I get back to it.

  • Let my reading drive my content rather than letting my content drive my reading. I wrote about this extensively last year and I feel really good about how it went this year.

#3 Stretch myself in my writing

Honestly, I’m not feeling great about my writing this year. I haven’t felt as inspired to write Reading in Public essays and I miss that kind of thinking and working. One of my plans for December is to really consider where I find inspiration for those posts and come up with a plan to get back into this type of writing.

The plan:

  • Write some long form literary criticism. This didn’t happen, but I think I’m starting to understand the real reason why. That will be a subject for a future Reading in Public post! But I have been enjoying recording Books That Matter episodes, which are the kind of conversational criticism I really enjoy.

  • Respond to other writers and thinkers in my writing. Kind of? I’m not sure about this. I feel more plugged into the literary space than ever, but I also don’t think much of my work has been written in response to others. I actually think this is part of my lack of inspiration—I haven’t been engaging as much as I would like with what other people are saying about books and reading. Essays about the state of reading and literature are often where I find sparks of ideas for my own essays, and I just haven’t been reading those as much this year.

  • Change up my publishing calendar (just a little). My plan here was to “not force specific types of posts into specific days.” This is still so hard for me!! I actually forgot about this goal and now that I’m seeing it again it serves as a great reminder to let go a bit.

  • Improve my vocabulary. I worked on this all year but I don’t think it shows in my writing or speaking. Maybe that’s a goal for next year.

The plan:

  • Get a hobby. I did some embroidery early in the year. I went ice skating a handful of times. I learned how to play mahjong and hope to start going to the preschool parents’ mahjong nights about Louise’s school. Nothing has become The Hobby for me, but dipping into other things that I find fun is really all I wanted. I think it’s less that I’m looking for a hobby and more that I’m looking for more opportunities to play, and I’ve done alright with that.

  • Volunteer somewhere as a family. I hate to admit that we didn’t do this at all. No excuses—just a commitment to make this a reality next year.

  • Attend more local events. In my original post I said that “I would like my weekends in particular to feel more novel.” With the addition of activities into my child’s life, I wouldn’t use the word “novel” to describe my weekend routine. But we did get out and do more things in our community both in our neighborhood, through Lou’s preschool, and in Denver at large, and that’s been great. Leaving the house with our kid almost always takes a great exertion of energy, but it’s also almost always worth it.

  • Keep a list of shows and movies in my notes app so I can easily decide what to watch when I’m in the mood to watch instead of read post Louise bedtime. I did this, but I barely watched anything at all this year. That was primarily because I usually felt in the mood to read, so I’m not made about it!

  • Continue not buying new things. I stopped buying clothes for myself after the 2024 election for about six months. Once I lifted my moratorium, I went a little crazy for about a month and then things evened out. I’m still trying to buy less and buy used as frequently as possible. I’ve actually been enjoying getting dressed more now that I’m figuring out how to work with what I have rather than thinking about what I’m missing.
