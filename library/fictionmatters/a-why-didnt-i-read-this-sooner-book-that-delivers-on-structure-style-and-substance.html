This week in books.

This week I read…

Lost Children Archive by Valeria Luiselli. One downside of reading so much and working in the book world, is I can start to feel like I’ve read books I haven’t. Many books are predictable. And sometimes I can begin to feel like I already understand books that have been in the zeitgeist and on my radar for a good bit of time. It’s a strange mental deception that has kept me from reading some books I’ll surely love and that will surely surprise me, which is exactly what happened with Lost Children Archive. Thank goodness I finally got over my block and selected this for the FictionMatters Book Club because I thought it was exceptional.

This novel begins with a family—a single mother with a daughter who meets, falls in love with, and marries a single father of a son. For many years, the mother and father have worked together recording and documenting the sounds of New York City, but the father has a growing interest in Apache history and wants to travel to Arizona to pursue a new project. As the family sets out together, the mother isn’t sure what lies at the end of their journey—a new life together or a part of the ways. She is simultaneously consumed with helping a woman she met as part of her recording project find her young daughters who were recently detained at the U.S.-Mexico border.

What I loved was how Luiselli’s plays with our sense of expectations to prod us into thinking more deeply about what we owe each other on both a micro and a macro scale. Road trip story tropes are interrupted by an creative structure that divides the family’s belongings and secrets into literal and figurative boxes. There are countless references to other works of literature as well as music and film, and a story within a story that is Luiselli’s own invention. None of it felt difficult to follow, but the result is a combination of traditional narrative and impressionistic mosaic that allowed me to view the individual characters and the larger themes with new eyes. This was also an excellent book for discussing with the FictionMatters Book Club because while everyone agreed that it was impressive, not every actually enjoyed reading it. We had a great conversation about the goals of this book, the character portrayals, and the function of the structure with quite a bit of disagreement, but also a lot of appreciate and respect. If you tend to like books I enjoy, this is one to finally pick up. Bookshop | Libro.fm
