You Want to Know About the Woman Behind Frankenstein

Yours, Creature by Jessica Cuello

I must have read Mary Shelley’s gothic horror classic Frankenstein in college, because I have a decades-old Penguin Classics copy, complete with browning sticky notes on which I scribbled cryptic messages to myself. For example: “creature relates history of De Laceys (what’s point?).” But honestly, I can’t remember reading it. And as for film adaptations (director Guillermo del Toro’s is the latest), I’ve only really seen Mel Brooks’ incredibly ridiculous and funny Young Frankenstein.

Still, I love the story behind the story: The novel’s origin as a ghost story told to a group of friends in a Swiss villa. Shelley’s fascinating biography as the daughter of 18th-century English radicals Mary Wollstonecraft and William Godwin, and as mistress, then wife, then widow of Romantic poet Percy Bysshe Shelley. Her life as a writer in 19th-century England until her death at age 53.

The haunting poetry collection Yours, Creature, by Jessica Cuello, tells Mary Shelley’s story in a fresh way. In these epistolary poems, Shelley writes to her dead mother, to her friend Fanny Blood, to places she’s been and years she’s lived through, and to her “creature” or “creation.” Cuello merges fictional retelling with historical narrative, starting each section of the book with a small biographical note. For example: “Percy’s wife Harriet was pregnant with her second child when Percy and Mary fell in love.” Notes at the back include even longer historical explanations and references to real-life letters between Shelley and others.

With this mix of fact and fiction, Cuello chronicles Mary Shelley’s many griefs and losses and her undying desire to create and make sense of the world. Yours, Creature is a moving experiment in feminist biography and poetics.

You Want a Follow-up to Hamnet

Enter Ghost by Isabella Hammad

I love Shakespeare, and I’m sad when people find the plays difficult or inaccessible. Even so, I sometimes wonder why this One Guy gets so much attention! (I’m complicated, no apologies.) When a friend gave me Hamnet for Christmas a few years ago, I was slightly reluctant. Then I read it. Inspired by the lives of William Shakespeare and his wife Anne Hathaway, the death of their son, and the writing of Hamlet, Maggie O’Farrell’s novel is a stunner. It’s sure to give you a taste for more Shakespeare-related storytelling that goes deep.

As a companion to O’Farrell’s book and this year’s movie adaptation, I recommend Enter Ghost, Isabella Hammad’s stirring novel about a group of actors staging Hamlet in Palestine’s occupied West Bank. Hamlet has been translated into and performed in Arabic since the early 20th century, and the play has taken on deep political overtones for Arab intellectuals, theater people and audiences since the 1960s.

In Enter Ghost, successful Palestinian British actress Sonia Nasir returns to Haifa, where her sister Haneen lives, after years away. She’s reeling from personal tragedy, and through Haneen, she meets strong-willed Palestinian director Mariam, who convinces her to play Gertrude opposite a young hotshot Palestinian popstar. Enter Ghost, is not your usual “actors put on Shakespeare” romp. Hammad shows in great detail how occupation and apartheid have divided, fragmented, estranged, and worn Palestinians down. In this story, the occupation army attends opening night, and Shakespeare’s “genius” is not going to save anyone, but making art together creates solidarity and resistance where they didn’t exist before.

You’re Conflicted About the Forthcoming M.J. Biopic

On Michael Jackson by Margo Jefferson

Only at a writing conference can I jump into a conversation with a new acquaintance and go from horror writing to Claire Dederer’s Monsters: A Fan’s Dilemma to “What are they thinking, releasing a Michael Jackson biopic?”

That’s how I learned about Pulitzer Prize–winning cultural critic Margo Jefferson’s On Michael Jackson. (Thanks, Natalia!) First published 20 years ago, before Jackson’s 2009 death, this slim book of five long essays reminded me how much tabloid fodder the superstar provided for three decades. Jefferson dissects Jackson’s child stardom in a turbulent family and follows his obsessions with P. T. Barnum, the Elephant Man, and Elvis, connecting him to an American lineage of hucksterism, freakshows, and racial exploitation. She offers fascinating close readings of several his videos and short films and analyzes Jackson’s 2004 trial on charges related to child sexual abuse and the media circus around it.

Jefferson is clearly a fan of Jackson’s work, interested in what his transformation from child star to public spectacle says about American culture. In the book’s original essays, she does not weigh in on whether Jackson is guilty of the crimes he has been accused of. But in a 2019 introduction included in the e-book —written after she watched the HBO documentary Leaving Neverland, in which two men vividly describe Jackson’s abuse of them when they were children—Jefferson admits to a sense of shame. When she wrote the book, she explains, “I couldn’t push myself to acknowledge that this damaged man was almost certainly a sexual predator.”

Jefferson concludes that when we learn an artist whose work we love or admire did terrible things, “We can’t erase or unknow that. We can only accept it, acknowledge what it stirs in us—despair, grief, anger, compassion—and try to turn it into wisdom.”
