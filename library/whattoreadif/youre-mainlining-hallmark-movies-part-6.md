# **You’re Bingeing Made-for-Cable Holiday RomComs**

### [A Holiday Hat Trick by CS Jane](https://bookshop.org/a/22722/9798301050985)

I have not watched that many Hallmark/Lifetime movies yet this year, but the month is young. I did enjoy “Christmas in the Clouds” — A *Christmas Carol* retelling set on a Christmas Eve overnight flight to Australia. (You can read my pal Lily Herman’s review [here](https://us12.campaign-archive.com/?u=40ab5190f8722bb56d1105e1f&id=07801bd18f)). If you’re looking for a different take on the *Christmas Carol* or want more holiday romance goodness, check out CS Jane’s *A Holiday Hat Trick.*

Sports reporter Belle Winters has spent years waiting for her big break, and it’s finally here. She’s assigned to cover hockey star Carter Blade’s return to his hometown team just before Christmas and has decided it will be her moment — as well as Carter’s. Her plan goes awry, though, when a hockey puck hits her in the head, and Carter reappears this time as her “Christmas Coach”. He’s there to take her to her Christmases past, present and future (sound familiar?), and the journey forces her to consider the cost of her ambition.

At the risk of giving too much away — but really, it is a romance — I like how the ending inverts the traditional Hallmark finale. The book is ultimately about her finding balance versus choosing between a career and love.

# **You Still Think Finals Week is Murder**

### [Very Dangerous Things by Lauren Munoz](https://bookshop.org/a/22722/9780593857892)

High school and college students nationwide are buckling down for finals and the end of semester right now (good luck and godspeed, if that’s you), but the students in *Very Dangerous Things* have a different kind of test: solving a classmate’s murder.

For decades, J. Everett High has staged an elaborate murder mystery for its criminology students, and Dulce Castillo is determined to win this year. Right on schedule, the teen playing the victim, Xavier Torres, is found lying dead in the greenhouse, except he’s not pretending to be poisoned. Someone has really murdered him.

Suspicion quickly falls on Dulce’s ex-best friend, Sierra Fox, who begs her former bestie to clear her name. Dulce initially declines but proves unable to resist the pull of a real-life mystery and joins the case.

I had a hard time writing this description because that blurb makes it sound like a fairly straightforward YA mystery, but Munoz also includes a series of flashbacks that add a layer of complexity and create a mystery-within-a-mystery. Fans of Maureen Johnson won’t want to miss this one.

# **You’re Still Processing Your Spotify Wrapped**

### [Mood Machine: The Rise of Spotify and the Cost of the Perfect Playlist by Liz Pelly](https://bookshop.org/a/22722/9781668083505)

According to Spotify Wrapped — the streaming app’s annual recap of a user’s listening habits — my top song of the year was “[High Hopes](https://open.spotify.com/track/1rqqCSm0Qe4I9rUvWncaom?si=bc507e653d8b4ffe)” by Panic! At the Disco. I wasn’t surprised because I pretty much listened to it on repeat while [my book](https://whattoreadif.substack.com/p/everyone-is-lying-to-you-dogland-under-lock-skeleton-key) was on submission with editors. (I *was* surprised that it said I had the musical taste of 60-year-old.) If the Wrapped phenomenon has you wondering more about Spotify, you’ll want to grab *Mood Machine* by Liz Pelly.

[

![Mood Machine: The Rise of Spotify and the Cost of the Perfect Playlist by Liz Pelly](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!525n!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F98000ce3-f717-4577-b4f4-17cffc00d675_662x1000.jpeg "Mood Machine: The Rise of Spotify and the Cost of the Perfect Playlist by Liz Pelly")



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!525n!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F98000ce3-f717-4577-b4f4-17cffc00d675_662x1000.jpeg)

*Mood Machine* documents the company’s roots in the Swedish piracy scene, its relationship with artists and record labels and how it transformed not just the music industry, but music itself. She takes us behind the scenes, showing readers how Spotify pads its popular ambiance playlists with music from “ghost artists” and how its ushered in a new era of payola-like practices. While much of the book is a downer — Pelly makes a compelling case that Spotify has flattened music and discouraged artistic experimentation — it also highlights artists working to build a better system.

*Mood Machine* has hit a few Best of 2025 lists, and it’s easy to see why. In the words of New Yorker critic (and author of the [beautiful memoir](https://whattoreadif.substack.com/p/we-are-bellingcat-death-conjuror-stay-true) *[Stay True](https://bookshop.org/a/22722/9780385547772)*) Hua Hsu, “Pelly is a romantic, but her book isn’t an exercise in nostalgia. It’s about how we have come to view art and creativity, what it means to be an individual, and what we learn when we first hum along to a beloved pop song.”
