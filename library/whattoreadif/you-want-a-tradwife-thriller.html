You Binged “Secret Lives of Mormon Wives”

Everyone is Lying to You by Jo Piazza

The third season Hulu’s “The Secret Lives of Mormon Wives,” which follows prominent MomTok influencers, premiered last week. The stars of the show make their (well-paid) livings posting about their picture-perfect lives, filled with spotless homes, well-behaved children and lavish, home-cooked meals. It is, in the words of Washington Post columnist Monica Hesse, “the perfect prosperity-gospel, purity-culture, Project 2025 distillation of femininity in the current bull market era of evangelical America.”

It’s also the perfect companion to Everyone is Lying to You, Jo Piazza’s delicious #TradWife thriller. Struggling journalist Lizzie is shocked to receive an Instagram DM from Rebecca Sommers, the it “traditional life” Instagram influencer, who abruptly ended their close friendship fifteen years earlier. Rebecca offers to give Lizzie a career-defining interview at an upcoming influencer conference, but the day after they reunite Rebecca goes missing and her handsome husband is found brutally murdered.

Scared that Rebecca — and her children — could be in danger, Lizzie begins poking around and learns about the unfiltered lives of Rebecca and her fellow influencers. Following down the rabbit hole, the journalist learns her former friend hadn’t changed as much as she once thought and that the smoke-and-mirrors world of influencing hides dangerous secrets.

Everyone is Lying to You is frothy, fun and thought provoking. It combines the twists of a thriller — including one that caused me to gasp out loud and text a friend who had already read it — with sharp criticism on our current obsession with #TradWives. Clear your calendar when you start this one.

You Watch the Dog Show Every Thanksgiving

Dogland by Tommy Tomlinson

This weekend, the National Dog Show was held in Philadelphia. The annual competition will then be shown after the Macy’s Thanksgiving Day Parade on NBC (I promise this is not sponsored content). My family cannot watch the contest because my dog Ellie loses it when she sees other pups on TV, but I still really enjoyed Tommy Tomlinson’s Dogland, a deep dive into dog show culture.

While watching a dog show a few years back, Tomlinson, a long-time sportswriter, wondered, “Are those dogs happy?” To answer the question, he spent three years crisscrossing the country following a champion show dog, Striker, a fluffy snow-white Samoyed, and his handler, Laura King, as they competed on the show dog circuit with an eye towards winning the prestigious 2022 Westminster Dog Show.

In between describing Striker’s life on the road — he and his handlers travel in a custom-built RV called Betty White — Tomlinson gives us the history of dog competitions, provides new insights on the human-dog bond and introduces us to many good boys and girls and the people who love them.

Dogland is my ideal nonfiction read — fun, funny and entertaining. (Obligatory warning for my fellow dog people: there is discussion of dog death.) Tomlinson is a sharp observer of the world around him, but always comes at it with curiosity and compassion. It’s just as much a celebration of mutts as it is of show dogs.

You Caught “Now You See Me” This Weekend

Under Lock & Skeleton Key by Gigi Pandian

The third installment of the magician heist franchise, “Now You See Me,” hit theaters this week. I really enjoyed the first movie, but somehow missed that there was a second one, let alone a third. I am sure I will catch up over a lazy weekend sometime soon, but in the meantime, if you’re looking for a book filled with illusions, check out Gigi Pandian’s Under Lock & Skeleton Key.

After a terrible accident shuts down her Las Vegas magic show, long-dormant rumors of a curse harming Tempest Raj’s family begin to swirl once again. Unable to find another job, Tempest moves back in with her father and grandparents. But, when visiting her dad on a job for his company, Secret Staircase Construction, which specializes in creating secret passages and hidden nooks, she finds a body hidden in a wall and is suddenly at the center of one of the locked-room mysteries she used to love.

There are a lot of threads in this book — stage magic, trick construction, the history of detective fiction — but Pandian weaves them together admirably. Pandian is an expert on locked-room mysteries, where a crime is committed in a seemingly impossible way (i.e., a victim is found in a locked, windowless room), and her knowledge shines through as Tempest turns to detective fiction to help her solve the murder. Under Lock & Skeleton Key is a lot of fun, and I really need to read the rest of the series.
