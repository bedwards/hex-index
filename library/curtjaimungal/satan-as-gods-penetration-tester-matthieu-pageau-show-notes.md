# **Abstract**

Matthieu Pageau says that Satan is first a *function* before a villain; a God‑created tester and accuser whose corruption occurs only when will‑to‑power takes over. Firstly, he lays out a symbolic grammar where heaven represents “the plan” and earth represents “the materials,” with water serving the dual role of destruction and renewal. Secondly, he reframes the “feminine” principle as that which *crowns* and renovates forms. Gödel’s incompleteness theorem, he claims, is Eve bringing a riddle to Adam. Thirdly, he speaks from his own exile… Matthieu is partial to Orthodoxy and Catholicism, and at the same time he’s unable to plant a flag until certain unanswered questions resolve (these questions are explored in the podcast). The stories of Tamar and Ruth trace exile and redemption, while the Adam and Eve story reveal what secrecy breaks. This is a rare interview. With a high degree of likelihood, I can say that if watched until the end, it will change your life.

## **Who Is Matthieu Pageau?**

Matthieu Pageau is a Canadian thinker and the author of *The Language of Creation: Cosmic Symbolism in Genesis* (2018). He is the brother of the Orthodox icon‑carver Jonathan Pageau ([who I spoke to here](https://youtu.be/X3co_AA6yec)). Matthieu approaches biblical symbolism from a background in computer science and mathematics, focusing on the fractal‑like patterns embedded in Genesis. While his brother emphasizes public teaching, Matthieu describes his work as “internal criticism”; doubting his own interpretations, seeking what he doesn’t yet understand, and allowing reality to correct him. He describes himself as spiritually homeless and is working toward joining the Orthodox or Catholic Church once certain unresolved questions find their answers.

## **Matthieu’s Top 3 Theses**

**Quick:**

1.  Satan is a God‑created *function* (tester and accuser) that becomes evil only when corrupted by will‑to‑power.
    
2.  The “feminine” principle in biblical symbolism represents renewal, updating, and crowning; Gödel’s incompleteness theorem *is* Eve’s riddle to Adam.
    
3.  Holy criticism lacks will‑to‑power; fallen criticism seeks dominion; knowing the difference is the key to escaping metaphysical exile.

## **Key Insights**

-   **Satan as Divine Penetration Tester:** Pageau argues that God created the Satan‑function to test systems for flaws, just as a company hires a hacker to probe its security. The function becomes fallen only when the tester uses discovered vulnerabilities for personal power, such as blackmail, coercion, or hostage‑taking. In the Book of Job, Satan isn’t fallen; he keeps asking God for permission and follows the laws God sets for him.
    
-   **Heaven as a Blueprint, Earth as the Lumber:** The opening of Genesis isn’t metaphysics in the abstract; it’s a construction manual. Heaven is the plan for what you want to build; earth is the raw materials. Before a plan exists, materials have no purpose. “I made a plan for my house and I bought the materials. Then I built my house.” That’s the structure of creation.
    
-   **Water’s Dual Symbolism:** In the first creation narrative, water is something to *get rid of*; God separates it, moves it to the periphery. However, in the second narrative, water becomes *positive*; a river to water the garden. Chaos (water) is bad when it has no function, good when it serves renewal. This is why baptism cleanses; it’s the same principle inverted.
    
-   **The Feminine as Crown:** A “crown” in biblical symbolism means *improvement*; pointing a system higher than it was. Pageau connects this to the feminine principle: testing limits, finding blind spots, renewing what’s grown stale. A wife is a “crown to her husband” not as a trophy, but as the force that keeps him from hardening into corruption.
    
-   **Gödel’s Incompleteness as Eve’s Riddle:** Mathematics is the masculine side; consisting of axioms, deductions, naming the animals. Then the feminine side brings a paradox that can’t be proved or disproved within the system. The liar’s paradox didn’t *break* mathematics; it improved it by revealing boundaries. “Now I know there’s some things outside my system, but I can still know things about it.”
    
-   **The Fall Was Avoidable by Asking:** Adam and Eve’s failure wasn’t complicated. The snake told Eve that God was lying. All she had to do was *ask God*. “This guy’s telling you that God is lying to you. Okay, I’m going to go ask God.” The snake’s power depends on preventing communication; keeping secrets, ensuring parties don’t talk. Subversion requires silence.
    
-   **Will‑to‑Power Corrupts Criticism:** Miriam criticized Moses and was struck with plague. Why? Because she said “I’m a prophet too”; she wanted recognition, leadership, power. Her criticism wasn’t in service of the system; it was a bid for dominance. Holy criticism has no ambition attached; it asks questions without demanding a throne.

## **Terms Defined**

-   **Satan (function):** The God‑created role of tester and accuser, the one who probes systems for flaws. Distinct from the fallen entity who uses that role for personal power.
    
-   **Heaven (symbolic):** The plan, blueprint, or seed of what you want to create. Analogous to Newton’s formula before it’s applied to reality.
    
-   **Earth (symbolic):** The materials, powers, and skills that serve a function. It’s purposeless without a plan (heaven) to organize them.
    
-   **Crown (symbolic):** That which improves a system; points it higher. The feminine role of renewal and renovation.
    
-   **Fallen:** Corrupt through misuse of given powers. Not merely “bad at the job” but actively using one’s function for unauthorized ends; will‑to‑power.
    
-   **Nature:** That which exists without artificial (human) law imposed. Water is the purest symbol; it flows without will. Returning to nature renews corrupted systems.
    
-   **Exile (spiritual):** A state of quest; having questions no authority can yet answer. Matthieu describes himself as in exile, unable to join a church until his questions are resolved.
    
-   **Will‑to‑Power:** The desire to dominate, lead, or be recognized. When attached to criticism, it corrupts the critic into a fallen Satan.
    
-   **Incompleteness (Gödelian):** The demonstration that any sufficiently powerful formal system contains truths it can’t prove. Pageau calls this the “feminine crown” to mathematics; a boundary that humbles and improves.
    
-   **Renewal:** Cleaning, updating, or improving a system. The function of water, the feminine, competitions, and democracy. Dangerous because it can also corrupt or destroy.

## **Open Questions & Counterarguments**

**Unresolved:**

-   What specific question caused Matthieu’s spiritual exile, and why can’t he speak it publicly without becoming “subversive”?
    
-   If Adam had asked God instead of eating, would Eve have been exiled alone, and could Adam have redeemed her from the garden?
    
-   Can symbolic interpretation be falsified, or does the absence of falsifiability make it arbitrary?
    

**Strongest Objection to Thesis 1 (Satan as God‑Created Function):** If God created Satan’s function, God is implicated in evil’s possibility. The Book of Job shows Satan acting with permission, but permission isn’t the same as creation. The objection: perhaps Satan’s function *emerged* through corruption rather than being designed; otherwise, God authored the instrument of temptation.

**Strongest Objection to Thesis 2 (Gödel as Feminine Crown):** Gödel’s incompleteness theorem is a precise logical result about formal systems, not a gendered metaphor. Mapping it onto Eve’s riddle risks aestheticizing mathematics and losing its actual content. The objection: symbolic readings that reduce technical results to archetypes may obscure more than they illuminate.

**Strongest Objection to Thesis 3 (Holy Criticism Lacks Will‑to‑Power):** Every critic has *some* interest; pure disinterest is impossible. Even Matthieu, by publishing a book and appearing on podcasts, seeks recognition and influence. The objection: the line between “holy” and “fallen” criticism may be a matter of degree, not kind; and the framing allows anyone to claim their criticism is the humble sort.
