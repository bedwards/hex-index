The Heat Geek Bods have addressed that limitation as well. A typical large water cylinder in a heat pump system has a heat exchanger coil inside it that carries hot water from the heat pump. The coil transfers that heat into the cylinder water which can then be used for your washing up or your shower or bath or whatever. Because water expands when it heats up, there also has to be an expansion vessel located somewhere nearby.

And regulations say there needs to be a pressure relief valve connected via 22 mm copper pipe that has to be rooted to the outside of the building. All a bit of a faf, not to mention all the space it takes up. Adam and his colleagues at HeatGeek have completely upended that design with a cylinder that they call the mini store. Instead of running hot water from the heat pump through the heat exchanger coil, the mini store runs that hot water straight into the main body of the cylinder and then lets it heat up the household water, which now runs through the coil and back out into the home.

Sounds like a very simple swap around, doesn't it? But it means the cylinder can be shrunk down to a size that can be fitted inside a kitchen base unit. Why? Well, because the cylinder can now be reloaded much more rapidly at the full power capacity of the heat pump, but running at a lower temperature because the hot water is no longer restricted by the small diameter of the copper pipe.

And because the water storage volume is now below a crucial industry threshold of 16 L, the mini store cylinder no longer requires an additional expansion vessel. And there's no need for a pressure relief valve and all that large pipe work that has to be run through the outside of the building. Plus, as an added bonus, there's no need to run a weekly anti-leionella cycle because the consumable water only flows in and out of the heat exchange coil as and when it's required, which means you save on that additional weekly running cost as well. In other words, the whole thing runs much more like a combi boiler and there's no problem fitting it into tight spaces in the house or a flat.
