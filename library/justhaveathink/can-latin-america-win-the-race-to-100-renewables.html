There's also plenty of wind and a huge amount of hydro power. That last one is potentially a bit of a double-edged sword to be fair, but we'll come back to that a bit later in the video. The continent's most conspicuous and arguably most important asset, at least from a climate point of view, is of course the huge natural carbon sink of the Amazon and other forestry areas. Most governments have recognized that a properly managed energy transition has the potential to reduce deforestation pressures in those regions.

So if countries like Brazil, Chile, Uruguay, and Argentina can scale renewables rapidly, it's going to be a huge win, not just for their environments and economies, but for all of us across the rest of the planet as well. Many South American countries have identified a huge opportunity for renewable energy to meet rapidly growing demand and to bridge the energy poverty gap, especially in rural communities and other remote areas that are not grid connected and don't yet enjoy the benefits of electrification. And there's export potential via interconnectors as well, which is the sort of thing that gives government beam counters a warm glowy feeling, isn't it? This recent article on the Clean Technica website by industry analyst Michael Barnard points out the really quite remarkable fact that for the first time in history, there are no new coal fired power plants being planned anywhere on the continent.

10 years ago, there were no fewer than 18 new plants in the pipeline. But that's all evaporated now, not least as a result of China's 2021 decision to cease funding all overseas coal power investments, which essentially shut down several key coal projects. Under the leadership of President Lula, Brazil has positioned itself as the obvious poster child for renewable transformation in Latin America. And with some justification, too.

According to PV magazine, last year no less than 88% of that country's electricity came from hydro, wind, solar, and biomass with wind and solar representing about 24% of the overall mix. That wind and solar share is more than double what it was just a few years ago during the Bolsinaro regime. Crucially, that means Brazil is placing far less emphasis on building big hydroelectric dams and far more emphasis on solar PV and wind. In other words, we're not just seeing marginal growth in alternatives to hydro.

We're starting to see real structural change, something that Lula will no doubt be glad to trumpet at the forthcoming COP 30 climate conference in BM this coming November. It's probably worth just expanding a bit on the whole hydroelectric power thing here because as I mentioned earlier, it really is a bit of a double-edged sword. On the face of it, it's a technology with immense appeal given the geographical conditions that South America enjoys. It's renewable, it's well established, and it's capable of providing large scale base load electricity.

But there are some significant downsides. Some of those really large reservoirs behind hydroelectric dams can emit methane and carbon dioxide from the decomposition of flooded organic matter which according to this 2022 article can in some cases be comparable to the emissions of local fossil fuel fired power plants. Those enormous dams also cause very well doumented impacts downstream. They can block fish migration, change river temperatures and sediment transport, reduce fertility of downstream flood plane soils, and destroy aquatic habitats.

Building a dam often requires relocating people and inundating forests or cultural sites, which has sometimes triggered social conflict and environmental justice issues. Releasing stored water for power often reduces flows needed downstream for agriculture and human consumption. And in regions with heavy rainfall or seismic risk, the actual fabric of the dam itself can become a hazard, especially if it's not properly maintained over time. But perhaps the biggest worry for hydropower is our rapidly warming climate.

Rivers and waterheds are already experiencing reduced or more erratic rain patterns and more frequent droughts. The IEA warns that Latin American hydro powers overall capacity factor is projected to decline about 8% on average by midentury simply as a result of the changing climate with some areas seeing far worse decreases than that as a result of reduced precipitation and runoff in places like the central Andes and Patagonian region. So, while the temptation to build new dams for a quick win of apparently base load energy must be quite high in the minds of some policy makers, there's a growing acceptance that it's not a silver bullet and that there are now other options that make more socioeconomic, environmental, and political sense. Nevertheless, the reality is that it still represents a vital resource for South American countries.

In Uruguay, for example, it's been a significant contributor to that country getting roughly 99% of its electricity from renewables in recent years, according to figures from the US Department of Commerce. Elsewhere on the continent, other countries are trying to catch up. Chile has transformed itself from a coal heavy nation to become a global leader in renewable energy. That country's Atakama desert is one of the sunniest places on Earth.

And it's now host to massive solar projects producing electrons at record low prices. Growth has been so rapid though that now Chile faces the dreaded cailment problem where renewable output exceeds what the grid can deal with causing wind turbines to be locked down and solar parks to be temporarily disconnected all at very high cost. That's frustrating because if that excess electricity generation could be put to use in other energy hungry critical infrastructure applications like water desalination for example or perhaps even green hydrogen it would effectively providing power at close to zero marginal cost. The folks at Ember tell us that almost 20% of Chile's wind and solar was cailed in 2024 costing the country millions in lost revenues and potential savings across industry transport and agriculture.

The Chilean government is on the case though thankfully in 2025 the country already has just over 5 gawatt hours worth of battery energy storage either already built or in progress to minimize cailment soak up those excess electrons and distribute them back into the grid when demand is high and renewable supply is low. Colombia is another good news story. It has historically been a very large coal exporter but in 2021 it joined a body called the powering past coal alliance and a year later under the opaces of president pro Colombia committed to winding down coal power and mining operations and supporting workers and communities into alternatives like renewable energy projects, agriculture and ecoourism. Even Argentina under the stewardship of a chainsaw wielding lunatic is showing some positive signs of renewable growth, albeit not quite on the scale of its neighbors.

By 2024, renewables in that country made up about 16% of national power generation. In 2025, installed renewable capacity exceeded 7.1 gawatt, driven by Argentina's national renewables target law. According to this analysis, Argentina's vast solar, wind, and hydroelectric renewable energy potential provides an opportunity to move towards the target of 30% of the energy mix by 2030. On the other hand, that renewables target law is set to expire at the end of this year, and observers say it may not be renewed under the current political leadership.

Argentina is also estimated to have the second largest shale gas reserves globally and the fourth largest shale oil reserves which of course President Javeier Mille with his drill baby drill attitude is likely to exploit to the fullest extent possible. And in fact methane gas could still prove to be the thorn in the side of all of South America's green progress. As Michael Barard explains, as of early 2025, roughly 100 active gas 5 projects are either under construction or in development across Latin America. Despite that though, the good news is that the scale of renewable energy development far surpasses even this level of new methane gas infrastructure.

And the continental picture really does look quite bright for renewables. International investors, development banks, and climate funds are pouring capital into renewables in Latin America. Domestic banks and pension funds appear to be getting increasingly comfortable financing grids scale renewable energy projects. And the promise of exportable renewable power via those interconnectors that I mentioned earlier is now attracting speculative forward-looking capital as well.

Against all that, we've got notorious currency and sovereign risks in some Latin American countries that can scare away the sort of long-term capital needed for the typical 20 to 25 year lifetime of a solar park. Then there's national policies that can lurch from one extreme to another as leaders come and go, often resulting in a general lack of strong regulatory and permitting frameworks in many jurisdictions. Despite rapid recent growth, utility scale battery energy storage capacity is still relatively small and long duration storage is currently expensive and yet to scale. Many grid systems aren't yet designed for high shares of variable renewables and need urgent upgrades to futureproof them.

All of which represent long-term capital investments that policymakers have to justify to their electorate. Not an easy thing to do, especially in today's feile political climate. Land acquisition, permitting, and local opposition can also slow projects down. And of course, quite rightly, ecosystems, the rights of indigenous peoples and environmental standards have to be rigorously respected.

Otherwise, projects can find themselves pushed back or even canceled altogether. So, you know, a mixed bag. But no one ever said this energy transition thing was going to be easy. At least no one that was properly paying attention anyway.

It's arguably the most ambitious and consequential challenge humanity has ever faced because it touches everything from economics to geopolitics, from basic engineering to cutting edge technology and even the fundamental way we look at growth and social equity. South America matters a lot.
