My children are eating breakfast.

The flurry of escalating demands—milk, toaster waffles, peanut butter and jelly (no, wait, no jelly!), syrup (no, not on top! On the side!), more syrup, more milk (in the blue cup!)—has given way to loud munching and silent smearing of peanut butter across cheeks.

Congratulating myself on nearing the downhill portion of today’s first challenge, I open the cabinet to an alarming discovery.

We are out of coffee.

I remain calm in crisis, and inform my children that after breakfast, we’ll be heading to the local coffee shop.

While wiping peanut butter off my one year old’s earlobe, I lob a vague, noncommittal statement into the ether. Something about how we just ate breakfast, so we don’t need to get any treats at the coffee shop, and instead will just be getting Mom a coffee.1

Can I pretend to be Mittens the cat? My three year old replies.2

When we arrive at the coffee shop, I unbuckle their carseats. I rest the diaper bag on one hip, sling 25 pounds of one year old onto the other, and reach out a hand to escort the three year old across the parking lot.

I am out of breath when I get to the counter. I deposit the kids next to the glass display case while reaching for my wallet.

There are donuts.

Mom! Mom! Mom! Can I have a donut? Mom! Please? My older one asks.

Choc-lat! My younger one assents.

I look at the barista, who is waiting expectantly, an iced half-caf Americano3 already in the works behind her. I envision my kids’ protests and cries. I think about how tired I am, and how much harder it will be if I say no. I imagine the looks of absolute delight on their faces if I say yes.

Sure! I say. Let’s get two.

Real life? Ah, I hadn’t considered that.

Before having kids, I imagined I’d be a “strict” parent. Loving, of course, but also firm, consistent, authoritative.

The idea was likely born out of my training in child psychology, where the evidence emphasizes limits and structure, and my own childhood, where my parents were admirably consistent in their enforcement of (very reasonable) rules.4

But when I dig in a little deeper, this idea of myself as a parent is harder to define. Look closely, and it turns to smoke, drifting away in wispy plumes. What exactly does this “strict” parent look like?

This idea I had of myself as a parent is just that—an idea.

A vague image of me, say, reciting some script about okay and not okay behaviors, or snuggling a child at the end of a perfect day filled with structure and routine. No real details, no consideration for how my kids or I might feel, no plan for how to stop a one year old from systematically ripping the pages out of a book while a three year old hugs a death grip on your legs.

Why didn’t I think of this?

In Experimental History last week, wrote about a concept psychologists call “unpacking.”5 It’s the process by which our minds actively fill in the details of hypothetical events or situations. The summary? We humans are pretty bad at it.

When we imagine a scenario, we tend to gloss over the particulars. It’s easier and more efficient that way.

When it comes to parenting, though, this leaves many of us in a strange situation. Sure, we’ve thought about what we might be like as a parent. We’ve imagined ourselves lovingly cradling a baby, or maybe, based on what our own parents did, we’ve made plans to emulate (or reject) their styles.

But we haven’t truly unpacked.

We haven’t pictured the moment when, after hours of keeping two tiny humans alive on the playground, we’ve raised our pregnancy-swollen legs up on the couch and have to decide whether to enforce our “no playing with the toilet paper” rule.6

We haven’t thought about how we’ll respond when we tell them we’ll read two books tonight, and then they snuggle their little bodies under our arms, look up at us, and ask if we can do just one more.

We haven’t imagined that, when we’re at another coffee shop one day at 8:30am, and we’re already two tantrums in, our children will beg us for a bag of pickle chips7 and we’ll think Oh, what the hell, and hand them right over.

Unpacking as we go

There are days when I compare myself to this impossible, ephemeral standard of parenting. The standard where I am flawlessly consistent. Where I somehow know, without any seed of doubt, when to hold firm and when to let things go. On those days, I wonder if I’m making mistakes. If I’m taking the easy way out. If I’m not living up to the type of parent I thought I should be.

Then there are days—the rational ones—where I see it as a type of freedom, rather than a failure. Where there is no real standard. Where the type of parent I am now, and the one I will be, are simply choices I get to make everyday, for myself and for my kids.

Because maybe it’s impossible to unpack all of this before becoming a parent. Maybe it’s better that way.

We can know the basics—of the importance of warmth and structure, of loving our kids and setting some limits—but we can never know exactly how we’ll approach it. How we’ll find the balance of being strict and permissive, rigid and flexible. What we’ll decide on that donut, and on the millions of other decisions we’ll make each day as parents.

We can’t know every scenario that parenting will send our way.

Instead, all we can do is wade through the smoke, and feel confident that we’re figuring it out, unpacking the details, as we go.
