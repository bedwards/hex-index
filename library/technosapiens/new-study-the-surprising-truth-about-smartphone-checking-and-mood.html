Something I learned early on in my career as a researcher is that, when you’re working on a study, people are often interested in hearing what it’s about—but if you provide a little too much detail, things start to go off the rails.

You’ll be at a party, making small talk, trying not to be weird, and the conversation will inevitably drift to what you do for a living.

Oh, what are you working on? They’ll ask.

It’s really interesting! you’ll say, I’m testing within- and between-person associations between objective measures of adolescent smartphone behavior and positive and negative affect using a 14-day ecological momentary assessment procedure…

You will soon have no friends.

Well, sapiens, I like to tell myself that Techno Sapiens is like one, big, nerdy digital party, and that we’re already all friends here, so buckle up!

We’re talking about a hot-off-the-presses study1 I recently published with Dr. Kaitlyn Burnell and other colleagues at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, and I can’t wait to share it with you all in plenty of (Fun! Interesting! Not the reason you wander away to “get another drink”) detail.

Let’s get our minds right

In this study, we2 investigated teens’ smartphone “pickups,” so to orient ourselves, let’s take a quick look at our own pickup behavior.

For those who have an iPhone,3 please take the following steps:

  • Go to “Settings”

  • Go to “Screen time”

  • Go to “See all app and website activity”

  • Scroll down to the section labeled “Pickups”

  • In the bar graph, click on the bar for yesterday.

How many “pickups” did you have?

A pickup is recorded anytime you turn on your phone’s screen, either by unlocking it or interacting with a notification.

Chances are, you do this a lot.45 You’ll be unsurprised to learn that teens do, too.

So, in this study, we set out to answer some basic questions about this behavior: does the number of times teens pick up their phones influence their mood? And does their mood influence how often they pick up their phones?

What we did

The study involved 135 teens (average age: 15) attending public schools in rural North Carolina.

  • For two weeks, we texted the teens three times per day, with links to a short survey.6

  • In these surveys, they reported how they were feeling at that moment, rating positive emotions (“happy,” “calm”) and negative emotions (“anxious,” “mad”) on a 5-point scale.

  • Each day, we also asked them to upload a screenshot of their smartphone pickups from the previous day. This “objective” measure is important because it avoids teens guessing (often incorrectly) how much they used their phones.

  • They also filled out a longer set of questionnaires, including one about how mindful7 they are in day-to-day life

Then, it was time for the analyses! Here’s what we tested:

  • Do teens who pick up their phones more often, on average, show more ups and downs (i.e., variability) in their emotions? How about the reverse?

  • When teens pick up their phones more than usual on a given day, do they show higher negative or positive emotions the next day? How about the reverse?

  • If teens are generally more mindful, do their smartphone pickups have less impact on their mood, and vice versa?

What we found

First, this was not the point of the study, but interesting nonetheless: teens picked up their phones an average of 112.6 times per day.

What did they do on their phones right after picking them up? Over thousands of pickups across all participants:

  • 49% of pickups were for Snapchat

  • 13% for Instagram

  • 12% for Messages

  • 7% for TikTok

Now, what about our main analyses? There are three major takeaways here.

Takeaway #1

Teens who checked their smartphones more frequently on average also showed more ups and downs in negative emotions (e.g., sad, angry, anxious).

Why? In this case, we do not know the “direction of effects.” So, it could be that kids who are looking at their phones more often are seeing things that are making them upset. It could also be the opposite: kids who already have more spikes in negative emotions are turning to their phones as a way to feel better. Or it could be some other factor driving both the phone pickups and negative ups and downs (e.g., difficulties with self-regulation).

Takeaway #2

When a teen had a bad day (i.e., lower positive emotions than usual), they picked up their phone more the next day, but only if they had lower mindfulness skills.

Why? This might be about coping. Kids who are less mindful may be less able to regulate their emotions. When they have a bad day, they might turn to their phones to help themselves feel better. This could be a bad thing, if there are more effective strategies they could be using. It could also be a good thing! If they’re using their phones to reach out to friends and family for support, for example, that would be something to encourage.

The good news is that we do not see the opposite direction of effects. Kids who pick up their phones more often than usual on a given day do not show worse moods the next day. So, it does not appear that the increase in phone pickups is creating a negative cycle.

Takeaway #3

We still have a lot to learn.

Why? As is often the case in research, many of our analyses revealed…nothing. The data did not support many of our expected findings. Teens’ phone pickups on a given day did not predict anything about their mood on that same day, or the next day. Having better mindfulness skills made no difference in the relationship between mood variability and smartphone pickups.

Was this a little bit of a bummer after many years of work on this study? Yes!8 Was it a surprise? Not at all.

There is, of course, only so much that we can learn from the number of times a teen picks up their smartphone. We do not know, exactly, what’s happening on that phone after they pick it up, nor do we know the many other things happening in their lives that are influencing their mood.

To me, this study confirms a few important truths: when it comes to teens’ emotions, smartphones matter, but the ways they matter depend a lot on what they’re doing on there and who they are. “Objective” measures of smartphone use (from screenshots of actual phone behavior, for example) may lead to different findings than self-reported measures.

And, of course: science is messy. That’s why it’s important to share—at your favorite digital party, surrounded by friends—the details.
