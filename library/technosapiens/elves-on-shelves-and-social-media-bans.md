###### ***8 min read***

A few days ago, I woke up abruptly to the sound of a crying baby. The clock read 4:55am.[1](#footnote-1) The next 90 minutes were filled with shushing and pacifiers and re-swaddling, a move from the crib in the nursery to the bassinet next to my bed, more shushing and rocking, until eventually, a waving white flag in the form of a feeding.

The feeding was interrupted by the desperate wailing of a sick toddler[2](#footnote-2) from the other side of the wall. I raced in to find him sitting upright in his crib. *MOM!* he cried, *Need to wipe my nose!* I set the baby down. She resumed crying. I grabbed a tissue. *Ready, blow!* I encouraged, holding the tissue to his nose. *1-2-...*

The nose-wiping was interrupted by a new sound: this one a mysterious banging from my preschooler’s room next door. With a crying baby in one arm and a snotty toddler in the other, I flung open the door to find him sitting on the floor, his bookshelf empty, a sea of colorful pages strewn across the rug.

*Let’s get ready for school!* I offered, a bead of sweat forming on my back. The baby spit up. My toddler rubbed his nose on my pants. My preschooler walked into the bathroom, dropped to the floor, curled up on the bathmat, and looked up at me. *I’m too tired.*

And just when all hope was lost, when I was one sneeze away from crawling back into bed, pulling the covers over my head, and telling the kids to fend for themselves, inspiration struck.

*Sneaky is waiting for you downstairs! Let’s get ready fast so we can go find him!*

## **Won’t someone think of the elves?**

I have a controversial take, and I think it’s time I stopped hiding it:

I love Elf on the Shelf.

Is it a little creepy? Sure. Is it yet another to-do on a never-ending holiday to-do list? Of course. Do I sometimes wake up in a cold sweat, panicked that I’ve forgotten to move Sneaky? Obviously!

And yet. This small, poorly designed, incredibly unsettling doll brings my children a joy I cannot describe.

I know, I know. I’ve seen the Internet vitriol directed at the Elf. I know it’s inspired thousands [of Reddit comments](https://www.reddit.com/r/toddlers/comments/1pble36/parents_that_dont_do_elf_on_the_shelf_how_do_you/) and a generation of Never-Elf parents.[3](#footnote-3) From a behavioral science perspective, it probably [won’t work](https://parentdata.org/elf-shelf-economic-theory/) to change your kids’ behavior in any meaningful way (sorry). And to be fair, I do skip out on any elaborate set-ups \[the Elf on the Shelf Pinterest community is a scary place\]. I’ve also sidestepped the whole “the Elf is watching you” surveillance narrative.

But you know what? It’s a holiday tradition that takes me less than a minute a day, elicits excited shrieks from my children, *and* that has saved us (me) from imploding on many a December morning.

When I take a step back and consider the many, many things I do for my children, the ROI on this one actually seems pretty good.

On that particular morning, minutes after I suggested going to find Sneaky, we were downstairs, dressed and teeth-brushed and hair-combed. Little feet danced across the floor, arms waving. *Mom! Mom! Mom! Look at Sneaky!* They giggled, pointing to our elf, with runny noses and tired bodies forgotten.

We sat and ate breakfast. I finished feeding the baby. There was, for a moment, contented silence. A Christmas miracle.

*Mom, will Sneaky be here tomorrow?* They asked.

To that, I answered honestly. *I hope so.*

## **Happy holidays, the Techno Sapiens way**

Now that we’ve gotten the truly controversial stuff out of the way, let’s tackle a few other tech topics that have been in the news this holiday season: social media bans, smartphones, and AI (oh my!).

### **1\. Australia enacts social media ban for kids under 16**

It happened! On December 10, the Australian “social media ban” went into effect. I’ve gotten lots of questions about this and seen strong opinions on either side. So, as always, I’m going to do the whole Techno Sapiens thing and try to consider *all* the facts, even if it leads me to tap dance right down the middle and infuriate both sides.

First, let’s clarify [what’s actually happening here](https://www.esafety.gov.au/young-people/social-media-age-restrictions).[4](#footnote-4) Australians under 16 will not be allowed to have accounts on the following platforms: Facebook, Instagram, Kick, Reddit, Snapchat, Threads, TikTok, Twitch, X and YouTube. They will still be able to see publicly available content on these platforms. Teens themselves will not be punished if “illegal” accounts are discovered, but platforms could face penalties. Platforms will verify age with a range of methods: age estimation from platform behavior, IDs, selfies, third-party services, etc.

There have been many questions about the ban: will it work? Will it actually keep kids off the platforms? Will it keep kids safer? Will it improve the desired outcomes (academic performance, well-being, etc.)? And, as an expert, I’m here to tell you: *we don’t know.* This is the first policy of its kind, and unfortunately, we cannot see into the future.

That said, here’s what I think. We need to set the age at which kids can create social media accounts somewhere. Right now it’s 13, but that is totally arbitrary. All else equal, it makes sense to me to raise that age. This assumes kids still have other ways to connect digitally with one another and seek out information online, and with Australia’s policy, it seems that they do.

My concern remains that with this policy, social media platforms have a convenient “out” when it comes to actually making platforms safer for kids. Children under 16 *will* still access them—this is not an argument against the ban, just a reality—but now, when they inevitably do, platforms may no longer be accountable for what happens. After all, the kids weren’t supposed to be there in the first place.

I’d love to see companies making the platforms safer and healthier for kids (and adults!), even as we raise the minimum age. I’m curious to see how this plays out, but overall, I think it’s a step in the right direction.

*For more on social media and mental health, see* [this Techno Sapiens guide](https://technosapiens.substack.com/p/teens-phones-and-mental-health).

### **2\. Brand new data on teens and tech**

The Pew Research Center is out with [its yearly data](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/12/09/teens-social-media-and-ai-chatbots-2025/) on teens and technology. Christmas come early, sapiens! Over the past few years, there’s been little change in the share of teens ages 13 to 17 who use YouTube (92%), TikTok (68%), Instagram (63%), and Snapchat (55%). A slightly greater percentage of teens say they use TikTok “almost constantly” (21% this year, up from 16% in 2022).

More interesting to me is the new data on teens’ use of AI. This is Pew’s first year asking about AI chatbots like ChatGPT, and here’s what they find:

-   64% of teens have ever used AI chatbots
    
-   Nearly half of teens use them several times a week or more
    
-   Nearly 3 in 10 using them daily.
    

Expect these numbers to continue increasing!

*For more on teens and AI, see:* [What should we do about kids and AI?](https://technosapiens.substack.com/p/what-should-we-do-about-kids-and) *and* [Are AI companions replacing real friends?](https://open.substack.com/pub/technosapiens/p/are-ai-companions-replacing-real)

### **3\. New study on when to get your child a smartphone**

There’s a [new study](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article-abstract/doi/10.1542/peds.2025-072941/205716/Smartphone-Ownership-Age-of-Smartphone-Acquisition) out in *Pediatrics* this month, which finds that children who had smartphones at age 12 showed higher rates of depression, obesity, and poor sleep compared to those who did not. Tweens who got phones between ages 12 and 13 also showed higher mental health symptoms and worse sleep compared to those who did not.

So, what to make of this? Here’s what I shared with Catherine Pearson, who [reported on this study](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/01/well/family/early-smartphone-ownership-study.html) for the *New York Times*:

> *Jacqueline Nesi, an assistant professor of psychiatry and human behavior at Brown University, who writes the newsletter Techno Sapiens, about parenting in the digital age, cautioned that the new study could not prove that smartphones were directly causing harm.*
> 
> *“It’s incredibly difficult, if not impossible, to get that kind of causal evidence on this topic,” she said, though the findings may “nudge” parents toward delaying giving children a smartphone when possible.*
> 
> *Caregivers “do not need to wait for perfect evidence to make these kinds of decisions,” Dr. Nesi said. They should feel empowered to trust their gut, she added, and to hold off on giving their child a smartphone until everyone is ready — including parents, who have to do the very hard work of putting protections and limits in place.*
> 
> *“Giving a child a device with access to everything on the internet is going to be risky,” she said.*

I share this full quote because it’s a good summary of my current thinking on this topic overall. We do not have perfect evidence on the age to get our kids smartphones, and we likely never will. We also do not *need* perfect evidence to make decisions for our kids. In many cases, this is going to mean delaying smartphones.

*For more on first smartphones, see* [When should kids get a smartphone?](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/when-should-kids-get-a-smartphone/)
