###### ***5 min read***

I’m sitting in a tiny, beige room at the doctor’s office, my elbow sticking slightly to the padded arm of the blood-draw chair. Next to me, the phlebotomist is clacking away at a keyboard.

She glances over at me. *Ready for another one?*

We’re onto the third of four blood draws this morning. It’s a three-hour glucose test, which means the only thing I’ve ingested in the past 14 hours is a warm, lime-flavored drink with 100 grams of sugar.

There are thoughts swirling around my brain, but I’m having trouble coalescing them into anything coherent. It feels like cotton balls have taken up residence behind my eyes. I’m squinting at a sign on the wall in front of me. I’m confident it’s written in English, with words like “insurance” and “Quest,” but no matter how many times I read it, its meaning fails to translate.

*Ready!* I lie, wondering if I might pass out.

She approaches with a small needle. And then, as she pokes my arm, it happens. She asks the question.

*So, this is your third boy?*

Or was it: *So, is it a third boy?*

Or maybe: *So, you’re having a third boy?*

I try to replay it a few times in my head, but, buried deep in the brain fog, it’s like a song lyric I can’t quite get right.

I laugh. *I don’t know,* I say, *it’s a surprise!*

## Baby science, just for me

With my first two children, my husband and I found out the sex of the baby as soon as possible. No fanfare, no balloons, no cake with blue frosting—just an eagerly-awaited, 12-week phone call and a *yes, we want to know right now.*

We now have two boys who are, objectively, the most perfect, loving, curious, smart, adorable children in the history of all children.

For our third child, we’re opting to wait until the birth to find out. And no matter how many times I try decoding that phlebotomist’s question—did she ruin the surprise after looking at my medical chart? Or was she simply asking?—the suspense is only building.

So, when the headlines about [a new study](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adu7402) popped up last week—*[Have all girls or all boys? Study suggests the odds aren't 50/50](https://www.npr.org/2025/07/22/nx-s1-5471382/births-boys-girls-odd-chance-research)*—I was pretty sure the researchers had peered right into my glucose-test-addled brain and designed it just for me.

I, at 33 weeks pregnant, immediately read and analyzed the study like my life depends on it—which, in some ways, it does.

So, what’s the deal? Are some people more likely to have all children of a single sex?

And more urgently: will my baby be a girl or a boy? *Science, please tell me!!!*

## **The study at hand (and belly)**

Here’s the basic premise of this new [study](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adu7402).

Researchers drew on a sample of over 58,000 U.S. women, collected from 1956-2015. They asked a seemingly simple question: were women who already had two or more children of the same sex more likely to have another one of that same sex?

I say “seemingly simple” because this quickly gets complicated. The main issue is that the choice to have more kids is not random—many families *want* a mix of boys and girls, so they may stop having kids only after they have both. This is called “coupon collecting.”[2](#footnote-2)

The cleanest way around this issue is for analyses to exclude the last birth of every woman in the sample. So, if a woman in the sample had 4 kids total,[3](#footnote-3) the researchers would ask: if her first two kids were boys, were the chances of a third boy greater than 50/50?

According to this study, the answer is yes. For families with boys, their statistical models predict the chances of a third boy are 58%, and of a fourth are 61%. For families with girls, the chances of a third girl are 55%, and of a fourth are 58%.

These results offer evidence that maybe, a baby’s sex is not totally random. Maybe certain families really *are* more likely to have only boys or only girls, due to genetic or environmental factors.[4](#footnote-4) The paper authors highlight, for example, that older women (over age 28) are more likely than younger (under age 23) to have all children of a single sex.

## So it’s settled?

Well, no.

When it comes to this new study, there are some reasons for doubt (or, in my case, *hope*).

As the inimitable Emily Oster helpfully [points out in a recent post](https://parentdata.org/you-cant-predict-babys-biological-sex/), the sample sizes here are small for the analyses the researchers are trying to do. A total of 58,000 is large, yes, but once you start excluding final births and limiting to families with multiples of the same sex, the numbers dwindle quickly.[5](#footnote-5)

This means their statistical models might not be accurate. One point in favor of this: whereas their statistical models predict a higher likelihood (58%) of having a fourth girl, the actual data indicates a *lower* likelihood (47%).

Then there’s the issue of [prior studies](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25825766/). Population-level studies generally do not find that people are genetically predisposed to produce children of a certain sex. [One massive study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32070249/), also highlighted by Dr. Oster, looks at the *entire population of Sweden* from 1932 onward (almost 5 million births),[6](#footnote-6) and finds no correlation between a person’s sex and their siblings’ sex after ruling out “coupon collecting.”

The authors of this most recent study, however, would likely argue that these contradictory findings are a result of faulty methods—basically, that prior studies are paying too much attention to the population as a whole, and not enough attention to what happens within individual families.

And so, the debate continues on.

## Surprise, surprise

As is so often the case in science, we’re left with competing opinions and contradictory findings. This *seems* like it should be such an easy question to answer, but of course, it is not. In the end, I’m slightly more convinced by the evidence suggesting this is all totally random, but this new study does give me pause.

For now, we’ll just have to wait for more research to give us a definitive answer.

And in approximately 7 weeks, I’ll have an answer of my own.

[2](#footnote-anchor-2)

Alright, I obviously had to dig into why this is called “coupon collecting.” I found [this study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7541714/), which describes: “reproductive behaviors reflecting a preference for having children of both sexes, much like the [coupon collector’s problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coupon_collector's_problem) in probability theory where collecting a complete set of distinct coupons is considered a win.”

So, remember how when you were younger, you’d buy a box of cereal and there would be a coupon inside, and you were supposed to “collect all the coupons and win”? So you’d beg your parents to go out and buy more and more Fruity Pebbles? This is the math problem that results from that: how many cereal boxes do you need to buy to collect all the different coupons? Applying that to reproduction, the question (I guess) is: how many children (cereal boxes) do you need to have to end up with at least one child of each sex (coupons)? So, yes, I suspect the scientists who named the “coupon collecting” phenomenon are what we’d call *math people*.
