Israel’s right wing has just endured a major disappointment with the apparent collapse of its hopes of reoccupying the Gaza Strip and settling it with Jews. The two-year war has ended, more or less, and Trump seems to have forgotten about his Gaza Riviera idea. In their despair, Israeli nationalists are turning their attention once more to the West Bank. Their dream, which has wide support in Benjamin Netanyahu’s government, is to annex the territory, which Israel has controlled since 1967 in an odd legal limbo, never claiming it officially despite settling the area with a half million Jews.

The complication is that the territory is also home to three million Palestinians. Many of them, as is known, want to establish a separate state. Others would welcome annexation and would be happy to become Israeli citizens in a “one-state solution.” As Israel already has 2 million Arab citizens, Arabs would then account for 40 percent of the citizens — and a majority if Gaza were part of the equation. Not much of a Jewish state. A little Jewish emigration might enable the Arabs to change the country’s name to Palestine, bringing peace to the fevered minds of campus protestors in America.

In their efforts to square this circle, Israel’s right wingers have discovered Puerto Rico as an inspiration. The idea is that Puerto Rico is a US territory without voting rights — so if that’s good enough for America, why should Israel be forced to allow Palestinians to vote? One high-profile proponent is Trump’s former ambassador to Israel, David Friedman, a staunch supporters of the Israeli right.

In a debate with me last week, Pesach Wolicki, a Jewish settler leader, makes this point below at 1:50 — arguing that there is more than one model for democracy, so Israel can simply annex the West Bank and everything will be fine.

And a weeks before, this. “Even the United States has Puerto Rico, in which the people have some representation, they certainly have civil rights, but they are not considered equal citizens,” Malkah Fleisher, a leading settler, asserted in another debate (10:25 in the below video). She projected an air of expertise.

It is tempting, this notion that the impossible might be possible. So let’s examine the Puerto Rico “model” — a very strange situation which I got to know quite well, having lived three years on the island as the Caribbean region bureau chief fir the Associated Press.

So, first and foremost, Puerto Ricans are American citizens. Every person born on the island is automatically a citizen, and their citizenship is not conditional or second-class. There is no concurrent Puerto Rican citizenship.

This alone dismantles the notion that Israel could annex the West Bank while keeping Palestinians in some nebulous non-citizen status. If Israel were to follow the Puerto Rico model, every Palestinian living in annexed territories would become an Israeli citizen, with all the rights and responsibilities that entails.

But what about the issue of voting? Yes, Puerto Ricans cannot vote in US presidential elections while residing on the island, and their representatives in Congress are non-voting delegates. This certainly constitutes a level of disenfranchisement. Yet here’s the critical distinction: Puerto Ricans have every right to move to any of the 50 US states, establish residency, and vote like any other American citizen. They would immediately gain full representation, including the right to vote for president and to elect senators and voting members of the House of Representatives.

In fact, this is exactly what happens in practice. Millions of Puerto Ricans have moved to Florida, New York, and other states, where they are fully enfranchised. There is nothing standing in their way — no border, customs or passport control. Conversely, if a US citizen from the mainland moves to Puerto Rico and does not maintain residency in another state, they too lose their right to vote in federal elections. I experienced this firsthand during the 2000 U.S. presidential election. As a mainlander living in Puerto Rico, I couldn’t vote. This rule applies equally to all US citizens, regardless of their origins.

The same situation applies to other US territories like the US Virgin Islands, Guam, and American Samoa, affecting about 1 percent of American citizens at any given time. However, any of them can leave the territory, move to the mainland, and immediately gain full voting rights. The disenfranchisement is geographical, not ethnic or racial.

No Israeli politician is suggesting that Palestinians in Jenin could gain Israeli citizenship and then move to Tel Aviv to vote in Israeli elections. Quite the opposite—they would be annexed into Israel without the ability to vote in Israeli national elections. This would effectively create a two-tier system of governance that is fundamentally undemocratic.

Moreover, the Puerto Rican political status is neither static nor imposed. The US has allowed multiple referendums in which Puerto Ricans have been given the option to pursue independence, statehood, or continued commonwealth status. At no time did significant numbers opt for independence. No one is proposing this for the West Bank.

It’s improbable that Israel will annex the entire West Bank just because Trump allows it – there would be too much local opposition. A likelier outcome would be annexation of certain areas where settlers live close to the border – or perhaps all of “Area C” in the West Bank which would leave disconnected islands of territory under the autonomy government known as the Palestinian Authority. This is no long-term solution either. Its proponents suggest that Palestinians could be given “autonomous” self-rule or local voting rights in these areas, which would be surrounded and effectively controlled by Israel.

I have always rejected the widespread comparison of the messy situation in the West Bank to “apartheid” – a word that specifically referred to the system of racial segregation enacted in South Africa until the early 1990s. But this idea is reminiscent not of Puerto Rico but of the “Bantustans” – the pseudo-autonomous regions for black South Africans. The world was not impressed by the “autonomy” solution there, nor will it be here.

In short, any of these annexation schemes would struggle to hide the fundamentally non-democratic reality. Indeed, they would codify a form of permanent political subjugation, a system where one group of people has full political rights, while another — living under the same sovereignty — does not.

The Puerto Rico model is messy but fundamentally democratic. It is an oddity of the American electoral system, rooted in history and geography, affecting both Puerto Ricans and mainland Americans who move to the territories that aren’t US states (or the District of Columbia, which itself had no role in the Electoral College until the 23rd Amendment to the Constitution which took effect in 1961). It does not discriminate based on ethnicity, and it offers an escape clause: the right to move and gain full political representation.

So the notion that the (itself flawed) Puerto Rico model offers a precedent for the West Bank is wrong. Israel faces difficult choices about its borders, its identity, and its relationship with the Palestinians. If Israel wants to annex the West Bank, or any part of it, it must be prepared to grant full citizenship and voting rights to the people who live there. I don’t recommend that. I recommend forgetting about annexations. American politicians who care about Israel should not indulge this dangerous fantasy.
