Across the map, strange doings are afoot. In Venezuela, a US military buildup has pushed the country to the edge of a confrontation that could escalate by accident as much as by intent. On Ukraine, new talks are scheduled in Miami as Washington’s mediation is starting to look like pressure for a deal that would lock in Russian territorial gains, Benjamin Netanyahu’s request for a pardon midway through his corruption trial — on the heels of his establishment of an Oct. 7 whitewash commission — tests whether democracy can survive such an actor; in Brazil, Jair Bolsonaro’s imprisonment shows what happens when a democracy insists that no one is untouchable. Israel, improbably enough, can learn from that.

Meanwhile, the United States, a country built on immigration, is urging other countries to crack down on immigration. As for the markets, they are telling a more complicated story than the headlines suggest: a booming S&P driven almost entirely by a handful of tech giants while much of the real economy lags behind.

### Hasta luego, Maduro?

The standoff between the United States and Venezuela intensified this weekend after [Trump announced the total closure of Venezuelan airspace](https://apnews.com/article/trump-venezuela-airspace-faa-maduro-pressure-campaign-39d624a0f5d5422cdfb62826a5a56373?user_email=c53271f05b4accee9813f055052b56356f7189e4323293af4747e0a0c4eab159&utm_medium=Morning_Wire&utm_source=Sailthru_AP&utm_campaign=MorningWire_EOY_Sun_Nov30_2025&utm_term=Morning%20Wire%20Subscribers) and signaled what he called “land strikes” against drug-trafficking infrastructure “very soon.” Venezuelan “President” Nicolas Maduro called it colonialist, which is somewhat true. But it is also true that he is an illegitimate leader and a criminal.

The move expands Operation Southern Spear, under which US forces have already launched lethal attacks on around 20 suspected smuggling vessels in the Caribbean since September, leaving more than 80 people dead. Caracas has described the actions as “extrajudicial executions,” and international monitors have raised concerns over proportionality and legality. And yes, some of the videos look like executions, which would basically be a war crime.

The US has deployed roughly 12,000 troops and a dozen warships, including an aircraft carrier, near Venezuela’s coast. Officials in Washington say the mission targets narcotics networks linked to Venezuela’s military hierarchy. The Biden administration previously labeled the so-called “Cartel de los Soles” a terrorist organization, alleging that senior Venezuelan officers —including President Nicolás Maduro — are directly involved in trafficking. The US has a $50 million reward for Maduro’s capture.

Authorities in Caracas have activated militias, distributed weapons to civilian groups, and conducted military exercises involving hundreds of thousands of personnel. Defense Minister Vladimir Padrino López said this week that Venezuelans must be prepared to “defend the nation with their lives.” Many of his soldiers might not agree.

Analysts doubt that Washington intends a full-scale invasion, noting the risks of a prolonged conflict and national rally-around-the-flag effects that could strengthen Maduro. Limited missile or air strikes targeting alleged smuggling hubs appear more likely. Others see Trump invoking his long-stated “escalate to negotiate” doctrine, raising pressure to force talks.

The broader question remains whether the alleged criminality and unquestionably brutal authoritarianism of Maduro’s government – which has caused millions to flee – justify intervention. International law (which some believe in, and others less so) permits force primarily in cases of self-defense or UN Security Council authorization—neither currently applies. Advocates argue intervention might be defensible only if tied to verifiable humanitarian aims, minimal civilian harm, and a credible plan for political transition. Without such conditions, critics warn intervention risks inflaming nationalism, entrenching the regime, and worsening a humanitarian crisis already driving millions to flee. On the other hand, Maduro’s ouster would be good news for almost everyone on earth who’s paying attention. As Claire Berlinski and I agree on the Critical Conditions podcast, he should be offered amnesty and safe passage to get the hell out, along with his despicable cabal.

### Ukraine: Peace or Capitulation?

Peace negotiations between Ukraine and Russia, facilitated by the United States, will resume Sunday in Miami, oddly enough, amid persistent battlefield violence and [growing unease among Ukraine’s European allies about the direction of the talks](https://www.reuters.com/world/europe/us-peace-plan-ukraine-drew-russian-document-sources-say-2025-11-26/). A Ukrainian delegation is set to meet US Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Trump envoy Steve Witkoff in Florida, with First Son-in-Law Jared Kushner also expected to attend. Kyiv is seeking binding international security guarantees and a ceasefire that maintains current frontlines while rejecting any surrender of territory not already under Russian control.

Moscow has shown little sign it is prepared to compromise further. Putin reiterated last week that the war would end only “once Ukrainian troops withdraw from the territories they occupy,” a reference to lands internationally recognized as Ukrainian but partially held by Russia since 2014 and 2022. The talks follow a Geneva session that significantly altered an earlier 28-point framework made publica last week and supposedly co-authored (or perhaps co-“authorized”) by Witkoff and Kirill Dmitriev, head of Russia’s sovereign wealth fund. That initial document — which circulated without broad allied consultation — was widely criticized, especially in Europe, as overly favorable to Moscow. Indeed, at AQL we compared it to the notorious 1938 Munich Agreement, which is remembered as one of history’s more tragic naïve sellouts. A leaner 19-point version is now under discussion after the [resignation of lead Ukrainian negotiator Andriy Yermak in a corruption scandal](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cz0nljm4y74o). Rustem Umerov, head of Ukraine’s National Security and Defense Council, has assumed the role.

The negotiations continue as Russia launches sustained missile and drone attacks on Ukrainian cities and infrastructure. President Volodymyr Zelensky says Russia deployed nearly 1,400 attack drones, 1,100 guided aerial bombs and 66 missiles in the past week alone. Ukraine has responded with long-range drone strikes on Russian oil facilities and shipping, including two sanctioned tankers in the Black Sea over the weekend.

[Some are suggesting a long-term cease-fire may be more likely than a settlement](https://edition.cnn.com/2025/11/30/europe/ukraine-russia-peace-talks-miami-florida-intl). Still, concerns persist that U.S. pressure for a rapid settlement could lock in Russian territorial gains and place limits on Ukraine’s future alliances, including NATO membership. Critics warn that a peace perceived as coerced or unjust could fracture Western unity and encourage autocrats to use military aggression to gain negotiating leverage.

### Netanyahu Asks for a Pardon

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has [formally requested a pardon in his ongoing corruption trial](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2025/nov/30/benjamin-netanyahu-asks-israel-president-isaac-herzog-for-pardon-corruption-case), arguing that managing the country amid security and political crises requires his full attention. The shameless request was submitted Sunday to President Isaac Herzog, along with documents suggesting a pardon would “heal national divisions.” That is rich and a bit of a shakedown, since it was Netanyahu who personally ignited those divisions by launching a ferocious campaign against the legal system the moment charges were introduced.

Netanyahu has been on trial since 2020 for alleged bribery, fraud, and breach of trust in three separate cases. He denies all charges. Throughout the legal process, Netanyahu has repeatedly maintained that a sitting prime minister can continue to serve while under indictment, and previously argued in court that the trial would not interfere with his duties.

The new request has sparked widespread criticism from political opponents, who point out that Netanyahu led efforts in recent years to delegitimize Israel’s law-enforcement and judicial institutions, including public attacks on police investigators, prosecutors, and former Attorney General Avichai Mandelblit. They argue that the prime minister now seeks relief from the same judicial system he has spent years undermining.

Netanyahu’s allies have referenced past instances in which senior officials received presidential pardons, including the “Bus 300 Affair” in the 1980s, when senior Shin Bet figures were pardoned following the killing of captured terrorists. Legal experts note, however, that those officials had admitted wrongdoing and resigned their positions, while Netanyahu continues to contest all allegations.

The request comes as Israel prepares for new elections, with recent polls indicating a possible loss for Netanyahu’s Likud party. Some voices in the opposition have raised the idea of a negotiated exit from political life in exchange for a pardon; many others would prefer to see him instead be crushed in the next elections.

President Herzog is expected to consult legal advisors before responding. Any move toward clemency would likely trigger a constitutional debate and intensify already deep political divisions within the country.

## America’s New Export: Migration Panic

A newly leaked State Department cable reveals that Secretary of State Marco Rubio has instructed US diplomats in Europe, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand to [pressure their host governments to adopt harsher immigration policies](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/us-directs-diplomats-lobby-governments-against-mass-migration-cable-says-2025-11-27/) and to report back whenever those governments appear too welcoming to newcomers. The directive tells diplomats to highlight crimes linked to people with a “migration background,” a phrase lifted directly from far-right European discourse, and to reframe pro-migrant policies as threats to public order and social cohesion to be included in human-rights reports. Reuters, which obtained the cable, describes it as part of a broader push to internationalize Trump’s anti-migration agenda and even to bake these themes into human-rights reporting itself. ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/us-directs-diplomats-lobby-governments-against-mass-migration-cable-says-2025-11-27/?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

The irony is striking. The United States is, more than any nation, a product of mass migration. Its language, culture, and prosperity arose from the very flows of people Washington now urges allies to resist. Yet the cable is not really about policy outcomes abroad. It is about reinforcing a domestic political project. Tough talk on borders polls well. Creating global “evidence” of immigrant menace gives the White House a steady supply of anecdotes with which to stoke public fears at home.

None of this dismisses legitimate challenges associated with rapid demographic change, particularly integration and cultural friction. But the cable bypasses problem-solving in favor of panic messaging. Allies will largely ignore the substance, yet the symbolism will linger. For decades, the United States exported democratic confidence. Today, it exports insecurity. That transformation is noticed, and it will shape how America is viewed long after this administration is gone.

## Bolsonaro Jailed

Jair Bolsonaro’s imprisonment last week marks an extraordinary moment in Brazil’s democratic evolution. Few expected that a former president, backed by a fervent movement and with strong ties to the security forces, would ever face full accountability for attempting to overturn an election. Yet Brazil’s Supreme Court has now upheld a 27-year sentence for his role in plotting a coup, and has ordered him to begin serving time under federal police custody. According to Reuters, justices concluded that Bolsonaro helped orchestrate efforts to subvert the 2022 vote and to prepare a plan for military intervention to keep him in power.

Brazil’s right is now splintered. Some loyalists insist he is a victim of persecution, but a broader segment of society appears relieved to see institutions reassert control. The contrast with the United States is difficult to miss. Both countries endured attempts to subvert democratic outcomes. One has put the instigator behind bars; the other continues to argue over whether accountability is politically safe.

Bolsonaro’s conviction will reshape Brazil’s political terrain for years. He is barred from office until at least 2033, and his movement, once ascendant, lacks a unifying successor. Internationally, his alignment with Donald Trump had strained Brasília’s credibility and contributed to punitive U.S. trade treatment during his tenure. Now, the roles have reversed: Washington’s democratic image has been tarnished by its own backsliding, while Brazil can, at least for the moment, claim to have enforced a clear line against would-be authoritarians.

Of course, while Bolsonaro is the first to be jailed for attempting a coup. The current president, Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, was also, imprisoned for corruption and money-laundering during the “Operation Car Wash” cases. He spent 580 days in prison after a 2017 conviction.

## Rule of Law vs. the Revenge Presidency

A US federal judge’s dismissal of indictments against former FBI director James Comey and New York Attorney General Letitia James struck a sharp blow against the administration’s escalating campaign to criminalize political opposition. The judge ruled that Lindsey Halligan, the interim US attorney who brought the charges, had been unlawfully appointed at Trump’s direction after a previous prosecutor refused to indict for lack of evidence. Because Halligan’s appointment violated statutory limits and bypassed normal checks, all of her actions, including the indictments, were declared invalid.

Rather than prompting restraint, the setback has been followed by ominous rhetoric about turning to military justice to punish critics, including suggestions that lawmakers who remind troops of their duty to refuse unlawful orders are engaging in sedition. Senator and former Navy officer Mark Kelly has been singled out in this context, a sign that the White House is willing to blur the line between democratic disagreement and disloyalty.

## DOGE: The Destruction That Remains

The [quiet collapse of the Department of Government Efficiency](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/fired-rehired-dizzying-confusion-trumps-government-overhaul-2025-02-21/) would be almost farcical if its legacy were not so damaging. Created to slash what it called bureaucratic waste, DOGE quickly became an ideological demolition project. Thousands of federal workers were fired across multiple agencies in a rapid purge overseen by Trump and Elon Musk, including specialists in nuclear oversight, health regulation, and financial supervision. Reuters has documented how the administration is now scrambling to rehire hundreds of those same workers after discovering that critical functions cannot be performed without them, even as the chaos has already weakened key institutions.

The dismantling of the development and humanitarian apparatus has been even more damaging, halting or degrading long-standing programs that supported global health, food security, and disaster response. The result is not leaner government, but hollowed-out capacity and fractured institutional memory. Simultaneously, DOGE aligned operatives gained broad access to sensitive data, eroding privacy norms and raising the prospect of political misuse of tax, benefits, and credit information.

All this unfolds while geopolitical competitors move in the opposite direction. China, for example, has expanded its role as a development partner in Africa and elsewhere, gaining influence through consistent funding and predictable delivery. As Beijing builds credibility by helping others solve problems, Washington is dismantling the machinery that once underpinned its global leadership. Rebuilding that machinery will take years. The damage, and the impression of a superpower busily eroding its own competence, will linger far longer.

Wall Street continues to post record-high numbers, but the celebration masks a troubling imbalance. Strip away the so-called “Magnificent Seven” megacap tech firms and the broader market looks far less exuberant. An [analysis in the Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2025/11/24/sp500-stock-market-tech-nvidia/) notes that an index excluding those seven companies — the “S&P 493” — reveals a much weaker underlying economy, with many smaller and lower-tech firms reporting lackluster sales, slower investment, and tighter margins under the weight of higher borrowing costs.

This divergence reflects a deeper structural split. One part of the economy is riding an AI-and-cloud arms race; another is struggling through a slower, more constrained post-pandemic reality. Capital and investor enthusiasm have concentrated in a handful of firms that promise technological transformation, while companies outside this narrow cluster have been largely ignored, even when they employ more people and are tightly tied to everyday economic life.

So when political leaders cite market highs as proof of broad national prosperity, they are leaning on an indicator increasingly driven by a few trillion-dollar companies whose fortunes may not track the well-being of most households or businesses. If those firms stumble or simply plateau, the illusion of a booming economy could evaporate quickly. What the S&P 493 shows is that much of corporate America is not sharing in the apparent boom. The economy is not roaring; parts of it are. That is a very different story, which AQL will investigate in a report on the potential AI bubble this week.

Beware: Turbulence may be coming.
